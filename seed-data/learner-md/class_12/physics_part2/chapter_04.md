## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter traces the development of atomic models from Thomson's model to Rutherford's nuclear model and then to Bohr's model of hydrogen. The main problem is this: atoms are stable and emit line spectra, but classical physics could not explain either fact. Bohr introduced quantum postulates to explain hydrogen spectra, and de Broglie's matter waves later gave a physical explanation for quantised orbits.

The chapter is conceptually important because it shows how classical mechanics, electromagnetism, spectroscopy, and quantum ideas meet.

# Unit 4A: Thomson's Atomic Model

J. J. Thomson discovered the electron and proposed an early atomic model.

According to Thomson's model:

- atom is a sphere of uniform positive charge;
- electrons are embedded inside it;
- total positive charge equals total negative charge;
- atom as a whole is neutral.

This is often called the plum pudding model.

## Limitation

Thomson's model could not explain large-angle scattering of alpha particles. If positive charge were spread throughout the atom, an alpha particle should experience only weak distributed repulsion and should not bounce back through large angles.

# Unit 4B: Geiger-Marsden Experiment

Geiger and Marsden performed alpha-particle scattering experiments under Rutherford's guidance.

Experimental arrangement:

- alpha particles from a radioactive substance were collimated into a narrow beam;
- beam was directed at a very thin gold foil;
- scattered alpha particles were detected by a zinc sulphide screen;
- scintillations were counted at different angles.

## Observations

The main observations were:

- most alpha particles passed straight through the foil;
- some were deflected through small angles;
- very few were deflected through angles greater than 90 degrees;
- about 1 in several thousand rebounded strongly.

## Interpretation

Most of the atom must be empty space because most alpha particles pass through.

Large-angle scattering means:

- positive charge is concentrated in a very small region;
- this region is massive enough to repel alpha particles strongly;
- it is at the centre of atom.

This central region is the nucleus.

# Unit 4C: Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Rutherford proposed:

- atom has a tiny positively charged nucleus;
- nearly all mass is concentrated in the nucleus;
- electrons revolve around the nucleus;
- atom is mostly empty space;
- nuclear radius is about `10^-15 m` to `10^-14 m`;
- atomic radius is about `10^-10 m`.

The atom is therefore about `10^4` to `10^5` times larger than its nucleus.

## Why Most Alpha Particles Pass Through

Because the nucleus occupies a tiny fraction of the atom's volume. An alpha particle usually passes through empty space without coming close to the nucleus.

# Unit 4D: Impact Parameter

Impact parameter `b` is the perpendicular distance between the initial path of the alpha particle and the centre of the nucleus.

Small `b`:

- alpha particle passes close to nucleus;
- strong repulsion;
- large scattering angle.

Large `b`:

- alpha particle remains far from nucleus;
- weak repulsion;
- small deflection.

Head-on approach:

- `b = 0`;
- alpha particle comes closest and may reverse direction.

# Unit 4E: Distance of Closest Approach

For head-on collision, alpha particle momentarily stops at closest approach. Its kinetic energy becomes electrostatic potential energy.

For alpha particle of charge `+2e` and nucleus of charge `+Ze`:

`K = (1/(4 pi epsilon0))(2Ze^2/d)`

So:

`d = (1/(4 pi epsilon0))(2Ze^2/K)`

This gives an upper limit on nuclear size.

## Physical Meaning

If the alpha particle turns around before touching the nucleus, the actual nuclear radius must be smaller than the closest approach distance.

# Unit 4F: Classical Electron Orbits

In Rutherford's model, electron revolves around nucleus. For hydrogen:

Coulomb attraction provides centripetal force:

`e^2/(4 pi epsilon0 r^2) = mv^2/r`

From this:

`mv^2 = e^2/(4 pi epsilon0 r)`

Kinetic energy:

`K = (1/2)mv^2 = e^2/(8 pi epsilon0 r)`

Potential energy:

`U = -e^2/(4 pi epsilon0 r)`

Total energy:

`E = K + U = -e^2/(8 pi epsilon0 r)`

Negative energy means the electron is bound to the nucleus.

# Unit 4G: Failure of Rutherford's Model

Rutherford's model used classical physics. It faced two major problems.

## Stability Problem

An electron in circular motion is accelerated. Classical electromagnetic theory says an accelerated charge emits radiation and loses energy.

Therefore:

- electron should lose energy continuously;
- orbit radius should decrease;
- electron should spiral into nucleus;
- atom should collapse.

But atoms are stable.

## Spectrum Problem

If electron energy changes continuously, emitted radiation should have continuous range of frequencies.

But atomic gases emit discrete line spectra.

Therefore Rutherford's model was incomplete.

# Unit 4H: Atomic Spectra

When a rarefied gas is excited, it emits light of only certain wavelengths. This is an emission line spectrum.

When white light passes through the gas, the gas absorbs the same wavelengths it can emit. This produces an absorption line spectrum.

## Importance

Line spectra act like fingerprints of elements. Hydrogen, helium, sodium, mercury, and other elements have characteristic spectral lines.

The existence of line spectra shows that atoms have discrete energy levels.

# Unit 4I: Bohr's Model of Hydrogen

Bohr kept the nuclear model but added quantum postulates.

## Postulate 1: Stationary Orbits

Electrons revolve only in certain stable orbits without radiating energy.

These are called stationary states.

## Postulate 2: Angular Momentum Quantisation

Only those orbits are allowed for which:

`mvr = nh/(2 pi)`

where:

- `n = 1, 2, 3, ...`;
- `n` is principal quantum number.

## Postulate 3: Radiation During Transition

Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when electron jumps between two allowed states.

For transition from higher energy `Ei` to lower energy `Ef`:

`h nu = Ei - Ef`

If electron absorbs photon:

`Ef - Ei = h nu`

# Unit 4J: Bohr Radius and Energy Levels

For hydrogen:

`rn = a0 n^2`

where:

`a0 = 5.3 x 10^-11 m`

Energy levels:

`En = -13.6/n^2 eV`

For important values:

| n | Energy |
|---|---|
| 1 | `-13.6 eV` |
| 2 | `-3.40 eV` |
| 3 | `-1.51 eV` |
| 4 | `-0.85 eV` |
| infinity | `0 eV` |

## Ground State

For `n = 1`, hydrogen is in ground state.

Energy:

`E1 = -13.6 eV`

Ionisation energy from ground state:

`13.6 eV`

## Excited States

For `n > 1`, atom is in excited state.

First excited state:

`n = 2`

Energy required to excite hydrogen from `n = 1` to `n = 2`:

`E2 - E1 = -3.40 - (-13.6) = 10.2 eV`

# Unit 4K: Hydrogen Spectral Lines

When electron jumps from `ni` to `nf`, photon energy is:

`h nu = Eni - Enf`

Using energy formula:

`E = -13.6/n^2 eV`

Photon energy:

`E_photon = 13.6(1/nf^2 - 1/ni^2) eV`

where `ni > nf`.

## Spectral Series

| Series | Final level | Region |
|---|---|---|
| Lyman | `nf = 1` | Ultraviolet |
| Balmer | `nf = 2` | Visible and near ultraviolet |
| Paschen | `nf = 3` | Infrared |
| Brackett | `nf = 4` | Infrared |
| Pfund | `nf = 5` | Infrared |

The visible hydrogen lines mainly belong to Balmer series.

# Unit 4L: de Broglie's Explanation of Quantisation

De Broglie proposed that electron has matter wave nature:

`lambda = h/mv`

For an electron in a stable circular orbit, the matter wave must form a standing wave. Therefore circumference must contain a whole number of wavelengths:

`2 pi r = n lambda`

Substitute `lambda = h/mv`:

`2 pi r = n h/(mv)`

So:

`mvr = nh/(2 pi)`

This is Bohr's angular momentum quantisation condition.

## Meaning

Only those orbits survive in which the electron wave fits perfectly around the orbit. Other orbits lead to destructive self-interference.

# Unit 4M: Limitations of Bohr's Model

Bohr's model was successful for hydrogen and hydrogen-like atoms, but limited.

Limitations:

- cannot explain spectra of multi-electron atoms;
- cannot explain relative intensities of spectral lines;
- cannot fully explain fine structure of spectral lines;
- does not include uncertainty principle;
- uses fixed electron orbits, which are not part of modern quantum mechanics;
- works mainly for one-electron systems.

Still, Bohr's model is important because it introduced quantised energy levels and explained hydrogen spectrum.

# Unit 4N: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Empty Space in Atom

Atomic radius is about `10^-10 m` and nuclear radius is about `10^-15 m`. Find ratio of atomic radius to nuclear radius.

`ratio = 10^-10 / 10^-15 = 10^5`

The atom is about `100000` times larger in radius than the nucleus. This shows that most of an atom is empty space.

## Example 2: Energy of Hydrogen Level

Find energy of hydrogen atom in `n = 3`.

`En = -13.6/n^2 eV`

`E3 = -13.6/9 = -1.51 eV`

## Example 3: Excitation Energy

Find energy needed to excite hydrogen from `n = 1` to `n = 3`.

`E1 = -13.6 eV`

`E3 = -1.51 eV`

Energy required:

`E3 - E1 = -1.51 - (-13.6) = 12.09 eV`

## Example 4: Photon Emitted in Transition

Electron falls from `n = 3` to `n = 2`. Find photon energy.

`E3 = -1.51 eV`

`E2 = -3.40 eV`

Photon energy:

`E = E3 - E2 = -1.51 - (-3.40) = 1.89 eV`

## Example 5: Radius of Second Orbit

For hydrogen, `r1 = a0 = 5.3 x 10^-11 m`. Find `r2`.

`rn = a0 n^2`

`r2 = 5.3 x 10^-11 x 4`

`r2 = 2.12 x 10^-10 m`

# Unit 4O: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain Rutherford experiment

1. Describe alpha beam, thin gold foil, and detector.
2. State observations: most pass, few deflect, very few rebound.
3. Explain empty space from straight passage.
4. Explain nucleus from large deflection.
5. State conclusion: small dense positive nucleus.

## To explain failure of Rutherford model

1. Electron revolving around nucleus is accelerated.
2. Accelerated charge should radiate energy.
3. Electron should spiral into nucleus.
4. This contradicts atomic stability.
5. Continuous energy loss predicts continuous spectrum, but atoms emit line spectra.

## To write Bohr postulates

1. Stationary orbits do not radiate.
2. Angular momentum is quantised: `mvr = nh/(2 pi)`.
3. Radiation occurs during transition: `h nu = Ei - Ef`.

## To solve energy level problems

1. Use `En = -13.6/n^2 eV`.
2. Find initial and final energies.
3. For emission, subtract lower energy from higher energy.
4. Convert to wavelength if required using `E = hc/lambda`.
5. Identify series from final level.

# Unit 4P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying Rutherford proved electrons are inside nucleus. | Rutherford showed positive charge and mass are in nucleus; electrons are outside. |
| Saying most alpha particles rebound. | Most pass straight; very few rebound. |
| Treating impact parameter as scattering angle. | It is perpendicular distance from initial path to nucleus centre. |
| Forgetting negative sign of energy. | Bound electron has negative total energy. |
| Confusing excitation and ionisation. | Excitation moves electron to higher bound level; ionisation removes it completely. |
| Using Bohr model for all atoms. | It works mainly for hydrogenic atoms. |
| Saying electron radiates in stationary orbit. | In Bohr model, stationary orbit has no radiation. |

## Final Revision

- Thomson model has diffuse positive charge; Rutherford showed positive charge is concentrated.
- Most alpha particles pass through because atom is mostly empty.
- Large-angle scattering proves small dense positive nucleus.
- Rutherford model fails because accelerating electron should radiate and collapse.
- Line spectra require discrete energy levels.
- Bohr postulates introduced stationary orbits, angular momentum quantisation, and transition radiation.
- Hydrogen energy: `En = -13.6/n^2 eV`.
- Ionisation from ground state needs `13.6 eV`.
- Emitted photon energy equals difference of two energy levels.
- de Broglie waves explain allowed orbits as standing waves.
