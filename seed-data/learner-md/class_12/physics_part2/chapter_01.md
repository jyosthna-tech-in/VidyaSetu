# Chapter 1: Ray Optics and Optical Instruments
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Ray optics studies light by treating its path as a straight line called a ray. This approximation works when the wavelength of light is much smaller than the size of mirrors, lenses, prisms, and apertures. The chapter builds from reflection and refraction to image formation, total internal reflection, lens systems, prisms, and optical instruments.

The learner must be able to draw rays correctly, use sign convention without confusion, identify real and virtual images, and solve numerical problems involving mirrors, lenses, prisms, microscopes, and telescopes.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain why ray optics is valid for ordinary optical instruments;
- apply the laws of reflection to plane and spherical mirrors;
- use the Cartesian sign convention consistently;
- derive and apply the mirror formula and magnification formula;
- explain refraction using Snell's law and refractive index;
- identify conditions for total internal reflection;
- explain applications of total internal reflection, especially optical fibres;
- apply the formula for refraction at a spherical surface;
- use the thin lens formula, lens maker's formula, and power of a lens;
- combine powers of lenses in contact;
- explain deviation through a prism and the minimum deviation condition;
- derive magnifying power of simple and compound microscopes;
- derive magnifying power of astronomical telescopes.

# Unit 1A: Ray Optics Approximation

Light is an electromagnetic wave, but in many optical situations its wavelength is very small compared with the size of the optical device. Visible light has wavelength about `400 nm to 750 nm`, whereas mirrors, lenses, prisms, and ordinary apertures are usually many millimetres or centimetres wide.

In this case:

- diffraction is negligible;
- light can be represented by rays;
- energy travels along straight paths in a homogeneous medium;
- reflection and refraction can be treated geometrically.

Ray optics is therefore a practical approximation, not a denial of wave nature.

## Important Limitation

Ray optics fails when the aperture or obstacle size becomes comparable with wavelength. Then diffraction and interference must be considered, which is the subject of wave optics.

# Unit 1B: Cartesian Sign Convention

The Cartesian sign convention is the backbone of numerical questions in this chapter.

Rules:

- All distances are measured from the pole of the mirror or optical centre of the lens.
- Direction of incident light is taken as positive.
- Distances measured opposite to incident light are negative.
- Heights above the principal axis are positive.
- Heights below the principal axis are negative.

For the common case of light incident from left to right:

- distances to the right are positive;
- distances to the left are negative;
- real image formed on the right of a lens has positive `v`;
- real image formed in front of a mirror is on the left, so `v` is negative.

## Why This Matters

Without sign convention, every case needs a separate formula. With sign convention, the same mirror formula and lens formula work for real and virtual images, concave and convex mirrors, and convex and concave lenses.

# Unit 1C: Reflection by Spherical Mirrors

The laws of reflection are:

- angle of incidence equals angle of reflection;
- incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane.

For a spherical mirror, the normal at any point is along the radius through the centre of curvature.

## Principal Focus

For a concave mirror, rays parallel to the principal axis actually meet at the focus after reflection.

For a convex mirror, rays parallel to the principal axis appear to diverge from the focus behind the mirror.

For paraxial rays:

`f = R/2`

where:

- `f` is focal length;
- `R` is radius of curvature.

## Concave Mirror

Main features:

- converging mirror;
- focal length is negative under the usual sign convention;
- can form real, inverted images or virtual, erect images depending on object position.

## Convex Mirror

Main features:

- diverging mirror;
- focal length is positive when light travels left to right and focus lies behind the mirror;
- always forms virtual, erect, diminished images.

# Unit 1D: Mirror Formula

The relation among object distance `u`, image distance `v`, and focal length `f` is:

`1/v + 1/u = 1/f`

This formula is valid for both concave and convex spherical mirrors when the sign convention is applied.

## Magnification by Mirror

Linear magnification is:

`m = h'/h = -v/u`

where:

- `h` is object height;
- `h'` is image height.

Interpretation:

- `m > 0`: image is erect;
- `m < 0`: image is inverted;
- `|m| > 1`: image is enlarged;
- `|m| < 1`: image is diminished.

## Standard Ray Choices

To draw images with spherical mirrors, use any two of these:

- ray parallel to principal axis reflects through focus, or appears from focus;
- ray through centre of curvature retraces its path;
- ray through focus reflects parallel to principal axis;
- ray incident at pole follows laws of reflection.

# Unit 1E: Refraction and Snell's Law

Refraction is the change in direction of light when it enters another transparent medium obliquely.

The laws of refraction are:

- incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane;
- `sin i / sin r` is constant for a given pair of media.

General form:

`n1 sin i = n2 sin r`

where `n1` and `n2` are absolute refractive indices.

## Refractive Index

Absolute refractive index is:

`n = c/v`

where:

- `c` is speed of light in vacuum;
- `v` is speed of light in the medium.

If light enters a medium with greater refractive index, it slows down and bends towards the normal. If it enters a medium with smaller refractive index, it speeds up and bends away from the normal.

## Optical Density Is Not Mass Density

Optically denser means lower speed of light, not necessarily greater mass density. A liquid can be optically denser than water even if it is less dense by mass.

# Unit 1F: Apparent Depth and Lateral Shift

When viewing an object inside water or glass from air, it appears raised.

For near-normal viewing:

`apparent depth = real depth / refractive index`

Therefore:

- a pond appears shallower than it really is;
- a coin inside water appears raised;
- a printed page under a glass slab appears raised.

## Glass Slab

For a rectangular glass slab:

- emergent ray is parallel to incident ray;
- direction is not permanently changed;
- ray suffers lateral displacement.

# Unit 1G: Total Internal Reflection

Total internal reflection occurs when:

- light travels from denser medium to rarer medium;
- angle of incidence in denser medium is greater than critical angle.

At critical angle:

`r = 90 degrees`

For `n1 > n2`:

`sin ic = n2/n1`

If the rarer medium is air:

`sin ic = 1/n`

## Features

Total internal reflection is almost complete reflection. Unlike ordinary reflection, no refracted ray carries energy into the second medium.

## Applications

Important applications:

- sparkling of diamonds;
- optical fibres;
- endoscopy;
- communication through fibre cables;
- totally reflecting prisms;
- binoculars and periscopes.

## Optical Fibre

An optical fibre guides light through repeated total internal reflection.

Basic structure:

- core has higher refractive index;
- cladding has lower refractive index;
- light entering within an acceptance range remains trapped and travels long distances.

Uses:

- telecommunication;
- medical endoscopy;
- decorative illumination;
- sensors.

# Unit 1H: Refraction at a Spherical Surface

For refraction at a spherical surface separating medium `n1` and medium `n2`:

`n2/v - n1/u = (n2 - n1)/R`

where:

- `u` is object distance;
- `v` is image distance;
- `R` is radius of curvature;
- distances follow Cartesian sign convention.

This formula is the starting point for deriving the thin lens formula.

## Physical Meaning

Refraction at a curved surface changes convergence or divergence of a ray bundle. A convex spherical surface can converge rays when light enters a denser medium, but the result depends on sign of `R` and relative refractive indices.

# Unit 1I: Thin Lenses

A thin lens is a transparent medium bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is spherical.

Types:

- convex lens: converging lens, positive focal length in air;
- concave lens: diverging lens, negative focal length in air.

Thin lens formula:

`1/v - 1/u = 1/f`

Lens magnification:

`m = h'/h = v/u`

## Ray Rules for Convex Lens

Use any two:

- ray parallel to principal axis passes through second focus after refraction;
- ray through optical centre goes undeviated;
- ray through first focus emerges parallel to principal axis.

## Ray Rules for Concave Lens

Use any two:

- ray parallel to principal axis appears to diverge from first focus;
- ray through optical centre goes undeviated;
- ray directed towards second focus emerges parallel to principal axis.

# Unit 1J: Lens Maker's Formula

For a thin lens in air:

`1/f = (n - 1)(1/R1 - 1/R2)`

where:

- `n` is refractive index of lens material relative to surrounding medium;
- `R1` and `R2` are radii of curvature of first and second surfaces.

General medium form:

`1/f = (n2/n1 - 1)(1/R1 - 1/R2)`

where:

- `n2` is refractive index of lens;
- `n1` is refractive index of surrounding medium.

## Consequence

If a lens is placed in a liquid having the same refractive index as the lens material, then:

`n2/n1 = 1`

So:

`1/f = 0`

The lens loses its converging or diverging action and behaves like a plane glass sheet.

# Unit 1K: Power of a Lens

Power measures the ability of a lens to converge or diverge light.

`P = 1/f`

where `f` is in metre.

Unit:

`1 dioptre = 1 m^-1`

Sign:

- convex lens: positive power;
- concave lens: negative power.

Examples:

- `f = 0.5 m` gives `P = +2 D`;
- `f = -0.25 m` gives `P = -4 D`.

# Unit 1L: Combination of Thin Lenses in Contact

If lenses are in contact:

`1/f = 1/f1 + 1/f2 + 1/f3 + ...`

In terms of power:

`P = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...`

This is an algebraic sum. A concave lens contributes negative power.

## Total Magnification

For a lens combination:

`m = m1 x m2 x m3 x ...`

## Why Lenses Are Combined

Combination of lenses helps to:

- obtain desired focal length;
- reduce optical defects;
- improve image sharpness;
- design microscopes, telescopes, cameras, and eyepieces.

# Unit 1M: Prism

A prism refracts light through two plane surfaces inclined at angle `A`, called the angle of prism.

Key relations:

`r1 + r2 = A`

`delta = i + e - A`

where:

- `i` is angle of incidence;
- `e` is angle of emergence;
- `delta` is angle of deviation;
- `A` is angle of prism.

## Minimum Deviation

At minimum deviation:

- path of light through prism is symmetric;
- `i = e`;
- `r1 = r2 = A/2`;
- `delta = Dm`.

Refractive index:

`n = sin[(A + Dm)/2] / sin(A/2)`

For a thin prism:

`Dm = (n - 1)A`

when angles are small and measured in radians.

# Unit 1N: Dispersion by Prism

Dispersion is splitting of white light into its component colours.

Reason:

- refractive index depends on wavelength;
- violet light has higher refractive index in glass than red light;
- violet deviates more than red.

Order in spectrum:

Violet, indigo, blue, green, yellow, orange, red.

## Angular Dispersion

Angular dispersion is the angular separation between extreme colours, usually violet and red.

## Dispersive Power

Dispersive power measures how strongly a material separates colours compared with its mean deviation.

# Unit 1O: Optical Instruments

Optical instruments use mirrors, lenses, and prisms to form enlarged, clear, or distant images.

Common instruments:

- simple microscope;
- compound microscope;
- telescope;
- human eye;
- binoculars;
- camera.

The least distance of distinct vision for a normal eye is:

`D = 25 cm`

# Unit 1P: Simple Microscope

A simple microscope is a single convex lens of small focal length.

It forms:

- virtual image;
- erect image;
- magnified image.

## Final Image at Near Point

Magnifying power:

`M = 1 + D/f`

## Final Image at Infinity

Magnifying power:

`M = D/f`

Image at infinity gives relaxed viewing and less eye strain.

# Unit 1Q: Compound Microscope

A compound microscope uses two convex lenses:

- objective lens: small focal length, forms enlarged real image;
- eyepiece: acts as simple microscope and magnifies the intermediate image.

For final image at near point:

`M = (L/fo)(1 + D/fe)`

For final image at infinity:

`M = (L/fo)(D/fe)`

where:

- `L` is tube length;
- `fo` is focal length of objective;
- `fe` is focal length of eyepiece.

## Design Idea

To get high magnification:

- objective focal length should be small;
- eyepiece focal length should be small;
- tube length should be large.

# Unit 1R: Astronomical Telescope

An astronomical telescope is used to view distant objects.

It has:

- objective of large focal length and large aperture;
- eyepiece of small focal length.

## Normal Adjustment

Final image is at infinity.

Magnifying power:

`M = fo/fe`

Tube length:

`L = fo + fe`

## Final Image at Near Point

Magnifying power:

`M = (fo/fe)(1 + fe/D)`

Tube length is slightly less than `fo + fe`.

## Aperture Matters

The objective should have large aperture because distant objects send very little light. A large aperture gathers more light and improves resolving ability.

# Unit 1S: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Concave Mirror Image

An object is placed `10 cm` in front of a concave mirror of radius `15 cm`. Find image distance and nature.

Given:

- `R = -15 cm`
- `f = R/2 = -7.5 cm`
- `u = -10 cm`

Use:

`1/v + 1/u = 1/f`

So:

`1/v - 1/10 = -1/7.5`

`1/v = -1/7.5 + 1/10`

`1/v = (-4 + 3)/30 = -1/30`

`v = -30 cm`

Magnification:

`m = -v/u = -(-30)/(-10) = -3`

Result:

- image is `30 cm` in front of mirror;
- real;
- inverted;
- enlarged three times.

## Example 2: Critical Angle

Find critical angle for glass-air interface if refractive index of glass is `1.5`.

For glass to air:

`sin ic = 1/n = 1/1.5 = 0.667`

`ic = sin^-1(0.667) = about 41.8 degrees`

If incidence angle in glass is greater than `41.8 degrees`, total internal reflection occurs.

## Example 3: Power of Lens

A concave lens has focal length `-25 cm`. Find power.

Convert:

`f = -25 cm = -0.25 m`

`P = 1/f = 1/(-0.25) = -4 D`

The negative sign confirms it is a diverging lens.

## Example 4: Lens Combination

Two lenses of powers `+3 D` and `-1 D` are in contact. Find equivalent focal length.

`P = P1 + P2 = 3 - 1 = +2 D`

`f = 1/P = 1/2 m = 0.5 m`

The combination is converging.

## Example 5: Prism Minimum Deviation

A prism has angle `60 degrees` and minimum deviation `40 degrees`. Find refractive index.

`n = sin[(A + Dm)/2] / sin(A/2)`

`n = sin[(60 + 40)/2] / sin(60/2)`

`n = sin 50 degrees / sin 30 degrees`

`n = 0.766 / 0.5 = 1.532`

# Unit 1T: Answer-Writing Framework

## To solve mirror problems

1. Draw rough ray direction.
2. Assign signs to `u`, `v`, `f`, and `R`.
3. Use `f = R/2` if radius is given.
4. Apply `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`.
5. Use `m = -v/u`.
6. Interpret signs of `v` and `m`.

## To solve lens problems

1. Identify convex or concave lens.
2. Assign signs using incident-light direction.
3. Use `1/v - 1/u = 1/f`.
4. Use `m = v/u`.
5. For lens combinations, add powers.
6. Convert focal length to metre before using power.

## To explain total internal reflection

1. State that light must go from denser to rarer medium.
2. Define critical angle.
3. State `sin ic = n2/n1`.
4. Explain that for `i > ic`, refracted ray is absent.
5. Mention complete reflection and one application.

## To answer optical instrument questions

1. Name the lenses used.
2. State image formed by objective.
3. State image formed by eyepiece.
4. Give magnifying power formula.
5. Explain design requirements such as focal length and aperture.

# Unit 1U: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Using mirror and lens formula interchangeably. | Mirror: `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`; lens: `1/v - 1/u = 1/f`. |
| Forgetting signs. | Assign signs before substituting values. |
| Treating optical density as mass density. | Optical density depends on speed of light in medium. |
| Saying convex mirror forms real image. | Convex mirror normally forms virtual, erect, diminished image. |
| Using focal length in cm for power. | Convert focal length to metre before `P = 1/f`. |
| Saying total internal reflection occurs from rarer to denser medium. | It occurs only from denser to rarer medium. |
| Assuming all virtual images are diminished. | A concave mirror can form virtual and enlarged image. |
| Forgetting that prism minimum deviation has symmetric path. | At minimum deviation, `i = e` and `r1 = r2`. |

## Final Revision

- Ray optics works when wavelength is much smaller than aperture size.
- Use Cartesian sign convention carefully.
- Mirror formula: `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`.
- Lens formula: `1/v - 1/u = 1/f`.
- Mirror magnification: `m = -v/u`.
- Lens magnification: `m = v/u`.
- Refractive index: `n = c/v`.
- Snell's law: `n1 sin i = n2 sin r`.
- Total internal reflection needs denser to rarer travel and `i > ic`.
- Thin lens power is `P = 1/f` in metre.
- Lenses in contact add by power.
- Prism minimum deviation gives `n = sin[(A + Dm)/2]/sin(A/2)`.
- Simple microscope and telescope use angular magnification, not just image size.
