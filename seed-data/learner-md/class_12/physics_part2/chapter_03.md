# Chapter 3: Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter explains why light cannot be understood only as a wave. Interference, diffraction, and polarisation show wave nature, but the photoelectric effect shows particle-like exchange of energy. Einstein explained this using photons. De Broglie then proposed that matter particles also have wave nature. The chapter therefore builds the idea of wave-particle duality.

The learner must understand experimental observations, not only formulas. Most exam questions test whether you can connect intensity, frequency, stopping potential, threshold frequency, work function, photon energy, and de Broglie wavelength correctly.

- state why classical wave theory fails for photoelectric effect;

- describe photon energy and momentum;
- explain wave-particle duality of radiation;
- apply de Broglie's relation to matter waves;
- explain why matter waves are noticeable only for microscopic particles.

# Unit 3A: Background to Dual Nature

Wave nature of light explains:

- interference;
- diffraction;
- polarisation.

But experiments involving energy exchange with matter showed that light sometimes behaves as if energy is delivered in packets.

Important developments:

- cathode rays showed existence of electrons;
- Millikan measured electronic charge;
- photoelectric effect revealed particle-like behaviour of light;
- de Broglie extended duality to matter.

# Unit 3B: Electron Emission

Metals contain free electrons, but these electrons cannot escape easily from the surface. The metal surface attracts an escaping electron back. So an electron needs minimum energy to leave the surface.

This minimum energy is called work function:

`phi0`

It is usually measured in electron volt.

`1 eV = 1.602 x 10^-19 J`

## Types of Electron Emission

| Emission | Energy supplied by |
|---|---|
| Thermionic emission | Heating the metal. |
| Field emission | Applying a very strong electric field. |
| Photoelectric emission | Illuminating metal with suitable light. |

# Unit 3C: Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric effect is emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of suitable frequency falls on it.

The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons.

The current produced by collecting these electrons is called photocurrent.

## Important Experimental Features

The photoelectric effect shows four major facts:

- photocurrent increases with intensity of light;
- maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency, not intensity;
- below threshold frequency, no emission occurs no matter how intense the light is;
- emission is almost instantaneous.

# Unit 3D: Experimental Arrangement

Main parts:

- evacuated glass or quartz tube;
- photosensitive emitter plate;
- collector plate;
- monochromatic light;
- variable battery;
- microammeter;
- voltmeter.

Light falls on emitter. Electrons are emitted and collected by collector if potential allows them to reach it.

By changing:

- intensity of light;
- frequency of light;
- collector potential;
- emitter material;

we study the photoelectric effect.

# Unit 3E: Effect of Intensity

If frequency is above threshold and collector potential is fixed positive:

- increasing intensity increases photocurrent;
- photocurrent is proportional to number of emitted photoelectrons per second.

But intensity does not increase maximum kinetic energy of individual photoelectrons.

## Photon Explanation

Greater intensity means more photons per second. More photons eject more electrons per second, so photocurrent increases.

Each photon still has the same energy `h nu` if frequency is unchanged.

# Unit 3F: Effect of Collector Potential

When collector is positive:

- it attracts photoelectrons;
- photocurrent increases with positive potential;
- eventually all emitted electrons are collected;
- current reaches saturation current.

When collector is negative:

- it repels photoelectrons;
- only energetic electrons reach collector;
- photocurrent decreases;
- at stopping potential, photocurrent becomes zero.

Stopping potential:

`V0`

Maximum kinetic energy:

`Kmax = e V0`

## Meaning of Saturation Current

Saturation current depends mainly on intensity of incident light because it represents total number of emitted electrons reaching collector per second.

# Unit 3G: Effect of Frequency

For a given metal:

- below threshold frequency `nu0`, no photoemission occurs;
- above threshold frequency, photoemission occurs;
- stopping potential increases linearly with frequency;
- maximum kinetic energy increases with frequency.

Graph of `V0` versus `nu`:

- straight line;
- slope `h/e`;
- intercept on frequency axis gives `nu0`;
- intercept on potential axis relates to work function.

# Unit 3H: Failure of Classical Wave Theory

Classical wave theory assumed light energy is spread continuously over the wavefront.

It predicted:

- higher intensity should give higher kinetic energy;
- low intensity should cause delayed emission;
- sufficiently intense low-frequency light should eject electrons eventually.

Experiments showed:

- kinetic energy depends on frequency, not intensity;
- emission is instantaneous;
- below threshold frequency, no emission occurs even with high intensity.

Therefore classical wave theory fails for photoelectric effect.

# Unit 3I: Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Einstein proposed that light energy is absorbed in packets called photons.

Energy of each photon:

`E = h nu`

When a photon is absorbed by an electron:

- part of energy removes electron from metal;
- remaining energy appears as kinetic energy.

So:

`h nu = phi0 + Kmax`

or:

`Kmax = h nu - phi0`

Since:

`phi0 = h nu0`

we get:

`Kmax = h(nu - nu0)`

Using stopping potential:

`e V0 = h nu - phi0`

## Explanation of Observations

Intensity:

- changes number of photons;
- changes photocurrent;
- does not change energy per photon.

Frequency:

- changes energy per photon;
- changes maximum kinetic energy.

Threshold frequency:

- if `h nu < phi0`, electron cannot escape.

Instantaneous emission:

- electron absorbs photon energy in one event.

# Unit 3J: Photon Properties

In interaction with matter, radiation behaves as photons.

Properties:

- energy: `E = h nu`;
- momentum: `p = h nu/c = h/lambda`;
- speed in vacuum: `c`;
- electrically neutral;
- not deflected by electric or magnetic fields;
- energy and momentum are conserved in photon-particle collisions;
- number of photons need not be conserved.

## Intensity in Photon Picture

For fixed frequency, higher intensity means more photons crossing unit area per second.

It does not mean each photon has more energy.

# Unit 3K: Wave-Particle Duality of Radiation

Radiation shows dual nature:

- wave nature in interference, diffraction, polarisation;
- particle nature in photoelectric effect, Compton effect, and energy transfer processes.

Which picture is useful depends on the experiment.

For propagation and superposition, wave picture is often useful.

For emission and absorption, photon picture is often necessary.

# Unit 3L: de Broglie Matter Waves

De Broglie proposed that moving matter particles have wave nature.

For a particle of momentum `p`:

`lambda = h/p`

For non-relativistic particle:

`p = mv`

So:

`lambda = h/mv`

This is the de Broglie wavelength.

## Meaning

The formula connects:

- wave quantity: wavelength;
- particle quantity: momentum.

Planck's constant connects both descriptions.

# Unit 3M: Why Matter Waves Are Not Seen Daily

For macroscopic objects, mass is large, so momentum is large. Therefore:

`lambda = h/p`

is extremely small.

Example:

A ball has a de Broglie wavelength far too small to measure.

For electrons, mass is tiny and de Broglie wavelength can be comparable to atomic spacing. Therefore electron diffraction is observable.

# Unit 3N: Electron Accelerated by Potential

If an electron is accelerated through potential difference `V`, it gains kinetic energy:

`K = eV`

For non-relativistic motion:

`K = p^2/(2m)`

So:

`p = sqrt(2meV)`

Therefore:

`lambda = h/sqrt(2meV)`

This formula is useful in electron diffraction and matter wave numericals.

# Unit 3O: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Photon Energy

A laser emits light of frequency `6.0 x 10^14 Hz`. Find energy per photon.

Use:

`E = h nu`

`E = (6.63 x 10^-34)(6.0 x 10^14)`

`E = 3.98 x 10^-19 J`

In eV:

`E = (3.98 x 10^-19)/(1.6 x 10^-19) = 2.49 eV`

## Example 2: Number of Photons per Second

Power of laser is `2.0 x 10^-3 W`. Photon energy is `3.98 x 10^-19 J`. Find photons per second.

Power is energy per second:

`P = N E`

`N = P/E`

`N = (2.0 x 10^-3)/(3.98 x 10^-19)`

`N = 5.0 x 10^15 photons/s`

## Example 3: Threshold Frequency

Work function of caesium is `2.14 eV`. Find threshold frequency.

Convert work function:

`phi0 = 2.14 x 1.6 x 10^-19 J`

`phi0 = 3.424 x 10^-19 J`

Use:

`nu0 = phi0/h`

`nu0 = (3.424 x 10^-19)/(6.63 x 10^-34)`

`nu0 = 5.16 x 10^14 Hz`

## Example 4: Stopping Potential

Light of frequency `8.0 x 10^14 Hz` falls on a metal of work function `2.0 eV`. Find stopping potential.

Photon energy:

`E = h nu = (6.63 x 10^-34)(8.0 x 10^14)`

`E = 5.304 x 10^-19 J`

In eV:

`E = 5.304/1.6 = 3.315 eV`

Maximum kinetic energy:

`Kmax = 3.315 - 2.0 = 1.315 eV`

Since `Kmax = eV0`, stopping potential in volts is:

`V0 = 1.315 V`

## Example 5: de Broglie Wavelength of Electron

An electron moves with speed `5.4 x 10^6 m/s`. Find de Broglie wavelength.

Given:

- `m = 9.11 x 10^-31 kg`
- `h = 6.63 x 10^-34 J s`

Momentum:

`p = mv = (9.11 x 10^-31)(5.4 x 10^6)`

`p = 4.92 x 10^-24 kg m/s`

Wavelength:

`lambda = h/p`

`lambda = (6.63 x 10^-34)/(4.92 x 10^-24)`

`lambda = 1.35 x 10^-10 m = 0.135 nm`

# Unit 3P: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain photoelectric effect

1. Define photoelectric emission.
2. Describe emitter, collector, and light.
3. State that current flows when emitted electrons are collected.
4. Mention threshold frequency.
5. Mention stopping potential and maximum kinetic energy.

## To compare intensity and frequency

1. Intensity controls number of photons per second.
2. Frequency controls energy per photon.
3. Photocurrent depends on intensity.
4. Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency.
5. No emission occurs below threshold frequency.

## To derive Einstein's equation

1. State photon energy `h nu`.
2. State work function `phi0`.
3. Apply energy conservation.
4. Write `h nu = phi0 + Kmax`.
5. Replace `Kmax` by `eV0` if stopping potential is involved.

## To solve de Broglie problems

1. Identify whether momentum is directly given.
2. If speed is given, use `p = mv`.
3. If accelerating potential is given, use `p = sqrt(2meV)`.
4. Apply `lambda = h/p`.
5. Check whether result is microscopic or macroscopic.

# Unit 3Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying intensity increases photon energy. | Frequency increases photon energy; intensity increases photon number. |
| Assuming high intensity below threshold gives emission. | Below threshold frequency, no emission occurs. |
| Confusing stopping potential with accelerating potential. | Stopping potential is retarding and makes photocurrent zero. |
| Forgetting work function. | Photon energy first pays work function, then leftover becomes kinetic energy. |
| Using wavelength formula without converting nm to metre. | `1 nm = 10^-9 m`. |
| Saying matter waves are visible for all objects. | Macroscopic wavelengths are too small to observe. |
| Treating photon as charged. | Photon is electrically neutral. |

## Final Revision

- Work function is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron.
- Photoelectric emission occurs only above threshold frequency.
- Photocurrent is proportional to intensity when frequency is suitable.
- Stopping potential measures maximum kinetic energy.
- Einstein equation: `Kmax = h nu - phi0`.
- Photon energy depends on frequency, not intensity.
- Photon momentum is `h/lambda`.
- Radiation has wave and particle nature.
- Matter has wave nature with `lambda = h/p`.
- Matter wave effects are significant for microscopic particles.
