# Chapter 5: Nuclei
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies the nucleus: its composition, size, mass, density, binding energy, stability, radioactivity, fission, and fusion. Atomic physics deals mainly with electrons outside the nucleus, but nuclear physics deals with protons and neutrons packed inside a tiny region. Nuclear energies are far larger than ordinary chemical energies because nuclear forces are much stronger and act over very short distances.

The key idea is binding energy. A nucleus has less mass than the sum of its free nucleons, and this mass defect corresponds to binding energy through Einstein's relation `E = mc^2`.

# Unit 5A: Composition of Nucleus

The nucleus contains:

- protons;
- neutrons.

Protons and neutrons are collectively called nucleons.

Atomic number:

`Z = number of protons`

Neutron number:

`N = number of neutrons`

Mass number:

`A = Z + N`

The nuclear notation is commonly written as:

`A over Z X`

In plain text:

`X(A,Z)`

Example:

Gold-197 has:

- `A = 197`;
- `Z = 79`;
- `N = 197 - 79 = 118`.

# Unit 5B: Atomic Mass Unit

Atomic and nuclear masses are very small in kilogram, so atomic mass unit is used.

Definition:

`1 u = 1/12 mass of one carbon-12 atom`

Value:

`1 u = 1.6605 x 10^-27 kg`

Energy equivalent:

`1 u = 931.5 MeV/c^2`

This conversion is extremely useful in binding energy and Q-value calculations.

# Unit 5C: Isotopes, Isobars, and Isotones

## Isotopes

Same atomic number `Z`, different neutron number `N`.

They are atoms of the same element with different masses.

Example:

- hydrogen-1;
- deuterium;
- tritium.

## Isobars

Same mass number `A`, different atomic number `Z`.

They are different elements with same total nucleon number.

## Isotones

Same neutron number `N`, different atomic number `Z`.

## Chemical Similarity of Isotopes

Chemical properties depend mainly on electronic arrangement, and isotopes of an element have the same number of electrons in neutral atoms. Therefore isotopes have nearly identical chemical properties.

# Unit 5D: Discovery and Role of Neutron

The existence of isotopes showed that nuclei must contain neutral mass in addition to protons.

Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932.

Important properties:

- charge is zero;
- mass is nearly equal to proton mass;
- free neutron is unstable;
- neutron is stable inside many nuclei.

Mass:

`mn = 1.00866 u`

The neutron explains why nuclei can have mass larger than total proton mass without increasing nuclear charge.

# Unit 5E: Nuclear Size

Rutherford scattering showed that the nucleus is much smaller than atom.

Nuclear radius:

`R = R0 A^(1/3)`

where:

`R0 = 1.2 x 10^-15 m`

## Consequence

Volume:

`V = (4/3) pi R^3`

Since:

`R^3 = R0^3 A`

nuclear volume is proportional to `A`.

Nuclear mass is also approximately proportional to `A`.

Therefore nuclear density is nearly constant for all nuclei.

# Unit 5F: Nuclear Density

Nuclear density is extremely large:

about `10^17 kg/m^3`.

This is much larger than ordinary matter because atom is mostly empty space, but the nucleus contains almost all atomic mass in a tiny volume.

## Physical Picture

If an atom were enlarged to a classroom, the nucleus would be like a tiny point or pinhead at the centre, yet it would contain almost all the mass.

# Unit 5G: Mass-Energy Relation

Einstein's mass-energy equivalence:

`E = mc^2`

Mass can be treated as a form of energy. In nuclear reactions, small mass differences correspond to very large energy changes because `c^2` is enormous.

Example:

Energy equivalent of `1 g` matter:

`E = (10^-3)(3 x 10^8)^2`

`E = 9 x 10^13 J`

# Unit 5H: Mass Defect

Actual nuclear mass is always less than the sum of masses of separated nucleons.

Mass defect:

`Delta M = Zmp + (A - Z)mn - M`

where:

- `mp` is mass of proton;
- `mn` is mass of neutron;
- `M` is actual nuclear mass;
- `A - Z` is number of neutrons.

## Meaning

When nucleons combine to form a nucleus, energy is released. The mass of the bound nucleus is lower by an amount equivalent to this released energy.

# Unit 5I: Binding Energy

Binding energy is the energy required to separate a nucleus completely into its protons and neutrons.

`Eb = Delta M c^2`

If mass defect is in atomic mass unit:

`Eb in MeV = Delta M in u x 931.5`

## Binding Energy per Nucleon

`binding energy per nucleon = Eb/A`

This is a measure of nuclear stability.

Higher binding energy per nucleon generally means more stable nucleus.

# Unit 5J: Binding Energy Curve

The graph of binding energy per nucleon versus mass number `A` has important features:

- low for very light nuclei;
- rises sharply up to medium nuclei;
- maximum near iron region around `A = 56`;
- nearly constant around `8 MeV` per nucleon for middle nuclei;
- decreases slowly for very heavy nuclei.

## Meaning for Fission

Heavy nuclei have lower binding energy per nucleon than medium nuclei. If a heavy nucleus splits into medium nuclei, final products are more tightly bound and energy is released.

## Meaning for Fusion

Light nuclei have lower binding energy per nucleon than heavier light nuclei. If light nuclei combine, final nucleus is more tightly bound and energy is released.

# Unit 5K: Nuclear Force

Nucleons are held together by strong nuclear force.

Properties:

- much stronger than Coulomb force at nuclear distances;
- very short range, only a few femtometres;
- attractive at normal nuclear separation;
- repulsive at extremely small separation;
- nearly charge independent, acting similarly between proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron pairs after allowing for Coulomb effects;
- shows saturation because each nucleon interacts mainly with nearest neighbours.

## Why Strong Force Is Needed

Protons repel each other electrically. If only Coulomb force acted, the nucleus would fly apart. Strong nuclear attraction overcomes proton-proton repulsion at short distances.

# Unit 5L: Radioactivity

Radioactivity is spontaneous transformation of unstable nuclei by emission of radiation.

Main types:

- alpha decay;
- beta decay;
- gamma decay.

## Alpha Decay

An alpha particle is a helium nucleus:

`alpha = 2 protons + 2 neutrons`

In alpha decay:

- mass number decreases by 4;
- atomic number decreases by 2.

## Beta Decay

In beta-minus decay, a neutron changes into proton, electron, and antineutrino.

Effect:

- mass number remains same;
- atomic number increases by 1.

In beta-plus decay, a proton changes into neutron, positron, and neutrino.

Effect:

- mass number remains same;
- atomic number decreases by 1.

## Gamma Decay

Gamma radiation is high-energy electromagnetic radiation.

In gamma decay:

- atomic number does not change;
- mass number does not change;
- excited nucleus loses energy.

# Unit 5M: Radioactive Decay Law

The number of undecayed nuclei decreases exponentially:

`N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`

where:

- `N0` is initial number of nuclei;
- `N` is number remaining after time `t`;
- `lambda` is decay constant.

Decay rate:

`R = lambda N`

Half-life:

`T1/2 = 0.693/lambda`

Mean life:

`tau = 1/lambda`

## Important Meaning

Radioactive decay is statistical. We cannot predict exactly when one nucleus will decay, but we can predict behaviour of a large sample.

# Unit 5N: Q Value of Nuclear Reaction

For nuclear reaction:

`A + b -> C + d`

Q value:

`Q = (mA + mb - mC - md)c^2`

If `Q > 0`:

- reaction releases energy;
- exothermic.

If `Q < 0`:

- reaction absorbs energy;
- endothermic.

Mass must be handled consistently, usually in atomic mass units.

# Unit 5O: Nuclear Fission

Fission is splitting of a heavy nucleus into two medium-mass nuclei, usually with emission of neutrons and energy.

Example idea:

`U-235 + neutron -> fragments + neutrons + energy`

Energy released per fission is about:

`200 MeV`

## Chain Reaction

Neutrons produced in fission can trigger further fission events.

Controlled chain reaction:

- nuclear reactor;
- energy released steadily.

Uncontrolled chain reaction:

- nuclear weapon;
- energy released explosively.

# Unit 5P: Nuclear Fusion

Fusion is combining of light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus.

Example:

hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium in stars.

Fusion releases energy because final nucleus is more tightly bound.

## Coulomb Barrier

Two nuclei are positively charged and repel each other. They must come very close for strong nuclear force to act.

Therefore fusion needs extremely high temperature.

Fusion at high temperature is called thermonuclear fusion.

## Energy in the Sun

The Sun gets energy mainly from proton-proton fusion cycle, where hydrogen is converted into helium with energy release.

# Unit 5Q: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Neutron Number

Find number of neutrons in gold-197 if `Z = 79`.

`N = A - Z`

`N = 197 - 79 = 118`

## Example 2: Nuclear Radius

Find radius of a nucleus with `A = 125`. Use `R0 = 1.2 x 10^-15 m`.

`R = R0 A^(1/3)`

Since `125^(1/3) = 5`:

`R = 1.2 x 10^-15 x 5`

`R = 6.0 x 10^-15 m`

## Example 3: Energy Equivalent of 1 u

Use:

`1 u = 1.6605 x 10^-27 kg`

`E = mc^2`

`E = (1.6605 x 10^-27)(3 x 10^8)^2`

`E = 1.494 x 10^-10 J`

Convert to MeV:

`1 MeV = 1.602 x 10^-13 J`

`E = 1.494 x 10^-10 / 1.602 x 10^-13`

`E = 932 MeV`, approximately `931.5 MeV`

## Example 4: Binding Energy

A nucleus has mass defect `0.20 u`. Find binding energy.

`Eb = Delta M x 931.5 MeV`

`Eb = 0.20 x 931.5`

`Eb = 186.3 MeV`

If `A = 40`, then:

`Eb/A = 186.3/40 = 4.66 MeV per nucleon`

## Example 5: Half-Life

A radioactive isotope has decay constant `2 x 10^-4 s^-1`. Find half-life.

`T1/2 = 0.693/lambda`

`T1/2 = 0.693/(2 x 10^-4)`

`T1/2 = 3465 s`

# Unit 5R: Answer-Writing Framework

## To define a nuclide

1. State symbol `X(A,Z)`.
2. `Z` gives protons.
3. `A` gives protons plus neutrons.
4. `N = A - Z`.
5. Mention isotopes if same element has different neutron number.

## To solve binding energy problems

1. Identify `Z`, `A`, and `N`.
2. Compute mass of free nucleons.
3. Subtract actual nuclear mass.
4. Convert mass defect to energy using `931.5 MeV/u`.
5. Divide by `A` if binding energy per nucleon is asked.

## To explain nuclear energy release

1. Refer to binding energy per nucleon curve.
2. State that final nuclei are more tightly bound.
3. Increase in binding energy appears as released energy.
4. Apply to fission for heavy nuclei.
5. Apply to fusion for light nuclei.

## To answer decay law questions

1. Write `N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`.
2. Use half-life relation if half-life is given.
3. Use `R = lambda N` for decay rate.
4. Remember decay is statistical.

# Unit 5S: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating atomic number as number of neutrons. | Atomic number `Z` is number of protons. |
| Forgetting `A = Z + N`. | Neutron number is `N = A - Z`. |
| Saying isotopes have different atomic number. | Isotopes have same `Z`, different `N`. |
| Using mass defect with wrong sign. | Mass defect is free nucleon mass minus actual nuclear mass. |
| Thinking high binding energy means easy to break. | High binding energy means more energy needed to break nucleus. |
| Saying gamma decay changes `A` or `Z`. | Gamma decay changes energy state, not `A` or `Z`. |
| Assuming fusion occurs easily at room temperature. | Fusion needs very high temperature to overcome Coulomb barrier. |
| Mixing chemical and nuclear energy scales. | Nuclear energies are about a million times larger. |

## Final Revision

- Nucleus contains protons and neutrons.
- `A = Z + N`.
- Isotopes have same `Z`; isobars have same `A`; isotones have same `N`.
- `1 u = 931.5 MeV/c^2`.
- Nuclear radius is `R = R0 A^(1/3)`.
- Nuclear density is nearly constant because mass and volume are both proportional to `A`.
- Mass defect produces binding energy: `Eb = Delta M c^2`.
- Binding energy per nucleon measures nuclear stability.
- Nuclear force is strong, short-range, and saturating.
- Radioactive decay is exponential.
- Fission splits heavy nuclei; fusion combines light nuclei.
