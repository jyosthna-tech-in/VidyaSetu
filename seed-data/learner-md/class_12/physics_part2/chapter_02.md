# Chapter 2: Wave Optics
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Wave optics studies light as a wave. It explains phenomena that ray optics cannot explain fully: interference, diffraction, and polarisation. The chapter begins with Huygens' principle, uses it to explain reflection and refraction, then studies coherent waves, Young's double-slit experiment, single-slit diffraction, and polarisation with polaroids.

The central idea is superposition. When waves overlap, their displacements add. This can create bright regions where waves reinforce and dark regions where waves cancel.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define wavefront and ray in wave optics;
- state and apply Huygens' principle;
- derive reflection and refraction of plane waves using Huygens' construction;
- explain coherent and incoherent addition of waves;
- distinguish constructive and destructive interference;
- derive fringe width in Young's double-slit experiment;
- solve numerical problems on fringe position and wavelength;
- explain single-slit diffraction and central maximum;
- distinguish interference from diffraction in exam language;
- explain why light is transverse using polarisation;
- apply Malus' law for polaroids.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Wavefront | Surface of constant phase. |
| Spherical wavefront | Wavefront produced by a point source. |
| Plane wavefront | Wavefront far from a source, where a small part of a sphere is nearly plane. |
| Ray | Direction of energy propagation, perpendicular to wavefront. |
| Huygens' principle | Every point on a wavefront behaves as a source of secondary wavelets. |

# Unit 2A: Need for Wave Optics

Ray optics explains:

- reflection;
- refraction;
- image formation by mirrors and lenses;
- prism deviation;
- optical instruments.

But ray optics cannot explain:

- interference fringes;
- diffraction around corners;
- spreading through narrow slits;
- polarisation.

Wave optics becomes necessary when the wave nature of light is important.

## Historical Direction

Newton supported a corpuscular model of light. Huygens proposed wave theory. Young's double-slit experiment strongly supported wave nature because particles travelling independently cannot naturally explain stable dark bands produced by adding light to light.

# Unit 2B: Wavefront

A wavefront is a surface on which all particles or field points are in the same phase.

Examples:

- point source nearby: spherical wavefront;
- very distant source: plane wavefront;
- line source: cylindrical wavefront.

The ray is perpendicular to the wavefront.

## Energy Direction

Energy flows normal to the wavefront. In a plane wave, rays are parallel. In a spherical wave, rays are radial.

# Unit 2C: Huygens' Principle

Huygens' principle states:

Every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets. The new wavefront after a time interval is the forward envelope of these secondary wavelets.

If wave speed is `v`, after time `t`, each secondary wavelet has radius:

`vt`

## Important Point

Huygens' construction predicts a backward wave too, but physical wave theory explains why the backward wave is absent or negligible in ordinary propagation.

# Unit 2D: Refraction from Huygens' Principle

When a plane wavefront reaches a boundary between two media:

- one end enters the second medium first;
- speed changes in the second medium;
- the wavefront turns;
- the ray bends.

If speeds are `v1` and `v2`:

`sin i / sin r = v1 / v2`

Using `n = c/v`:

`n1 sin i = n2 sin r`

This is Snell's law.

## Key Prediction

If light bends towards the normal, then speed in the second medium is smaller. This supports the wave theory prediction.

# Unit 2E: Reflection from Huygens' Principle

For reflection at a plane surface:

- the part of wavefront touching the surface first starts reflected secondary wavelets;
- the rest of the wavefront continues until it reaches the surface;
- the reflected wavefront is constructed as a tangent envelope.

The geometry gives:

`angle of incidence = angle of reflection`

Thus Huygens' principle explains the law of reflection.

# Unit 2F: Coherent and Incoherent Waves

Two waves are coherent if they have:

- same frequency;
- constant phase difference;
- stable relation over time.

They are incoherent if phase difference changes randomly.

## Why Coherence Matters

Stable interference requires coherence. Two independent lamps do not usually produce steady interference because their phase relation changes extremely fast.

## Coherent Light in Young's Experiment

Young obtained coherent sources by splitting light from one source into two secondary sources. Since both secondary sources come from the same original wave, their phase changes occur together.

# Unit 2G: Superposition and Interference

Principle of superposition:

When two or more waves meet, resultant displacement is the algebraic or vector sum of individual displacements.

For two waves of equal amplitude `a`:

- if in phase, amplitude becomes `2a`;
- intensity becomes `4I0`;
- constructive interference occurs.

If opposite in phase:

- amplitude becomes zero;
- intensity becomes zero;
- destructive interference occurs.

## Conditions

Constructive interference:

`path difference = n lambda`

Destructive interference:

`path difference = (n + 1/2) lambda`

where `n = 0, 1, 2, ...`.

# Unit 2H: Young's Double-Slit Experiment

Setup:

- a monochromatic source illuminates a narrow slit;
- this slit illuminates two close slits `S1` and `S2`;
- these act as coherent secondary sources;
- interference pattern forms on a distant screen.

Let:

- `d` = slit separation;
- `D` = distance between slits and screen;
- `x` = distance of a point on screen from central maximum;
- `lambda` = wavelength.

For `D >> d`, path difference is approximately:

`path difference = xd/D`

## Bright Fringes

For constructive interference:

`xd/D = n lambda`

So:

`x_n = n lambda D / d`

## Dark Fringes

For destructive interference:

`xd/D = (n + 1/2) lambda`

So:

`x_n = (n + 1/2) lambda D / d`

## Fringe Width

Distance between successive bright or dark fringes:

`beta = lambda D / d`

## What Fringe Width Depends On

Fringe width increases when:

- wavelength increases;
- screen distance increases;
- slit separation decreases.

# Unit 2I: Intensity in Interference

If two coherent waves have equal intensity `I0`, resultant intensity depends on phase difference `phi`:

`I = 4I0 cos^2(phi/2)`

Special cases:

- `phi = 0`: `I = 4I0`, maximum;
- `phi = pi`: `I = 0`, minimum;
- `phi = 2 pi`: `I = 4I0`, maximum.

If two sources are incoherent, the interference term averages to zero and intensities simply add:

`I = I1 + I2`

# Unit 2J: Diffraction

Diffraction is the spreading of light when it passes through a small aperture or around an obstacle.

It is noticeable when aperture size is comparable with wavelength.

Examples:

- sound heard around a corner;
- light spreading through a narrow slit;
- colours on a CD;
- resolution limit of telescopes and microscopes.

## Diffraction and Ray Optics

Ray optics predicts sharp shadows. Diffraction explains why edges of shadows are not perfectly sharp.

# Unit 2K: Single-Slit Diffraction

For a slit of width `a`, the diffraction pattern has:

- broad central maximum;
- weaker secondary maxima on both sides;
- dark minima between maxima.

Condition for minima:

`a sin theta = n lambda`

where:

`n = 1, 2, 3, ...`

For small angle:

`theta = n lambda/a`

## Central Maximum

The central maximum is twice as wide as the other secondary maxima regions between minima.

First minima occur at:

`sin theta = lambda/a`

So angular width of central maximum is approximately:

`2 lambda/a`

# Unit 2L: Interference vs Diffraction

Interference usually refers to superposition from a small number of coherent sources, such as two slits.

Diffraction usually refers to superposition from many parts of the same wavefront, such as many points across a single slit.

## Practical Difference

Young's double-slit pattern:

- fringes are nearly equally spaced;
- intensity of bright fringes is nearly equal if slit width is very small.

Single-slit diffraction pattern:

- central maximum is widest and brightest;
- secondary maxima become weaker.

# Unit 2M: Polarisation

Polarisation proves that light is a transverse wave.

In a transverse wave, vibration is perpendicular to direction of propagation. Light has electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to the direction of travel.

Natural light is unpolarised:

- electric field vibrates in many random transverse directions.

Plane-polarised light:

- electric field is restricted to one plane.

# Unit 2N: Polaroids and Malus' Law

A polaroid transmits the component of electric field along its pass axis and absorbs the perpendicular component.

Unpolarised light passing through one ideal polaroid:

`I = I_unpolarised/2`

If plane-polarised light of intensity `I0` passes through a polaroid whose pass axis makes angle `theta` with the electric field:

`I = I0 cos^2 theta`

This is Malus' law.

## Crossed Polaroids

If two polaroids are crossed:

`theta = 90 degrees`

`I = I0 cos^2 90 degrees = 0`

No light is transmitted ideally.

# Unit 2O: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Wavelength in Water

Monochromatic light of wavelength `589 nm` in air enters water of refractive index `1.33`. Find wavelength in water.

Frequency remains constant.

`lambda_water = lambda_air / n`

`lambda_water = 589/1.33 = 443 nm`

Speed in water:

`v = c/n = 3 x 10^8 / 1.33 = 2.26 x 10^8 m/s`

## Example 2: Young's Experiment Wavelength

In a double-slit experiment:

- slit separation `d = 0.28 mm`;
- screen distance `D = 1.4 m`;
- fourth bright fringe distance from centre `x4 = 1.2 cm`.

For fourth bright:

`x4 = 4 lambda D/d`

So:

`lambda = x4 d/(4D)`

Convert:

`x4 = 1.2 x 10^-2 m`

`d = 0.28 x 10^-3 m`

`D = 1.4 m`

`lambda = (1.2 x 10^-2)(0.28 x 10^-3)/(4 x 1.4)`

`lambda = 6.0 x 10^-7 m = 600 nm`

## Example 3: Fringe Width

Light of wavelength `600 nm` is used in Young's experiment. Slit separation is `0.3 mm` and screen distance is `1.5 m`. Find fringe width.

`beta = lambda D/d`

`beta = (600 x 10^-9)(1.5)/(0.3 x 10^-3)`

`beta = 3.0 x 10^-3 m = 3 mm`

## Example 4: Malus' Law

Plane-polarised light of intensity `80 W/m^2` passes through a polaroid whose axis is at `60 degrees` to the plane of vibration. Find transmitted intensity.

`I = I0 cos^2 theta`

`I = 80 x cos^2 60 degrees`

`I = 80 x (1/2)^2 = 20 W/m^2`

## Example 5: Single-Slit Minimum

A slit of width `0.2 mm` is illuminated by light of wavelength `500 nm`. Find angle of first minimum.

`a sin theta = lambda`

`sin theta = lambda/a`

`sin theta = (500 x 10^-9)/(0.2 x 10^-3)`

`sin theta = 2.5 x 10^-3`

For small angle:

`theta = 2.5 x 10^-3 rad`

# Unit 2P: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain Huygens' principle

1. Define wavefront.
2. State that each point acts as a secondary wavelet source.
3. State that new wavefront is forward envelope.
4. Mention rays are normal to wavefronts.
5. Use it to explain reflection or refraction if asked.

## To solve Young's double-slit problems

1. Write given `lambda`, `D`, `d`, and fringe order.
2. Use `x_n = n lambda D/d` for bright fringe.
3. Use `x_n = (n + 1/2) lambda D/d` for dark fringe.
4. Use `beta = lambda D/d` for fringe width.
5. Convert all units to metre.

## To explain diffraction

1. Define diffraction as bending or spreading around aperture or edge.
2. State it is significant when aperture size is comparable with wavelength.
3. For single slit, mention central maximum and weaker side maxima.
4. Write minima condition `a sin theta = n lambda`.

## To explain polarisation

1. State only transverse waves can be polarised.
2. Light is transverse electromagnetic wave.
3. Polaroid transmits one component of electric field.
4. Use Malus' law if intensity is involved.

# Unit 2Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying frequency changes during refraction. | Frequency remains same; speed and wavelength change. |
| Using `lambda D/d` without unit conversion. | Convert wavelength, distance, and slit separation to metre. |
| Confusing fringe width with nth fringe position. | Fringe width is spacing; nth bright position is `n beta`. |
| Saying diffraction needs two slits. | Diffraction occurs even with one slit. |
| Saying polarisation proves longitudinal nature. | Polarisation proves transverse nature. |
| Treating independent lamps as coherent. | Independent lamps are generally incoherent. |
| Forgetting that central diffraction maximum is widest. | Central maximum width is about twice the width of secondary maxima. |

## Final Revision

- Wavefront is a surface of constant phase.
- Rays are perpendicular to wavefronts.
- Huygens' principle explains reflection and refraction.
- In refraction, frequency remains constant but speed and wavelength change.
- Coherent waves have constant phase difference.
- Interference redistributes energy; it does not create or destroy energy.
- Young's fringe width is `beta = lambda D/d`.
- Diffraction becomes important when aperture size is comparable to wavelength.
- Single-slit minima satisfy `a sin theta = n lambda`.
- Polarisation proves light is transverse.
- Malus' law is `I = I0 cos^2 theta`.
