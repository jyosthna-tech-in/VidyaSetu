# Chapter 2: The World Population - Distribution, Density and Growth
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography Human

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies where people live, how densely they are distributed, and how population changes over time. World population is unevenly distributed because physical, economic, social, and historical factors vary across regions.

Population geography is not only about numbers. It explains why river valleys, plains, coasts, industrial regions, and urban areas attract people while deserts, polar regions, high mountains, and dense forests have sparse population.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define population distribution and density;
- identify densely and sparsely populated regions of the world;
- explain physical, economic, and social factors affecting distribution;
- calculate population density;
- explain population growth, natural growth, and actual growth;
- distinguish birth rate, death rate, and migration;
- explain demographic transition theory;
- understand population composition and age-sex structure;
- write map-based and cause-effect answers.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Population distribution | Spatial pattern of people across Earth's surface. |
| Population density | Number of people per unit area. |
| Birth rate | Number of live births per thousand population in a year. |
| Death rate | Number of deaths per thousand population in a year. |
| Natural growth | Difference between births and deaths. |
| Actual growth | Natural growth plus net migration. |
| Migration | Movement of people from one place to another. |
| Demographic transition | Change from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates. |

# Unit 2A: Population Distribution

Population distribution refers to how people are spread over space. It is highly uneven.

Many people live in:

- East Asia;
- South Asia;
- Europe;
- eastern North America;
- river valleys and plains;
- coastal regions;
- industrial and urban areas.

Few people live in:

- deserts;
- polar regions;
- high mountains;
- dense equatorial forests;
- remote interiors.

# Unit 2B: Population Density

Population density is:

`Population density = total population / total area`

It is usually expressed as persons per square kilometre.

Example:

If a region has 1,000,000 people and area of 10,000 square kilometres:

`density = 1,000,000 / 10,000 = 100 persons per square kilometre`

Density helps compare population pressure across regions.

# Unit 2C: Physical Factors Affecting Distribution

## Relief

Plains attract people because farming, transport, construction, and settlement are easier.

Mountains often have sparse population due to steep slopes, cold climate, and transport difficulty.

## Climate

Moderate climates attract population. Extreme cold, extreme heat, and very dry regions discourage dense settlement.

## Soil

Fertile soils support agriculture and dense rural settlement.

Examples:

- river valleys;
- delta regions;
- volcanic soils in some regions.

## Water

Water is essential for drinking, farming, industry, and transport. River valleys and coastal areas often support dense population.

## Minerals

Mineral-rich regions may attract population due to mining and industry.

# Unit 2D: Economic Factors

## Agriculture

Fertile agricultural areas support dense population, especially where intensive farming is possible.

## Industry

Industrial regions attract workers because of employment opportunities.

## Transport

Transport routes, ports, railway junctions, and highways encourage settlement and economic activity.

## Urbanisation

Cities attract population due to jobs, education, health care, and services.

# Unit 2E: Social and Cultural Factors

Population distribution is also shaped by:

- historical settlement;
- political stability;
- religious and cultural centres;
- government policies;
- education and health facilities;
- migration networks;
- safety and security.

People may avoid areas with conflict or disaster risk even if physical conditions are favourable.

# Unit 2F: Population Growth

Population growth means change in number of people over time.

Natural growth:

`births - deaths`

Actual growth:

`births - deaths + in-migration - out-migration`

Growth may be positive, zero, or negative.

# Unit 2G: Birth Rate and Death Rate

Birth rate:

`Birth rate = (live births in a year / mid-year population) x 1000`

Death rate:

`Death rate = (deaths in a year / mid-year population) x 1000`

If birth rate is higher than death rate, natural increase occurs.

If death rate is higher than birth rate, natural decrease occurs.

# Unit 2H: Migration

Migration changes population distribution. It may be internal or international.

## Push Factors

Push factors force people to leave a place.

Examples:

- unemployment;
- poverty;
- conflict;
- disasters;
- lack of services;
- social discrimination.

## Pull Factors

Pull factors attract people to a place.

Examples:

- jobs;
- education;
- safety;
- better wages;
- health services;
- urban facilities.

# Unit 2I: Demographic Transition Theory

Demographic transition theory explains population change as society develops.

## Stage 1

High birth rate and high death rate. Population grows slowly. This stage is linked with low technology, poor health care, and agrarian economy.

## Stage 2

Death rate falls due to better food, sanitation, and medicine, but birth rate remains high. Population grows rapidly.

## Stage 3

Birth rate begins to fall due to education, urbanisation, women empowerment, and family planning. Growth slows.

## Stage 4

Both birth and death rates are low. Population is stable or grows slowly.

Some countries may enter very low fertility and ageing population conditions.

# Unit 2J: Population Composition

Population composition studies characteristics of people, not just total number.

Important aspects:

- age;
- sex;
- literacy;
- occupation;
- rural-urban residence;
- marital status;
- language;
- religion.

# Unit 2K: Age-Sex Pyramid

An age-sex pyramid shows population by age groups and sex.

## Expanding Pyramid

Wide base and narrow top. Indicates high birth rate and youthful population.

## Stable Pyramid

Moderate base and balanced sides. Indicates low growth.

## Declining Pyramid

Narrow base and larger older population. Indicates low birth rate and ageing.

# Unit 2L: Answer-Writing Framework

For population distribution:

1. Define distribution.
2. State that it is uneven.
3. Explain physical factors.
4. Explain economic factors.
5. Add examples of dense and sparse regions.

For demographic transition:

1. Define the theory.
2. Explain each stage.
3. Link birth and death rates to development.
4. Mention population growth pattern.
5. Add examples if known.

# Unit 2M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Confusing distribution and density | Distribution is pattern; density is people per unit area. |
| Explaining density without area | Density requires population divided by area. |
| Ignoring migration | Actual growth includes migration. |
| Saying all high-density areas are rich | Some dense regions are agricultural and poor. |
| Treating demographic transition as only two stages | Use four-stage explanation. |
