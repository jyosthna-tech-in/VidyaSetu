# Chapter 8: International Trade
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography Human

## Chapter at a Glance

International trade is exchange of goods and services between countries. It exists because resources, climate, skills, technology, capital, and demand vary across the world. Trade connects economies, supports specialisation, and shapes global interdependence.

The chapter explains bases of trade, balance of trade, types of trade, free trade, protection, trade blocs, ports, and the role of World Trade Organization.

# Unit 8A: Meaning of International Trade

International trade is exchange between countries. It may involve goods, services, technology, capital, or intellectual property.

Examples:

- India exports software services;
- Japan exports automobiles;
- West Asian countries export petroleum;
- countries import food, machinery, or minerals they lack.

# Unit 8B: Why Countries Trade

Countries trade because no country has everything it needs in ideal quantity, quality, and cost.

Major bases:

- difference in natural resources;
- difference in climate;
- difference in labour skills;
- difference in technology;
- difference in capital;
- difference in production cost;
- difference in demand;
- specialisation and comparative advantage.

# Unit 8C: Imports and Exports

Exports bring foreign exchange and expand markets for domestic producers.

Imports provide goods, raw materials, technology, and services not sufficiently available domestically.

Both are important. A country may import capital goods to improve future production.

# Unit 8D: Balance of Trade

Balance of trade is:

`Balance of trade = value of exports of goods - value of imports of goods`

If exports exceed imports, balance of trade is favourable or surplus.

If imports exceed exports, balance of trade is unfavourable or deficit.

Balance of trade covers goods only. Balance of payments is wider and includes goods, services, income, transfers, and capital flows.

# Unit 8E: Types of International Trade

## Bilateral Trade

Trade between two countries.

## Multilateral Trade

Trade among many countries.

## Entrepot Trade

Goods are imported by one country and then re-exported to another country, often after storage or minor processing.

# Unit 8F: Free Trade

Free trade means goods and services move between countries with few restrictions.

Advantages:

- wider choice for consumers;
- specialisation;
- competition;
- efficiency;
- larger markets;
- lower prices in some cases.

Concerns:

- domestic industries may suffer;
- dependence on imports may rise;
- unequal competition can hurt weaker economies.

# Unit 8G: Protectionism

Protectionism restricts imports to protect domestic producers.

Tools:

- tariffs;
- quotas;
- import licences;
- quality restrictions;
- subsidies to domestic producers.

Protection can help new industries, but excessive protection may reduce efficiency and increase prices.

# Unit 8H: Trade Blocs

Trade blocs are groups of countries that reduce trade barriers among members.

Examples:

- European Union;
- ASEAN;
- USMCA;
- MERCOSUR.

Benefits:

- larger regional markets;
- easier movement of goods;
- stronger bargaining power;
- regional cooperation.

Possible issues:

- non-members may face barriers;
- stronger members may benefit more;
- domestic sectors may face regional competition.

# Unit 8I: World Trade Organization

World Trade Organization, or WTO, works to regulate and promote international trade rules.

Functions:

- provides forum for trade negotiations;
- settles trade disputes;
- promotes reduction of trade barriers;
- monitors trade policies;
- supports rule-based trade system.

Criticism:

- some argue it favours powerful economies;
- developing countries may face pressure to open markets;
- trade rules may not fully protect labour and environment.

# Unit 8J: Ports and International Trade

Ports are gateways of international trade. They connect sea routes with hinterlands.

Functions:

- loading and unloading cargo;
- storage;
- customs clearance;
- passenger movement;
- ship repair;
- container handling;
- trade documentation.

## Types of Ports

| Type | Main role |
|---|---|
| Industrial port | Handles industrial raw materials and products |
| Commercial port | Handles general trade cargo |
| Comprehensive port | Handles many cargo types and services |
| Oil port | Handles petroleum and related products |
| Naval port | Defence role |

# Unit 8K: Factors Affecting Trade Pattern

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Resource endowment | Mineral or agricultural exports |
| Technology | High-value manufactured exports |
| Labour skills | Service exports and specialised manufacturing |
| Transport | Reduces cost and expands trade |
| Trade policy | Tariffs and agreements shape flows |
| Political relations | Friendly relations encourage trade |
| Exchange rate | Affects export and import prices |

# Unit 8L: Benefits and Problems of Trade

## Benefits

- access to goods not locally available;
- efficient specialisation;
- employment;
- foreign exchange;
- technology transfer;
- cultural contact;
- economic growth.

## Problems

- dependency;
- trade deficits;
- environmental stress;
- unequal gains;
- vulnerability to global shocks;
- pressure on local producers.

5. Give examples.

For free trade vs protection:

1. Define free trade.
2. Define protectionism.

5. Conclude with need for balance.

# Unit 8N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Confusing balance of trade and balance of payments | BoT covers goods; BoP is wider. |
| Saying imports are always bad | Imports can provide technology, raw materials, and essential goods. |
| Saying free trade benefits all equally | Gains may be unequal. |
| Treating ports as only ship stops | Ports are trade, storage, customs, and logistics centres. |
| Ignoring services | Modern trade includes services as well as goods. |
