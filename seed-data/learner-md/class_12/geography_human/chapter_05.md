# Chapter 5: Secondary Activities
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography Human

## Chapter at a Glance

Secondary activities transform raw materials into finished or semi-finished goods. They include manufacturing, processing, construction, and energy production. These activities add value to primary products and are central to industrial development.

This chapter explains types of manufacturing, factors affecting industrial location, household and large-scale industries, high technology industry, and major industrial regions.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define secondary activities;
- explain manufacturing and value addition;
- classify industries by size, raw material, product, and ownership;
- explain factors influencing industrial location;
- compare cottage, small-scale, and large-scale industries;
- describe high technology industry;
- identify major industrial regions;
- write answers using location factors and examples.

# Unit 5A: Meaning of Secondary Activities

Secondary activities change the form and value of raw materials.

Examples:

- cotton to cloth;
- iron ore to steel;
- wheat to flour;
- wood to furniture;
- milk to cheese;
- crude oil to petroleum products.

Secondary activities connect primary production with market demand.

# Unit 5B: Manufacturing

Manufacturing is the major secondary activity. It involves mechanical, chemical, or manual transformation of materials into useful products.

Manufacturing creates:

- higher value;
- employment;
- urban growth;
- trade opportunities;
- technological development;
- regional economic change.

# Unit 5D: Factors Affecting Industrial Location

Industries choose locations to reduce cost and increase profit.

## Raw Material

Industries using bulky, heavy, or weight-losing raw materials locate near raw materials.

Example:

Sugar mills are often near sugarcane areas because sugarcane loses weight and freshness quickly.

## Power

Industries need power for machines. Earlier, coalfields strongly attracted industries. Today electricity grids allow more flexibility.

## Labour

Labour-intensive industries need abundant and skilled workers.

## Market

Industries producing perishable or bulky goods may locate near markets.

## Transport

Good transport reduces cost of moving raw materials and finished goods.

## Capital

Industrial growth requires investment in machines, land, buildings, and technology.

## Government Policy

Governments influence location through taxes, subsidies, industrial estates, and regional planning.

# Unit 5E: Agglomeration Economies

Agglomeration economies occur when industries benefit by locating near each other.

Benefits:

- shared infrastructure;
- skilled labour pool;
- supplier networks;
- repair and service facilities;
- information flow;
- large market.

This explains why industrial clusters develop.

# Unit 5F: Cottage and Small-Scale Industries

Cottage industries are household-based and use family labour and simple tools.

Small-scale industries use more capital and hired labour but remain smaller than large factories.

Importance:

- employment generation;
- use of local skills;
- low capital requirement;
- support to rural economy;
- preservation of crafts.

# Unit 5G: Large-Scale Manufacturing

Large-scale manufacturing uses machines, large capital, hired labour, and organised production.

Features:

- mass production;
- specialisation;
- high energy use;
- large markets;
- advanced transport needs;
- division of labour.

Examples:

- iron and steel;
- automobiles;
- petrochemicals;
- shipbuilding;
- electronics.

# Unit 5H: High Technology Industry

High technology industry uses advanced scientific knowledge, research, skilled labour, and innovation.

Examples:

- electronics;
- aerospace;
- biotechnology;
- robotics;
- computer hardware;
- precision instruments.

Location factors:

- skilled labour;
- research institutions;
- clean environment;
- capital;
- good communication;
- airports;
- quality urban services.

High technology clusters are sometimes called technopolises.

# Unit 5I: Industrial Regions

Industrial regions develop where many industries concentrate.

Favourable conditions:

- raw materials;
- power;
- transport;
- labour;
- market;
- capital;
- ports;
- government support.

Examples around the world include industrial belts in eastern North America, western and central Europe, Russia-Ukraine region, and East Asia.

# Unit 5J: Industrial Location Change

Industrial location changes over time because:

- raw material dependence may reduce;
- transport improves;
- labour cost changes;
- markets shift;
- environmental rules strengthen;
- technology advances;
- global production networks expand.

Modern industries can be more footloose, especially if products are lightweight and high-value.

# Unit 5K: Environmental Concerns

Secondary activities can create:

- air pollution;
- water pollution;
- solid waste;
- noise;
- greenhouse gas emissions;
- land degradation.

Sustainable industry requires cleaner technology, waste treatment, energy efficiency, and regulation.

# Unit 5L: Answer-Writing Framework

For industrial location:

1. Begin with cost and profit logic.
2. Explain raw material and market.
3. Add power, labour, transport, capital, and policy.
4. Mention agglomeration economies.
5. Use one industry example.

For high technology industry:

1. Define it.
2. Mention research and skilled labour.
3. Explain location factors.
4. Give examples.
5. Add role in modern economy.

# Unit 5M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling all industries secondary | Manufacturing is secondary; trade and transport are tertiary. |
| Ignoring value addition | Secondary activity transforms and adds value. |
| Saying all industries locate near raw materials | Market, labour, power, and transport also matter. |
| Treating high technology industry as heavy industry | It is skill, research, and innovation intensive. |
| Forgetting environmental impact | Industrial development needs sustainability. |
