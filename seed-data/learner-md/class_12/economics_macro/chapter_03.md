# Chapter 3: Money and Banking
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

Money is anything generally accepted as a medium of exchange. Banking institutions create credit and influence money supply. This chapter explains functions of money, demand for money, supply of money, commercial banking, credit creation, central banking, and monetary policy tools.

The core idea is that money solves the problems of barter, and banks multiply deposits into credit. The central bank regulates this process to maintain stability in the economy.

# Unit 3A: Barter System and Its Problems

Barter means exchange of goods directly for goods. It works only when wants match exactly.

Problems of barter:

- lack of double coincidence of wants;
- difficulty in measuring value;
- indivisibility of many goods;
- difficulty in storing wealth;
- difficulty in deferred payments.

Money solves these problems by acting as a common medium of exchange and unit of account.

# Unit 3B: Functions of Money

## Medium of Exchange

Money is used to buy and sell goods and services. It removes the need for double coincidence of wants.

## Unit of Account

Money measures value of goods and services in common units. Prices are expressed in money.

## Store of Value

Money can be stored for future use, though inflation may reduce its purchasing power.

## Standard of Deferred Payment

Loans and future payments are expressed in money terms.

# Unit 3C: Demand for Money

People demand money because it is liquid.

Two broad motives:

1. transaction motive;
2. speculative motive.

## Transaction Motive

People hold money to carry out daily purchases. Transaction demand depends positively on income.

Higher income means more transactions, so more money is needed.

## Speculative Motive

People hold money as an asset when they expect bond prices to fall or interest rates to rise.

There is an inverse relation between interest rate and speculative demand for money.

When interest rate is high, people prefer bonds because return is attractive. When interest rate is low, people may hold more money because bond prices may fall.

# Unit 3D: Supply of Money

Money supply is stock of money available with the public at a point of time.

It includes:

- currency held by public;
- demand deposits with banks;
- other deposits depending on measure used.

Money held by government or banking system is not counted as money with public.

# Unit 3E: Measures of Money Supply

Common monetary aggregates include:

| Measure | Basic idea |
|---|---|
| M1 | Currency with public plus demand deposits plus other deposits with central bank |
| M2 | M1 plus savings deposits with post office savings banks |
| M3 | M1 plus net time deposits with commercial banks |
| M4 | M3 plus total deposits with post office savings organisations excluding national savings certificates |

M1 is the most liquid narrow money measure. M3 is a broader measure.

# Unit 3F: Commercial Banks

Commercial banks are financial institutions that accept deposits and provide loans.

## Main Functions

| Function | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Accept deposits | Current, savings, and fixed deposits |
| Give loans | Loans, overdrafts, cash credit, discounting bills |
| Agency services | Payments, collections, fund transfers |
| General services | Locker, cards, online banking |

Commercial banks connect savers and borrowers.

# Unit 3G: Credit Creation

Banks do not keep all deposits as cash. They keep a fraction as reserves and lend the rest. Loans become deposits in the banking system, leading to multiple deposit creation.

Money multiplier:

`money multiplier = 1 / reserve ratio`

If reserve ratio is `20 percent`, multiplier is:

`1 / 0.20 = 5`

Initial deposit of 100 can support total deposits of 500 in the banking system, assuming no leakages.

## Credit Creation Process

1. A bank receives deposit.
2. It keeps required reserve.
3. It lends excess reserve.
4. Borrower spends loan.
5. The amount returns as deposit in another bank.
6. Process repeats until total deposit expansion reaches multiplier limit.

# Unit 3H: Limits to Credit Creation

Credit creation is limited by:

- reserve requirements;
- public's cash holding habit;
- willingness of banks to lend;
- willingness of borrowers to borrow;
- central bank policy;
- economic conditions and confidence.

# Unit 3I: Central Bank

The central bank is the apex monetary institution. In India, the Reserve Bank of India performs central banking functions.

## Main Functions

| Function | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Currency authority | Issues currency notes. |
| Banker to government | Manages government accounts and borrowing. |
| Banker's bank | Holds reserves of commercial banks and lends to them. |
| Controller of credit | Uses monetary policy tools. |
| Custodian of foreign exchange | Manages foreign exchange reserves. |
| Lender of last resort | Provides emergency support to banks. |
| Clearing house | Settles interbank claims. |

# Unit 3J: Monetary Policy Tools

## Repo Rate

Repo rate is the rate at which central bank lends to commercial banks against securities.

Higher repo rate makes borrowing costlier, reducing credit creation.

Lower repo rate makes borrowing cheaper, encouraging credit.

## Reverse Repo Rate

Reverse repo rate is the rate at which central bank borrows from commercial banks.

Higher reverse repo rate encourages banks to park funds with central bank, reducing liquidity.

## Cash Reserve Ratio

CRR is the fraction of deposits banks must keep with central bank.

Higher CRR reduces lendable funds and credit creation.

Lower CRR increases lendable funds.

## Statutory Liquidity Ratio

SLR is the fraction of deposits banks must keep in liquid assets such as government securities.

Higher SLR reduces lending capacity.

## Open Market Operations

Open market operations are buying and selling of government securities by central bank.

Buying securities injects money into economy.

Selling securities absorbs money from economy.

## Bank Rate

Bank rate is the rate at which central bank lends long-term funds to commercial banks. It influences general lending rates.

# Unit 3K: Expansionary and Contractionary Monetary Policy

## Expansionary Policy

Used when economy needs more demand or credit.

Tools:

- reduce repo rate;
- reduce CRR;
- reduce SLR;
- buy government securities;
- encourage lending.

## Contractionary Policy

Used to control inflationary pressure.

Tools:

- increase repo rate;
- increase CRR;
- increase SLR;
- sell government securities;
- restrict credit.

# Unit 3L: Formula Bank

| Concept | Formula |
|---|---|
| Money multiplier | `1 / reserve ratio` |
| Total deposit creation | `initial deposit x money multiplier` |
| Reserve required | `deposit x reserve ratio` |
| Excess reserve | `deposit - required reserve` |

# Unit 3M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling all bank deposits money supply | Only deposits with public are counted, depending on measure. |
| Confusing repo and reverse repo | Repo: central bank lends to banks; reverse repo: central bank borrows from banks. |
| Thinking banks can lend unlimited amounts | Credit creation is limited by reserve ratio and demand for loans. |
| Saying higher CRR increases lending | Higher CRR reduces lendable funds. |
| Confusing central bank with commercial bank | Central bank regulates; commercial banks deal with public. |
