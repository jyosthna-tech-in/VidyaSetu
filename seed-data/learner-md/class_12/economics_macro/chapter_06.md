# Chapter 6: Open Economy Macroeconomics
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

An open economy trades goods, services, assets, and currencies with the rest of the world. This chapter explains balance of payments, current account, capital account, foreign exchange market, exchange rate determination, depreciation, appreciation, devaluation, revaluation, and how open economy transactions affect national income.

The core idea is that international transactions require foreign currency. Demand and supply of foreign exchange determine exchange rate under flexible exchange rate systems.

# Unit 6A: Meaning of Open Economy

An open economy interacts with other countries through:

- export and import of goods;
- export and import of services;
- international borrowing and lending;
- foreign investment;
- remittances;
- currency transactions.

A closed economy has no external sector. Most real economies are open.

# Unit 6B: Balance of Payments

Balance of payments, or BoP, is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world during a period.

BoP uses double-entry bookkeeping. Every transaction has a credit entry and a debit entry.

## Credit and Debit

| Entry | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Credit | Brings foreign exchange | Export of goods |
| Debit | Uses foreign exchange | Import of goods |

# Unit 6C: Current Account

Current account records transactions that do not create future asset or liability claims in the same way as capital flows. It includes:

- trade in goods;
- trade in services;
- income receipts and payments;
- current transfers.

## Components

| Component | Examples |
|---|---|
| Goods | Merchandise exports and imports |
| Services | Tourism, transport, insurance, software |
| Income | Interest, profit, dividend, wages |
| Transfers | Remittances, gifts, grants |

## Trade Balance

`Trade balance = exports of goods - imports of goods`

If imports exceed exports, there is trade deficit.

# Unit 6D: Capital Account

Capital account records transactions that change assets or liabilities of residents with the rest of the world.

Examples:

- foreign direct investment;
- foreign portfolio investment;
- external commercial borrowing;
- loans from abroad;
- banking capital;
- changes in foreign exchange reserves.

Capital account surplus can finance current account deficit.

# Unit 6E: Balance of Payments Equilibrium

In accounting sense, BoP always balances because every transaction has two entries. But in economic analysis, imbalance can appear in autonomous transactions.

Autonomous transactions are made for profit or economic motive independent of BoP position.

Accommodating transactions are made to settle BoP imbalance, such as use of foreign exchange reserves or official borrowing.

# Unit 6F: Foreign Exchange Rate

Foreign exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another.

Example:

If `1 US dollar = 83 Indian rupees`, the rupee price of one dollar is 83.

An increase from 83 to 85 rupees per dollar means the rupee has depreciated because more rupees are needed to buy one dollar.

# Unit 6G: Demand for Foreign Exchange

People demand foreign exchange to make payments abroad.

Reasons:

- imports of goods;
- imports of services;
- foreign travel;
- education abroad;
- investment abroad;
- repayment of foreign loans;
- sending gifts or transfers abroad.

Demand for foreign exchange usually slopes downward with respect to exchange rate when exchange rate is measured as rupees per dollar. If dollar becomes costlier, imports and foreign payments become more expensive, reducing demand.

# Unit 6H: Supply of Foreign Exchange

Foreign exchange supply comes from receipts from abroad.

Reasons:

- exports of goods;
- exports of services;
- foreign tourists spending domestically;
- remittances from abroad;
- foreign investment inflow;
- borrowing from abroad.

Supply of foreign exchange usually slopes upward. If dollar becomes more valuable in rupee terms, exporters and foreign investors may bring in more foreign currency.

# Unit 6I: Exchange Rate Determination

Under flexible exchange rate, exchange rate is determined by demand and supply of foreign exchange.

Equilibrium occurs where:

`demand for foreign exchange = supply of foreign exchange`

If demand for foreign exchange rises, domestic currency tends to depreciate.

If supply of foreign exchange rises, domestic currency tends to appreciate.

# Unit 6J: Depreciation, Appreciation, Devaluation, Revaluation

## Depreciation

Depreciation is fall in value of domestic currency under flexible exchange rate.

Example:

`1 dollar = Rs 83` changes to `1 dollar = Rs 85`.

More rupees are needed per dollar, so rupee depreciates.

## Appreciation

Appreciation is rise in value of domestic currency under flexible exchange rate.

Example:

`1 dollar = Rs 83` changes to `1 dollar = Rs 80`.

Fewer rupees are needed per dollar, so rupee appreciates.

## Devaluation

Devaluation is official reduction in currency value by monetary authority under fixed exchange rate.

## Revaluation

Revaluation is official increase in currency value under fixed exchange rate.

# Unit 6K: Fixed, Flexible, and Managed Exchange Rates

| System | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Fixed exchange rate | Government or central bank fixes exchange rate. |
| Flexible exchange rate | Market demand and supply determine exchange rate. |
| Managed floating | Market determines rate, but central bank intervenes to reduce volatility. |

Managed floating is common because completely fixed and completely free systems both have limitations.

# Unit 6L: Effects of Currency Depreciation

Depreciation makes foreign currency costlier in domestic currency.

Possible effects:

- imports become costlier;
- exports become cheaper for foreigners;
- import bill may rise;
- export revenue may rise if demand responds;
- inflation may rise through costlier imported inputs.

The final effect on trade balance depends on price elasticity of export and import demand.

# Unit 6M: Current Account Deficit

Current account deficit occurs when current account payments exceed current account receipts.

It may arise due to:

- high imports;
- low exports;
- large income payments abroad;
- weak remittance inflows.

It can be financed by capital inflows, borrowing, or use of foreign exchange reserves.

# Unit 6N: BoP Classification Toolkit

| Transaction | Account | Credit or debit idea |
|---|---|---|
| Export of goods | Current account | Credit |
| Import of goods | Current account | Debit |
| Software service export | Current account | Credit |
| Indian tourist spends abroad | Current account | Debit |
| Remittance received | Current account | Credit |
| Foreign investment in India | Capital account | Credit |

| External loan received | Capital account | Credit |
| Repayment of foreign loan | Capital account | Debit |

# Unit 6O: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying BoP and trade balance are same | Trade balance covers goods only; BoP covers all external transactions. |
| Confusing current and capital account | Current records goods, services, income, transfers; capital records asset-liability flows. |
| Misreading rupee-dollar change | If more rupees are needed per dollar, rupee depreciates. |
| Using devaluation for market-driven fall | Market-driven fall is depreciation; official fixed-rate fall is devaluation. |
| Saying BoP never has problems because it always balances | Accounting balance differs from economic imbalance in autonomous transactions. |
| Treating imports as credit | Imports use foreign exchange and are debit entries. |

## Final Revision

- Open economies transact with the rest of the world.
- BoP records all external transactions.
- Current account includes goods, services, income, and transfers.
- Capital account includes investment, borrowing, lending, and reserve changes.
- Exchange rate is price of one currency in another.
- Demand for foreign exchange comes from payments abroad.
- Supply of foreign exchange comes from receipts from abroad.
- Depreciation is market-driven fall; devaluation is official fall.
