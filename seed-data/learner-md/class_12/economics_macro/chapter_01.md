# Chapter 1: Introduction to Macroeconomics
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole. Instead of studying one consumer, one firm, or one commodity market, it studies aggregate output, employment, inflation, national income, money, banking, government budget, and external trade. This chapter introduces why macroeconomics emerged and what makes it different from microeconomics.

The central shift is from individual behaviour to aggregate behaviour. A single household saving more may be good for that household, but if all households reduce consumption sharply, total demand may fall and national income may decline. Macroeconomics studies such economy-wide interactions.

# Unit 1A: Meaning of Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies aggregate economic variables. It looks at the economy as a single large system.

Examples of macro variables:

- national income;
- total consumption;
- total investment;
- general price level;
- total employment;
- money supply;
- exports and imports;
- government expenditure.

Macroeconomics does not ignore individuals completely. Rather, it groups individual actions into aggregates and studies how these aggregates interact.

# Unit 1B: Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics

| Basis | Microeconomics | Macroeconomics |
|---|---|---|
| Unit of study | Individual consumer, firm, market | Economy as a whole |
| Main variables | Price and quantity of one good | Output, income, employment, price level |
| Key question | How is price of a commodity determined? | How is national income determined? |
| Method | Partial equilibrium often | Aggregate analysis |
| Example | Demand for wheat | Total demand in economy |

Both branches are connected. The aggregate economy is built from individual decisions, but aggregate outcomes can behave differently from individual outcomes.

# Unit 1C: Emergence of Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics became especially important after the Great Depression of the 1930s. Many economies faced large unemployment and falling output. Classical thinking expected markets to automatically restore full employment, but the crisis persisted.

John Maynard Keynes argued that insufficient aggregate demand could keep the economy below full employment. This made output, employment, and demand central macroeconomic questions.

## Why the Great Depression Was Important

It showed that:

- unemployment can be economy-wide and persistent;
- output can remain below capacity;
- individual wage and price adjustment may not quickly solve the problem;
- government policy can affect aggregate demand.

# Unit 1D: Sectors in a Macroeconomy

## Household Sector

Households supply factors of production and receive income. They spend on consumption and save part of income.

## Firm Sector

Firms hire factors of production and produce goods and services. They invest in capital and sell output.

## Government Sector

Government collects taxes, spends on goods and services, transfers income, and regulates the economy.

## External Sector

The external sector includes exports, imports, foreign investment, and exchange rate transactions.

# Unit 1E: Circular Flow of Income

Circular flow shows how income and output move through the economy.

In a simple two-sector economy:

1. households provide factors to firms;
2. firms pay factor incomes to households;
3. households spend income on goods and services;
4. firms receive revenue and continue production.

Real flow:

- factor services from households to firms;
- goods and services from firms to households.

Money flow:

- factor payments from firms to households;
- consumption expenditure from households to firms.

# Unit 1F: Injections and Leakages

Leakages are withdrawals from circular flow.

Examples:

- saving;
- taxes;
- imports.

Injections add spending to circular flow.

Examples:

- investment;
- government expenditure;
- exports.

For equilibrium in an expanded economy, planned leakages and planned injections must balance.

# Unit 1G: Stock and Flow Variables

## Stock Variable

A stock variable is measured at a point of time.

Examples:

- wealth on 31 March;
- money supply on a specific date;
- capital stock at year end;
- inventory at a point of time.

## Flow Variable

A flow variable is measured over a period of time.

Examples:

- income per month;
- output per year;
- consumption per week;
- investment during a year.

## Comparison

| Basis | Stock | Flow |
|---|---|---|
| Time reference | Point of time | Period of time |
| Example | Wealth | Income |
| Measurement | At a date | Per hour, month, or year |

# Unit 1H: Major Macroeconomic Concerns

## Output and Growth

An economy aims to increase production of goods and services over time. Growth means expansion of productive capacity and output.

## Employment

Unemployment means labour willing to work at prevailing wage does not find work. Macroeconomics studies causes and remedies of unemployment.

## Price Stability

Inflation reduces purchasing power and creates uncertainty. Deflation can reduce profits and employment. Price stability is a major policy goal.

## External Balance

Countries trade with each other. Exports, imports, exchange rates, and foreign payments affect income and stability.

## Economic Policy

Government and central bank influence the economy through fiscal policy and monetary policy.

# Unit 1I: Why Aggregates Need Care

Aggregates must be defined carefully. Adding all transactions without distinction can lead to double counting. For example, counting both wheat and bread at full value would count wheat twice if wheat is used to produce bread.

Macroeconomics therefore focuses on final goods and value added.

# Unit 1K: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying macroeconomics is just large-scale microeconomics | Aggregates can behave differently from individual units. |
| Confusing income and wealth | Income is flow; wealth is stock. |
| Counting intermediate goods in national output | Use final goods or value added to avoid double counting. |
| Ignoring government and foreign sector | Advanced circular flow includes both. |
| Treating saving only as good for economy | Excess saving can reduce aggregate demand in Keynesian analysis. |

## Final Revision

- Macroeconomics studies aggregate variables.
- Great Depression highlighted unemployment and demand failure.
- Circular flow links production, income, and expenditure.
- Saving, taxes, and imports are leakages.
- Investment, government spending, and exports are injections.
- Stock is measured at a point; flow is measured over time.
