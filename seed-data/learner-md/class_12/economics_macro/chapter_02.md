# Chapter 2: National Income Accounting
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

National income accounting measures the value of production and income generated in an economy during a period. It gives a systematic way to calculate GDP, GNP, NDP, NNP, national income, personal income, and disposable income. The chapter also explains value added, final and intermediate goods, depreciation, net factor income from abroad, and methods of national income calculation.

The main skill is avoiding double counting. Count only final goods or count value added at each production stage, not both.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define final goods and intermediate goods;
- explain consumption goods and capital goods;
- distinguish stock and flow variables in national accounting;
- define GDP, GNP, NDP, NNP, and national income;
- distinguish market price and factor cost;
- distinguish gross and net values;
- explain depreciation and capital loss;
- calculate value added;
- use product, income, and expenditure methods;
- identify precautions in each method;
- connect domestic income with national income.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Final good | Good purchased for final use, not for resale or further production. |
| Intermediate good | Good used as input for further production or resale. |
| Capital good | Durable producer good used in production. |
| Consumption good | Good used directly by consumers. |
| Gross investment | Total investment including replacement of depreciated capital. |
| Depreciation | Loss in value of fixed capital due to wear and tear or obsolescence. |
| Net investment | Gross investment minus depreciation. |
| GDP | Value of final goods and services produced within domestic territory. |
| GNP | GDP plus net factor income from abroad. |
| National income | Net national product at factor cost. |
| Value added | Value of output minus intermediate consumption. |

## Quick Learning Map

| Adjustment | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gross to net | Subtract depreciation |
| Domestic to national | Add net factor income from abroad |
| Market price to factor cost | Subtract net indirect taxes |
| Factor cost to market price | Add net indirect taxes |

# Unit 2A: Final and Intermediate Goods

## Final Goods

Final goods are used for final consumption or final investment. They are not used for further production in the same accounting period.

Examples:

- bread bought by a household;
- machine bought by a firm for production;
- car bought by a household.

## Intermediate Goods

Intermediate goods are used as inputs or purchased for resale.

Examples:

- wheat used by a bakery to make bread;
- cotton used by textile mill;
- goods bought by a shop for resale.

## Same Good, Different Use

A good is final or intermediate depending on use, not physical nature.

Milk bought by household is final. Milk bought by sweet shop to make sweets is intermediate.

# Unit 2B: Problem of Double Counting

Double counting occurs when value of intermediate goods is counted along with final goods.

Example:

If wheat worth 20 is used to produce flour worth 30 and flour is used to produce bread worth 50, counting 20 + 30 + 50 exaggerates output. The final value already includes intermediate values.

Two ways to avoid double counting:

1. count only final goods;
2. count value added at each stage.

# Unit 2C: Value Added Method

Value added is:

`Value added = value of output - intermediate consumption`

Gross value added at market price:

`GVAmp = value of output - intermediate consumption`

Net value added at factor cost:

`NVAfc = GVAmp - depreciation - net indirect taxes`

## Value Added Table

| Stage | Output value | Intermediate cost | Value added |
|---|---|---|---|
| Farmer | 20 | 0 | 20 |
| Miller | 30 | 20 | 10 |
| Baker | 50 | 30 | 20 |
| Total | - | - | 50 |

Final bread value is 50, equal to total value added.

# Unit 2D: Domestic Territory and Normal Residents

## Domestic Territory

Domestic territory includes economic territory controlled by a country where goods and services are produced.

It includes:

- territory within political boundaries;
- ships and aircraft operated by residents between countries;
- embassies and military establishments abroad.

## Normal Resident

A normal resident is a person or institution whose centre of economic interest lies in the country.

National income belongs to normal residents, whether earned inside or outside domestic territory.

# Unit 2E: GDP, GNP, NDP, and NNP

## GDP at Market Price

GDPmp is market value of final goods and services produced within domestic territory during a year.

## NDP at Market Price

`NDPmp = GDPmp - depreciation`

## GNP at Market Price

`GNPmp = GDPmp + NFIA`

NFIA means net factor income from abroad.

## NNP at Market Price

`NNPmp = GNPmp - depreciation`

## National Income

National income is:

`NNPfc`

`NNPfc = NNPmp - net indirect taxes`

Net indirect taxes:

`NIT = indirect taxes - subsidies`

# Unit 2F: Domestic Income and National Income

Domestic income is income generated within domestic territory.

National income is income earned by normal residents.

Relation:

`National income = Domestic income + NFIA`

If residents earn more from abroad than foreigners earn from domestic territory, NFIA is positive.

If foreigners earn more domestically than residents earn abroad, NFIA is negative.

# Unit 2G: Product Method

Product method measures national income by adding value added by all producing units.

Steps:

1. Identify producing enterprises.
2. Calculate value of output.
3. Subtract intermediate consumption to get GVA.
4. Subtract depreciation and net indirect taxes to get NVA at factor cost.
5. Add net factor income from abroad if moving from domestic to national income.

## Precautions

- Do not include intermediate goods.
- Do not include second-hand goods unless brokerage service is provided.
- Include own-account production if reliably estimated.
- Do not include illegal production in standard accounting.
- Include imputed rent of owner-occupied houses where required.

# Unit 2H: Income Method

Income method measures factor incomes earned in production.

Components:

- compensation of employees;
- operating surplus;
- mixed income of self-employed.

Operating surplus includes:

- rent;
- interest;
- profit.

Domestic income:

`NDPfc = compensation of employees + operating surplus + mixed income`

National income:

`NNPfc = NDPfc + NFIA`

## Precautions

- Include only factor incomes.
- Exclude transfer incomes such as old-age pension and scholarships.
- Exclude capital gains and windfall gains.
- Include employer contribution to social security.
- Include income from self-consumption production where estimated.

# Unit 2I: Expenditure Method

Expenditure method measures final expenditure on goods and services.

Components:

`GDPmp = C + I + G + (X - M)`

where:

- `C` is private final consumption expenditure;
- `I` is gross domestic capital formation;
- `G` is government final consumption expenditure;
- `X - M` is net exports.

To get national income:

`NNPfc = GDPmp - depreciation + NFIA - net indirect taxes`

## Precautions

- Include only final expenditure.
- Exclude expenditure on intermediate goods.
- Exclude purchase of second-hand goods.
- Include broker commission on second-hand transactions.
- Exclude financial asset purchases such as shares and bonds.
- Include imputed services where national accounting rules require them.

# Unit 2J: Private Income, Personal Income, and Disposable Income

## Private Income

Private income is income received by private sector from all avenues, including transfer income from government and rest of world.

## Personal Income

Personal income is income actually received by households.

It excludes items not received by households, such as corporate tax and retained earnings, and includes transfers received by households.

## Personal Disposable Income

Personal disposable income is income available to households after paying personal taxes.

`PDI = personal income - personal taxes - non-tax payments`

It is used for consumption and saving.

# Unit 2K: Nominal and Real GDP

Nominal GDP is measured at current-year prices.

Real GDP is measured at constant base-year prices.

Real GDP is better for comparing physical output over time because it removes price change effect.

GDP deflator:

`GDP deflator = (nominal GDP / real GDP) x 100`

# Unit 2L: Formula Bank

| Conversion | Formula |
|---|---|
| NDPmp | `GDPmp - depreciation` |
| GNPmp | `GDPmp + NFIA` |
| NNPmp | `GNPmp - depreciation` |
| NNPfc | `NNPmp - net indirect taxes` |
| National income | `NDPfc + NFIA` |
| Net indirect taxes | `indirect taxes - subsidies` |
| Value added | `value of output - intermediate consumption` |
| GDPmp expenditure | `C + I + G + (X - M)` |
| Net investment | `gross investment - depreciation` |

# Unit 2M: Calculation Strategy

1. Identify the target aggregate: GDP, NDP, GNP, NNP, market price, or factor cost.
2. Check whether value is gross or net.
3. Check whether value is domestic or national.
4. Check whether value is at market price or factor cost.
5. Apply one adjustment at a time.
6. Write signs carefully: add NFIA to go domestic to national; subtract depreciation to go gross to net; subtract net indirect taxes to go market price to factor cost.

# Unit 2N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Counting all goods sold | Count final goods or value added only. |
| Confusing domestic with national | Domestic is territory-based; national is resident-based. |
| Adding depreciation when converting gross to net | Subtract depreciation. |
| Adding net indirect taxes when converting market price to factor cost | Subtract net indirect taxes. |
| Including transfer payments in income method | Include only factor incomes. |
| Treating purchase of shares as final expenditure | Financial asset purchase is not final goods expenditure. |

## Final Revision

- National income accounting measures production, income, and expenditure.
- Final goods are counted; intermediate goods are excluded.
- Value added avoids double counting.
- Gross minus depreciation gives net.
- Domestic plus NFIA gives national.
- Market price minus net indirect taxes gives factor cost.
- National income is `NNPfc`.
- Product, income, and expenditure methods should give same result if measured correctly.
