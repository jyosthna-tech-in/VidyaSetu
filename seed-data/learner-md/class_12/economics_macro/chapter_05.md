# Chapter 5: Government Budget and the Economy
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

Government budget is an annual financial statement showing estimated receipts and expenditure of the government for a financial year. It is also a policy instrument. Through taxes, spending, borrowing, and transfers, the government influences allocation of resources, distribution of income, economic stability, and growth.

This chapter explains budget objectives, revenue and capital items, plan of receipts and expenditure, budget deficits, fiscal deficit, primary deficit, and the economic meaning of deficits.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define government budget;
- explain objectives of budget;
- distinguish revenue receipts and capital receipts;
- distinguish revenue expenditure and capital expenditure;
- classify budget items correctly;
- define balanced, surplus, and deficit budget;
- calculate revenue deficit, fiscal deficit, and primary deficit;
- explain implications of fiscal deficit;
- understand how budget policy affects economy.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|

| Revenue receipt | Receipt that neither creates liability nor reduces asset. |

# Unit 5A: Meaning of Government Budget

Government budget is a statement of estimated receipts and estimated expenditure of the government for a financial year.

It performs two roles:

1. accounting role: records expected receipts and spending;
2. policy role: guides allocation, redistribution, stabilisation, and growth.

# Unit 5B: Objectives of Government Budget

## Allocation of Resources

Government influences allocation by spending on public goods and essential services and by taxing or subsidising different activities.

Example:

- subsidy for clean energy encourages production and consumption of clean energy;
- tax on harmful goods discourages their use.

## Redistribution of Income

Government uses taxes and transfer payments to reduce inequality.

Example:

- progressive income tax;
- pensions;
- scholarships;
- food subsidies.

## Economic Stability

Budget can reduce fluctuations in income and employment.

During recession, government may increase spending or reduce taxes. During inflation, it may reduce spending or increase taxes.

## Economic Growth

Government spending on infrastructure, education, health, and technology can raise productive capacity.

## Management of Public Enterprises

Budget provides funds for public sector enterprises and records income from them.

# Unit 5C: Revenue Receipts

Revenue receipts do not create liability and do not reduce government assets.

They are divided into:

- tax revenue;
- non-tax revenue.

## Tax Revenue

Tax is a compulsory payment imposed by government without direct benefit in return.

Types:

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Direct tax | Burden cannot be shifted easily | Income tax |
| Indirect tax | Burden can be shifted | GST, excise duty |

## Non-Tax Revenue

Examples:

- fees;
- fines and penalties;
- interest receipts;
- profits of public enterprises;
- dividends;
- licence fees.

# Unit 5D: Capital Receipts

Capital receipts either create liability or reduce assets of government.

Examples:

- borrowings;
- recovery of loans;
- disinvestment proceeds;
- small savings collections.

## Classification Test

Ask:

1. Does this receipt create future repayment liability?
2. Does it reduce government assets?

If yes to either, it is capital receipt.

# Unit 5E: Revenue Expenditure

Revenue expenditure neither creates assets nor reduces liabilities.

Examples:

- salaries;
- pensions;
- interest payments;
- subsidies;
- grants;
- defence revenue expenditure;
- administrative expenses.

Revenue expenditure may be important, but it does not directly create government-owned assets.

# Unit 5F: Capital Expenditure

Capital expenditure either creates assets or reduces liabilities.

Examples:

- construction of roads, schools, hospitals;
- purchase of machinery;
- loans given to states;
- repayment of government borrowings;
- investment in public enterprises.

## Classification Test

Ask:

1. Does expenditure create an asset?
2. Does it reduce a liability?

If yes to either, it is capital expenditure.

# Unit 5G: Balanced, Surplus, and Deficit Budget

| Budget type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Balanced budget | Receipts equal expenditure. |
| Surplus budget | Receipts exceed expenditure. |
| Deficit budget | Expenditure exceeds receipts. |

Modern governments often use deficit budgets to support development or stabilisation, but persistent large deficits can create debt pressure.

# Unit 5H: Revenue Deficit

Revenue deficit occurs when revenue expenditure exceeds revenue receipts.

`Revenue deficit = revenue expenditure - revenue receipts`

It shows that government is borrowing for current consumption-type expenditure, not for asset creation.

High revenue deficit is generally considered less desirable than capital expenditure financed by borrowing.

# Unit 5I: Fiscal Deficit

Fiscal deficit is the excess of total expenditure over total receipts excluding borrowings.

`Fiscal deficit = total expenditure - total receipts excluding borrowings`

It shows government's borrowing requirement.

Alternative form:

`Fiscal deficit = borrowing`

if all gap is financed by borrowing.

## Implications

Fiscal deficit can be useful if borrowing finances productive capital expenditure. But large persistent fiscal deficit may:

- increase interest burden;
- crowd out private investment;
- create inflationary pressure;
- raise debt for future generations.

# Unit 5J: Primary Deficit

Primary deficit is fiscal deficit minus interest payments.

`Primary deficit = fiscal deficit - interest payments`

It shows current borrowing requirement excluding past debt interest.

If primary deficit is zero, current borrowing is only to pay interest on past loans.

# Unit 5K: Deficit Comparison

| Deficit | Formula | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue deficit | `revenue expenditure - revenue receipts` | Shortfall in revenue account |
| Fiscal deficit | `total expenditure - receipts excluding borrowings` | Total borrowing need |
| Primary deficit | `fiscal deficit - interest payments` | Borrowing need excluding interest burden |

# Unit 5L: Fiscal Policy and Demand

Government budget affects aggregate demand.

## Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Used during deficient demand.

Measures:

- increase government expenditure;
- reduce taxes;
- increase transfers.

Effect: raises aggregate demand.

## Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Used during excess demand or inflationary pressure.

Measures:

- reduce government expenditure;
- increase taxes;
- reduce transfers.

Effect: lowers aggregate demand.

# Unit 5M: Budget Classification Toolkit

| Item | Classification logic |
|---|---|
| Income tax | Revenue receipt, tax revenue |
| GST | Revenue receipt, tax revenue |
| Fees | Revenue receipt, non-tax revenue |
| Borrowing | Capital receipt because it creates liability |
| Recovery of loan | Capital receipt because it reduces asset |
| Disinvestment | Capital receipt because it reduces asset |
| Salary payment | Revenue expenditure |
| Interest payment | Revenue expenditure |
| Road construction | Capital expenditure |
| Loan repayment | Capital expenditure because it reduces liability |

# Unit 5N: Calculation Toolkit

1. Separate revenue receipts from capital receipts.
2. Separate revenue expenditure from capital expenditure.
3. For revenue deficit, compare only revenue account.
4. For fiscal deficit, subtract non-borrowing receipts from total expenditure.
5. For primary deficit, subtract interest payments from fiscal deficit.
6. Always check whether borrowings are included in receipts; fiscal deficit excludes borrowing receipts.

# Unit 5O: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating all government receipts as revenue receipts | Borrowing and disinvestment are capital receipts. |
| Treating all development spending as capital expenditure | Classification depends on asset creation or liability reduction. |
| Saying fiscal deficit is always bad | It depends on size, use, and sustainability. |
| Including borrowings in receipts while calculating fiscal deficit | Fiscal deficit excludes borrowing receipts. |
| Confusing primary deficit with fiscal deficit | Primary deficit excludes interest payments. |
| Calling interest payment capital expenditure | Interest payment is revenue expenditure. |

## Final Revision

- Budget records expected receipts and expenditure.
- Revenue receipts do not create liability or reduce assets.
- Capital receipts create liability or reduce assets.
- Revenue expenditure does not create asset or reduce liability.
- Capital expenditure creates asset or reduces liability.
- Revenue deficit is revenue expenditure minus revenue receipts.
- Fiscal deficit is government's borrowing requirement.
- Primary deficit is fiscal deficit minus interest payments.
