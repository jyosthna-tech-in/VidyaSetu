# Chapter 4: Determination of Income and Employment
### NCERT Class 12 - Economics Macro

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter explains how equilibrium income and employment are determined in a Keynesian framework. The key idea is effective demand. Output and employment depend on aggregate demand, and aggregate demand depends mainly on consumption and investment in a simple economy.

The chapter also explains consumption function, saving function, marginal propensity to consume, marginal propensity to save, investment multiplier, inflationary gap, deflationary gap, and policy responses.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define aggregate demand and aggregate supply;
- explain consumption function and saving function;
- calculate APC, MPC, APS, and MPS;
- explain equilibrium income using `AD = AS`;
- explain equilibrium using `S = I`;
- calculate investment multiplier;
- explain excess demand and deficient demand;
- distinguish inflationary gap and deflationary gap;
- describe fiscal and monetary measures to correct demand gaps.

# Unit 4A: Aggregate Demand

Aggregate demand is total planned spending on final goods and services in an economy.

In a simple two-sector economy:

`AD = C + I`

Here:

- `C` is consumption expenditure;
- `I` is investment expenditure.

In a more complete economy:

`AD = C + I + G + (X - M)`

# Unit 4B: Aggregate Supply

Aggregate supply is the total value of goods and services produced, or national income.

In income terms:

`AS = Y`

Income is either consumed or saved:

`Y = C + S`

Therefore:

`AS = C + S`

# Unit 4C: Consumption Function

Consumption function shows relationship between consumption and income.

Simple form:

`C = Cbar + cY`

Here:

- `Cbar` is autonomous consumption;
- `c` is marginal propensity to consume;
- `Y` is income.

Autonomous consumption is consumption when income is zero. It may be financed by borrowing or past savings.

# Unit 4D: Propensity to Consume

## Average Propensity to Consume

`APC = C / Y`

APC is the ratio of total consumption to total income.

## Marginal Propensity to Consume

`MPC = Delta C / Delta Y`

MPC is the fraction of additional income spent on consumption.

If income rises by 100 and consumption rises by 80:

`MPC = 80/100 = 0.8`

# Unit 4E: Saving Function

Saving is income not consumed.

`S = Y - C`

If `C = Cbar + cY`, then:

`S = -Cbar + (1-c)Y`

The term `(1-c)` is marginal propensity to save.

## Average Propensity to Save

`APS = S / Y`

## Marginal Propensity to Save

`MPS = Delta S / Delta Y`

Important identities:

`APC + APS = 1`

`MPC + MPS = 1`

# Unit 4F: Equilibrium Income

Equilibrium income is determined where planned aggregate demand equals aggregate supply.

`AD = AS`

In a two-sector model:

`C + I = C + S`

Therefore:

`I = S`

So both conditions are equivalent:

- `AD = AS`;
- `S = I`.

# Unit 4G: Adjustment Toward Equilibrium

If `AD > AS`, buyers demand more than firms produce. Inventories fall. Firms increase output and employment. Income rises.

If `AD < AS`, firms cannot sell all output. Inventories rise. Firms reduce output and employment. Income falls.

Equilibrium is reached when planned spending equals planned output.

# Unit 4H: Investment Multiplier

Investment multiplier shows how a change in investment causes a larger change in income.

`K = Delta Y / Delta I`

Also:

`K = 1/MPS`

or

`K = 1/(1-MPC)`

If `MPC = 0.8`, then:

`K = 1/(1-0.8) = 5`

An investment increase of 100 raises income by:

`Delta Y = K x Delta I = 5 x 100 = 500`

# Unit 4I: Why Multiplier Works

One person's expenditure becomes another person's income. When investment rises, income rises for those producing investment goods. They spend part of additional income, creating further income for others. This chain continues, but each round becomes smaller because some income is saved.

Higher MPC means stronger multiplier because more of each income increase is spent.

Higher MPS means weaker multiplier because more income leaks into saving.

# Unit 4J: Full Employment Equilibrium

Full employment equilibrium occurs when aggregate demand is sufficient to buy output produced at full employment level.

But equilibrium can occur below full employment if aggregate demand is deficient. Keynes argued that an economy can settle at underemployment equilibrium.

# Unit 4K: Excess Demand and Inflationary Gap

Excess demand occurs when aggregate demand is greater than aggregate supply at full employment level.

Inflationary gap is the amount by which aggregate demand exceeds full-employment output.

Effects:

- general price level rises;
- demand-pull inflation appears;
- output cannot rise much beyond full employment in short run.

## Correcting Excess Demand

Fiscal measures:

- reduce government expenditure;
- increase taxes;
- reduce transfer payments.

Monetary measures:

- increase repo rate;
- increase CRR;
- sell government securities;
- restrict credit.

# Unit 4L: Deficient Demand and Deflationary Gap

Deficient demand occurs when aggregate demand is less than aggregate supply at full employment level.

Deflationary gap is the shortfall of aggregate demand from full-employment output.

Effects:

- output falls;
- employment falls;
- inventories rise;
- recessionary pressure increases.

## Correcting Deficient Demand

Fiscal measures:

- increase government expenditure;
- reduce taxes;
- increase transfer payments.

Monetary measures:

- reduce repo rate;
- reduce CRR;
- buy government securities;
- encourage credit expansion.

# Unit 4M: Formula Bank

# Unit 4N: Calculation Toolkit

1. If consumption and income are given, calculate APC as `C/Y`.
2. If changes are given, calculate MPC as `Delta C / Delta Y`.
3. Get MPS from `1 - MPC`.
4. Get multiplier from `1/MPS`.
5. Get change in income from `K x change in investment`.
6. For equilibrium, use `AD = AS` or `S = I`.

# Unit 4O: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Confusing APC and MPC | APC uses total values; MPC uses changes. |
| Forgetting `MPC + MPS = 1` | Additional income is either consumed or saved. |
| Saying multiplier equals MPC | Multiplier equals `1/(1-MPC)`. |
| Treating all equilibrium as full employment | Equilibrium can occur below full employment. |
| Confusing inflationary and deflationary gap | Inflationary gap is excess AD; deflationary gap is deficient AD. |
| Using expansionary policy for excess demand | Excess demand needs contractionary policy. |

## Final Revision

- In a simple economy, `AD = C + I`.
- Income is consumed or saved: `Y = C + S`.
- Equilibrium occurs when `AD = AS` or `S = I`.
- `MPC + MPS = 1`.
- Multiplier is larger when MPC is higher.
- Excess demand creates inflationary pressure.
- Deficient demand creates unemployment and output loss.
- Fiscal and monetary policy can correct demand gaps.
