# Chapter 3: Contemporary South Asia

## Learner Orientation

This chapter shifts from global Cold War changes to India's own region: South Asia. It examines domestic politics, democracy, monarchy, military rule, ethnic conflict, inter-state disputes, regional cooperation, SAARC, SAFTA, and the role of outside powers.

Study this chapter through two parallel themes. First, South Asian countries have internal political struggles over democracy, identity, military rule, monarchy, and ethnic conflict. Second, South Asian countries also have external relations involving conflict and cooperation, especially around India because of its size and central location.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Contemporary South Asia is marked by democracy, military rule, ethnic conflict, regional disputes, and repeated efforts at cooperation through SAARC, SAFTA, bilateral agreements, and people-to-people contact.

## 2. What Is South Asia?

In this chapter, South Asia includes:

- Bangladesh.
- Bhutan.
- India.
- Maldives.
- Nepal.
- Pakistan.
- Sri Lanka.

Afghanistan and Myanmar are sometimes included in wider regional discussions, but the chapter uses the seven-country definition.

## 3. Geography Of South Asia

South Asia is shaped by:

- Himalayas in the north.
- Indian Ocean in the south.
- Arabian Sea in the west.
- Bay of Bengal in the east.

This geography creates a sense of natural insularity and helps explain the region's cultural distinctiveness.

## 4. South Asia As Diversity

South Asia is diverse in:

- Languages.
- Religions.
- Ethnic groups.
- Political systems.
- Economic conditions.
- Historical experiences.
- Social structures.

Despite this diversity, it forms one geopolitical space because the countries share borders, histories, rivers, cultures, security concerns, and regional institutions.

## 5. Rivalry And Goodwill

South Asian affairs often combine rivalry and goodwill.

India-Pakistan cricket matches show this duality:

- Strong tension and rivalry.
- Hospitality and cultural connection.
- Emotional public involvement.
- Shared histories and memories.

The region is not only conflict; it also contains possibilities of trust and cooperation.

## 6. Political Systems In South Asia

South Asian states do not all have the same political experience.

India and Sri Lanka have maintained democratic systems since independence.

Pakistan and Bangladesh have experienced both military and civilian governments.

Nepal moved from monarchy to democratic republic.

Bhutan became a constitutional monarchy and multi-party democracy.

Maldives moved from sultanate to republic and later multi-party politics.

## 7. Democracy In India

India has remained democratic since independence.

This is significant because India is:

- Large.
- Diverse.
- Unequal.
- Multilingual.
- Multi-religious.

India's democratic success expanded global imagination about democracy in developing societies.

## 8. Democracy In Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka has maintained democracy since independence in 1948.

However, democracy faced a severe ethnic challenge due to conflict between the Sinhala majority and Tamil minority.

Sri Lanka shows that electoral democracy can coexist with deep ethnic conflict.

## 9. Pakistan's Democratic Instability

Pakistan has repeatedly shifted between elected governments and military rule.

Civilian rule has existed at different moments, but the military has often intervened.

Democracy has struggled because unelected institutions remained powerful.

## 10. Bangladesh's Democratic Journey

Bangladesh emerged from East Pakistan after the 1971 war.

It began with commitments to secularism, democracy, and socialism.

It later faced authoritarianism, military rule, assassinations, and public movements for democracy.

Since 1991, representative democracy based on multi-party elections has operated.

## 11. Nepal's Transition

Nepal was a Hindu kingdom and later a constitutional monarchy.

Political parties and citizens demanded a more open system.

Maoist insurgency, monarchy, and democratic forces created a triangular conflict.

After the 2006 movement and later political changes, Nepal became a democratic republic in 2008 and adopted a new constitution in 2015.

## 12. Bhutan's Political Change

Bhutan became a constitutional monarchy in 2008.

Under the king's leadership, it moved toward multi-party democracy.

This shows a controlled and peaceful transition rather than a violent overthrow.

## 13. Maldives

Maldives was a sultanate until 1968.

It became a republic with a presidential system.

In 2005, the parliament voted to introduce a multi-party system.

The Maldivian Democratic Party became influential in island politics.

## 14. Support For Democracy In South Asia

Surveys in the region show widespread support for democracy.

Ordinary citizens, rich and poor, across religions, tend to:

- Support representative institutions.
- Prefer democracy over alternatives.
- Believe democracy is suitable for their country.

This challenges the idea that democracy can flourish only in wealthy countries.

## 15. Pakistan: Ayub Khan

After Pakistan framed its first constitution, General Ayub Khan took control and later got himself elected.

Popular dissatisfaction forced him to leave office.

This began a pattern in which military rulers tried to use elections to legitimise authority.

## 16. Pakistan: Yahya Khan And Bangladesh Crisis

General Yahya Khan ruled Pakistan during the Bangladesh crisis.

Under his rule, East Pakistan's demand for autonomy was suppressed.

The 1971 war with India led to the creation of Bangladesh.

This was a major turning point in Pakistan's political history.

## 17. Pakistan: Bhutto, Zia, Benazir, Nawaz, Musharraf

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto led an elected government from 1971 to 1977.

General Zia-ul-Haq removed him in 1977.

Benazir Bhutto became Prime Minister in 1988 after a pro-democracy movement.

Politics later centred around the Pakistan People's Party and Muslim League.

In 1999, General Pervez Musharraf removed Nawaz Sharif through a military coup.

Since 2008, Pakistan has again had civilian governments.

## 18. Why Democracy Has Been Weak In Pakistan

Several factors weakened democracy:

- Dominance of military.
- Influence of clergy.
- Power of landowning elites.
- India-Pakistan conflict.
- Pro-military arguments about national security.
- Lack of consistent international support for democracy.
- Western support for military rule at different times.

These factors allowed military regimes to justify their control.

## 19. Pro-Democracy Forces In Pakistan

Pakistan also has important democratic strengths:

- Strong pro-democracy sentiment.
- Courageous press.
- Human rights movement.
- Civil society activism.
- Electoral politics.

The military has been powerful, but democratic aspirations have not disappeared.

## 20. Bangladesh As East Pakistan

Bangladesh was part of Pakistan from 1947 to 1971.

It consisted of areas of Bengal and Assam partitioned from British India.

People in East Pakistan resented domination by West Pakistan.

Major issues included:

- Imposition of Urdu.
- Cultural neglect.
- Unequal representation.
- Unequal share in power.
- Economic and political domination.

## 21. Sheikh Mujib And Autonomy

Sheikh Mujib-ur Rahman led the movement against West Pakistani domination.

His Awami League won all seats in East Pakistan in the 1970 elections and secured a majority in the proposed constituent assembly.

The West Pakistani leadership refused to convene the assembly.

This refusal deepened the crisis.

## 22. Bangladesh Liberation

The Pakistani army suppressed the Bengali movement violently.

Large numbers of people fled to India, creating a refugee crisis.

India supported East Pakistan's demand for independence financially and militarily.

The December 1971 India-Pakistan war ended with the surrender of Pakistani forces in East Pakistan and the formation of Bangladesh.

## 23. Bangladesh After Independence

Bangladesh's constitution declared faith in:

- Secularism.
- Democracy.
- Socialism.

In 1975, Sheikh Mujib shifted the system from parliamentary to presidential and restricted political parties.

His assassination led to military rule and later struggles for democracy.

## 24. Ershad And Democratic Restoration

After Ziaur Rahman, Lt Gen H. M. Ershad took power.

Students and citizens supported the demand for democracy.

Public protests forced Ershad to step down in 1990.

Elections were held in 1991, and multi-party democracy resumed.

## 25. Nepal's Monarchy

Nepal's king, supported by the army, retained strong control for many years.

Political parties and citizens demanded an open and responsive system.

The king accepted a democratic constitution in 1990 after protests, but democratic governments remained unstable.

## 26. Maoists In Nepal

The Maoists spread influence in the 1990s.

They believed in armed struggle against monarchy and ruling elites.

This produced conflict between:

- Monarchist forces.
- Democratic parties.
- Maoist guerrillas.

## 27. Nepal's 2006 Movement

In 2002, the king abolished parliament and dismissed the government.

In April 2006, massive pro-democracy protests forced the restoration of the House of Representatives.

The movement included the Seven Party Alliance, Maoists, and social activists.

It was largely non-violent and became a major step toward republic.

## 28. Nepal As Democratic Republic

Nepal abolished monarchy in 2008.

It became a democratic republic.

It adopted a new constitution in 2015.

The transition involved debates over monarchy, Maoist demands, social restructuring, and India's role.

## 29. Ethnic Conflict In Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka retained democracy but faced ethnic conflict.

Politics after independence was dominated by Sinhala majority interests.

Tamil concerns were neglected, including concerns of Tamils who had migrated from India.

This led to militant Tamil nationalism.

## 30. Sinhala Nationalism And Tamil Concerns

Sinhala nationalists argued that Sri Lanka belonged mainly to Sinhala people.

They opposed concessions to Tamils.

Tamil groups felt politically and culturally excluded.

This created conditions for militancy.

## 31. LTTE

The Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam, or LTTE, fought an armed struggle from 1983.

Its goal was a separate Tamil state called Tamil Eelam.

At one stage, the LTTE controlled parts of northeastern Sri Lanka.

## 32. India's Role In Sri Lanka

India was drawn into the Sri Lankan Tamil issue because Tamil people in India pressured the government to protect Tamils in Sri Lanka.

In 1987, India signed an accord with Sri Lanka and sent the Indian Peace Keeping Force.

The IPKF later fought the LTTE and withdrew in 1989 without achieving its objective.

## 33. End Of Sri Lankan Armed Conflict

International actors such as Norway and Iceland tried to support negotiations.

The armed conflict ended in 2009 when the LTTE was defeated.

Sri Lanka maintained democracy and achieved high human development despite internal conflict.

## 34. India's Central Location

India is centrally located in South Asia.

It shares borders with most South Asian countries.

Because of its size, economy, population, and location, many regional issues involve India.

Smaller neighbours often fear Indian domination, while India worries about instability and outside influence in its neighbourhood.

## 35. India-Pakistan Conflict

India-Pakistan conflict is the most serious inter-state conflict in South Asia.

It involves:

- Kashmir.
- Wars.
- Nuclear weapons.
- Siachen.
- Terrorism allegations.
- River waters.
- Sir Creek.
- Mutual mistrust.

## 36. Kashmir Conflict

After partition, India and Pakistan fought over Kashmir.

Wars in 1947-48 and 1965 did not settle the dispute.

The 1947-48 war divided the region into Pakistan-occupied Kashmir and Jammu and Kashmir on the Indian side, separated by the Line of Control.

The 1971 war changed South Asia but did not resolve Kashmir.

## 37. Nuclearisation

Both India and Pakistan acquired nuclear weapons in the 1990s.

India conducted nuclear tests at Pokhran in 1998.

Pakistan responded with nuclear tests in the Chagai Hills.

Nuclear weapons reduced the likelihood of full-scale war but increased the danger of escalation.

## 38. Low-Key Violence And Allegations

India accuses Pakistan of supporting militants in Kashmir through arms, training, money, and protection.

India has also alleged Pakistani support for pro-Khalistani militants and anti-India activity through neighbouring territories.

Pakistan accuses India of creating trouble in Sindh and Balochistan.

These allegations deepen mistrust.

## 39. Indus Waters Treaty

India and Pakistan signed the Indus Waters Treaty in 1960 with World Bank assistance.

The treaty survived wars and crises.

It shows that even hostile countries can cooperate on practical issues.

There are still disputes over interpretation and use of river waters.

## 40. Sir Creek And Siachen

Sir Creek is a dispute over demarcation in the Rann of Kutch.

It appears minor but may affect control over sea resources.

Siachen is a strategic glacier dispute.

Both issues show how geography, resources, and security overlap.

## 41. India-Bangladesh Differences

India and Bangladesh have had differences over:

- Ganga and Brahmaputra waters.
- Illegal immigration.
- Anti-India groups.
- Movement of Indian troops to the northeast.
- Natural gas exports.
- Boundary issues.
- Trade imbalance.

Bangladesh has sometimes viewed India as a regional bully.

## 42. India-Bangladesh Cooperation

India and Bangladesh also cooperate.

Areas include:

- Economic relations.
- Disaster management.
- Environmental issues.
- Act East connectivity through Myanmar.
- Boundary settlement.
- Exchange of enclaves in 2015.
- Common threat identification.

The relationship combines grievance and partnership.

## 43. India-Nepal Relations

India and Nepal share a special relationship.

Citizens can travel to and work in each other's country without visas and passports.

This open relationship is unusual in world politics.

However, tensions exist over trade, China, security, water, hydropower, and perceptions of interference.

## 44. India-Nepal Cooperation

Despite tensions, relations are stable because of:

- Trade.
- Scientific cooperation.
- Shared natural resources.
- Electricity generation.
- Water management.
- Cultural ties.
- Open border movement.

Nepal's democratic consolidation is expected to improve ties.

## 45. India-Sri Lanka Relations

India-Sri Lanka difficulties mainly relate to ethnic conflict and Tamil concerns.

India now generally prefers non-interference in Sri Lanka's internal troubles after the IPKF experience.

India and Sri Lanka have strengthened relations through:

- Free trade agreement.
- Post-tsunami reconstruction support.
- Economic cooperation.

## 46. India-Bhutan Relations

India has a special relationship with Bhutan.

There is no major conflict between the two governments.

Bhutan's action against militants from India's northeast helped India.

India supports hydropower projects and remains a major development partner.

## 47. India-Maldives Relations

India's relations with Maldives are generally warm.

In 1988, India quickly responded when mercenaries attacked Maldives.

India has supported economic development, tourism, and fisheries.

## 48. India And Smaller Neighbours

Smaller neighbours often fear India because of its size and power.

India often fears:

- Political instability nearby.
- Anti-India groups.
- Outside powers gaining influence.
- Security spillover.

Regional trust is difficult because geography gives India a central role.

## 49. Conflicts Not Only Involving India

Not all South Asian conflicts involve India.

Examples include:

- Nepal-Bhutan issues over ethnic Nepali migration.
- Bangladesh-Myanmar issues over Rohingyas.
- Bangladesh-Nepal concerns over Himalayan river waters.

However, India is involved in many major regional questions because of geography.

## 50. SAARC

The South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation, or SAARC, began in 1985.

It is a regional initiative for cooperation through multilateral means.

It includes South Asian countries and seeks cooperation despite political differences.

## 51. Limits Of SAARC

SAARC has not been very successful because of:

- India-Pakistan conflict.
- Political mistrust.
- Security concerns.
- Fear of Indian dominance.
- Weak implementation.
- Bilateral disputes affecting regional cooperation.

The idea is strong, but political obstacles remain.

## 52. SAFTA

The South Asian Free Trade Agreement was signed in 2004 and came into effect on 1 January 2006.

It aims to lower trade tariffs and create a free trade zone in South Asia.

Its logic is that trade can support peace and cooperation.

## 53. Debates Over SAFTA

Some neighbours fear SAFTA may allow India to dominate their markets and societies through commercial influence.

India argues that free trade can benefit all and create conditions for better political cooperation.

Some Indians argue that bilateral agreements are already enough.

## 54. India-Pakistan Peace Efforts

Despite conflict, India and Pakistan have attempted peace through:

- Confidence building measures.
- Summit meetings.
- Bus routes.
- Easier visas at certain times.
- Increased trade.
- Social activists and public diplomacy.
- Agreements to reduce nuclear risks.

The relationship has improved and worsened at different times.

## 55. Outside Powers In South Asia

No region exists in isolation.

China and the United States influence South Asian politics.

Their involvement affects:

- India-Pakistan relations.
- Security calculations.
- Trade.
- Development.
- Strategic alignments.
- Regional balance of power.

## 56. China In South Asia

China's strategic partnership with Pakistan is a major concern for India.

At the same time, India-China economic ties have grown since 1991.

China's role creates both competition and interdependence.

## 57. United States In South Asia

US involvement increased after the Cold War.

The US has relations with both India and Pakistan.

It sometimes acts as a moderator in India-Pakistan tensions.

Economic reforms, markets, and the South Asian diaspora have increased US interest in the region.

## 58. Cooperation Or Conflict?

South Asia may continue as a conflict-prone region or develop into a stronger regional bloc.

The result depends mainly on:

- Governments.
- People.
- Regional trust.
- Trade.
- Democratic stability.
- Conflict management.
- Sensitivity to smaller states.

Outside powers matter, but regional actors matter more.

## 59. Comparing Key South Asian Political Experiences

| Country | Main political pattern |
|---|---|
| India | Continuous democracy since independence |
| Sri Lanka | Democracy with severe ethnic conflict |
| Pakistan | Alternation between civilian rule and military dominance |
| Bangladesh | Liberation struggle, military rule, later multi-party democracy |
| Nepal | Monarchy, Maoist conflict, democratic republic |
| Bhutan | Constitutional monarchy and multi-party democracy |
| Maldives | Sultanate to republic to multi-party politics |

## 60. Common Student Confusions

South Asia is not politically uniform. Countries have very different democratic experiences.

India-Pakistan conflict is central, but it is not the only regional issue.

SAARC is not a military alliance. It is a cooperation forum.

SAFTA is about trade, not a complete political union.

India's size gives it influence, but also creates suspicion among neighbours.

Democracy can survive even with conflict, as Sri Lanka shows, but conflict can severely damage social harmony.

## 61. Model Answer: Challenges To Democracy In Pakistan

Democracy in Pakistan has faced challenges from the dominance of the military, clergy, and landowning elites. Conflict with India strengthened pro-military arguments that national security required army rule. Political parties were portrayed as selfish or chaotic, and international powers often supported military regimes for strategic reasons. However, Pakistan also has a strong pro-democracy sentiment, a courageous press, and human rights movements.

## 62. Model Answer: Sri Lankan Ethnic Conflict

Sri Lanka's ethnic conflict arose from Sinhala majoritarian politics and the neglect of Tamil concerns. Tamil nationalism grew militant, and the LTTE fought for a separate Tamil Eelam from 1983. India became involved because of Tamil pressure in India and sent the IPKF after the 1987 accord, but withdrew in 1989. The armed conflict ended in 2009 when the LTTE was defeated. Sri Lanka retained democracy and achieved high human development despite the conflict.

## 63. Model Answer: SAARC And SAFTA

SAARC is the South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation, formed in 1985 to promote cooperation through multilateral means. Its success has been limited by political disputes, especially India-Pakistan tensions. SAFTA, signed in 2004 and effective from 2006, aimed to lower tariffs and create a free trade zone. It reflects the belief that freer trade can support cooperation, though some smaller neighbours fear Indian economic dominance.
