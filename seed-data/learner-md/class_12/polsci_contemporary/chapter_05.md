# Chapter 5: Security In The Contemporary World

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains the meaning of security and shows why the concept has expanded beyond military threats. Traditional security focuses mainly on the state, borders, war, military force, alliances, and balance of power. Non-traditional security focuses on people, communities, humanity, poverty, disease, migration, environmental threats, terrorism, and human rights.

The key learning is that security is not a single fixed idea. It depends on whose values are threatened, what kind of threat exists, and whether military force, cooperation, democracy, development, or international institutions are the best response.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Define security as freedom from serious threats to core values.
- Distinguish traditional and non-traditional security.
- Explain external military threats in traditional security.
- Describe deterrence, defence, balance of power, and alliance.
- Explain internal security in newly independent states.
- Describe disarmament, arms control, and confidence building.
- Explain human security and global security.
- Distinguish narrow and broad concepts of human security.
- Identify terrorism, human rights violations, poverty, migration, refugees, and epidemics as security concerns.
- Explain cooperative security.
- Describe India's four-part security strategy.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Security in the contemporary world includes both traditional protection of the state from military threats and non-traditional protection of people and humanity from violence, poverty, disease, displacement, environmental danger, and rights violations.

## 2. Why Security Needs Debate

The term security is often used to stop debate.

Governments may say that an issue is too important or secret for public discussion.

In a democracy, citizens must understand security because security decisions affect:

- War and peace.
- Public spending.
- Civil liberties.
- Foreign policy.
- Development.
- Human rights.
- Daily life.

Security cannot be left only to experts and leaders.

## 3. Basic Meaning Of Security

At the simplest level, security means freedom from threats.

But not every threat is a security threat.

Everyday life contains many risks. If every risk became a security issue, the idea would lose meaning.

Therefore, security usually refers to extremely dangerous threats to core values.

## 4. Core Values

Core values are values so important that their destruction would cause severe damage.

For a state, core values may include:

- Sovereignty.
- Independence.
- Territorial integrity.
- Political order.

For people, core values may include:

- Life.
- Dignity.
- Livelihood.
- Health.
- Freedom.
- Community survival.

The question "whose core values?" is central to the chapter.

## 5. Why Security Is A Slippery Idea

Security is difficult to define because:

- Societies change over time.
- Different countries face different threats.
- Governments and citizens may define core values differently.
- Threats vary in intensity.
- Military and non-military issues can overlap.

This is why the chapter divides security into traditional and non-traditional views.

## 6. Traditional Security

Traditional security is state-centred.

It focuses mainly on military threats from other countries.

The referent, or the thing being secured, is the state.

The main values protected are sovereignty, independence, and territorial integrity.

## 7. External Military Threat

In traditional security, the greatest danger is a military threat from another country.

Military attack can threaten:

- Borders.
- Independence.
- Government institutions.
- Territorial integrity.
- Citizens' lives.

War rarely affects only soldiers; civilians are often harmed.

## 8. Three Choices Under Traditional Security

When threatened by war, a government has three basic choices:

- Surrender.
- Deter the attacker.
- Defend itself if war occurs.

Surrender may happen in extreme cases, but governments do not usually present surrender as official security policy.

## 9. Deterrence

Deterrence means preventing attack by convincing the other side that the cost will be too high.

It is based on the threat of retaliation or resistance.

Example:

A state builds strong military capability so that an enemy believes attack would be too costly.

## 10. Defence

Defence means limiting or ending war after it begins.

It involves:

- Repelling attack.
- Protecting territory.
- Defeating attacking forces.
- Denying the enemy its objectives.

Defence operates when deterrence fails.

## 11. Balance Of Power

Balance of power refers to the distribution of power among states.

States watch whether neighbours or rivals are becoming stronger.

If a nearby state becomes much more powerful, it may become a future threat even if it does not currently announce hostile intentions.

## 12. Maintaining Balance Of Power

States maintain balance of power by strengthening:

- Military capability.
- Economy.
- Technology.
- Alliances.
- Strategic partnerships.

Military strength depends on economic and technological bases.

## 13. Alliance

An alliance is a coalition of states that coordinate actions to deter or defend against military attack.

Alliances are usually formalised in treaties.

They are based on national interests.

Because interests change, alliances can also change.

## 14. Alliances And Changing Interests

The US supported Islamic militants in Afghanistan against the Soviet Union in the 1980s.

Later, the US attacked Al Qaeda after the 11 September 2001 attacks.

This shows that alliances are not permanent moral friendships; they are shaped by perceived national interests.

## 15. International System And No Central Authority

Within a country, government regulates violence.

In world politics, there is no world government with unquestioned authority.

The UN exists, but it has authority only when states allow it to act and obey its decisions.

Therefore, states remain responsible for their own security.

## 16. Traditional Internal Security

Traditional security also includes internal security.

A country cannot be secure if there is violence inside its borders.

Internal peace is necessary for external defence.

However, during the Cold War, powerful states often focused more on external threats because their internal security seemed relatively settled.

## 17. Security Context After The Second World War

After 1945, the US and Soviet Union felt internally secure.

Western Europe also faced fewer internal threats compared to colonial and newly independent regions.

Therefore, the main focus of these powers was external military threat from the rival bloc.

## 18. Security In Newly Independent States

Newly independent states in Asia and Africa faced different challenges.

They often faced:

- Border disputes.
- Neighbouring-state conflicts.
- Internal separatist movements.
- Civil wars.
- Former colonial threats.
- Cold War pressures.

Their security concerns were both external and internal.

## 19. External And Internal Threats Can Merge

Sometimes a neighbour supports or encourages a separatist movement inside another state.

Then internal conflict becomes linked with external rivalry.

This makes security more complex for newly independent states.

## 20. Civil Wars

Internal wars became a major form of armed conflict after the Second World War.

The chapter notes that internal wars now make up most armed conflicts in the world.

This shows why internal security is central for many states.

## 21. Traditional Security And Cooperation

Traditional security does not mean only war preparation.

It also recognises cooperation to limit violence.

Important forms include:

- Rules on just reasons for war.
- Limits on how war is fought.
- Disarmament.
- Arms control.
- Confidence building.

## 22. Limits On War

A widely accepted idea is that war should be fought only for valid reasons such as:

- Self-defence.
- Protection of people from genocide.

Even when war happens, states should avoid:

- Killing non-combatants.
- Attacking surrendering soldiers.
- Excessive violence.

Force should be used only after alternatives fail.

## 23. Disarmament

Disarmament means states give up certain kinds of weapons.

Examples:

- Biological Weapons Convention, 1972.
- Chemical Weapons Convention, 1997.

These agreements banned production and possession of biological and chemical weapons.

## 24. Arms Control

Arms control regulates acquisition or development of weapons.

It does not necessarily abolish weapons.

It limits:

- Numbers.
- Types.
- Deployment.
- Testing.
- Development.

Arms control reduces arms race dangers.

## 25. ABM Treaty

The Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty of 1972 aimed to stop the US and Soviet Union from building large defensive missile shields.

The logic was that if one side could defend itself completely, it might feel able to launch a nuclear attack.

Limiting missile defence preserved deterrence stability.

## 26. SALT, START, And NPT

Important arms control arrangements include:

- SALT II: Strategic Arms Limitation Treaty.
- START: Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty.
- NPT: Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, 1968.

The NPT allowed countries that had tested and made nuclear weapons before 1967 to keep them, while others were expected not to acquire them.

## 27. Criticism Of NPT

The NPT is criticised as unequal because it divides the world into nuclear weapon states and non-nuclear weapon states.

It does not abolish nuclear weapons.

It mainly limits which countries may possess them.

## 28. Confidence Building

Confidence building means sharing ideas and information with rivals to reduce misunderstanding.

States may share:

- Military intentions.
- Some military plans.
- Forces possessed.
- Locations of forces.
- Exercises and movements.

The purpose is to avoid war caused by misperception or surprise.

## 29. Summary Of Traditional Security

Traditional security is mainly about the use or threat of military force.

Force is both:

- The main threat to security.
- The main means used to achieve security.

This is why non-traditional security expands the concept.

## 30. Non-Traditional Security

Non-traditional security goes beyond military threats.

It includes threats to:

- Individuals.
- Communities.
- Humanity.
- Livelihoods.
- Health.
- Environment.
- Rights.

It asks: security for whom?

## 31. Referent Of Security

The referent is the thing or person whose security is being discussed.

Traditional security answers: the state.

Non-traditional security answers: not only the state, but also individuals, communities, and humankind.

This shift changes the whole security agenda.

## 32. Human Security

Human security is about protecting people more than protecting states.

State security and human security can support each other.

But a secure state does not automatically mean secure people.

Some governments have killed more people than foreign armies.

## 33. Narrow Human Security

The narrow view of human security focuses on violent threats to individuals.

It includes protection from:

- Civil war.
- Genocide.
- Terrorism.
- Ethnic violence.
- State violence.
- Community violence.

This view expands beyond state security but keeps violence at the centre.

## 34. Broad Human Security

The broad view includes threats such as:

- Hunger.
- Disease.
- Natural disasters.
- Poverty.
- Environmental damage.
- Displacement.

Supporters argue these threats kill more people than war, genocide, and terrorism combined.

## 35. Global Security

Global security focuses on threats that affect the entire planet or large parts of humanity.

Examples include:

- Global warming.
- International terrorism.
- Pandemics.
- Epidemics.
- Climate-related disasters.
- Environmental degradation.

No country can solve these alone.

## 36. New Threat Categories

The chapter discusses several important non-traditional threats:

- Terrorism.
- Human rights violations.
- Global poverty.
- Migration and refugees.
- Health epidemics.
- Environmental threats.

These threats differ from conventional military attack.

## 37. Terrorism

Terrorism is political violence that deliberately and indiscriminately targets civilians.

International terrorism involves citizens or territory of more than one country.

Terrorist groups use violence to change political conditions or pressure governments.

## 38. Civilian Targets

Terrorist groups often choose civilian targets such as:

- Trains.
- Markets.
- Cafes.
- Planes.
- Crowded public spaces.

The aim is to terrorise the public and use fear as political pressure.

## 39. 11 September 2001

The attacks on the World Trade Center in the United States on 11 September 2001 made many governments pay greater attention to terrorism.

Terrorism itself was not new, but global attention to it increased sharply after the attacks.

## 40. Human Rights

Human rights are often classified into three types:

- Political rights, such as freedom of speech and assembly.
- Economic and social rights.
- Rights of colonised people, ethnic minorities, and indigenous groups.

There is broad agreement on the classification, but disagreement over which rights are universal and how violations should be addressed.

## 41. Human Rights Intervention Debate

Since the 1990s, cases such as Iraq's invasion of Kuwait, genocide in Rwanda, and violence in East Timor raised debate over intervention.

Some argue that the international community can use force to protect human rights.

Others warn that powerful states may use human rights language to pursue national interests.

## 42. Global Poverty

Global poverty is a major security concern because it affects survival and dignity.

Population growth and poverty often reinforce each other.

Rich states with high incomes and low population growth may grow richer, while poor states with low incomes and high population growth may become poorer.

## 43. North-South Divide

Global poverty contributes to the gap between:

- Northern developed countries.
- Southern developing countries.

Within the South, some countries have improved income and population conditions while others remain trapped in poverty and conflict.

## 44. Poverty And Conflict

Many armed conflicts occur in very poor regions.

Sub-Saharan Africa has faced severe poverty and many wars.

Poverty can intensify insecurity by weakening states, reducing opportunities, and increasing displacement.

## 45. Migration

Poverty and conflict lead people to migrate in search of safety or better opportunities.

Migration can create political tensions between countries, especially when receiving countries fear job competition or cultural change.

## 46. Refugees

Refugees are people who flee across borders because of war, natural disaster, or political persecution.

International norms generally expect states to accept refugees.

Refugee flows often match conflict zones.

## 47. Migrants Versus Refugees

Migrants voluntarily leave their country, usually for better economic opportunities.

Refugees flee because survival or safety is threatened.

States are more obligated to accept refugees than ordinary migrants.

## 48. Internally Displaced People

Internally displaced people leave their homes but remain inside their own country.

Example:

Kashmiri Pandits who fled violence in the Kashmir Valley in the early 1990s are an internally displaced community.

## 49. Health Epidemics

Diseases can spread rapidly through:

- Migration.
- Tourism.
- Business travel.
- Military movement.
- Global transport.

One country's success or failure in controlling disease affects others.

## 50. Examples Of Health Threats

The chapter discusses:

- HIV-AIDS.
- Bird flu.
- SARS.
- Corona.
- Ebola virus.
- Hantavirus.
- Hepatitis C.
- Tuberculosis.
- Malaria.
- Dengue fever.
- Cholera.

Some diseases become drug-resistant, making them harder to control.

## 51. Epidemics And Inequality

Rich countries may access expensive treatments more easily.

Poor regions may suffer higher death rates and deeper poverty because treatments are unaffordable.

This shows that health security is linked with economic inequality.

## 52. Criteria For Security Issues

Expanding security does not mean every problem becomes a security issue.

An issue should threaten the very existence or survival of the referent.

For example:

- Sea-level rise may threaten the territory of Maldives.
- HIV-AIDS may threaten social survival in badly affected regions.
- Genocide may threaten the existence of a group.

## 53. Cooperative Security

Many non-traditional threats require cooperation rather than military confrontation.

Military force cannot solve poverty, epidemics, migration, or environmental damage.

Cooperative security creates shared strategies.

## 54. Levels Of Cooperation

Cooperation may be:

- Bilateral.
- Regional.
- Continental.
- Global.

The level depends on the nature of the threat and the willingness of actors to respond.

## 55. Actors In Cooperative Security

Cooperative security may involve:

- States.
- UN.
- WHO.
- World Bank.
- IMF.
- NGOs.
- Red Cross.
- Private foundations.
- Religious organisations.
- Trade unions.
- Social organisations.
- Businesses.
- Public figures.

Security is no longer only a military affair.

## 56. Force As Last Resort

Cooperative security may sometimes involve force, but only as a last resort.

Examples:

- Stopping mass killing by a government.
- Acting against international terrorists.
- Responding when governments ignore catastrophic suffering.

The chapter emphasises collective sanction by the international community rather than unilateral action by one state.

## 57. India's Security Context

India faces both traditional and non-traditional threats.

These threats come from:

- Outside its borders.
- Inside its borders.
- Military conflict.
- Internal movements.
- Poverty and inequality.
- Regional instability.

India's strategy uses multiple components.

## 58. India's First Component: Military Capability

India has strengthened military capability because of conflicts with neighbours.

Major conflicts include:

- Pakistan in 1947-48.
- Pakistan in 1965.
- Pakistan in 1971.
- Pakistan in 1999.
- China in 1962.

India is also surrounded by nuclear-armed states.

## 59. India's Nuclear Tests

India first tested a nuclear device in 1974.

India conducted further nuclear tests in 1998.

The government justified the 1998 tests in terms of safeguarding national security, especially in a nuclearised regional environment.

## 60. India's Second Component: International Norms

India has tried to strengthen international norms and institutions.

Jawaharlal Nehru supported:

- Asian solidarity.
- Decolonisation.
- Disarmament.
- The UN as a forum for peaceful settlement.
- Non-alignment.

India wanted a more equal and peaceful global order.

## 61. Non-Discriminatory Non-Proliferation

India has supported a universal and non-discriminatory non-proliferation regime.

This means all countries should have the same rights and obligations regarding weapons of mass destruction.

India has opposed unequal arrangements that permanently privilege some states.

## 62. NIEO And Non-Alignment

India argued for a New International Economic Order, or NIEO, to reduce global economic inequality.

India used non-alignment to create space outside superpower bloc politics.

This was also a security strategy because it reduced dependence on military blocs.

## 63. India And Cooperative Security

India has supported cooperative security by:

- Participating in UN peacekeeping missions.
- Supporting international institutions.
- Ratifying the Kyoto Protocol.
- Supporting disarmament debates.
- Advocating fair global rules.

India's security strategy includes global engagement.

## 64. India's Third Component: Internal Security

India has faced internal security challenges from militant and separatist movements.

Examples include movements in:

- Nagaland.
- Mizoram.
- Punjab.
- Kashmir.

India has responded partly through democratic politics and power sharing.

## 65. Democracy And Internal Security

India tries to preserve national unity through a democratic system that allows groups to express grievances and share political power.

Democracy is not only a political ideal.

It can also be a security strategy because it reduces alienation and creates institutional channels for demands.

## 66. India's Fourth Component: Development

India has tried to develop its economy so that poverty, misery, and extreme inequality are reduced.

Development supports security by improving:

- Livelihoods.
- Education.
- Health.
- Political participation.
- Social stability.

Economic growth must be combined with human development.

## 67. Why Development Is Security

If citizens face hunger, unemployment, disease, and exclusion, they are insecure even if borders are protected.

Human development reduces vulnerability.

Democratic pressure can push governments to combine growth with welfare.

## 68. Comparing Traditional And Non-Traditional Security

| Basis | Traditional security | Non-traditional security |
|---|---|---|
| Main referent | State | Individuals, communities, humanity |
| Main threat | Military attack | Violence, poverty, disease, environment, displacement |
| Main method | Force, deterrence, defence, alliances | Cooperation, development, institutions, rights protection |
| Main actors | States and militaries | States, organisations, NGOs, communities |
| Example | Border war | Epidemic or climate threat |

## 69. Common Student Confusions

Security is not only military security.

Human security does not replace state security; it expands the focus.

Deterrence prevents attack; defence responds when attack happens.

Disarmament removes weapons; arms control regulates them.

Refugees cross borders; internally displaced people remain inside their country.

Cooperative security does not mean force is never used; it means force should be collective and last resort.

## 70. Model Answer: Traditional And Non-Traditional Security

Traditional security is state-centred and focuses mainly on military threats to sovereignty, independence, and territorial integrity. It uses deterrence, defence, balance of power, alliances, disarmament, arms control, and confidence building. Non-traditional security expands the focus to individuals, communities, and humanity. It includes terrorism, human rights violations, poverty, migration, refugees, epidemics, and environmental threats. It usually requires cooperation rather than military confrontation.

## 71. Model Answer: Balance Of Power

Balance of power means the relative distribution of power among states. A country watches the strength of other countries, especially neighbours and rivals, because a powerful state may become a future threat. A state can maintain favourable balance by increasing military capability, strengthening the economy and technology, and forming alliances. Balance of power is a traditional security strategy.

## 72. Model Answer: India's Security Strategy

India's security strategy has four broad components. First, India strengthened military capability because of wars with Pakistan and China and the presence of nuclear-armed neighbours. Second, India supported international norms and institutions, including disarmament, decolonisation, non-alignment, the UN, and a non-discriminatory non-proliferation regime. Third, India addressed internal security challenges through democratic politics and power sharing. Fourth, India pursued economic development to reduce poverty and inequality, because human development is also a form of security.
