# Chapter 2: Contemporary Centres Of Power

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how world politics after the Cold War did not remain only about the United States. Alternative centres of power began to matter in Europe and Asia. The chapter focuses especially on the European Union, ASEAN, China, India-China relations, Japan, and South Korea.

Read this chapter as a map of power beyond military force. Economic strength, regional cooperation, trade networks, technology, diplomacy, population, and strategic location can all turn countries or regional organisations into important centres of power.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

After the end of Cold War bipolarity, the European Union, ASEAN, China, Japan, and South Korea became important centres of power because economic strength, regional cooperation, and strategic influence began to shape world politics in new ways.

## 2. Why Alternative Centres Became Important

The collapse of the Soviet Union left the United States as the only superpower.

However, US dominance did not mean that other regions became irrelevant.

Alternative centres of power emerged because:

- Europe integrated economically and politically.
- Southeast Asia created regional cooperation through ASEAN.
- China became a major economic power.
- Japan remained a technological and economic giant.
- South Korea grew rapidly into an advanced industrial economy.

These centres could limit, balance, or influence US power in different ways.

## 3. Power Is More Than Military Power

In contemporary world politics, power includes:

- Economic size.
- Trade influence.
- Technology.
- Diplomatic reach.
- Military capacity.
- Population.
- Energy access.
- Regional leadership.
- Institutional influence.

The EU, ASEAN, China, Japan, and South Korea show that power may arise through cooperation and development, not only through war.

## 4. The European Question After The Second World War

After the Second World War, Europe faced a major question: should it return to old rivalries, or build institutions for cooperation?

The war had damaged:

- Economies.
- Cities.
- Political confidence.
- Relations among European states.
- The old balance of power.

European integration was a response to destruction and rivalry.

## 5. Role Of The Cold War In European Integration

The Cold War encouraged Western European states to cooperate.

The United States supported European recovery through the Marshall Plan.

NATO created a collective security structure.

Economic recovery and security cooperation helped European countries work together instead of fighting old rivalries.

## 6. Marshall Plan

The Marshall Plan was a US financial aid programme to revive Western Europe after the Second World War.

It mattered because:

- It helped rebuild European economies.
- It reduced the appeal of communism in Western Europe.
- It encouraged cooperation among European states.
- It helped create conditions for later integration.

## 7. OEEC

The Organisation for European Economic Cooperation was established in 1948.

It channelled Marshall Plan aid to Western European states.

It also became a forum for cooperation on trade and economic issues.

This was an early institutional step toward integration.

## 8. Council Of Europe

The Council of Europe was established in 1949.

It represented a step toward political cooperation.

It showed that European integration was not only about markets but also about political coordination and shared institutions.

## 9. ECSC

In 1951, six countries signed the Treaty of Paris creating the European Coal and Steel Community.

The six countries were:

- France.
- West Germany.
- Italy.
- Belgium.
- Netherlands.
- Luxembourg.

Coal and steel had been central to war industries, so pooling them helped reduce rivalry.

## 10. EEC And Euratom

In 1957, the same six countries signed the Treaties of Rome.

These created:

- European Economic Community.
- European Atomic Energy Community.

The EEC deepened economic integration and created the foundation for the later European Union.

## 11. From Economic Integration To Political Union

European integration began mainly with economic cooperation.

Over time, it acquired a political dimension through:

- European Parliament.
- Common policies.
- Expansion of membership.
- Cooperation on justice.
- Cooperation on foreign and security policy.
- Common currency.

The EU evolved from an economic project into a political actor.

## 12. Maastricht Treaty

The Maastricht Treaty was signed in 1992.

It established the European Union.

It laid the foundation for:

- Common foreign and security policy.
- Cooperation on justice and home affairs.
- Creation of a single currency.
- Deeper political identity.

The treaty marked a major shift from community to union.

## 13. Symbols Of The EU

The European Union has symbols similar to a state:

- Flag.
- Anthem.
- Founding date.
- Common currency among many members.

These symbols show that the EU acts like more than a normal international organisation in some areas.

## 14. The Euro

The euro is the common currency used by many EU members.

It matters because:

- It deepens economic integration.
- It strengthens the EU's financial identity.
- It can challenge the dominance of the US dollar.
- It makes trade and travel easier among eurozone members.

Not all EU members use the euro.

## 15. Schengen Area

The Schengen arrangement removed border controls among many European states.

It allows easier movement across participating countries.

This shows that European integration affects ordinary travel, not only high-level diplomacy.

## 16. EU Expansion

The EU expanded by including many countries from the former Soviet bloc and other European regions.

Expansion increased:

- Market size.
- Political influence.
- Diversity.
- Strategic reach.

It also created tensions over sovereignty, identity, migration, and unequal development.

## 17. EU Economic Power

The EU has major economic influence.

Its power comes from:

- Large GDP.
- Large share in world trade.
- Strong currency.
- Advanced industries.
- Common market.
- Influence over neighbouring regions.
- Trade bargaining power with the US and China.

Economic strength is the EU's strongest base of power.

## 18. EU Political And Diplomatic Power

The EU can influence international issues through:

- Diplomacy.
- Trade negotiations.
- Development assistance.
- Environmental policy.
- Human rights dialogue.
- Economic sanctions.

France, an EU member, holds a permanent seat in the UN Security Council.

The EU also includes several non-permanent members at different times, giving it influence in UN debates.

## 19. EU Military Influence

The EU's combined armed forces are among the largest in the world.

Its total defence spending is second only to the United States.

France has nuclear weapons.

The EU is also important in space and communication technology.

However, EU military power is limited by differences among member states.

## 20. Limits On EU Power

The EU faces internal limits:

- Member states retain their own foreign policies.
- Defence policies are not always unified.
- Some states resist deeper integration.
- Public opinion may oppose giving more power to EU institutions.
- Euroscepticism exists in parts of Europe.
- Economic interests differ among members.

The EU is powerful but not fully united like a single nation-state.

## 21. Example Of EU Division

During the Iraq War, some EU members supported the US-led coalition while Germany and France opposed US policy.

This showed that the EU cannot always act with one voice in foreign and defence policy.

## 22. ASEAN's Background

Southeast Asia experienced:

- Colonial rule.
- Japanese occupation.
- Poverty.
- Economic backwardness.
- Nation-building challenges.
- Cold War pressure.

These conditions made regional cooperation necessary.

## 23. Formation Of ASEAN

ASEAN was established in 1967 through the Bangkok Declaration.

The five founding members were:

- Indonesia.
- Malaysia.
- Philippines.
- Singapore.
- Thailand.

Later, Brunei Darussalam, Vietnam, Lao PDR, Myanmar, and Cambodia joined.

ASEAN became a ten-member organisation.

## 24. ASEAN's Objectives

ASEAN was formed to:

- Accelerate economic growth.
- Promote social progress.
- Encourage cultural development.
- Promote regional peace.
- Promote stability based on rule of law.
- Respect principles of the UN Charter.

Economic development and peace were connected goals.

## 25. The ASEAN Way

The ASEAN Way means an informal, cooperative, non-confrontationist style of interaction.

It emphasises:

- Respect for national sovereignty.
- Non-interference.
- Consultation.
- Consensus.
- Avoiding direct confrontation.
- Gradual cooperation.

This is different from the EU's more formal and supranational model.

## 26. ASEAN And Sovereignty

ASEAN countries strongly protect sovereignty.

They do not want a powerful supranational authority like the EU.

This helps members cooperate despite political differences, but it can also limit collective action.

## 27. ASEAN Community

In 2003, ASEAN agreed to establish an ASEAN Community.

It has three pillars:

- ASEAN Security Community.
- ASEAN Economic Community.
- ASEAN Socio-Cultural Community.

This broadened ASEAN beyond basic economic cooperation.

## 28. ASEAN Security Community

The ASEAN Security Community is based on the idea that territorial disputes should not escalate into armed confrontation.

Member states agreed to uphold:

- Peace.
- Neutrality.
- Cooperation.
- Non-interference.
- Respect for national differences.
- Sovereign rights.

The goal is stability without creating a military bloc.

## 29. ASEAN Regional Forum

The ASEAN Regional Forum, or ARF, was established in 1994.

It coordinates security and foreign policy dialogue.

It provides a platform where Asian countries and major powers can talk about political and security concerns.

## 30. ASEAN Economic Community

ASEAN remains strongly economic in orientation.

The ASEAN Economic Community aims to:

- Create a common market.
- Create a production base.
- Promote social and economic development.
- Improve dispute settlement.
- Encourage free trade in investment, labour, and services.

ASEAN's economic growth has increased its influence.

## 31. ASEAN Free Trade And External Partners

ASEAN has focused on free trade arrangements.

The US and China have negotiated with ASEAN.

ASEAN's importance comes from its role as:

- A growing market.
- A production region.
- A trading partner.
- A diplomatic forum.

## 32. ASEAN Vision 2020

ASEAN Vision 2020 defines an outward-looking role for ASEAN.

It encourages:

- Regional cooperation.
- Negotiation over conflicts.
- East Asian cooperation.
- Engagement with dialogue partners.
- Greater international relevance.

ASEAN's strength lies in interaction and consultation.

## 33. India And ASEAN

During the Cold War, India did not pay enough attention to ASEAN.

Since the early 1990s, India has tried to strengthen ties through the Look East policy.

Since 2014, this has become the Act East policy.

India signed trade agreements with ASEAN members and the ASEAN-India FTA came into effect in 2010.

## 34. Why ASEAN Matters To India

ASEAN matters to India because:

- It connects India to Southeast Asia.
- It supports India's Act East policy.
- It offers trade and investment opportunities.
- It links India's northeast to regional networks.
- It provides a forum for dialogue with Asian powers.
- It helps India engage China, Japan, and South Korea regionally.

## 35. China As A Centre Of Power

China is an immediate neighbour of India and one of the most important centres of power in world politics.

Its rise is based mainly on:

- Economic growth.
- Population.
- Manufacturing capacity.
- Foreign trade.
- Foreign exchange reserves.
- Strategic location.
- Military power.
- Diplomatic influence.

## 36. People's Republic Of China

The People's Republic of China was established in 1949 after the communist revolution led by Mao.

China adopted a Soviet-style model.

The economy was based on:

- State ownership.
- Heavy industry.
- Central planning.
- Import substitution.
- Limited links with the capitalist world.

## 37. Early Chinese Development Model

China used agricultural surplus to build heavy industry.

It aimed to rely on its own resources because it lacked foreign exchange and had limited global links.

The model helped establish an industrial base but also created limits.

## 38. Achievements Of Early Communist China

The early model achieved:

- Industrial foundations.
- Employment security.
- Social welfare support.
- Education gains.
- Health improvements.
- Moderate economic growth.

China moved ahead of many developing countries in health and education.

## 39. Problems Of The Command Economy

China's command economy faced problems:

- Low per capita income.
- Population growth absorbing gains.
- Insufficient agricultural surplus.
- Limited international trade.
- Slow industrial growth.
- Limited access to technology.

By the 1970s, reform became necessary.

## 40. China And The United States In 1972

China ended political and economic isolation by establishing relations with the United States in 1972.

This opened the way for wider engagement with the global economy.

It also showed China's shift toward a more pragmatic foreign policy.

## 41. Four Modernisations

Premier Zhou Enlai proposed the Four Modernisations in 1973.

They covered:

- Agriculture.
- Industry.
- Science and technology.
- Military.

These areas became central to China's reform agenda.

## 42. Deng Xiaoping And Open Door Policy

Deng Xiaoping announced the open door policy and economic reforms in 1978.

The aim was to increase productivity by bringing in:

- Foreign capital.
- Foreign technology.
- Foreign trade.
- Market incentives.

China did not follow shock therapy. It opened gradually.

## 43. Gradual Reform Path

China's reforms occurred step by step:

- Agriculture was privatised in 1982.
- Industry was privatised in 1998.
- Special Economic Zones were created.
- Trade barriers were reduced selectively.
- Foreign investors were encouraged.

The state continued to play a central role.

## 44. Special Economic Zones

Special Economic Zones are areas where foreign investors can set up enterprises with favourable conditions.

They helped China:

- Attract FDI.
- Increase exports.
- Learn foreign technology.
- Build manufacturing capacity.
- Link with world markets.

SEZs became engines of China's outward economic growth.

## 45. China's WTO Accession

China joined the WTO in 2001.

This deepened its integration into the global economy.

It allowed China to shape world trade more directly and strengthened its role as a global economic actor.

## 46. Effects Of Chinese Reforms

China's reforms produced:

- Faster industrial growth.
- Rise in agricultural production.
- Higher rural incomes.
- Growth of rural industry.
- Massive foreign trade.
- Huge FDI inflows.
- Large foreign exchange reserves.
- Greater global influence.

The reforms helped China become a major economic power.

## 47. Problems Created By Chinese Reforms

Not everyone benefited equally.

Problems included:

- Unemployment.
- Poor working conditions.
- Gendered labour problems.
- Environmental degradation.
- Corruption.
- Rural-urban inequality.
- Coastal-inland inequality.
- Social disparities.

China's growth created power but also domestic strain.

## 48. China And Regional Influence

China's economic integration increased its influence over:

- ASEAN.
- Japan.
- Russia.
- Taiwan.
- The United States.
- Developing economies in Africa and Latin America.

Economic interdependence reduced some conflicts but did not remove all disputes.

## 49. China And Taiwan

China regards Taiwan as a renegade province.

It hopes to resolve differences partly by integrating Taiwan more closely with China's economy.

This shows how economic ties can be used as a political strategy.

## 50. India And China Before Modern Conflict

India and China were major Asian powers before Western imperialism.

Their zones of influence rarely overlapped.

As a result, they had limited political and cultural interaction for long periods.

In the twentieth century, both had to learn how to deal with each other as modern nation-states.

## 51. Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai

After Indian independence and the Chinese revolution, there was hope that India and China would cooperate in shaping Asia and the developing world.

For a time, the slogan Hindi-Chini bhai-bhai symbolised friendship.

This hope was later damaged by border tensions.

## 52. Tibet And Border Disputes

India-China relations worsened after:

- China's takeover of Tibet in 1950.
- Disagreement over the Sino-Indian border.
- Competing claims in Arunachal Pradesh.
- Competing claims in Aksai Chin in Ladakh.

These issues became central to conflict.

## 53. The 1962 War

India and China fought a border war in 1962.

India suffered military reverses.

The war had long-term effects:

- Trust collapsed.
- Diplomatic relations were downgraded.
- Indian security thinking changed.
- Border settlement became more difficult.

Relations remained frozen for many years.

## 54. Improvement After 1976

Diplomatic relations improved slowly after 1976.

China became less ideological and more pragmatic after leadership changes.

Both sides began border talks in 1981.

They were willing to improve relations while postponing final settlement of contentious issues.

## 55. Rajiv Gandhi's 1988 Visit

Rajiv Gandhi's visit to China in December 1988 gave new momentum to relations.

After this, both countries took steps to:

- Reduce conflict.
- Maintain peace and tranquility on the border.
- Increase cultural exchanges.
- Cooperate in science and technology.
- Open border posts for trade.

## 56. Economic Dimension Of India-China Relations

After the Cold War, India-China relations gained an economic dimension.

Trade grew rapidly from the 1990s.

The raw chapter notes bilateral trade rising from $338 million in 1992 to more than $84 billion in 2017.

Economic interdependence created incentives for stability.

## 57. Strategic Dimension Of India-China Relations

Both India and China see themselves as rising powers.

Both want a major role in:

- Asian politics.
- Global economy.
- International institutions.
- Energy security.
- Regional order.

This creates both cooperation and competition.

## 58. Areas Of Cooperation

India and China have cooperated in:

- Trade.
- Energy bidding abroad.
- WTO-related positions.
- Cultural exchanges.
- Border confidence measures.
- Scientific cooperation.

The relationship is not only conflictual.

## 59. Areas Of Tension

Tensions include:

- Boundary dispute.
- China's relationship with Pakistan.
- China-Pakistan Economic Corridor.
- China's support to Pakistan in some international settings.
- Indian concerns over China's ties with Bangladesh and Myanmar.
- Strategic competition in Asia.

The relationship has both cooperation and mistrust.

## 60. Japan As A Centre Of Power

Japan has very few natural resources and imports most raw materials.

Even so, it became a major economic power after the Second World War.

It is known for high-technology brands such as Sony, Panasonic, Canon, Suzuki, Honda, Toyota, and Mazda.

## 61. Japan's Power Profile

Japan's significance includes:

- Advanced technology.
- Large economy.
- G-7 membership.
- OECD membership since 1964.
- Major contribution to the UN budget.
- Security alliance with the US since 1951.
- Strong industrial brands.

Japan is economically powerful despite constitutional limits on war.

## 62. Article 9 Of Japan's Constitution

Article 9 says the Japanese people renounce war as a sovereign right and reject the threat or use of force for settling international disputes.

Even so, Japan has significant defence spending and advanced capabilities.

This creates debate over whether Japan can become a fuller strategic power.

## 63. South Korea As A Centre Of Power

The Korean peninsula was divided after the Second World War along the 38th parallel.

The Korean War from 1950 to 1953 deepened rivalry between North Korea and South Korea.

Both Koreas became UN members on 17 September 1991.

South Korea later emerged as a major Asian economic power.

## 64. Miracle On The Han River

South Korea's rapid development from the 1960s to the 1980s is called the Miracle on the Han River.

It involved:

- Industrialisation.
- Export orientation.
- Land reforms.
- Rural development.
- Human resource development.
- Public infrastructure.
- Strong institutions.
- Equitable growth policies.

South Korea became known for brands such as Samsung, LG, and Hyundai.

## 65. South Korea's Human Development

South Korea achieved high human development.

Contributing factors include:

- Education.
- Health.
- Rural reforms.
- Economic growth.
- Redistribution policies.
- Governance.
- Infrastructure.

Its rise shows that economic growth and human development can reinforce each other.

## 67. Common Student Confusions

The EU is not a country, but it acts like a political actor in some areas.

ASEAN is not like the EU. It protects sovereignty and avoids supranational authority.

China did not use shock therapy. It reformed gradually.

Economic growth does not remove all political problems.

India-China relations are neither only hostile nor only friendly; they include cooperation and competition.

Japan's pacifist constitution does not mean Japan has no strategic importance.

## 68. Model Answer: EU As An Influential Regional Organisation

The European Union is influential because it has a large economy, a major share in world trade, a strong currency, advanced technology, diplomatic influence, and significant combined military capacity. It can shape trade disputes, influence neighbouring regions, and act in international organisations. However, its power is limited because member states often retain separate foreign and defence policies, and some publics resist deeper integration.

## 69. Model Answer: ASEAN Way

The ASEAN Way is the informal, cooperative, and non-confrontationist style of interaction among ASEAN members. It emphasises respect for sovereignty, non-interference, consultation, consensus, and gradual cooperation. This style helped Southeast Asian countries cooperate despite political differences, but it also limits ASEAN's ability to act forcefully.

## 70. Model Answer: China's Economic Reforms

China moved from a state-controlled command economy to a state-guided market economy through gradual reforms. After establishing relations with the United States in 1972 and adopting the Four Modernisations, Deng Xiaoping introduced the open door policy in 1978. Agriculture was privatised first, industry later, and Special Economic Zones encouraged foreign investment. China joined the WTO in 2001. These reforms increased trade, FDI, agricultural production, rural incomes, and industrial growth, but also caused inequality, unemployment, corruption, and environmental degradation.

## 71. Model Answer: India-China Relations

India and China initially hoped to cooperate after independence and revolution, but relations worsened over Tibet and the boundary dispute. The 1962 war caused long-term mistrust and diplomatic downgrading. Relations improved after 1976, border talks began in 1981, and Rajiv Gandhi's 1988 visit gave new momentum. Since the Cold War, trade and cooperation have grown, but tensions remain over the boundary, China's ties with Pakistan, and strategic competition in Asia.
