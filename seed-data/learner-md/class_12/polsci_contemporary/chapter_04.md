# Chapter 4: International Organisations

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains why international organisations are needed, how the United Nations was created, why reform is demanded, why reform is difficult, and how India views UN restructuring. It also introduces several important international and transnational organisations such as the IMF, World Bank, WTO, IAEA, Amnesty International, and Human Rights Watch.

The central idea is that international organisations are imperfect but necessary. They are not world governments, yet they create forums, rules, information, and mechanisms through which states and societies can cooperate on war, peace, development, rights, health, trade, finance, refugees, nuclear safety, and global crises.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

International organisations like the UN are needed because states face conflicts and common problems that cannot be handled effectively by any one country alone, even though these organisations remain limited by state power and political disagreement.

## 2. Why International Organisations Matter

Countries often disagree.

Disagreement does not have to lead to war.

International organisations help countries:

- Talk.
- Negotiate.
- Share information.
- Build rules.
- Monitor agreements.
- Cooperate on common problems.
- Reduce chances of conflict.

They provide structure to international life.

## 3. International Organisations Are Not Super-States

An international organisation is not a world government.

It does not stand above states with unlimited authority.

It is created by states and depends on the willingness of states to support and obey it.

This is why international organisations can be useful but not all-powerful.

## 4. Two Main Uses Of International Organisations

International organisations are useful in two broad ways.

First, they help with war and peace by giving states a place to negotiate.

Second, they help with cooperation on issues such as:

- Disease.
- Climate change.
- Refugees.
- Development.
- Trade.
- Finance.
- Human rights.
- Nuclear safety.

Many such problems cross borders.

## 5. Why Cooperation Is Difficult

Even when states know cooperation is necessary, they may disagree over:

- Who pays the cost.
- Who receives the benefit.
- How rules will be enforced.
- Whether others will cheat.
- Who has more responsibility.
- What counts as a fair solution.

International organisations help reduce these doubts through information, rules, and procedures.

## 6. Examples Of Problems Requiring Cooperation

Some problems cannot be solved by one country alone.

Examples include:

- Global warming.
- Epidemics.
- Refugee crises.
- Nuclear proliferation.
- International terrorism.
- Financial crises.
- Ocean pollution.
- War crimes.

These problems require organised international cooperation.

## 7. League Of Nations

The First World War encouraged the creation of an international organisation to prevent future wars.

The League of Nations was created for this purpose.

It had some early successes but failed to prevent the Second World War.

Its failure showed the need for a stronger global organisation.

## 8. Birth Of The United Nations

The United Nations was founded in 1945 after the Second World War.

It was created through the signing of the UN Charter.

It began with 51 original founding members.

24 October is celebrated as UN Day because the UN officially came into existence on that date in 1945.

India joined the UN on 30 October 1945.

## 9. Objectives Of The UN

The UN was created to:

- Prevent international conflict.
- Stop conflicts from escalating into war.
- Limit hostilities if war breaks out.
- Facilitate cooperation among states.
- Promote social and economic development.
- Improve conditions for peace.

The UN connects security with development.

## 10. UN Membership

By 2011, the UN had 193 member states.

This includes almost all independent states.

The wide membership gives the UN global legitimacy.

However, wide membership also makes agreement difficult because states have different interests.

## 11. General Assembly

The General Assembly includes all member states.

Each member has one vote.

It debates global issues and reflects the principle of sovereign equality.

It is broad and representative but its decisions are often less binding than Security Council decisions.

## 12. Security Council

The Security Council has primary responsibility for international peace and security.

It includes:

- Five permanent members.
- Ten non-permanent members.

The permanent members are:

- United States.
- Russia.
- United Kingdom.
- France.
- China.

They were selected because they were the most powerful victors after the Second World War.

## 13. Non-Permanent Members

Non-permanent members serve for two years.

They are elected in a way that represents different regions of the world.

A country cannot be immediately re-elected after completing a two-year term.

Non-permanent members do not have veto power.

## 14. Veto Power

The veto is a negative vote by a permanent member of the Security Council.

Even if other members support a resolution, a veto by one permanent member can block it.

The veto gives permanent members special power.

It is one of the most controversial features of the UN.

## 15. Why The Veto Exists

The veto was created after the Second World War to keep the great powers inside the UN system.

The idea was that if major powers were denied special status, they might ignore the UN.

The veto therefore reflects power realities more than democratic equality.

## 16. Criticism Of The Veto

Critics argue that the veto:

- Violates sovereign equality.
- Gives too much power to five states.
- Blocks urgent action.
- Makes the UN less democratic.
- Allows powerful states to protect themselves and allies.

Many want the veto abolished or modified.

## 17. Why Veto Reform Is Difficult

Reforming the veto is difficult because permanent members are unlikely to surrender their own privilege.

Any major reform needs their consent.

Without their support, the reform cannot move forward.

This is why Security Council reform is slow.

## 18. UN Secretary-General

The Secretary-General is the most visible public figure of the UN and serves as its representative head.

The Secretary-General coordinates, speaks, mediates, and represents the organisation.

Antonio Guterres became the ninth Secretary-General on 1 January 2017.

## 19. UN Agencies And Bodies

The UN system includes many agencies and bodies that deal with specialised issues.

Examples include:

- WHO for health.
- UNDP for development.
- UNHCR for refugees.
- UNICEF for children.
- UNESCO for education, science, and culture.
- Human rights bodies.

This shows that the UN is not only about war and peace.

## 20. Reform After The Cold War

The UN was designed around the realities of 1945.

After the Cold War, the world changed:

- The Soviet Union collapsed.
- The US became the strongest power.
- Russia and the US became more cooperative.
- China emerged rapidly.
- India grew in importance.
- Asian economies expanded.
- Many new countries joined the UN.
- New challenges emerged.

This created demand for UN reform.

## 21. Two Kinds Of UN Reform

UN reform has two broad meanings.

First, reform of structures and processes.

This includes the Security Council, membership, veto, budget, administration, and decision-making.

Second, reform of the UN's jurisdiction and priorities.

This includes what issues the UN should handle: peacekeeping, development, human rights, terrorism, climate, epidemics, and humanitarian crises.

## 22. Security Council Reform

The biggest reform debate concerns the Security Council.

Many argue that the Council no longer represents contemporary world politics.

The Council reflects 1945 power realities more than twenty-first century realities.

## 23. Complaints About The Security Council

Major complaints include:

- It does not represent contemporary political realities.
- It is dominated by a few powers.
- It reflects Western values and interests too often.
- It lacks equitable representation.
- Asia, Africa, and Latin America need more voice.
- Developing countries are underrepresented.

These complaints led to reform proposals.

## 24. Criteria Proposed For New Members

Suggested criteria for new permanent or non-permanent members include:

- Major economic power.
- Major military power.
- Major contribution to UN budget.
- Large population.
- Respect for democracy.
- Respect for human rights.
- Representation of world diversity.
- Regional balance.
- Cultural diversity.

Each criterion has supporters and critics.

## 25. Why Criteria Are Controversial

Criteria are controversial because countries gain or lose depending on which criterion is chosen.

Questions arise:

- How large must an economy be?
- How powerful must a military be?
- Should population be an asset or burden?
- Should budget contribution buy influence?
- Should democracy be required?
- Which region should get new seats?
- Should developing countries get priority?

Reform is therefore politically difficult.

## 26. Representation Debate

Representation can be understood in many ways:

- Geographical representation.
- Regional representation.
- Developmental representation.
- Cultural representation.
- Population representation.
- Economic representation.

No single method satisfies all states.

## 27. Reform Of Jurisdiction

At the 2005 summit, leaders supported steps to make the UN more relevant.

These included:

- Peacebuilding Commission.
- Responsibility to protect people from atrocities.
- Human Rights Council.
- Millennium Development Goals.
- Condemnation of terrorism.
- Democracy Fund.
- Winding up the Trusteeship Council.

These steps expanded the UN's relevance beyond traditional inter-state war.

## 28. Peacebuilding Commission

The Peacebuilding Commission is meant to help countries recover from conflict.

It can support:

- Reconstruction.
- Institution building.
- Reconciliation.
- Development.
- Preventing relapse into conflict.

The difficulty is deciding where, when, and how to intervene.

## 29. Responsibility To Protect

The idea of responsibility to protect says the international community has a responsibility when governments fail to protect citizens from mass atrocities.

This raises difficult questions:

- Who decides intervention?
- What counts as atrocity?
- Can powerful states misuse the idea?
- How can sovereignty and human rights be balanced?

This is one of the most contested parts of reform.

## 30. Human Rights Council

The Human Rights Council became operational in 2006.

It addresses human rights situations around the world.

Human rights debates are difficult because states disagree on definitions, priorities, and external scrutiny.

## 31. Terrorism And The UN

The UN condemns terrorism in all forms.

However, states often disagree about:

- Definition of terrorism.
- Distinction between terrorism and liberation struggle.
- Use of force.
- State responsibility.
- Human rights during counter-terrorism.

Agreement on broad condemnation is easier than agreement on details.

## 32. Development Goals

The Millennium Development Goals and later Sustainable Development Goals show the UN's development role.

Development goals matter because poverty, health, education, gender inequality, and environment affect peace and security.

The challenge is implementation.

## 33. India And UN Reform

India supports restructuring of the UN.

India believes a strengthened and revitalised UN is necessary in a changing world.

India supports a greater UN role in:

- Development.
- Cooperation.
- Peace.
- Security.
- Fair representation.

For India, development is central to international peace.

## 34. India's Concern About Security Council Composition

India argues that the Security Council has remained largely static while UN membership has expanded greatly.

This harms the Council's representative character.

Most UN General Assembly members are now developing countries, but developing countries do not have corresponding influence in the Security Council.

## 35. India's Argument For Expansion

India supports expansion of both permanent and non-permanent members.

It argues that a broader Council will:

- Enjoy greater legitimacy.
- Gain wider political support.
- Reflect current realities.
- Include more developing countries.
- Improve effectiveness.

## 36. India's Claim To Permanent Membership

India seeks permanent membership in a restructured Security Council.

Its arguments include:

- It is the world's largest democracy.
- It has a huge population.
- It has participated in UN initiatives.
- It has a long peacekeeping record.
- It has a growing economy.
- It makes regular financial contributions.
- It is an important voice of the developing world.

Permanent membership also has symbolic importance.

## 37. Objections To India's Claim

Some countries oppose or question India's claim.

Reasons include:

- Pakistan's opposition due to troubled relations.
- Concerns about India's nuclear weapons.
- Fear that including India means including other aspirants.
- Demand for African and South American representation.
- Questions about regional balance.

These objections make reform difficult.

## 38. UN In A Unipolar World

After the Soviet collapse, the US became the dominant power.

Many hoped UN reform could help balance US dominance.

However, US power cannot be easily checked because of its military, economic, financial, and diplomatic strength.

## 39. Why The UN Cannot Fully Balance The US

The UN cannot fully balance the US because:

- The US is militarily powerful.
- The US is economically influential.
- It is a major UN budget contributor.
- The UN is located in US territory.
- Many US nationals work in UN bureaucracy.
- The US has veto power.
- The US can influence selection of the Secretary-General.

The UN cannot act like an equal counter-power.

## 40. Why The UN Still Matters In A Unipolar World

The UN still matters because it:

- Brings over 190 states into dialogue.
- Gives smaller states a platform.
- Allows criticism of powerful states.
- Shapes compromises.
- Provides legitimacy.
- Coordinates development and humanitarian work.
- Creates space for global debate.

Even powerful states often find the UN useful.

## 41. The UN As An Imperfect Necessity

The UN has failed to prevent all wars and suffering.

Yet without the UN, the world would lack a central forum for global negotiation.

The UN is imperfect, but its absence would make cooperation harder.

## 42. IMF

The International Monetary Fund oversees international financial institutions and regulations.

It has many member countries, but voting power is unequal.

Major economies hold larger shares of votes.

The IMF influences economic policy, especially during financial crises.

## 43. World Bank

The World Bank was created in 1944.

It focuses on developing countries.

It works in areas such as:

- Education.
- Health.
- Agriculture.
- Rural development.
- Environmental protection.
- Infrastructure.
- Governance.

It gives loans and grants but is often criticised for attaching strict conditions.

## 44. WTO

The World Trade Organisation was established in 1995 as successor to GATT.

It sets rules for global trade.

Decisions are formally taken unanimously, but major economic powers often exercise great influence.

Developing countries often complain about unequal procedures and pressure from big powers.

## 45. IAEA

The International Atomic Energy Agency was established in 1957.

It promotes peaceful use of nuclear energy and tries to prevent military use.

IAEA teams inspect nuclear facilities to check whether civilian reactors are being used for military purposes.

## 46. Amnesty International

Amnesty International is an NGO that campaigns for protection of human rights.

It prepares reports on human rights conditions.

Governments may dislike its reports because they often criticise official misconduct.

Its work is important for research and advocacy.

## 47. Human Rights Watch

Human Rights Watch is an international NGO involved in research and advocacy on human rights.

It draws attention to human rights abuses.

It has supported campaigns on issues such as landmines, child soldiers, and the International Criminal Court.

## 48. International Organisations Versus NGOs

International organisations such as the UN, IMF, World Bank, WTO, and IAEA are created by states.

NGOs such as Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch are not created by states.

Both matter in world politics, but their authority and methods differ.

## 50. Common Student Confusions

The UN is not a world government.

The General Assembly is more representative, but the Security Council is more powerful on peace and security.

The veto is held only by the five permanent Security Council members.

Reforming the UN does not only mean expanding the Security Council; it can also mean changing priorities and jurisdiction.

India's claim to permanent membership is strong but politically contested.

NGOs are not the same as intergovernmental organisations.

## 51. Model Answer: Need For International Organisations

International organisations are needed because countries face conflicts and common problems that cannot be solved alone. They provide forums for dialogue, rules for cooperation, information, mechanisms for monitoring agreements, and procedures for resolving disputes peacefully. They are useful in matters of war and peace, development, health, climate, trade, finance, refugees, and human rights. They are not perfect or all-powerful, but they reduce the chances that disagreement must lead to war.

## 52. Model Answer: Veto Power

The veto is the negative vote of a permanent member of the UN Security Council. The five permanent members are the US, Russia, UK, France, and China. If any one of them votes negatively on a substantive resolution, the decision can be blocked even if other members support it. The veto was created to keep the great powers invested in the UN system, but it is criticised as undemocratic and unequal.

## 53. Model Answer: India's Case For Permanent Membership

India supports restructuring of the Security Council because the present structure does not reflect contemporary realities. India argues that it is the world's largest democracy, has a huge population, is economically significant, has participated actively in UN initiatives, has contributed to peacekeeping, and represents developing countries. India also argues that more developing countries should be included for legitimacy. However, Pakistan and some other countries oppose India's claim due to regional conflict, nuclear concerns, and competing demands for representation.

## 54. Model Answer: UN In A Unipolar World

The UN cannot fully balance US dominance because the US has unmatched military and economic power, contributes heavily to the UN budget, hosts UN headquarters, has veto power, and influences UN bureaucracy and leadership selection. However, the UN still matters because it provides a platform for dialogue, criticism, compromise, legitimacy, and cooperation among more than 190 countries. It cannot control the US completely, but it can shape debate and policy.
