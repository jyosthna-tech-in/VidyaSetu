# Chapter 7: Globalisation

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains globalisation as worldwide interconnectedness created by flows of ideas, capital, commodities, people, technology, services, and culture. It also examines political, economic, and cultural consequences of globalisation, its impact on India, and resistance to it.

The main point is that globalisation is not one simple thing. It is multi-dimensional, uneven, contested, and context-dependent. It creates opportunities for some groups while increasing insecurity for others. It can weaken some state functions while strengthening state capacity through technology. It can create cultural uniformity and also new cultural combinations.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Define globalisation as worldwide interconnectedness.
- Identify flows of ideas, capital, commodities, and people.
- Explain why contemporary globalisation is different in scale and speed.
- Describe the role of technology in globalisation.
- Explain political consequences for state capacity and sovereignty.
- Explain economic consequences for trade, capital, labour, and inequality.
- Explain cultural homogenisation and heterogenisation.
- Describe India's experience of globalisation before and after 1991.
- Explain different kinds of resistance to globalisation.
- Describe left and right critiques of globalisation.
- Explain examples of anti-globalisation movements in India and the world.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Globalisation | Worldwide interconnectedness created by cross-border flows | Core concept |
| Flow | Movement of ideas, capital, commodities, services, or people | Building block of globalisation |
| Capital | Money or investment that can move across borders | Important economic flow |
| Commodity | Good traded across borders | Shows market integration |
| Services | Work provided across distance, such as call centre services | Modern form of globalisation |
| State capacity | Ability of a government to perform functions and make decisions | Debated under globalisation |
| Welfare state | State that provides social and economic welfare functions | Often reduced under market-led reform |
| Minimalist state | State focused mainly on law, order, and security | Often associated with globalisation |
| MNC | Multinational company operating in more than one country | Major actor in economic globalisation |
| Cultural homogenisation | Growth of a uniform culture under global influence | Major cultural concern |
| Cultural heterogenisation | Creation of new differences and mixtures through cultural exchange | Counter-effect of globalisation |
| Westernisation | Spread of Western cultural forms | Often confused with globalisation |
| Protectionism | Policy of protecting domestic producers from foreign competition | India's early post-independence approach |
| Liberalisation | Reducing state controls and opening markets | Part of India's 1991 reforms |
| Deregulation | Reducing government restrictions on economic activity | Economic globalisation policy |
| WSF | World Social Forum | Platform for movements opposing neo-liberal globalisation |

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Globalisation is worldwide interconnectedness through flows of ideas, capital, commodities, services, and people, producing political, economic, and cultural changes that are uneven, contested, and deeply connected to India's recent history.

## 2. Why Globalisation Is Hard To Define

Globalisation is used in many contexts.

People use it to talk about:

- Trade.
- Call centres.
- Foreign goods.
- Migration.
- Western clothes.
- MNCs.
- Technology.
- Culture.
- Jobs.
- Social change.
- Protests.

Because the word is used widely, it must be defined carefully.

## 3. Janardhan's Call Centre Example

Janardhan works in a call centre.

He serves clients thousands of miles away.

He works at night because it is daytime for overseas customers.

He changes accent and name at work.

This example shows globalisation of services.

## 4. Ramdhari's Purchases

Ramdhari buys a cycle manufactured in China and a Barbie doll originally from the United States but sold in India.

This shows movement of commodities across borders.

Goods made in one country are consumed in another country.

## 5. Sarika's Opportunity

Sarika gets an opportunity for a career that women in her family did not earlier imagine.

Some relatives oppose her choice, but she moves forward.

This example shows globalisation's connection with values, social change, and new opportunities.

## 6. Globalisation Can Be Negative Too

The chapter also gives examples of negative effects:

- Farmers affected by expensive MNC seeds.
- Retail shopkeepers fearing foreign retail chains.
- Cultural conflict over Western clothing.
- Accusations of copying foreign films.
- Anxiety about livelihood loss.

Globalisation creates gains and losses.

## 7. Globalisation Is Not Only Economic

Globalisation includes economic flows, but it is not purely economic.

It also includes:

- Political changes.
- Cultural influence.
- Social values.
- Technology.
- Migration.
- Media.
- Services.

Reducing globalisation to markets alone gives an incomplete picture.

## 8. Globalisation Is Not Always From Rich To Poor

Influence does not always flow from rich countries to poor countries.

Ideas, people, culture, services, and companies can move in many directions.

For example:

- Indian professionals provide services abroad.
- Indian firms buy foreign companies.
- Indian culture travels through film, food, yoga, and diaspora networks.

Globalisation is uneven, but it is not one-way.

## 9. Globalisation As Flows

Globalisation fundamentally deals with flows.

Important flows include:

- Ideas.
- Capital.
- Commodities.
- People.
- Services.
- Technology.
- Culture.

These flows create worldwide interconnectedness.

## 10. Worldwide Interconnectedness

Worldwide interconnectedness means events, decisions, products, and ideas in one part of the world affect people in other parts.

Examples:

- Disease spreads across borders.
- Financial crises affect distant economies.
- Foreign investment changes local jobs.
- Global media shapes fashion.
- Wars can affect oil prices.

Globalisation makes distance less protective.

## 11. Multi-Dimensional Nature

Globalisation is multi-dimensional.

It has:

- Political dimensions.
- Economic dimensions.
- Cultural dimensions.
- Social dimensions.
- Technological dimensions.

Each dimension should be studied separately before drawing conclusions.

## 12. Uneven Impact

Globalisation does not affect everyone equally.

It varies by:

- Country.
- Class.
- Region.
- Occupation.
- Gender.
- Education.
- Access to technology.
- Market position.

Some gain opportunities; others lose security.

## 13. Causes Of Globalisation

Globalisation is not caused by a single factor.

Important causes include:

- Historical movement of goods and people.
- Technological change.
- Faster communication.
- Transport improvements.
- Economic liberalisation.
- Global capitalism.
- Recognition of interdependence.

Technology is especially important.

## 14. Globalisation Has A History

Flows of people, ideas, capital, and commodities existed long before the present.

Trade, migration, empires, religions, and cultural exchange connected societies for centuries.

Contemporary globalisation is different not because flows are new, but because their scale and speed are much greater.

## 15. Scale And Speed

Contemporary globalisation is marked by:

- Faster communication.
- Faster financial flows.
- Faster trade.
- Larger markets.
- Wider reach of corporations.
- Quicker spread of images and ideas.

Scale and speed make present globalisation distinctive.

## 16. Role Of Technology

Technology is a critical cause of globalisation.

Important technologies include:

- Telegraph.
- Telephone.
- Microchip.
- Internet.
- Computers.
- Satellite communication.
- Transport systems.

Technology makes distant interaction faster and cheaper.

## 17. Printing And Nationalism Analogy

Printing helped create nationalism by spreading shared languages, news, and ideas.

Similarly, modern communication technology changes how people think about personal and collective life.

Technology shapes political imagination.

## 18. Recognition Of Interconnection

Globalisation requires not only technology but awareness.

People must recognise that events elsewhere can affect them.

Examples:

- Bird flu.
- Tsunami.
- Global financial changes.
- Oil prices.
- Climate events.

Awareness of interdependence is part of globalisation.

## 19. Political Consequences

Globalisation affects politics by changing state capacity and sovereignty.

The key debate is whether states are becoming weaker or stronger.

The answer is mixed.

Some state functions weaken, while some capacities become stronger.

## 20. Erosion Of State Capacity

Globalisation can reduce the ability of governments to do what they once did.

The old welfare state often gives way to a more minimalist state.

Governments may withdraw from many social and economic welfare functions.

Markets become more important in setting priorities.

## 21. Welfare State To Minimalist State

The welfare state tries to provide social and economic well-being through public services.

The minimalist state focuses mainly on:

- Law and order.
- Security.
- Core administration.

Under market-led globalisation, governments may reduce welfare responsibilities.

## 22. Role Of MNCs In Political Change

Multinational companies operate across countries.

Their investment and production decisions can influence national policy.

Governments may feel pressure to:

- Lower regulations.
- Attract investment.
- Reduce taxes.
- Modify labour rules.
- Open markets.

This can reduce independent policy space.

## 23. State Still Matters

Globalisation does not make the state irrelevant.

States still:

- Maintain law and order.
- Defend territory.
- Control borders.
- Make laws.
- Regulate citizens.
- Conduct foreign policy.
- Provide identity and citizenship.

The state remains the main political community.

## 24. Globalisation Can Strengthen State Capacity

New technology can help states collect information and govern more effectively.

States can use:

- Digital databases.
- Surveillance systems.
- Communication networks.
- Tax systems.
- Identification systems.
- Border technologies.

In this sense, globalisation may make states more powerful, not less.

## 25. Economic Consequences

Economic globalisation is one of the most debated dimensions.

It involves greater economic flows across countries.

These flows include:

- Trade in commodities.
- Movement of capital.
- Movement of services.
- Movement of ideas.
- Some movement of labour.

## 26. International Economic Institutions

Institutions such as IMF and WTO influence economic policy.

However, economic globalisation is broader than these institutions.

It also involves:

- MNCs.
- Investors.
- Workers.
- Consumers.
- National governments.
- Technology firms.
- Trade networks.

## 27. Trade In Commodities

Globalisation has increased trade in goods.

Many countries have reduced restrictions on imports.

Consumers may get more choices and lower prices.

Domestic producers may face competition.

## 28. Movement Of Capital

Restrictions on movement of capital have also reduced.

Investors from rich countries can invest in other economies where returns may be higher.

This can bring funds and technology but may also create dependence and vulnerability.

## 29. Flow Of Ideas

Ideas move across borders through:

- Internet.
- Education.
- Media.
- Travel.
- Social movements.
- Scientific exchange.
- Professional networks.

Ideas can support democracy, markets, feminism, environmentalism, religious movements, or consumer culture.

## 30. Movement Of People Is Unequal

Globalisation has not allowed people to move as freely as goods or capital.

Developed countries often use visa rules to protect domestic jobs.

This means:

- Capital moves easily.
- Goods move widely.
- People face borders and restrictions.

This is one of the inequalities of globalisation.

## 31. Uneven Economic Outcomes

The same policies do not produce the same results everywhere.

Outcomes depend on:

- Domestic institutions.
- Education.
- Infrastructure.
- Social inequality.
- State capacity.
- Global market position.
- Bargaining power.

Context matters.

## 32. Critics Of Economic Globalisation

Critics worry that economic globalisation:

- Benefits a small elite.
- Hurts poor workers.
- Weakens welfare policies.
- Reduces state protection.
- Increases inequality.
- Exposes small producers to global competition.
- Creates economic dependency.

Some describe it as re-colonisation.

## 33. Social Safety Nets

Some supporters of reform accept that globalisation can harm vulnerable people.

They propose social safety nets such as:

- Welfare support.
- Education access.
- Health care.
- Unemployment protection.
- Skill training.
- Public support for affected groups.

Critics argue that safety nets may be insufficient.

## 34. Advocates Of Economic Globalisation

Advocates argue that globalisation:

- Increases trade.
- Promotes growth.
- Improves efficiency.
- Allows economies to specialise.
- Attracts investment.
- Generates jobs.
- Encourages innovation.
- Deepens interdependence.

They often argue that resisting globalisation is unrealistic.

## 35. Moderate View

A moderate view says globalisation is a challenge that must be handled intelligently.

It should not be accepted uncritically.

It should not be rejected completely.

States need policies that maximise benefits and protect vulnerable groups.

## 36. Cultural Consequences

Globalisation affects everyday life.

It shapes:

- Food.
- Clothes.
- Music.
- Films.
- Language.
- Values.
- Identity.
- Consumption.
- Entertainment.

Cultural globalisation is visible in daily habits.

## 37. Cultural Homogenisation

Cultural homogenisation means the rise of a uniform culture.

Critics argue that this often means Western culture, especially American culture, spreading across the world.

They call this the soft power of US hegemony.

## 38. McDonaldisation

McDonaldisation refers to the spread of fast-food style, standardised, Americanised consumer culture.

Critics argue it reduces cultural diversity and makes the world resemble the dominant power's lifestyle.

This is seen as dangerous for the cultural heritage of humanity.

## 39. Global Culture Or Western Imposition?

The chapter cautions that what is called global culture may actually be the culture of politically and economically powerful societies.

This raises questions:

- Is global culture equal exchange?
- Or is it imposition by dominant societies?
- Are local cultures losing choices?

These are political questions.

## 40. Cultures Are Not Static

Cultures constantly change.

They absorb outside influences, reject some, and transform others.

External influence is not always harmful.

It may:

- Reduce choices.
- Add choices.
- Create new combinations.
- Revive local identity.

## 41. Burger And Masala Dosa Example

A burger does not necessarily replace a masala dosa.

It may simply become an additional food choice.

This shows that outside influence can expand choices rather than destroy existing culture.

## 42. Jeans And Khadi Kurta Example

Blue jeans can be worn with a khadi kurta.

This creates a new cultural combination.

The combination may even travel back to other countries.

This shows that cultural flow can create hybrid forms.

## 43. Cultural Heterogenisation

Cultural heterogenisation means globalisation can make cultures more different and distinctive by creating new mixtures.

It challenges the idea that globalisation only produces sameness.

Culture exchange is rarely one-way.

## 44. India And Early Global Flows

India has been connected to global flows for centuries.

Flows of people, capital, commodities, and ideas existed in pre-colonial and colonial times.

Globalisation did not begin in India in 1991, though 1991 marked a major policy shift.

## 45. Colonial Period

Under British colonialism, India became:

- Exporter of primary goods.
- Exporter of raw materials.
- Consumer of finished British goods.

This experience shaped India's later suspicion of open foreign economic control.

## 46. Post-Independence Protectionism

After independence, India chose protectionism.

The goals were:

- Build domestic industry.
- Avoid dependence on foreign goods.
- Protect Indian producers.
- Promote self-reliance.
- Learn to produce goods domestically.

This policy was influenced by colonial experience.

## 47. Problems Of Protectionism

Protectionism also created problems.

India experienced:

- Sluggish economic growth.
- Limited competition.
- Slow improvement in some sectors.
- Inadequate attention to health.
- Inadequate attention to housing.
- Inadequate attention to primary education.

Self-reliance had achievements but also limits.

## 48. 1991 Economic Reforms

In 1991, India faced a financial crisis and wanted higher growth.

India began economic reforms involving:

- Liberalisation.
- Deregulation.
- Opening trade.
- Encouraging foreign investment.
- Reducing controls.

This marked deeper integration with globalisation.

## 49. Test Of Globalisation For India

High growth alone is not enough.

The real test is whether benefits are shared so that everyone is better off.

Questions include:

- Who gets jobs?
- Who loses livelihood?
- Are poor people protected?
- Does growth improve health and education?
- Are regional inequalities reduced?
- Are small producers supported?

## 50. India Impacting Globalisation

India is not only affected by globalisation; it also affects globalisation.

Examples include:

- IT services.
- Call centres.
- Skilled professionals.
- Indian companies investing abroad.
- Indian films and culture.
- Diaspora networks.
- Yoga and cultural exports.
- Indian market size.

India is both receiver and contributor.

## 51. Resistance To Globalisation

Globalisation has produced resistance across the world.

Resistance comes from different ideological positions.

Critics do not always reject all global connections.

Many oppose a specific neo-liberal programme of globalisation that they see as unjust.

## 52. Left Critique

Critics on the left argue that contemporary globalisation represents global capitalism.

They say it:

- Makes the rich richer.
- Makes the poor poorer.
- Weakens the state.
- Reduces protection for workers.
- Helps corporations.
- Increases inequality.

They emphasise social justice.

## 53. Right Critique

Critics from the political right worry about:

- Weakening of the state.
- Loss of self-reliance.
- Foreign economic dependence.
- Harm to traditional culture.
- Westernisation.
- Loss of values.

They may support protectionism in selected areas.

## 54. Anti-Globalisation Movements Are Global

Anti-globalisation movements often use global networks.

They connect activists across countries.

This creates an irony: resistance to globalisation may itself use global communication, global meetings, and global solidarity.

## 55. Seattle Protests

In 1999, protests occurred at the WTO Ministerial Meeting in Seattle.

Protesters argued that powerful states shaped global trade rules unfairly.

They claimed developing countries' interests were not sufficiently considered.

This became a major moment in global resistance.

## 56. World Social Forum

The World Social Forum brings together groups opposed to neo-liberal globalisation.

Participants include:

- Human rights activists.
- Environmentalists.
- Labour groups.
- Youth groups.
- Women activists.
- Social movements.

The first meeting was held in Porto Alegre, Brazil in 2001.

The fourth was held in Mumbai in 2004.

The raw chapter notes a later meeting in Nepal in February 2024.

## 57. Resistance In India

Resistance to globalisation in India has come from different quarters.

It includes:

- Left protests against economic liberalisation.
- Indian Social Forum.
- Trade unions.
- Farmers' organisations.
- Protests against MNC entry.
- Opposition to patenting of plants such as Neem.
- Cultural criticism from the political right.

## 58. Trade Unions And Farmers

Trade unions and farmer groups have protested against:

- Entry of multinationals.
- Threats to workers.
- Threats to small producers.
- Market insecurity.
- Foreign control over seeds or agriculture.

These protests focus on livelihood and economic justice.

## 59. Patenting Neem

The patenting of Neem by American and European firms generated opposition.

Critics argued that traditional knowledge and biological resources should not be controlled by foreign corporations.

This connects globalisation with intellectual property and indigenous knowledge.

## 60. Cultural Resistance In India

Resistance from the political right has focused on cultural influence.

Examples include objections to:

- Foreign TV channels.
- Cable networks.
- Valentine's Day.
- Western clothing.
- Westernisation of youth culture.

This form of resistance worries about identity and values.

## 61. Globalisation And Choice

Globalisation can expand choice by bringing new goods, ideas, and opportunities.

It can also reduce choice when local producers disappear, cultural options narrow, or people are forced into market dependence.

The key question is not whether globalisation creates change, but who controls the change and who benefits.

## 63. Common Student Confusions

Globalisation is not purely economic.

Globalisation did not begin only in 1991.

Globalisation is not exactly the same as Westernisation.

Globalisation does not affect all people equally.

The state does not disappear under globalisation.

Cultural influence can produce both sameness and new mixtures.

Resistance to globalisation may itself be globally networked.

## 64. Model Answer: Concept Of Globalisation

Globalisation means worldwide interconnectedness created through flows of ideas, capital, commodities, services, and people across borders. These flows are not entirely new, but contemporary globalisation is distinctive because of its scale and speed. Technology, especially communication and transport technology, has made cross-border interaction faster. Globalisation is multi-dimensional, involving political, economic, and cultural consequences, and its impact is uneven across societies and groups.

## 65. Model Answer: Political Consequences

Globalisation affects state capacity in mixed ways. On one hand, it can reduce state capacity because the welfare state gives way to a minimalist state, markets set more priorities, and MNCs influence government decisions. On the other hand, the state remains central in law, order, security, citizenship, and foreign policy. New technologies may even strengthen states by improving their ability to collect information and govern citizens.

## 66. Model Answer: Economic Consequences

Economic globalisation increases flows of commodities, capital, ideas, and services across borders. Trade barriers and restrictions on capital movement have reduced, allowing investors to move money globally and consumers to access foreign goods. However, movement of people remains restricted by visa policies. Critics argue that economic globalisation benefits elites and harms workers and poor communities. Supporters argue that it increases growth, efficiency, investment, and interdependence.

## 67. Model Answer: Cultural Consequences

Globalisation affects food, clothes, media, language, values, and identity. Critics fear cultural homogenisation, where Western or American culture spreads and weakens local cultures. This is sometimes called McDonaldisation. However, cultures are not static. Globalisation can also produce cultural heterogenisation, where new combinations emerge, such as jeans with a khadi kurta. Cultural exchange is rarely only one-way.

## 68. Model Answer: India And Globalisation

India experienced global flows for centuries, but colonialism made India an exporter of raw materials and consumer of finished goods. After independence, India adopted protectionism to build self-reliance and domestic industry. This produced some gains but also slow growth and inadequate attention to health, housing, and education. In 1991, India began economic reforms after a financial crisis, opening trade and foreign investment. The key question is whether globalisation's benefits are shared widely.
