# Chapter 6: Environment And Natural Resources

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains why environment and natural resources are part of world politics. Environmental problems are not only scientific or geographical questions; they are political because they involve power, responsibility, development, inequality, ownership, survival, and conflict over resources.

Study this chapter through five linked ideas: environmental concerns, global commons, common but differentiated responsibilities, resource geopolitics, and indigenous peoples' rights. The chapter repeatedly asks who caused environmental damage, who pays the price, who benefits from resources, and who should take responsibility.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Environment and natural resources are part of world politics because ecological damage, resource control, development needs, North-South inequality, and indigenous rights all involve power, responsibility, and global cooperation.

## 2. Why Environment Is Political

Environmental issues appear natural, but they are deeply political.

They involve questions such as:

- Who caused the damage?
- Who pays the price?
- Who benefits from resource use?
- Who decides rules?
- Who must reduce consumption?
- Who gets access to forests, water, oil, minerals, and land?
- Who is displaced by development?

These are questions of power and justice.

## 3. Environmental Problems In The World

Major environmental concerns include:

- Limited expansion of cultivable land.
- Declining fertility of agricultural land.
- Overgrazed grasslands.
- Over-harvested fisheries.
- Water depletion.
- Water pollution.
- Lack of safe drinking water.
- Lack of sanitation.
- Deforestation.
- Displacement of people.
- Loss of biodiversity.
- Ozone depletion.
- Coastal pollution.
- Climate change.

These problems affect food, health, livelihoods, and survival.

## 4. Agriculture And Land

Cultivable land is barely expanding.

At the same time, much existing agricultural land is losing fertility.

This creates pressure because the world population needs food, but the land base is under stress.

Land degradation therefore becomes a political issue because states must decide how to balance food production, conservation, and development.

## 5. Water

Water bodies are facing depletion and pollution.

This restricts food production and threatens health.

Lack of safe water and sanitation kills millions, especially children.

Water is political because it crosses borders, supports agriculture, sustains cities, and can generate conflict.

## 6. Forests And Biodiversity

Natural forests help:

- Stabilise climate.
- Moderate water supplies.
- Protect biodiversity.
- Support local communities.
- Preserve ecological balance.

Deforestation destroys habitats and displaces people.

Loss of biodiversity reduces the planet's capacity to sustain varied life.

## 7. Ozone And Coastal Pollution

Ozone depletion in the stratosphere threatens ecosystems and human health.

Coastal pollution is increasing because of land-based activities and intensive settlement along coastlines.

Open seas may appear cleaner, but coastal waters face severe pressure.

## 8. Why One Government Cannot Solve These Problems

Many environmental problems cross borders.

Examples:

- Climate change.
- Ozone depletion.
- Ocean pollution.
- Biodiversity loss.
- River water disputes.
- Air pollution.

One government acting alone cannot solve problems created by global patterns of production and consumption.

## 9. Rise Of Environmentalism

Environmental concerns became more politically visible from the 1960s onward.

Economic growth had produced ecological damage.

People and organisations began asking whether development based only on industrial expansion could continue indefinitely.

Environmentalism became a global political movement.

## 10. Club Of Rome And Limits To Growth

The Club of Rome published Limits to Growth in 1972.

The book warned about depletion of Earth's resources in the context of rapid population growth.

It helped dramatise the idea that growth without ecological limits could produce crisis.

## 11. UNEP And Global Conferences

International agencies such as the United Nations Environment Programme encouraged studies, conferences, and coordinated responses.

Environmental issues moved from local concern to global agenda.

This process helped make the environment a serious issue in world politics.

## 12. Brundtland Report

The 1987 Brundtland Report, Our Common Future, warned that traditional patterns of economic growth were not sustainable.

It especially considered the development needs of the South.

The report helped popularise sustainable development.

## 13. Earth Summit

The United Nations Conference on Environment and Development was held in Rio de Janeiro in June 1992.

It is known as the Earth Summit.

It was attended by:

- 170 states.
- Thousands of NGOs.
- Many multinational corporations.

It firmly placed environmental issues at the centre of global politics.

## 14. North-South Difference At Rio

The global North and global South had different priorities.

Northern developed countries focused on:

- Ozone depletion.
- Global warming.
- Conservation.

Southern developing countries focused on:

- Development.
- Poverty reduction.
- Environmental management.
- Fairness in responsibility.

This difference shaped environmental negotiations.

## 15. Outcomes Of The Rio Summit

The Rio Summit produced:

- Climate change convention.
- Biodiversity convention.
- Forestry-related principles.
- Agenda 21.
- Broad acceptance of sustainable development.

However, many disagreements remained unresolved.

## 16. Sustainable Development

Sustainable development means combining economic growth with ecological responsibility.

It recognises that development is necessary, especially for poor countries, but it must not destroy the ecological basis of life.

The difficulty is deciding exactly how to balance growth and conservation.

## 17. Criticism Of Agenda 21

Some critics argue that Agenda 21 favoured economic growth more than ecological conservation.

This criticism reflects a recurring tension:

- Should development come first?
- Should ecological limits come first?
- Who decides the balance?

## 18. Global Commons

Global commons are areas or resources outside the sovereign jurisdiction of any one state.

They require common governance by the international community.

Examples include:

- Earth's atmosphere.
- Antarctica.
- Ocean floor.
- Outer space.

## 19. Why Global Commons Are Difficult To Protect

Protecting global commons is difficult because:

- No single state owns them.
- Benefits are shared unevenly.
- Costs are contested.
- Scientific evidence may be uncertain.
- Time frames are long.
- Rich and poor countries have different capabilities.
- Technology is unequally distributed.

Common resources require common rules, but agreement is difficult.

## 20. Antarctica

Antarctica is a major example of global commons.

It contains:

- A vast continental region.
- A huge share of terrestrial ice.
- A large share of planetary fresh water.
- A fragile ecosystem.
- Important climate information in ice cores.

Many states treat it as a shared region rather than territory of one state.

## 21. Antarctic Claims And Cooperation

Some countries have made territorial claims in Antarctica.

Others argue it belongs to global commons.

Despite these differences, rules have been created for its protection.

Activities have mostly been limited to scientific research, fishing, and tourism.

## 22. Environmental Agreements For Commons

Important agreements include:

- Antarctic Treaty, 1959.
- Montreal Protocol, 1987.
- Antarctic Environmental Protocol, 1991.

These show that international cooperation is possible, though difficult.

## 23. Ozone Hole

The discovery of the ozone hole over Antarctica in the mid-1980s showed both danger and opportunity.

It revealed a serious global environmental problem.

It also showed that coordinated international action can respond to scientific warnings.

## 24. Outer Space

Outer space is also a global commons.

Its management reflects North-South inequalities because access to space depends on advanced technology.

The benefits of space activity are not equally shared by all countries.

## 25. Common But Differentiated Responsibilities

Common but differentiated responsibilities means all states share responsibility for protecting the environment, but they do not share equal blame or equal capacity.

Developed countries caused much historical ecological damage through industrialisation.

Therefore, they should bear greater responsibility.

## 26. Developed Countries' View

Developed countries often want all states to accept equal responsibility for environmental protection.

They focus on current ecological danger and global cooperation.

Their view stresses common responsibility.

## 27. Developing Countries' View

Developing countries argue that developed countries caused most environmental damage through past industrialisation.

They also argue that they still need economic development.

Therefore, restrictions should not be applied equally to countries at different stages of development.

## 28. Rio Declaration

The Rio Declaration accepted the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities.

It stated that states should cooperate to protect Earth's ecosystem, but developed countries must acknowledge their greater responsibility because of their historical pressures on the environment and their greater financial and technological resources.

## 29. UNFCCC

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change also uses the principle of equity and differentiated responsibilities.

It recognises that developed countries contributed the largest historical and current share of greenhouse gas emissions.

It also recognises that per capita emissions in developing countries are still relatively low.

## 30. Kyoto Protocol

The Kyoto Protocol was agreed in 1997 in Kyoto, Japan.

It set targets for industrialised countries to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Gases such as carbon dioxide, methane, and hydrofluorocarbons contribute to global warming.

China, India, and other developing countries were exempted from the requirements because of their lower historical contribution.

## 31. India's Position On Kyoto

India signed and ratified the Kyoto Protocol in August 2002.

India argues that developed countries should carry major responsibility for reducing emissions because they produced most historical emissions.

India also points to low per capita emissions in developing countries.

## 32. Historical Responsibility

India's position relies on historical responsibility.

This means countries that created most greenhouse gas emissions during industrialisation should carry greater responsibility for correcting the damage.

This does not mean developing countries can ignore environmental protection; it means responsibility should be fair.

## 33. India's Development Priority

India argues that economic and social development are overriding priorities for developing countries.

Environmental rules should not prevent poor countries from reducing poverty and improving living standards.

The challenge is to pursue development in cleaner and more sustainable ways.

## 34. Indian Environmental Measures

India has participated in global environmental efforts and adopted domestic measures such as:

- Cleaner fuel policies.
- Energy conservation efforts.
- Laws and programmes for more efficient energy use.
- Participation in climate negotiations.

The broader position is that responsibility must be fair and development-sensitive.

## 35. Common Property Resources

Common property resources are resources shared by a group.

Members have both rights and duties.

They must decide:

- Who can use the resource.
- How much can be used.
- How it will be maintained.
- How misuse will be controlled.

Examples include village forests, grazing lands, ponds, and community spaces.

## 36. Common Property In India

Many village communities in India developed rules for managing common resources.

These rules were based on mutual understanding and long practice.

Such systems helped prevent overuse when community norms were strong.

## 37. Decline Of Common Property

Common property resources have declined due to:

- Privatisation.
- Agricultural intensification.
- Population growth.
- Ecosystem degradation.
- State control.
- Commercial extraction.

Poor people often suffer most because they depend heavily on common resources.

## 38. Sacred Groves

Sacred groves are forest patches traditionally protected for religious, cultural, or ancestral reasons.

They are found in many parts of India, especially along forest belts.

They represent community-based conservation.

## 39. Ecological Importance Of Sacred Groves

Sacred groves can preserve:

- Biodiversity.
- Local ecological functions.
- Cultural memory.
- Community identity.
- Traditional conservation practices.

They show that conservation can arise from cultural values, not only modern state law.

## 40. Problems In Sacred Grove Management

Problems arise when legal ownership and actual community control are separated.

The state may own forest land, but communities may have traditional management practices.

New forest policies and settlement expansion may weaken the identity and protection of sacred groves.

## 41. Environmental Movements

Environmental movements challenge harmful patterns of development and resource use.

They may focus on:

- Forest protection.
- Species protection.
- Anti-mining struggles.
- Anti-dam struggles.
- River protection.
- Indigenous rights.
- Community control.

## 42. Forest Movements In North And South

Forest movements differ between the developed North and developing South.

In the North, forests are often imagined as wilderness without human habitation.

In the South, forests are often lived spaces where communities depend on land, water, and forest resources.

This difference shapes environmental politics.

## 43. Wilderness And Biodiversity

Some movements focus on protecting individual species and wilderness habitats.

Examples include campaigns for:

- Eagles in the Philippines.
- Bengal tigers in India.
- Elephants in Africa.
- Forests in Brazil and Indonesia.

Many campaigns are now framed as biodiversity struggles.

## 44. Mining And Environmental Resistance

The minerals industry is powerful and often controversial.

Criticism focuses on:

- Extraction of earth.
- Chemical use.
- Water pollution.
- Land pollution.
- Clearing native vegetation.
- Displacement of communities.
- Corporate control.

Communities and organisations in many countries resist destructive mining.

## 45. Anti-Dam Movements

Mega-dams often generate opposition because they affect rivers, valleys, forests, and communities.

Anti-dam movements increasingly become pro-river movements.

They demand sustainable and equitable management of river systems.

## 46. Franklin River And Narmada Bachao Andolan

The campaign to save the Franklin River in Australia was an early anti-dam and wilderness movement in the North.

In India, Narmada Bachao Andolan became one of the best-known anti-dam movements.

Indian environmental movements have often emphasised non-violence.

## 47. Resource Geopolitics

Resource geopolitics asks: who gets what, when, where, and how?

Control over resources has shaped:

- Colonial expansion.
- Trade routes.
- War.
- Maritime power.
- Industrial strategy.
- Strategic alliances.

Resources are not only economic goods; they are power assets.

## 48. Timber And Sea Power

European powers historically needed timber for naval power.

Control over timber supply became important because sea power required ships.

This shows how a natural resource can become a strategic resource.

## 49. Oil

Oil was central to the global economy in the twentieth century.

It supports:

- Transport.
- Industry.
- Military mobility.
- Petrochemical products.
- Global trade.

Control over oil creates wealth and conflict.

## 50. Oil In West Asia And Central Asia

West Asia, especially the Gulf, is extremely important for oil.

The raw chapter notes that the Gulf region accounts for about 30 percent of global oil production but around 64 percent of known reserves.

Saudi Arabia has a very large share of reserves, and Iraq also has major reserves.

Major consumers such as the US, Europe, Japan, India, and China are geographically distant from the region.

## 51. Oil And Power

Oil politics includes:

- Control of production.
- Control of transport routes.
- Alliances with producing states.
- Military presence near oil regions.
- Support to friendly governments.
- Competition among consuming powers.

The history of petroleum is also a history of war, influence, and strategic bargaining.

## 52. Water

Water is another crucial resource in global politics.

Scarcity and unequal access can create disputes.

Countries sharing rivers may disagree over:

- Dams.
- Pollution.
- Irrigation.
- Diversion.
- Water quality.
- Downstream flow.

## 53. Upstream And Downstream Tensions

An upstream state controls water before it reaches a downstream state.

Downstream states may object when upstream states:

- Build dams.
- Divert water.
- Pollute rivers.
- Use excessive irrigation.

This can create severe political tension.

## 54. Water Conflict Examples

The chapter refers to conflicts and threats involving:

- Israel, Syria, and Jordan over the Jordan and Yarmuk rivers.
- Turkey, Syria, and Iraq over dams on the Euphrates.

These examples show why water can become a security issue.

## 55. Indigenous Peoples

Indigenous peoples are descendants of communities that inhabited a territory before people of different culture or origin arrived and dominated them.

They continue to live with distinct social, economic, cultural, and ecological practices.

Their struggles connect environment, resources, identity, and politics.

## 56. Indigenous Peoples In World Politics

Indigenous peoples across the world call for admission to the world community as equals.

They want recognition of:

- Identity.
- Land rights.
- Cultural survival.
- Political voice.
- Economic resource base.
- Traditional knowledge.

## 57. Land And Survival

For indigenous communities, land is not only property.

It is linked to:

- Livelihood.
- Culture.
- Spiritual life.
- Identity.
- Community survival.
- Ecological knowledge.

Loss of land threatens both economic and cultural existence.

## 58. Indigenous Peoples In India

In India, the term indigenous people is usually applied to Scheduled Tribes.

They constitute nearly eight percent of India's population.

Many depend on cultivation, forests, and land-based livelihoods.

They have constitutional protection in representation but have not received equal benefits of development.

## 59. Development And Displacement

Scheduled Tribes have paid a heavy cost for development.

They are among the largest groups displaced by development works since independence.

This shows how development can create environmental and social injustice when communities lose land and livelihoods.

## 60. World Council Of Indigenous Peoples

International contact among indigenous leaders grew in the 1970s.

The World Council of Indigenous Peoples was formed in 1975.

It later became one of the indigenous NGOs to receive consultative status at the UN.

This helped indigenous concerns enter world politics.

## 61. Environment, Development, And Justice

The chapter's deepest theme is justice.

Environmental politics asks how to balance:

- Development and conservation.
- Growth and equity.
- National sovereignty and global responsibility.
- State control and community rights.
- Industrial needs and indigenous survival.

These are political questions, not only ecological questions.

## 63. Common Student Confusions

Environment is not outside politics. It is about power and responsibility.

Global commons are not owned by one state.

Common but differentiated responsibilities does not mean only developed countries must act; it means responsibility should be fair.

Sustainable development is not simply economic growth with a green label.

Common property does not mean nobody cares for the resource; it means a community shares rights and duties.

Indigenous rights are not only cultural; they are tied to land, livelihood, and survival.

## 64. Model Answer: Why Environmental Issues Are Political

Environmental issues are political because they involve questions of power, responsibility, ownership, and justice. Environmental damage raises questions about who caused the damage, who suffers, who should pay, and who should change behaviour. Many problems such as climate change, ocean pollution, and biodiversity loss cross borders and cannot be solved by one government alone. Environmental decisions also affect development, poverty, indigenous peoples, and control over natural resources.

## 65. Model Answer: Common But Differentiated Responsibilities

Common but differentiated responsibilities means all states share responsibility for protecting the environment, but they do not have equal responsibility. Developed countries caused much of the historical ecological damage through industrialisation and have greater financial and technological capacity. Developing countries still need development and have lower per capita emissions. Therefore, environmental rules should be based on equity, historical responsibility, and respective capabilities.

## 66. Model Answer: Global Commons

Global commons are areas outside the sovereign jurisdiction of any one state and shared by the international community. Examples include Earth's atmosphere, Antarctica, ocean floor, and outer space. They require common governance because no single state owns them. Protection is difficult because states disagree over responsibility, technology, development needs, and distribution of benefits. Treaties such as the Antarctic Treaty and Montreal Protocol show that cooperation is possible.

## 67. Model Answer: Resource Geopolitics

Resource geopolitics is the politics of who gets control over strategic resources, when, where, and how. Historically, resources such as timber, minerals, oil, and water have shaped trade, war, colonial expansion, and strategic alliances. Oil is especially important because the global economy depends heavily on it, and major reserves are concentrated in regions such as the Gulf. Water can also cause conflict when upstream and downstream states disagree over dams, diversion, pollution, or irrigation.
