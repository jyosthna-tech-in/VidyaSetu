# Unit 2A: Meaning of Financial Market

A financial market is a market for creation and exchange of financial assets. It transfers money from households and institutions with surplus funds to businesses and governments needing funds.

It includes banks, financial institutions, investors, companies, brokers, stock exchanges, and regulators.

## Simple Flow

Savers -> financial market -> investors or fund users

The market helps money move from idle savings into productive investment.

# Unit 2B: Functions of Financial Market

## 1. Mobilisation of Savings

Financial markets collect savings from individuals and institutions.

## 2. Channelising Funds to Productive Uses

Funds move toward businesses and sectors that can use them productively.

## 3. Price Discovery

Demand and supply help determine price of financial assets.

## 4. Providing Liquidity

Investors can sell financial assets and convert them into cash.

## 5. Reducing Transaction Cost

Organised markets provide information, systems, and intermediaries that reduce search and transaction cost.

## Function Table

| Function | Learner Meaning |
|---|---|
| Mobilisation | Collects savings. |
| Allocation | Sends funds to productive uses. |
| Price discovery | Determines value through demand and supply. |
| Liquidity | Allows buying and selling. |
| Cost reduction | Makes transactions easier and cheaper. |

# Unit 2C: Money Market

Money market deals in short-term funds with maturity up to one year.

## Features

1. Short maturity.
2. High liquidity.
3. Low risk compared with long-term securities.
4. Large transaction size.
5. Participants include banks, companies, government, and financial institutions.
6. Usually no single physical location.

## Uses

1. Meet temporary cash shortages.
2. Invest surplus funds for short period.
3. Finance working capital.
4. Maintain liquidity in financial system.

# Unit 2D: Money Market Instruments

## Treasury Bill

Treasury bills are short-term instruments issued by government. They are issued at discount and redeemed at face value.

Example: A bill with face value Rs. 1,00,000 may be issued at Rs. 98,000 and redeemed at Rs. 1,00,000.

## Commercial Paper

Commercial paper is an unsecured short-term promissory note issued by financially strong companies.

Used for:

1. Short-term borrowing.
2. Working capital.
3. Bridge finance.

## Call Money

Call money is very short-term finance used mainly by banks. It may be repayable on demand or within a very short period.

## Certificate of Deposit

Certificate of deposit is a negotiable short-term instrument issued by banks or financial institutions against deposited funds.

## Commercial Bill

Commercial bill is a bill of exchange used to finance credit sales. Seller can discount it with bank to get funds earlier.

## Instrument Comparison

| Instrument | Issuer or Main User | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| Treasury bill | Government | Short-term borrowing at discount. |
| Commercial paper | Creditworthy company | Unsecured short-term borrowing. |
| Call money | Banks | Very short-term inter-bank funds. |
| Certificate of deposit | Bank or financial institution | Negotiable time deposit. |
| Commercial bill | Business firms and banks | Trade credit instrument. |

# Unit 2E: Capital Market

Capital market deals in medium-term and long-term funds.

It includes:

1. Primary market.
2. Secondary market.

## Features

1. Deals in long-term securities.
2. Higher risk than money market.
3. Supports capital formation.
4. Includes individuals and institutions.
5. Provides funds for growth and expansion.

# Unit 2F: Money Market vs Capital Market

| Basis | Money Market | Capital Market |
|---|---|---|
| Maturity | Up to one year. | More than one year. |
| Instruments | Treasury bill, commercial paper, call money. | Shares, debentures, bonds. |
| Risk | Generally lower. | Generally higher. |
| Liquidity | Very high. | Depends on security and market. |
| Purpose | Short-term funds and liquidity. | Long-term investment and growth. |
| Participants | Banks, government, companies, institutions. | Companies, investors, institutions, stock exchanges. |

# Unit 2G: Primary Market

Primary market is the market where securities are issued for the first time by companies or institutions.

It is also called new issue market.

## Purpose

1. Raise fresh capital.
2. Finance new business.
3. Expand operations.
4. Modernise assets.
5. Repay debt.

## Methods of Floatation

| Method | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Offer through prospectus | Public issue inviting investors to subscribe. |
| Offer for sale | Intermediary buys securities and offers them to public. |
| Private placement | Securities sold privately to selected investors. |
| Rights issue | Existing shareholders get right to buy new shares. |
| e-IPOs | Public issue through electronic system. |

# Unit 2H: Secondary Market

Secondary market is the market where existing securities are bought and sold.

It does not directly provide new funds to company, but it provides liquidity to investors.

## Why Secondary Market Matters

Investors are more willing to buy new securities in primary market if they know they can sell them later in secondary market.

# Unit 2I: Primary vs Secondary Market

| Basis | Primary Market | Secondary Market |
|---|---|---|
| Securities | New securities. | Existing securities. |
| Funds flow | From investors to issuing company. | From buyer to seller. |
| Price | Often fixed or book-built at issue. | Determined by demand and supply. |
| Location | No fixed geographical location. | Organised through stock exchanges and trading platforms. |
| Frequency | Security issued once. | Security can be traded many times. |
| Role | Capital formation. | Liquidity and price discovery. |

# Unit 2J: Stock Exchange

Stock exchange is an organised market where existing securities are bought and sold.

## Functions

1. Provides liquidity and marketability.
2. Helps price discovery.
3. Ensures safety of transactions.
4. Contributes to economic growth.
5. Spreads equity culture.
6. Provides scope for speculation within regulation.
7. Acts as economic barometer.

## Economic Barometer Meaning

Stock market movements often reflect investor expectations about business and economy. It is not perfect, but it provides signals.

# Unit 2K: Trading and Settlement - Conceptual Flow

## Basic Steps

1. Investor opens trading account and demat account.
2. Investor places buy or sell order through broker or platform.
3. Order is matched on exchange system.
4. Trade is confirmed.
5. Clearing corporation calculates obligations.
6. Funds and securities are exchanged through settlement system.
7. Securities are credited or debited in demat account.

## Why Electronic Trading Matters

1. Faster execution.
2. More transparency.
3. Lower paperwork.
4. Wider access.
5. Better record keeping.

# Unit 2L: Dematerialisation and Depository System

Dematerialisation means converting physical securities into electronic form.

## Depository

A depository holds securities electronically. Investors hold accounts with depository participants.

## Benefits

1. No risk of loss of physical certificates.
2. Faster transfer.
3. Lower paperwork.
4. Reduced forgery risk.
5. Easier portfolio holding.

# Unit 2M: SEBI

SEBI regulates the securities market and protects investor interests.

## Objectives

1. Protect investors.
2. Regulate securities market.
3. Promote market development.
4. Prevent unfair trade practices.

## Protective Functions

1. Prohibits fraudulent and unfair trade practices.
2. Checks insider trading.
3. Educates investors.
4. Promotes fair practices.

## Regulatory Functions

1. Registers brokers and intermediaries.
2. Regulates stock exchanges.
3. Regulates mutual funds and other market participants as applicable.
4. Calls for information and conducts inspection.
5. Frames rules and guidelines.

## Development Functions

1. Promotes training of intermediaries.
2. Encourages fair and efficient markets.
3. Supports modernisation of trading systems.
4. Promotes investor education.

# Unit 2O: Case Answer Framework

1. Identify the market, instrument, or institution.
2. Define it.
3. Explain the case clue.
4. State function or importance.
5. Add investor or company impact.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A large company needs funds for only three months to meet seasonal inventory demand. It has high credit rating and wants an unsecured instrument cheaper than bank borrowing.

Answer:

1. Commercial paper may be suitable.
2. Commercial paper is an unsecured short-term promissory note issued by creditworthy companies.
3. The need is short-term and the company has strong credit standing.
4. It can help meet working capital needs.
5. However, it is suitable mainly for financially sound companies because it is unsecured.

# Unit 2P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Primary market and stock exchange are same | Stock exchange is part of secondary market. |
| Secondary market gives fresh funds to company | It gives liquidity to investors; company usually does not receive funds from resale. |
| Money market means small retail money | It usually involves large short-term funds. |
| Capital market deals only with shares | It also includes debentures and bonds. |
| Treasury bills pay regular interest | They are issued at discount and redeemed at face value. |
| Demat account is same as bank account | Demat holds securities; bank account holds money. |
| SEBI only punishes | It also protects, regulates, and develops the market. |

## Final Revision

Financial markets move funds from savers to users. Money market handles short-term liquidity; capital market handles long-term finance. Primary market creates new securities, secondary market gives liquidity, stock exchange organises trading, and SEBI protects and regulates the system.
