# Unit 1A: Meaning of Business Finance

Business finance means money required for business activities. Every business needs finance to start, operate, modernise, expand, diversify, and survive.

Finance is needed for:

1. Buying land and building.
2. Buying machinery and equipment.
3. Purchasing raw material.
4. Paying wages and salaries.
5. Paying rent, electricity, and insurance.
6. Holding inventory.
7. Giving credit to customers.
8. Advertising and distribution.
9. Research and development.
10. Expansion and modernisation.

## Why Finance Must Be Managed

Finance has cost. Borrowed funds carry interest. Equity funds expect return. Idle money reduces profitability. Shortage of money can stop operations. Therefore, finance must be planned and controlled carefully.

# Unit 1B: Meaning of Financial Management

Financial management is concerned with optimal procurement and effective utilisation of funds.

It aims to:

1. Arrange funds at minimum cost.
2. Keep financial risk under control.
3. Invest funds in profitable proposals.
4. Maintain liquidity.
5. Avoid idle finance.
6. Increase value of business.

## Simple Interpretation

Financial management answers three big questions:

1. Where should money be invested?
2. From where should money be raised?
3. How should profit be distributed or retained?

# Unit 1C: Role of Financial Management

Financial management affects almost every item in financial statements.

## Examples

| Financial Decision | Balance Sheet or Profit Effect |
|---|---|
| Buy machinery | Increases fixed assets. |
| Raise loan | Increases debt and interest cost. |
| Issue shares | Increases share capital. |
| Hold more inventory | Increases current assets and storage cost. |
| Give more credit | Increases trade receivables and sales risk. |
| Retain profit | Increases reserves and internal funds. |

## Wider Role

1. Determines size and composition of assets.
2. Determines debt-equity mix.
3. Affects profitability.
4. Affects liquidity.
5. Affects market value.
6. Affects ability to grow.

# Unit 1D: Objective of Financial Management

The primary objective is wealth maximisation, which means maximising market value of shareholders' investment.

## Why Not Only Profit Maximisation?

Profit maximisation has limitations:

1. It may ignore timing of returns.
2. It may ignore risk.
3. It may encourage short-term thinking.
4. It may be unclear which profit is meant.
5. It may ignore social and ethical responsibilities.

## Wealth Maximisation Is Better Because

1. It considers present value of future returns.
2. It considers risk.
3. It focuses on long-term value.
4. It reflects market judgement.
5. It aligns with shareholders' interests.

## Wealth Maximisation Does Not Mean

It does not mean unethical profit seeking. Long-term wealth depends on trust, legal compliance, customer satisfaction, employee welfare, and responsible conduct.

# Unit 1E: Investment Decision

Investment decision concerns how funds are invested in assets.

## Types

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Long-term investment decision | Investment in fixed assets and long-term proposals. |
| Short-term investment decision | Investment in current assets such as inventory, cash, and receivables. |

Long-term investment decisions are also called capital budgeting decisions.

## Importance of Investment Decision

1. Involves large amount of funds.
2. Affects long-term profitability.
3. Often difficult to reverse.
4. Involves risk and uncertainty.
5. Determines future growth.

## Factors Affecting Investment Decision

| Factor | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Cash flows | Expected inflows and outflows must be estimated. |
| Rate of return | Proposal should generate adequate return. |
| Risk | Higher uncertainty needs careful evaluation. |
| Investment criteria | Business may use payback, net present value, or other methods. |

# Unit 1F: Financing Decision

Financing decision concerns choice of funds from different channels such as equity, preference shares, debentures, loans, and retained earnings.

## Main Question

How much should be raised from owners' funds and how much from borrowed funds?

## Debt vs Equity

| Basis | Debt | Equity |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | Interest is fixed. | Dividend is not fixed. |
| Risk | Increases financial risk. | Lower financial risk. |
| Control | Does not dilute ownership control. | May dilute control if new shares issued. |
| Tax | Interest may provide tax benefit. | Dividend is appropriation of profit. |
| Obligation | Repayment required. | No fixed repayment during company life. |

## Factors Affecting Financing Decision

1. Cost of funds.
2. Risk.
3. Floatation cost.
4. Cash flow position.
5. Level of fixed operating cost.
6. Control considerations.
7. State of capital market.
8. Tax rate.
9. Return on investment.

# Unit 1G: Dividend Decision

Dividend decision concerns how much profit should be distributed to shareholders and how much should be retained in business.

## If More Profit Is Distributed

Shareholders get immediate return, but internal funds for growth reduce.

## If More Profit Is Retained

Business gets funds for expansion, but shareholders receive less current dividend.

## Factors Affecting Dividend Decision

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Earnings | Higher stable earnings support higher dividend. |
| Stability of dividend | Companies often avoid frequent dividend changes. |
| Growth opportunities | More growth needs more retained earnings. |
| Cash flow position | Dividend requires cash, not just accounting profit. |
| Shareholder preference | Some shareholders prefer regular dividend. |
| Taxation | Tax rules may influence preference. |
| Legal constraints | Companies must follow law. |
| Access to capital market | Firms with easy access may pay higher dividend. |
| Contractual constraints | Loan agreements may restrict dividend. |

# Unit 1H: Financial Planning

Financial planning means estimating fund requirements and deciding how funds will be arranged and used.

## Objectives

1. Ensure availability of funds when needed.
2. Avoid shortage of finance.
3. Avoid excess idle funds.
4. Coordinate investment and financing decisions.
5. Support growth and stability.

## Importance

| Importance | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Smooth operations | Funds are available for daily work. |
| Expansion support | Growth plans get financial backing. |
| Better fund mix | Debt and equity can be balanced. |
| Avoid shocks | Cash shortages are reduced. |
| Control | Actual financial performance can be compared with plan. |

# Unit 1I: Capital Structure

Capital structure means the mix of debt and equity in long-term financing.

## Example

If a company has Rs. 60 lakh equity and Rs. 40 lakh debt, its capital structure uses 60 per cent equity and 40 per cent debt.

## Debt Can Increase Return

If return on investment is higher than cost of debt, using debt can increase return to equity shareholders. This is trading on equity.

## Debt Also Increases Risk

Debt requires fixed interest and repayment. If profits fall, debt burden can create financial risk.

# Unit 1J: Factors Affecting Capital Structure

| Factor | Effect on Debt Use |
|---|---|
| Cash flow position | Strong cash flow supports more debt. |
| Interest coverage ratio | Higher coverage supports more debt. |
| Debt service coverage | Higher ability to repay supports more debt. |
| Return on investment | If ROI exceeds debt cost, debt may be attractive. |
| Cost of debt | Lower debt cost encourages debt. |
| Tax rate | Higher tax rate may favour debt due to interest tax benefit. |
| Cost of equity | High equity cost may make debt attractive. |
| Floatation cost | Lower issue cost affects choice. |
| Risk | High business risk reduces debt capacity. |
| Control | Debt avoids dilution of control. |
| Flexibility | Firm should keep ability to raise funds later. |
| Capital market conditions | Market sentiment affects issue of shares or debt. |

# Unit 1K: Fixed Capital

Fixed capital refers to funds invested in long-term assets such as land, building, plant, machinery, equipment, and technology.

## Factors Affecting Fixed Capital Requirement

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Nature of business | Manufacturing needs more fixed capital than trading. |
| Scale of operations | Large-scale firms need more fixed assets. |
| Choice of technique | Capital-intensive technique needs more fixed capital. |
| Technology upgradation | Rapid technology changes need more investment. |
| Growth prospects | Expansion plans increase fixed capital need. |
| Diversification | New products or markets may need new assets. |
| Financing alternatives | Leasing may reduce immediate fixed capital need. |
| Collaboration | Sharing facilities may reduce requirement. |

# Unit 1L: Working Capital

Working capital refers to funds required for current assets such as cash, inventory, bills receivable, and trade receivables.

## Gross and Net Working Capital

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gross working capital | Total current assets. |
| Net working capital | Current assets minus current liabilities. |

## Operating Cycle

Operating cycle is the time between purchase of raw material and collection of cash from customers.

Longer operating cycle means higher working capital requirement.

# Unit 1M: Factors Affecting Working Capital Requirement

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Nature of business | Trading firms often need less fixed capital but may need inventory funds; service firms may need less inventory. |
| Scale of operations | Larger operations need more working capital. |
| Business cycle | Boom increases working capital need; recession may reduce it. |
| Seasonal factors | Peak season needs more inventory and cash. |
| Production cycle | Longer production cycle needs more working capital. |
| Credit allowed | Longer credit to customers increases receivables. |
| Credit availed | More supplier credit reduces working capital need. |
| Operating efficiency | Better efficiency reduces blocked funds. |
| Raw material availability | Irregular supply may require higher inventory. |
| Growth prospects | Growing firms need more working capital. |
| Level of competition | More competition may force higher credit and inventory. |
| Inflation | Rising prices increase working capital need. |

# Unit 1O: Case Answer Framework

1. Identify the financial concept.
2. Define it.
3. Link the case clue to the concept.
4. Explain financial effect: cost, risk, return, liquidity, control, or growth.
5. Suggest a balanced decision if asked.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A company has stable cash flows and high tax rate. It wants to expand but existing owners do not want control dilution. The finance manager suggests raising part of the funds through debentures.

Answer:

1. This is a financing and capital structure decision.
2. Debentures provide debt capital and do not dilute ownership control.
3. Stable cash flows support payment of fixed interest.
4. High tax rate makes debt attractive because interest reduces taxable profit.
5. However, excessive debt increases financial risk, so the debt-equity mix must remain balanced.

# Unit 1P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Profit maximisation and wealth maximisation are identical | Wealth maximisation considers risk, timing, and long-term value. |
| Dividend decision means paying all profit | It balances dividend and retained earnings. |
| Debt is always cheaper and therefore always better | Debt increases financial risk. |
| High working capital is always good | Excess working capital may mean idle funds. |
| Fixed capital and working capital are same | Fixed capital is long-term asset investment; working capital supports daily operations. |
| Accounting profit is enough to pay dividend | Dividend needs cash availability and legal compliance. |

## Final Revision

Financial management is about value, not just money handling. Every finance decision should be judged by return, risk, cost, liquidity, control, and long-term growth. The chapter becomes easy when you identify whether a case concerns investment, financing, dividend, fixed capital, or working capital.
