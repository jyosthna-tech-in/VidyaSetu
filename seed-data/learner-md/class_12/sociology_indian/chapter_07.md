# Chapter 7: Suggestions for research work
### NCERT Class 12 - Sociology Indian Society

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter introduces learners to small practical research exercises in sociology. Reading about society is important, but doing research teaches how evidence is collected, how questions are framed, and how method affects findings. The chapter explains surveys, interviews, observation, archival work, and combinations of methods. It also offers themes such as public transport, communication media, household appliances, public space, changing aspirations, and the biography of a commodity.

The chapter's main lesson is that good sociological research begins with a clear question and uses a suitable method.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain why practical research is important in sociology;
- distinguish topic, research question, and method;
- identify suitable methods for different questions;
- explain survey, interview, observation, and archival methods;
- compare strengths and limitations of each method;
- design small research questions from broad themes;
- combine methods where useful;
- recognise practical problems in fieldwork;
- organise findings ethically and systematically;
- write a short learner-level sociological research report.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Research question | Specific question that guides evidence collection. |
| Method | Systematic way of collecting evidence. |
| Survey | Method using fixed questions with many respondents. |
| Questionnaire | Written set of questions used in survey. |
| Interview | In-person conversation for collecting detailed responses. |
| Structured interview | Interview using fixed sequence of questions. |
| Unstructured interview | Flexible interview guided by broad themes. |
| Observation | Systematic watching and recording of behaviour or setting. |
| Archival method | Use of records, newspapers, old documents, photographs, or other stored material. |
| Respondent | Person who answers survey or interview questions. |
| Field notes | Written record of observations during research. |
| Sampling | Choosing people, places, or documents to study. |

## Research Design Map

| Step | Question to ask |
|---|---|
| Choose theme | What broad area interests me? |
| Narrow the topic | Which specific aspect can I study? |
| Frame question | What exactly do I want to find out? |
| Choose method | What evidence will answer this question? |

# Unit 7A: Why Do Practical Research?

Sociology is not only about reading theory. It is also about observing, asking, comparing, recording, and interpreting social life.

Practical research helps learners understand:

- how difficult it is to ask a clear question;
- why evidence matters;
- how people's answers vary;
- how observation can challenge assumptions;
- why method shapes findings;
- why social reality is complex.

## Reading vs Doing

Reading gives concepts.

Research gives experience in applying concepts to real situations.

# Unit 7B: Topic, Question, and Method

A topic is broad.

A research question is specific.

A method is the way evidence is collected.

## Example

Broad topic:

Public transport.

Specific question:

"How do women students experience safety while travelling by bus after sunset?"

Possible method:

- interviews with women students;
- observation at bus stops;
- short survey;
- review of local newspaper reports.

## Why Specific Questions Matter

If the question is too broad, research becomes confused. A clear question helps decide whom to ask, what to observe, and what evidence to collect.

# Unit 7C: Survey Method

A survey asks the same fixed questions to a relatively large number of people.

It may use:

- printed questionnaire;
- oral questions read by investigator;
- online form;
- classroom-based form.

## Strengths

Surveys can:

- cover many respondents;
- produce comparable answers;
- show patterns;
- be counted and tabulated;
- represent a larger group if sampling is careful.

## Limitations

Surveys may fail because:

- questions may be misunderstood;
- fixed answers may not capture complexity;
- respondents may skip questions;
- people may give socially acceptable answers;
- follow-up questions are difficult;
- a survey captures one moment, not long-term change.

# Unit 7D: Interview Method

An interview is a conversation used to collect information.

Interviews usually involve fewer people than surveys but allow deeper answers.

## Structured Interview

The researcher asks fixed questions in a fixed order.

## Unstructured Interview

The researcher has broad themes, but questions can change during conversation.

## Strengths

Interviews allow:

- detailed stories;
- clarification;
- follow-up questions;
- emotional and personal meaning;
- understanding of reasons and experiences.

## Limitations

Interviews:

- take time;
- cover fewer people;
- depend on trust;
- may be influenced by interviewer;
- are harder to compare numerically.

# Unit 7E: Observation Method

Observation means systematically watching and recording what happens.

It is useful when:

- actual behaviour matters;
- people may not describe behaviour accurately;
- public settings are being studied;
- space, interaction, or routine is important.

## What to Observe

A researcher may record:

- who enters a space;
- who avoids it;
- how people use it;
- who controls it;
- how long people stay;
- gender or class patterns;
- conflicts;
- rules;
- silence and absence.

## Important Lesson

What does not happen may be as important as what happens. If women never use a public park after dark, that absence is sociologically meaningful.

# Unit 7F: Archival Method

Archival method uses existing records.

Materials may include:

- newspapers;
- magazines;
- old photographs;
- school records;
- advertisements;
- government reports;
- letters;
- maps;
- pamphlets;
- television archives;
- digital archives.

## Strengths

Archival work helps study change over time.

## Limitations

Records may be incomplete, biased, inaccessible, or produced from the viewpoint of powerful groups.

# Unit 7G: Combining Methods

Many research questions are best answered by combining methods.

Example:

Topic: changing role of mobile phones.

Possible combination:

- survey students about phone use;
- observe phone use in public places;
- interview parents and teachers;
- compare advertisements over time.

## Benefit

Using more than one method allows comparison between what people say and what people do.

# Unit 7H: Practical Problems in Research

Small research exercises face practical difficulties.

Possible problems:

- not getting permission;
- people refusing to answer;
- incomplete questionnaires;
- respondents giving joking answers;
- lack of time;
- cost of printing forms;
- difficulty reaching respondents;
- unclear questions;
- unsafe field settings;
- language barriers;
- researcher bias.

## How to Handle Problems

Good researchers:

- keep questions simple;
- respect respondents;
- avoid forcing answers;
- record limitations;
- protect privacy;
- do not invent data;
- ask for help when needed;
- revise tools after pilot testing.

# Unit 7I: Theme 1 - Public Transport

Possible research questions:

- Who depends most on public transport?
- How do students experience bus travel?
- How do women and men experience safety differently?
- What problems do elderly or disabled people face?
- How has a local transport system changed over time?
- How do class differences shape transport choices?

Suitable methods:

- observation at bus stop or railway station;
- interviews with commuters;
- survey of students;
- newspaper archive on transport changes.

## Concepts to Use

- class;
- gender;
- public space;
- urbanisation;
- access;
- safety;
- mobility.

# Unit 7J: Theme 2 - Communication Media

Communication media include:

- newspapers;
- radio;
- television;
- films;
- mobile phones;
- internet;
- social media;
- email;
- messaging apps.

Possible research questions:

- How much television do different age groups watch?
- Do students still read newspapers?
- How has mobile phone use changed family communication?
- How do people use phones in public spaces?

Suitable methods:

# Unit 7K: Theme 3 - Household Appliances and Domestic Work

Household appliances include:

- stove;
- pressure cooker;
- mixer;
- grinder;
- refrigerator;
- washing machine;
- iron;
- fan;
- air conditioner;
- water purifier.

Possible research questions:

- Who uses household appliances most?
- Have appliances reduced domestic labour or changed its form?
- Do men and women use appliances equally?
- How do appliances change time spent on housework?
- How do older family members remember domestic work before these appliances?

Suitable methods:

- interviews with family members across generations;
- observation of household routines;
- survey of households;
- advertisement analysis.

## Concepts to Use

- gender division of labour;
- unpaid work;
- technology;
- class;
- family;
- domestic sphere.

# Unit 7L: Theme 4 - Use of Public Space

Public spaces include:

- parks;
- footpaths;
- roadsides;
- empty plots;
- markets;
- bus stops;
- railway stations;
- spaces near public offices;
- playgrounds.

Possible research questions:

- Who uses a public park and at what time?
- Are women, elderly people, children, vendors, or homeless people equally welcome?
- How do class differences shape use of public space?
- How do conflicts arise over roadside vending?
- What happens to an open plot over time?

Suitable methods:

- observation;
- mapping;
- interviews;
- photographs if ethically allowed;
- archival records.

# Unit 7M: Theme 5 - Changing Aspirations

Aspirations are goals people have for education, work, marriage, lifestyle, mobility, and future.

Possible research questions:

- How do career goals differ across Class 5, Class 8, and Class 11 students?
- Do boys and girls express different aspirations?
- How do parents' occupations affect children's goals?
- How do rural and urban students imagine success?
- How do adults remember their childhood aspirations?

Suitable methods:

- survey;
- interviews;
- essays;
- group comparison;
- memory-based interviews with adults.

# Unit 7N: Theme 6 - Biography of a Commodity

A commodity biography traces the social life of an object.

Possible objects:

- mobile phone;
- television;
- sofa;
- motorcycle;
- school bag;
- pressure cooker;
- wedding dress;
- packaged food item.

Questions:

- Where was it produced?
- Who made it?
- How did it reach the shop?
- Who bought it?
- What status or emotion is attached to it?
- Who uses it?
- What happens when it becomes old?

## Sociological Value

This theme connects market, labour, family, consumption, status, and globalisation.

# Unit 7O: Ethics in Small Research

Even small learner research should be ethical.

Basic rules:

- do not force anyone to answer;
- explain why you are asking questions;
- avoid humiliating questions;
- protect names if topic is sensitive;
- do not record secretly;
- do not invent responses;
- avoid unsafe fieldwork;
- be respectful across class, gender, caste, age, and occupation.

# Unit 7P: Writing the Report

A short report can follow this structure:

1. Title.
2. Research question.
3. Reason for choosing topic.
4. Method used.
5. Sample or setting.
6. Main findings.
7. Examples or patterns.
8. Difficulties faced.
9. Limitations.
10. Conclusion.

## Good Conclusion

A good conclusion does not exaggerate. It states what was found and what remains uncertain.

# Unit 7Q: Worked Research Designs

## Example 1: Public Space

Topic:

Use of a neighbourhood park.

Research question:

"Who uses the park between 5 pm and 8 pm, and how does use differ by age and gender?"

Method:

Observation for five evenings, plus short interviews with users.

Possible findings:

Men may dominate after dark; children may use the park earlier; elderly people may prefer morning; women may avoid certain areas.

## Example 2: Domestic Work

Topic:

Household appliances and gender.

Research question:

"Has the washing machine changed who does laundry in middle-class households?"

Method:

Interviews with three households and observation of weekly laundry routine.

Concepts:

Gender division of labour, technology, unpaid work.

## Example 3: Media Use

Topic:

Mobile phones among students.

Research question:

"How do students use mobile phones for study and entertainment?"

Method:

Anonymous survey plus two group interviews.

Possible caution:

Students may under-report entertainment use if they think teachers will judge them.

# Unit 7R: Answer-Writing Framework

## To compare survey and interview

1. Define survey.
2. Define interview.
3. Survey covers more people with fixed questions.
4. Interview covers fewer people in more depth.
5. State one strength and one limitation of each.

## To explain observation

1. Define observation.
2. Explain systematic watching and recording.
3. Mention that absence can be meaningful.
4. Give example of public space.
5. State limitation such as observer bias.

## To design a research question

1. Start with broad topic.
2. Narrow it by group, place, time, or issue.
3. Make it answerable with available method.
4. Avoid vague words.
5. Decide what evidence is needed.

# Unit 7S: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Starting with a huge topic. | Narrow it into a specific research question. |
| Choosing method before question. | Let the question guide the method. |
| Asking leading questions. | Use neutral wording. |
| Treating one interview as proof for everyone. | State limits of sample. |
| Ignoring non-response. | Record incomplete or missing responses as a limitation. |
| Observing casually without notes. | Keep systematic field notes. |
| Embarrassing respondents. | Respect privacy and dignity. |
| Making up data to complete report. | Report real findings, even if limited. |

## Final Revision

- Sociological research begins with a clear question.
- Topic is broad; research question is specific.
- Method must fit the question.
- Surveys cover more people but are less flexible.
- Interviews give depth but cover fewer people.
- Observation records behaviour and use of space.
- Archival work helps study change over time.
- Combining methods can improve understanding.
- Research has practical problems and ethical responsibilities.
- A good report states findings, evidence, difficulties, and limits.
