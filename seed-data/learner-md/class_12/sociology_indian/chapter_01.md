# Chapter 1: Introducing Indian Society
### NCERT Class 12 - Sociology Indian Society

## Chapter at a Glance

This opening chapter explains what makes sociology different from many other subjects. Everyone already knows something about society because everyone grows up inside social relationships. But this familiarity is both useful and dangerous. It helps us connect with the subject, yet it can also trap us inside common-sense assumptions shaped by our own social background.

The chapter asks learners to move from common sense to sociological understanding. Sociology requires unlearning, self-reflexivity, and the ability to connect personal experiences with wider social structures. It also introduces the themes of the book: population, caste, tribe, family, markets, inequality, exclusion, diversity, and practical sociological research.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain why sociology begins from familiar everyday life;
- distinguish common-sense knowledge from sociological knowledge;
- explain why "unlearning" is important in sociology;
- define reflexivity and self-reflexivity;
- understand the idea of a social map;
- connect personal troubles with social issues;
- identify major themes in the study of Indian society;
- write sociological answers using concepts rather than only opinions;
- recognise how identity is shaped by age, class, caste, region, religion, gender, language, and family background.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Sociology | Systematic study of society, social relationships, groups, institutions, and social change. |
| Common sense | Everyday knowledge acquired through social experience, often partial and shaped by one's group location. |
| Unlearning | Questioning and moving beyond taken-for-granted assumptions. |
| Socialisation | Process through which individuals learn norms, values, roles, and expectations of society. |
| Reflexivity | Ability to examine oneself and one's society critically. |
| Self-reflexivity | Turning the sociological gaze back on oneself and one's own social location. |
| Social map | A way of locating oneself or others within networks of social groups and relations. |
| Personal trouble | A problem experienced at the individual level. |
| Social issue | A problem connected to wider social structures and affecting groups. |
| Social group | A collectivity of people sharing some identity, location, interest, or relationship. |

## Concept Map

| Everyday experience | Sociological move |
|---|---|
| "I know society because I live in it." | Ask how that knowledge was formed. |
| "My view is natural." | Ask which social group shaped that view. |
| "This is my personal problem." | Ask whether many people face it due to social conditions. |
| "My identity is only personal." | Locate identity on a social map. |
| "Society is obvious." | Treat society as something to be studied systematically. |

# Unit 1A: Why Sociology Feels Familiar

Sociology studies society, and society is something every learner already experiences. A child may not know physics or economics before studying them, but every child knows something about family, friendship, gender expectations, age hierarchy, language, work, school, authority, and rules.

This prior knowledge comes from:

- family life;
- neighbourhood;
- school;
- caste or community setting;
- religion;
- region;
- language;
- media;
- peer groups;
- everyday observation.

Because of this, sociology does not begin from zero. It begins from the learner's lived world.

## Advantage

Students are usually less afraid of sociology because it speaks about familiar things.

## Disadvantage

Familiarity can make people overconfident. They may assume that their own experience represents the whole society.

# Unit 1B: Common Sense as Partial Knowledge

Common sense is not useless. It gives us starting points. But it is often partial in two ways:

- incomplete, because it shows only part of social reality;
- biased, because it is shaped by the position and interests of one's own group.

For example:

- a privileged student may think schooling is equally available to all;
- a person from a dominant caste may not notice caste humiliation;
- an urban resident may misunderstand village life;
- a man may fail to notice restrictions imposed on women;
- an adult may dismiss the anxieties of young people.

Sociology asks us to treat such assumptions as questions, not conclusions.

# Unit 1C: The Need for Unlearning

Unlearning means questioning what appears natural or automatic.

It does not mean rejecting everything we know. It means checking:

- where the knowledge came from;
- whose interests it reflects;
- whose experience it ignores;
- whether evidence supports it;
- whether it applies beyond one's own social location.

## Example

Common-sense view:

"Poor people remain poor because they do not work hard."

Sociological question:

"What social, economic, educational, caste, gender, and regional barriers shape people's opportunities?"

This shift is the beginning of sociological thinking.

# Unit 1D: Social Context Shapes Viewpoint

Every person sees society from a particular location. This location is shaped by:

- age;
- gender;
- family income;
- caste or tribe;
- religion;
- language;
- region;
- rural or urban residence;
- educational access;
- occupation of parents;
- social networks.

These locations influence what people notice, ignore, fear, value, or take for granted.

## Sociological Lesson

No viewpoint is completely outside society. Even our opinions about society are socially shaped.

# Unit 1E: Reflexivity and Self-Reflexivity

Reflexivity is the ability to think critically about one's own assumptions and location.

Self-reflexivity means looking at oneself from outside, as if one were also a subject of sociological study.

It involves asking:

- What social groups do I belong to?
- What privileges or disadvantages come with these memberships?
- Which norms did I learn without noticing?
- How do others see people like me?
- Which experiences are invisible to me because of my social location?

## Critical Self-Inspection

The chapter emphasises that self-inspection should be critical. It should be slow to praise and quick to examine. Sociology does not exist to make us feel superior; it trains us to see society more clearly.

# Unit 1F: Social Map

A social map locates individuals in society.

A learner may be located through:

- age group, such as young people;
- region, such as a particular state or district;
- language community;
- religion;
- caste or tribe;
- gender;
- class position;
- family occupation;
- rural or urban background;
- school type;
- citizenship;
- disability status.

## Why Social Location Matters

Social location affects:

- opportunities;
- expectations;
- treatment by others;
- access to resources;
- marriage possibilities;
- educational pathways;
- occupational choices;
- sense of belonging.

Sociology helps us understand how these locations connect individuals to larger social structures.

# Unit 1G: Personal Troubles and Social Issues

C. Wright Mills argued that sociology helps connect personal troubles with social issues.

## Personal Trouble

A personal trouble is experienced by an individual as a private difficulty.

Examples:

- worry about employment;
- family conflict;
- anxiety about marriage;
- humiliation at school;
- pressure to perform;
- fear of social judgment.

## Social Issue

A social issue affects many people and is rooted in wider institutions or structures.

Examples:

- unemployment among youth;
- gender discrimination;
- caste exclusion;
- rural-urban inequality;
- unequal schooling;
- dowry;
- communal conflict;
- lack of accessible public spaces.

## Sociological Connection

If one student cannot find work, it may appear personal. If millions of educated youth cannot find secure employment, it is a social issue connected to the economy, education system, state policy, class structure, and labour market.

# Unit 1H: The Sociological Imagination in Indian Society

Indian society is complex because many institutions and identities overlap.

A person is never only an individual. They are also connected to:

- family and kinship;
- caste and tribe;
- religious community;
- region and language;
- class and occupation;
- gender relations;
- state policies;
- markets;
- education;
- media.

Sociology studies how these institutions shape life chances.

## Life Chances

Life chances refer to the opportunities people have for health, education, income, dignity, security, and social mobility.

Life chances are not distributed randomly. They are patterned by social structure.

# Unit 1I: Preview of the Book

This chapter introduces the themes that appear in the rest of the book.

## Demographic Structure

Population is not just a number. Sociologists ask:

- What is the age structure?
- What is the sex ratio?
- How do birth and death rates change?
- How does population affect development?
- How do state policies influence demographic change?

## Caste, Tribe, and Family

These are central institutions of Indian society.

Questions include:

- How has caste changed?
- What does tribe mean in India?
- How are tribal identities formed?
- How do family forms differ?
- How do migration and work reshape family life?

## Market as Social Institution

Markets are not only economic mechanisms. They are shaped by caste, kinship, trust, community networks, colonial history, capitalism, consumption, and globalisation.

## Inequality and Exclusion

The book studies how caste, tribe, gender, disability, and other identities are linked to exclusion and struggle.

## Cultural Diversity

India's diversity includes language, religion, caste, tribe, region, ethnicity, and culture. Diversity can enrich society, but it can also produce conflict when combined with inequality and competition over resources.

## Practical Research

The final chapter encourages learners to do small sociological research exercises using methods such as survey, interview, observation, and archival work.

# Unit 1J: How to Write Sociologically

A sociological answer should:

- define concepts clearly;
- avoid unsupported opinion;
- connect individual experience with wider structures;
- use examples from Indian society;
- recognise diversity within groups;
- avoid blaming victims;
- consider historical change;
- show how institutions shape behaviour.

## Weak Answer Style

"Society is changing because people are modern now."

## Better Sociological Style

"Social change is shaped by institutions such as education, markets, media, state policy, urbanisation, and family structure. These institutions affect groups differently depending on class, caste, gender, region, and generation."

# Unit 1K: Worked Sociological Examples

## Example 1: Personal Trouble to Social Issue

Personal statement:

"I am worried about getting a job."

Sociological expansion:

This worry is not only personal if many young people face similar insecurity. It may be connected to the education system, labour market, technology, economic policy, class background, gender norms, English-language access, and region.

## Example 2: Social Map of a Student

A student may be:

- 17 years old;
- from a middle-income family;
- from a particular caste;
- a speaker of a regional language;
- living in a city;
- studying in an English-medium school;
- part of a religious minority or majority;
- first-generation learner or from an educated family.

Each location affects how the student experiences society.

## Example 3: Common Sense to Sociological Question

Common-sense claim:

"Cities give everyone freedom."

Sociological question:

"Freedom in cities may vary by gender, class, caste, disability, work, neighbourhood, policing, transport, and housing. Who experiences the city as freedom, and who experiences it as danger or exclusion?"

# Unit 1L: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain why sociology requires unlearning

1. State that everyone has prior knowledge of society.
2. Explain that this knowledge comes from one's own social location.
3. Show how common sense can be partial and biased.
4. Define unlearning as questioning taken-for-granted assumptions.
5. Add an example such as poverty, gender, caste, or schooling.

## To explain reflexivity

1. Define reflexivity.
2. Explain self-reflexivity as looking at oneself from outside.
3. Mention social location.
4. Show that identity is shaped by group memberships.
5. Explain why critical self-inspection is important.

## To connect personal trouble and social issue

1. Define personal trouble.
2. Define social issue.
3. Give one example.
4. Show the link between individual experience and wider structure.
5. Conclude that sociology studies this connection.

# Unit 1M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating common sense as always wrong. | Common sense is a starting point, but it must be examined critically. |
| Writing only personal opinions. | Use sociological concepts and evidence. |
| Assuming one's own group represents all Indians. | Indian society is diverse and unequal. |
| Confusing reflexivity with self-praise. | Reflexivity means critical self-examination. |
| Treating personal problems as always private. | Many personal problems are linked to social structures. |
| Forgetting history. | Institutions change over time and must be understood historically. |

## Final Revision

- Sociology starts from familiar everyday life.
- Familiarity can hide inequality and bias.
- Common sense is partial because it reflects social location.
- Unlearning is central to sociological thinking.
- Reflexivity means turning the sociological gaze back on oneself.
- A social map locates people in networks of social identities and relations.
- Sociology connects personal troubles with social issues.
- Indian society must be studied through institutions, identities, inequalities, and change.
