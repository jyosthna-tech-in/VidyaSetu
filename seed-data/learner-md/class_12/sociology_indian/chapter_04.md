# Chapter 4: The Market as a Social Institution
### NCERT Class 12 - Sociology Indian Society

## Chapter at a Glance

Markets are often treated as economic spaces where goods and services are bought and sold. This chapter shows that markets are also social institutions. They are shaped by caste, kinship, trust, community networks, colonialism, capitalism, consumption, globalisation, state policy, and culture.

The chapter moves from everyday markets to broader market systems. It explains sociological perspectives on markets, weekly tribal markets, caste-based trade networks, colonial market transformation, capitalism, commodification, consumption, globalisation, liberalisation, and the debate between market and state.

# Unit 4A: Meanings of Market

The word market can mean several things.

## Physical Place

Example:

- fruit market;
- vegetable market;
- weekly market near a village;
- wholesale market.

## Gathering of Buyers and Sellers

A market may be a temporary gathering, such as a weekly haat that shifts location on different days.

## Category of Trade

Examples:

- market for cars;
- market for readymade clothes;
- market for software services;
- market for labour.

## Broad Economic System

In a general sense, "the market" can refer to the whole economy or system of exchange.

# Unit 4B: Economic vs Sociological View

Economics often studies markets as systems of price, demand, supply, investment, production, and exchange.

Sociology asks:

- Who controls the market?
- Which communities dominate particular trades?
- How are trust and credit organised?
- How do caste and kinship affect business?
- How do markets reproduce inequality?
- How do markets create social meanings?
- How does consumption communicate status?

## Main Difference

Economics may treat the market as relatively separate. Sociology studies markets as embedded in society.

# Unit 4C: Embedded Economy

An economy is embedded when economic action is shaped by social relations.

Markets are embedded in:

- caste;
- kinship;
- religion;
- gender;
- class;
- region;
- state law;
- trust networks;
- culture;
- political power.

## Example

A trader may give credit to someone from the same caste or kin network because trust is socially produced, not only legally enforced.

# Unit 4D: Weekly Tribal Market

A weekly haat in a tribal area is not only a place of buying and selling.

It is also:

- a meeting place;
- a site for exchanging news;
- a place to meet relatives;
- a setting for arranging marriages;
- a point of contact with traders, officials, moneylenders, and specialists;
- a link between local economy and wider regional economy.

## Goods in a Weekly Market

Common exchanges include:

- forest produce;
- agricultural produce;
- salt;
- cloth;
- ornaments;
- tools;
- bamboo baskets;
- oilseeds;
- local food.

## Power Relations

In many tribal markets, local adivasis sell forest produce while outside traders control purchase and resale. This can create unequal exchange and indebtedness.

# Unit 4E: Colonial Opening of Tribal Markets

Colonial rule transformed tribal markets by:

- building roads;
- expanding state control;
- extracting forest and mineral resources;
- bringing outside traders and moneylenders;
- recruiting tribal labour for plantations and mines;
- connecting local economies to wider capitalist markets.

## Consequences

Many adivasis:

- lost land;
- entered debt relations;
- became wage labourers;
- lost control over forest resources;
- became dependent on outside markets.

The market therefore became a site of both exchange and exploitation.

# Unit 4F: Caste-Based Trade Networks

Indian trade has often been organised through caste and community networks.

Merchant groups used:

- kinship;
- caste trust;
- marriage alliances;
- common religious institutions;
- community reputation;
- credit networks.

## Hundi

A hundi was a traditional bill of exchange. It allowed merchants to conduct long-distance trade through trust networks.

The hundi worked because merchants trusted other members of their trading community.

## Sociological Point

Business is not only calculation. It also depends on social trust, reputation, and community institutions.

# Unit 4G: Traditional Business Communities

Many Indian business communities have historically specialised in trade.

Examples include:

- Banias;
- Nakarattars;
- Parsis;
- Sindhis;
- Bohras;
- Jains;
- Marwaris;
- other regional merchant groups.

These groups often built dense networks across regions.

## Caste and Mobility

Some groups claimed Vaishya status through trade success. Caste status and occupation have had a complex relationship, with both inheritance and mobility present in different contexts.

# Unit 4H: Colonialism and New Markets

Colonialism brought major changes in Indian economy.

Important changes:

- India became more deeply linked to world capitalism;
- land revenue had to be paid in cash;
- commercial crops expanded;
- handloom industry declined due to machine-made imports;
- India became supplier of raw materials;
- India became market for British manufactured goods;
- new cities, plantations, ports, railways, and industries grew.

## Before Colonialism

The chapter also warns against imagining pre-colonial India as fully self-sufficient villages. India already had trade, money, manufacturing, merchant communities, and long-distance networks.

## Sociological Lesson

Colonialism did not create markets from nothing, but it reorganised them in a new exploitative global structure.

# Unit 4I: Capitalism

Capitalism is an economic system based on:

- private ownership of means of production;
- wage labour;
- production for profit;
- market exchange;
- competition;
- accumulation of capital.

Karl Marx analysed capitalism as a system in which capitalists own means of production and workers sell labour power.

## Surplus Value

Marx argued that capitalists profit by paying workers less than the value produced by their labour.

## Sociological Importance

Capitalism changes not only economy but also class relations, family life, consumption, migration, work discipline, and social identities.

# Unit 4J: Commodification

Commodification occurs when something not earlier bought and sold becomes a commodity.

Examples:

- labour power;
- bottled water;
- education through private coaching;
- marriage bureaus and matrimonial websites;
- personality development classes;
- health services;
- cultural performances;
- tourism experiences;
- spirituality and yoga packages.

## Moral Questions

Some things are controversial as commodities, such as human organs. This shows that markets are linked to moral values, law, inequality, and power.

# Unit 4K: Consumption and Status

Consumption is not only use of goods. It also communicates social meaning.

People use goods to express:

- class position;
- taste;
- lifestyle;
- success;
- modernity;
- gender identity;
- cultural belonging.

## Status Symbol

A status symbol is an object or practice that communicates social status.

Examples:

- car model;
- phone brand;
- clothing label;
- house location;
- school attended;
- wedding style;
- holiday destination.

## Sociological Insight

Advertisements often attach social meanings to goods. They sell not only products but lifestyles.

# Unit 4L: Globalisation

Globalisation means increasing interconnection across the world.

It includes movement of:

- goods;
- money;
- people;
- services;
- information;
- images;
- technologies;
- cultural symbols.

## Market Integration

A change in one country can affect employment, prices, and opportunities in another. For example, software services in India can be affected by demand from foreign clients.

## Culture as Commodity

Global tourism can turn fairs, rituals, spirituality, clothing, food, and images into marketable commodities.

Example:

A local cattle fair can also become an international tourist attraction.

# Unit 4M: Liberalisation and Marketisation

Liberalisation refers to policy changes that reduce state controls and expand market processes.

It may include:

- privatisation of public sector enterprises;
- reducing import duties;
- allowing foreign investment;
- reducing regulations;
- encouraging private sector;
- opening markets to global competition.

Marketisation means using market mechanisms instead of direct state control.

## Arguments in Favour

Supporters argue that liberalisation:

- increases efficiency;
- attracts investment;
- expands consumer choice;
- creates growth;
- connects India to global markets.

## Criticisms

Critics argue that liberalisation can:

- hurt small producers;
- expose farmers to global competition;
- reduce support prices and subsidies;
- increase unorganised employment;
- weaken labour security;
- widen inequality;
- make essential services less affordable.

# Unit 4N: Market vs State

The chapter presents a debate:

- Should markets solve economic and social problems?
- Or should the state regulate, protect, and redistribute?

## Market Strengths

Markets can:

- encourage innovation;
- respond to demand;
- create efficiency;
- expand production.

## State Responsibilities

The state is needed for:

- protecting workers;
- supporting farmers;
- regulating unfair practices;
- providing public goods;
- ensuring health and education;
- reducing inequality;
- preventing exploitation.

## Balanced View

A sociological answer should avoid simple celebration or rejection of markets. It should ask who benefits, who loses, and how power shapes outcomes.

# Unit 4O: Worked Sociological Examples

## Example 1: Weekly Market as Social Institution

A weekly village market is economic because goods are exchanged. It is social because people meet relatives, exchange news, build trust, negotiate marriages, interact with officials, and reproduce power relations.

## Example 2: Bottled Water as Commodity

Water was once mostly treated as a shared resource or household provision. Bottled water turns drinking water into a branded commodity. This shows commodification and also raises questions about class, access, health, and environment.

## Example 3: Pushkar Fair and Globalisation

A local fair may continue to serve pastoralists and pilgrims, but tourism markets can turn it into a spectacle for national and international visitors. Culture itself becomes part of market exchange.

# Unit 4Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating market as only a physical place. | Market can be place, network, sector, or whole economy. |
| Saying sociology ignores economics. | Sociology studies economic life within social relations. |
| Assuming trust in business is only legal. | Trust is often built through caste, kinship, community, and reputation. |
| Treating colonialism as the start of all Indian trade. | India had extensive pre-colonial trade, but colonialism reorganised it. |
| Confusing globalisation and liberalisation. | Globalisation is worldwide interconnection; liberalisation is policy opening markets. |
| Saying all commodification is automatically good. | Commodification can create access, but also inequality and moral conflict. |
| Treating consumption as only personal taste. | Consumption also communicates status and identity. |

## Final Revision

- Markets are social institutions, not only economic mechanisms.
- Economic activity is embedded in social relations.
- Weekly markets combine trade, social meeting, ritual, services, and power.
- Caste and kinship networks can organise trust and credit.
- Colonialism reorganised Indian markets within world capitalism.
- Capitalism turns labour and many services into commodities.
- Commodification changes the social meaning of things and services.
- Consumption communicates status and identity.
- Globalisation connects local markets with world markets.
- Liberalisation expands market processes but has mixed consequences.
