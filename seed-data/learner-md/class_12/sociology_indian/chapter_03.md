# Chapter 3: Social Institutions: Continuity and Change
### NCERT Class 12 - Sociology Indian Society

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies three central institutions of Indian society: caste, tribe, and family. These institutions are old, but they are not frozen. They have changed through history, colonialism, nationalism, development, law, urbanisation, education, migration, markets, and social movements.

The chapter asks learners to understand continuity and change together. Caste persists but not in exactly the same form. Tribal identities are shaped through interaction with state, market, and mainstream society. Families remain important but vary greatly in structure, residence, inheritance, authority, and emotional life.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- describe major features of caste in the past;
- explain caste as separation, hierarchy, and division of labour;
- analyse colonial impact on caste;
- explain changes in caste after independence;
- explain why caste became invisible to some groups but visible to others;
- classify tribes by permanent and acquired traits;
- explain debates around tribe-caste distinction;
- analyse tribal identity in relation to development and displacement;
- distinguish nuclear and extended family;
- explain diverse family forms such as matrilocal, patrilocal, matrilineal, and patrilineal;
- distinguish matriliny and matriarchy.

# Unit 3A: Social Institutions

Social institutions are stable arrangements through which society organises important areas of life.

Examples:

- family;
- caste;
- tribe;
- market;
- religion;
- education;
- state;
- marriage;
- property.

Institutions give continuity, but they also change. They structure behaviour, distribute status, define duties, and shape identities.

# Unit 3B: Caste as an Indian Institution

Caste is closely associated with the Indian subcontinent. Though most often linked with Hindu society, caste-like practices and identities have also influenced Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, and other communities in South Asia.

Two terms are important:

- `varna`: broad four-fold classification;
- `jati`: local caste group, the practical unit of caste identity.

## Varna

The four varnas are:

- Brahmana;
- Kshatriya;
- Vaishya;
- Shudra.

Groups outside this classification were treated as outcastes or placed beyond the varna order.

## Jati

Jati is more local and complex. India has thousands of jatis and sub-jatis. Jati is usually more important in actual marriage, food, occupation, and local hierarchy.

# Unit 3C: Features of Caste

Major features traditionally associated with caste:

1. Caste is determined by birth.
2. Caste membership is not a matter of individual choice.
3. Castes are endogamous.
4. Food-sharing and social interaction are regulated.
5. Castes are arranged in hierarchy.
6. Castes have sub-divisions.
7. Occupations were traditionally hereditary.
8. Caste status was linked with ideas of purity and pollution.

## Birth-Based Status

A person is born into a caste and traditionally could not change it.

## Endogamy

Marriage within caste preserves caste boundaries. Even today, endogamy is one of the strongest surviving features of caste.

## Occupation

Traditional caste society linked occupation with birth. This made caste a rigid division of labour and also a division of labourers.

# Unit 3D: Separation and Hierarchy

Caste is based on two connected principles:

- separation;
- hierarchy.

## Separation

Castes are kept apart through rules about:

- marriage;
- food sharing;
- touch;
- occupation;
- residence;
- ritual practice;
- social distance.

## Hierarchy

Castes are ranked from high to low. This ranking is often justified through ritual ideas of purity and pollution.

## Complementarity

Traditional caste ideology also claimed that castes were complementary because each caste had its own role. But this complementarity hid inequality because some roles were honoured while others were degraded.

# Unit 3E: Colonialism and Caste

Colonial rule changed caste in major ways.

British administrators tried to understand Indian society in order to govern it. They conducted:

- surveys;
- ethnographic reports;
- census operations;
- classification of castes and tribes.

## Census and Caste

The census counted and ranked caste groups. This made caste identities more fixed in official records.

Many caste groups petitioned for higher rank, showing that colonial classification affected social self-understanding.

## Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes

Colonial administration recognised lists of disadvantaged groups for special treatment. Later, terms such as Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes became part of constitutional and policy language.

## Sociological Point

Colonialism did not invent caste, but it reshaped caste by counting, classifying, ranking, and administering it.

# Unit 3F: Caste after Independence

Independent India officially rejected caste discrimination.

The Constitution:

- abolished untouchability;
- prohibited caste discrimination;
- provided safeguards and reservations for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes.

## Forces Weakening Caste

Some processes weakened older caste rules:

- industrialisation;
- urbanisation;
- modern education;
- democratic politics;
- new occupations;
- law;
- social reform movements;
- migration.

## Forces Keeping Caste Alive

Caste remained strong through:

- endogamy;
- kinship networks;
- political mobilisation;
- land ownership patterns;
- local status hierarchies;
- social prejudice;
- access to education and jobs;
- domestic and ritual practices.

# Unit 3G: Caste in Politics

Democratic politics changed caste and was also shaped by caste.

Caste groups became political actors. They organised to demand:

- representation;
- reservations;
- dignity;
- resources;
- recognition;
- protection from discrimination.

## Paradox

Democracy weakened caste hierarchy in some ways, but it also made caste identities politically visible.

This does not simply mean caste became stronger in the old sense. It means caste became a basis for collective rights and political bargaining.

# Unit 3H: Invisibility of Caste for Some Groups

For many urban upper-caste people, caste may appear invisible because their caste privilege has become normalised.

They may see themselves as:

- modern;
- individual;
- merit-based;
- casteless.

But caste remains visible to those who face discrimination, humiliation, exclusion, or blocked opportunities.

## Sociological Lesson

Privilege often appears invisible to those who benefit from it.

# Unit 3I: Understanding Tribe

Tribe is a complex category in India.

Tribes have been classified by:

- language;
- region;
- physical traits;
- ecological location;
- mode of livelihood;
- degree of incorporation into Hindu society;
- relation with state and market.

The term tribe is not merely descriptive. It has political, administrative, and historical meanings.

# Unit 3J: Tribe-Caste Debate

Sociologists debated whether tribes are:

- completely different from caste society; or
- one end of a continuum with caste-peasant society.

## Difference Argument

Tribes were said to differ from castes because:

- they had more egalitarian organisation;
- kinship was central;
- purity-pollution hierarchy was absent or weaker;
- community ownership was more common.

## Continuum Argument

Others argued that tribes and caste society interacted for centuries and cannot be sharply separated.

Many tribal groups:

- practiced settled agriculture;
- traded with caste groups;
- joined regional political systems;
- were absorbed into Hindu society at different levels;
- formed kingdoms in some regions.

## Modern View

Tribal identity is shaped historically through interaction with state, market, caste society, colonialism, and development.

# Unit 3K: Tribal Communities and Development

Many tribal regions are rich in:

- forests;
- minerals;
- water resources;
- land suitable for dams and power projects.

Development projects often displaced tribal communities.

Major issues:

- loss of land;
- loss of forest access;
- displacement by dams and mines;
- in-migration of non-tribals;
- cultural marginalisation;
- weakening of community ownership;
- exploitation by traders and moneylenders.

## National Development vs Tribal Development

Large development schemes presented as national development may impose disproportionate costs on tribal communities. This creates conflict between mainstream development and tribal rights.

# Unit 3L: Tribal Identity Today

Tribal identities today are often shaped by resistance.

Important bases of tribal movements:

- control over land;
- forest rights;
- protection of culture;
- political autonomy;
- statehood demands;
- opposition to displacement;
- access to education and jobs;
- dignity and recognition.

## Tribal Middle Class

Education and reservations have helped create a tribal middle class in some regions. This group may articulate identity through culture, rights, and political representation, while poorer tribal groups may focus more on land, livelihood, and survival.

# Unit 3M: Family and Kinship

Family is a central institution of care, socialisation, reproduction, inheritance, and emotional life.

But family is not always harmonious. It can also be a site of:

- conflict;
- gender inequality;
- property disputes;
- violence;
- control over marriage;
- generational tension.

Sociology studies both care and conflict within family.

# Unit 3N: Nuclear and Extended Family

## Nuclear Family

A nuclear family consists of:

- one set of parents;
- their children.

## Extended Family

An extended family includes:

- more than one couple;
- more than two generations;
- siblings with spouses and children;
- grandparents, parents, children, and other kin.

The extended family is often called joint family, but the chapter reminds us that this term itself is not a native category in many Indian languages.

## Important Point

India has never had only one family form. Family structure varies across class, caste, region, religion, occupation, and migration patterns.

# Unit 3O: Diverse Family Forms

Family forms differ by residence, inheritance, and authority.

## Residence

Patrilocal:

- couple lives with or near husband's family.

Matrilocal:

- couple lives with or near wife's family.

## Descent and Inheritance

Patrilineal:

- descent or property passes through male line.

Matrilineal:

- descent or property passes through female line.

## Authority

Patriarchy:

- men exercise authority and dominance.

Matriarchy:

- women exercise authority and dominance.

The chapter notes that matriarchy is mostly a theoretical concept; matrilineal societies exist, but this does not necessarily mean women dominate public authority.

# Unit 3P: Family and Social Change

Family changes with wider society.

Examples:

- male migration can create women-headed households;
- software and service jobs can change care arrangements;
- grandparents may become caregivers when parents work long hours;
- education can change marriage choices;
- urban housing can encourage nuclear households;
- same-sex relationships and love marriages challenge older norms;
- conflict over inheritance can reshape kinship relations.

## Sociological Lesson

The private family is connected to public institutions such as economy, education, law, politics, and culture.

# Unit 3Q: Worked Sociological Examples

## Example 1: Caste and Occupation

Traditional rule:

Occupation is fixed by caste.

Modern change:

Education, urban jobs, industry, and law weaken hereditary occupation.

Continuity:

Networks, discrimination, and inherited resources still affect occupational opportunities.

## Example 2: Tribe and Development

A dam may produce electricity for cities and industries. But it may also displace tribal villages, submerge forests, and destroy livelihoods.

Sociological answer:

Development must be studied by asking who benefits, who pays the cost, and who controls resources.

## Example 3: Family Change

If parents migrate for work and grandparents care for children, family structure changes due to labour market pressures.

This shows that family change is linked to economy.

# Unit 3S: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating caste as only ancient and absent today. | Caste has changed but remains socially and politically significant. |
| Confusing varna and jati. | Varna is broad classification; jati is local caste group. |
| Saying colonialism created caste from nothing. | Colonialism reshaped and fixed caste identities, but caste existed earlier. |
| Treating tribes as isolated stone-age communities. | Tribal groups have long interacted with states, markets, caste society, and development. |
| Assuming all tribal people have same interests. | Tribal societies also have internal class and regional differences. |
| Treating joint family as universal in India. | Family forms are diverse and changing. |
| Confusing matriliny with matriarchy. | Matriliny is inheritance through women; matriarchy means women hold dominance. |
