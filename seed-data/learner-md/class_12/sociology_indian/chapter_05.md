# Chapter 5: Patterns of Social Inequality and Exclusion
### NCERT Class 12 - Sociology Indian Society

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies how social institutions create and sustain inequality and exclusion. It explains that inequality is not only about individual talent or effort. It is patterned by social structures such as caste, tribe, gender, disability, class, religion, and region. The chapter introduces social stratification, prejudice, stereotypes, discrimination, social exclusion, and struggles for justice by Dalits, adivasis, women, and differently abled people.

The central lesson is that inequality becomes durable when it is socially organised, justified by beliefs, and reproduced across generations.

# Unit 5A: Why Inequality Appears Natural

In everyday life, people often see poverty, child labour, caste humiliation, gender violence, or disability exclusion so frequently that they begin to appear normal.

This is dangerous because it hides injustice.

Common mistaken belief:

"People are poor because they do not work hard."

Sociological response:

Many poor people work extremely hard. Their life chances are limited by social structures such as caste, class, education, land ownership, gender, health, and employment conditions.

# Unit 5B: Social Inequality

Social inequality refers to unequal access to valued resources.

Valued resources include:

- money;
- property;
- education;
- health;
- political power;
- social respect;
- security;
- networks;
- cultural recognition.

## Three Forms of Capital

Economic capital:

- income;
- land;
- property;
- savings;
- business assets.

Cultural capital:

- education;
- degrees;
- language skills;
- manners;
- confidence;
- cultural knowledge.

Social capital:

- contacts;
- family networks;
- caste networks;
- professional links;
- political connections.

## Conversion of Capital

One form of capital can become another.

Example:

A wealthy family can buy better education. Education becomes cultural capital. It may lead to a better job, producing more economic capital.

# Unit 5C: Social Stratification

Social stratification is a system in which categories of people are ranked in a hierarchy.

Three key features:

1. It is a feature of society, not only individual difference.
2. It persists across generations.
3. It is supported by beliefs and ideology.

## Society-Wide Pattern

Stratification distributes resources unequally across groups, not randomly across individuals.

## Intergenerational Persistence

Family, inheritance, caste, schooling, property, and marriage reproduce advantage and disadvantage.

## Ideological Support

Systems of inequality survive when people believe they are natural, fair, deserved, divinely ordained, or inevitable.

# Unit 5D: Prejudice, Stereotype, and Discrimination

## Prejudice

Prejudice is a pre-judgement about a group.

It may be negative or positive, but in social inequality it often means negative attitudes toward another group.

## Stereotype

A stereotype is a fixed image of an entire group.

Examples:

- "This group is lazy."
- "That community is aggressive."
- "Women are not suited for leadership."
- "Disabled people are helpless."

Stereotypes ignore individual variation and changing contexts.

## Discrimination

Discrimination is actual unequal treatment.

Examples:

- refusing a house to someone because of religion;
- denying a job due to caste;
- paying women less for similar work;
- designing schools without access for disabled students.

# Unit 5E: Social Exclusion

Social exclusion refers to ways in which individuals or groups are cut off from full participation in society.

It can involve denial of access to:

- education;
- health care;
- housing;
- work;
- transport;
- banking;
- police protection;
- legal justice;
- public spaces;
- dignity and respect.

## Systematic Nature

Exclusion is not accidental. It is produced by social structures, institutions, beliefs, and practices.

# Unit 5F: Caste as a Discriminatory System

Caste is a birth-based social institution that historically linked:

- occupation;
- status;
- marriage;
- food rules;
- social distance;
- ritual hierarchy.

It is discriminatory because some castes were treated as superior while others were degraded and excluded.

## Caste and Class Today

The link between caste and occupation has weakened, but caste and class remain connected at the macro level.

Privileged groups are still more likely to have:

- land;
- education;
- secure jobs;
- social networks;
- political influence.

Historically oppressed groups are more likely to face:

- poverty;
- landlessness;
- low literacy;
- humiliation;
- insecure work;
- violence.

# Unit 5G: Untouchability

Untouchability is an extreme form of caste exclusion.

It includes:

- physical segregation;
- denial of temple entry;
- denial of access to wells or public facilities;
- separate seating;
- forced degrading labour;
- social humiliation;
- violence;
- stigma attached to birth and occupation.

## Dimensions of Untouchability

Untouchability is:

- social, because it regulates interaction;
- economic, because it confines people to low-paid work;
- political, because it denies power;
- cultural, because it creates stigma;
- spatial, because it segregates settlements and public spaces.

# Unit 5H: Policies Against Caste Inequality

Independent India introduced legal and policy measures.

Important measures include:

- abolition of untouchability;
- prohibition of caste discrimination;
- reservations in education, jobs, and legislatures for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes;
- protective laws against atrocities;
- welfare schemes;
- scholarships;
- land and development programmes.

## Limits of Law

Law is necessary but not sufficient. Social attitudes, local power, economic inequality, and everyday practices must also change.

# Unit 5I: Other Backward Classes and Dalits

Dalits generally refer to communities historically treated as untouchable and listed as Scheduled Castes.

Other Backward Classes refer to socially and educationally backward caste groups that were not treated as untouchable in the same way but were disadvantaged in status, education, and representation.

## Difference in Location

Dalits faced extreme ritual exclusion and untouchability.

OBC groups are diverse: some are poor and marginalised, while others have regional power or landholding strength.

# Unit 5J: Adivasis and Exclusion

Adivasis have faced exclusion through:

- loss of forest rights;
- displacement by dams and mines;
- land alienation;
- indebtedness;
- cultural marginalisation;
- lack of schooling and health services;
- in-migration of non-tribals;
- administrative control over forests;
- violence and insecurity.

## Main Struggles

Adivasi struggles often focus on:

- land;
- forest;
- water;
- livelihood;
- autonomy;
- cultural identity;
- protection from displacement;
- share in benefits of development.

# Unit 5K: Women's Inequality and Movements

Women face inequality in:

- family;
- inheritance;
- education;
- paid work;
- political representation;
- safety;
- marriage;
- domestic labour;
- media representation;
- law;
- bodily autonomy.

## Historical Concerns

Nineteenth-century reform movements raised issues such as:

- sati;
- child marriage;
- widow remarriage;
- women's education.

## Twentieth-Century and Later Concerns

Women's movements later addressed:

- equal rights;
- voting rights;
- employment;
- violence against women;
- dowry;
- domestic violence;
- representation in media;
- legal reform;
- declining child sex ratio;
- political participation.

## Key Sociological Point

Gender inequality is not only about attitudes. It is built into family, property, work, law, culture, and public space.

# Unit 5L: Disability as Social Issue

The chapter asks us to rethink disability.

A person may have an impairment, but society often makes that impairment disabling through:

- inaccessible buildings;
- absence of ramps;
- lack of inclusive schooling;
- stigma;
- insulting labels;
- lack of assistive devices;
- exclusion from jobs;
- public transport barriers;
- pity rather than rights.

## Medical vs Social View

Medical view:

- sees disability mainly as individual impairment.

Social view:

- asks how society creates barriers.

## Disability and Poverty

Disability and poverty are linked.

Poverty can increase disability through:

- malnutrition;
- poor health care;
- unsafe work;
- lack of immunisation;
- accidents.

Disability can deepen poverty by:

- increasing care burden;
- reducing employment;
- increasing health costs;
- creating social isolation.

# Unit 5M: Struggles Against Exclusion

Inequality also produces resistance.

Important struggles include:

- anti-caste movements;
- Dalit movements;
- tribal movements;
- women's movements;
- disability rights movements;
- campaigns for legal reform;
- movements for dignity and recognition;
- struggles for access to education, land, work, and public spaces.

## Sociological Lesson

Excluded groups are not passive victims. They organise, resist, reinterpret identity, and demand rights.

# Unit 5N: Worked Sociological Examples

## Example 1: Prejudice vs Discrimination

Prejudice:

"People from this community are unreliable."

Discrimination:

Refusing to rent a house to someone because they belong to that community.

## Example 2: Social Exclusion

A disabled student may be admitted to school, but if classrooms are upstairs, toilets are inaccessible, and textbooks are unavailable in suitable format, the student is still excluded.

## Example 3: Caste and Economic Inequality

Even if occupations are no longer legally fixed by caste, caste can still affect schooling, networks, marriage, land ownership, and job access. Therefore caste inequality can continue in new forms.

# Unit 5O: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain social stratification

1. Define social stratification.
2. State that it is society-wide.
3. Explain intergenerational persistence.
4. Mention ideology and belief systems.
5. Give an Indian example such as caste or gender.

## To distinguish prejudice, stereotype, and discrimination

1. Prejudice is attitude.
2. Stereotype is fixed group image.
3. Discrimination is unequal action.
4. Give one example of each.
5. Show how they reinforce exclusion.

## To explain disability sociologically

1. Mention impairment.
2. Explain social barriers.
3. Give examples: buildings, schools, transport, jobs, stigma.
4. Connect disability and poverty.
5. Conclude that accessibility and rights are necessary.

# Unit 5P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying inequality is only due to talent. | Social inequality is structured by group location and institutions. |
| Confusing prejudice and discrimination. | Prejudice is attitude; discrimination is action. |
| Treating stereotypes as harmless. | Stereotypes justify exclusion and unequal treatment. |
| Saying caste has disappeared because occupations changed. | Caste persists in marriage, politics, networks, status, and discrimination. |
| Treating law as enough to end inequality. | Legal change must be supported by social change. |
| Seeing disabled people only as victims. | Disability rights approach emphasises access, dignity, and social barriers. |
| Ignoring internal differences within groups. | Groups such as women, Dalits, adivasis, and OBCs are internally diverse. |
