## Chapter at a Glance

Demography is the systematic study of population. This chapter explains how population is counted, measured, compared, and interpreted. # Unit 2A: Meaning and Scope of Demography

Demography comes from Greek roots meaning description of people.

It studies:

- population size;
- birth patterns;
- death patterns;
- migration;
- age composition;
- sex composition;
- rural-urban distribution;
- literacy;
- population policies.

Demography is important because population affects:

- planning;
- health care;
- education;
- employment;
- housing;
- infrastructure;
- welfare policy;
- political representation;
- economic development.

# Unit 2B: Formal Demography and Social Demography

## Formal Demography

Formal demography focuses on measurement.

It uses:

- statistics;
- rates and ratios;
- mathematical models;
- projections;
- census data;
- surveys.

It asks questions such as:

- How fast is population growing?
- What is the fertility rate?
- What is the age structure?
- How many people will live in a region after 20 years?

## Social Demography

Social demography asks why population patterns occur and what effects they have.

It studies links between population and:

- class;
- caste;
- gender;
- education;
- religion;
- migration;
- employment;
- health;
- state policy;
- cultural norms.

## Difference

Formal demography counts and measures. Social demography explains social causes and consequences.

# Unit 2C: Census and Social Statistics

A census is a systematic official count of people in a territory.

In India:

- census operations began under colonial rule in the late nineteenth century;
- regular ten-year census practice began in 1881;
- independent India continued the decennial census;
- the raw chapter's demographic discussion relies heavily on Census 2011.

## Why Census Matters

Census data helps the state plan:

- schools;
- hospitals;
- roads;
- food distribution;
- welfare schemes;
- elections;
- urban planning;
- employment policy.

## Sociological Importance

Large-scale statistics show that social facts exist beyond individual cases. For example, a death may be individual, but the death rate is a social fact because it shows patterned conditions across a population.

# Unit 2D: Malthusian Theory of Population

Thomas Robert Malthus argued that population grows faster than food supply.

His claim:

- population tends to grow in geometric progression: `2, 4, 8, 16`;
- food supply grows in arithmetic progression: `2, 4, 6, 8`;
- population will outrun subsistence resources;
- poverty and starvation are therefore likely unless population growth is controlled.

## Checks on Population

Malthus described:

- preventive checks, such as delayed marriage or abstinence;
- positive checks, such as famine, disease, and epidemics.

## Criticism

Malthus was criticised because:

- food production increased through technology;
- economic growth often outpaced population growth;
- poverty is not caused only by population growth;
- unequal distribution of resources matters;
- social systems can create hunger even when food exists.

## Sociological Lesson

Population cannot be understood only biologically. It is connected to production, distribution, inequality, technology, and policy.

# Unit 2E: Demographic Transition Theory

Demographic transition theory links population growth with economic development.

It has three broad stages.

## Stage 1: Low Growth in Underdeveloped Society

Features:

- high birth rate;
- high death rate;
- low population growth;
- poor medical facilities;
- low food security;
- high infant mortality.

## Stage 2: Population Explosion

Features:

- death rate falls quickly;
- birth rate remains high for some time;
- population grows rapidly.

Death rate falls because of:

- public health improvement;
- disease control;
- better nutrition;
- sanitation;
- modern medicine.

Birth rate falls more slowly because reproductive behaviour is shaped by culture, family norms, gender relations, child survival, and economic expectations.

## Stage 3: Low Growth in Developed Society

Features:

- low birth rate;
- low death rate;
- stable or slow-growing population.

## India's Position

The chapter explains that India has moved through demographic transition but has not completed it uniformly across all regions. Some states have reached near replacement levels; others have higher fertility.

# Unit 2F: Common Demographic Indicators

## Birth Rate

`birth rate = live births in a year / total population x 1000`

It shows how many live births occur per 1000 population in a year.

## Death Rate

`death rate = deaths in a year / total population x 1000`

It shows mortality conditions.

## Natural Growth Rate

Natural growth depends on the difference between birth rate and death rate.

If birth rate is much higher than death rate, population grows quickly.

## Fertility Rate

Fertility rate focuses on women in reproductive age group, usually 15 to 49 years.

It is more specific than crude birth rate.

## Infant Mortality Rate

Infant mortality rate measures deaths of babies before age one per 1000 live births.

High infant mortality indicates:

- poverty;
- malnutrition;
- poor maternal health;
- inadequate medical care;
- sanitation problems.

## Maternal Mortality Rate

Maternal mortality rate measures deaths of women due to childbirth per 100000 live births.

It reflects:

- health infrastructure;
- gender inequality;
- access to trained care;
- nutrition;
- transport and emergency services.

# Unit 2G: Sex Ratio

Sex ratio is:

`number of females per 1000 males`

In many societies, women tend to live longer than men. So a balanced or female-favouring sex ratio is common in many contexts.

## Declining Sex Ratio

A declining sex ratio is a sign of gender bias.

Possible causes:

- son preference;
- neglect of girl children;
- unequal nutrition and health care;
- sex-selective practices;
- patriarchal inheritance norms;
- dowry pressures;
- lower value attached to daughters.

## Consequences

Declining sex ratio can lead to:

- worsening gender inequality;
- increased violence and trafficking;
- imbalance in marriage patterns;
- reinforcement of patriarchal control;
- social anxiety around women and marriage.

## Policy Direction

The chapter refers to state efforts such as campaigns encouraging survival, education, and dignity of girls.

# Unit 2H: Age Structure and Dependency Ratio

Age structure refers to the proportion of people in different age groups.

Common categories:

- children below working age;
- working-age population;
- elderly population.

Dependency ratio compares dependents with working-age population.

`dependency ratio = population below 15 plus population above 64 / population aged 15 to 64`

## High Dependency Ratio

This means fewer workers support more dependents. It can pressure households and the state.

## Low Dependency Ratio

This means a larger share of working-age people. It can help economic growth if education, skills, health, and jobs are available.

This potential benefit is called demographic dividend.

## Warning

Demographic dividend is not automatic. It requires:

- employment;
- education;
- gender inclusion;
- health;
- skill development;
- economic opportunity.

# Unit 2I: Size and Growth of India's Population

The textbook chapter uses Census 2011 to discuss India as one of the world's most populous countries.

Important points:

- population growth was modest in the early twentieth century;
- 1911 to 1921 saw negative growth due to influenza epidemic and mortality;
- growth increased after death rates fell;
- population growth later began to slow as fertility declined;
- regional differences remain significant.

## Population Momentum

Even when fertility declines, population may continue growing because many people are in reproductive age groups.

This is called population momentum.

# Unit 2J: Literacy

Literacy is essential for empowerment.

It affects:

- career awareness;
- health awareness;
- political participation;
- economic opportunity;
- cultural participation;
- gender equality.

## Patterns in India

The raw chapter highlights:

- literacy has improved after independence;
- female literacy has risen faster because it began at lower levels;
- gender gap remains;
- Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes often have lower literacy levels;
- regional inequalities remain, with states such as Kerala far ahead and states such as Bihar lagging.

## Sociological Importance

Literacy inequalities reproduce inequality across generations. Illiterate parents often face difficulties supporting children's education.

# Unit 2K: Rural-Urban Differences

India has historically been largely rural, but urban share has increased.

Urbanisation is not only a numerical shift. It reflects:

- declining relative importance of agriculture;
- growth of industry and services;
- rural-to-urban migration;
- expansion of markets;
- media influence;
- changing consumption aspirations.

## Rural Push Factors

People may leave villages because of:

- lack of work;
- landlessness;
- declining common property resources;
- low agricultural income;
- caste oppression;
- lack of services.

## Urban Pull Factors

Cities attract people because of:

- employment;
- anonymity;
- education;
- health care;
- cash income;
- social mobility;
- wider networks.

## Continuing Rural Importance

Even with urbanisation, rural India remains politically and socially important because a large share of the population still lives in rural areas.

# Unit 2L: Population Policy in India

India was among the earliest countries to announce an official population policy.

The National Family Planning Programme began in 1952.

Original goals:

- slow population growth;
- promote birth control;
- improve public health;
- raise awareness.

## Emergency Period and Coercion

During the National Emergency of 1975 to 1976, coercive sterilisation created widespread opposition.

After this, the programme was renamed the National Family Welfare Programme and moved away from coercion.

## Newer Direction

The later approach connects population with:

- health;
- education;
- women's empowerment;
- child survival;
- family welfare;
- reproductive choice;
- socio-demographic development.

## Sociological Lesson

Demographic change cannot be achieved only through law or force. It depends on economic, social, cultural, and gender changes.

# Unit 2M: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Birth Rate

If a district has `20000` live births in a year and a population of `1000000`, birth rate is:

`birth rate = 20000/1000000 x 1000`

`birth rate = 20 per 1000`

## Example 2: Death Rate

If deaths in a year are `8000` and total population is `1000000`:

`death rate = 8000/1000000 x 1000`

`death rate = 8 per 1000`

Natural increase:

`20 - 8 = 12 per 1000`

## Example 3: Dependency Ratio

Population below 15 is `30 million`, above 64 is `5 million`, and age 15 to 64 is `65 million`.

Dependents:

`30 + 5 = 35 million`

Dependency ratio:

`35/65 x 100 = 53.8%`

This means roughly 54 dependents per 100 working-age persons.

## Example 4: Sex Ratio

If a region has `950000` females and `1000000` males:

`sex ratio = 950000/1000000 x 1000`

`sex ratio = 950 females per 1000 males`

## To explain demographic dividend

1. Define age structure.
2. Define dependency ratio.

5. Warn that without employment, dividend can become a crisis.

# Unit 2O: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating population only as a number. | Demography studies structure, composition, and social consequences. |
| Confusing birth rate with fertility rate. | Birth rate uses total population; fertility rate focuses on women in reproductive ages. |
| Saying death rate falls slowly in transition. | Death rate often falls faster due to health and sanitation improvements. |
| Assuming demographic dividend is automatic. | It requires productive employment and human development. |
| Treating sex ratio as purely biological. | Social norms strongly affect sex ratio. |
| Treating urbanisation as only city growth. | It also changes economy, culture, migration, and aspirations. |
| Supporting coercive population control. | Sustainable demographic change requires social welfare and rights-based policy. |
