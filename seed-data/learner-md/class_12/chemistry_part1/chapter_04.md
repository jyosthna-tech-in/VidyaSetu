# Chapter 4: The d- and f-Block Elements
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

The d-block elements are transition elements in which the differentiating electron enters a d orbital. The f-block elements are inner transition elements in which the differentiating electron enters an f orbital. These elements are important because they show variable oxidation states, coloured ions, complex formation, catalytic behaviour, magnetic properties, and alloy formation.

This chapter is concept-heavy. Strong answers connect properties to electronic configuration, atomic size, nuclear charge, unpaired electrons, and stability of oxidation states.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define transition and inner transition elements;
- write general electronic configuration of d-block and f-block elements;
- explain atomic and ionic size trends in transition series;
- explain variable oxidation states;
- connect unpaired electrons with colour and magnetic behaviour;
- explain catalytic activity of transition metals;
- explain formation of interstitial compounds and alloys;
- describe important trends in lanthanoids and actinoids;
- explain lanthanoid contraction and its consequences;
- outline preparation and oxidising behaviour of potassium dichromate and potassium permanganate.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Transition element | Element with partially filled d subshell in atom or common ion. |
| d-block element | Element in which differentiating electron enters d orbital. |
| Inner transition element | Element in which differentiating electron enters f orbital. |
| Lanthanoids | 4f series elements following lanthanum. |
| Actinoids | 5f series elements following actinium. |
| Lanthanoid contraction | Gradual decrease in atomic and ionic radii across lanthanoids. |
| Paramagnetism | Attraction by magnetic field due to unpaired electrons. |
| Diamagnetism | Weak repulsion by magnetic field due to absence of unpaired electrons. |
| Interstitial compound | Compound formed when small atoms occupy holes in metal lattice. |
| Alloy | Mixture of metals or metal with non-metal having metallic properties. |

## Quick Learning Map

| Property | Cause |
|---|---|
| Variable oxidation states | Similar energies of ns and (n-1)d electrons |
| Colour | d-d transitions in partly filled d orbitals |
| Paramagnetism | Unpaired electrons |
| Complex formation | Small size, high charge, vacant orbitals |
| Catalytic activity | Variable oxidation states and adsorption |
| Alloy formation | Similar atomic sizes |

# Unit 4A: Position and Electronic Configuration

d-block elements lie in groups 3 to 12. Their general electronic configuration is:

`(n-1)d^(1-10) ns^(0-2)`

Transition elements are those d-block elements that have incomplete d subshells in their atoms or in at least one common oxidation state.

Zinc, cadmium, and mercury are d-block elements but are not considered typical transition elements because their atoms and common ions have completely filled d subshells.

## Series of d-Block Elements

| Series | Orbitals filled | Elements |
|---|---|---|
| 3d series | 3d | Sc to Zn |
| 4d series | 4d | Y to Cd |
| 5d series | 5d | La/Hf to Hg |
| 6d series | 6d | Ac/Rf onward |

# Unit 4B: Atomic and Ionic Size Trends

Across a transition series, atomic size decreases at first because nuclear charge increases. Later, the decrease becomes small because added d electrons shield poorly but also repel each other. The result is a gradual and less regular size trend than in s-block and p-block.

## Important Consequence

Elements of the 4d and 5d series often have similar sizes because lanthanoid contraction reduces the size of 5d elements. For example, zirconium and hafnium have very similar radii and properties.

# Unit 4C: Ionisation Enthalpy

Ionisation enthalpy generally increases across a transition series, but the trend is irregular because of:

- shielding by d electrons;
- exchange energy stability;
- half-filled and fully filled subshell effects;
- participation of both ns and (n-1)d electrons.

Transition elements often have high ionisation enthalpies compared with s-block metals, so they are less electropositive.

# Unit 4D: Oxidation States

Transition elements show variable oxidation states because ns and (n-1)d electrons have comparable energies and can both participate in bonding.

Example for manganese:

`Mn` can show `+2`, `+3`, `+4`, `+6`, and `+7` oxidation states.

## Stability Patterns

| Pattern | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Early transition metals show high oxidation states | They have more available empty or partly filled d orbitals. |
| Middle elements show maximum variability | More d electrons can participate in bonding. |
| Later elements favour lower states | Increasing nuclear charge holds d electrons more strongly. |
| `+2` state common in 3d series | Loss of two ns electrons is common. |

## Oxidising Power

High oxidation states often make compounds good oxidising agents. For example, `MnO4-` contains manganese in `+7` state and is a strong oxidising agent.

# Unit 4E: Magnetic Properties

Magnetic behaviour depends on unpaired electrons.

Paramagnetic substances have unpaired electrons and are attracted by magnetic field. Diamagnetic substances have no unpaired electrons and are weakly repelled.

Magnetic moment:

`mu = sqrt(n(n+2)) BM`

where `n` is number of unpaired electrons.

## Quick Examples

| Ion | d-electron count | Unpaired electrons | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Sc3+` | d0 | 0 | Diamagnetic |
| `Ti3+` | d1 | 1 | Paramagnetic |
| `Mn2+` | d5 | 5 | Strongly paramagnetic |
| `Zn2+` | d10 | 0 | Diamagnetic |

# Unit 4F: Colour of Transition Metal Ions

Many transition metal ions are coloured because electrons can jump between split d orbitals by absorbing visible light. The observed colour is complementary to the absorbed colour.

Colour usually requires partially filled d orbitals. Ions with d0 or d10 configuration are generally colourless.

Examples:

| Ion | Configuration idea | Colour tendency |
|---|---|---|
| `Ti3+` | d1 | Coloured |
| `Cu2+` | d9 | Coloured |
| `Sc3+` | d0 | Colourless |
| `Zn2+` | d10 | Colourless |

# Unit 4G: Complex Formation

Transition metals form complexes readily because they often have:

- small ionic size;
- high positive charge density;
- vacant orbitals;
- variable oxidation states;
- ability to accept electron pairs from ligands.

Examples include:

- `[Fe(CN)6]4-`
- `[Cu(NH3)4]2+`
- `[Co(NH3)6]3+`

# Unit 4H: Catalytic Properties

Transition metals and their compounds are good catalysts because they can:

- show variable oxidation states;
- adsorb reactants on their surface;
- form intermediate compounds;
- provide a lower-energy reaction pathway.

Examples:

| Catalyst | Process |
|---|---|
| Iron | Haber process |
| Vanadium(V) oxide | Contact process |
| Nickel | Hydrogenation |
| Platinum | Catalytic converters |

# Unit 4I: Interstitial Compounds

Small atoms such as hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen, and boron can occupy interstitial spaces in transition metal lattices.

Properties:

- very hard;
- high melting points;
- retain metallic conductivity;
- often chemically inert;
- non-stoichiometric composition.

Examples include transition metal carbides and nitrides.

# Unit 4J: Alloy Formation

Transition metals form alloys because their atomic sizes are similar. One metal atom can replace another in the crystal lattice without much distortion.

Examples:

- brass: copper and zinc;
- bronze: copper and tin;
- steel: iron with carbon and other elements;
- stainless steel: iron, chromium, nickel, and carbon.

# Unit 4K: Potassium Dichromate

Potassium dichromate, `K2Cr2O7`, is an important oxidising agent. Chromium is in `+6` oxidation state.

## Chromate-Dichromate Equilibrium

In acidic medium:

`2CrO4^2- + 2H+ -> Cr2O7^2- + H2O`

In basic medium:

`Cr2O7^2- + 2OH- -> 2CrO4^2- + H2O`

Chromate ion is yellow; dichromate ion is orange.

## Oxidising Action in Acidic Medium

Dichromate is reduced from chromium(VI) to chromium(III):

`Cr2O7^2- + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr3+ + 7H2O`

It oxidises:

- ferrous ion to ferric ion;
- iodide to iodine;
- sulphite to sulphate.

# Unit 4L: Potassium Permanganate

Potassium permanganate, `KMnO4`, is a powerful oxidising agent. Manganese is in `+7` oxidation state.

## Reduction in Different Media

| Medium | Product | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Acidic | `Mn2+` | Strong oxidation |
| Neutral | `MnO2` | Brown precipitate |
| Alkaline | `MnO4^2-` | Green manganate |

In acidic medium:

`MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn2+ + 4H2O`

## Uses

- redox titrations;
- oxidising agent in laboratory reactions;
- disinfectant in dilute form.

# Unit 4M: Lanthanoids

Lanthanoids involve filling of 4f orbitals. Their common oxidation state is `+3`. Some show `+2` or `+4` when these states give stable f configurations.

## Lanthanoid Contraction

Lanthanoid contraction is the steady decrease in atomic and ionic radii across the lanthanoid series.

Cause: 4f electrons shield the nuclear charge poorly, so effective nuclear charge increases across the series.

## Consequences

| Consequence | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Similarity of lanthanoids | Size changes are small, making separation difficult. |
| Similarity of 4d and 5d elements | 5d elements become smaller than expected. |
| Basicity of hydroxides decreases | Smaller ions hold hydroxide more strongly. |
| Zirconium and hafnium similarity | Nearly identical sizes due to contraction. |

# Unit 4N: Actinoids

Actinoids involve filling of 5f orbitals. They show more variable oxidation states than lanthanoids because 5f, 6d, and 7s orbitals have comparable energies.

Important features:

- many are radioactive;
- oxidation states are variable;
- compounds are often coloured;
- ions are often paramagnetic;
- elements after uranium are called transuranium elements.

# Unit 4O: Comparison of Lanthanoids and Actinoids

| Feature | Lanthanoids | Actinoids |
|---|---|---|
| Orbital filled | 4f | 5f |
| Common oxidation state | `+3` | `+3`, but more variable |
| Radioactivity | Mostly non-radioactive except promethium | Mostly radioactive |
| Contraction | Lanthanoid contraction | Actinoid contraction |
| Chemical behaviour | More similar | More complex |
| Bonding | Mostly ionic | More covalent character possible |

# Unit 4Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling all d-block elements transition elements | Zn, Cd, and Hg are not typical transition elements. |
| Explaining colour without d orbitals | Colour usually comes from d-d transitions. |
| Forgetting that d0 and d10 ions are often colourless | No d-d transition is possible. |
| Saying lanthanoid contraction is due to poor shielding by d electrons | It is due to poor shielding by 4f electrons. |
| Treating all actinoids like lanthanoids | Actinoids show greater oxidation-state variability. |
| Confusing chromate and dichromate colours | Chromate is yellow; dichromate is orange. |

## Final Revision

- Transition properties arise mainly from incomplete d orbitals.
- Variable oxidation states come from comparable ns and d orbital energies.
- Colour and magnetism depend on d-electron arrangement.
- Lanthanoid contraction is caused by poor shielding by 4f electrons.
- Actinoids show more variable oxidation states than lanthanoids.
- `K2Cr2O7` and `KMnO4` are important oxidising agents.
