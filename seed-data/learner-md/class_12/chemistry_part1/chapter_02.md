# Chapter 2: Electrochemistry
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

Electrochemistry studies the connection between chemical change and electrical energy. Some reactions produce electricity spontaneously, as in galvanic cells. Other reactions need electricity to occur, as in electrolytic cells. The chapter also covers electrode potential, Nernst equation, conductivity, electrolysis, batteries, fuel cells, and corrosion.

The central idea is electron transfer. If a redox reaction can push electrons through an external circuit, it can work as a cell. If an external power supply pushes electrons against the natural direction, the same chemical system may work as an electrolytic cell.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- distinguish galvanic cells from electrolytic cells;
- write cell notation for redox reactions;
- calculate cell potential from standard electrode potentials;
- use Nernst equation for non-standard concentrations;
- connect cell potential with Gibbs energy and equilibrium constant;
- define resistance, conductance, resistivity, conductivity, and molar conductivity;
- explain variation of conductivity and molar conductivity with concentration;
- apply Kohlrausch's law;
- solve quantitative electrolysis problems using Faraday's laws;
- explain working of common batteries and fuel cells;
- explain rusting as an electrochemical process.

# Unit 2A: Galvanic and Electrolytic Cells

A galvanic cell uses a spontaneous redox reaction to produce electrical energy. A classic example is the Daniell cell, based on zinc and copper electrodes.

Oxidation half reaction:

`Zn(s) -> Zn2+(aq) + 2e-`

Reduction half reaction:

`Cu2+(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)`

Overall reaction:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

The electrons move from zinc electrode to copper electrode through the external circuit.

## Galvanic Cell Features

| Feature | Galvanic cell |
|---|---|
| Energy conversion | Chemical to electrical |
| Reaction | Spontaneous |
| Anode sign | Negative |
| Cathode sign | Positive |
| Electron flow | Anode to cathode through wire |
| Salt bridge | Maintains electrical neutrality |

## Electrolytic Cell Features

| Feature | Electrolytic cell |
|---|---|
| Energy conversion | Electrical to chemical |
| Reaction | Non-spontaneous |
| Anode sign | Positive |
| Cathode sign | Negative |
| Electron supply | External source |
| Use | Electrolysis, electroplating, extraction |

Remember: oxidation is always at anode and reduction is always at cathode. The signs change between galvanic and electrolytic cells, but the definitions do not.

# Unit 2B: Cell Notation

Cell notation writes an electrochemical cell in compact form.

For Daniell cell:

`Zn(s) | Zn2+(aq) || Cu2+(aq) | Cu(s)`

Rules:

- anode is written on the left;
- cathode is written on the right;
- single vertical line means phase boundary;
- double vertical line means salt bridge;
- concentrations may be written in parentheses.

## Reading Cell Notation

For:

`M(s) | M2+(aq) || X+(aq) | X(s)`

Left side shows oxidation of `M`; right side shows reduction of `X+`.

# Unit 2C: Electrode Potential

Electrode potential is the tendency of an electrode to gain or lose electrons when it is in contact with its ions. A more positive reduction potential means stronger tendency to get reduced.

The standard hydrogen electrode is assigned a potential of zero. Other electrode potentials are measured relative to it.

## Cell Potential

`Ecell = Ecathode - Eanode`

When using standard potentials:

`E0cell = E0cathode - E0anode`

If `Ecell` is positive, the cell reaction is spontaneous in the written direction.

# Unit 2D: Nernst Equation

The standard cell potential applies only under standard conditions. Actual concentration changes the cell potential. Nernst equation corrects this.

General form:

`Ecell = E0cell - (R T / n F) ln Q`

At 298 K:

`Ecell = E0cell - (0.0591 / n) log Q`

Here:

- `n` is number of electrons transferred;
- `Q` is reaction quotient;
- `F` is Faraday constant;
- `R` is gas constant;
- `T` is temperature in kelvin.

## For Daniell Cell

Reaction:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

Reaction quotient:

`Q = [Zn2+] / [Cu2+]`

Nernst equation:

`Ecell = E0cell - (0.0591 / 2) log ([Zn2+] / [Cu2+])`

# Unit 2E: Cell Potential, Gibbs Energy, and Equilibrium

Electrical work and Gibbs energy are connected:

`Delta G = -n F Ecell`

Under standard conditions:

`Delta G0 = -n F E0cell`

At equilibrium, `Ecell = 0` and `Q = K`.

At 298 K:

`E0cell = (0.0591 / n) log K`

## Interpretation

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `Ecell > 0` | Spontaneous cell reaction. |
| `Ecell = 0` | Equilibrium. |
| `Ecell < 0` | Non-spontaneous in written direction. |
| Large `K` | Products strongly favoured. |
| Positive `E0cell` | Negative `Delta G0`. |

# Unit 2F: Conductance of Electrolytic Solutions

Metallic conduction happens through electrons. Electrolytic conduction happens through movement of ions.

Resistance:

`R = rho l / A`

Conductance:

`G = 1 / R`

Conductivity:

`kappa = 1 / rho`

In a conductivity cell:

`kappa = G x (l/A)`

The term `l/A` is called cell constant.

## Molar Conductivity

Molar conductivity is:

`Lambda_m = kappa x 1000 / C`

where `C` is concentration in `mol L^-1`.

## Effect of Dilution

Conductivity decreases on dilution because number of ions per unit volume decreases.

Molar conductivity increases on dilution because ions face less interionic attraction and move more freely.

# Unit 2G: Strong and Weak Electrolytes

## Strong Electrolytes

Strong electrolytes are almost completely ionised. Their molar conductivity increases slowly on dilution. The graph of `Lambda_m` against `sqrt(C)` is nearly linear.

`Lambda_m = Lambda_m0 - A sqrt(C)`

## Weak Electrolytes

Weak electrolytes ionise only partly. Their molar conductivity increases sharply on dilution because degree of ionisation increases.

For weak electrolytes, limiting molar conductivity is found using Kohlrausch's law, not by direct extrapolation.

# Unit 2H: Kohlrausch's Law

Kohlrausch's law states that at infinite dilution, each ion makes an independent contribution to total molar conductivity.

For `NaCl`:

`Lambda_m0(NaCl) = lambda0(Na+) + lambda0(Cl-)`

For acetic acid:

`Lambda_m0(CH3COOH) = Lambda_m0(CH3COONa) + Lambda_m0(HCl) - Lambda_m0(NaCl)`

## Uses

- finding limiting molar conductivity of weak electrolytes;
- calculating degree of dissociation;
- calculating dissociation constant;
- comparing ionic conductance.

# Unit 2I: Electrolysis

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to bring about chemical change. The amount of substance produced depends on charge passed.

Charge:

`Q = I t`

Moles of electrons:

`moles e- = Q / F`

Mass deposited:

`mass = (atomic mass x I x t) / (n x F)`

where `n` is number of electrons required per ion.

## Faraday's Laws

First law: mass deposited is proportional to quantity of electricity passed.

Second law: for same charge, masses deposited are proportional to chemical equivalents.

# Unit 2J: Products of Electrolysis

Products depend on:

- nature of electrolyte;
- electrode material;
- concentration of ions;
- standard electrode potentials;
- overpotential.

Example: electrolysis of aqueous sodium chloride does not simply produce sodium metal because water is easier to reduce than sodium ion.

# Unit 2K: Batteries

A battery is a cell or collection of cells used as a practical electrical energy source.

## Primary Batteries

Primary batteries cannot be recharged efficiently. The reaction is not easily reversible.

Example: dry cell.

## Secondary Batteries

Secondary batteries can be recharged because the cell reaction can be reversed by external current.

Example: lead storage battery.

During discharge in lead storage battery:

- lead acts as anode;
- lead dioxide acts as cathode;
- sulphuric acid is consumed;
- lead sulphate forms at both electrodes.

# Unit 2L: Fuel Cells

Fuel cells convert energy of fuel directly into electrical energy. In a hydrogen-oxygen fuel cell:

Anode:

`2H2 + 4OH- -> 4H2O + 4e-`

Cathode:

`O2 + 2H2O + 4e- -> 4OH-`

Overall:

`2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O`

Advantages include high efficiency and low pollution when hydrogen is cleanly produced.

# Unit 2M: Corrosion

Corrosion is an electrochemical process. Rusting of iron occurs when iron is exposed to oxygen and moisture.

Anodic region:

`Fe -> Fe2+ + 2e-`

Cathodic region:

`O2 + 4H+ + 4e- -> 2H2O`

Further oxidation and hydration form rust, commonly represented as hydrated ferric oxide.

## Prevention of Corrosion

| Method | Idea |
|---|---|
| Painting | Blocks contact with air and moisture. |
| Galvanisation | Zinc protects iron by acting as sacrificial metal. |
| Cathodic protection | Iron is made cathode so it does not oxidise. |
| Alloying | Stainless steel resists corrosion better. |
| Oiling or greasing | Prevents direct contact with moisture and oxygen. |

# Unit 2N: Problem-Solving Toolkit

# Unit 2O: High-Quality Answer Framework

For a cell-potential answer:

1. Write oxidation and reduction half reactions.
2. Identify anode and cathode.
3. Write cell notation.
4. Use `Ecell = Ecathode - Eanode`.
5. Interpret sign of `Ecell`.

For an electrolysis answer:

1. Write ion discharged at electrode.
2. Write electrode reaction.
3. Find charge using `Q = It`.
4. Convert charge into moles of electrons.
5. Use stoichiometry to find product amount.

# Unit 2P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Thinking anode is always positive | Anode is oxidation; sign depends on cell type. |
| Reversing cell potential formula | Use cathode minus anode for reduction potentials. |
| Forgetting to balance electrons in Nernst equation | `n` must come from balanced redox reaction. |
| Using concentration of solids in `Q` | Pure solids and liquids are not included in `Q`. |
| Confusing conductivity and molar conductivity | Conductivity is per unit volume; molar conductivity is per mole. |
| Forgetting time conversion in electrolysis | Use seconds in `Q = It`. |

## Final Revision

- Oxidation occurs at anode; reduction occurs at cathode.
- Galvanic cells are spontaneous; electrolytic cells are driven by external current.
- Positive `Ecell` means spontaneous reaction in written direction.
- Nernst equation adjusts potential for actual concentration.
- Conductivity decreases on dilution; molar conductivity increases on dilution.
- Faraday's laws connect electric charge with chemical amount.
- Corrosion is local electrochemical oxidation of metal.
