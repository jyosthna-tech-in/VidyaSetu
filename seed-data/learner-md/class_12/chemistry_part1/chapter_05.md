# Chapter 5: Coordination Compounds
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

Coordination compounds contain a central metal atom or ion bonded to surrounding ions or molecules called ligands. These compounds are important in qualitative analysis, metallurgy, biological systems, medicines, dyes, and industrial catalysis.

This chapter requires careful language. Terms such as ligand, coordination number, oxidation number, coordination sphere, counter ion, denticity, chelation, isomerism, hybridisation, and crystal field splitting must be used precisely.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define coordination entity, ligand, coordination number, and coordination sphere;
- identify central metal ion, ligands, counter ions, charge, and oxidation number;
- classify ligands by charge and denticity;
- name coordination compounds using IUPAC rules;
- write formulas from names and names from formulas;
- explain Werner's theory;
- identify structural and stereoisomerism in coordination compounds;
- explain bonding using valence bond theory;
- explain crystal field splitting in octahedral and tetrahedral fields;
- connect crystal field theory with colour and magnetic behaviour;
- describe applications of coordination compounds.

# Unit 5A: Coordination Entity

A coordination entity consists of a central metal atom or ion bonded to a fixed number of ligands.

Example:

`[Co(NH3)6]Cl3`

Coordination entity:

`[Co(NH3)6]3+`

Counter ions:

`3Cl-`

Central metal ion:

`Co3+`

Ligands:

`NH3`

Coordination number:

`6`

# Unit 5B: Werner's Theory

Werner explained coordination compounds using two types of valencies.

## Primary Valency

Primary valency corresponds to oxidation state. It is ionisable and is satisfied by negative ions.

## Secondary Valency

Secondary valency corresponds to coordination number. It is non-ionisable and is satisfied by ligands directly attached to metal.

## Example Series

For cobalt ammine chlorides:

| Compound | Ionisable chloride ions | Coordination sphere idea |
|---|---|---|
| `[Co(NH3)6]Cl3` | 3 | All six `NH3` inside sphere |
| `[Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2` | 2 | One `Cl-` inside sphere |
| `[Co(NH3)4Cl2]Cl` | 1 | Two `Cl-` inside sphere |
| `[Co(NH3)3Cl3]` | 0 | Three `Cl-` inside sphere |

This explains why compounds with same overall elements can give different numbers of ions in solution.

# Unit 5C: Ligands and Denticity

Ligands donate electron pairs to the metal ion. The donor atom is the atom directly bonded to metal.

## Classification by Charge

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Neutral ligands | `NH3`, `H2O`, `CO`, `NO` |
| Negative ligands | `Cl-`, `CN-`, `OH-`, `NO2-` |
| Positive ligands | `NO+` in special cases |

## Classification by Denticity

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Monodentate | One donor atom | `Cl-`, `NH3`, `H2O` |
| Bidentate | Two donor atoms | ethane-1,2-diamine |
| Polydentate | Many donor atoms | EDTA |

## Ambidentate Ligands

Ambidentate ligands can bind through either of two donor atoms.

Examples:

- `NO2-` can bind through N or O;
- `SCN-` can bind through S or N.

# Unit 5D: Chelation

Chelation occurs when a polydentate ligand forms a ring with the central metal ion. Chelate complexes are often more stable than similar complexes with monodentate ligands.

Example:

Ethane-1,2-diamine binds through two nitrogen atoms and forms a ring.

EDTA is a powerful chelating ligand used to bind metal ions.

# Unit 5E: Coordination Number and Shape

Coordination number is the number of donor atoms directly attached to the central metal.

Common coordination numbers:

| Coordination number | Common shape |
|---|---|
| 2 | Linear |
| 4 | Tetrahedral or square planar |
| 6 | Octahedral |

Examples:

- `[Ag(NH3)2]+` is linear;
- `[Ni(CO)4]` is tetrahedral;
- `[Ni(CN)4]2-` is square planar;
- `[Co(NH3)6]3+` is octahedral.

# Unit 5F: Oxidation Number in Complexes

To find oxidation number:

1. Assign known ligand charges.
2. Let metal oxidation number be `x`.
3. Add metal charge and ligand charges.
4. Equate to charge on coordination entity.

Example:

`[Fe(CN)6]4-`

Each `CN-` has charge `-1`.

`x + 6(-1) = -4`

`x = +2`

So iron is in `+2` oxidation state.

# Unit 5G: Naming Coordination Compounds

## Basic Naming Order

1. Name cation before anion.
2. In coordination sphere, name ligands before metal.
3. Ligands are named alphabetically.
4. Use prefixes di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa for number of simple ligands.
5. Put oxidation state of metal in Roman numeral.
6. If complex ion is anionic, metal name ends with `-ate`.

## Common Ligand Names

| Formula | Name in complex |
|---|---|
| `NH3` | ammine |
| `H2O` | aqua |
| `CO` | carbonyl |
| `NO` | nitrosyl |
| `Cl-` | chlorido |
| `CN-` | cyanido |
| `OH-` | hydroxido |
| `NO2-` | nitrito or nitro depending on donor atom |

## Examples

`[Co(NH3)6]Cl3`

Name: hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride.

`K4[Fe(CN)6]`

Name: potassium hexacyanidoferrate(II).

`[Pt(NH3)2Cl2]`

Name: diamminedichloridoplatinum(II).

# Unit 5H: Isomerism in Coordination Compounds

Isomers have same formula but different arrangements.

## Structural Isomerism

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Ionisation isomerism | Different ion produced in solution. |
| Solvate or hydrate isomerism | Solvent molecule inside or outside coordination sphere differs. |
| Linkage isomerism | Ambidentate ligand attaches through different donor atoms. |
| Coordination isomerism | Exchange of ligands between cationic and anionic complex ions. |

## Stereoisomerism

Stereoisomers have same bonds but different spatial arrangement.

### Geometrical Isomerism

In square planar and octahedral complexes:

- cis means similar ligands are adjacent;
- trans means similar ligands are opposite.

### Optical Isomerism

Optical isomers are non-superimposable mirror images. They rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions.

# Unit 5I: Valence Bond Theory

Valence bond theory explains bonding in coordination compounds through hybridisation of metal orbitals.

Ligands donate electron pairs into vacant hybrid orbitals of metal ion.

## Inner and Outer Orbital Complexes

| Type | Feature |
|---|---|
| Inner orbital complex | Uses inner `(n-1)d` orbitals; often low spin. |
| Outer orbital complex | Uses outer `nd` orbitals; often high spin. |

## Common Hybridisations

| Coordination number | Shape | Hybridisation |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | Linear | `sp` |
| 4 | Tetrahedral | `sp3` |
| 4 | Square planar | `dsp2` |
| 6 | Octahedral inner orbital | `d2sp3` |
| 6 | Octahedral outer orbital | `sp3d2` |

VBT explains shape and magnetic behaviour, but does not explain colour well. Crystal field theory handles colour better.

# Unit 5J: Crystal Field Theory

Crystal field theory treats ligands as point charges or dipoles that interact with metal d orbitals. In a free metal ion, the five d orbitals have equal energy. In a ligand field, they split into groups of different energy.

## Octahedral Splitting

In octahedral complexes:

- `dxy`, `dyz`, and `dxz` form lower-energy `t2g` set;
- `dx2-y2` and `dz2` form higher-energy `eg` set.

Crystal field splitting energy is written as `Delta_o`.

## Tetrahedral Splitting

In tetrahedral complexes:

- splitting is smaller than octahedral splitting;
- tetrahedral complexes are usually high spin.

## Strong and Weak Field Ligands

Strong field ligands cause large splitting and may force electron pairing. Weak field ligands cause small splitting and usually give high-spin complexes.

| Weak field ligands | Strong field ligands |
|---|---|
| `I-`, `Br-`, `Cl-`, `F-`, `H2O` | `NH3`, `en`, `NO2-`, `CN-`, `CO` |

# Unit 5K: Colour in Coordination Compounds

Colour arises when an electron absorbs visible light and jumps from lower-energy d orbitals to higher-energy d orbitals. The colour seen is complementary to the colour absorbed.

Factors affecting colour:

- metal ion;
- oxidation state;
- ligand strength;
- geometry;
- number of d electrons.

Complexes with d0 or d10 configuration are often colourless because d-d transition is not possible.

# Unit 5L: Stability of Complexes

Stability depends on:

- charge density of metal ion;
- ligand strength;
- chelation;
- size of chelate ring;
- oxidation state of metal;
- solvent and temperature.

Chelating ligands generally form more stable complexes because multiple bonds must be broken to remove the ligand.

# Unit 5O: High-Quality Example

Question: Find oxidation state and coordination number of cobalt in `[Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2`.

Answer:

The coordination entity is `[Co(NH3)5Cl]2+` because two chloride ions are outside the coordination sphere. Let cobalt oxidation state be `x`. Ammonia is neutral and chlorido ligand has charge `-1`.

`x + 5(0) + (-1) = +2`

So, `x = +3`.

The coordination number is 6 because five nitrogen donor atoms from ammonia and one chloride donor atom are directly attached to cobalt.

# Unit 5P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Counting counter ions in coordination number | Count only donor atoms inside coordination sphere. |
| Treating ligand count and coordination number as always same | Polydentate ligands can give more donor atoms than ligand molecules. |
| Forgetting charge of ligands | Use ligand charges before solving oxidation state. |
| Naming metal before ligands inside sphere | Ligands are named before metal. |
| Using normal metal name for anionic complex | Use `-ate` form for anionic complex. |
| Calling every isomer cis-trans | Identify exact type: ionisation, linkage, hydrate, geometrical, or optical. |

## Final Revision

- Coordination sphere is the part inside square brackets.
- Coordination number counts donor atoms, not counter ions.
- Ligands donate electron pairs to metal.
- Oxidation number comes from ligand charges and complex charge.
- Naming requires ligands first, then metal, then oxidation state.
- Isomerism is a major scoring area.
- VBT explains shape and magnetism; CFT explains splitting and colour.
- Chelation increases stability.
