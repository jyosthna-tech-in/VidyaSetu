# Chapter 1: Solutions
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures in which one or more solutes are uniformly dispersed in a solvent. This chapter connects concentration terms, solubility, vapour pressure, ideal and non-ideal behaviour, colligative properties, and abnormal molar mass into one continuous idea: the properties of a solution depend on the number, nature, and interactions of its particles.

The most important exam skill in this chapter is choosing the correct expression. Many questions look similar, but one may require mole fraction, another molality, another Raoult's law, and another van't Hoff factor. A strong answer always begins by identifying what property is being measured and what particles are actually present in solution.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define solution, solute, solvent, binary solution, and homogeneous mixture;
- classify solutions using physical state of solute and solvent;
- express concentration as mass percentage, volume percentage, mass by volume percentage, ppm, mole fraction, molarity, and molality;
- explain how temperature and pressure affect solubility of solids and gases;
- apply Henry's law to gas solubility;
- apply Raoult's law to volatile and non-volatile solutes;
- distinguish ideal solutions from non-ideal solutions;
- explain positive and negative deviations from Raoult's law;
- use colligative properties to calculate molar mass;
- explain osmosis, osmotic pressure, reverse osmosis, and isotonic solutions;
- interpret association, dissociation, abnormal molar mass, and van't Hoff factor.

# Unit 1A: What Is a Solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture. Homogeneous means the composition is uniform throughout the sample. If a spoonful is taken from the top, middle, or bottom of a properly mixed sugar solution, the composition should be the same.

The component present in larger amount is usually called the solvent. The component present in smaller amount is usually called the solute. This rule is convenient, but not absolute. In aqueous solutions, water is generally treated as the solvent even if another component is also present in large amount.

## Types of Solutions by Physical State

| Solute | Solvent | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Gas | Gas | Air, mainly nitrogen and oxygen |
| Gas | Liquid | Carbon dioxide in soft drinks |
| Gas | Solid | Hydrogen in palladium |
| Liquid | Gas | Water vapour in air |
| Liquid | Liquid | Ethanol in water |
| Liquid | Solid | Amalgam of mercury with metal |
| Solid | Gas | Camphor vapour in air |
| Solid | Liquid | Salt in water |
| Solid | Solid | Alloys such as brass |

The most common school-level examples are solid in liquid, liquid in liquid, and gas in liquid solutions.

# Unit 1B: Concentration Terms

Concentration tells how much solute is present. Different concentration terms are used because different experiments measure different quantities: mass, volume, moles, or very small traces.

## Mass Percentage

Mass percentage is the mass of solute in 100 parts by mass of solution.

`mass percent = (mass of solute / mass of solution) x 100`

Use this when both solute and solution are given by mass.

## Volume Percentage

Volume percentage is the volume of solute in 100 parts by volume of solution.

`volume percent = (volume of solute / volume of solution) x 100`

This is common for liquid-liquid solutions such as alcohol-water mixtures.

## Mass by Volume Percentage

Mass by volume percentage is mass of solute present in 100 mL of solution.

`mass by volume percent = (mass of solute in g / volume of solution in mL) x 100`

This is common in medicine and laboratory solutions.

## Parts Per Million

Parts per million is used for very dilute solutions.

`ppm = (amount of component / total amount of solution) x 10^6`

It is useful for pollutants, dissolved ions in water, or trace impurities.

## Mole Fraction

Mole fraction of a component is:

`xA = moles of A / total moles of all components`

For a binary solution containing A and B:

`xA + xB = 1`

Mole fraction has no unit. It is especially important in Raoult's law and Henry's law.

## Molarity

Molarity is moles of solute per litre of solution.

`M = moles of solute / volume of solution in L`

Molarity changes with temperature because volume changes with temperature.

## Molality

Molality is moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

`m = moles of solute / mass of solvent in kg`

Molality does not change with temperature because it is based on mass, not volume.

## Molarity vs Molality

| Basis | Molarity | Molality |
|---|---|---|
| Formula | `moles solute / L solution` | `moles solute / kg solvent` |
| Depends on | Volume of solution | Mass of solvent |
| Temperature effect | Changes with temperature | Independent of temperature |
| Symbol | `M` | `m` |
| Common use | Titration, solution preparation | Colligative properties |

# Unit 1C: Solubility

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a particular temperature and pressure. A saturated solution contains the maximum dissolved solute under those conditions.

## Solubility of Solids in Liquids

For most solids, solubility increases with temperature because dissolution is often endothermic. If heat is absorbed during dissolution, increasing temperature favours dissolution.

For some solids, solubility decreases with temperature because dissolution is exothermic. The temperature effect must therefore be judged from the heat change of dissolution.

## Solubility of Gases in Liquids

Gas solubility usually decreases with rise in temperature. This is why warm soft drinks lose fizz more quickly and why aquatic life is affected by thermal pollution.

Gas solubility increases with pressure. When pressure above the liquid is high, more gas molecules are forced into solution.

# Unit 1D: Henry's Law

Henry's law states that at constant temperature, the partial pressure of a gas above a solution is proportional to the mole fraction of the gas in the solution.

`p = KH x`

Here:

- `p` is partial pressure of the gas;
- `x` is mole fraction of the gas in solution;
- `KH` is Henry's law constant.

## Interpreting `KH`

A higher value of `KH` means lower solubility. Since `p = KH x`, for the same pressure a larger `KH` gives a smaller mole fraction of dissolved gas.

## Applications of Henry's Law

| Situation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Soft drinks | Carbon dioxide is dissolved under high pressure. |
| Deep-sea diving | High pressure dissolves more nitrogen in blood; sudden ascent can release bubbles. |
| High altitude | Lower oxygen partial pressure reduces oxygen dissolved in blood. |
| Aquatic life | Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen. |

# Unit 1E: Vapour Pressure of Liquid Solutions

Vapour pressure is the pressure exerted by vapour in equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature. A liquid with higher escaping tendency has higher vapour pressure.

When a non-volatile solute is added to a solvent, fewer solvent molecules occupy the surface. This reduces the escaping tendency of solvent molecules, so vapour pressure decreases.

# Unit 1F: Raoult's Law

For a solution of volatile liquids, Raoult's law states:

`pA = xA pA0`

`pB = xB pB0`

Total vapour pressure is:

`ptotal = pA + pB`

Here `pA0` and `pB0` are vapour pressures of pure A and pure B.

For a non-volatile solute in a volatile solvent:

`p = xsolvent p0`

Lowering of vapour pressure:

`Delta p = p0 - p`

Relative lowering of vapour pressure:

`Delta p / p0 = xsolute`

For dilute solutions:

`Delta p / p0 = nsolute / nsolvent`

# Unit 1G: Ideal and Non-Ideal Solutions

## Ideal Solutions

An ideal solution obeys Raoult's law at all compositions and temperatures. In ideal solutions:

- solute-solvent interactions are nearly equal to solute-solute and solvent-solvent interactions;
- enthalpy of mixing is zero: `Delta Hmix = 0`;
- volume change on mixing is zero: `Delta Vmix = 0`.

Examples are benzene and toluene, or n-hexane and n-heptane.

## Positive Deviation

Positive deviation occurs when observed vapour pressure is greater than predicted by Raoult's law. This means A-B interactions are weaker than A-A and B-B interactions. Molecules escape more easily.

Examples include ethanol and acetone.

## Negative Deviation

Negative deviation occurs when observed vapour pressure is lower than predicted by Raoult's law. This means A-B interactions are stronger than A-A and B-B interactions. Molecules escape less easily.

Examples include chloroform and acetone.

## Deviation Table

| Feature | Positive deviation | Negative deviation |
|---|---|---|
| A-B attraction | Weaker | Stronger |
| Vapour pressure | Higher than ideal | Lower than ideal |
| Enthalpy of mixing | Positive | Negative |
| Volume change | Positive | Negative |
| Boiling tendency | Lower boiling azeotrope may form | Higher boiling azeotrope may form |

# Unit 1H: Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles, not on their chemical nature. They are especially useful for calculating molar mass of solutes.

The four colligative properties are:

1. relative lowering of vapour pressure;
2. elevation of boiling point;
3. depression of freezing point;
4. osmotic pressure.

# Unit 1I: Elevation of Boiling Point

A liquid boils when its vapour pressure becomes equal to external pressure. Since a non-volatile solute lowers vapour pressure, the solution must be heated to a higher temperature to boil.

`Delta Tb = Kb m`

Here:

- `Delta Tb` is elevation in boiling point;
- `Kb` is molal elevation constant;
- `m` is molality.

For molar mass:

`M2 = (Kb x w2 x 1000) / (Delta Tb x w1)`

where `w2` is mass of solute and `w1` is mass of solvent in grams.

# Unit 1J: Depression of Freezing Point

Freezing point decreases when a non-volatile solute is added. The solution freezes at a lower temperature than the pure solvent.

`Delta Tf = Kf m`

For molar mass:

`M2 = (Kf x w2 x 1000) / (Delta Tf x w1)`

Applications include antifreeze mixtures and salt used to melt ice.

# Unit 1K: Osmosis and Osmotic Pressure

Osmosis is the movement of solvent through a semipermeable membrane from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution. The pressure required to stop osmosis is osmotic pressure.

For dilute solutions:

`pi = C R T`

Since `C = n/V`:

`pi V = n R T`

For molar mass:

`M2 = (w2 R T) / (pi V)`

Osmotic pressure is very useful for biomolecules because it can be measured at room temperature and needs only dilute solutions.

## Important Osmosis Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Isotonic solutions | Same osmotic pressure at same temperature. |
| Hypotonic solution | Lower osmotic pressure than another solution. |
| Hypertonic solution | Higher osmotic pressure than another solution. |
| Reverse osmosis | Solvent forced from solution to pure solvent side by applying pressure greater than osmotic pressure. |

# Unit 1L: Abnormal Molar Mass

If solute particles associate or dissociate, the number of particles changes. Since colligative properties depend on particle number, calculated molar mass becomes abnormal.

## Association

Association means particles combine. Number of particles decreases. Observed colligative property becomes smaller. Calculated molar mass becomes larger.

Example: acetic acid in benzene forms dimers.

## Dissociation

Dissociation means particles split into ions. Number of particles increases. Observed colligative property becomes larger. Calculated molar mass becomes smaller.

Example: sodium chloride dissociates into `Na+` and `Cl-`.

## van't Hoff Factor

`i = normal molar mass / abnormal molar mass`

Also:

`i = observed colligative property / calculated colligative property`

Corrected relations:

- `Delta Tb = i Kb m`
- `Delta Tf = i Kf m`
- `pi = i C R T`
- `Delta p / p0 = i xsolute`

## Reading `i`

| Value of `i` | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `i = 1` | No association or dissociation. |
| `i < 1` | Association. |
| `i > 1` | Dissociation. |

# Unit 1N: Worked Pattern Examples

## Pattern 1: Finding Mole Fraction

Steps:

1. Convert each component mass into moles.
2. Add all moles to get total moles.
3. Divide moles of required component by total moles.

Common mistake: dividing by total mass instead of total moles.

## Pattern 2: Molar Mass from Freezing Point Depression

Steps:

1. Identify `Kf`, mass of solute, mass of solvent, and `Delta Tf`.
2. Convert solvent mass to the formula format, usually grams in NCERT form.
3. Substitute in `M2 = (Kf x w2 x 1000) / (Delta Tf x w1)`.
4. If electrolyte is involved, include `i`.

## Pattern 3: Osmotic Pressure Calculation

Steps:

1. Convert mass of solute to moles or use molar mass relation.
2. Convert volume to litres if using `R = 0.083 L bar K^-1 mol^-1`.
3. Use absolute temperature in kelvin.
4. Keep pressure unit consistent with `R`.

# Unit 1O: High-Quality Answer Framework

For conceptual questions, use this structure:

1. Define the law or property.
2. State the mathematical expression.
3. Explain each symbol.
4. Add one physical interpretation.
5. Add one example or application if relevant.

Example for Henry's law:

"Henry's law states that at constant temperature, the partial pressure of a gas above a solution is proportional to its mole fraction in the solution. The relation is `p = KH x`. Here `KH` is Henry's law constant. A high value of `KH` means lower solubility of the gas. This explains why gas solubility increases in soft drinks when carbon dioxide is kept under high pressure."

# Unit 1P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Using Celsius in gas or osmotic pressure formulas | Use kelvin. |
| Confusing molarity and molality | Molarity uses litre of solution; molality uses kg of solvent. |
| Assuming higher `KH` means higher solubility | Higher `KH` means lower solubility. |
| Forgetting solute mass is part of solution mass | In mass percentage, denominator is mass of solution. |
| Using colligative formulas without checking electrolytes | Add van't Hoff factor when association or dissociation occurs. |
| Calling all deviations positive | Compare observed vapour pressure with ideal vapour pressure. |

## Final Revision

- Mole fraction is central to vapour pressure and gas solubility.
- Molality is central to boiling point and freezing point changes.
- Osmotic pressure is central to dilute solutions and biomolecules.
- Ideal solutions obey Raoult's law because interactions are balanced.
- Non-ideal solutions deviate because interactions change after mixing.
- Colligative properties count particles, not chemical identity.
- Association lowers particle count; dissociation raises particle count.
- van't Hoff factor corrects colligative formulas for real particle count.
