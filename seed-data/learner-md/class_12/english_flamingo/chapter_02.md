# Chapter 2: Lost Spring
### NCERT Class 12 - English Flamingo

## Chapter at a Glance

Anees Jung's "Lost Spring" exposes the stolen childhood of poor children in India. The chapter has two major parts: the life of Saheb, a ragpicker in Seemapuri, and the life of Mukesh, a child from the bangle-making town of Firozabad. Both children represent communities trapped by poverty, social neglect, exploitation, and inherited occupations.

The title is ironic. Spring suggests childhood, hope, freshness, and growth. These children should be in the springtime of life, but their childhood has been lost to survival labour.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- summarise both sections clearly;
- analyse Saheb's and Mukesh's situations;
- explain the symbolism of garbage, shoes, bangles, and dreams;
- connect poverty with child labour;
- explain how social structures trap families;
- compare resignation and aspiration in the chapter;
- write answers on exploitation, lost childhood, and dignity;
- avoid reducing the chapter to only "poverty is bad".

# Unit 2A: Structure of the Chapter

The chapter is built in two portraits.

## Part 1

Saheb is a ragpicker from a migrant family living in Seemapuri. He searches garbage for survival. For him, garbage is not waste; it is livelihood and sometimes a source of wonder.

## Part 2

Mukesh belongs to a family of bangle makers in Firozabad. He works in conditions that damage health and crush childhood. Yet he dreams of becoming a motor mechanic, which makes him different from many around him.

Together, the two parts show that child labour takes different forms but comes from the same root: poverty and lack of opportunity.

# Unit 2B: Saheb's World

Saheb's family migrated from Bangladesh, pushed by poverty and lack of livelihood. In Seemapuri, they live without stable facilities, but survival is possible through ragpicking.

Saheb's name means "lord of the universe", but his life is marked by deprivation. The irony is sharp: his name suggests grandeur, while his reality is insecurity.

## Garbage as Symbol

For adults, garbage is survival. It brings daily income.

For children, garbage can also hold surprise, because they may find something valuable in it.

This dual meaning shows how poverty changes the meaning of ordinary things.

# Unit 2C: Saheb and Education

Saheb wants school in a casual but meaningful way. The narrator's promise of opening a school exposes how easy it is for privileged people to speak and how hard it is for poor children to access education.

His exclusion from school is not because of lack of intelligence. It is caused by:

- poverty;
- migration;
- lack of documents and support;
- need to earn;
- social indifference.

# Unit 2D: Saheb's Job at the Tea Stall

When Saheb gets a job at a tea stall, he earns wages but loses freedom. His steel canister becomes a symbol of burden. As a ragpicker, he was poor but moved freely. As a worker, he is tied to an employer.

This moment shows that not every job gives dignity. Work without freedom and childhood protection becomes another form of bondage.

# Unit 2E: Mukesh's World

Mukesh belongs to Firozabad, famous for bangles. Families there have worked in bangle-making for generations. Children work near furnaces and in dim rooms, damaging their eyes and bodies.

The beauty of bangles contrasts with the suffering of those who make them. Bangles symbolise marriage, colour, and celebration for buyers, but for workers they represent exploitation and inherited poverty.

# Unit 2F: Firozabad and the Bangle Industry

The bangle industry traps workers through:

- poverty;
- middlemen;
- debt;
- lack of education;
- caste-linked occupation;
- fear of authorities;
- absence of leadership;
- normalisation of child labour.

Families accept suffering as destiny because they have seen no alternative for generations.

# Unit 2G: Mukesh's Dream

Mukesh wants to become a motor mechanic. This dream matters because it shows a desire to break inherited occupation.

He does not dream of something glamorous or impossible. His dream is practical, work-based, and rooted in visible reality. He has seen garages and cars. He wants skill, movement, and a life outside the bangle furnaces.

## Why Mukesh Is Important

Mukesh represents:

- hope within oppression;
- desire for self-direction;
- courage to imagine another future;
- quiet resistance to inherited labour.

# Unit 2H: Major Themes

## Lost Childhood

Children in the chapter work when they should learn, play, and grow. Their childhood is consumed by survival.

## Poverty and Exploitation

Poverty forces families to accept unsafe work. Exploiters benefit from their helplessness.

## Social Injustice

The chapter shows how systems, not only individuals, keep people poor. Bureaucracy, middlemen, caste, and lack of education combine to trap families.

## Dreams and Denial

Saheb and Mukesh both have desires, but society gives them little support. Mukesh's dream remains a fragile but powerful sign of possibility.

## Irony of Beauty

Bangles are beautiful, colourful, and culturally meaningful, but their production is linked with darkness, heat, and damaged lives.

# Unit 2J: Narrative Style

Anees Jung uses a documentary style. She observes, questions, describes, and reflects. The chapter blends reporting with empathy.

Features:

- real places and communities;
- direct social observation;
- irony in names and situations;
- contrast between beauty and suffering;
- restrained but sharp criticism;
- focus on individual children to reveal larger injustice.

# Unit 2L: Exam Answer Frameworks

## Lost Childhood

1. Begin with the title's meaning.
2. Use Saheb as example of ragpicking and lost education.
3. Use Mukesh as example of hazardous inherited labour.
4. Mention social causes, not only individual poverty.
5. Conclude with how dreams survive but remain threatened.

## Bangle Industry

1. Describe Firozabad's association with bangles.
2. Show contrast between beauty of bangles and suffering of workers.
3. Explain role of poverty, middlemen, and inherited occupation.
4. Mention health hazards and child labour.
5. End with Mukesh's dream as a sign of resistance.

# Unit 2M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating Saheb and Mukesh as isolated cases | They represent larger communities. |
| Saying garbage is only dirty | For ragpickers, it is livelihood and possibility. |
| Ignoring irony of title | "Spring" refers to childhood and hope lost too early. |
| Calling Mukesh's dream unrealistic | It is practical and meaningful. |
| Blaming parents alone | The chapter shows structural poverty and exploitation. |

## Final Revision

- The chapter presents two portraits of lost childhood.
- Saheb represents migrant ragpickers of Seemapuri.
- Mukesh represents child workers in Firozabad's bangle industry.
- Garbage and bangles carry deep symbolic meaning.
- Poverty, middlemen, and inherited work trap families.
- Mukesh's dream of becoming a mechanic gives the chapter a note of hope.
