# Chapter 5: Indigo
### NCERT Class 12 - English Flamingo

## Chapter at a Glance

Louis Fischer's "Indigo" presents an important episode from Mahatma Gandhi's leadership in Champaran. The chapter shows how Gandhi helped poor indigo peasants resist exploitation by British landlords. It is not only a political story; it is also a lesson in leadership, courage, self-reliance, and practical service.

The central idea is that freedom begins when ordinary people lose fear. Gandhi's work in Champaran combines legal protest, moral courage, social reform, and grassroots organisation.

# Unit 5A: Background of Indigo Exploitation

British landlords forced peasants in Champaran to grow indigo on part of their land. This system benefited landlords and trapped peasants in poverty.

When synthetic indigo reduced demand for natural indigo, landlords tried to release peasants from the old arrangement in exchange for compensation. Many peasants signed without understanding the situation. When they later learned that landlords were gaining unfairly, they wanted justice.

# Unit 5B: Rajkumar Shukla's Role

Rajkumar Shukla was a poor peasant from Champaran. He followed Gandhi persistently and requested him to visit Champaran.

His importance:

- he brings Gandhi to the issue;
- he represents peasant suffering;
- his persistence shows faith in Gandhi;
- he proves that history can be moved by ordinary people.

Shukla is not powerful, educated, or wealthy, but his determination becomes the starting point of change.

# Unit 5C: Gandhi's Arrival in Champaran

Gandhi does not rush into action blindly. He first gathers facts. He meets lawyers, peasants, officials, and local people. He wants to understand the truth before deciding strategy.

This shows his method:

- listen carefully;
- investigate directly;
- involve local people;
- act openly;
- accept consequences.

# Unit 5D: Civil Disobedience

When ordered to leave Champaran, Gandhi refuses because he believes his duty to suffering peasants is higher than an unjust order. He does not hide or use violence. He calmly accepts legal consequences.

This is civil disobedience:

- open refusal to obey an unjust order;
- non-violent conduct;
- willingness to accept punishment;
- moral appeal to authority and public conscience.

# Unit 5E: The Court Scene

The court scene is a turning point. Large numbers of peasants gather in support of Gandhi. Their presence shows that fear is weakening.

Gandhi also teaches discipline. He does not want chaos or violence. He wants courage guided by responsibility.

The British authorities become uncertain because Gandhi's moral position is strong and public support is visible.

# Unit 5F: Role of Lawyers

Local lawyers initially think in legal terms. Gandhi makes them examine their responsibility toward peasants. If Gandhi goes to jail, should they return home or continue helping?

They decide to support the peasants. This matters because educated Indians begin to share risk with the poor.

# Unit 5G: The Settlement

An official inquiry is conducted. Evidence of exploitation is collected. The landlords agree to refund part of the money taken from peasants.

The exact percentage is less important than the psychological result. The peasants learn that landlords are not unbeatable. The British planters lose moral authority.

## Why the Settlement Was Important

It:

- broke the fear of peasants;
- proved non-violent pressure could work;
- established Gandhi's leadership method;
- weakened landlord dominance;
- turned a local issue into a national lesson.

# Unit 5H: Social Work in Champaran

Gandhi does not stop at legal victory. He works on social uplift.

Areas of work:

- education;
- hygiene;
- health;
- sanitation;
- self-confidence;
- village organisation.

This shows that political freedom is incomplete without social reform.

# Unit 5I: Self-Reliance

Gandhi believes Indians must learn to stand on their own. He discourages dependence on outsiders, even sympathetic ones, because real freedom requires inner strength.

Self-reliance means:

- local people participate;
- peasants speak for themselves;
- lawyers take responsibility;
- communities improve their own conditions;
- fear is replaced by confidence.

# Unit 5J: Gandhi's Leadership

Gandhi's leadership is:

- patient;
- fact-based;
- fearless;
- non-violent;
- morally grounded;
- practical;
- inclusive;
- focused on ordinary people.

He does not lead from distance. He goes to the village, listens to peasants, faces officials, and works on daily problems.

# Unit 5K: Major Themes

## Freedom from Fear

The greatest victory in Champaran is psychological. Peasants stop seeing landlords as invincible.

## Justice through Non-Violence

Gandhi's method shows that disciplined non-violence can challenge injustice effectively.

## Leadership and Service

Leadership is presented as service, not command. Gandhi serves peasants before representing them.

## Truth and Investigation

Gandhi insists on facts. Moral protest must stand on truth.

## Political Freedom and Social Reform

Education, health, and sanitation are part of freedom because oppressed people need dignity in daily life.
