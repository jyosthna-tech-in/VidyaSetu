# Chapter 8: Going Places
### NCERT Class 12 - English Flamingo

## Chapter at a Glance

A. R. Barton's "Going Places" explores adolescence, fantasy, class aspiration, and the difference between dreams and reality. The story centres on Sophie, a young girl from a working-class family who imagines a glamorous future and creates a fantasy involving footballer Danny Casey.

The chapter is subtle because it does not simply mock dreams. It shows why fantasies become attractive when real life feels limited. Sophie's dreams reveal both imagination and dissatisfaction.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- analyse Sophie's character and fantasies;
- compare Sophie and Jansie;
- explain Geoff's role in Sophie's imagination;
- describe the family background;
- explain the Danny Casey fantasy;
- connect the story with adolescence and class aspiration;
- identify irony, realism, and open ending;

# Unit 8A: Plot Overview

Sophie dreams of a glamorous future. She imagines owning a boutique, becoming an actress, or entering a sophisticated world beyond her current life. Her friend Jansie is more practical and reminds her of their likely future in factory work.

Sophie is fascinated by her older brother Geoff because he is quiet and has access to a world outside the home. She imagines him as someone who knows exciting people and places.

Sophie claims she met Danny Casey, a famous footballer. Her family is doubtful, but she continues to build the fantasy. She imagines a meeting with him and waits for him, but he does not come.

The story ends with Sophie still drawn into fantasy, even though reality contradicts it.

# Unit 8B: Sophie's Character

Sophie is imaginative, restless, ambitious, and dissatisfied with ordinary life.

She wants:

- glamour;
- recognition;
- beauty;
- escape from factory future;
- emotional excitement;
- access to a larger world.

Her flaw is not dreaming itself. Her flaw is refusing to distinguish dream from reality.

# Unit 8C: Jansie as Contrast

Jansie is practical and grounded. She understands the limits of their social situation. She expects that both girls will likely work in a biscuit factory.

Jansie functions as a contrast to Sophie:

| Sophie | Jansie |
|---|---|
| Dreamy | Practical |
| Escapist | Realistic |
| Secretive | Socially alert |
| Seeks glamour | Accepts ordinary life |
| Builds fantasies | Questions fantasies |

Jansie is not dull; she is realistic about class limitations.

# Unit 8D: Geoff's Role

Geoff is Sophie's older brother. He is quiet and works as an apprentice mechanic. Because he speaks little, Sophie imagines that his silence hides a mysterious adult world.

To Sophie, Geoff represents:

- access to the outside world;
- masculine independence;
- possible escape;
- mystery;
- someone who might believe her.

She wants Geoff's attention and approval because he connects home life with the larger world she desires.

# Unit 8E: Family Background

Sophie's family is working-class. Her father's behaviour, home atmosphere, and expected factory future all suggest limited economic opportunity.

This background is important because Sophie's fantasies are not random. They arise from a desire to escape social limits.

# Unit 8F: Danny Casey Fantasy

Danny Casey is a footballer admired by Sophie and her family. Sophie's claim that she met him becomes the centre of her fantasy.

Why Danny Casey?

- he is famous;
- he belongs to a glamorous public world;
- he is admired by men in her family;
- he represents success beyond class limits;
- he allows Sophie to imagine being special.

The imagined meeting gives Sophie emotional importance she lacks in ordinary life.

# Unit 8G: The Waiting Scene

Sophie waits for Danny Casey even though readers sense he will not come. This scene is important because fantasy meets reality.

Her waiting shows:

- emotional investment in fantasy;
- loneliness;
- refusal to surrender dream;
- vulnerability;
- pain of self-deception.

The scene is not comic only. It is quietly sad.

# Unit 8H: Major Themes

## Dreams and Reality

The story asks whether dreams inspire or deceive. Sophie's dreams give her excitement but also detach her from reality.

## Adolescence

Sophie shows adolescent longing for identity, admiration, and a larger life.

## Class Limitation

The expected factory future shapes the story. Sophie's fantasy is also a protest against social confinement.

## Escapism

Fantasy becomes a way to avoid ordinary life. But escapism cannot change reality by itself.

## Need for Recognition

Sophie wants to be noticed, believed, and valued.

# Unit 8I: Title Significance

"Going Places" has two meanings.

It can mean becoming successful in life. Sophie dreams of going places socially and professionally.

It can also suggest imaginary movement. Sophie travels mentally into glamorous worlds, while her real life remains limited.

The title is therefore hopeful and ironic at the same time.

# Unit 8J: Narrative Technique

The story uses realism with access to Sophie's imagination. It does not loudly judge her. Instead, it shows the gap between what she imagines and what is likely true.

Features:

- realistic family setting;
- contrast between dialogue and fantasy;
- open ending;
- irony;
- psychological focus;
- sympathetic but critical view of dreams.

# Unit 8M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling Sophie only a liar | Her fantasy comes from longing and dissatisfaction. |
| Treating Jansie as negative | Jansie is practical and socially aware. |
| Ignoring class background | Working-class limitation is central. |
| Assuming Danny Casey actually comes | The story strongly suggests the meeting is fantasy. |
| Reading the title only literally | It is both aspirational and ironic. |
