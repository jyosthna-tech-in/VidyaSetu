# Chapter 4: The Rattrap
### NCERT Class 12 - English Flamingo

## Chapter at a Glance

Selma Lagerlof's "The Rattrap" is a story about human goodness, temptation, and redemption. A poor peddler who sells rattraps sees the world as a giant rattrap that tempts people with riches and pleasures. Ironically, he himself falls into the trap of theft. Yet the kindness of Edla Willmansson helps him recover his dignity.

The story argues that trust and compassion can awaken goodness even in a person hardened by poverty and suspicion.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- summarise the plot clearly;
- explain the rattrap metaphor;
- analyse the peddler's moral journey;
- compare the ironmaster and Edla;
- explain how kindness leads to transformation;
- identify irony, symbolism, and contrast;
- write answers on redemption, trust, and human dignity;
- avoid treating the story as a simple moral tale only.

# Unit 4A: Plot Overview

The peddler sells small rattraps but lives in poverty. He begins thinking of the world as a large rattrap. Just as a rattrap offers bait to catch rats, the world offers riches and pleasures to trap human beings.

One evening, he receives shelter from an old crofter. The crofter is lonely and kind. He shares food, tobacco, and conversation. He also shows the peddler his money.

The peddler steals the money and runs away. Afraid of being caught, he avoids the main road and enters a forest. There he gets lost and realises that his own rattrap idea has come true: he has been trapped by temptation.

At an ironworks, the ironmaster mistakes him for an old acquaintance. He invites him home. The peddler hesitates because he fears discovery. Edla, the ironmaster's daughter, persuades him gently.

When the mistake is discovered, the ironmaster wants the peddler to leave. Edla insists he should stay because they had invited him as a guest. Her kindness changes him. The next day, he leaves behind the stolen money and a letter, signing himself as a captain.

# Unit 4B: The Rattrap Metaphor

The peddler's idea is that the world is a rattrap. It offers bait in the form of:

- money;
- food;
- shelter;
- comfort;
- status;
- pleasure.

People are tempted by these things and then become trapped by greed, crime, guilt, or social consequences.

The metaphor is powerful because the peddler first uses it cynically for others, then experiences it personally after stealing the crofter's money.

# Unit 4C: The Peddler's Character

## At the Beginning

He is:

- poor;
- lonely;
- suspicious;
- bitter;
- tempted by theft;
- cynical about the world.

His poverty has made him distrustful, but it does not fully destroy his moral awareness.

## After the Theft

He feels trapped. The forest becomes a physical version of his moral confusion. He cannot find his way because he has lost moral direction.

## At the End

He returns the stolen money and writes a grateful letter. He accepts Edla's trust and tries to become worthy of it.

# Unit 4D: The Crofter

The crofter is generous and lonely. He welcomes the peddler, feeds him, and talks openly. His trust makes the peddler's theft more morally serious.

The crofter represents simple human warmth. He is not foolish; he is emotionally hungry for company. The betrayal exposes the peddler's weakness.

# Unit 4E: The Ironmaster

The ironmaster mistakes the peddler for an old regimental comrade. His kindness depends partly on this mistaken identity.

When he discovers the truth, he becomes angry and wants to send the peddler away. He represents social respectability, but his compassion is limited.

# Unit 4F: Edla Willmansson

Edla is perceptive, gentle, and morally courageous. She notices that the peddler is afraid. She persuades him with kindness rather than force.

When the truth comes out, she still insists that he should stay for Christmas because he was invited as a guest. Her goodness is not based on social status or mistaken identity.

## Why Edla Changes the Peddler

Edla gives him:

- trust;
- respect;
- safety;
- dignity;
- a chance to see himself differently.

He behaves like a better person because she treats him as capable of goodness.

# Unit 4G: Major Themes

## Temptation and Trap

The peddler's theft shows how easily people fall into moral traps when tempted by money.

## Human Kindness

The story shows kindness as transformative. Edla's compassion succeeds where suspicion and punishment would not.

## Redemption

The peddler is not permanently evil. He makes a wrong choice but later corrects it.

## Dignity

People may rise morally when treated with dignity. Edla's behaviour restores the peddler's self-respect.

## Appearance and Reality

The ironmaster misreads the peddler's identity. Edla sees more deeply: she recognises fear and suffering.

| Symbol | Meaning |

# Unit 4I: Irony in the Story

The central irony is that the peddler, who thinks others are trapped by the world, becomes trapped himself after stealing money.

Another irony is that he is treated as a captain by mistake, but this mistaken dignity helps him act honourably in the end.

# Unit 4J: Edla vs Ironmaster

| Aspect | Ironmaster | Edla |
|---|---|---|
| First response | Mistaken recognition | Careful observation |
| Basis of kindness | Assumed social identity | Human sympathy |
| After truth | Anger and rejection | Keeps her promise of hospitality |
| Moral depth | Limited | Strong |
| Effect on peddler | Fear | Transformation |

# Unit 4K: Exam Answer Frameworks

## Rattrap Metaphor

1. Explain literal rattrap and bait.
2. Explain peddler's view of the world.
3. Show how riches and pleasures trap people.
4. Link the metaphor to his theft and forest experience.
5. Conclude that he escapes through Edla's kindness.

## Edla's Role

1. Introduce Edla as compassionate and perceptive.
2. Explain how she notices the peddler's fear.
3. Mention her insistence on Christmas hospitality.
4. Show how trust awakens his conscience.
5. Conclude that she is the agent of redemption.

# Unit 4L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling the peddler purely evil | He is flawed but capable of reform. |
| Ignoring the crofter's kindness | His kindness makes the theft more serious. |
| Treating the rattrap as only an object | It is the central metaphor of the story. |
| Saying the ironmaster transforms the peddler | Edla's trust transforms him. |
| Missing the forest's symbolic meaning | It reflects guilt and entrapment. |

## Final Revision

- The world-as-rattrap metaphor controls the story.
- The peddler steals and becomes trapped by guilt.
- The crofter represents trust and warmth.
- The ironmaster's kindness depends on mistaken identity.
- Edla's kindness is deeper and more humane.
- Trust and dignity redeem the peddler.
