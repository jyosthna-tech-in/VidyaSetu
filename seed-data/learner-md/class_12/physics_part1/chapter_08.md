# Chapter 8: Electromagnetic Waves
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

Electromagnetic waves are waves of electric and magnetic fields that can travel through vacuum. This chapter explains displacement current, Maxwell's correction to Ampere's law, nature of electromagnetic waves, speed of light, and the electromagnetic spectrum.

The central idea is that changing electric fields produce magnetic fields, and changing magnetic fields produce electric fields. This self-sustaining interaction travels as an electromagnetic wave.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, learners should be able to:

- Explain displacement current.
- Understand why Maxwell modified Ampere's law.
- Describe nature of electromagnetic waves.
- State relation between electric and magnetic field amplitudes.
- Calculate speed of electromagnetic waves in vacuum.
- Identify main regions of electromagnetic spectrum.
- State sources and uses of radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.
- Understand that all electromagnetic waves have same speed in vacuum but different frequencies and wavelengths.

# Unit 8A: Need for Displacement Current

Ampere's law originally related magnetic field around a closed loop to conduction current.

But a problem appears in a charging capacitor. Current flows in wires, but there is no conduction current through the gap between capacitor plates.

Still, magnetic field exists in the region. Maxwell resolved this by introducing displacement current.

# Unit 8B: Displacement Current

Displacement current is associated with changing electric flux.

`Id = epsilon0 dPhiE/dt`

It is not actual flow of charges through the gap. It is a term that produces magnetic field just like conduction current in Maxwell's equations.

## In a Charging Capacitor

Between plates:

- Conduction current is zero.
- Electric field changes with time.
- Electric flux changes.
- Displacement current exists.

This makes current continuous in the circuit.

# Unit 8C: Maxwell's Correction

Corrected Ampere law:

`integral B.dl = mu0(Ic + Id)`

where:

- `Ic` is conduction current.
- `Id` is displacement current.

## Importance

This correction predicted electromagnetic waves.

Changing electric field produces magnetic field, and changing magnetic field produces electric field.

# Unit 8D: Nature of Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic waves consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

## Properties

- Electric field, magnetic field, and direction of propagation are mutually perpendicular.
- They are transverse waves.
- They do not require material medium.
- They travel in vacuum with speed `c`.
- They carry energy and momentum.

## Direction

If electric field is along x-axis and magnetic field is along y-axis, the wave travels along z-axis.

# Unit 8E: Speed of Electromagnetic Waves

Maxwell showed that speed of electromagnetic waves in vacuum is:

`c = 1/sqrt(mu0 epsilon0)`

Using known values of `mu0` and `epsilon0`, this equals:

`c = 3 x 10^8 m/s`

This is the speed of light, showing that light is an electromagnetic wave.

# Unit 8F: Relation Between E and B

For an electromagnetic wave in vacuum:

`E0/B0 = c`

or:

`E0 = c B0`

where:

- `E0` is electric field amplitude.
- `B0` is magnetic field amplitude.

## Example

If `B0 = 2 x 10^-8 T`, then:

`E0 = cB0 = (3 x 10^8)(2 x 10^-8) = 6 V/m`

# Unit 8G: Production of Electromagnetic Waves

Accelerated charges produce electromagnetic waves.

Examples:

- Oscillating charges in antennas produce radio waves.
- Molecular vibrations produce infrared radiation.
- Atomic transitions produce visible and ultraviolet light.
- Sudden deceleration of fast electrons can produce X-rays.
- Nuclear transitions can produce gamma rays.

# Unit 8H: Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum is arranged by frequency or wavelength.

Increasing frequency order:

Radio waves -> Microwaves -> Infrared -> Visible -> Ultraviolet -> X-rays -> Gamma rays

Increasing wavelength order is the reverse.

All electromagnetic waves travel at same speed in vacuum:

`c = frequency x wavelength`

# Unit 8I: Radio Waves

Radio waves have long wavelengths and relatively low frequencies.

## Origin

Accelerated charges in conducting wires and antennas.

## Uses

- Radio broadcasting.
- Television transmission.
- Mobile communication.
- Long-distance communication.

# Unit 8J: Microwaves

Microwaves have shorter wavelength than radio waves.

## Uses

- Radar.
- Satellite communication.
- Microwave ovens.
- Wireless communication.

## Microwave Oven

Microwaves cause water molecules in food to oscillate, producing heating.

# Unit 8K: Infrared Waves

Infrared radiation is associated with heat.

## Origin

Hot bodies, molecules, and thermal radiation.

## Uses

- Remote controls.
- Thermal imaging.
- Night vision devices.
- Heating applications.

# Unit 8L: Visible Light

Visible light is the part of the electromagnetic spectrum detected by human eyes.

Approximate wavelength range:

`400 nm to 700 nm`

It includes colours from violet to red.

# Unit 8M: Ultraviolet Rays

Ultraviolet radiation has frequency higher than visible violet light.

## Origin

- Sun.
- Special lamps.
- Electric arcs.

## Uses

- Sterilisation.
- Detecting forged documents.
- Studying atomic transitions.

## Risk

Excess UV can damage skin and eyes.

# Unit 8N: X-rays

X-rays have high frequency and high penetrating power.

## Origin

Produced when high-speed electrons strike a metal target.

## Uses

- Medical imaging.
- Security scanning.
- Crystal structure studies.

## Risk

Excess exposure can damage living tissue.

# Unit 8O: Gamma Rays

Gamma rays have the highest frequencies in the electromagnetic spectrum.

## Origin

- Nuclear reactions.
- Radioactive decay.
- Cosmic processes.

## Uses

- Cancer treatment.
- Sterilisation of medical equipment.
- Nuclear studies.

## Risk

Gamma rays are highly penetrating and can be biologically harmful.

# Unit 8P: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Electric Field from Magnetic Field

For an electromagnetic wave, `B0 = 5 x 10^-8 T`.

`E0 = cB0`

`E0 = (3 x 10^8)(5 x 10^-8)`

`E0 = 15 V/m`

## Example 2: Wavelength from Frequency

Radio wave frequency is `10 MHz = 10 x 10^6 Hz`.

`c = f lambda`

`lambda = c/f`

`lambda = (3 x 10^8)/(10 x 10^6)`

`lambda = 30 m`

## Example 3: Displacement Current

If electric flux changes at rate `2 x 10^10 V m/s`, then:

`Id = epsilon0 dPhiE/dt`

`Id = (8.85 x 10^-12)(2 x 10^10)`

`Id = 0.177 A`

# Unit 8Q: Answer-Writing Framework

## To explain displacement current

Write in this order:

1. Mention charging capacitor issue.
2. State that no conduction current crosses the gap.
3. State that electric flux changes between plates.
4. Define `Id = epsilon0 dPhiE/dt`.
5. Explain it completes Ampere's law.

## To describe electromagnetic waves

Write in this order:

1. State they consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.
2. Mention fields are perpendicular to each other and to direction of propagation.
3. State they are transverse.
4. State they do not need medium.
5. State speed in vacuum.

## To identify spectrum region

Write in this order:

1. Compare frequency or wavelength.
2. Place wave in spectrum order.
3. State one source.
4. State one use.

# Unit 8R: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Thinking displacement current is actual charge flow through capacitor gap | It is due to changing electric flux. |
| Saying EM waves need medium | They can travel in vacuum. |
| Saying electric and magnetic fields are parallel | They are perpendicular to each other. |
| Forgetting `E0/B0 = c` | Electric and magnetic amplitudes are related. |
| Mixing wavelength order and frequency order | Higher frequency means shorter wavelength. |
| Saying all spectrum regions have different speeds in vacuum | All EM waves travel at speed `c` in vacuum. |
| Treating gamma rays as harmless | They are highly penetrating and can damage tissue. |

Remember these points:

- Changing electric flux gives displacement current.
