# Chapter 1: Electric Charges and Fields
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter introduces electrostatics: the study of electric charges at rest. It explains electric charge, conductors, insulators, Coulomb's law, superposition, electric field, field lines, electric flux, electric dipole, continuous charge distribution, Gauss's law, and the use of Gauss's law for symmetric charge distributions.

The key idea is that charges produce electric fields, and electric fields exert forces on other charges.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, learners should be able to:

- Explain electric charge and its basic properties.
- Distinguish conductors and insulators.
- Use charge conservation and quantisation.
- Apply Coulomb's law for two point charges.
- Use superposition for multiple charges.
- Define electric field and calculate field due to point charges.
- Interpret electric field lines.
- Calculate electric flux.
- Understand electric dipoles and torque on a dipole.
- Explain continuous charge distributions.
- Apply Gauss's law to line charge, plane sheet, and spherical shell.

# Unit 1A: Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. There are two kinds of charge:

- Positive charge.
- Negative charge.

Like charges repel each other. Unlike charges attract each other.

## Charging by Friction

When two suitable bodies are rubbed, electrons may transfer from one body to the other.

- Body losing electrons becomes positively charged.
- Body gaining electrons becomes negatively charged.

No new charge is created. Charge is only transferred.

# Unit 1B: Conductors and Insulators

## Conductors

Conductors allow electric charges to move through them easily. Metals are good conductors because they have free electrons.

Examples:

- Copper
- Aluminium
- Silver
- Iron

## Insulators

Insulators do not allow charges to move freely through them.

Examples:

- Glass
- Rubber
- Plastic
- Dry wood

## Physical Meaning

In conductors, excess charge can spread over the surface. In insulators, charge tends to remain where it is placed.

# Unit 1C: Basic Properties of Charge

## Additivity of Charge

Total charge of a system is the algebraic sum of all charges.

Example:

If charges are `+3 microC`, `-2 microC`, and `+5 microC`, then total charge is:

`+3 - 2 + 5 = +6 microC`

## Conservation of Charge

Total charge of an isolated system remains constant.

Charge can move from one body to another, but it cannot be created or destroyed.

## Quantisation of Charge

Charge exists as integral multiples of elementary charge:

`q = ne`

where:

- `n` is an integer.
- `e = 1.6 x 10^-19 C`.

This means a body cannot have charge `0.5e` or `2.3e`.

# Unit 1D: Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's law gives the electrostatic force between two point charges.

For charges `q1` and `q2` separated by distance `r`:

`F = k |q1 q2|/r^2`

where:

`k = 1/(4 pi epsilon0) = 9 x 10^9 N m^2/C^2`

## Direction

- Repulsive for like charges.
- Attractive for unlike charges.
- Acts along the line joining the charges.

## Vector Nature

Force is a vector. Magnitude alone is not enough when more than two charges are involved.

# Unit 1E: Superposition Principle

When several charges act on a charge, the net force is the vector sum of individual forces.

If charges `q1`, `q2`, and `q3` act on charge `q`, then:

`F_net = F1 + F2 + F3`

Each force is calculated as if the other charges were absent, then all forces are added vectorially.

## Common Trap

Do not add magnitudes blindly. Directions matter.

# Unit 1F: Electric Field

Electric field at a point is force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point.

`E = F/q0`

Unit:

`N/C`

Electric field is a vector.

## Field Due to a Point Charge

For point charge `q`:

`E = k q/r^2`

Direction:

- Away from positive charge.
- Toward negative charge.

# Unit 1G: Electric Field Due to a System of Charges

Electric field obeys superposition.

For several charges:

`E_net = E1 + E2 + E3 + ...`

Each field is calculated at the point of interest, then added vectorially.

## Problem Method

1. Draw the charges and point.
2. Mark direction of field due to each charge.
3. Compute magnitudes.
4. Resolve into components if needed.
5. Add components.

# Unit 1H: Electric Field Lines

Electric field lines are imaginary lines that show direction of electric field.

## Properties

- Field lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges.
- Tangent to a field line gives direction of electric field.
- Closer lines mean stronger field.
- Field lines never intersect.
- In electrostatics, field lines do not form closed loops.

## Why Lines Never Intersect

If two field lines intersected, the electric field at the intersection would have two directions. That is impossible.

# Unit 1I: Electric Flux

Electric flux through a surface measures how much electric field passes through the surface.

For uniform field over a flat surface:

`Phi = E A cos theta`

where:

- `E` is electric field.
- `A` is area.
- `theta` is angle between field and area vector.

## Area Vector

Area vector is perpendicular to the surface.

For a closed surface, area vector is outward.

## Special Cases

- If field is perpendicular to surface: `theta = 0`, so `Phi = EA`.
- If field is parallel to surface: `theta = 90 degrees`, so `Phi = 0`.

# Unit 1J: Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance.

If charges are `+q` and `-q`, separated by distance `2a`, dipole moment is:

`p = q(2a)`

Direction of `p` is from negative charge to positive charge.

Unit:

`C m`

## Examples

Many molecules behave like electric dipoles because centres of positive and negative charge do not coincide.

# Unit 1K: Electric Field of a Dipole

For a dipole with moment `p`, at a point far from the dipole:

## Axial Line

Field on the axial line:

`E_axial = (1/(4 pi epsilon0)) (2p/r^3)`

Direction is along dipole moment.

## Equatorial Line

Field on the equatorial line:

`E_equatorial = (1/(4 pi epsilon0)) (p/r^3)`

Direction is opposite to dipole moment.

## Important Comparison

For the same distance `r`, axial field is twice the equatorial field in magnitude.

# Unit 1L: Dipole in a Uniform Electric Field

A dipole in a uniform electric field experiences no net force but experiences a torque.

Torque:

`tau = p E sin theta`

where `theta` is angle between `p` and `E`.

## Stable and Unstable Positions

- Stable equilibrium: `p` parallel to `E`, `theta = 0`.
- Unstable equilibrium: `p` antiparallel to `E`, `theta = 180 degrees`.

## Physical Meaning

The field tries to rotate the dipole so that dipole moment aligns with the field.

# Unit 1M: Continuous Charge Distribution

Sometimes charge is spread continuously over a body.

## Linear Charge Density

For charge distributed along a line:

`lambda = dq/dl`

Unit: `C/m`

## Surface Charge Density

For charge distributed over a surface:

`sigma = dq/dA`

Unit: `C/m^2`

## Volume Charge Density

For charge distributed through a volume:

`rho = dq/dV`

Unit: `C/m^3`

# Unit 1N: Gauss's Law

Gauss's law states:

`Total electric flux through a closed surface = q_enclosed/epsilon0`

Formula:

`Phi = q_enclosed/epsilon0`

## Important Points

- Surface must be closed.
- Only charge enclosed by the surface contributes to net flux.
- Charges outside may affect field at points on the surface but do not affect net flux.
- Gauss's law is most useful when symmetry is high.

# Unit 1O: Choosing a Gaussian Surface

Gauss's law becomes useful when electric field has constant magnitude over parts of the surface and known direction.

## Common Choices

| Charge Distribution | Gaussian Surface |
|---|---|
| Infinite line charge | Coaxial cylinder |
| Infinite plane sheet | Pillbox cylinder |
| Spherical charge or shell | Concentric sphere |

## Strategy

Choose a surface that matches symmetry.

# Unit 1P: Field Due to Infinite Line Charge

For an infinite line charge with linear charge density `lambda`, choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius `r` and length `L`.

Electric field is radial and constant on curved surface.

Flux:

`Phi = E(2 pi r L)`

Charge enclosed:

`q = lambda L`

Gauss's law:

`E(2 pi r L) = lambda L/epsilon0`

Therefore:

`E = lambda/(2 pi epsilon0 r)`

# Unit 1Q: Field Due to Infinite Plane Sheet

For an infinite plane sheet with surface charge density `sigma`, choose a pillbox Gaussian surface.

Field is perpendicular to the sheet on both sides.

Flux:

`Phi = 2EA`

Charge enclosed:

`q = sigma A`

Gauss's law:

`2EA = sigma A/epsilon0`

Therefore:

`E = sigma/(2 epsilon0)`

The field is independent of distance from the sheet.

# Unit 1R: Field Due to Spherical Shell

For a uniformly charged thin spherical shell of charge `Q` and radius `R`:

## Outside the Shell

At distance `r > R`:

`E = kQ/r^2`

The shell behaves as if all charge were concentrated at the centre.

## On the Surface

At `r = R`:

`E = kQ/R^2`

## Inside the Shell

At `r < R`:

`E = 0`

This is an important result for electrostatic shielding.

# Unit 1S: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Number of Electrons

A body has charge `-3.2 x 10^-19 C`. How many excess electrons does it have?

Use:

`q = ne`

`n = |q|/e = (3.2 x 10^-19)/(1.6 x 10^-19) = 2`

The body has 2 excess electrons.

## Example 2: Coulomb Force

Two charges `2 microC` and `3 microC` are separated by `0.3 m`. Find force magnitude.

`F = k q1 q2/r^2`

`F = (9 x 10^9)(2 x 10^-6)(3 x 10^-6)/(0.3)^2`

`F = 0.6 N`

Since charges are like, force is repulsive.

## Example 3: Electric Field

Find electric field at distance `0.2 m` from charge `4 microC`.

`E = kq/r^2`

`E = (9 x 10^9)(4 x 10^-6)/(0.2)^2`

`E = 9 x 10^5 N/C`

Direction is away from charge if charge is positive.

# Unit 1T: Answer-Writing Framework

## To solve Coulomb law problems

Write in this order:

1. Convert charges to coulomb and distance to metre.
2. Use `F = k |q1q2|/r^2`.
3. Determine direction using signs of charges.
4. If multiple charges exist, add forces vectorially.

## To solve electric field problems

Write in this order:

1. Identify source charge or charge distribution.
2. Use formula for electric field.
3. Mark direction.
4. For many charges, use superposition.
5. State unit `N/C`.

## To use Gauss's law

Write in this order:

1. Identify symmetry.
2. Choose suitable Gaussian surface.
3. Write flux through surface.
4. Write charge enclosed.
5. Apply `Phi = q_enclosed/epsilon0`.
6. Solve for `E`.

# Unit 1U: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating charge as continuous in all cases | Charge is quantised, though quantisation is often negligible macroscopically. |
| Forgetting vector nature of force | Use directions and components. |
| Confusing electric field and force | `E = F/q0`; force depends on test charge. |
| Drawing field lines crossing | Electric field lines never intersect. |
| Using open surface in Gauss's law | Gauss's law applies to closed surfaces. |
| Counting outside charge in `q_enclosed` | Only enclosed charge contributes to net flux. |
| Choosing poor Gaussian surface | Match symmetry to make field constant. |

## Final Revision

Remember these points:

- Charge is additive, conserved, and quantised.
- Coulomb force follows inverse-square law.
- Electric field is force per unit positive test charge.
- Field lines show direction and strength qualitatively.
- Electric flux depends on field, area, and angle.
- Dipoles experience torque in uniform fields.
- Gauss's law is powerful when symmetry is high.
