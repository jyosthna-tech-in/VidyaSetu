# Chapter 5: Magnetism and Matter
### NCERT Class 12 - Physics Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies magnets, magnetic dipoles, magnetic field lines, Earth's magnetism, Gauss's law for magnetism, magnetisation, magnetic intensity, and magnetic properties of materials. It connects the behaviour of bar magnets with current loops and explains why different materials respond differently to magnetic fields.

The central idea is that isolated magnetic poles do not exist. Every magnet behaves as a dipole.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, learners should be able to:

- Describe properties of a bar magnet.
- Draw and interpret magnetic field lines.
- Treat a bar magnet as a magnetic dipole.
- Calculate torque and potential energy of a magnetic dipole in a field.
- State Gauss's law for magnetism.
- Understand magnetisation and magnetic intensity.
- Relate `B`, `H`, and `M`.
- Define magnetic susceptibility and relative permeability.
- Distinguish diamagnetic, paramagnetic, and ferromagnetic materials.
- Understand basic ideas of permanent magnets and electromagnets.

# Unit 5A: Bar Magnet

A bar magnet has two poles:

- North pole.
- South pole.

Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.

If a magnet is broken into pieces, each piece becomes a smaller magnet with both north and south poles. Isolated magnetic poles have not been observed.

## Key Idea

Magnetic monopoles do not appear in ordinary magnetism. Magnets always behave as dipoles.

# Unit 5B: Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines show direction and strength of magnetic field.

## Properties

- Outside a magnet, field lines go from north pole to south pole.
- Inside a magnet, field lines go from south pole to north pole.
- Magnetic field lines form closed loops.
- Tangent to a field line gives direction of magnetic field.
- Closer lines indicate stronger field.
- Field lines never intersect.

## Difference from Electric Field Lines

Electrostatic field lines begin on positive charges and end on negative charges. Magnetic field lines form closed loops because isolated magnetic poles do not exist.

# Unit 5C: Bar Magnet as Magnetic Dipole

A bar magnet can be treated as a magnetic dipole with magnetic moment `m`.

Direction of magnetic moment is from south pole to north pole inside the magnet.

For a short bar magnet at large distance:

## Axial Field

`B_axial = (mu0/(4 pi))(2m/r^3)`

## Equatorial Field

`B_equatorial = (mu0/(4 pi))(m/r^3)`

Direction of equatorial field is opposite to magnetic moment.

# Unit 5D: Magnetic Dipole in Uniform Field

A magnetic dipole in a uniform magnetic field experiences torque:

`tau = mB sin theta`

where `theta` is angle between magnetic moment and magnetic field.

Potential energy:

`U = -mB cos theta`

## Stable Equilibrium

When `theta = 0`, `U` is minimum. Magnetic moment aligns with field.

## Unstable Equilibrium

When `theta = 180 degrees`, `U` is maximum.

# Unit 5E: Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero.

This means:

`closed surface magnetic flux = 0`

## Physical Meaning

As many magnetic field lines enter a closed surface as leave it. This reflects absence of magnetic monopoles.

## Comparison with Electrostatics

Electric Gauss law:

`electric flux = q_enclosed/epsilon0`

Magnetic Gauss law:

`magnetic flux = 0`

# Unit 5F: Earth's Magnetism

Earth behaves approximately like a giant magnet.

Magnetic compass aligns with Earth's magnetic field.

## Important Terms

- Magnetic declination: angle between geographic meridian and magnetic meridian.
- Magnetic inclination or dip: angle made by Earth's magnetic field with horizontal.
- Horizontal component: component of Earth's magnetic field along horizontal direction.

## Note

Earth's geographic north is near a magnetic south pole, which is why the north pole of a compass points north.

# Unit 5G: Magnetisation

When a material is placed in a magnetic field, atomic magnetic dipoles may align.

Magnetisation is magnetic moment per unit volume:

`M = net magnetic moment/volume`

Unit:

`A/m`

## Physical Meaning

Large magnetisation means strong alignment of microscopic magnetic moments.

# Unit 5H: Magnetic Intensity and Magnetic Field

Magnetic field inside matter is described using:

- `B`: magnetic field.
- `H`: magnetic intensity.
- `M`: magnetisation.

Relation:

`B = mu0(H + M)`

If material response is linear:

`M = chi H`

Then:

`B = mu0(1 + chi)H`

So:

`mu_r = 1 + chi`

# Unit 5I: Magnetic Susceptibility

Susceptibility `chi` measures how strongly a material gets magnetised in an applied magnetic field.

`M = chi H`

## Meaning of Sign

- `chi < 0`: diamagnetic.
- `chi > 0` small: paramagnetic.
- `chi > 0` large: ferromagnetic.

# Unit 5J: Diamagnetic Materials

Diamagnetic materials are weakly repelled by magnetic fields.

Properties:

- Susceptibility is small and negative.
- Relative permeability is slightly less than 1.
- Magnetisation is opposite to applied field.
- Effect is weak.

Examples:

- Bismuth.
- Copper.
- Water.
- Gold.

# Unit 5K: Paramagnetic Materials

Paramagnetic materials are weakly attracted by magnetic fields.

Properties:

- Susceptibility is small and positive.
- Relative permeability is slightly greater than 1.
- Magnetisation is in direction of applied field.
- They do not retain magnetisation strongly after field is removed.

Examples:

- Aluminium.
- Oxygen.
- Platinum.

# Unit 5L: Ferromagnetic Materials

Ferromagnetic materials are strongly attracted by magnetic fields.

Properties:

- Susceptibility is large and positive.
- Relative permeability is very high.
- Domains align strongly in applied field.
- They can retain magnetisation.

Examples:

- Iron.
- Cobalt.
- Nickel.

## Domains

Ferromagnetic materials contain small regions called domains. In each domain, magnetic moments are aligned. Magnetisation occurs when many domains align in the same direction.

# Unit 5M: Permanent Magnets and Electromagnets

## Permanent Magnets

Permanent magnets should have:

- High retentivity.
- High coercivity.

They retain magnetisation after the external field is removed.

## Electromagnets

Electromagnets should have:

- High permeability.
- Low retentivity.

They become magnetic when current flows and lose magnetism when current is switched off.

# Unit 5N: Worked Examples

## Example 1: Torque on Bar Magnet

A bar magnet of magnetic moment `0.5 A m^2` is placed in a field `0.2 T` at `30 degrees`.

`tau = mB sin theta`

`tau = (0.5)(0.2)(sin 30 degrees)`

`tau = 0.05 N m`

## Example 2: Potential Energy

For same magnet when parallel to field:

`U = -mB cos 0`

`U = -0.5 x 0.2 = -0.1 J`

## Example 3: Susceptibility

If `M = 40 A/m` and `H = 200 A/m`, then:

`chi = M/H = 40/200 = 0.2`

Positive susceptibility indicates paramagnetic or ferromagnetic behaviour depending on magnitude.

# Unit 5O: Answer-Writing Framework

## To describe magnetic field lines

Write in this order:

1. State direction outside magnet.
2. State direction inside magnet.
3. Mention closed loops.
4. Mention non-intersection.
5. Link closeness to field strength.

## To solve magnetic dipole problems

Write in this order:

1. Identify magnetic moment `m`, field `B`, and angle.
2. Use torque or energy formula.
3. Substitute values.
4. Interpret stable or unstable position if asked.

## To classify magnetic materials

Write in this order:

1. State sign and size of susceptibility.
2. State attraction or repulsion.
3. Mention relative permeability.
4. Give examples.

# Unit 5P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Thinking isolated magnetic poles exist | A magnet always has both poles. |
| Drawing magnetic field lines starting and ending separately | Magnetic field lines form closed loops. |
| Confusing electric and magnetic Gauss laws | Magnetic flux through closed surface is zero. |
| Taking magnetic moment direction from north to south | For bar magnet, moment is from south to north. |
| Confusing diamagnetic and paramagnetic | Diamagnetic has negative susceptibility; paramagnetic positive. |
| Saying ferromagnets and paramagnets are equally strong | Ferromagnets are much stronger. |

Remember these points:

- A bar magnet is a magnetic dipole.
