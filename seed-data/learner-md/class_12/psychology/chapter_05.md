# Chapter 5: Therapeutic Approaches

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how trained mental health professionals use psychological methods to reduce distress, modify maladaptive patterns, and support healthier functioning. Study it as a conceptual chapter, not as a manual for treating yourself or others. Psychotherapy requires professional training, supervision, ethics, and careful clinical judgement.

The main idea is that therapy is not just advice. It is a planned, confidential, professional relationship in which psychological principles are applied to help a client understand problems, change behaviour and thinking, express emotions safely, and improve functioning.

## 1. Therapeutic Approaches In One Sentence

Therapeutic approaches are systematic psychological methods used by trained professionals to reduce distress, change maladaptive behaviour and thinking, and improve the client's ability to live, relate, decide, and function.

## 2. Why This Chapter Follows Psychological Disorders

The previous chapter described psychological disorders. This chapter answers the next question: how can psychological distress be helped?

The chapter does not present one single method. It presents several approaches because psychological problems can arise from different combinations of:

- Past experiences.
- Faulty learning.
- Irrational beliefs.
- Distorted thinking.
- Emotional conflicts.
- Difficulty finding meaning.
- Lack of social or occupational functioning.

Different therapies emphasise different causes and different methods of change.

## 3. What Psychotherapy Means

Psychotherapy is a voluntary professional relationship between a client and a therapist.

It is voluntary because the client seeks and receives help. It is professional because the therapist has training and follows ethical standards. It is psychological because the treatment uses communication, reflection, behavioural methods, cognitive methods, and relationship-based change.

Psychotherapy aims to help the client:

- Reduce distress.
- Modify maladaptive behaviours.
- Change faulty thinking patterns.
- Improve relationships.
- Improve adjustment.
- Increase self-awareness.
- Make better decisions.
- Use personal strengths.
- Adapt more effectively to the environment.

## 4. Psychotherapy Is Not Ordinary Advice

Ordinary advice may be casual, quick, biased, or based only on personal experience.

Psychotherapy is different because it involves:

- A planned relationship.
- A theoretical framework.
- Systematic assessment.
- A clinical formulation.
- Professional training.
- Ethical responsibility.
- Confidentiality.
- Evaluation of progress.
- Techniques matched to the client's problem.

This is why the chapter stresses that untrained people should not practise psychotherapy.

## 5. Common Goals Of Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy may aim at one or more of the following goals:

- Strengthening the client's resolve for improvement.
- Reducing emotional pressure.
- Encouraging positive growth.
- Modifying maladaptive habits.
- Changing faulty thinking patterns.
- Increasing self-awareness.
- Improving communication.
- Improving interpersonal relationships.
- Helping decision-making.
- Helping the client become aware of choices.
- Supporting more creative and self-aware adjustment to the social environment.

Different therapies prioritise these goals differently.

## 6. Characteristics Shared By Psychotherapies

Although therapies differ, they share some features:

- They apply principles from psychological theories.
- They are practised by trained professionals.
- They involve a therapist and a client.
- They focus on emotional, behavioural, cognitive, or interpersonal problems.
- They require interaction between therapist and client.
- They build a therapeutic relationship.
- They aim at corrective and helping change.

The method may differ, but the central purpose remains helping the client deal with distress and improve functioning.

## 7. The Client

The client is the person who seeks help and becomes the focus of therapy.

The client may be dealing with:

- Anxiety.
- Depressed mood.
- Phobia.
- Relationship problems.
- Maladaptive habits.
- Confusion about choices.
- Low self-worth.
- Disturbed functioning.
- Difficulties after stress or trauma.

Therapy begins by understanding the client's problem, not by forcing a ready-made solution.

## 8. The Therapist

The therapist is a trained professional who applies psychological knowledge in a careful and ethical way.

The therapist's role may include:

- Listening.
- Assessing.
- Building trust.
- Formulating the problem.
- Choosing techniques.
- Helping the client examine thoughts, emotions, and behaviours.
- Supporting behaviour change.
- Maintaining confidentiality.
- Protecting professional boundaries.

The therapist is not a friend, family member, or moral judge. The relationship is warm but professional.

## 9. The Therapeutic Relationship

The special relationship between the client and therapist is called the therapeutic relationship or therapeutic alliance.

It is neither a casual acquaintance nor a permanent personal bond. It is a professional partnership with the purpose of helping the client overcome problems and regain control over life.

## 10. Two Components Of The Therapeutic Alliance

The therapeutic alliance has two important components.

First, it is contractual. The client and therapist enter a helping relationship with a shared purpose.

Second, it has limited duration. Therapy continues until the client can deal with problems more effectively or until the agreed goals have been addressed.

This makes the relationship structured, purposeful, and professional.

## 11. Trust In Therapy

Trust allows the client to talk about painful, confusing, embarrassing, or frightening experiences.

Without trust, the client may hide important material. With trust, the client can explore problems more honestly.

Trust is built through:

- Acceptance.
- Warmth.
- Empathy.
- Genuineness.
- Reliability.
- Confidentiality.
- Respectful listening.

## 12. Empathy

Empathy means understanding the client's experience from the client's point of view.

It does not mean agreeing with everything. It means entering the client's perspective carefully enough to understand what the problem feels like from inside the client's world.

Empathy helps the client feel seen rather than judged.

## 13. Empathy Versus Sympathy

Sympathy means feeling pity or compassion for another person's suffering.

Empathy means understanding the person's experience as the person experiences it.

In therapy, empathy is more useful than sympathy because it helps the therapist understand the client's meanings, fears, conflicts, and needs without turning the relationship into pity.

## 14. Unconditional Positive Regard

Unconditional positive regard means the therapist accepts the client as a person even when the client reports painful, rude, confused, or socially disapproved thoughts and behaviours.

It does not mean approving every action. It means separating the worth of the person from the problem being examined.

This non-judgmental attitude helps the client explore hidden feelings and conflicts.

## 15. Confidentiality

Confidentiality means information shared in therapy is protected.

Confidentiality is essential because clients often reveal deeply personal experiences.

Ethical practice also recognises limits to confidentiality in situations of serious risk, legal requirement, or safety concerns. The textbook focus is that confidentiality is central to the trust of therapy.

## 16. Professional Boundaries

The therapist must not exploit the client's trust.

Professional boundaries protect the client. They include:

- Respectful communication.
- Avoiding personal exploitation.
- Keeping the relationship focused on the client's welfare.
- Maintaining competence.
- Using the relationship for treatment, not for personal gain.

Boundaries make therapy safer.

## 17. Major Types Of Psychotherapy

The chapter groups therapies into broad types:

- Psychodynamic therapy.
- Behaviour therapy.
- Cognitive therapy.
- Humanistic-existential therapy.

Each type has a different view of what causes problems and how change occurs.

## 18. Classification Parameters

Therapies may be compared by asking:

- What is the cause of the problem?
- How did the cause develop?
- What is the main method of treatment?
- What is the therapist-client relationship like?
- What is the main benefit to the client?
- How long does treatment usually take?

This comparison is important because it shows that therapies are not random techniques. Each therapy has an underlying model.

## 19. Psychodynamic Therapy: Cause Of Problems

Psychodynamic therapy views psychological distress as arising from intrapsychic conflicts.

Intrapsychic conflicts are conflicts inside the mind. They may involve:

- Unfulfilled desires.
- Unresolved fears.
- Childhood conflicts.
- Hidden emotions.
- Conflicts between wishes and moral demands.

The client may not fully understand these conflicts at first.

## 20. Psychodynamic Therapy: Method

Psychodynamic therapy uses methods such as:

- Free association.
- Reporting dreams.
- Exploring past experiences.
- Interpreting unconscious material.
- Understanding emotional conflicts.

The therapist helps the client gain insight into conflicts that may be shaping present distress.

## 21. Psychodynamic Therapy: Benefit

The main benefit is emotional insight.

Emotional insight is deeper than intellectual knowledge. It means the client understands the conflict, accepts it emotionally, and changes emotional reactions connected with it.

When insight develops, symptoms and distress may reduce.

## 22. Behaviour Therapy: Cause Of Problems

Behaviour therapy views distress as arising from faulty learning.

The problem may involve:

- Maladaptive behaviours.
- Avoidance patterns.
- Reinforced habits.
- Fear responses.
- Faulty conditioning.
- Unhelpful behavioural consequences.

Behaviour therapy focuses on present behaviour more than reliving the past.

## 23. Behaviour Therapy: Method

Behaviour therapy identifies malfunctioning behaviours and replaces them with adaptive behaviours.

The therapist studies:

- What behaviour is causing distress.
- What came before the behaviour.
- What keeps the behaviour going.
- What consequences maintain the behaviour.
- What healthier behaviour can replace it.

This makes behaviour therapy practical and problem-focused.

## 24. Behaviour Therapy: Benefit

The main benefit is change in faulty behaviour and thought patterns.

The client learns adaptive patterns and reduces maladaptive responses.

For example, a client with avoidance may gradually learn to face feared situations in a controlled way.

## 25. Existential And Humanistic Therapies: Cause Of Problems

Humanistic-existential therapies view distress as arising from:

- Loneliness.
- Alienation.
- Meaninglessness.
- Blocked emotional growth.
- Inability to become one's real self.
- Failure to find genuine fulfilment.

These therapies emphasise the present and the person's capacity for growth.

## 26. Existential And Humanistic Therapies: Method

The therapist provides a warm, accepting, non-judgmental environment.

The client explores:

- Feelings.
- Choices.
- Responsibility.
- Meaning.
- Personal growth.
- Obstacles to self-actualisation.

The therapist acts as facilitator rather than commander.

## 27. Existential And Humanistic Therapies: Benefit

The main benefit is personal growth.

The client becomes more aware, more integrated, and more capable of authentic choice.

Healing is connected with self-understanding and self-actualisation.

## 28. Duration Of Therapies

Classical psychoanalysis may last for years.

Recent versions of psychodynamic therapy may be much shorter, sometimes 10-15 sessions.

Behaviour therapy, cognitive therapy, CBT, and existential therapies are often shorter and may be completed in a few months.

Duration depends on:

- Severity of problem.
- Therapy type.
- Goals.
- Client motivation.
- Resources.
- Progress.

## 29. Clinical Formulation

Clinical formulation means understanding the client's problem within the model of therapy being used.

It is a bridge between assessment and treatment.

A therapist does not simply label the problem and begin techniques. The therapist asks what is maintaining the problem and what needs to change.

## 30. Why Clinical Formulation Matters

Clinical formulation helps in three ways.

First, it helps the therapist understand the full implications of the client's distress.

Second, it identifies target areas for therapy.

Third, it guides the choice and timing of techniques.

Formulation is not fixed forever. It may be revised as new information emerges.

## 31. Example Of Clinical Formulation

Suppose a client reports inability to face superiors at work.

In a behaviour therapy formulation, the target areas may be:

- Lack of assertiveness skills.
- Anxiety in authority situations.
- Avoidance of direct communication.

The treatment may then focus on assertiveness training, anxiety reduction, and behavioural practice.

The same complaint could be understood differently in another therapy model.

## 32. Behavioural Analysis

Behavioural analysis is the careful study of maladaptive behaviour.

It examines:

- Malfunctioning behaviours.
- Antecedent factors.
- Maintaining factors.
- Reinforcers.
- Situations in which behaviour occurs.
- Situations in which behaviour reduces.

It is the foundation of behaviour therapy.

## 33. Malfunctioning Behaviours

Malfunctioning behaviours are behaviours that cause distress or interfere with functioning.

Examples may include:

- Avoiding all social contact.
- Repeated angry outbursts.
- Smoking to reduce exam anxiety.
- Avoiding school because of fear.
- Compulsively checking locks.

The behaviour is studied in context rather than judged morally.

## 34. Antecedent Factors

Antecedent factors are conditions that come before the behaviour and make it more likely.

For example, exam pressure may precede smoking if a student has learned that smoking reduces anxiety.

Antecedents may include:

- Situations.
- People.
- Thoughts.
- Feelings.
- Bodily states.
- Environmental cues.

## 35. Maintaining Factors

Maintaining factors are consequences that keep the behaviour going.

For example, if smoking reduces anxiety for a short time, the relief may maintain smoking.

The person continues the behaviour because it appears to work in the moment, even if it causes long-term harm.

## 36. Antecedent Operations

Antecedent operations change what happens before the behaviour.

They may alter the situation so that the maladaptive behaviour becomes less likely and adaptive behaviour becomes more likely.

Example: if a child refuses dinner because snacks were too heavy, reducing evening snacks may increase hunger at dinner.

## 37. Consequent Operations

Consequent operations change what happens after the behaviour.

If a desired behaviour is followed by praise, attention, or another reinforcer, it may increase.

If a maladaptive behaviour no longer produces its usual reward, it may reduce.

## 38. Establishing Operation

An establishing operation changes the value of a consequence.

In the dinner example, reducing food at tea time increases the reinforcing value of dinner.

This shows that behaviour therapy often changes the environment around behaviour, not only the behaviour itself.

## 39. Negative Reinforcement

Negative reinforcement occurs when a response increases because it removes or avoids an unpleasant stimulus.

Example:

- Wearing warm clothes increases because it removes discomfort from cold.
- Avoiding a feared situation may increase because it temporarily reduces anxiety.

Negative reinforcement is not punishment. It strengthens behaviour by removing discomfort.

## 40. Aversive Conditioning

Aversive conditioning pairs an undesired response with an unpleasant consequence so that the response becomes less attractive.

The textbook gives the example of associating alcohol-related cues with an aversive experience.

This technique must be understood as a professional therapeutic method, not as something to imitate casually. Ethical use requires training, consent, and clinical judgement.

## 41. Positive Reinforcement

Positive reinforcement increases adaptive behaviour by following it with a positive consequence.

Examples:

- Praising a child for eating dinner properly.
- Encouraging a student for speaking calmly.
- Rewarding consistent practice of a new skill.

The goal is to strengthen healthier behaviour.

## 42. Token Economy

Token economy is a behaviour therapy technique in which tokens are given for desired behaviours and later exchanged for rewards.

It is useful when behaviour needs to be shaped gradually.

Example:

- A client earns tokens for completing self-care tasks.
- Tokens are exchanged for agreed privileges.

The important point is that reinforcement is systematic and tied to specific behaviours.

## 43. Systematic Desensitisation

Systematic desensitisation is used for fears and phobias.

The client learns relaxation and is gradually exposed in imagination or real life to feared situations arranged from least frightening to most frightening.

The basic logic is:

- Fear and relaxation cannot dominate at the same time.
- Gradual exposure reduces fear.
- The client learns that the feared stimulus can be faced safely.

## 44. Modelling

Modelling is learning by observing another person.

In therapy, the client may observe the therapist or another model performing an adaptive behaviour.

Example:

- A socially anxious client watches someone greet another person calmly.
- The client then practises the behaviour.

Modelling uses the principle of observational learning.

## 45. Cognitive Therapy

Cognitive therapy focuses on thoughts, beliefs, interpretations, and assumptions.

It is based on the idea that distress is influenced by how a person interprets events.

The same event may produce different emotional reactions depending on the person's beliefs.

## 46. Ellis's Rational Emotive Therapy

Albert Ellis proposed rational emotive therapy.

It uses the ABC model:

- A means activating event.
- B means belief about the event.
- C means emotional and behavioural consequence.

The key idea is that the belief, not only the event, shapes the emotional consequence.

## 47. Irrational Beliefs

Irrational beliefs are rigid, unrealistic, or extreme beliefs that create distress.

Examples:

- "Everyone must like me."
- "I must never fail."
- "If one thing goes wrong, everything is ruined."

Rational emotive therapy challenges such beliefs and replaces them with more realistic ones.

## 48. Example Of The ABC Model

Activating event: A student receives low marks.

Belief: "This proves I am useless and my future is finished."

Consequence: despair, avoidance, and loss of motivation.

Therapy would examine the belief and help the student form a more rational response:

"The marks are disappointing, but they show where I need to improve."

## 49. Beck's Cognitive Therapy

Aaron Beck explained distress such as anxiety and depression through core schemas and negative automatic thoughts.

Core schemas are deep belief systems developed through experience.

If a child repeatedly feels neglected, a schema such as "I am unwanted" may develop.

Later incidents may trigger this schema and produce negative automatic thoughts.

## 50. Negative Automatic Thoughts

Negative automatic thoughts are quick, repeated, distress-producing thoughts.

Examples:

- "Nobody likes me."
- "I will fail."
- "I am stupid."
- "Nothing will improve."

These thoughts may feel true because they arise automatically, but cognitive therapy teaches the client to examine them.

## 51. Cognitive Distortions

Cognitive distortions are faulty patterns of thinking that distort reality in a negative direction.

Examples include:

- Overgeneralising from one event.
- Ignoring positive evidence.
- Thinking in all-or-nothing terms.
- Assuming the worst outcome.
- Treating feelings as facts.

The therapist uses gentle questioning to help the client notice and challenge these distortions.

## 52. Cognitive Restructuring

Cognitive restructuring means changing dysfunctional cognitive structures.

The therapist may ask questions such as:

- What evidence supports this thought?
- What evidence goes against it?
- Is there another explanation?
- What would you say to a friend in the same situation?
- Is the conclusion too extreme?

The goal is not forced positive thinking. The goal is realistic and balanced thinking.

## 53. Cognitive Behaviour Therapy

Cognitive Behaviour Therapy, or CBT, combines cognitive and behavioural methods.

It addresses:

- Biological aspects through relaxation or bodily regulation methods.
- Psychological aspects through cognitive and behavioural techniques.
- Social aspects through environmental changes and skill building.

CBT is popular because it is structured, problem-focused, and useful for many disorders such as anxiety and depression.

## 54. Why CBT Is Called Bio-Psycho-Social

CBT recognises that distress may involve:

- Body reactions such as tension or arousal.
- Thoughts such as fear or hopelessness.
- Behaviour such as avoidance.
- Social situations such as conflict or lack of support.

By combining methods, CBT can work on several parts of the problem at once.

## 55. Humanistic-Existential View Of Distress

Humanistic-existential therapies hold that people have an innate need for growth.

Distress may arise when this growth is blocked by:

- Social pressure.
- Family restrictions.
- Fear of emotional expression.
- Loneliness.
- Alienation.
- Meaninglessness.

Therapy helps the client become more aware and integrated.

## 56. Self-Actualisation

Self-actualisation means moving toward fuller development of one's potential.

In this view, people are not only driven by problems. They are also motivated by growth, meaning, and integration.

Blocked self-actualisation can create distress, just as blocked basic needs can create discomfort.

## 57. Free Emotional Expression

Humanistic-existential therapy values free emotional expression.

When emotions are constantly suppressed, the person may become fragmented or disconnected from real feelings.

Therapy provides a safe environment where emotions can be expressed, understood, and integrated.

## 58. Logotherapy

Viktor Frankl developed logotherapy.

Logotherapy focuses on meaning. It holds that people can find meaning even in difficult circumstances.

The therapist helps the client recognise responsibility, choice, and purpose in the present.

The emphasis is on the here and now.

## 59. Client-Centred Therapy

Carl Rogers developed client-centred therapy.

Its core conditions are:

- Empathy.
- Warmth.
- Genuineness.
- Unconditional positive regard.

The therapist reflects the client's feelings in a non-judgmental way. This helps the client reconnect with disowned or confused feelings.

## 60. Reflection In Client-Centred Therapy

Reflection means rephrasing or clarifying what the client expresses.

It is not mechanical repetition. It helps the client hear and understand personal feelings more clearly.

Example:

- Client: "I keep trying, but I feel invisible."
- Therapist reflection: "You feel that your effort is not being noticed, and that leaves you feeling unseen."

Reflection deepens self-awareness.

## 61. Gestalt Therapy

Gestalt therapy was developed by Fritz Perls and Laura Perls.

The word gestalt refers to a whole.

The goal is to increase self-awareness and self-acceptance. The client learns to notice emotions, bodily processes, and conflicts that have been blocked from awareness.

Gestalt therapy may use enactment and awareness exercises under professional guidance.

## 62. Factors Contributing To Healing

Several factors contribute to healing in psychotherapy:

- Techniques used by the therapist.
- Therapeutic alliance.
- Catharsis.
- Client motivation.
- Client expectation of improvement.
- Therapist's warmth and competence.
- Therapist's own emotional health.

Healing is not due to technique alone. The relationship and client variables also matter.

## 63. Catharsis

Catharsis means emotional unburdening.

In early sessions, a client may speak about painful experiences that have been held inside for a long time.

This expression can itself have healing value because it reduces emotional pressure and makes the problem clearer.

## 64. Non-Specific Factors

Non-specific factors are factors that help across different therapy systems.

Client variables include:

- Motivation for change.
- Hope.
- Expectation of improvement.
- Willingness to participate.

Therapist variables include:

- Warmth.
- Good mental health.
- Skill.
- Absence of unresolved emotional conflicts that interfere with therapy.

## 65. Ethics In Psychotherapy

Professional psychotherapy must follow ethical standards.

Major ethical standards include:

- Informed consent.
- Confidentiality.
- Relief of distress as the goal.
- Integrity of the practitioner-client relationship.
- Respect for human rights and dignity.
- Professional competence and skill.

Ethics protect the client and the profession.

## 66. Informed Consent

Informed consent means the client understands the nature of therapy and agrees to participate.

It may include information about:

- Goals.
- Methods.
- Confidentiality.
- Limits of confidentiality.
- Fees, if relevant.
- Expected responsibilities.
- Possible discomfort during therapy.

Consent supports autonomy and trust.

## 67. Alternative Therapies In The Chapter

The chapter mentions practices such as yoga, meditation, mindfulness-based meditation, breathing practices, and related approaches.

They are presented as possible supports or adjuncts for well-being and some forms of distress. They should not be treated as replacements for professional mental health care when a disorder is severe, risky, or disabling.

Proper training and guidance are important.

## 68. Yoga

Yoga is an ancient Indian practice associated with Patanjali's Yoga Sutras.

In common use, yoga may refer to:

- Asanas or body postures.
- Pranayama or breathing practices.
- A combination of posture, breathing, and attention.

The chapter links yoga with well-being, mood, attention, mental focus, and stress tolerance.

## 69. Meditation And Mindfulness

Meditation involves focusing attention on breath, an object, a thought, or a mantra.

Mindfulness-based meditation, also called Vipasana in the chapter, involves observing bodily sensations and thoughts without clinging to a fixed object.

These practices may help people observe thoughts and emotions with greater awareness.

## 70. Rehabilitation

Treatment has two major components:

- Reduction of symptoms.
- Improvement of functioning and quality of life.

For some mild disorders, symptom reduction may quickly improve life quality. For severe disorders such as schizophrenia, reducing active symptoms may not be enough.

Rehabilitation helps the person become more self-sufficient and socially functional.

## 71. Why Rehabilitation Is Needed

Some people may continue to experience:

- Low motivation.
- Social withdrawal.
- Poor work habits.
- Cognitive difficulties.
- Reduced daily functioning.
- Difficulty interacting with others.

Rehabilitation aims to improve practical functioning, not just reduce symptoms.

## 72. Occupational Therapy

Occupational therapy teaches work-related habits and practical skills.

Examples in the chapter include:

- Candle making.
- Paper bag making.
- Weaving.

The deeper goal is work discipline, routine, responsibility, and productive engagement.

## 73. Social Skills Training

Social skills training helps the person function better in social settings.

It may use:

- Role play.
- Imitation.
- Instruction.
- Feedback.

The aim is to improve communication, cooperation, and social participation.

## 74. Cognitive Retraining

Cognitive retraining works on basic cognitive functions such as:

- Attention.
- Memory.
- Planning.
- Executive functions.

This is important when a disorder affects thinking and daily functioning.

## 75. Vocational Training

Vocational training helps the person gain skills for productive employment.

It is usually considered after the person has improved enough to benefit from work-related training.

The goal is dignity, self-reliance, and meaningful participation in society.

## 76. Comparing Major Therapies

| Therapy | Main cause emphasised | Main method | Main benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Psychodynamic | Intrapsychic conflicts | Free association, dreams, interpretation | Emotional insight |
| Behaviour | Faulty learning and maladaptive behaviour | Behavioural analysis and learning techniques | Adaptive behaviour |
| Cognitive | Faulty beliefs and distorted thinking | Questioning, disputation, restructuring | Balanced thinking |
| CBT | Biological, psychological, and social factors | Cognitive plus behavioural methods | Practical symptom reduction and coping |
| Humanistic-existential | Blocked growth, loneliness, meaninglessness | Accepting therapeutic environment | Personal growth and self-awareness |

## 77. Common Student Confusions

Psychotherapy is not the same as giving advice.

Empathy is not the same as sympathy.

Negative reinforcement is not punishment.

CBT is not only "thinking positive"; it is realistic cognitive and behavioural change.

Confidentiality is central, but it exists within professional ethical limits.

Alternative therapies may support well-being, but they do not remove the need for trained professional care when serious problems exist.

## 78. Model Answer: Nature And Scope Of Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy is a voluntary professional relationship between a client and a trained therapist. Its purpose is to reduce psychological distress, modify maladaptive behaviour, change faulty thinking patterns, increase self-awareness, and improve adjustment. It uses principles from psychological theories and is practised only by trained professionals. The therapeutic relationship is central because trust, empathy, confidentiality, warmth, and unconditional positive regard allow the client to express problems honestly and work toward change.

## 79. Model Answer: Therapeutic Relationship

The therapeutic relationship, or alliance, is a professional partnership between client and therapist. It is contractual and limited in duration. It is based on trust, empathy, acceptance, confidentiality, and professional boundaries. The therapist does not judge or exploit the client. This relationship becomes the vehicle for change because the client feels safe enough to explore painful thoughts, emotions, and behaviours.

## 80. Model Answer: Behaviour Therapy Techniques

Behaviour therapy is based on learning principles. It begins with behavioural analysis, which identifies malfunctioning behaviours, antecedent factors, and maintaining factors. Techniques include negative reinforcement, aversive conditioning, positive reinforcement, token economy, systematic desensitisation, and modelling. The goal is to remove maladaptive behaviours and replace them with adaptive behaviours.

## 81. Model Answer: Cognitive Distortions

Cognitive distortions are faulty ways of thinking that distort reality in a negative direction. For example, if a student fails one test and thinks, "I will fail in everything," the student is overgeneralising. Cognitive therapy helps the client examine such thoughts, test evidence, and replace extreme conclusions with more realistic interpretations. This process is called cognitive restructuring.

## 83. Quick Revision

- Psychotherapy is a professional psychological treatment.
- The therapeutic alliance is central to change.
- Empathy means understanding the client's perspective.
- Unconditional positive regard means non-judgmental acceptance.
- Confidentiality protects trust.
- Clinical formulation guides treatment.
- Psychodynamic therapy focuses on intrapsychic conflict and insight.
- Behaviour therapy focuses on faulty learning and behaviour change.
- Cognitive therapy focuses on distorted thinking and beliefs.
- CBT combines cognitive and behavioural methods.
- Humanistic-existential therapy focuses on growth, meaning, and self-awareness.
- Healing comes from techniques, alliance, catharsis, and client and therapist variables.
- Ethics include consent, confidentiality, dignity, competence, and integrity.
- Rehabilitation improves functioning and quality of life.
