## 1. Big Picture

This chapter connects psychology with real-life concerns. It shows that behaviour does not occur in isolation. People influence the environment, and the environment influences people. Psychological principles can help us understand crowding, noise, disasters, poverty, discrimination, aggression, violence, peace, media influence, and environmental responsibility.

The central pattern is:

```text
Environment/social condition
-> psychological experience
-> behaviour
-> consequences
-> possible intervention
```

## 2. Human-Environment Relationship

Human beings and the environment influence each other continuously.

| Direction | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Environment affects humans | Physical surroundings shape feelings and behaviour. | Noise can reduce concentration and increase irritation. |
| Humans affect environment | Human actions change natural and built surroundings. | Pollution, deforestation, waste, conservation. |
| Interactional view | Humans and environment form a two-way system. | Crowded cities shape behaviour, and human planning shapes cities. |

## 3. Views of Human-Environment Relationship

| View | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Minimalist view | Physical environment has little influence; human behaviour is mainly shaped by social/cultural factors. |
| Instrumental view | Environment exists mainly as a resource for human use. |
| Spiritual view | Environment is sacred and must be respected. |
| Ecological view | Humans are part of nature and must maintain balance with it. |

The ecological view is most useful for sustainable living because it recognises interdependence.

## 4. Environmental Effects on Behaviour

### Noise

Noise is unwanted, unpleasant, or disturbing sound.

Effects of noise:

1. Irritation.
2. Headache.
3. Fatigue.
4. Reduced concentration.
5. Sleep disturbance.
6. Lower task performance.
7. Increased stress.
8. Possible increase in aggression.

Noise becomes more harmful when it is loud, unpredictable, uncontrollable, and prolonged.

### Crowding

Crowding is the subjective feeling of having too many people in a limited space. It is not the same as density.

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Density | Number of people per unit space; objective measure. |
| Crowding | Feeling of discomfort due to too many people; psychological experience. |

Effects of crowding:

1. Stress.
2. Loss of privacy.
3. Irritability.
4. Reduced helping behaviour.
5. Conflict.
6. Lower performance on complex tasks.
7. Feeling of lack of control.

### Personal Space

Personal space is the comfortable physical distance a person maintains from others.

It is important because it protects:

1. Privacy.
2. Comfort.
3. Sense of control.
4. Emotional safety.

Violation of personal space can produce discomfort, anger, or withdrawal.

## 5. Natural Disasters and Trauma

Disasters include events such as earthquakes, floods, cyclones, fires, industrial accidents, and other sudden destructive events.

### Psychological Effects

1. Shock.
2. Fear.
3. Grief.
4. Anxiety.
5. Sleep problems.
6. Helplessness.
7. Survivor guilt.
8. Post-traumatic stress symptoms.

### Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

PTSD may involve:

1. Intrusive memories.
2. Nightmares.
3. Avoidance of reminders.
4. Hypervigilance.
5. Emotional numbness.
6. Irritability.
7. Difficulty sleeping.

### Support and Remedy

1. Psychological first aid.
2. Safety and shelter.
3. Social support.
4. Counselling.
5. Restoring routine.
6. Community support.
7. Professional treatment when symptoms persist.

## 6. Human Influence on the Environment

Humans damage the environment through:

1. Pollution.
2. Overuse of resources.
3. Deforestation.
4. Waste generation.
5. Noise creation.
6. Unsustainable consumption.

Psychology helps explain why people may ignore environmental damage: habit, convenience, lack of awareness, diffusion of responsibility, and short-term thinking.

## 7. Pro-Environmental Behaviour

Pro-environmental behaviour means actions that protect or improve the environment.

Examples:

1. Saving water.
2. Reducing electricity use.
3. Avoiding unnecessary plastic.
4. Using public transport.
5. Planting trees.
6. Recycling.
7. Reducing noise.
8. Supporting conservation.

### Strategies to Promote It

| Strategy | How It Helps |
| --- | --- |
| Awareness | People understand consequences. |
| Modelling | People imitate responsible behaviour. |
| Incentives | Rewards encourage change. |
| Feedback | People see impact of their action. |
| Social norms | Group expectations guide behaviour. |
| Commitment | Public promises increase follow-through. |

## 8. Poverty and Discrimination

Poverty is not only lack of money. It can involve lack of education, health care, safety, housing, dignity, and opportunities.

### Psychological Effects of Poverty

1. Low self-esteem.
2. Hopelessness.
3. Stress.
4. Anxiety.
5. Reduced sense of control.
6. Social exclusion.
7. Lower educational opportunity.
8. Intergenerational disadvantage.

### Poverty and Discrimination

Discrimination can keep people poor by denying access to education, jobs, housing, respect, and opportunities. Poverty can also increase vulnerability to further discrimination.

## 9. Aggression, Violence, and Peace

### Aggression

Aggression is behaviour intended to harm another person or object.

### Types

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hostile aggression | Aggression driven by anger and desire to harm. | Hitting someone in rage. |
| Instrumental aggression | Aggression used to achieve another goal. | Threatening someone to get money. |

### Causes of Aggression

1. Biological tendencies.
2. Frustration.
3. Learning and imitation.
4. Harsh child-rearing.
5. Media violence.
6. Alcohol or drugs.
7. Crowding and heat.
8. Perceived injustice.
9. Group prejudice.

### Frustration-Aggression

Frustration occurs when a goal is blocked. It can increase aggression, but it does not always cause aggression. Other factors also matter.

### Displacement

Displacement occurs when aggression is directed toward a weaker or safer target rather than the real source of frustration.

## 10. Reducing Aggression and Violence

Strategies:

1. Teach emotional regulation.
2. Reduce frustration and injustice.
3. Encourage non-violent conflict resolution.
4. Reduce exposure to violent models.
5. Promote empathy.
6. Improve communication.
7. Use positive parenting.
8. Build cooperative group activities.
9. Challenge prejudice.
10. Encourage peace education.

## 11. Television and Behaviour

Television and screen media can influence behaviour depending on content, viewing time, age, supervision, and interpretation.

Possible effects:

1. Learning information.
2. Imitating models.
3. Desensitisation to violence.
4. Consumerism.
5. Stereotype formation.
6. Reduced physical activity.
7. Fear or anxiety from disturbing content.

Reducing negative effects:

1. Select age-appropriate content.
2. Limit viewing time.
3. Discuss content critically.
4. Encourage educational programming.
5. Balance screen time with reading, play, and social interaction.

## 12. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Treating environment as only nature | Environment includes physical, social, cultural, and built surroundings. |
| Confusing density and crowding | Density is objective; crowding is subjective. |
| Saying frustration always causes aggression | Frustration may increase aggression but does not always cause it. |
| Ignoring social causes of poverty | Poverty is linked to discrimination, opportunity, and social structure. |
| Seeing disaster response as only physical relief | Psychological support is also essential. |

## 13. Exam Answer Frames

### Human-Environment Relationship

Human beings affect and are affected by the environment. Noise, crowding, pollution, and disasters influence stress, health, emotions, and behaviour. At the same time, human actions such as deforestation, waste generation, and conservation change the environment. Therefore, the relationship is interactional.

### Crowding

Crowding is the subjective feeling that available space is insufficient. It differs from density, which is an objective measure of number of people per unit space. Crowding can produce stress, irritation, loss of privacy, reduced control, and lower performance.

### Aggression

Aggression is behaviour intended to harm. Hostile aggression is driven by anger, while instrumental aggression is used to achieve another goal. Aggression may arise from frustration, learning, harsh discipline, biological arousal, media models, or social injustice. It can be reduced through empathy, non-violent conflict resolution, emotional control, and positive social conditions.

## 16. Final Learning Takeaway

Psychology is useful because it explains everyday social and environmental problems in terms of behaviour, emotion, perception, stress, learning, and social influence. Understanding these links helps us design better environments, reduce violence, support disaster survivors, challenge discrimination, and build more responsible behaviour.
