# Chapter 7: Social Influence And Group Processes

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how groups are formed, why people join them, how groups develop structure, and how groups influence individual behaviour. It also explains important social processes such as groupthink, ingroup-outgroup bias, social loafing, social facilitation, and group polarisation.

The main idea is that people do not act only as isolated individuals. Much of human behaviour happens inside families, classes, teams, peer groups, institutions, communities, and societies. Groups can support growth, identity, security, and achievement, but they can also create pressure, poor decision-making, reduced effort, and extreme opinions.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Groups shape identity, behaviour, effort, decision-making, and social judgments by creating interdependence, roles, norms, status, belonging, and pressure.

## 2. Why Groups Matter

Human life is organised through groups.

People are part of:

- Families.
- Classes.
- Friend circles.
- Teams.
- Neighbourhoods.
- Workplaces.
- Religious groups.
- Political groups.
- Online communities.
- Nations.

Groups provide support and identity, but they also influence what people think, how they behave, and how much effort they put in.

## 3. What Is A Group?

A group is an organised system of two or more individuals who:

- Interact with one another.
- Are interdependent.
- Have common motives or goals.
- Have role relationships.
- Follow norms regulating behaviour.
- Perceive themselves as belonging to the group.

This definition is more precise than simply saying "many people together."

## 4. Group Versus Collection Of People

Not every collection of people is a group.

People waiting at a bus stop may stand near each other, but they may not have:

- Common goals.
- Role relationships.
- Interdependence.
- Shared norms.
- A sense of belonging.

A family, class, or playgroup is different because members interact, influence each other, and have expectations from one another.

## 5. Main Characteristics Of Groups

Groups have several characteristics:

- Two or more individuals.
- A sense of membership.
- Common motives and goals.
- Interdependence.
- Interaction.
- Roles.
- Norms.
- Expectations.
- Group identity.

These characteristics help distinguish groups from crowds or audiences.

## 6. Group Identity

Group identity means members perceive themselves as belonging to the group.

This creates a sense of "we."

Group identity can provide:

- Belonging.
- Pride.
- Security.
- Meaning.
- Direction.

But it can also create bias if "we" becomes opposed to "they."

## 7. Common Motives And Goals

Groups usually exist because members share motives or goals.

Examples:

- A football team wants to win matches.
- A study group wants to prepare for exams.
- A committee wants to organise an event.
- A family wants safety and care.

Common goals make members coordinate effort.

## 8. Interdependence

Interdependence means members affect one another.

Example:

If a fielder drops an important catch in a cricket match, the whole team is affected.

In a group, one person's behaviour can change the outcome for others.

## 9. Interaction

Group members interact directly or indirectly.

Direct interaction may include conversation, cooperation, and shared work.

Indirect interaction may occur through rules, messages, or coordinated roles.

Interaction creates regular patterns in group life.

## 10. Roles And Norms

Roles and norms make group behaviour organised.

Roles tell members what they are expected to do.

Norms tell members what behaviour is acceptable.

Without roles and norms, group activity becomes confused and unstable.

## 11. Crowd

A crowd is a collection of people present at the same place, often by chance.

Example:

People gathering after a road accident.

A crowd usually lacks:

- Structure.
- Stable roles.
- Mutual interdependence.
- Strong belongingness.

Crowd behaviour may become impulsive or irrational in some situations.

## 12. Audience

An audience is a collection of people assembled for a purpose such as watching a match, film, speech, or school function.

An audience is usually passive.

However, if strong emotion spreads, an audience may become more active and may even turn into a mob.

## 13. Mob

A mob is a collection of people with a definite purpose, common direction, and high emotional arousal.

Mob behaviour may involve:

- Homogeneity of thought.
- Impulsivity.
- Strong shared attention.
- Reduced individual restraint.

Mobs are more intense and potentially more dangerous than ordinary crowds.

## 14. Team

A team is a special kind of group.

Team members usually have:

- Complementary skills.
- Commitment to a common goal.
- Mutual accountability.
- Coordinated effort.
- Positive synergy.

All teams are groups, but not all groups are teams.

## 15. Group Versus Team

In a group, performance may depend mainly on individual contributions.

In a team, both individual contribution and teamwork matter.

In a group, responsibility may be held mainly by the leader.

In a team, members hold themselves mutually responsible.

## 16. Why People Join Groups

People join groups because groups satisfy important needs.

Major reasons include:

- Security.
- Status.
- Self-esteem.
- Psychological and social needs.
- Goal achievement.
- Knowledge and information.

Different groups may satisfy different needs at the same time.

## 17. Security

Being alone may create insecurity.

Groups provide:

- Comfort.
- Protection.
- Support.
- Strength.
- Reduced vulnerability.

This is one reason people prefer belonging to families, peer groups, and communities.

## 18. Status

Membership in a respected group can increase status.

Example:

If a school wins an interinstitutional debate, students may feel proud to belong to that school.

Status gives recognition and a sense of power.

## 19. Self-Esteem

Groups can increase self-worth.

Belonging to a valued group strengthens social identity.

Example:

A student may feel more confident when part of a successful team, club, or class.

## 20. Psychological And Social Needs

Groups satisfy needs such as:

- Belongingness.
- Love.
- Attention.
- Friendship.
- Support.
- Influence.
- Shared enjoyment.

Humans need connection, and groups provide a setting for it.

## 21. Goal Achievement

Some goals cannot be achieved alone.

Example:

Teaching children in a slum area may be difficult for one student, but a group of students can divide the work and continue regularly.

Groups provide combined effort and resources.

## 22. Knowledge And Information

Groups broaden knowledge.

Members share:

- Experiences.
- Skills.
- Information.
- Opinions.
- Feedback.

No individual knows everything. Groups fill gaps in knowledge.

## 23. Pressure From Multiple Group Memberships

People belong to many groups at once.

Example:

A student may be a family member, classmate, friend, team member, and club member.

Different groups may create different expectations. This can create pressure when demands conflict.

## 24. Group Formation

Groups form when people come into contact and begin interacting.

Three important conditions help group formation:

- Proximity.
- Similarity.
- Common motives and goals.

These conditions increase the chance that people will know, like, and coordinate with each other.

## 25. Proximity

Proximity means physical or social nearness.

People are more likely to form groups when they:

- Live near each other.
- Study in the same class.
- Play in the same area.
- Work in the same office.

Repeated contact provides opportunity for interaction.

## 26. Similarity

Similarity means shared interests, attitudes, background, or values.

People often like others who are similar because:

- Similarity creates consistency.
- Similar others validate opinions.
- Shared interests make interaction easier.

Example:

Two students who both love football may become friends more easily.

## 27. Common Motives And Goals In Formation

People may form groups because they want to achieve something together.

Example:

Students who want to help children learn may form a teaching group.

The goal creates cooperation and gives the group direction.

## 28. Stages Of Group Formation

Bruce Tuckman suggested that groups often pass through five stages:

- Forming.
- Storming.
- Norming.
- Performing.
- Adjourning.

These stages describe how a group develops over time.

## 29. Forming Stage

In the forming stage, members first come together.

There is uncertainty about:

- Goals.
- Rules.
- Roles.
- Leadership.
- Acceptance.
- Future direction.

Members try to know each other and judge whether they fit into the group.

## 30. Storming Stage

The storming stage involves conflict.

Members may disagree about:

- Who will lead.
- How goals should be achieved.
- Who will do which task.
- How resources should be used.
- What standards should be followed.

This stage may be uncomfortable but can clarify power and direction.

## 31. Norming Stage

In the norming stage, group norms develop.

Members begin to agree on:

- Expected behaviour.
- Working style.
- Communication.
- Responsibilities.
- Group identity.

The group becomes more stable.

## 32. Performing Stage

In the performing stage, the group works toward its goals effectively.

The structure is accepted, roles are clearer, and members coordinate effort.

For many groups, this is the main productive stage.

## 33. Adjourning Stage

Some groups end after completing their purpose.

Example:

A committee formed for a school function may disband after the event.

This is the adjourning stage.

## 34. Stages Are Not Always Linear

Groups do not always move neatly through all stages.

Sometimes:

- Stages overlap.
- Groups skip stages.
- Groups return to earlier stages.
- Conflict appears after performance begins.

Tuckman's model is a guide, not a rigid rule.

## 35. Group Structure

Group structure develops as members interact.

It includes regular patterns in:

- Tasks.
- Responsibilities.
- Status.
- Roles.
- Norms.
- Communication.

Structure helps members know how the group functions.

## 36. Roles

Roles are socially defined expectations that individuals are expected to fulfil.

Examples:

- A captain is expected to guide the team.
- A teacher is expected to teach and guide.
- A student is expected to study and follow school rules.
- A son or daughter may be expected to respect elders and take responsibility.

Roles make behaviour predictable.

## 37. Role Expectations

Role expectations are behaviours expected from a person in a particular role.

If someone occupies a role but does not fulfil expectations, group tension may arise.

Example:

If a team captain refuses to coordinate members, the team may become confused.

## 38. Norms

Norms are expected standards of behaviour and belief established by group members.

They are often unspoken rules.

Examples:

- Speak respectfully in class.
- Attend practice on time.
- Help family members when needed.
- Do not interrupt during meetings.

Norms regulate behaviour without needing constant instruction.

## 39. Status

Status refers to relative social position in a group.

Status may be:

- Ascribed: given due to age, seniority, background, or position.
- Achieved: earned through skill, effort, expertise, or contribution.

Status affects influence, respect, and responsibility.

## 40. Status Within Groups

Members of the same group may have different status.

Example:

In a cricket team, the captain may have higher status, but all members are important for success.

High status can increase influence, but it also creates responsibility.

## 41. Cohesiveness

Cohesiveness means togetherness, mutual attraction, and "we feeling" among group members.

Highly cohesive groups:

- Attract members.
- Make members want to remain.
- Increase loyalty.
- Strengthen group identity.
- Encourage cooperation.

Cohesiveness is usually useful, but extreme cohesion can create problems.

## 42. Groupthink

Groupthink is a process in which a cohesive group values unanimity so strongly that it stops evaluating decisions realistically.

Members may avoid disagreement because they fear disturbing harmony.

The group may appear united but make poor decisions.

## 43. Conditions That Encourage Groupthink

Groupthink is more likely when groups are:

- Very cohesive.
- Socially homogeneous.
- Isolated from outside views.
- Under pressure.
- Led in a closed manner.
- Without a tradition of considering alternatives.
- Facing high-cost decisions.

These conditions reduce critical thinking.

## 44. Signs Of Groupthink

Groupthink may involve:

- Illusion of agreement.
- Suppression of dissent.
- Ignoring warning signs.
- Overconfidence.
- Seeing outsiders as wrong or weak.
- Pressure on members who question the plan.
- Failure to examine alternatives.

The group protects harmony at the cost of reality testing.

## 45. Consequences Of Groupthink

Groupthink can lead to:

- Irrational decisions.
- Poor planning.
- Ignoring risks.
- Moral blindness.
- Overconfidence.
- Major failures.

The textbook mentions the Vietnam War escalation as an example of disastrous group decision-making.

## 46. Preventing Groupthink

Groupthink can be reduced by:

- Encouraging critical thinking.
- Rewarding reasoned disagreement.
- Asking for alternative plans.
- Inviting outside experts.
- Seeking feedback from trusted outsiders.
- Separating evaluation from pressure for harmony.

Healthy groups allow disagreement without treating it as disloyalty.

## 47. Types Of Groups

The chapter describes major types of groups:

- Primary and secondary groups.
- Formal and informal groups.
- Ingroup and outgroup.

These categories help compare different group relationships.

## 48. Primary Groups

Primary groups are close, face-to-face groups that are usually given to the individual.

Examples:

- Family.
- Caste group.
- Religious group in many contexts.

Primary groups involve emotional bonds and play a major role in early socialisation.

## 49. Secondary Groups

Secondary groups are usually joined by choice and are more impersonal.

Examples:

- Political party.
- Professional association.
- Club.
- Committee.

Relationships are usually less close, less frequent, and more goal-oriented than in primary groups.

## 50. Primary Versus Secondary Groups

| Basis | Primary group | Secondary group |
|---|---|---|
| Formation | Usually given | Often chosen |
| Interaction | Close and face-to-face | More indirect or formal |
| Bonds | Warm and emotional | More impersonal |
| Duration | Often long-lasting | May be temporary |
| Example | Family | Political party |

## 51. Formal Groups

Formal groups are formed according to explicit rules or laws.

They have:

- Stated goals.
- Defined roles.
- Clear structure.
- Formal norms.
- Organised authority.

Examples include universities, offices, army units, and committees.

## 52. Informal Groups

Informal groups form through personal relationships.

They are not created by formal rules.

Examples:

- Friend circles.
- Peer groups.
- Lunch groups.
- Neighbourhood playgroups.

Informal groups may still have strong norms and influence.

## 53. Ingroup

An ingroup is one's own group.

People use "we" for the ingroup.

Ingroup members are often viewed as:

- Similar.
- Trustworthy.
- Favourable.
- Deserving.
- Close.

This can support identity but can also produce bias.

## 54. Outgroup

An outgroup is a group to which a person does not belong.

People often use "they" for outgroups.

Outgroup members may be seen as:

- Different.
- Less favourable.
- Less trustworthy.
- Less deserving.

These perceptions can create prejudice and conflict.

## 55. Ingroup-Outgroup Distinction

The ingroup-outgroup distinction is created by categorisation.

People divide the social world into "we" and "they."

This categorisation is common, but it is not always based on real or deep differences.

India's plural culture shows that multiple identities can coexist.

## 56. Minimal Group Paradigm

Henri Tajfel studied the minimum conditions needed for intergroup behaviour.

In the minimal group paradigm, children were grouped according to preference for paintings by two artists, Kandinsky and Klee.

The groups had no past history, no real conflict, and no meaningful future.

Yet participants still favoured their own group when distributing money.

## 57. Meaning Of Minimal Group Findings

The minimal group findings show that even a weak group label can create ingroup favouritism.

This means bias does not always require deep hatred or long conflict.

Sometimes categorisation itself is enough to produce unequal treatment.

## 58. Influence Of Groups On Individual Behaviour

Groups influence individual behaviour in many ways.

This chapter focuses especially on:

- Social facilitation.
- Social loafing.
- Group polarisation.

These processes show that the presence of others changes performance, effort, and opinions.

## 59. Social Facilitation

Social facilitation refers to the effect of others' presence on performance.

The presence of others may improve performance when:

- The task is simple.
- The task is well learned.
- The person is skilled.
- Individual performance is being evaluated.

But the presence of others may worsen performance when:

- The task is new.
- The task is difficult.
- The person lacks skill.
- Arousal creates pressure.

## 60. Social Facilitation Example

A skilled dancer may perform better in front of an audience because the audience increases arousal and motivation.

A beginner may make more mistakes in front of the same audience because arousal interferes with concentration.

The effect depends on task difficulty and skill level.

## 61. Social Loafing

Social loafing is a reduction in individual effort when working on a collective task.

It happens when individual contributions are pooled and cannot be easily identified.

Example:

In tug-of-war, it may be difficult to know exactly how much force each person contributes.

Some members may reduce effort and become free riders.

## 62. Latane's Study

Latane and associates studied noise-making tasks such as clapping or cheering.

As group size increased, total noise increased, but the noise produced by each individual decreased.

This showed that individual effort may drop when people work in larger groups.

## 63. Why Social Loafing Occurs

Social loafing may occur because:

- Individual effort is not identifiable.
- Members feel less responsible.
- Individual contribution is not evaluated.
- Motivation decreases.
- Coordination is poor.
- Group outcome is not compared with other groups.
- Members do not value group membership.

The common factor is reduced personal accountability.

## 64. Reducing Social Loafing

Social loafing can be reduced by:

- Making each person's effort identifiable.
- Increasing commitment to the task.
- Showing the importance of the task.
- Making each contribution feel necessary.
- Strengthening group cohesiveness.
- Comparing group performance with standards.
- Assigning clear roles.

Good group design reduces opportunities for free riding.

## 65. Group Polarisation

Group polarisation means the strengthening of a group's initial position after group interaction.

If members begin with a mildly favourable view, the group may become more strongly favourable after interaction.

If members begin with a negative view, the group may become more strongly negative.

The group moves toward a more extreme version of its starting tendency.

## 66. Group Polarisation Example

Suppose members of a group already favour strict punishment for a corrupt employee.

After talking with like-minded people, they may move from favouring mild punishment to demanding termination.

The group interaction strengthens the initial position.

## 67. Causes Of Group Polarisation

Group polarisation occurs for several reasons:

- Members hear new arguments supporting their existing view.
- Agreement from others validates the view.
- Members want to fit in with the ingroup.
- Like-minded people increase confidence.
- The group creates pressure toward the dominant position.

These factors make initial opinions stronger.

## 68. Bandwagon Effect

The bandwagon effect occurs when people strengthen a view because many others appear to support it.

In group polarisation, hearing others agree may make a person feel the view is correct and socially validated.

This can push the group toward extremes.

## 69. Group Polarisation And Risk

Group polarisation can be harmful when it produces extreme decisions without careful evaluation.

It may affect:

- Political opinions.
- Punishment decisions.
- Intergroup conflict.
- Online debates.
- School group choices.
- Organisational decisions.

The remedy is not silence, but balanced exposure to evidence and alternative viewpoints.

## 70. Comparing Groupthink And Group Polarisation

| Basis | Groupthink | Group polarisation |
|---|---|---|
| Main problem | Pressure for unanimity reduces critical thinking | Group's initial view becomes stronger |
| Key condition | High cohesion and closed decision-making | Interaction among like-minded members |
| Main risk | Poor, unrealistic decisions | Extreme positions |
| Prevention | Encourage criticism and alternatives | Include diverse views and evidence |

## 71. Comparing Social Facilitation And Social Loafing

| Basis | Social facilitation | Social loafing |
|---|---|---|
| Situation | Individual performs in presence of others | Individual works as part of pooled group task |
| Effect | Performance may improve or worsen | Individual effort usually decreases |
| Key factor | Arousal and evaluation | Identifiability and responsibility |
| Example | Performing before an audience | Pulling in tug-of-war |

## 72. Constructive And Harmful Effects Of Groups

Groups can be constructive when they provide:

- Support.
- Identity.
- Cooperation.
- Skill development.
- Shared knowledge.
- Motivation.
- Protection.

Groups can be harmful when they produce:

- Blind conformity.
- Prejudice.
- Social loafing.
- Groupthink.
- Extreme decisions.
- Suppression of individuality.

The goal is not to avoid groups, but to understand them wisely.

## 73. Common Student Confusions

A crowd is not automatically a group.

A team is a specialised group with mutual accountability.

Cohesiveness is useful, but extreme cohesion may create groupthink.

Ingroup identity is normal, but ingroup bias can lead to unfair treatment of outgroups.

Social facilitation is not always improvement; it depends on task difficulty and skill.

Social loafing is about reduced individual effort in collective tasks.

Group polarisation is not the same as groupthink.

## 74. Model Answer: Define A Group

A group is an organised system of two or more individuals who interact and are interdependent, have common motives or goals, possess role relationships, and follow norms that regulate behaviour. A family, class, or playgroup is a group because members influence one another, have expectations, and share a sense of belonging. A crowd is different because it may lack structure, interdependence, and stable roles.

## 75. Model Answer: Why People Join Groups

People join groups because groups satisfy several needs. Groups provide security by reducing feelings of vulnerability. They provide status when membership is valued by others. They enhance self-esteem and social identity. They satisfy psychological and social needs such as belongingness, love, attention, and power. Groups also help achieve goals that cannot be achieved alone and provide knowledge and information.

## 76. Model Answer: Tuckman's Stages

Tuckman described five stages of group formation. In forming, members first come together and feel uncertainty. In storming, conflict appears over goals, leadership, and roles. In norming, members develop norms and a positive group identity. In performing, the group works toward its goal effectively. In adjourning, some groups disband after completing their purpose. Groups may not always follow these stages in a fixed order.

## 77. Model Answer: Groupthink

Groupthink occurs when a cohesive group values unanimity so much that it fails to evaluate alternatives realistically. Members avoid dissent because they fear harming group harmony. This can lead to irrational decisions, ignoring danger signals, and overconfidence. It can be prevented by encouraging critical thinking, rewarding reasoned disagreement, inviting outside experts, and considering alternative plans.

## 78. Model Answer: Social Loafing

Social loafing is the reduction of individual effort when people work on a collective task and individual contributions are not easily identifiable. It occurs because members feel less responsible, expect that their effort will not be evaluated, or do not value the task. It can be reduced by making each person's effort identifiable, increasing commitment, showing the task's importance, strengthening cohesiveness, and assigning clear roles.
