# Chapter 2: Self and Personality

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how people understand themselves and how their relatively stable patterns of behaviour form personality. It studies the self, self-esteem, self-efficacy, self-regulation, culture and self, personality theories, and methods of personality assessment.

The central idea is that self and personality are closely related. Self lies at the core of personality. How people define themselves, evaluate themselves, regulate themselves, and relate to others shapes their behaviour across situations.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Define self and personality.
- Distinguish personal identity and social identity.
- Explain self as subject and self as object.
- Describe personal self, social self, and relational self.
- Explain self-concept, self-esteem, self-efficacy, and self-regulation.
- Compare Indian and western views of self.
- Identify major features of personality.
- Compare type and trait approaches.
- Explain psychodynamic, behavioural, cultural, and humanistic approaches.
- Understand major personality assessment methods.
- Explain self-report measures, indirect techniques, and behavioural analysis.

## 1. Self And Personality

Self and personality refer to the characteristic ways in which people define their existence and organise experience.

Self answers questions such as:

- Who am I?
- What makes me different from others?
- What groups do I belong to?
- What do I value?
- What can I do?

Personality answers questions such as:

- Why do people behave differently in the same situation?
- Why does a person behave in a fairly stable way across time?
- What makes a person unique?

## 2. Meaning Of Self

Self refers to the totality of an individual's conscious experiences, ideas, thoughts, and feelings regarding themselves.

It develops through:

- Interaction with parents.
- Interaction with friends.
- Teachers.
- Significant others.
- Personal experiences.
- Social and cultural environment.

A newborn does not have a developed idea of self. Self develops gradually through experience.

## 3. Personal Identity

Personal identity refers to attributes that make a person different from others.

Examples:

- Name.
- Honesty.
- Hard work.
- Singing ability.
- Dancing ability.
- Belief in God.
- Interest in science.
- Personal goals.

Personal identity gives a sense of uniqueness.

## 4. Social Identity

Social identity refers to aspects of self derived from membership in social or cultural groups.

Examples:

- Hindu.
- Muslim.
- Sikh.
- Christian.
- Brahmin.
- Adivasi.
- North Indian.
- South Indian.
- Student.
- Member of a sports team.

Social identity links the person with a wider group.

## 5. Self As Subject And Object

Self has a dual status.

As subject, the self is the knower. It thinks, acts, observes, and makes choices.

As object, the self is what is known. A person can observe, describe, evaluate, and judge themselves.

When a person says, "I know who I am," the self is both the knower and the known.

## 6. Kinds Of Self

Different kinds of self develop through interaction with biological, psychological, social, and cultural environments.

Major forms include:

- Biological self.
- Personal self.
- Social self.
- Relational self.

## 7. Biological Self

The biological self begins with bodily needs and awareness.

For example, a hungry child cries. Later the child develops awareness such as "I am hungry."

Even biological needs are shaped by culture. A child in one culture may desire chocolate, while another may desire a different food.

## 8. Personal Self

The personal self emphasises:

- Personal freedom.
- Personal achievement.
- Personal responsibility.
- Personal comfort.
- Individual goals.

It focuses on the person as an individual.

## 9. Social Or Relational Self

The social self emerges in relation with others.

It emphasises:

- Cooperation.
- Unity.
- Family.
- Affiliation.
- Sacrifice.
- Sharing.
- Support.

It is also called familial or relational self.

## 10. Self-Concept

Self-concept is the way a person views their competencies and attributes.

A person may have:

- Positive academic self-concept.
- Negative mathematical self-concept.
- Positive athletic self-concept.
- Negative social self-concept.

Self-concept can be general or specific.

## 11. Self-Esteem

Self-esteem is the value judgment a person makes about themselves.

High self-esteem means a person feels worthy and competent.

Low self-esteem means a person may feel inadequate, disliked, or unable.

Children develop self-esteem in areas such as:

- Academic competence.
- Social competence.
- Physical or athletic competence.
- Physical appearance.

## 12. Effects Of Self-Esteem

Self-esteem affects everyday behaviour.

High academic self-esteem is linked with better school performance.

High social self-esteem is linked with peer acceptance.

Low self-esteem may be associated with:

- Anxiety.
- Depression.
- Antisocial behaviour.
- Avoidance.
- Lack of confidence.

Warm and positive parenting helps develop self-esteem.

## 13. Self-Efficacy

Self-efficacy is belief in one's ability to perform required actions in a situation.

It is based on Bandura's social learning theory.

People with high self-efficacy:

- Believe they can influence outcomes.
- Take more initiative.
- Persist longer.
- Feel less fearful.
- Select and shape life circumstances.

Self-efficacy can be developed through positive experiences, models, and support.

## 14. Self-Regulation

Self-regulation refers to the ability to organise and monitor one's own behaviour.

It includes:

- Self-monitoring.
- Will power.
- Delay of gratification.
- Self-control.
- Goal-directed behaviour.

Self-regulation helps people resist immediate pressure and work toward long-term goals.

## 15. Techniques Of Self-Control

Psychological techniques include:

- Observation of one's own behaviour.
- Self-instruction.
- Self-reinforcement.

### Observation

The person records or notices their own behaviour.

### Self-instruction

The person gives themselves instructions, such as "stay calm" or "complete one section first."

### Self-reinforcement

The person rewards desired behaviour, such as taking a break after completing study goals.

## 16. Culture And Self

Culture shapes the boundary between self and others.

Western cultures are often described as individualistic. They may emphasise:

- Individual choice.
- Personal achievement.
- Independence.
- Clear boundary between self and group.

Many Asian cultures, including Indian cultural traditions, are often described as collectivistic. They may emphasise:

- Family.
- Group harmony.
- Interdependence.
- Relational identity.
- Duty.

## 17. Indian View Of Self

The Indian view often sees self as flexible and connected.

The boundary between self and others can shift.

At one moment, the self may expand to include family, community, nature, or cosmos. At another moment, it may focus on individual needs.

This view does not sharply separate self and other, human and nature, subjective and objective.

## 18. Meaning Of Personality

The word personality comes from the Latin persona, meaning mask. In psychology, personality does not mean outer appearance.

Personality refers to unique and relatively stable qualities that characterise a person's behaviour across situations and time.

It includes:

- Thoughts.
- Emotions.
- Behaviour patterns.
- Motives.
- Attitudes.
- Adaptation style.

## 19. Features Of Personality

Personality has these features:

- It has physical and psychological components.
- Its behavioural expression is fairly unique.
- Its main features are relatively stable.
- It is dynamic and can change in response to internal or external demands.
- It helps people adapt to situations.

## 20. Type Approaches

Type approaches classify people into broad categories.

Examples include:

- Introvert and extravert.
- Type A and Type B.
- Ancient classifications based on body fluids or body types.

Type approaches are easy to understand but may oversimplify people.

## 21. Trait Approaches

Trait approaches describe personality through specific traits.

Traits are relatively stable tendencies.

Examples:

- Sociability.
- Dominance.
- Emotional stability.
- Conscientiousness.
- Openness.
- Agreeableness.

Trait approaches allow more detailed descriptions than broad types.

## 22. Allport's Trait Theory

Gordon Allport described traits at different levels.

- Cardinal traits dominate a person's whole life.
- Central traits are major characteristics.
- Secondary traits are more situation-specific.

This approach recognises that some traits are more important than others.

## 23. Cattell And Eysenck

Cattell used factor analysis to identify basic personality factors.

Eysenck described personality using broad dimensions such as:

- Extraversion versus introversion.
- Neuroticism versus emotional stability.
- Psychoticism.

These trait theories try to make personality description more systematic.

## 24. Five-Factor Model

The five-factor model describes personality through five broad dimensions:

- Openness to experience.
- Conscientiousness.
- Extraversion.
- Agreeableness.
- Neuroticism.

This model is widely used because it summarises many personality differences.

## 25. Psychodynamic Approach

The psychodynamic approach was developed by Sigmund Freud.

It emphasises:

- Unconscious motives.
- Inner conflict.
- Childhood experiences.
- Instinctual drives.
- Defence mechanisms.

Freud divided personality into id, ego, and superego.

## 26. Id, Ego, And Superego

| Part | Main function |
|---|---|
| Id | Instinctual drives and immediate pleasure |
| Ego | Reality-based decision-making and balance |
| Superego | Moral standards and conscience |

Personality conflict arises when these forces clash.

## 27. Defence Mechanisms

Defence mechanisms protect the ego from anxiety.

Examples:

- Repression.
- External attribution of one's own unacceptable thoughts or feelings.
- Rationalisation.
- Reaction formation.
- Regression.

They may reduce anxiety temporarily but can distort reality if overused.

## 28. Post-Freudian Views

Post-Freudian theorists such as Jung, Adler, Fromm, Horney, and Erikson emphasised social and interpersonal factors more than Freud did.

They studied:

- Ego development.
- Inferiority.
- Social forces.
- Culture.
- Identity.
- Interpersonal relationships.

## 29. Behavioural Approach

The behavioural approach sees personality as learned responses to the environment.

It emphasises:

- Conditioning.
- Reinforcement.
- Rewards.
- Punishments.
- Observational learning.

Personality is viewed as a pattern of learned behaviours.

## 30. Cultural Approach

The cultural approach studies personality in relation to the demands of a culture.

Culture shapes:

- Child-rearing.
- Values.
- Roles.
- Norms.
- Economic life.
- Expectations.

Personality cannot be fully understood outside cultural context.

## 31. Humanistic Approach

The humanistic approach focuses on:

- Subjective experience.
- Freedom.
- Choice.
- Meaning.
- Personal growth.
- Self-actualisation.

Rogers and Maslow are key figures.

## 32. Rogers' View

Carl Rogers emphasised:

- Real self.
- Ideal self.
- Unconditional positive regard.
- Congruence.
- Fully functioning person.

When real self and ideal self are close, the person is more adjusted.

## 33. Maslow's View

Maslow arranged needs in a hierarchy.

Lower needs include:

- Physiological needs.
- Safety needs.

Higher needs include:

- Love and belonging.
- Esteem.
- Self-actualisation.

Self-actualisation means realising one's potential.

## 34. Healthy Personality

A healthy person is likely to show:

- Self-awareness.
- Self-acceptance.
- Emotional balance.
- Realistic perception.
- Meaningful goals.
- Good relationships.
- Autonomy.
- Responsibility.
- Personal growth.

## 35. Personality Assessment

Personality assessment is the systematic evaluation of personality characteristics.

It aims to:

- Understand behaviour.
- Predict behaviour.
- Diagnose difficulties.
- Guide counselling or therapy.
- Support selection or guidance.

## 36. Self-Report Measures

Self-report measures ask people to answer structured questions about themselves.

Examples include personality inventories.

Strengths:

- Easy to administer.
- Standardised.
- Quantifiable.

Limitations:

- Social desirability.
- Lack of self-awareness.
- Dishonest responses.
- Limited access to unconscious motives.

## 37. indirect personality techniques

indirect personality techniques use ambiguous or unstructured material so that people express underlying motives, conflicts, and feelings.

Examples include:

- Inkblot responses.
- Story writing around pictures.
- Sentence completion.
- Draw-a-person tasks.
- Frustration-response tasks.

These methods require expert training and qualitative interpretation.

## 38. Rorschach Inkblot Test

The Rorschach Inkblot Test uses inkblot cards.

The person reports what they see. Responses are analysed to understand aspects of personality.

The test requires extensive training for administration and interpretation.

## 39. Thematic Apperception Test

The Thematic Apperception Test uses picture cards.

The person is asked to tell a story about each picture:

- What led to the situation?
- What is happening?
- What are characters thinking and feeling?
- What will happen next?

Stories are interpreted to understand motives, conflicts, and personality themes.

## 40. Sentence Completion And Drawing Methods

Sentence completion tests give incomplete sentences that the person finishes.

Draw-a-person tasks ask the person to draw and sometimes tell a story about the drawing.

Interpretation must be careful because responses can have many meanings.

## 41. Behavioural Analysis

Behavioural analysis studies actual behaviour in different situations.

Methods include:

- Interview.
- Observation.
- Ratings.
- Nomination.
- Situational tests.

These methods use observable behaviour as data.

## 42. Interview

Interviews may be structured or unstructured.

Structured interviews follow a set format.

Unstructured interviews allow flexible exploration.

The way a person answers, speaks, and presents themselves can reveal personality patterns.

## 43. Observation

Observation involves trained recording of behaviour.

For example, a psychologist may observe a client's interaction with family members.

Observation requires clear guidelines because the observer's presence may influence behaviour.

## 44. Behavioural Ratings

Ratings are given by people who know the person well.

They can be used in schools or workplaces.

Limitations include:

- Halo effect.
- Middle category bias.
- Extreme response bias.
- Personal prejudice.

## 45. Nomination

Nomination asks group members to choose persons they would like to work, play, study, or interact with.

It can reveal social acceptance and behavioural qualities.

It is useful when people know each other well.

## 46. Situational Tests

Situational tests observe how a person behaves in a specific situation.

For example, a stress situation may be created to observe coping, leadership, or emotional control.

They are useful because they place behaviour in a context.

## 47. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Personality means good looks

In psychology, personality means stable patterns of behaviour, thought, and emotion, not physical appearance.

### Misunderstanding 2: Self is fixed

Self develops through experience and can change through new relationships and reflection.

### Misunderstanding 3: High self-esteem means arrogance

Healthy self-esteem means realistic self-worth, not superiority over others.

### Misunderstanding 4: Personality tests are simple

Personality assessment requires trained administration and interpretation.

## 48. Model Answer: Self And Personality

Self refers to the totality of conscious experiences, ideas, thoughts, and feelings a person has about themselves. Personality refers to relatively stable and unique patterns of behaviour, thought, and emotion. Self lies at the core of personality because how people define and evaluate themselves influences how they behave. Self includes personal identity and social identity, while personality includes patterns that remain fairly consistent across situations and time. Both develop through biological, psychological, social, and cultural influences.

## 49. Model Answer: Self-Regulation

Self-regulation is the ability to organise and monitor one's own behaviour. It includes self-control, will power, and delay of gratification. People use self-regulation when they resist immediate temptations in order to achieve long-term goals. Psychological techniques include observing one's own behaviour, giving self-instructions, and rewarding oneself for desired behaviour. Self-regulation is important for study habits, emotional control, health behaviour, and goal achievement.
