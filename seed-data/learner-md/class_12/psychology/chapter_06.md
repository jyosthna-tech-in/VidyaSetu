# Chapter 6: Attitude And Social Cognition

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how people form views about objects, people, groups, issues, and events, and how those views influence social behaviour. The chapter also explains why attitudes do not always predict behaviour, why prejudice develops, and how prejudice can be reduced.

The main idea is that social behaviour is not random. It is shaped by cognition, emotion, learning, group membership, media exposure, values, stereotypes, and the social environment.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Attitudes are learned evaluative tendencies that shape how people think, feel, and may act toward social objects, while prejudice shows how attitudes can become harmful when linked to groups and discrimination.

## 2. Why Social Behaviour Needs Explanation

Human beings constantly interpret others.

We form ideas about:

- People.
- Groups.
- Events.
- Social issues.
- Institutions.
- Norms.
- Cultural practices.

Many social behaviours appear simple, but the processes behind them are complex. People may act due to attitudes, group pressure, values, beliefs, fear, habit, identity, or social expectations.

Social psychology goes beyond common sense by studying these processes systematically.

## 3. Social Psychology

Social psychology investigates how the behaviour of individuals is affected by others and by the social environment.

The presence of others may be:

- Actual: people are physically present.
- Imagined: the person imagines what others may think.
- Implied: social norms are felt even when others are not present.

Example:

A student may study harder because parents are present, because the student imagines teacher expectations, or because school norms imply that good students prepare regularly.

## 4. Social Cognition

Social cognition refers to how people think about themselves and others in social situations.

It includes:

- Forming impressions.
- Making judgments.
- Explaining behaviour.
- Categorising people.
- Remembering social information.
- Forming attitudes.
- Using stereotypes.

Social cognition influences social behaviour because how we interpret a situation affects how we respond.

## 5. What Is An Attitude?

An attitude is a state of mind or set of views toward an attitude object.

It has three features:

- It is about something.
- It has an evaluative quality: positive, negative, or neutral.
- It includes thought, feeling, and action tendency.

Attitudes are not directly visible. We infer them from what people say, feel, and do.

## 6. Attitude Object

An attitude object is the focus of an attitude.

It may be:

- A person.
- A group.
- A subject.
- A policy.
- A food item.
- A profession.
- A social practice.
- A national issue.
- An environmental movement.

Example:

"I support tree planting" is an attitude toward a green environment.

## 7. Opinion Versus Attitude

An opinion may be only a thought or view.

An attitude is stronger because it includes:

- Thought.
- Emotion.
- Tendency to act.

Example:

If a student says, "I think clean surroundings are useful," this may be an opinion.

If the student values cleanliness, feels upset when public places are dirty, and participates in cleaning drives, it has become an attitude.

## 8. A-B-C Components Of Attitude

Attitudes have three components:

- Affective component.
- Behavioural component.
- Cognitive component.

Together, these are called the A-B-C components.

## 9. Affective Component

The affective component is the emotional part of attitude.

It includes:

- Liking.
- Disliking.
- Anger.
- Pride.
- Fear.
- Sadness.
- Admiration.

Example:

Feeling happy when seeing trees and feeling sad when trees are cut are affective responses toward a green environment.

## 10. Behavioural Component

The behavioural component is the tendency to act in a certain way.

It may include:

- Joining a campaign.
- Avoiding a group.
- Buying a product.
- Voting for a policy.
- Speaking in favour of an issue.

It is important to remember that attitude is not behaviour itself. It is a tendency toward behaviour.

## 11. Cognitive Component

The cognitive component includes thoughts, beliefs, and information about the attitude object.

Example:

Believing that trees reduce pollution and support life is a cognitive component of a positive attitude toward the environment.

## 12. Green Environment Example

A positive attitude toward a green environment may include:

- Cognitive: "Trees are important for clean air."
- Affective: "I feel happy when I see greenery."
- Behavioural: "I participate in tree plantation."

The three components often move in the same direction, but not always.

## 13. Inconsistency Among Components

The A-B-C components may be inconsistent.

Example:

A person may believe exercise is important and feel proud of fit people, but still not exercise.

This means strong cognitive and affective components do not always produce action.

Predicting behaviour from attitude requires looking at the whole situation.

## 14. Attitudes And Beliefs

Beliefs are the cognitive base of attitudes.

Examples:

- Belief in democracy.
- Belief that education improves society.
- Belief that hard work matters.

An attitude may include beliefs, but it also includes evaluation and emotion.

## 15. Attitudes And Values

Values are attitudes or beliefs with a "should" or "ought" quality.

Examples:

- People should be honest.
- One ought to respect human dignity.
- Hard work is important.

Values become deeply connected to a person's worldview and are therefore harder to change.

## 16. Why Attitudes Are Useful

Attitudes help people respond quickly to new situations.

They provide a mental layout for behaviour.

For example:

If a person has a positive attitude toward community service, the person may be more likely to volunteer when a need appears.

Attitudes simplify decisions, but they can also become biased or rigid.

## 17. Properties Of Attitudes

The chapter describes four important properties:

- Valence.
- Extremeness.
- Simplicity or complexity.
- Centrality.

These properties tell us how an attitude is organised and how strong it may be.

## 18. Valence

Valence means whether an attitude is positive, negative, or neutral.

Example:

On a 5-point scale toward nuclear research:

- 1 or 2 indicates negative valence.
- 3 indicates neutral valence.
- 4 or 5 indicates positive valence.

Valence tells the direction of evaluation.

## 19. Extremeness

Extremeness tells how strongly positive or negative the attitude is.

On a 5-point scale:

- 1 is extremely negative.
- 5 is extremely positive.
- 3 is neutral and least extreme.

Two people may both have positive attitudes, but one may be much more extreme than the other.

## 20. Simplicity Or Complexity

An attitude system may be simple or complex.

A simple attitude system has one or a few member attitudes.

A complex attitude system has many member attitudes.

Example:

Attitude toward health may include attitudes toward exercise, diet, sleep, mental health, medical care, happiness, and discipline.

## 21. Multiplexity

Multiplexity is another term for complexity in an attitude system.

High multiplexity means the attitude contains many related attitudes.

Low multiplexity means it contains only a few.

This should not be confused with A-B-C components. Each member attitude may still have affective, behavioural, and cognitive components.

## 22. Centrality

Centrality means the importance of one attitude within the whole attitude system.

A central attitude strongly influences other attitudes.

Example:

In a person's attitude toward world peace, a negative attitude toward excessive military expenditure may become central and influence many related views.

## 23. Attitude Formation

Attitudes are mostly learned through experience and interaction.

They may form through:

- Association.
- Reward and punishment.
- Modelling.
- Group and cultural norms.
- Exposure to information.

Learning does not always happen consciously. Many attitudes develop gradually.

## 24. Learning By Association

A person may develop an attitude toward something because it becomes associated with something positive or negative.

Example:

A student may like a subject because the teacher is supportive and inspiring.

The positive feeling toward the teacher becomes linked with the subject.

## 25. Learning By Reward And Punishment

Attitudes may strengthen when they are rewarded and weaken when they are punished.

Example:

If a student is praised for regular yoga practice and receives recognition for health, the student may develop a positive attitude toward yoga and health.

If junk food repeatedly leads to illness, the person may develop a negative attitude toward junk food.

## 26. Learning Through Modelling

People form attitudes by observing others.

Children may learn respect for elders by observing parents respectfully interacting with elders and being appreciated for it.

Modelling is powerful because people do not need to experience every consequence directly.

## 27. Learning Through Group And Cultural Norms

Norms are unwritten rules about expected behaviour.

Over time, norms may become attitudes.

Example:

If a religious or cultural group approves of offering flowers at a place of worship, members may develop a positive attitude toward that practice.

Norm-based learning may involve association, reward, punishment, and modelling together.

## 28. Learning Through Information

Attitudes may be formed through reading, media, textbooks, conversations, speeches, films, internet content, and biographies.

Example:

Reading about self-actualised persons may create a positive attitude toward hard work, discipline, and social contribution.

Information can create both constructive and harmful attitudes.

## 29. Family And School Environment

Family strongly influences attitudes in early life.

Children observe:

- Parents' values.
- Family rules.
- Reward and punishment.
- Communication style.
- Treatment of others.

School later becomes an important environment through teachers, peers, textbooks, discipline, and school culture.

## 30. Reference Groups

Reference groups provide standards for acceptable thinking and behaviour.

They may include:

- Peer groups.
- Religious groups.
- Political groups.
- Occupational groups.
- Online communities.
- Social movements.

Adolescents are especially influenced by reference groups because belonging becomes highly important.

## 31. Personal Experiences

A powerful personal experience can change attitude deeply.

Example:

A person who narrowly survives a dangerous event may begin to value community service, simplicity, or social contribution more strongly.

Personal experiences are often emotionally intense, so the attitudes formed through them can be strong.

## 32. Media-Related Influences

Media can shape attitudes by strengthening cognitive and affective components.

Media includes:

- Television.
- Radio.
- Newspapers.
- Films.
- Internet platforms.
- Advertisements.
- Textbooks.

Media may promote social harmony, scientific thinking, and public health. It may also create consumerist, biased, or prejudiced attitudes if information is unverified or manipulative.

## 33. Attitude Change

Attitudes can change after they form.

Some attitudes are easier to change than others.

Attitudes are easier to change when they are:

- New.
- Weak.
- More like opinions.
- Less central.
- Based on limited information.

Attitudes are harder to change when they are:

- Strong.
- Connected to values.
- Central to identity.
- Supported by group norms.

## 34. Why Attitude Change Matters

Attitude change is important for:

- Health campaigns.
- Education.
- Advertisements.
- Political communication.
- Social reform.
- Reducing prejudice.
- Environmental awareness.

Changing attitude is not always manipulation. It can be used for constructive social purposes when done ethically.

## 35. Balance Theory

Fritz Heider proposed the concept of balance.

It is often shown through the P-O-X triangle:

- P is the person whose attitude is being studied.
- O is another person.
- X is the attitude object.

People prefer balance among these relations because imbalance creates psychological discomfort.

## 36. Balanced And Imbalanced States

Balance exists when:

- All three relations are positive.
- Two relations are negative and one is positive.

Imbalance exists when:

- All three relations are negative.
- Two relations are positive and one is negative.

When imbalance occurs, one relation may change to restore balance.

## 37. Balance Theory Example

Suppose:

- P supports dowry.
- O opposes dowry.
- P likes O.

There are two positive relations and one negative relation, so the situation is imbalanced.

To restore balance:

- P may change attitude toward dowry.
- P may stop liking O.
- P may reinterpret O's position.

The theory explains one way attitudes may shift.

## 38. Cognitive Dissonance

Cognitive dissonance means discomfort caused by inconsistency between thoughts, beliefs, or behaviour.

Leon Festinger proposed that people try to reduce this discomfort.

They may reduce dissonance by:

- Changing attitude.
- Changing behaviour.
- Adding a new explanation.
- Reducing the importance of the inconsistency.

## 39. Festinger And Carlsmith Example

In the classic experiment, participants performed a boring task and then told another person it was interesting.

Those paid a small amount had less external justification for lying. To reduce dissonance, they changed their attitude and came to believe the task was more interesting.

This shows that behaviour can sometimes shape attitude.

## 40. Two-Step Concept

The two-step concept says attitude change may occur in two stages.

First, the person identifies with the communicator or accepts the communicator's influence.

Second, the person changes attitude in the direction of the communicator's position.

This explains why admired or credible people may influence attitudes.

## 41. Congruent And Incongruent Change

Congruent attitude change moves in the same direction as the existing attitude.

Example:

A mildly positive attitude becomes more positive.

Incongruent attitude change moves in the opposite direction.

Example:

A negative attitude becomes positive.

Incongruent change is usually harder.

## 42. Factors In Attitude Change

Attitude change depends on:

- Characteristics of the existing attitude.
- Characteristics of the message sender.
- Characteristics of the message.
- Characteristics of the target person.

No single factor works in every case.

## 43. Existing Attitude Characteristics

Change is influenced by:

- Strength of the attitude.
- Centrality of the attitude.
- Extremeness of the attitude.
- Simplicity or complexity.
- Whether the attitude is value-based.

Strong, central, value-based attitudes are harder to change.

## 44. Message Sender Characteristics

The person or group giving the message affects attitude change.

Important qualities include:

- Credibility.
- Expertise.
- Trustworthiness.
- Attractiveness.
- Similarity to the audience.

An expert may influence adults buying a laptop, while students may be more influenced by another student if the product is for students.

## 45. Message Characteristics

The message itself matters.

Attitude change is influenced by:

- Amount of information.
- Rational appeal.
- Emotional appeal.
- Motives activated.
- Clarity.
- Repetition.
- Mode of communication.

Too little information may not persuade. Too much information may overload or irritate the audience.

## 46. Rational And Emotional Appeals

A rational appeal uses logic, facts, cost, efficiency, and evidence.

Example:

Pressure cooking saves fuel.

An emotional appeal uses care, fear, pride, love, or belonging.

Example:

Pressure cooking protects family nutrition.

Both can work, but effectiveness depends on audience and situation.

## 47. Fear Appeals

Fear can sometimes change attitude, especially when the message gives a clear solution.

But too much fear may cause denial or avoidance.

Example:

Showing extreme images of dental cavities may make people reject the message instead of improving dental care.

Effective persuasion balances concern with believable action.

## 48. Mode Of Communication

Face-to-face communication is often powerful because it allows trust, feedback, and direct clarification.

Mass media can reach many people quickly.

Visual media and internet content can feel direct, but they are not always substitutes for personal communication.

Example:

A health worker personally explaining oral rehydration may be more effective than a distant message alone.

## 49. Target Characteristics

The target is the person whose attitude may change.

Attitude change is influenced by:

- Persuasibility.
- Openness.
- Prejudice.
- Self-esteem.
- Intelligence.
- Confidence.
- Prior knowledge.

People with flexible personalities may change more easily. People with strong prejudices may resist change.

## 50. Attitude-Behaviour Relationship

We usually expect behaviour to follow attitude.

However, behaviour does not always match attitude.

Reasons include:

- Social pressure.
- Fear of evaluation.
- Habit.
- Lack of opportunity.
- Weak attitude.
- Conflicting attitudes.
- External rewards or punishments.

This is why psychologists study conditions under which attitudes predict behaviour.

## 51. When Attitudes Predict Behaviour

Attitudes are more likely to predict behaviour when:

- The attitude is strong.
- The attitude is central.
- The person is aware of the attitude.
- There is little external pressure.
- The behaviour is not being watched or judged.
- The person expects positive consequences from the behaviour.

The stronger and more relevant the attitude, the more likely it is to guide action.

## 52. LaPiere's Study

Richard LaPiere found a gap between reported attitude and actual behaviour.

In the study, a Chinese couple was served in many hotels and tourist homes in the United States. Later, many managers reported that they would not accommodate Chinese guests.

The reported attitude was negative, but the actual behaviour had been mostly positive.

This study shows that questionnaire answers may not always predict real behaviour.

## 53. Behaviour Can Shape Attitude

Sometimes behaviour comes first and attitude changes afterward.

In the Festinger and Carlsmith study, participants who lied for a small reward changed attitude to reduce dissonance.

This means people may infer their attitudes from their own behaviour when external justification is weak.

## 54. Prejudice

Prejudice is usually a negative attitude toward a group.

It may include:

- Cognitive component: stereotypes.
- Affective component: dislike, fear, hatred, contempt.
- Behavioural component: discrimination.

Prejudice is dangerous because it can justify unfair treatment.

## 55. Stereotype

A stereotype is a cluster of beliefs about a group.

It assumes that all members of a group share the same characteristics.

Stereotypes are often inaccurate because they ignore individual differences.

Even when a stereotype appears positive, it still reduces people to group labels.

## 56. Discrimination

Discrimination is behaviour that treats a group or person unfairly because of group membership.

It may occur in:

- Education.
- Employment.
- Housing.
- Social interaction.
- Health care.
- Public spaces.

Discrimination is the behavioural expression of prejudice, but discrimination can also occur due to laws, institutions, habits, or group pressure even when a person does not strongly feel prejudice.

## 57. Prejudice Without Discrimination

Prejudice can exist without visible discrimination.

Example:

A person may privately dislike a group but not act unfairly because of law, social pressure, or personal restraint.

The attitude remains harmful, but it may not be expressed openly.

## 58. Discrimination Without Personal Prejudice

Discrimination can occur without strong personal prejudice.

Example:

A person may follow an unfair rule at work because the institution demands it.

This is why reducing discrimination requires changes in both attitudes and social systems.

## 59. Learning As A Cause Of Prejudice

Prejudice can be learned like other attitudes.

It may develop through:

- Family messages.
- Peer approval.
- Media content.
- Group norms.
- Repeated stereotypes.
- Reward for biased behaviour.
- Punishment for questioning group bias.

Learning can create a prejudiced personality marked by hostility, anxiety, and poor adjustment.

## 60. Strong Social Identity And Ingroup Bias

People often value their own group.

This becomes harmful when positive ingroup identity depends on negative views of outgroups.

Ingroup bias means favouring one's own group.

It can create prejudice when "we" are seen as better and "they" are seen as inferior or threatening.

## 61. Scapegoating

Scapegoating occurs when a majority group blames a weaker minority group for social, economic, or political problems.

The minority may be too weak or too small to defend itself.

Scapegoating expresses frustration by directing blame at an outgroup.

## 62. Kernel Of Truth Concept

Sometimes people defend stereotypes by claiming there is a small truth in them.

This is called the kernel of truth concept.

The problem is that a few examples are wrongly used to judge an entire group.

It ignores individual variation and exaggerates selective evidence.

## 63. Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

A self-fulfilling prophecy occurs when expectations make people behave in ways that confirm the expectation.

Example:

If a group is repeatedly treated as incapable, its members may receive fewer opportunities, lose confidence, and then appear less successful. The original prejudice is then falsely treated as proven.

This process maintains prejudice.

## 64. Handling Prejudice

Handling prejudice requires working on its causes.

Strategies should aim to:

- Reduce opportunities for learning prejudice.
- Change prejudiced attitudes.
- Reduce narrow social identity based only on ingroup membership.
- Discourage self-fulfilling prophecy among targeted groups.

Prejudice reduction requires both knowledge and contact.

## 65. Education And Information

Education can correct stereotypes by providing accurate information.

It can:

- Challenge false beliefs.
- Explain diversity within groups.
- Reduce fear.
- Promote equal dignity.
- Encourage critical thinking.

Information alone may not be enough, but it is an important first step.

## 66. Intergroup Contact

Intergroup contact can reduce prejudice when it is structured well.

It works best when:

- Groups meet in a cooperative context.
- Groups have equal status.
- Interaction is close enough to know each other.
- The situation supports common goals.
- Members can discover positive qualities in the outgroup.

Competition without equality may increase prejudice instead of reducing it.

## 67. Highlighting Individual Identity

Prejudice weakens when people see others as individuals rather than only as group members.

Individual identity reduces overgeneralisation.

Instead of "they are all like this," the person begins to notice personal traits, life stories, and unique qualities.

## 68. A Complete Example: Attitude Toward Waste Management

A person may develop a positive attitude toward waste management through several routes:

- Cognitive: learning that waste segregation improves recycling.
- Affective: feeling concern when seeing polluted streets.
- Behavioural: separating dry and wet waste.
- Family influence: seeing parents practise segregation.
- School influence: learning environmental responsibility.
- Media influence: watching campaigns.
- Social norm: colony members expect cleanliness.

This example shows attitude formation, attitude strength, and behaviour together.

## 69. Common Student Confusions

Attitude is not the same as behaviour. It is a tendency that may or may not be expressed.

Belief is mainly cognitive, while attitude includes evaluation and emotion.

Value is deeper and more stable than a casual opinion.

Stereotype is cognitive, prejudice is attitudinal, and discrimination is behavioural.

Media can create both positive and negative attitudes.

Ingroup pride is not automatically prejudice, but it can become prejudice when linked to outgroup dislike.

## 70. Model Answer: Components Of Attitude

An attitude is an evaluative state of mind toward an attitude object. It has three components. The cognitive component includes thoughts and beliefs about the object. The affective component includes feelings toward it. The behavioural component includes the tendency to act in a particular way. For example, a positive attitude toward a green environment may involve believing trees are useful, feeling happy around greenery, and participating in tree plantation.

## 71. Model Answer: Attitude Formation

Attitudes are mostly learned through experience and interaction. They may be learned by association, reward and punishment, modelling, group and cultural norms, and exposure to information. Family and school are important in early life. Reference groups influence acceptable behaviour and thinking. Personal experiences may transform attitudes, and media may strengthen cognitive and affective components.

## 72. Model Answer: Attitude And Behaviour

Attitudes do not always predict behaviour. Behaviour is more likely to match attitude when the attitude is strong, central, and clearly known to the person; when external pressure is low; when behaviour is not being watched; and when the person expects positive consequences. LaPiere's study showed that reported attitudes may differ from actual behaviour.

## 73. Model Answer: Prejudice And Discrimination

Prejudice is usually a negative attitude toward a group. It may be based on stereotypes, which are cognitive beliefs about group characteristics. Discrimination is behaviour that treats people unfairly because of group membership. Prejudice can exist without discrimination, and discrimination can occur without strong personal prejudice, but the two often occur together.

## 75. Quick Revision

- Social psychology studies behaviour in social contexts.
- Attitudes have affective, behavioural, and cognitive components.
- Beliefs are cognitive; values include strong "should" ideas.
- Attitudes vary in valence, extremeness, multiplexity, and centrality.
- Attitudes form through association, reward, modelling, norms, and information.
- Family, school, reference groups, personal experiences, and media shape attitudes.
- Attitude change can occur through balance, dissonance, and persuasion.
- Attitudes predict behaviour best when strong, central, and free from pressure.
- Prejudice is a negative group attitude.
- Stereotype is a belief cluster about a group.
- Discrimination is unfair behaviour.
- Prejudice may arise from learning, ingroup bias, scapegoating, kernel of truth thinking, and self-fulfilling prophecy.
- Prejudice can be reduced through education, structured intergroup contact, and individual identity.
