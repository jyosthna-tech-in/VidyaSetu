# Chapter 4: Psychological Disorders

## Learner Orientation

This chapter introduces psychological disorders, their classification, possible causes, and major categories. It should be studied with sensitivity. The chapter is meant for conceptual understanding, not for self-diagnosis or diagnosing others. Only trained mental health professionals can assess and diagnose psychological disorders.

The central idea is that abnormal behaviour is best understood through multiple perspectives. Biological, psychological, social, and cultural factors interact. Stigma and superstition should be replaced with scientific understanding, compassion, and professional help.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Explain the meaning of abnormality.
- Describe the four Ds: deviance, distress, dysfunction, and danger.
- Distinguish deviation from social norms and maladaptive behaviour.
- Understand historical views of abnormal behaviour.
- Explain classification systems such as DSM and ICD.
- Identify biological, psychological, and socio-cultural factors.
- Explain the bio-psycho-social approach.
- Describe major psychological disorder categories.
- Distinguish anxiety, obsessive-compulsive, trauma-related, somatic, dissociative, mood, psychotic, neurodevelopmental, conduct, eating, and substance-related disorders.
- Avoid casual labelling and understand the importance of professional assessment.

## 1. Why Study Psychological Disorders Carefully?

People may experience sadness, anxiety, fear, anger, or confusion at different points in life. These experiences are not automatically disorders.

A psychological disorder is considered when patterns are:

- Severe.
- Persistent.
- Distressing.
- Dysfunctional.
- Maladaptive.
- Risky.
- Outside expected cultural or situational responses.

Casual labelling can harm people. Scientific understanding reduces stigma.

## 2. Adaptation And Maladaptation

Adaptation is the ability to modify behaviour according to environmental demands.

Maladaptation occurs when behaviour cannot be modified effectively and begins to interfere with life.

For example:

- Normal exam anxiety may motivate preparation.
- Extreme anxiety that blocks functioning may become problematic.

The key is intensity, duration, context, and effect on functioning.

## 3. The Four Ds Of Abnormality

Many definitions of abnormality include the four Ds:

- Deviance.
- Distress.
- Dysfunction.
- Danger.

These are useful starting points, not rigid rules.

## 4. Deviance

Deviance means the behaviour, thought, or emotion is different from what society expects.

But deviance alone is not enough. Creative, unusual, or nonconforming behaviour may not be abnormal.

Social norms differ across cultures and time.

## 5. Distress

Distress means the person feels suffering, pain, fear, anxiety, sadness, or discomfort.

However, some disorders may involve low distress in the person but high distress in others.

## 6. Dysfunction

Dysfunction means the pattern interferes with daily life.

It may affect:

- Work.
- Study.
- Relationships.
- Self-care.
- Sleep.
- Eating.
- Decision-making.
- Social participation.

Dysfunction is often a strong indicator that help is needed.

## 7. Danger

Danger means risk of harm to self or others.

Not all people with psychological disorders are dangerous. This stereotype is harmful.

Danger is only one possible feature and must be assessed professionally.

## 8. Abnormality As Deviation From Social Norms

One view says abnormal behaviour is behaviour that violates social norms.

This view is limited because norms differ across cultures and can change over time.

Behaviour accepted in one culture may be rejected in another.

Also, conformity is not always healthy. A person may conform to an unjust norm.

## 9. Abnormality As Maladaptive Behaviour

Another view says behaviour is abnormal when it harms well-being, growth, or functioning.

This view focuses less on social approval and more on whether the behaviour helps or hurts the person and group.

For example, silence in class may be socially acceptable, but if fear prevents a student from asking needed questions, it may be maladaptive.

## 10. Stigma

Stigma refers to shame, fear, and negative labelling attached to mental illness.

Stigma may prevent people from seeking professional help.

Scientific understanding helps replace:

- Superstition.
- Blame.
- Ridicule.
- Fear.
- Shame.

with compassion and treatment.

## 11. Historical Views Of Abnormal Behaviour

Three broad perspectives have appeared repeatedly in history:

- Supernatural.
- Biological or organic.
- Psychological.

Modern views combine these into an interactional model.

## 12. Supernatural View

The supernatural view explains abnormal behaviour through evil spirits, demons, magic, or possession.

Practices such as exorcism were used in many societies.

This view still exists in some communities, but psychology relies on scientific explanations.

## 13. Biological View

The biological view explains abnormal behaviour through body and brain processes.

Ancient Greek thinkers such as Hippocrates and Galen linked behaviour to bodily fluids or humours.

Indian traditions also discussed balance through vata, pitta, and kapha.

Modern biological views study genes, brain functioning, hormones, and neurotransmitters.

## 14. Psychological View

The psychological view explains abnormal behaviour through:

- Thoughts.
- Emotions.
- Perceptions.
- Conflicts.
- Learning.
- Relationships.
- Stress.

This view became stronger through psychodynamic, behavioural, cognitive, and humanistic approaches.

## 15. Reform And Deinstitutionalisation

The Reform Movement promoted more compassionate treatment of people with mental disorders.

Deinstitutionalisation emphasised community care for recovered individuals rather than long-term confinement in institutions.

This reflected a more humane approach, though community care needs proper support.

## 16. Bio-Psycho-Social Approach

The bio-psycho-social approach says disorders are influenced by:

- Biological factors.
- Psychological factors.
- Social factors.

For example, depression may involve genetic vulnerability, negative thinking patterns, stressful life events, and lack of support.

## 17. Classification Of Disorders

Classification helps professionals communicate and plan treatment.

Two major systems are:

- DSM, published by the American Psychiatric Association.
- ICD, prepared by the World Health Organization.

Classification includes descriptions of symptoms and diagnostic guidelines.

## 18. Why Classification Is Useful

Classification helps:

- Identify patterns.
- Communicate clearly.
- Study causes.
- Plan treatment.
- Conduct research.
- Compare cases.

But classification must not reduce a person to a label.

## 19. Biological Factors

Biological factors may include:

- Faulty genes.
- Neurotransmitter imbalances.
- Brain injury.
- Endocrine imbalance.
- Malnutrition.
- Illness.
- Developmental problems.

Examples:

- Low GABA activity has been linked with anxiety disorders.
- Excess dopamine activity has been linked with schizophrenia.
- Low serotonin activity has been linked with depression.

These are associations, not simple one-cause explanations.

## 20. Genetic Factors

Genetics may increase vulnerability to some disorders, such as:

- Bipolar disorders.
- Schizophrenia.
- Intellectual disability.
- Some anxiety and mood disorders.

Usually no single gene causes a disorder. Many genes interact with environment.

## 21. Psychological Factors

Psychological factors include:

- Maternal deprivation.
- Rejection.
- Overprotection.
- Over-permissiveness.
- Faulty discipline.
- Disturbed family structure.
- Severe stress.
- Unconscious conflicts.
- Faulty learning.
- Irrational beliefs.
- Negative thinking.

## 22. Psychodynamic Model

The psychodynamic model says abnormal symptoms may express unconscious conflicts.

Freud linked symptoms to conflict among id, ego, and superego, often rooted in early childhood.

## 23. Behavioural Model

The behavioural model says abnormal behaviours are learned.

Learning may occur through:

- Classical conditioning.
- Operant conditioning.
- Social learning.

If maladaptive behaviour is learned, it can also be unlearned or replaced.

## 24. Cognitive Model

The cognitive model says abnormal functioning may arise from faulty thinking.

Examples:

- Irrational beliefs.
- Negative self-statements.
- Overgeneralisation.
- Catastrophic thinking.
- Unrealistic assumptions.

Changing thinking can reduce distress.

## 25. Humanistic-Existential Model

This model says psychological distress may arise when people cannot find meaning, authenticity, freedom, or self-actualisation.

It emphasises:

- Choice.
- Responsibility.
- Personal growth.
- Meaning.
- Loneliness.
- Alienation.

## 26. Diathesis-Stress Model

The diathesis-stress model says a disorder may develop when vulnerability combines with stress.

Diathesis means predisposition or vulnerability.

Stress means environmental pressure or life events.

A person may have vulnerability but not develop a disorder unless stress activates it.

## 27. Socio-Cultural Factors

Socio-cultural factors include:

- Poverty.
- Discrimination.
- War.
- Violence.
- Migration.
- Family conflict.
- Social isolation.
- Community breakdown.
- Cultural expectations.

These factors can increase risk or shape symptom expression.

## 28. Major Psychological Disorders

The chapter introduces many categories. A learner should understand broad features, not memorise labels mechanically.

Major categories include:

- Anxiety disorders.
- Obsessive-compulsive and related disorders.
- Trauma- and stressor-related disorders.
- Somatic symptom and related disorders.
- Dissociative disorders.
- Depressive disorders.
- Bipolar and related disorders.
- Schizophrenia spectrum and other psychotic disorders.
- Neurodevelopmental disorders.
- Disruptive, impulse-control, and conduct disorders.
- Feeding and eating disorders.
- Substance-related and addictive disorders.

## 29. Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety becomes a disorder when fear or worry is excessive, persistent, and interferes with functioning.

Types include:

- Generalised anxiety disorder.
- Panic disorder.
- Phobias.
- Social anxiety.
- Separation anxiety.

Anxiety may involve bodily symptoms such as trembling, sweating, rapid heartbeat, and stomach discomfort.

## 30. Phobias

A phobia is an intense and irrational fear of a specific object or situation.

Examples:

- Fear of snakes.
- Fear of heights.
- Fear of closed spaces.
- Fear of social evaluation.

Phobias may develop through faulty learning, traumatic experiences, observation, or reinforcement.

## 31. Obsessive-Compulsive And Related Disorders

Obsessions are intrusive, unwanted, repetitive thoughts, images, or impulses.

Compulsions are repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety.

Example:

- Obsession: fear of contamination.
- Compulsion: repeated washing.

The person may know the behaviour is excessive but still feel compelled.

## 32. Trauma- And Stressor-Related Disorders

These disorders follow exposure to traumatic or highly stressful events.

Post-traumatic stress symptoms may include:

- Intrusive memories.
- Nightmares.
- Avoidance.
- Hyperarousal.
- Emotional numbness.
- Fear and irritability.

Professional help is important when symptoms persist and impair functioning.

## 33. Somatic Symptom And Related Disorders

These involve distressing bodily symptoms or health concerns that are strongly linked with psychological factors.

The person's suffering is real, even when medical explanations are unclear or incomplete.

Sensitivity and professional assessment are essential.

## 34. Dissociative Disorders

Dissociation means disruption in consciousness, memory, identity, or perception.

Forms may include:

- Dissociative amnesia.
- Dissociative identity disturbance.
- Depersonalisation.
- Derealisation.

These disorders may be linked with severe stress or trauma.

## 35. Depressive Disorders

Depressive disorders involve persistent low mood or loss of interest along with other symptoms.

Symptoms may include:

- Sadness.
- Hopelessness.
- Loss of pleasure.
- Sleep changes.
- Appetite changes.
- Fatigue.
- Poor concentration.
- Feelings of worthlessness.

Depression is not ordinary sadness. It can seriously affect functioning and requires care.

## 36. Bipolar And Related Disorders

Bipolar disorders involve mood episodes that may include depression and mania.

Mania may involve:

- Elevated or irritable mood.
- Increased energy.
- Decreased need for sleep.
- Grand ideas.
- Risky behaviour.
- Rapid speech.

Professional assessment is necessary.

## 37. Schizophrenia Spectrum And Psychotic Disorders

Schizophrenia involves serious disturbances in thinking, perception, emotion, and behaviour.

Symptoms are often grouped as:

- Positive symptoms.
- Negative symptoms.
- Psychomotor symptoms.

## 38. Positive Symptoms

Positive symptoms are additions or distortions.

They include:

- Delusions.
- Hallucinations.
- Disorganised speech.
- Disorganised behaviour.

Delusions are false beliefs strongly held despite evidence.

Hallucinations are perceptions without external stimuli, such as hearing voices.

## 39. Negative Symptoms

Negative symptoms are reductions or deficits.

They include:

- Poverty of speech.
- Blunted affect.
- Flat affect.
- Lack of motivation.
- Social withdrawal.

These symptoms often reduce daily functioning.

## 40. Psychomotor Symptoms

Psychomotor symptoms involve unusual movement or posture.

Catatonia may include:

- Remaining motionless.
- Maintaining rigid posture.
- Holding awkward positions.

## 41. Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Neurodevelopmental disorders appear early in development and affect personal, social, academic, or occupational functioning.

Examples include:

- ADHD.
- Autism spectrum disorder.
- Intellectual disability.
- Specific learning disorder.

## 42. ADHD

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder involves inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity.

Features may include:

- Difficulty sustaining attention.
- Disorganisation.
- Forgetfulness.
- Not finishing work.
- Impulsivity.
- Difficulty waiting.
- Excessive movement.
- Constant talking.

## 43. Autism Spectrum Disorder

Autism spectrum disorder involves difficulties in social interaction and communication, along with restricted or repetitive patterns of behaviour.

Features may include:

- Difficulty relating to others.
- Limited social responsiveness.
- Repetitive speech or movements.
- Strong preference for routine.
- Narrow interests.
- Difficulty understanding relationships.

## 44. Intellectual Disability

Intellectual disability involves below-average intellectual functioning and deficits in adaptive behaviour before age 18.

Adaptive behaviour includes:

- Communication.
- Self-care.
- Home living.
- Social skills.
- Academic skills.
- Work skills.

## 45. Specific Learning Disorder

Specific learning disorder involves difficulty in processing information efficiently and accurately.

It may affect:

- Reading.
- Writing.
- Mathematics.

The child may perform below age expectations but can improve with support and additional inputs.

## 46. Disruptive, Impulse-Control, And Conduct Disorders

These involve behaviour that violates rules, rights, or expectations.

Oppositional defiant disorder may involve stubbornness, irritability, defiance, and hostile behaviour.

Conduct disorder may involve aggression, property damage, deceitfulness, theft, or serious rule violations.

## 47. Feeding And Eating Disorders

Eating disorders involve disturbed eating patterns and body-related concerns.

Examples:

- Anorexia nervosa.
- Bulimia nervosa.
- Binge eating.

These disorders can be physically dangerous and require professional care.

## 48. Anorexia Nervosa

Anorexia nervosa involves distorted body image and severe restriction of food intake.

The person may see themselves as overweight despite being underweight.

## 49. Bulimia Nervosa

Bulimia nervosa involves episodes of excessive eating followed by purging behaviours such as vomiting or misuse of medicines.

The person may experience shame and loss of control.

## 50. Binge Eating

Binge eating involves repeated episodes of eating large amounts of food with a sense of loss of control.

It is not followed by regular purging in the same way as bulimia.

## 51. Substance-Related And Addictive Disorders

These involve maladaptive use of substances that alter thinking, feeling, or behaviour.

Examples include:

- Alcohol.
- Tobacco.
- Opioids.
- Cocaine.
- Cannabis.
- Stimulants.
- Sedatives.

Substance disorders may involve tolerance, dependence, withdrawal, and social or occupational impairment.

## 52. Alcohol Use Disorder

Alcohol misuse can interfere with:

- Judgment.
- Work.
- Family life.
- Social relationships.
- Driving safety.
- Physical health.
- Emotional functioning.

Alcohol affects the brain and can damage health when misused.

## 53. Heroin And Cocaine

Heroin misuse can lead to dependence, withdrawal, social impairment, and life-threatening overdose.

Cocaine misuse can impair memory, attention, mood, sleep, relationships, and health.

These substances require urgent professional intervention when dependence develops.

## 54. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Unusual behaviour is always abnormal

Unusual behaviour may be creative, cultural, or temporary. Abnormality depends on deviance, distress, dysfunction, danger, and context.

### Misunderstanding 2: People with disorders are always dangerous

Most are not dangerous. This stereotype increases stigma.

### Misunderstanding 3: Mental illness is shameful

Psychological disorders are health conditions and should be treated with care and professional help.

### Misunderstanding 4: Reading symptoms is enough to diagnose

Diagnosis requires trained assessment. Students should not diagnose themselves or others.

## 55. Model Answer: Four Ds

The four Ds of abnormality are deviance, distress, dysfunction, and danger. Deviance means behaviour differs from social or cultural norms. Distress means the person experiences emotional suffering. Dysfunction means the behaviour interferes with daily life, work, study, or relationships. Danger means the behaviour may risk harm to self or others. These criteria help psychologists identify abnormal behaviour, but they must be used carefully because social norms vary and not every unusual behaviour is a disorder.

## 56. Model Answer: Bio-Psycho-Social Approach

The bio-psycho-social approach explains psychological disorders as the result of interaction among biological, psychological, and social factors. Biological factors may include genes, neurotransmitters, brain processes, or hormones. Psychological factors may include unconscious conflict, faulty learning, irrational beliefs, or stress. Social factors may include poverty, discrimination, family problems, trauma, or lack of support. This approach is useful because most disorders cannot be explained by one cause alone.

- Social norms alone cannot define abnormality.
- Maladaptive behaviour interferes with well-being and growth.
- Stigma prevents people from seeking help.
- Historical explanations include supernatural, biological, and psychological views.
- Modern psychology uses the bio-psycho-social approach.
- DSM and ICD classify disorders.
- Biological, psychological, and socio-cultural factors interact.
- Major disorders include anxiety, OCD-related, trauma-related, somatic, dissociative, depressive, bipolar, psychotic, neurodevelopmental, conduct, eating, and substance-related disorders.
