# Chapter 5: Biomolecules
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Biomolecules are chemical compounds that build and regulate living systems. This chapter studies carbohydrates, proteins, enzymes, vitamins, nucleic acids, and hormones from a chemistry point of view. The aim is not to memorise biology facts only, but to understand how functional groups, hydrolysis, polymerisation, bonding, and molecular structure explain life processes.

The chapter is conceptually broad. A good learner should organise it around four questions: What is the biomolecule made of? How is it classified? What bonds hold it together? What function does it perform?

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define biomolecules and explain their role in living systems;
- classify carbohydrates into monosaccharides, oligosaccharides, and polysaccharides;
- distinguish reducing and non-reducing sugars;
- describe glucose, fructose, sucrose, maltose, lactose, starch, cellulose, and glycogen;
- classify amino acids and proteins;
- explain peptide bond, protein structure, denaturation, and enzymes;
- classify vitamins as water-soluble and fat-soluble;
- describe nucleosides, nucleotides, DNA, and RNA;
- compare DNA and RNA;
- explain basic role of hormones.

# Unit 5A: Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates were once thought of as hydrates of carbon because many have formula close to `Cx(H2O)y`. This definition is not fully reliable. A better chemical definition is:

Carbohydrates are optically active polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or compounds that produce such units on hydrolysis.

Examples:

- glucose;
- fructose;
- sucrose;
- starch;
- cellulose.

# Unit 5B: Classification of Carbohydrates

## Based on Hydrolysis

| Class | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Monosaccharides | Cannot be hydrolysed further | glucose, fructose, ribose |
| Oligosaccharides | Give 2 to 10 monosaccharide units | sucrose, maltose, lactose |
| Polysaccharides | Give many monosaccharide units | starch, cellulose, glycogen |

## Based on Functional Group

| Class | Functional group | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Aldose | Aldehyde group | glucose |
| Ketose | Ketone group | fructose |

## Based on Number of Carbon Atoms

| Carbon atoms | Name |
|---|---|
| 3 | Triose |
| 4 | Tetrose |
| 5 | Pentose |
| 6 | Hexose |
| 7 | Heptose |

Glucose is an aldohexose. Fructose is a ketohexose.

# Unit 5C: Reducing and Non-Reducing Sugars

Reducing sugars reduce Tollens' reagent and Fehling's solution. They have a free aldehydic or ketonic group, or can generate one in solution.

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Reducing sugars | Can reduce mild oxidising reagents | glucose, fructose, maltose, lactose |
| Non-reducing sugars | Do not reduce these reagents | sucrose |

Sucrose is non-reducing because the reducing groups of glucose and fructose are involved in glycosidic linkage.

# Unit 5D: Glucose

Glucose has molecular formula `C6H12O6`. It is an aldohexose and is also called dextrose.

Important structural evidence:

1. It gives oxime with hydroxylamine, showing carbonyl group.
2. It gives cyanohydrin with HCN, supporting carbonyl group.
3. Mild oxidation gives gluconic acid, showing aldehyde group.
4. Acetylation gives pentaacetate, showing five hydroxyl groups.
5. Strong oxidation gives saccharic acid, showing terminal groups can be oxidised.
6. It does not give some expected aldehyde reactions in cyclic form, showing ring structure.

## Cyclic Structure

Glucose mainly exists in cyclic hemiacetal form. The aldehyde group reacts internally with an `-OH` group to form a ring.

The two cyclic forms are called alpha-D-glucose and beta-D-glucose. They differ in arrangement around the anomeric carbon.

# Unit 5E: Fructose

Fructose has formula `C6H12O6` and is a ketohexose. It is found in fruits and honey.

Fructose also forms cyclic structures. It is a reducing sugar because under test conditions it can rearrange and reduce Fehling's solution or Tollens' reagent.

# Unit 5F: Disaccharides

Disaccharides contain two monosaccharide units joined by glycosidic linkage.

| Disaccharide | Hydrolysis products | Reducing? |
|---|---|---|
| Sucrose | Glucose + fructose | No |
| Maltose | Glucose + glucose | Yes |
| Lactose | Glucose + galactose | Yes |

## Sucrose

Sucrose is common table sugar. On hydrolysis, it gives equimolar glucose and fructose. The mixture changes optical rotation and is called invert sugar.

## Maltose

Maltose is a reducing sugar because one glucose unit retains a free reducing end.

## Lactose

Lactose is milk sugar. It is also a reducing sugar.

# Unit 5G: Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides are large carbohydrate polymers. They are usually not sweet and are generally insoluble or sparingly soluble in water.

## Starch

Starch is the main storage polysaccharide in plants. It contains two components:

- amylose: mostly linear;
- amylopectin: branched.

Starch gives blue colour with iodine.

## Cellulose

Cellulose is a structural polysaccharide in plants. It is a polymer of beta-D-glucose. Humans cannot digest cellulose because they lack enzymes required to break its beta glycosidic linkages.

## Glycogen

Glycogen is the storage polysaccharide in animals. It is highly branched and stored mainly in liver and muscles.

# Unit 5H: Amino Acids

Amino acids contain both amino group and carboxyl group. Proteins are polymers of alpha-amino acids.

General structure:

`H2N-CH(R)-COOH`

The `R` group changes from one amino acid to another and controls properties.

## Zwitterion

In aqueous solution, amino acids can exist as zwitterions:

`+H3N-CH(R)-COO-`

Because of zwitterion formation, amino acids often have high melting points and behave like salts.

## Essential and Non-Essential Amino Acids

Essential amino acids must be supplied in diet because the body cannot synthesise enough of them.

Non-essential amino acids can be synthesised by the body.

# Unit 5I: Proteins

Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.

Peptide bond:

`-CO-NH-`

Formation:

`amino acid + amino acid -> dipeptide + H2O`

## Classification by Shape

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Fibrous proteins | Long, thread-like, generally insoluble | keratin, collagen |
| Globular proteins | Compact, folded, often soluble | enzymes, albumin |

## Classification by Composition

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Simple proteins | Give only amino acids on hydrolysis |
| Conjugated proteins | Contain amino acid part plus non-protein part |

# Unit 5J: Structure of Proteins

## Primary Structure

Primary structure is the sequence of amino acids in a protein chain. If the sequence changes, the protein may lose function.

## Secondary Structure

Secondary structure refers to local folding patterns such as alpha helix and beta pleated sheet. These are stabilised mainly by hydrogen bonding.

## Tertiary Structure

Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional folding of a protein. It is stabilised by hydrogen bonding, ionic interactions, disulphide bridges, and van der Waals forces.

## Quaternary Structure

Quaternary structure exists when a protein has more than one polypeptide chain arranged together.

# Unit 5K: Denaturation of Proteins

Denaturation is loss of native protein structure due to heat, pH change, or chemicals. Primary structure usually remains intact, but secondary and tertiary structures are disrupted.

Examples:

- boiling egg changes soluble egg white proteins into insoluble mass;
- curdling of milk involves protein changes.

Denaturation often destroys biological activity because protein shape controls function.

# Unit 5L: Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts. Most enzymes are proteins.

Features:

- highly specific;
- work under mild conditions;
- increase reaction rate;
- not consumed in reaction;
- sensitive to temperature and pH.

Enzyme specificity comes from the shape of active site.

# Unit 5M: Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds required in small amounts for normal growth and health. Most cannot be synthesised sufficiently by the body.

## Classification

| Type | Vitamins | Key point |
|---|---|---|
| Fat-soluble | A, D, E, K | Stored in body fat; excess may accumulate. |
| Water-soluble | B group, C | Usually not stored much; regular intake needed. |

## Deficiency Examples

| Vitamin | Deficiency disease or issue |
|---|---|
| A | Night blindness |
| B1 | Beriberi |
| B12 | Pernicious anaemia |
| C | Scurvy |
| D | Rickets |
| K | Blood clotting problems |

# Unit 5N: Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. The two major types are DNA and RNA.

## Components

A nucleotide contains:

1. nitrogenous base;
2. pentose sugar;
3. phosphate group.

A nucleoside contains:

1. nitrogenous base;
2. pentose sugar.

So:

`nucleoside + phosphate = nucleotide`

## Bases

Purines:

- adenine;
- guanine.

Pyrimidines:

- cytosine;
- thymine in DNA;
- uracil in RNA.

# Unit 5O: DNA and RNA

## DNA

DNA contains deoxyribose sugar and stores genetic information. It usually has a double-helical structure.

Base pairing:

- adenine pairs with thymine;
- guanine pairs with cytosine.

## RNA

RNA contains ribose sugar and usually exists as a single strand. It participates in protein synthesis.

Base pairing in RNA uses uracil instead of thymine.

## DNA vs RNA

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar | Deoxyribose | Ribose |
| Bases | A, G, C, T | A, G, C, U |
| Usual structure | Double strand | Single strand |
| Main role | Genetic storage | Protein synthesis and gene expression |
| Stability | More stable | Less stable |

# Unit 5P: Hormones

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands. They regulate physiological processes such as growth, metabolism, reproduction, and stress response.

Chemically, hormones may be:

- steroids;
- peptides or proteins;
- amino-acid derivatives.

Examples include insulin, adrenaline, thyroxine, and steroid hormones.

"Proteins are polymers of alpha-amino acids joined by peptide bonds. They may be fibrous or globular. Their function depends on their three-dimensional structure. Examples include collagen and enzymes. They serve structural, catalytic, and regulatory roles in living systems."

# Unit 5R: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling all carbohydrates sweet | Polysaccharides such as starch and cellulose are not sweet. |
| Saying sucrose is reducing | Sucrose is non-reducing because both reducing ends are involved in linkage. |
| Confusing nucleoside and nucleotide | Nucleotide has phosphate; nucleoside does not. |
| Thinking denaturation breaks all peptide bonds | Denaturation mainly disrupts higher structures, not primary sequence. |
| Saying DNA contains uracil | DNA contains thymine; RNA contains uracil. |
| Treating all vitamins as stored | Water-soluble vitamins are not stored much and need regular intake. |

## Final Revision

- Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or hydrolysis products of such compounds.
- Glucose is an aldohexose; fructose is a ketohexose.
- Sucrose is non-reducing; maltose and lactose are reducing.
- Starch and glycogen store energy; cellulose provides structure.
- Proteins are amino acid polymers linked by peptide bonds.
- Protein shape determines protein function.
- Denaturation disrupts higher protein structure.
- DNA stores genetic information; RNA helps express it.
- Nucleotide equals base plus sugar plus phosphate.
