# Chapter 4: Amines
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Amines are derivatives of ammonia in which one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by alkyl or aryl groups. They are basic because nitrogen has a lone pair of electrons. Amines are important in dyes, drugs, proteins, vitamins, hormones, polymers, and many synthetic compounds. The chapter covers classification, nomenclature, preparation, basicity, reactions, distinction tests, and diazonium salts.

The main learning challenge is comparing aliphatic and aromatic amines. In aliphatic amines, alkyl groups usually increase electron density on nitrogen. In aromatic amines, the lone pair on nitrogen is delocalised into the benzene ring, making it less available for protonation.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- classify amines as primary, secondary, and tertiary;
- name aliphatic and aromatic amines;
- explain structure and pyramidal geometry of amines;
- prepare amines by reduction, ammonolysis, Gabriel synthesis, and Hoffmann bromamide reaction;
- compare basicity of ammonia, aliphatic amines, and aromatic amines;
- explain reactions of amines with acids, acyl chlorides, nitrous acid, and electrophiles;
- distinguish primary, secondary, and tertiary amines;
- prepare diazonium salts;
- explain reactions and synthetic importance of diazonium salts.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Amine | Organic derivative of ammonia. |
| Primary amine | Nitrogen attached to one carbon group and two hydrogens. |
| Secondary amine | Nitrogen attached to two carbon groups and one hydrogen. |
| Tertiary amine | Nitrogen attached to three carbon groups. |
| Arylamine | Amine in which nitrogen is directly attached to aromatic ring. |
| Basicity | Tendency to accept proton. |

# Unit 4A: Classification

Amines are classified according to the number of hydrogen atoms of ammonia replaced by carbon groups.

| Type | General formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary amine | `R-NH2` | methylamine |
| Secondary amine | `R-NH-R'` | dimethylamine |
| Tertiary amine | `R3N` | trimethylamine |

If all carbon groups are same, the amine is simple. If different groups are attached, it is mixed.

## Aliphatic vs Aromatic Amines

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Aliphatic amine | Nitrogen attached to alkyl group | `CH3NH2` |
| Aromatic amine | Nitrogen directly attached to aryl group | `C6H5NH2` |

Aniline is the simplest aromatic amine.

# Unit 4B: Structure of Amines

Nitrogen in amines is approximately `sp3` hybridised and pyramidal. It has one lone pair and three sigma bonds.

Bond angle is slightly less than tetrahedral angle because lone pair-bond pair repulsion is stronger than bond pair-bond pair repulsion.

The lone pair makes amines:

- basic;
- nucleophilic;
- able to form salts with acids;
- able to react with acyl chlorides and alkyl halides.

# Unit 4C: Nomenclature

## Common Names

Common names are formed by naming alkyl groups followed by "amine".

Examples:

| Formula | Common name |
|---|---|
| `CH3NH2` | methylamine |
| `C2H5NH2` | ethylamine |
| `(CH3)2NH` | dimethylamine |
| `(CH3)3N` | trimethylamine |

## IUPAC Names

Primary amines are named as alkanamines.

Examples:

| Formula | IUPAC name |
|---|---|
| `CH3NH2` | methanamine |
| `CH3CH2NH2` | ethanamine |
| `CH3CH(NH2)CH3` | propan-2-amine |
| `C6H5NH2` | benzenamine, also called aniline |

For secondary and tertiary amines, groups attached to nitrogen are shown with `N-` locants.

Example:

`CH3NHCH2CH3` is N-methylethanamine.

# Unit 4D: Preparation of Amines

## Reduction of Nitro Compounds

Nitro compounds reduce to amines.

`R-NO2 -> R-NH2`

Common reducing systems include hydrogen with metal catalyst or metals in acidic medium.

Aniline is commonly prepared by reduction of nitrobenzene.

## Ammonolysis of Alkyl Halides

Alkyl halides react with ammonia to form amines.

`R-X + NH3 -> R-NH2`

The primary amine can react further to give secondary amine, tertiary amine, and quaternary ammonium salt. Excess ammonia favours primary amine.

Reactivity of alkyl halides:

`RI > RBr > RCl`

## Reduction of Nitriles

Nitriles reduce to primary amines with one extra carbon in the chain compared with the original alkyl halide route.

`R-CN -> R-CH2NH2`

This is useful for chain extension.

## Reduction of Amides

Amides reduce to amines.

`R-CONH2 -> R-CH2NH2`

## Gabriel Phthalimide Synthesis

Gabriel synthesis prepares primary aliphatic amines.

Steps:

1. Phthalimide reacts with alcoholic potassium hydroxide.
2. Potassium phthalimide reacts with alkyl halide.
3. Hydrolysis gives primary amine.

Limitation: aromatic primary amines are not prepared by this method because aryl halides do not undergo this nucleophilic substitution easily.

## Hoffmann Bromamide Reaction

Amide reacts with bromine and sodium hydroxide to give primary amine with one carbon less.

`R-CONH2 -> R-NH2`

This is a degradation reaction because one carbon is lost.

# Unit 4E: Basicity of Amines

Amines are basic because nitrogen lone pair can accept a proton.

`R-NH2 + H+ -> R-NH3+`

Basicity depends on:

- electron density on nitrogen;
- solvation of conjugate acid;
- steric hindrance;
- resonance;
- inductive effect.

## Aliphatic Amines

Alkyl groups donate electron density through `+I` effect, increasing basicity compared with ammonia.

In aqueous solution, order may be affected by solvation. A common school-level order for simple methyl amines is:

`(CH3)2NH > CH3NH2 > (CH3)3N > NH3`

The exact order can differ with alkyl group and solvent.

## Aromatic Amines

Aniline is less basic than ammonia because nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring. The lone pair is less available to accept a proton.

Electron-donating groups increase basicity of substituted anilines. Electron-withdrawing groups decrease basicity.

# Unit 4F: Reactions of Amines

## Salt Formation

Amines react with mineral acids to form ammonium salts.

`R-NH2 + HCl -> R-NH3+Cl-`

The salt is often water-soluble.

## Alkylation

Amines react with alkyl halides to form higher amines and quaternary ammonium salts.

This reaction can over-alkylate, so mixtures may form.

## Acylation

Primary and secondary amines react with acyl chlorides or acid anhydrides to form amides.

`R-NH2 + R'COCl -> R'CONHR + HCl`

Tertiary amines do not undergo acylation because they do not have `N-H`.

## Carbylamine Reaction

Primary amines react with chloroform and alcoholic potassium hydroxide to form isocyanides with unpleasant smell.

Only primary amines give this test.

`R-NH2 + CHCl3 + 3KOH -> R-NC + 3KCl + 3H2O`

# Unit 4G: Reaction with Nitrous Acid

Nitrous acid is generated in situ from `NaNO2` and `HCl`.

## Primary Aliphatic Amines

Primary aliphatic amines give alcohols with evolution of nitrogen gas.

`R-NH2 -> R-OH + N2`

## Primary Aromatic Amines

Aniline forms diazonium salt at low temperature.

`C6H5NH2 -> C6H5N2+Cl-`

Temperature is kept around `273-278 K` because diazonium salts are unstable at higher temperature.

## Secondary Amines

Secondary amines form N-nitroso compounds.

## Tertiary Amines

Tertiary aliphatic amines form salts. Tertiary aromatic amines undergo electrophilic substitution reactions under suitable conditions.

# Unit 4H: Electrophilic Substitution in Aniline

The `-NH2` group strongly activates benzene ring and directs electrophiles to ortho and para positions.

However, in strongly acidic medium, aniline forms anilinium ion, which is deactivating and meta directing. Therefore, protection of `-NH2` is often needed.

## Acetylation as Protection

Aniline is converted to acetanilide before nitration or bromination to reduce excessive activation and avoid side reactions.

After substitution, hydrolysis restores `-NH2`.

# Unit 4I: Diazonium Salts

Benzene diazonium chloride is prepared by treating aniline with nitrous acid at `273-278 K`.

`C6H5NH2 + NaNO2 + HCl -> C6H5N2+Cl-`

Diazonium salts are highly useful because the diazonium group can be replaced by many other groups.

## Replacement Reactions

| Product | Reagent or condition |
|---|---|
| Chlorobenzene | `CuCl/HCl` |
| Bromobenzene | `CuBr/HBr` |
| Iodobenzene | `KI` |
| Benzonitrile | `CuCN/KCN` |
| Phenol | Warm water |
| Benzene | Hypophosphorous acid |
| Fluorobenzene | Fluoroboric acid route |

## Coupling Reactions

Diazonium salts couple with phenols or aromatic amines to form azo compounds, which are often coloured dyes.

Example:

benzene diazonium chloride plus phenol in alkaline medium gives an azo dye.

# Unit 4J: Distinguishing Amines

| Test or reaction | Primary amine | Secondary amine | Tertiary amine |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carbylamine test | Positive | Negative | Negative |
| Acylation | Yes | Yes | No |
| Nitrous acid | Alcohol or diazonium salt | N-nitroso compound | Salt or ring reaction |
| Has `N-H` bond | Yes | Yes | No |

# Unit 4K: Reaction Selection Toolkit

| Desired conversion | Reagent or route |
|---|---|
| Nitrobenzene to aniline | Reduction |
| Alkyl halide to primary amine | Excess ammonia |
| Nitrile to amine with one extra carbon | Reduction |
| Amide to amine with one less carbon | Hoffmann bromamide |
| Primary aliphatic amine selectively | Gabriel synthesis |
| Aniline to diazonium salt | `NaNO2/HCl`, `273-278 K` |
| Diazonium salt to phenol | Warm water |
| Diazonium salt to chlorobenzene | `CuCl/HCl` |
| Aniline controlled nitration | Acetylate, nitrate, hydrolyse |

# Unit 4L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying tertiary amines undergo acylation | Tertiary amines lack `N-H`, so they do not form amides by acylation. |
| Preparing arylamines by Gabriel synthesis | Gabriel synthesis is for primary aliphatic amines. |
| Ignoring temperature in diazotisation | Keep `273-278 K` for stable diazonium salt formation. |
| Saying aniline is more basic than ammonia | Aniline is less basic due to resonance delocalisation of lone pair. |
| Forgetting over-alkylation in ammonolysis | Excess ammonia helps favour primary amine. |
| Treating carbylamine as test for all amines | Only primary amines give positive carbylamine test. |

## Final Revision

- Amines are basic because nitrogen has a lone pair.
- Aliphatic amines are generally more basic than ammonia; aniline is less basic.
- Gabriel synthesis gives primary aliphatic amines.
- Hoffmann bromamide reaction gives amine with one carbon less.
- Primary amines give carbylamine test.
- Aniline forms diazonium salt at low temperature.
- Diazonium salts are valuable synthetic intermediates for haloarenes, phenols, nitriles, benzene, and azo dyes.
