# Chapter 1: Haloalkanes and Haloarenes
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Haloalkanes and haloarenes are organic compounds containing carbon-halogen bonds. In haloalkanes, the halogen is attached to an aliphatic carbon. In haloarenes, the halogen is attached directly to an aromatic ring. The chapter connects structure, nomenclature, preparation, physical properties, nucleophilic substitution, elimination, stereochemistry, organometallic formation, and environmental impact.

The key to this chapter is the carbon-halogen bond. Its polarity makes haloalkanes reactive toward nucleophiles, while resonance makes haloarenes much less reactive toward ordinary nucleophilic substitution.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- classify haloalkanes and haloarenes;
- name halogen compounds using common and IUPAC systems;
- explain preparation from alcohols, hydrocarbons, and alkenes;
- compare reactivity of alkyl halides and aryl halides;
- explain SN1 and SN2 mechanisms;
- predict stereochemical outcome of substitution reactions;
- explain elimination reactions and Saytzeff orientation;
- describe reactions with metals, including Grignard reagent formation;
- explain why haloarenes resist nucleophilic substitution;
- discuss environmental concerns of polyhalogen compounds using scientific reasons.

# Unit 1A: Classification

Haloalkanes and haloarenes can be classified in several ways.

## By Number of Halogen Atoms

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Monohalogen compound | One halogen atom | `CH3Cl` |
| Dihalogen compound | Two halogen atoms | `CH2Cl2` |
| Trihalogen compound | Three halogen atoms | `CHCl3` |
| Polyhalogen compound | Many halogen atoms | `CCl4` |

## By Carbon Attached to Halogen

| Type | Structure idea | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary alkyl halide | Halogen carbon attached to one carbon | `CH3CH2Cl` |
| Secondary alkyl halide | Halogen carbon attached to two carbons | `CH3CHClCH3` |
| Tertiary alkyl halide | Halogen carbon attached to three carbons | `(CH3)3CCl` |
| Allylic halide | Halogen on carbon next to `C=C` | `CH2=CHCH2Cl` |
| Benzylic halide | Halogen on carbon next to benzene ring | `C6H5CH2Cl` |
| Vinylic halide | Halogen directly on alkene carbon | `CH2=CHCl` |
| Aryl halide | Halogen directly on aromatic ring | `C6H5Cl` |

# Unit 1B: Nomenclature

In IUPAC naming, halogens are treated as prefixes: fluoro, chloro, bromo, and iodo.

Examples:

| Formula | IUPAC name |
|---|---|
| `CH3CH2Cl` | chloroethane |
| `CH3CHBrCH3` | 2-bromopropane |
| `CH2Cl2` | dichloromethane |
| `CHCl3` | trichloromethane |
| `CCl4` | tetrachloromethane |
| `C6H5Cl` | chlorobenzene |

For substituted benzene rings, use locants or common o-, m-, p- notation where appropriate.

# Unit 1C: Nature of the Carbon-Halogen Bond

Halogens are more electronegative than carbon, so the `C-X` bond is polar:

`C(delta+) - X(delta-)`

This makes the carbon atom electron-deficient and open to nucleophilic attack.

Bond strength generally follows:

`C-F > C-Cl > C-Br > C-I`

Leaving group ability generally follows:

`I- > Br- > Cl- > F-`

So alkyl iodides are usually most reactive in substitution reactions.

# Unit 1D: Preparation of Haloalkanes

## From Alcohols

Alcohols react with hydrogen halides or reagents such as `PCl5`, `PCl3`, `SOCl2`, or red phosphorus with halogen.

General reaction:

`R-OH + HX -> R-X + H2O`

Lucas reagent, a mixture of concentrated hydrochloric acid and anhydrous zinc chloride, is used to distinguish alcohol classes through rate of turbidity.

## From Alkenes

Addition of hydrogen halide to alkene gives haloalkane.

`CH2=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CH2Br`

Unsymmetrical alkenes usually follow Markovnikov's rule: hydrogen goes to carbon already having more hydrogen atoms.

In the presence of peroxides, addition of `HBr` may follow anti-Markovnikov orientation.

## Free Radical Halogenation

Alkanes react with chlorine or bromine in the presence of light or heat.

`R-H + Cl2 -> R-Cl + HCl`

This method often gives mixtures because different hydrogens may be replaced.

# Unit 1E: Preparation of Haloarenes

## Electrophilic Substitution

Benzene reacts with chlorine or bromine in presence of Lewis acid catalyst.

`C6H6 + Cl2 -> C6H5Cl + HCl`

Catalyst: `FeCl3` or `AlCl3`.

## Sandmeyer-Type Routes

Aryl diazonium salts can be converted into aryl chlorides, bromides, and iodides.

Example:

`C6H5N2+Cl- + CuCl -> C6H5Cl + N2`

These routes are important because direct substitution on benzene is selective and useful from diazonium salts.

# Unit 1F: Physical Properties

| Property | Trend |
|---|---|
| Boiling point | Increases with molecular mass and surface area. |
| Density | Many bromo, iodo, and polyhalo compounds are denser than water. |
| Solubility in water | Low, because they cannot form strong hydrogen bonds with water. |
| Solubility in organic solvents | Usually good. |
| Boiling point among isomers | Straight-chain isomers usually have higher boiling points than branched isomers. |

Bond polarity exists, but water solubility remains low because energy released by solvation is not enough to break strong water-water hydrogen bonding.

# Unit 1G: Nucleophilic Substitution in Haloalkanes

Nucleophilic substitution replaces halogen by a nucleophile.

General:

`R-X + Nu- -> R-Nu + X-`

Examples:

| Reagent | Product |
|---|---|
| Aqueous `KOH` | Alcohol |
| Alcoholic `KCN` | Nitrile |
| `AgCN` | Isocyanide |
| `NH3` | Amine |
| `KNO2` | Alkyl nitrite |
| `AgNO2` | Nitroalkane |

The difference between `KCN` and `AgCN` is important. `KCN` is ionic and attacks mainly through carbon to form nitrile. `AgCN` has more covalent character and often gives isocyanide.

# Unit 1H: SN2 Mechanism

SN2 is a one-step substitution. The nucleophile attacks from the backside while the leaving group leaves.

Rate law:

`rate = k[R-X][Nu-]`

Features:

- one-step mechanism;
- no carbocation intermediate;
- backside attack;
- inversion of configuration;
- favoured by methyl and primary alkyl halides;
- hindered by bulky groups.

Reactivity order for SN2:

`CH3X > 1 degree > 2 degree > 3 degree`

# Unit 1I: SN1 Mechanism

SN1 is a two-step substitution. First the leaving group departs to form carbocation. Then nucleophile attacks.

Rate law:

`rate = k[R-X]`

Features:

- two-step mechanism;
- carbocation intermediate;
- rate depends only on substrate concentration;
- favoured by stable carbocations;
- gives racemisation if carbocation is planar;
- favoured by polar protic solvents.

Reactivity order for SN1:

`3 degree > 2 degree > 1 degree > CH3X`

# Unit 1J: SN1 vs SN2

| Feature | SN1 | SN2 |
|---|---|---|
| Molecularity | Unimolecular rate-determining step | Bimolecular |
| Rate law | `k[R-X]` | `k[R-X][Nu-]` |
| Intermediate | Carbocation | None |
| Favoured substrate | Tertiary | Methyl, primary |
| Stereochemistry | Racemisation often | Inversion |
| Solvent | Polar protic | Polar aprotic often |
| Steric hindrance | Less harmful | Strongly harmful |

# Unit 1K: Elimination Reactions

When haloalkanes react with alcoholic potassium hydroxide, elimination of hydrogen halide can form alkene.

General:

`R-CH2-CHX-R' + base -> R-CH=CH-R' + HX`

Saytzeff rule: the major alkene is usually the more substituted alkene.

Example:

2-bromobutane mainly gives but-2-ene rather than but-1-ene.

# Unit 1L: Reactions with Metals

## Wurtz Reaction

Alkyl halides react with sodium in dry ether to form higher alkanes.

`2R-X + 2Na -> R-R + 2NaX`

Best for symmetrical alkanes.

## Grignard Reagent Formation

Alkyl halides react with magnesium in dry ether:

`R-X + Mg -> R-Mg-X`

Grignard reagents are highly reactive and must be protected from water because they behave like strong bases.

`R-Mg-X + H2O -> R-H + Mg(OH)X`

# Unit 1M: Why Haloarenes Are Less Reactive

Haloarenes are much less reactive toward nucleophilic substitution because:

- resonance gives partial double-bond character to the `C-X` bond;
- the carbon attached to halogen is `sp2` hybridised and holds electrons more tightly;
- phenyl cation is unstable, so SN1 is unfavourable;
- backside attack required for SN2 is difficult due to aromatic ring geometry.

## When Haloarenes React

Haloarenes can undergo nucleophilic substitution under harsh conditions or when strong electron-withdrawing groups are present at ortho or para positions.

For example, chlorobenzene reacts more easily if nitro groups are attached at ortho or para positions because they stabilise the intermediate.

# Unit 1N: Electrophilic Substitution in Haloarenes

Halogens deactivate benzene ring because of their `-I` effect, but they direct incoming electrophiles to ortho and para positions because of resonance donation.

So haloarenes are:

- deactivating;
- ortho-para directing.

Examples:

- chlorobenzene gives ortho and para nitrochlorobenzene on nitration;
- para product often dominates due to less steric hindrance.

# Unit 1O: Polyhalogen Compounds

| Compound | Important points |
|---|---|
| Dichloromethane | Used as solvent; harmful by inhalation. |
| Chloroform | Former anaesthetic; can oxidise to poisonous phosgene. |
| Iodoform | Yellow solid with antiseptic history. |

# Unit 1P: Reaction Selection Toolkit

| Desired conversion | Reagent or condition |
|---|---|
| Alcohol to chloroalkane | `HCl/ZnCl2` or `SOCl2` |
| Alkene to haloalkane | `HX` |
| Alkane to haloalkane | `Cl2` or `Br2` with light |
| Haloalkane to alcohol | Aqueous `KOH` |
| Haloalkane to alkene | Alcoholic `KOH` |
| Haloalkane to nitrile | `KCN` |
| Haloalkane to amine | Excess `NH3` |
| Aryl diazonium salt to aryl chloride | `CuCl/HCl` |
| Alkyl halide to Grignard reagent | `Mg`, dry ether |

# Unit 1Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Using aqueous `KOH` for elimination | Aqueous `KOH` favours substitution; alcoholic `KOH` favours elimination. |
| Saying haloarenes are more reactive because benzene is reactive | Haloarenes resist nucleophilic substitution due to resonance in `C-X`. |
| Confusing SN1 and SN2 stereochemistry | SN2 gives inversion; SN1 often gives racemisation. |
| Ignoring dryness in Grignard preparation | Grignard reagents are destroyed by water. |
| Treating halogens as activating in haloarenes | Halogens are deactivating but ortho-para directing. |

## Final Revision

- Haloalkanes react readily because the `C-X` bond is polar.
- Reactivity as leaving group usually follows `I > Br > Cl > F`.
- SN1 depends on carbocation stability.
- SN2 depends strongly on low steric hindrance.
- Aqueous base favours substitution; alcoholic base favours elimination.
- Haloarenes are less reactive due to resonance and `sp2` carbon-halogen bond strength.
- Halogens deactivate benzene but direct electrophiles to ortho and para positions.
