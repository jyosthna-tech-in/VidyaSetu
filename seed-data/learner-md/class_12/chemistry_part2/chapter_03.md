# Chapter 3: Aldehydes, Ketones and Carboxylic Acids
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Aldehydes, ketones, and carboxylic acids contain the carbonyl group in different environments. Aldehydes contain `-CHO`, ketones contain `>C=O` between two carbon groups, and carboxylic acids contain `-COOH`. The carbonyl carbon is electrophilic, so aldehydes and ketones undergo nucleophilic addition. Carboxylic acids are acidic and often undergo reactions involving the `O-H` bond or replacement of the hydroxyl part.

This chapter is one of the most conversion-heavy organic chapters. The best way to master it is to group reactions by functional group and by reaction logic: preparation, nucleophilic addition, oxidation, reduction, acidity, and named reactions.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- name aldehydes, ketones, and carboxylic acids;
- explain structure and polarity of carbonyl group;
- prepare aldehydes and ketones from alcohols, hydrocarbons, acyl chlorides, nitriles, and esters;
- prepare carboxylic acids from alcohols, aldehydes, nitriles, amides, Grignard reagents, and alkylbenzenes;
- compare reactivity of aldehydes and ketones toward nucleophilic addition;
- explain important reactions with HCN, sodium bisulphite, ammonia derivatives, alcohols, and Grignard reagents;
- distinguish aldehydes and ketones using oxidation tests;
- explain aldol condensation and Cannizzaro reaction;
- describe acidity and reactions of carboxylic acids;
- solve conversion-based questions using reagent logic.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Carbonyl group | `C=O` functional group. |
| Aldehyde | Compound containing `-CHO`. |
| Ketone | Compound containing carbonyl carbon bonded to two carbon groups. |
| Carboxylic acid | Compound containing `-COOH`. |
| Nucleophilic addition | Addition of nucleophile to electrophilic carbonyl carbon. |
| Acetal | Product formed by reaction of aldehyde with alcohol under dry acid condition. |
| Ketal | Product formed by reaction of ketone with alcohol under dry acid condition. |
| Aldol condensation | Reaction of aldehydes or ketones with alpha hydrogen in dilute base. |
| Cannizzaro reaction | Disproportionation of aldehydes without alpha hydrogen in concentrated base. |
| Decarboxylation | Removal of carbon dioxide from carboxylic acid derivative. |

| Functional group | Main behaviour |

# Unit 3A: Nomenclature

## Aldehydes

The IUPAC suffix is `-al`.

Examples:

| Formula | Name |
|---|---|
| `HCHO` | methanal |
| `CH3CHO` | ethanal |
| `CH3CH2CHO` | propanal |
| `C6H5CHO` | benzaldehyde |

## Ketones

The IUPAC suffix is `-one`.

Examples:

| Formula | Name |
|---|---|
| `CH3COCH3` | propanone |
| `CH3COCH2CH3` | butan-2-one |
| `C6H5COCH3` | acetophenone |

## Carboxylic Acids

The IUPAC suffix is `-oic acid`.

Examples:

| Formula | Name |
|---|---|
| `HCOOH` | methanoic acid |
| `CH3COOH` | ethanoic acid |
| `C6H5COOH` | benzoic acid |

# Unit 3B: Structure of Carbonyl Group

The carbonyl carbon is `sp2` hybridised and trigonal planar. Oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, so the bond is polar:

`C(delta+) = O(delta-)`

This makes carbonyl carbon electrophilic and oxygen nucleophilic/basic in character.

## Reactivity of Aldehydes vs Ketones

Aldehydes are generally more reactive than ketones toward nucleophilic addition because:

- aldehydes have less steric hindrance;
- aldehydes have only one electron-donating alkyl group or none;
- ketones have two alkyl or aryl groups that reduce electrophilicity of carbonyl carbon.

Reactivity order:

`HCHO > RCHO > ArCHO > RCOR'`

# Unit 3C: Preparation of Aldehydes and Ketones

## Oxidation of Alcohols

Primary alcohols give aldehydes under controlled oxidation.

`R-CH2OH -> R-CHO`

Secondary alcohols give ketones.

`R-CHOH-R' -> R-CO-R'`

## Dehydrogenation of Alcohols

Alcohol vapours passed over heated copper can form aldehydes or ketones.

Primary alcohol:

`R-CH2OH -> R-CHO + H2`

Secondary alcohol:

`R-CHOH-R' -> R-CO-R' + H2`

## Ozonolysis of Alkenes

Ozonolysis followed by reductive workup gives aldehydes and ketones depending on alkene structure.

This method is also used to locate double bonds.

## Hydration of Alkynes

Ethyne gives ethanal. Other alkynes usually give ketones under acidic mercuric salt conditions.

# Unit 3D: Special Preparation of Aldehydes

## Rosenmund Reduction

Acid chlorides are reduced to aldehydes using hydrogen over poisoned palladium catalyst.

`RCOCl -> RCHO`

The catalyst is poisoned to prevent further reduction to alcohol.

## Stephen Reduction

Nitriles are reduced to imines using stannous chloride and hydrochloric acid, followed by hydrolysis to aldehydes.

`R-CN -> R-CHO`

## From Aromatic Hydrocarbons

Important routes to benzaldehyde include controlled oxidation of toluene and related side-chain reactions.

# Unit 3E: Preparation of Ketones

## From Acyl Chlorides

Acyl chlorides react with dialkylcadmium to form ketones.

`2RCOCl + R'2Cd -> 2RCO-R' + CdCl2`

## From Nitriles

Nitriles react with Grignard reagents followed by hydrolysis to give ketones.

`R-CN + R'MgX -> R-CO-R'`

## From Benzene

Friedel-Crafts acylation gives aromatic ketones.

`C6H6 + RCOCl -> C6H5COR + HCl`

Catalyst: anhydrous `AlCl3`.

# Unit 3F: Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

Carbonyl compounds undergo nucleophilic addition because carbonyl carbon is electron-deficient.

General steps:

1. Nucleophile attacks carbonyl carbon.
2. Pi bond electrons move to oxygen.
3. Protonation gives addition product.

## Addition of HCN

Aldehydes and ketones form cyanohydrins.

`RCHO + HCN -> RCH(OH)CN`

Cyanohydrins are useful because the `CN` group can be converted to acids or amines.

## Addition of Sodium Bisulphite

Aldehydes and many methyl ketones form crystalline bisulphite addition compounds. This helps in purification and separation.

## Addition of Grignard Reagents

Grignard reagent adds to carbonyl group, followed by hydrolysis.

| Carbonyl compound | Product after hydrolysis |
|---|---|
| Formaldehyde | Primary alcohol |
| Other aldehyde | Secondary alcohol |
| Ketone | Tertiary alcohol |

# Unit 3G: Reactions with Ammonia Derivatives

Aldehydes and ketones react with ammonia derivatives to form condensation products.

| Reagent | Product type |
|---|---|
| Hydroxylamine | Oxime |
| Hydrazine | Hydrazone |
| Phenylhydrazine | Phenylhydrazone |
| 2,4-DNP | 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazone |
| Semicarbazide | Semicarbazone |

These reactions are useful for identification because products often have sharp melting points.

# Unit 3H: Oxidation and Reduction

## Oxidation

Aldehydes oxidise easily to carboxylic acids.

`RCHO -> RCOOH`

Ketones resist mild oxidation and require strong conditions, often causing carbon-carbon bond cleavage.

## Tollens' Test

Aldehydes reduce Tollens' reagent to metallic silver, giving silver mirror.

Ketones generally do not give this test.

## Fehling's Test

Aliphatic aldehydes reduce Fehling's solution to red precipitate of cuprous oxide. Aromatic aldehydes generally do not.

## Reduction

Aldehydes reduce to primary alcohols.

Ketones reduce to secondary alcohols.

Strong reductions such as Clemmensen or Wolff-Kishner convert carbonyl group to methylene group.

# Unit 3I: Aldol Condensation

Aldehydes or ketones with alpha hydrogen react in dilute base to form beta-hydroxy aldehydes or ketones. On heating, these dehydrate to alpha,beta-unsaturated carbonyl compounds.

Condition:

- carbonyl compound must have alpha hydrogen;
- dilute base is used.

Example:

`2CH3CHO -> CH3CH(OH)CH2CHO`

On heating:

`CH3CH(OH)CH2CHO -> CH3CH=CHCHO + H2O`

# Unit 3J: Cannizzaro Reaction

Aldehydes without alpha hydrogen undergo Cannizzaro reaction in concentrated base. One molecule is oxidised to carboxylate and another is reduced to alcohol.

Example:

`2HCHO + NaOH -> HCOONa + CH3OH`

Benzaldehyde also undergoes Cannizzaro reaction.

Important contrast:

- alpha hydrogen present: aldol condensation;
- alpha hydrogen absent: Cannizzaro reaction possible for aldehydes.

# Unit 3K: Carboxylic Acids - Preparation

## Oxidation

Primary alcohols and aldehydes oxidise to carboxylic acids.

`RCH2OH -> RCHO -> RCOOH`

## From Nitriles and Amides

Hydrolysis of nitriles or amides gives carboxylic acids.

`R-CN -> R-COOH`

## From Grignard Reagents

Grignard reagent reacts with carbon dioxide, followed by hydrolysis, to give carboxylic acid.

`R-MgX + CO2 -> R-COOH`

This adds one carbon atom to the chain.

## From Alkylbenzenes

Strong oxidation of alkylbenzene side chain gives benzoic acid if at least one benzylic hydrogen is present.

# Unit 3L: Acidity of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are more acidic than alcohols and phenols because carboxylate ion is resonance-stabilised.

`RCOOH -> RCOO- + H+`

Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity by stabilising carboxylate ion. Electron-donating groups decrease acidity.

## Acid Strength Examples

`ClCH2COOH > CH3COOH`

Chloroacetic acid is stronger because chlorine withdraws electron density.

# Unit 3M: Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

## Salt Formation

Carboxylic acids react with bases and carbonates.

`RCOOH + NaHCO3 -> RCOONa + CO2 + H2O`

Effervescence with sodium bicarbonate is a useful test.

## Esterification

Carboxylic acids react with alcohols in presence of acid to form esters.

`RCOOH + R'OH -> RCOOR' + H2O`

## Formation of Acid Chlorides

Carboxylic acids react with `SOCl2` to give acid chlorides.

`RCOOH + SOCl2 -> RCOCl + SO2 + HCl`

## Reduction

Strong reducing agents such as `LiAlH4` reduce carboxylic acids to primary alcohols.

## Decarboxylation

Sodium salts of carboxylic acids heated with soda lime lose carbon dioxide to form alkanes.

`RCOONa + NaOH -> R-H + Na2CO3`

# Unit 3N: Named Reaction Toolkit

| Name | Main conversion |
|---|---|
| Rosenmund reduction | Acid chloride to aldehyde |
| Stephen reduction | Nitrile to aldehyde |
| Friedel-Crafts acylation | Benzene to aromatic ketone |
| Aldol condensation | Carbonyl with alpha hydrogen to aldol product |
| Cannizzaro reaction | Aldehyde without alpha hydrogen to alcohol and carboxylate |
| Clemmensen reduction | Carbonyl to methylene in acidic medium |
| Wolff-Kishner reduction | Carbonyl to methylene in basic medium |
| Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky reaction | Alpha halogenation of carboxylic acids |

# Unit 3O: Conversion Logic

| Starting compound | Target | Route idea |
|---|---|---|
| Alcohol | Aldehyde or ketone | Controlled oxidation |
| Aldehyde | Acid | Oxidation |
| Ketone | Alcohol | Reduction |
| Nitrile | Acid | Hydrolysis |
| Grignard reagent | Acid with one extra carbon | `CO2` then hydrolysis |
| Acid | Ester | Alcohol plus acid catalyst |
| Acid | Acid chloride | `SOCl2` |
| Acid salt | Alkane with one less carbon | Soda lime |

# Unit 3P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating aldehydes and ketones as equally reactive | Aldehydes are usually more reactive. |
| Using Tollens' reagent for ketones generally | Tollens' test is mainly for aldehydes. |
| Applying aldol to compounds without alpha hydrogen | Aldol requires alpha hydrogen. |
| Applying Cannizzaro to ketones | Cannizzaro is for aldehydes without alpha hydrogen. |
| Forgetting Grignard reagents are destroyed by water | Hydrolysis is done only after addition step. |
| Missing one-carbon increase in Grignard plus carbon dioxide | `CO2` adds one carbon. |

## Final Revision

- Carbonyl carbon is electrophilic.
- Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones.
- Aldehydes oxidise easily; ketones resist mild oxidation.
- Aldol condensation requires alpha hydrogen.
- Cannizzaro reaction requires aldehyde without alpha hydrogen.
- Carboxylic acids are acidic due to resonance-stabilised carboxylate ion.
- `SOCl2` converts acids to acid chlorides.
- Soda lime decarboxylation shortens the carbon chain by one carbon.
