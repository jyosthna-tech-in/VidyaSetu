# Chapter 2: Alcohols, Phenols and Ethers
### NCERT Class 12 - Chemistry Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

Alcohols, phenols, and ethers are oxygen-containing organic compounds. Alcohols contain `-OH` attached to saturated carbon. Phenols contain `-OH` attached directly to an aromatic ring. Ethers contain an oxygen atom between two carbon groups. Their behaviour depends on the polarity of the `C-O` and `O-H` bonds, hydrogen bonding, resonance, and the nature of the carbon group attached to oxygen.

This chapter is reaction-rich. Learners should organise it by functional group, then by reaction type: preparation, acidity, substitution, dehydration, oxidation, electrophilic substitution, and ether cleavage.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- classify alcohols as primary, secondary, and tertiary;
- name alcohols, phenols, and ethers;
- explain hydrogen bonding and its effect on boiling point and solubility;
- compare acidity of alcohols and phenols;
- prepare alcohols from alkenes, carbonyl compounds, and esters;
- prepare phenols from haloarenes, diazonium salts, and cumene;
- prepare ethers by Williamson synthesis;
- predict reactions of alcohols, phenols, and ethers;
- explain electrophilic substitution in phenol;
- distinguish alcohols, phenols, and ethers using chemical behaviour.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Alcohol | Compound containing `-OH` group attached to saturated carbon. |
| Phenol | Compound containing `-OH` group directly attached to benzene ring. |
| Ether | Compound containing `R-O-R'` linkage. |
| Primary alcohol | `-OH` carbon attached to one carbon atom. |
| Secondary alcohol | `-OH` carbon attached to two carbon atoms. |
| Tertiary alcohol | `-OH` carbon attached to three carbon atoms. |
| Alkoxide | Conjugate base of alcohol, `RO-`. |
| Phenoxide | Conjugate base of phenol, `C6H5O-`. |
| Williamson synthesis | Preparation of ether from alkoxide and primary alkyl halide. |
| Dehydration | Removal of water, often forming alkene or ether. |

## Quick Learning Map

| Compound family | Main structural feature | Important behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Alcohols | `R-OH` | Hydrogen bonding, oxidation, substitution, dehydration |
| Phenols | `Ar-OH` | More acidic, ring activation, electrophilic substitution |
| Ethers | `R-O-R'` | Less reactive, cleavage by strong hydrogen halides |

# Unit 2A: Classification and Nomenclature

## Alcohol Classification

| Type | General form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | `R-CH2OH` | ethanol |
| Secondary | `R-CHOH-R'` | propan-2-ol |
| Tertiary | `R3C-OH` | 2-methylpropan-2-ol |

## Names

| Formula | IUPAC name |
|---|---|
| `CH3OH` | methanol |
| `CH3CH2OH` | ethanol |
| `CH3CH(OH)CH3` | propan-2-ol |
| `C6H5OH` | phenol |
| `CH3OCH3` | methoxymethane |
| `C2H5OC2H5` | ethoxyethane |

For alcohols, choose the longest chain containing `-OH` and give the hydroxyl carbon the lowest possible locant.

# Unit 2B: Structure and Bonding

Oxygen is electronegative, so `C-O` and `O-H` bonds are polar. Alcohols and phenols can form hydrogen bonds with themselves and water.

Ethers can accept hydrogen bonds from water but cannot donate hydrogen bonds because they do not have `O-H`.

## Boiling Point Trend

For comparable molar masses:

`alcohols > ethers > alkanes`

Alcohols have higher boiling points due to intermolecular hydrogen bonding.

## Solubility in Water

Lower alcohols are soluble in water due to hydrogen bonding. Solubility decreases as hydrocarbon chain length increases.

Phenol is moderately soluble. Ethers are less soluble than alcohols but more soluble than hydrocarbons of similar size.

# Unit 2C: Preparation of Alcohols

## From Alkenes

Acid-catalysed hydration of alkenes gives alcohols following Markovnikov orientation.

`CH2=CH2 + H2O -> CH3CH2OH`

For unsymmetrical alkenes, `-OH` goes to the more substituted carbon in acid-catalysed hydration.

## Hydroboration-Oxidation

Hydroboration followed by oxidation gives anti-Markovnikov alcohol.

Useful idea:

`alkene -> primary alcohol` for terminal alkenes.

## From Carbonyl Compounds

Aldehydes reduce to primary alcohols.

`R-CHO -> R-CH2OH`

Ketones reduce to secondary alcohols.

`R-CO-R' -> R-CHOH-R'`

Common reducing agents include `LiAlH4`, `NaBH4`, or catalytic hydrogenation.

## From Carboxylic Acids and Esters

Carboxylic acids and esters can be reduced to primary alcohols using strong reducing agents such as `LiAlH4`.

# Unit 2D: Preparation of Phenols

## From Haloarenes

Chlorobenzene can be converted to phenol under harsh conditions with sodium hydroxide, followed by acidification.

`C6H5Cl -> C6H5ONa -> C6H5OH`

## From Diazonium Salts

Benzene diazonium chloride gives phenol on warming with water.

`C6H5N2+Cl- + H2O -> C6H5OH + N2 + HCl`

## From Cumene

Cumene is oxidised to cumene hydroperoxide, which on acid hydrolysis gives phenol and acetone.

This is an important industrial method.

# Unit 2E: Preparation of Ethers

## Williamson Synthesis

Williamson synthesis uses an alkoxide and primary alkyl halide.

`R-O-Na+ + R'-X -> R-O-R' + NaX`

Best conditions:

- primary alkyl halide;
- strong alkoxide nucleophile;
- dry conditions.

Tertiary alkyl halides give elimination instead of substitution, so they are poor choices for Williamson synthesis.

# Unit 2F: Acidity of Alcohols and Phenols

Alcohols and phenols are weak acids because they can release `H+` from `O-H`.

Phenols are more acidic than alcohols because phenoxide ion is resonance-stabilised. Alkoxide ions from alcohols do not get similar resonance stabilisation.

## Acidity Order

`phenol > water > alcohol`

Electron-withdrawing groups on phenol increase acidity. Electron-donating groups decrease acidity.

## Reaction with Sodium

Alcohols and phenols react with sodium metal to release hydrogen gas.

`2ROH + 2Na -> 2RONa + H2`

Phenol also reacts with sodium hydroxide:

`C6H5OH + NaOH -> C6H5ONa + H2O`

Most alcohols are not acidic enough to react significantly with `NaOH`.

# Unit 2G: Reactions of Alcohols

## With Hydrogen Halides

Alcohols react with hydrogen halides to form alkyl halides.

`R-OH + HX -> R-X + H2O`

Reactivity order:

`3 degree alcohol > 2 degree alcohol > 1 degree alcohol`

## With Thionyl Chloride

`R-OH + SOCl2 -> R-Cl + SO2 + HCl`

This method is useful because gaseous by-products escape, helping product purification.

## Dehydration

Alcohols dehydrate to alkenes in presence of acid and heat.

`R-CH2-CH2OH -> R-CH=CH2 + H2O`

Ease of dehydration:

`3 degree > 2 degree > 1 degree`

## Oxidation

| Alcohol type | Oxidation product |
|---|---|
| Primary | Aldehyde, then carboxylic acid |
| Secondary | Ketone |
| Tertiary | Resistant under mild conditions |

Mild oxidising agent can stop primary alcohol at aldehyde stage. Strong oxidising agents continue to acid.

# Unit 2H: Reactions of Phenols

Phenol is highly reactive toward electrophilic substitution because `-OH` activates the ring and directs to ortho and para positions.

## Bromination

With bromine water, phenol forms 2,4,6-tribromophenol, a white precipitate.

## Nitration

Dilute nitric acid gives a mixture of ortho-nitrophenol and para-nitrophenol.

Concentrated nitric acid gives picric acid, also called 2,4,6-trinitrophenol.

## Kolbe Reaction

Sodium phenoxide reacts with carbon dioxide under pressure, followed by acidification, to form salicylic acid.

## Reimer-Tiemann Reaction

Phenol reacts with chloroform and aqueous sodium hydroxide, followed by acidification, to form salicylaldehyde.

# Unit 2I: Reactions of Ethers

Ethers are relatively less reactive because the `C-O-C` linkage is stable. They undergo cleavage with strong hydrogen halides.

General:

`R-O-R' + HI -> R-I + R'-OH`

With excess `HI`, alcohol can also convert to alkyl iodide.

For aryl alkyl ethers such as anisole:

`C6H5-O-CH3 + HI -> C6H5OH + CH3I`

The aryl-oxygen bond is not easily broken because of partial double-bond character.

# Unit 2J: Electrophilic Substitution in Anisole

Anisole, `C6H5OCH3`, has a methoxy group that activates the benzene ring and directs electrophiles to ortho and para positions.

Examples:

- bromination gives ortho-bromoanisole and para-bromoanisole;
- nitration gives ortho-nitroanisole and para-nitroanisole.

Para product often dominates due to lower steric hindrance.

# Unit 2K: Distinguishing Tests and Clues

| Compound type | Useful clue |
|---|---|
| Phenol | Reacts with `NaOH`; gives violet colour with neutral ferric chloride in many cases. |
| Alcohol | Reacts with sodium; does not usually react with `NaOH`. |
| Primary alcohol | Oxidises to aldehyde and then acid. |
| Secondary alcohol | Oxidises to ketone. |
| Tertiary alcohol | Resists mild oxidation. |
| Ether | Cleaved by hot concentrated `HI` or `HBr`. |

# Unit 2L: Reaction Selection Toolkit

| Desired conversion | Reagent or condition |
|---|---|
| Alkene to Markovnikov alcohol | Acid-catalysed hydration |
| Alkene to anti-Markovnikov alcohol | Hydroboration-oxidation |
| Aldehyde to primary alcohol | `NaBH4` or `LiAlH4` |
| Ketone to secondary alcohol | `NaBH4` or `LiAlH4` |
| Alcohol to alkyl chloride | `SOCl2` |
| Alcohol to alkene | Concentrated acid and heat |
| Phenol to salicylic acid | Kolbe reaction |
| Phenol to salicylaldehyde | Reimer-Tiemann reaction |
| Alkoxide to ether | Williamson synthesis |
| Ether cleavage | Hot concentrated `HI` |

# Unit 2M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying alcohols are more acidic than phenols | Phenols are more acidic due to resonance-stabilised phenoxide ion. |
| Using tertiary halide in Williamson synthesis | Use primary alkyl halide to avoid elimination. |
| Forgetting phenol is ortho-para directing | `-OH` strongly activates the ring. |
| Expecting ether to donate hydrogen bonds | Ethers accept but do not donate hydrogen bonds. |
| Oxidising tertiary alcohol under mild conditions | Tertiary alcohols resist mild oxidation. |

## Final Revision

- Alcohols and phenols contain `-OH`; ethers contain `C-O-C`.
- Hydrogen bonding explains boiling point and solubility trends.
- Phenol is more acidic than alcohol because phenoxide ion is resonance-stabilised.
- Primary alcohols oxidise to aldehydes and acids; secondary alcohols oxidise to ketones.
- Phenol is strongly activating and ortho-para directing.
- Williamson synthesis works best with primary alkyl halides.
- Ethers are cleaved by strong hydrogen halides.
