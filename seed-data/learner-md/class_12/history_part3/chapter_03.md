## 1. Big Picture

This chapter studies urbanisation in colonial India through three major cities: Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta. These cities began as coastal trading settlements of the English East India Company and later became major centres of colonial power.

Colonial cities were different from older Indian towns. They were shaped by:

1. Overseas trade.
2. Forts and ports.
3. Colonial administration.
4. Racial segregation.
5. Municipal regulation.
6. Railways.
7. European architectural styles.
8. New forms of labour and housing.

The chapter also teaches how historians use maps, censuses, municipal records, surveys, paintings, photographs, and official reports to study urban history.

## 2. Pre-Colonial Towns

Before British rule, Indian towns had their own social and economic character.

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Fortified spaces | Many towns had walls marking separation from countryside. |
| Ruling elites | Kings, nobles, administrators, and religious elites shaped urban life. |
| Artisan and trading groups | Towns housed craftspeople, merchants, and service providers. |
| Relationship with countryside | Towns depended on agricultural surplus and taxes. |
| Fluid boundary | People, goods, pilgrims, traders, and labourers moved between town and village. |

Important pre-colonial cities included Delhi, Agra, Lahore, Surat, Masulipatnam, Dhaka, and others.

## 3. Shift Under Colonial Rule

From the eighteenth century, urban importance shifted.

### Declining Centres

Older commercial centres such as Surat, Masulipatnam, and Dhaka declined when trade routes and political power shifted.

### Rising Colonial Cities

Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta rose because they were linked to the English East India Company's trade and later to British political control.

| City | Early Colonial Importance |
| --- | --- |
| Madras | Company settlement with Fort St George. |
| Bombay | Port city that grew through trade, reclamation, and later cotton industry. |
| Calcutta | Company settlement with Fort William; later capital of British India until 1911. |

## 4. Why Port Cities Grew

Colonial port cities grew because they were convenient for:

1. Collecting goods from the hinterland.
2. Exporting Indian raw materials and manufactures.
3. Importing British manufactured goods.
4. Housing Company officials and merchants.
5. Establishing forts and administrative offices.
6. Linking sea trade with inland markets.

These cities became economic and political centres of colonial India.

## 5. Colonial Records and Urban History

Colonial rule produced large amounts of data. This helps historians study colonial cities in detail.

### Major Sources

| Source | What It Reveals |
| --- | --- |
| Census records | Population size, occupations, caste, religion, migration. |
| Municipal records | Water, sanitation, roads, taxes, public health, urban services. |
| Maps | Layout, roads, forts, neighbourhoods, expansion, planning. |
| Surveys | Land use, property, topography, revenue. |
| Official reports | Administrative concerns and policy decisions. |
| Paintings/photographs | Visual evidence of buildings, streets, dress, and urban life. |
| Travel accounts | Observations by visitors and officials. |

## 6. Reading Maps Critically

Maps are useful but not neutral.

They reveal:

1. Street layouts.
2. Forts and public buildings.
3. Administrative priorities.
4. Expansion of settlements.
5. Racial and class divisions.

They may conceal:

1. Everyday life of poor residents.
2. Informal settlements.
3. Social conflicts.
4. Experiences of women, workers, and migrants.
5. Biases of colonial planners.

Historical skill: ask who made the map, why it was made, and which areas are shown in detail.

## 7. Urbanisation Trends

Urbanisation in colonial India was uneven.

Important points:

1. Overall urban growth was slow in the nineteenth century.
2. Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta grew rapidly.
3. Many older towns declined.
4. Railways changed the importance of towns.
5. New colonial cities grew at the cost of some traditional centres.

The rise of the three presidency cities did not mean India became highly industrialised. Colonial economic policy limited industrial development.

## 8. Ports, Forts, and Services

The English East India Company built forts to protect trade and settlements.

| City | Fort |
| --- | --- |
| Madras | Fort St George |
| Calcutta | Fort William |
| Bombay | The Fort |

Around these forts, separate European and Indian settlements developed.

### White Town and Black Town

| Area | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| White Town | European settlement near the fort; associated with colonial officials and merchants. |
| Black Town | Indian settlement outside the fort; included merchants, artisans, workers, and service groups. |

This division reflected racial segregation and colonial hierarchy.

## 9. New Urban Economy

Colonial port cities developed new economic spaces:

1. Docks.
2. Warehouses.
3. Godowns.
4. Mercantile offices.
5. Insurance agencies.
6. Banking establishments.
7. Transport depots.
8. Administrative offices.

Indian merchants, brokers, interpreters, suppliers, artisans, and workers were essential to this economy.

## 10. Railways and Urban Change

Railways changed the geography of trade.

Effects:

1. Port cities became connected to the hinterland.
2. Raw materials could be moved to ports more easily.
3. Imported goods could be distributed inland.
4. Railway stations became new commercial centres.
5. Some older river-route towns declined.

Railways strengthened colonial economic extraction by linking interior production to ports.

## 11. Industrial Development

Modern industries appeared in some cities:

| Place | Industry |
| --- | --- |
| Bombay | Cotton mills. |
| Calcutta outskirts | Jute mills. |
| Kanpur | Leather, woollen and cotton textiles. |
| Jamshedpur | Steel. |

However, Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta were not primarily industrial cities. Much of their workforce was in the tertiary sector: trade, transport, services, administration, and domestic work.

## 12. Social Life in New Cities

Colonial cities attracted many groups:

1. Merchants.
2. Clerks.
3. Labourers.
4. Artisans.
5. Domestic servants.
6. Interpreters.
7. Professionals.
8. Migrants from villages and small towns.

Urban life was marked by mobility, opportunity, crowding, poverty, and new social interactions.

## 13. Housing and Chawls

Bombay's growth created a housing crisis. Workers often lived in chawls.

### Chawl

A chawl was a multi-storeyed tenement building divided into small one-room units, usually with shared facilities.

| Feature | Impact |
| --- | --- |
| Small rooms | Crowded living conditions. |
| Shared water/toilets | Poor sanitation and disease risk. |
| Low rent | Accessible to workers. |
| Dense community | Social networks and neighbourhood cultures formed. |

## 14. Segregation and Town Planning

Colonial planning often separated Europeans from Indians.

### Reasons for Segregation

1. Racial prejudice.
2. Fear of disease.
3. Desire for security.
4. Need to display power.
5. Belief that Indian areas were overcrowded and unsanitary.

### Civil Lines and Cantonments

Civil Lines were spacious European residential areas with bungalows, gardens, and wide roads. Cantonments were military settlements.

These areas were racially exclusive and physically separated from dense Indian neighbourhoods.

## 15. Sanitation and Control

The British often linked Indian neighbourhoods with disease and disorder. Sanitary improvement became a way to regulate Indian urban spaces.

Municipal authorities focused on:

1. Drainage.
2. Water supply.
3. Road building.
4. Public health.
5. Removal of "congestion".
6. Demolition or relocation of settlements.

Public health concerns were real, but they were often shaped by racial assumptions and colonial control.

## 16. Hill Stations

Hill stations were another feature of colonial urban development.

Examples include Shimla, Darjeeling, Ootacamund, and others.

### Why They Developed

1. Cooler climate for Europeans.
2. Military recovery and health.
3. Administrative retreats.
4. Summer capitals.
5. Spaces resembling European environments.

Hill stations also became symbols of racial privilege.

## 17. Architecture of Colonial Cities

Buildings in colonial cities displayed power and identity.

### Major Styles

| Style | Features | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Neo-classical | Columns, symmetry, grand proportions. | Linked British rule with classical authority. |
| Neo-Gothic | Pointed arches, high roofs, towers, decorative detail. | Associated with modernity, civic pride, and European institutions. |
| Indo-Saracenic | Combined Indian and Islamic elements with European design. | Tried to present British rule as connected to Indian tradition. |

Architecture was not only practical. It expressed imperial power.

## 19. Bombay as an Example

Bombay grew through:

1. Port trade.
2. Land reclamation joining islands.
3. Cotton trade.
4. Railway connections.
5. Textile mills.
6. Migration of workers.
7. Expansion of commercial and residential areas.

Its growth also produced congestion, chawls, class divisions, and conflicts over urban space.

## 20. Calcutta as an Example

Calcutta grew as:

1. A trading post.
2. A fortified settlement.
3. A colonial administrative capital.
4. A city divided into European and Indian areas.
5. A centre of jute, commerce, education, and administration.

It became a major symbol of British power in India.

## 21. Madras as an Example

Madras developed around Fort St George. It had a White Town near the fort and a Black Town outside it. Its growth reflected trade, European settlement, Indian merchant participation, and colonial planning.

## 22. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Thinking colonial cities grew only because of industry | They grew mainly through trade, administration, ports, and services; industry was limited. |
| Treating maps as neutral | Maps reflect colonial priorities and may hide many social realities. |
| Ignoring racial segregation | White Town/Black Town and Civil Lines were central to colonial urban form. |
| Assuming sanitation was only welfare | It was also a tool of regulation and control. |
| Thinking architecture was only decorative | It displayed imperial authority and racial hierarchy. |

## 23. Exam Answer Frames

### Rise of Colonial Cities

Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta grew as colonial cities because they were coastal settlements connected to the trade of the English East India Company. They developed forts, ports, warehouses, administrative offices, and European residential areas. After British political power expanded, these cities became centres of administration, trade, services, and imperial display.

### Colonial Records and Urban History

Colonial rule generated detailed records such as censuses, maps, municipal reports, surveys, and official documents. These sources help historians study population, planning, sanitation, taxation, and urban expansion. However, they must be read critically because they often reflect colonial priorities and may ignore ordinary people's experiences.

### Segregation in Colonial Cities

Colonial cities were marked by racial segregation. European settlements near forts were called White Towns, while Indian settlements outside were called Black Towns. Later, Civil Lines and cantonments became exclusive European spaces. This segregation reflected racial hierarchy, fear of disease, security concerns, and the desire to display colonial power.

## 26. Final Learning Takeaway

Colonial cities were not simply larger versions of older towns. They were shaped by colonial trade, racial hierarchy, administrative control, new records, new architecture, and new forms of urban life. Studying them shows how empire was built into streets, forts, maps, houses, stations, and neighbourhoods.
