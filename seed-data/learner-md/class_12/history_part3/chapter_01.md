# Chapter 1: Colonialism and the Countryside
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 3

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter explains how colonial rule changed rural India through land revenue systems, legal contracts, surveys, credit networks, and market pressures. It begins with Bengal, where the Permanent Settlement created a new revenue order around zamindars, then moves to the Rajmahal hills, where Paharias and Santhals experienced colonial expansion differently, and finally studies the Bombay Deccan, where revenue demand, debt, moneylenders, cotton prices, and legal bonds produced agrarian anger.

The central idea is simple but powerful: colonial rule in the countryside was not only about collecting taxes. It reorganised authority, property, forests, credit, law, and everyday relations between peasants, zamindars, moneylenders, traders, officials, and tribal communities.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, learners should be able to:

- Explain why the Permanent Settlement was introduced in Bengal in 1793.
- Identify why many zamindars failed to pay revenue after the Permanent Settlement.
- Explain the rise of jotedars and why they weakened zamindari power.
- Describe how zamindars used fictitious sales and local loyalty to retain control.
- Understand why the Fifth Report is important but must be read critically.
- Compare the worlds of Paharias and Santhals in the Rajmahal hills.
- Explain why Santhals rebelled in 1855-56.
- Analyse the ryotwari system in the Bombay Deccan.
- Connect revenue demand, debt, cotton prices, and moneylenders to the Deccan riots of 1875.
- Evaluate colonial enquiry reports as historical evidence.

# Unit 1A: Colonial Rule in the Countryside

Colonial rule first became deeply established in Bengal after the East India Company acquired the Diwani rights in 1765. Diwani gave the Company the right to collect revenue in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa. From this point, the Company was no longer only a trading body. It became a revenue-collecting power.

The countryside became central to colonial rule because land revenue was the main financial base of the state. The British believed that if they could fix rights over land and create regular revenue collection, they could stabilise their rule, increase income, and encourage agricultural expansion.

The chapter shows that this plan did not work smoothly. Once colonial policies entered villages, they interacted with older power structures, caste relations, local loyalties, ecological differences, market fluctuations, and forms of resistance.

## The Core Question

The chapter repeatedly asks:

How did colonial laws and revenue systems reshape rural society, and how did rural people respond to them?

The answer is not one-sided. Colonial laws affected people, but people also modified the working of those laws through delay, evasion, rebellion, petitioning, alliance-building, and everyday resistance.

# Unit 1B: The Permanent Settlement in Bengal

The Permanent Settlement was introduced in Bengal in 1793 under Governor-General Cornwallis. Its main feature was that the land revenue demand was fixed permanently. The Company expected the zamindars to pay a fixed amount of revenue every year.

The British hoped this would solve several problems:

- It would create a regular flow of income for the Company.
- It would encourage zamindars to invest in agriculture because any increase in production would increase their profit.
- It would create a loyal class of landlords who would support British rule.
- It would reduce uncertainty in revenue collection.
- It would make land a more secure form of property.

In theory, the arrangement looked stable. In practice, it created tensions almost immediately.

## What the British Assumed

British officials assumed that zamindars were landowners who could improve agriculture if their rights were secured. But in many areas, zamindars had mainly been revenue collectors and local chiefs with layered claims over villages. The Permanent Settlement simplified a complex rural world into a legal structure that suited colonial revenue needs.

## What Zamindars Had To Do

Zamindars collected rent from villages, paid the fixed revenue demand to the Company, and kept the difference as income. If they failed to pay on time, their estates could be auctioned.

This turned revenue punctuality into a matter of survival. A bad harvest, low prices, or local resistance could threaten a zamindar's control over an estate.

# Unit 1C: The Burdwan Auction

The auction in Burdwan in 1797 shows how the Permanent Settlement worked in practice. The Raja of Burdwan had accumulated large arrears, so many of his estates were put up for auction.

At first glance, it seemed that the Company's law had worked: a defaulting zamindar's estate was publicly sold. But the Collector later discovered that many purchasers were actually the raja's servants and agents. They had bought the estate on his behalf.

This case teaches three lessons:

- Colonial legal procedures did not automatically remove old power holders.
- Zamindars learned to manipulate auctions.
- Local authority could survive behind the appearance of legal change.

The Burdwan case is therefore not just a story of revenue default. It is a story of negotiation between colonial law and local power.

# Unit 1D: Why Zamindars Defaulted

Many zamindars defaulted after the Permanent Settlement. The reasons were structural, not merely personal.

## High Revenue Demand

The Company fixed the revenue demand at a very high level. Officials feared that if demand was fixed forever, the Company would lose future gains from rising prices and expanding cultivation. To avoid that loss, they set the initial demand high.

This made the system risky from the beginning.

## Low Agricultural Prices in the 1790s

The demand was imposed at a time when agricultural prices were low. Ryots found it difficult to pay rent. If zamindars could not collect rent, they could not pay revenue to the Company.

## Inflexible Payment Rules

Revenue had to be paid punctually, regardless of harvest conditions. The Sunset Law made delay dangerous. If payment did not arrive by the fixed time, the estate could be auctioned.

## Weakening of Zamindari Authority

The Company wanted zamindars to collect revenue, but it also wanted to control them. It disbanded their troops, abolished their customs duties, brought their courts under supervision, and reduced their police and judicial powers.

This created a contradiction: zamindars were expected to collect rent efficiently, but their older means of enforcing authority were restricted.

## Resistance by Ryots and Jotedars

Ryots sometimes delayed payment. Rich peasants and village headmen, especially jotedars, often benefited when zamindars were in difficulty. They could mobilise poorer cultivators, obstruct rent collection, and even buy auctioned estates.

# Unit 1E: Rise of the Jotedars

Jotedars were rich peasants who became powerful in many parts of rural Bengal, especially North Bengal. They controlled large areas of land, local trade, moneylending, and the labour of poorer cultivators.

Many jotedars had land cultivated by sharecroppers called adhiyars or bargadars. These sharecroppers brought their own ploughs and labour but gave a share of the produce, often half, to the jotedar.

## Why Jotedars Became Powerful

Jotedars lived in villages, unlike many zamindars who lived in towns. This gave them direct influence over rural society.

They were powerful because they could:

- Lend money to peasants.
- Control grain and local trade.
- Influence poor cultivators.
- Resist zamindari officials.
- Delay revenue payments.
- Purchase estates at auction.

## Jotedars versus Zamindars

| Zamindars | Jotedars |
|---|---|
| Often controlled many villages from a distance | Lived inside or near villages |
| Had recognised revenue obligations to the Company | Had direct control over poorer peasants |
| Depended on collection through officials | Controlled labour, credit, and local trade |
| Faced auction if revenue was unpaid | Often gained from zamindari weakness |

The rise of jotedars shows that rural power was not located only at the top. A rich peasant group could become more effective locally than a large zamindar.

# Unit 1F: How Zamindars Resisted Displacement

Zamindars did not simply collapse under colonial pressure. Many used creative strategies to protect their estates.

## Fictitious Sales

Zamindars arranged for their agents or relatives to buy auctioned estates in their name. In some cases, property was transferred to women because Company rules initially made it harder to take over women's property.

The usual pattern was:

- The zamindar allowed arrears to accumulate.
- Part of the estate was auctioned.
- Agents of the zamindar bought it.
- The purchase money was not paid.
- The estate was auctioned again.
- The process exhausted rival bidders and officials.
- The estate returned to the zamindar at a lower price.

## Local Loyalty

Outsiders who bought zamindari estates often struggled to take possession. Former zamindars had armed retainers, local influence, and the loyalty of ryots who saw them as familiar authority figures.

Some ryots resisted new buyers because the sale of a zamindari disturbed their sense of identity and belonging. They saw themselves as proja, or subjects, of the zamindar.

## Flexible Rules After Crisis

By the early nineteenth century, agricultural prices improved and the rules of payment became somewhat more flexible. Zamindars who survived the 1790s often regained strength.

# Unit 1G: The Fifth Report

The Fifth Report was submitted to the British Parliament in 1813. It was one of a series of reports on the administration and activities of the East India Company in India.

It contained:

- Petitions by zamindars and ryots.
- Reports by Collectors.
- Revenue statistics.
- Notes on administration.
- Evidence on judicial and revenue systems.

## Why It Matters

The report is valuable because it provides detailed information on early Company rule, revenue collection, petitions, and official debates.

It also shows that Company rule in India was being debated in Britain. Many groups in Britain criticised Company monopoly, corruption, and misrule. Parliament demanded information and tried to regulate the Company.

## How To Read It Critically

The Fifth Report is not a neutral mirror of rural life. It was produced for Parliament, shaped by official anxieties, and often framed Company problems in ways useful to British debate.

Learners should ask:

- Who produced the report?
- For whom was it written?
- What information was included?
- What voices were filtered through officials?
- What might be missing?

This critical method is essential for all colonial records.

# Unit 1H: Paharias and the Rajmahal Hills

The chapter next turns to the Rajmahal hills. The Paharias lived in this region and depended on forests, shifting cultivation, hunting, gathering, and some trade.

Their livelihood was closely tied to hill ecology. They cleared patches of forest for shifting cultivation, collected forest produce, hunted, and moved through the landscape in ways that did not fit the settled agricultural model preferred by colonial officials.

## Paharia Relations with Outsiders

Paharias sometimes raided plains settlements, especially during times of scarcity. They also extracted tribute or protection payments from settled agricultural communities and traders.

For the British, this appeared as disorder. For Paharias, these practices were part of survival and political assertion in a frontier region.

## British Policy Toward Paharias

British officials first tried military campaigns, but these were difficult in hill terrain. Later, they attempted pacification by giving allowances to Paharia chiefs and encouraging settled life.

However, these policies did not fully integrate Paharias into the colonial order. The deeper change came when the British encouraged Santhal settlement in the region.

# Unit 1I: Santhals in the Rajmahal Region

Santhals entered the Rajmahal region in large numbers in the early nineteenth century. British officials saw them as hardworking settlers who could clear forests, expand cultivation, and increase revenue.

The area called Damin-i-Koh was marked out for Santhal settlement. Santhals cleared forests, built villages, and created settled agricultural communities.

## Why the British Encouraged Santhal Settlement

The Company wanted:

- More cultivated land.
- More settled villages.
- Higher revenue.
- A more controllable population.
- A buffer against Paharia mobility.

## Effect on Paharias

Santhal settlement reduced the forest space available to Paharias. Forest clearings, settled cultivation, and state-backed expansion changed the ecology of the hills.

The arrival of Santhals therefore created new tensions among communities, the forest, and the colonial state.

# Unit 1J: The Santhal Rebellion

The Santhals rebelled in 1855-56. Their anger was directed against the dikus, or outsiders, who included moneylenders, traders, zamindars, and colonial agents.

The rebellion grew out of several pressures:

- Land alienation.
- Debt and exploitation by moneylenders.
- High demands by revenue agents.
- Loss of autonomy.
- Expansion of outside control over Santhal villages.

Santhals had initially been encouraged to settle and cultivate, but over time they became trapped in a network of debt, revenue demand, and outside domination.

## Historical Meaning

The rebellion shows that colonial expansion in tribal regions was not a peaceful process of cultivation. It involved dispossession, ecological change, credit exploitation, and conflict over authority.

# Unit 1K: Bombay Deccan and Ryotwari Settlement

The chapter then moves west to the Bombay Deccan. Unlike Bengal, this region did not receive a permanent zamindari settlement. Instead, a ryotwari system was introduced.

Under ryotwari settlement, revenue was settled directly with the ryot, or peasant cultivator. The state assessed the revenue-paying capacity of land and fixed its demand from the cultivator.

## Why the British Chose Ryotwari

By the early nineteenth century, British officials were influenced by economic ideas that criticised absentee rentiers. They believed Bengal zamindars had become rent-receiving intermediaries who did not improve agriculture.

In the Deccan, officials wanted a system that would deal directly with cultivators and allow revenue to be revised periodically.

## Key Feature

The revenue demand was not permanent. Land was resurveyed and revenue rates could be increased after a period, often around thirty years.

# Unit 1L: Revenue Demand and Peasant Debt

The first revenue settlement in the Bombay Deccan was made in the 1820s. The demand was often very high.

In areas with poor soil and unreliable rainfall, peasants struggled. When crops failed, they still had to pay revenue. Officials pressured them to pay because revenue collection was a measure of administrative efficiency.

When peasants could not pay, they borrowed from moneylenders.

## Debt Cycle

The debt cycle usually worked like this:

- Revenue demand had to be paid.
- Harvests failed or prices fell.
- Ryots borrowed from sahukars.
- Interest accumulated.
- New bonds replaced older bonds.
- Unpaid interest was added to principal.
- Peasants lost land, cattle, carts, or crops.
- Dependence on moneylenders increased.

This was not only an economic problem. It affected dignity, autonomy, and everyday survival.

# Unit 1M: Cotton Boom and Cotton Bust

The American Civil War began in 1861 and disrupted cotton supplies from America to Britain. British manufacturers looked to India for cotton.

This produced a cotton boom in the Bombay Deccan.

## During the Boom

Cotton prices rose. Merchants wanted more cotton. Credit became easier. Urban sahukars gave money to rural moneylenders, who gave advances to cultivators.

Some ryots received advances for planting cotton. Cotton acreage expanded quickly.

## Limits of the Boom

The boom did not make most peasants secure. Many expanded cultivation through loans. If prices remained high, they might manage. But if prices fell, they would be trapped.

## After the American Civil War

When American cotton production revived, demand for Indian cotton declined. Prices fell. Credit dried up. Moneylenders demanded repayment and refused new loans.

At the same time, revenue demand was increased under new settlements. This combination was explosive:

- Falling cotton prices.
- Reduced credit.
- Rising revenue demand.
- Accumulated debt.
- Moneylender pressure.

# Unit 1N: Experience of Injustice

The Deccan ryots were angry not only because they were poor, but because they believed moneylenders had violated customary norms.

Earlier rural credit relations were unequal, but they were often regulated by custom. One important norm was that interest should not exceed the principal. Under colonial legal systems, written contracts and bonds became more important than customary fairness.

Moneylenders used legal documents to strengthen their claims. Peasants often signed bonds they could not read or fully understand.

## Common Complaints by Ryots

Ryots complained that moneylenders:

- Charged excessive interest.
- Refused receipts after repayment.
- Entered false figures in bonds.
- Took crops at unfair prices.
- Added unpaid interest to fresh bonds.
- Took over land, cattle, and carts.
- Used courts to enforce written documents.

## Why Written Bonds Became Hated

To colonial officials, written contracts represented clarity and legal order. To many peasants, they represented deception, loss, and fear.

This is why the burning of account books became such a powerful form of protest in 1875.

# Unit 1O: Deccan Riots of 1875

In 1875, peasants in parts of the Bombay Deccan rose against moneylenders. The riots often involved attacks on sahukars' houses and the destruction of debt bonds, deeds, and account books.

The main target was not random property. It was the documentary basis of debt.

## Why Account Books Were Burned

Account books and bonds were symbols of:

- Accumulated debt.
- Legal power of moneylenders.
- Loss of peasant property.
- Colonial courts enforcing creditor claims.
- A new regime of written obligation.

By destroying these documents, peasants attacked the system that made them vulnerable.

# Unit 1P: Deccan Riots Commission

The Government of Bombay initially underestimated the seriousness of the riots. But the Government of India, remembering the revolt of 1857, pressed for an enquiry.

The Deccan Riots Commission investigated the causes of the uprising and produced a report presented to the British Parliament in 1878.

## Value of the Report

It contains:

- Statements by ryots.
- Statements by sahukars.
- Eyewitness accounts.
- Revenue statistics.
- Price data.
- Interest rate information.
- Reports by district officials.

## Limitation of the Report

The Commission was asked to judge whether government revenue demand caused the riots. It concluded that the moneylenders, rather than the government, were mainly responsible.

This must be read critically. Colonial officials were often reluctant to admit that state policy produced popular anger.

## Best Historical Method

Use official reports, but compare them with:

- Petitions.
- Local accounts.
- Newspapers.
- Court records.
- Oral memory where available.

History becomes stronger when evidence is compared rather than accepted at face value.

# Unit 1R: Cause and Effect Chains

## Permanent Settlement

High fixed revenue demand -> zamindar pressure -> difficulty collecting from ryots -> arrears -> auctions -> fictitious sales and jotedar rise.

## Santhal Rebellion

Forest settlement -> cultivation expansion -> outside moneylenders and traders -> debt and land pressure -> resentment against dikus -> rebellion.

## Deccan Riots

High revenue demand -> borrowing -> cotton boom credit -> cotton bust -> credit withdrawal -> debt enforcement -> anger against moneylenders -> burning of bonds and account books.

# Unit 1S: Answer-Writing Framework

## If asked: Why did zamindars default after the Permanent Settlement?

Write in this order:

1. State that default was common in the 1790s.
2. Explain high revenue demand.
3. Add low prices and poor rent collection.
4. Explain rigid punctuality and the Sunset Law.
5. Mention reduced zamindari authority.
6. Add jotedar and ryot resistance.
7. Conclude that the system ignored rural realities.

## If asked: Why did jotedars become powerful?

Write in this order:

1. Define jotedars as rich peasants.
2. Explain their control over land, trade, and credit.
3. Mention sharecroppers.
4. Explain village-level presence.
5. Show how they resisted zamindars.
6. Conclude that they weakened zamindari authority.

## If asked: Why did Deccan ryots rebel?

Write in this order:

1. Begin with the 1875 riots.
2. Explain high revenue demand and debt.
3. Explain the cotton boom and later fall in prices.
4. Explain moneylender practices and legal bonds.
5. Show why account books became targets.
6. Conclude with the role of colonial law and revenue policy.

# Unit 1T: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying the Permanent Settlement benefited all zamindars | Many zamindars defaulted and faced auction. |
| Treating zamindars as simple landowners | They were revenue intermediaries with complex local authority. |
| Ignoring jotedars | Jotedars were crucial to village-level power in Bengal. |
| Saying Santhals rebelled only because of British officials | They rebelled against a wider world of dikus, including moneylenders and traders. |
| Saying ryotwari was light on peasants | It could be harsh because revenue demand was high and revisable. |
| Blaming only moneylenders for Deccan riots | Moneylenders mattered, but revenue demand, courts, bonds, and market shifts also mattered. |
| Accepting official reports without question | Colonial reports must be read critically and compared with other evidence. |
