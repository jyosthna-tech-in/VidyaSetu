# Chapter 2: Rebels and the Raj
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 3

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies the revolt of 1857, how it began as sepoy mutinies and widened into a major rebellion in north India. It examines the pattern of uprising, the role of leaders and ordinary people, the importance of rumours and religious fears, the special case of Awadh, the aims of the rebels, British repression, and the ways in which the revolt was represented in images and memories.

The chapter is not only about "what happened" in 1857. It is also about how historians reconstruct a rebellion when most surviving records were produced by the colonial state and by the victors.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, learners should be able to:

- Explain the sequence of events from Meerut to Delhi in May 1857.
- Distinguish between mutiny and revolt.
- Identify the common pattern followed in many cantonments.
- Explain why rebels turned to older rulers for leadership.
- Analyse the role of rumours, prophecies, chapattis, and religious anxieties.
- Understand why Awadh became a major centre of rebellion.
- Explain what rebels wanted and how their aims differed across social groups.
- Describe British laws and military measures used to suppress the rebellion.
- Read British visual representations as political documents, not neutral pictures.
- Explain why later nationalist memory treated 1857 as the First War of Independence.

# Unit 2A: Meerut and Delhi

The revolt began in Meerut on 10 May 1857. Sepoys in the cantonment mutinied, attacked symbols of British authority, and were joined by ordinary people from the town and nearby villages.

They attacked:

- European officers and residents.
- Bungalows and property.
- Government buildings.
- Record offices.
- Courts.
- Jails.
- Treasuries.
- Telegraph lines.

By cutting the telegraph line to Delhi, the rebels attacked both colonial communication and colonial control.

The sepoys then rode to Delhi. On 11 May 1857, they entered the city and appealed to the Mughal emperor Bahadur Shah Zafar for leadership. The old emperor was not militarily powerful, but his name carried symbolic authority. Once he accepted, the rebellion could be carried out in the name of the Mughal emperor.

## Why Delhi Mattered

Delhi had historical and symbolic importance. It represented Mughal sovereignty and an older political order. For rebels, capturing Delhi meant that the revolt was not merely a military disturbance. It now had a political centre.

# Unit 2B: Mutiny Becomes Revolt

At first, the uprising began in sepoy lines. But in many places, it quickly spread to the civilian population. This is why historians distinguish between mutiny and revolt.

## Mutiny

A mutiny is a collective act of disobedience within the army. In 1857, sepoys refused authority, attacked officers, seized weapons, and left cantonments.

## Revolt

A revolt is wider. It involves civilians, peasants, zamindars, artisans, townspeople, religious figures, and local leaders. In 1857, many places witnessed this transformation.

The uprising was therefore not just a military rebellion. In several regions, it became a social and political rebellion against British rule and its local allies.

# Unit 2C: Pattern of the Uprising

Many cantonments followed a similar pattern:

1. A signal was given, often the firing of a gun or the sounding of a bugle.
2. Sepoys seized the bell of arms.
3. Treasuries were attacked.
4. Jails, courts, record rooms, and telegraph offices were destroyed.
5. European bungalows and official property were burned.
6. Proclamations called on Hindus and Muslims to unite.
7. Ordinary people joined.
8. Targets expanded to moneylenders, traders, and local elites seen as British allies.

## Why Records Were Burned

Records symbolised authority. Land records, court papers, debt documents, and official files were instruments through which colonial rule operated. Burning them was a political act.

## Why Moneylenders Were Attacked

In many towns and villages, moneylenders were seen as exploiters and allies of colonial rule. Peasants who were losing land and facing debt saw them as part of the system of oppression.

# Unit 2D: Communication and Coordination

The revolt spread quickly because information moved through sepoy lines, messengers, rumours, letters, local networks, and shared military culture.

There is evidence of communication between cantonments. Sepoys and emissaries moved from one military station to another. In some places, panchayats or collective meetings discussed future action.

## What This Means

The rebellion was not a simple spontaneous outburst. It had elements of planning, discussion, and coordination. But historians cannot always identify a single central command. Much of the planning was local and network-based.

This is why the revolt appears both coordinated and decentralised.

# Unit 2E: Leaders and Followers

The rebels needed leadership, legitimacy, and organisation. They often turned to older rulers who had lost power under British expansion.

## Major Leaders

| Leader | Region | Historical Role |
|---|---|---|
| Bahadur Shah Zafar | Delhi | Symbolic Mughal leader of the rebellion. |
| Nana Sahib | Kanpur | Successor of Peshwa Baji Rao II; joined as a major leader. |
| Rani Lakshmi Bai | Jhansi | Became a powerful symbol of resistance. |
| Birjis Qadr | Awadh | Son of Nawab Wajid Ali Shah; hailed in Lucknow. |
| Kunwar Singh | Arrah, Bihar | Local zamindar who led rebellion. |
| Shah Mal | Barout, Uttar Pradesh | Mobilised Jat cultivators and village networks. |
| Maulvi Ahmadullah Shah | Awadh and Faizabad | Religious leader and military figure in the revolt. |

## Why Older Leaders Were Chosen

Older rulers represented a familiar political order. Their names could unite people who were angry for different reasons. A sepoy, a peasant, a dispossessed taluqdar, and a town artisan might not have identical goals, but they could rally around a recognised figure.

## Ordinary Leaders

Not all leadership came from courts. Religious figures, village headmen, peasants, tribal leaders, and local organisers also mobilised people. This widened the rebellion beyond elite politics.

# Unit 2F: Rumours, Prophecies, and Chapattis

Rumours played a major role in 1857. The most famous rumour concerned the new Enfield rifle cartridges. Sepoys believed the cartridges were greased with cow and pig fat and had to be bitten before use. This threatened both Hindu and Muslim religious beliefs.

Another rumour claimed that the British had mixed bone dust from cows and pigs into flour. Many people feared that the British intended to destroy caste and religion and convert Indians to Christianity.

## Why Rumours Were Believed

Rumours are powerful when they connect with existing fears. By the 1850s, many Indians already distrusted British reforms and annexations.

These fears were shaped by:

- Western education policies.
- Missionary activity.
- Abolition of some social customs by law.
- Widow remarriage legislation.
- Annexation of Indian states.
- New land revenue settlements.
- Interference in local power structures.

Therefore, the cartridge rumour seemed believable to many sepoys because it fitted a larger pattern of suspicion.

## Chapatti Circulation

Reports suggested that chapattis were passed from village to village before the revolt. Their meaning remains uncertain. But many people treated the circulation as a sign that something major was about to happen.

The historian's task is not to prove every rumour true. It is to understand what rumours reveal about fear, trust, and political imagination.

# Unit 2G: Awadh in Revolt

Awadh was one of the strongest centres of the rebellion. It had been annexed by the British in 1856 on the claim of misgovernment. This caused deep resentment.

## Why Annexation Hurt Awadh

The removal of Nawab Wajid Ali Shah was not only a political event. It disrupted court culture, employment, patronage, and local identity. Soldiers, officials, artisans, performers, religious figures, and dependents of the court all felt the loss.

## Taluqdars and Revenue Settlements

After annexation, British revenue settlements weakened many taluqdars. Their estates were reduced and their authority was challenged. Many peasants also suffered from harsh revenue demands and administrative intrusion.

This created a rare alignment:

- Taluqdars wanted their power restored.
- Peasants were angry about revenue and local oppression.
- Sepoys from Awadh resented British actions.
- Court dependents lost patronage.
- Religious and cultural groups feared change.

## Why Awadh Was Different

In Awadh, the revolt had deep rural roots. It was not limited to cantonments. British officials found it difficult to suppress because villages, landholders, and local networks supported resistance.

# Unit 2H: What the Rebels Wanted

The rebels did not produce one unified manifesto for modern nationhood. Their aims varied, but several shared themes can be seen.

## Defence of Religion and Custom

Many proclamations accused the British of attacking Hindu and Muslim faiths. Calls for unity often stressed that both communities should defend their religion and social order.

## Restoration of Older Authority

Many rebels wanted to restore rulers and systems displaced by British expansion. This is why the Mughal emperor, nawabs, rajas, ranis, and taluqdars became important.

## Attack on Oppressors

In several places, rebels attacked moneylenders, traders, officials, police stations, courts, and record rooms. The rebellion became an attack on local injustice as well as foreign rule.

## Alternative Power

Where British rule collapsed, rebels attempted to create administrative arrangements. They collected revenue, paid troops, issued orders, and tried to stop disorder. These efforts were often short-lived, but they show that the rebels wanted power, not only destruction.

# Unit 2I: Hindu-Muslim Unity

Many rebel proclamations called on Hindus and Muslims to unite. The cartridge rumour itself was powerful because it seemed to insult both communities: cow fat offended Hindus, pig fat offended Muslims.

Rebel leaders used shared religious anxiety to build political unity. They also invoked older ideas of justice, kingship, and protection.

## Important Point

Unity did not mean that all groups had identical aims. It meant that different groups could cooperate against a common enemy. The revolt brought together many grievances under the banner of anti-British action.

# Unit 2J: British Repression

The British response was severe. In May and June 1857, laws were passed that placed much of north India under martial law. Military officers and even ordinary Britons were given powers to punish suspected rebels.

Normal legal procedures were suspended. The punishment for rebellion was often death.

## Reconquest

The British recognised the symbolic value of Delhi. They launched attacks from different directions and finally captured Delhi in September 1857 after heavy fighting.

In the Gangetic plain and Awadh, reconquest was slow. Villages had to be subdued one by one. British officials realised that they were facing not merely mutinous soldiers but a rebellion with popular support.

## Divide and Reward

The British also used political methods. They rewarded loyal landholders and punished rebels. In some regions, they tried to break the unity between big landholders and peasants by promising the return of estates to loyal elites.

## Performance of Terror

Public executions, hangings, and blowing rebels from guns were meant to instil fear. Punishment was staged openly so that people would see the consequences of rebellion.

# Unit 2K: Images of the Revolt

The chapter gives special attention to visual representations. Paintings, cartoons, prints, and photographs shaped how people understood 1857.

## British Images

British images often:

- Celebrated British officers as heroes.
- Presented British women and children as helpless victims.
- Portrayed rebels as brutal and savage.
- Justified revenge and violent repression.
- Showed ruins and executions as warnings.

These images were not neutral. They were part of imperial politics. They helped create public support in Britain for harsh punishment.

## Nationalist Images

In the twentieth century, Indian nationalist memory presented 1857 as the First War of Independence. Leaders such as Rani Lakshmi Bai became heroic symbols of anti-colonial courage.

Nationalist images:

- Celebrated sacrifice.
- Emphasised unity against foreign rule.
- Turned leaders into icons.
- Helped later generations remember the revolt as part of the freedom struggle.

# Unit 2L: How Historians Reconstruct 1857

Historians face a difficult problem: rebel voices are limited. Many surviving records were written by British officials, soldiers, missionaries, journalists, and artists.

## Available Evidence

Historians use:

- Official reports.
- Military records.
- Letters and diaries.
- Newspaper reports.
- Rebel proclamations.
- Court records.
- Visual images.
- Later oral and literary traditions.

## Critical Reading

Each type of evidence has limits.

British accounts often reveal British fears, prejudices, and anger. Rebel proclamations reveal political aims but may not represent every participant. Visual images reveal emotional and ideological messages, not plain facts.

The best historical answer compares evidence and asks who created it, why, and for which audience.

# Unit 2N: Cause and Effect Chains

## From Cartridge Rumour to Mutiny

New cartridges -> fear of religious pollution -> sepoy distrust -> refusal and punishment -> anger in cantonments -> mutiny.

## From Annexation to Revolt in Awadh

Annexation of Awadh -> removal of Nawab -> disruption of court and patronage -> taluqdar displacement -> peasant anger -> broad rural rebellion.

## From British Images to Repression

Reports of violence -> public anger in Britain -> demand for revenge -> visual images of British suffering -> justification of brutal punishment.

# Unit 2O: Answer-Writing Framework

## If asked: Why did rebels turn to older rulers for leadership?

Write in this order:

1. State that rebels needed legitimacy and organisation.
2. Explain the symbolic value of older rulers.
3. Use Bahadur Shah Zafar as the strongest example.
4. Add Nana Sahib, Rani Lakshmi Bai, Birjis Qadr, or Kunwar Singh.
5. Explain that older authority could unite different groups.
6. Conclude that leadership gave the revolt political meaning.

## If asked: How did rumours shape 1857?

Write in this order:

1. Define rumours as politically powerful information.
2. Explain the cartridge rumour.
3. Add bone dust and conversion fears.
4. Connect rumours to British reforms, annexations, and missionary activity.
5. Mention chapattis and prophecy if relevant.
6. Conclude that rumours reveal fear and distrust.

## If asked: Why was Awadh a major centre of revolt?

Write in this order:

1. Mention annexation in 1856.
2. Explain removal of Wajid Ali Shah.
3. Explain court culture and lost patronage.
4. Explain taluqdar displacement.
5. Add peasant revenue grievances.
6. Conclude that Awadh brought together elite and popular anger.

## If asked: What do visual representations tell us about 1857?

Write in this order:

1. State that images are historical evidence.
2. Explain British images of heroism, suffering, and revenge.
3. Explain how images justified repression.
4. Explain nationalist images of Rani Lakshmi Bai and 1857 as freedom struggle.
5. Conclude that images shape memory as well as record it.

# Unit 2P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling 1857 only a sepoy mutiny | In many regions it became a wider civilian revolt. |
| Saying the revolt had one central plan | It had coordination, but much planning was local and network-based. |
| Treating rumours as unimportant because they may be false | Rumours reveal real fears and political suspicion. |
| Ignoring Awadh | Awadh was central because annexation affected soldiers, peasants, taluqdars, and court society. |
| Saying all rebels wanted exactly the same thing | Different groups had different aims, though they shared anti-British anger. |
| Reading British images as factual photographs of truth | Visuals were shaped by political emotion and imperial ideology. |
| Ignoring repression | British suppression was military, legal, psychological, and political. |
