## 1. Big Picture

Mahatma Gandhi returned to India from South Africa in 1915. Before his arrival, the Indian National Congress was important but limited in social reach. Gandhi changed the nature of the national movement by connecting politics with peasants, workers, women, students, merchants, and ordinary people.

His methods included:

1. Satyagraha.
2. Non-cooperation.
3. Civil disobedience.
4. Constructive work.
5. Hindu-Muslim unity.
6. Opposition to untouchability.
7. Use of symbols such as khadi, charkha, and salt.

The chapter also teaches how historians reconstruct Gandhi's life and politics through sources such as letters, newspapers, government reports, speeches, autobiographies, and oral memories.

## 3. Gandhi's South African Experience

Gandhi developed many of his political methods in South Africa.

There he:

1. Faced racial discrimination.
2. Organised Indian migrants.
3. Developed satyagraha.
4. Promoted inter-religious harmony.
5. Became aware of caste and gender inequalities among Indians.

This experience prepared him for leadership in India.

## 4. Satyagraha

### Meaning

Satyagraha means holding firmly to truth. It was Gandhi's method of non-violent resistance.

### Key Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Truth | Protest must be based on moral truth. |
| Non-violence | Violence corrupts the cause and the protester. |
| Self-suffering | The satyagrahi accepts suffering rather than inflicting it. |
| Moral appeal | The aim is to convert the opponent, not destroy them. |
| Discipline | Protesters must control anger and action. |

## 5. Gandhi's Early Actions in India

### Champaran, 1917

Gandhi supported peasants forced to grow indigo under oppressive conditions. The movement showed that Gandhi could connect with rural distress and challenge colonial authority.

### Kheda, 1918

Gandhi supported peasants in Gujarat who wanted revenue relief during crop failure.

### Ahmedabad, 1918

Gandhi intervened in a dispute between mill owners and workers. This showed his ability to work with labour issues as well as peasant concerns.

## 6. Gandhi at Banaras Hindu University

Gandhi's speech at BHU in 1916 was important because he criticised the gap between nationalist elites and ordinary Indians.

He objected to:

1. Excessive display of wealth.
2. Elite politics disconnected from the poor.
3. Lack of attention to peasants and workers.
4. Nationalism that did not represent the masses.

This speech signalled his desire to make nationalism more inclusive.

## 7. Rowlatt Act and Jallianwala Bagh

### Rowlatt Act, 1919

The Rowlatt Act allowed detention without trial. Gandhi called for protest against it.

### Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

In April 1919, British troops fired on a peaceful gathering in Amritsar, killing hundreds. The massacre deeply shocked India and intensified anti-colonial feeling.

### Significance

1. Destroyed faith in British justice for many Indians.
2. Expanded nationalist anger.
3. Helped prepare the ground for Non-Cooperation.

## 8. Non-Cooperation Movement

### Period

1920-1922.

### Main Idea

Indians were asked to stop cooperating with British rule.

### Forms of Non-Cooperation

1. Boycott of government schools and colleges.
2. Boycott of law courts.
3. Boycott of foreign cloth.
4. Surrender of titles.
5. Picketing of liquor shops.
6. Promotion of khadi.
7. Resignation from government service where possible.

### Why It Became a Mass Movement

1. It used simple methods.
2. It connected with local grievances.
3. It involved students, workers, peasants, merchants, and women.
4. It used symbols ordinary people understood.
5. It linked nationalism with everyday life.

### Withdrawal After Chauri Chaura

In 1922, a violent incident at Chauri Chaura led to the killing of policemen. Gandhi withdrew the movement because he believed violence made satyagraha morally unacceptable.

## 9. Khadi and Charkha

Khadi and the spinning wheel became symbols of:

1. Self-reliance.
2. Swadeshi.
3. Dignity of labour.
4. Rejection of foreign cloth.
5. Unity between rich and poor.
6. Simple living.

For Gandhi, clothing was political. Wearing khadi showed commitment to national struggle and rural labour.

## 10. Salt March and Civil Disobedience

### Salt March, 1930

Gandhi marched from Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi to break the salt law.

### Why Salt Was Chosen

Salt was a brilliant political symbol because:

1. Everyone needed it.
2. The tax affected rich and poor.
3. It connected politics with daily life.
4. It exposed the unfairness of colonial control.
5. It allowed mass participation.

### Civil Disobedience Movement

After the Salt March, people across India broke laws, boycotted goods, refused taxes in some areas, and joined protests. Many were arrested.

## 11. Gandhi-Irwin Pact and Round Table Conference

In 1931 Gandhi and Viceroy Irwin reached an agreement. Gandhi agreed to suspend civil disobedience and attend the Second Round Table Conference in London.

The negotiations showed that Gandhi and the Congress had become central to discussions about India's future.

## 12. Quit India Movement

### Year

1942.

### Context

During the Second World War, the British involved India in the war without proper Indian consent. The Cripps Mission failed to satisfy nationalist demands.

### Demand

The central demand was British withdrawal from India.

### Features

1. Gandhi gave the call to do or die.
2. Congress leaders were arrested.
3. Protests spread across the country.
4. Many actions became underground or local because leadership was imprisoned.
5. The British suppressed the movement harshly.

## 13. Gandhi and Communal Harmony

Gandhi considered Hindu-Muslim unity essential to freedom. During the violence around Partition, he travelled to riot-torn areas and tried to restore peace.

He worked in:

1. Noakhali.
2. Bihar.
3. Calcutta.
4. Delhi.

His final days were spent trying to stop communal hatred and protect minorities.

## 14. Gandhi's Last Days

After independence and Partition, Gandhi was deeply distressed by communal violence. He fasted and appealed for peace. He was assassinated on 30 January 1948.

His death highlighted the intensity of communal tensions, but also the moral force of his life.

## 15. How Gandhi Made Nationalism a Mass Movement

| Method | Impact |
| --- | --- |
| Simple language and symbols | Ordinary people could understand the movement. |
| Khadi and charkha | Connected nationalism to daily practice. |
| Satyagraha | Gave disciplined method of protest. |
| Local issues | Linked national politics with peasant and worker grievances. |
| Religious idiom | Reached people emotionally, though it required careful handling. |
| Travel across India | Built direct contact with people. |
| Women participation | Expanded the social base of nationalism. |

## 16. Sources for Studying Gandhi

### 1. Writings and Speeches

Gandhi's own writings reveal his ideas, but they present his viewpoint.

### 2. Autobiography

Autobiographies provide personal detail but are selective and shaped by memory.

### 3. Newspapers

Newspapers reported Gandhi's activities and public responses. They may reflect editorial bias.

### 4. Government Reports

British officials prepared confidential reports on nationalist activity. These are useful for state response but may portray protesters as threats.

### 5. Letters

Letters show personal networks, negotiations, and private concerns.

### 6. Visual Sources

Photographs and posters show how Gandhi was represented and remembered.

## 17. Reading Sources Critically

Ask these questions:

1. Who created the source?
2. When was it created?
3. What was its purpose?
4. Whose viewpoint does it represent?
5. What does it reveal?
6. What does it ignore?
7. How does it compare with other sources?

## 18. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Treating Gandhi as the only nationalist leader | Gandhi was central, but many leaders, workers, peasants, women, and local activists shaped the movement. |
| Memorising events without links | Connect each movement to causes, methods, participation, and consequences. |
| Ignoring non-violence | Non-violence was not just a tactic; it was a moral principle for Gandhi. |
| Seeing Salt March as symbolic only | It was symbolic and practical because salt affected everyone. |
| Treating sources as neutral | Every source has perspective and limits. |
| Forgetting Gandhi's work after 1947 | His last struggle was against communal violence. |

## 19. Exam Answer Frames

### Gandhi's Role in Nationalism

Gandhi transformed Indian nationalism by making it a mass movement. He used satyagraha, non-cooperation, civil disobedience, khadi, and simple symbols to involve peasants, workers, women, students, and merchants. He connected national politics with local grievances and gave the movement a moral language based on truth and non-violence.

### Salt March

The Salt March was significant because salt was used by everyone and the salt tax exposed the injustice of colonial rule. By marching to Dandi and breaking the salt law, Gandhi turned a common necessity into a national symbol. The movement encouraged mass civil disobedience and showed the British that Indians were willing to defy unjust laws peacefully.

### Studying Gandhi Through Sources

Historians study Gandhi through writings, speeches, letters, newspapers, government reports, autobiographies, photographs, and oral memories. Each source is useful but limited. Gandhi's writings reveal his ideas, newspapers show public response, and government reports show colonial anxiety. Historians compare sources to build a fuller picture.

## 22. Final Learning Takeaway

Gandhi's importance lies not only in leading movements but in changing the meaning of politics. He brought nationalism into everyday life through salt, cloth, spinning, village work, and disciplined protest. He made freedom a moral and mass question, while his final struggle against communal hatred showed that independence without social peace was incomplete.
