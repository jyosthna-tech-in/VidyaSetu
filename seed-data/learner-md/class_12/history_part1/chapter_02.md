# Chapter 2: Kings, Farmers and Towns
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter examines early states, agriculture, towns, inscriptions, coins, and political changes from about 600 BCE to 600 CE. It focuses on how historians reconstruct the history of mahajanapadas, the Mauryan Empire, post-Mauryan polities, land grants, urban centres, and social-economic change.

The chapter's central skill is reading inscriptions and material evidence to understand power, economy, and society.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain the rise of mahajanapadas;
- describe features of Magadha's rise;
- understand Mauryan administration and Ashoka's dhamma;
- explain the importance of inscriptions;
- describe agriculture and land relations;
- understand towns, trade, and coinage;
- explain changes in political power after the Mauryas;
- use evidence from inscriptions, coins, and texts in answers.

# Unit 2A: Mahajanapadas

By about the sixth century BCE, several mahajanapadas emerged in the subcontinent. These were large political units with rulers, capitals, armies, and revenue systems.

Some were monarchies, while others were ganas or sanghas where power was shared among groups of elites.

Important mahajanapadas included Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa, Avanti, Vajji, Kuru, Panchala, and Gandhara.

# Unit 2B: Rise of Magadha

Magadha became powerful because of several advantages:

- fertile land in the middle Ganga valley;
- access to iron ore;
- elephants for warfare;
- rivers for transport and communication;
- strategic capitals such as Rajagaha and Pataliputra;
- ambitious rulers.

Magadha's rise shows how geography, resources, military power, and political strategy interact.

# Unit 2C: The Mauryan Empire

The Mauryan Empire was one of the earliest large empires in the subcontinent.

Important rulers:

- Chandragupta Maurya;
- Bindusara;
- Ashoka.

The empire covered large areas but control varied by region. Core areas were more directly administered, while frontier regions may have been controlled through local intermediaries.

# Unit 2D: Ashoka and Dhamma

Ashoka's inscriptions present his dhamma, an ethical policy meant to promote moral behaviour and social harmony.

Dhamma included:

- respect for elders;
- kindness to servants;
- non-violence in social conduct;
- respect for all religious traditions;
- welfare measures;
- moral self-control.

Ashoka used inscriptions to communicate with subjects across a large empire.

# Unit 2E: Inscriptions as Evidence

Inscriptions are valuable because they are often contemporary records.

They can reveal:

- names and titles of rulers;
- administrative orders;
- donations;
- religious patronage;
- land grants;
- political claims;
- languages and scripts;
- social groups.

Limitations:

- they often present official viewpoints;
- prashastis exaggerate praise;
- not all people are represented equally;
- some inscriptions are damaged.

# Unit 2F: Agriculture and Farmers

Agriculture expanded in many regions during this period.

Factors:

- use of iron tools;
- plough agriculture;
- wet-rice cultivation in some areas;
- forest clearing;
- settlement expansion;
- irrigation works in some regions.

Farmers paid taxes and produced surplus that supported rulers, towns, and religious institutions.

# Unit 2G: Land Grants

Land grants were gifts of land, often recorded on copper plates. They were given to Brahmanas, religious institutions, or officials.

They show:

- expansion of agriculture;
- claims over land and revenue;
- role of religious groups;
- changing rural power;
- rights transferred by rulers.

Land grants must be read carefully because they record ideal claims, not always actual conditions.

# Unit 2H: Towns and Urban Life

Towns grew due to trade, administration, crafts, and agriculture surplus.

Urban features:

- fortifications;
- markets;
- craft production;
- coins;
- traders;
- guilds;
- roads;
- religious centres.

Important early towns included Pataliputra, Ujjayini, Taxila, Mathura, and Varanasi.

# Unit 2I: Trade and Coins

Trade expanded within the subcontinent and beyond.

Coins helped exchange. Punch-marked coins were among early coin forms. Later rulers issued coins with names, images, and symbols.

Coins help historians study:

- political authority;
- economic exchange;
- metal use;
- trade networks;
- iconography;
- chronology.

# Unit 2J: Post-Mauryan Polities

After the Mauryas, many regional powers emerged.

Examples:

- Shungas;
- Satavahanas;
- Kushanas;
- Shakas;
- Indo-Greeks;
- local chiefdoms.

This period saw trade networks, religious patronage, inscriptions, and coinage expand in new ways.

# Unit 2K: Prashastis and Political Claims

Prashastis praise rulers and present ideal images of power. They may describe conquest, generosity, learning, bravery, and divine favour.

Historians use them carefully:

- identify claims made by rulers;
- compare with other evidence;
- separate praise from factual information;
- study political culture.

# Unit 2L: Answer-Writing Framework

For Magadha's rise:

1. Mention fertile land.
2. Add iron ore and elephants.
3. Explain river routes and strategic capitals.
4. Add strong rulers.
5. Conclude with combined geographical and political advantages.

For inscriptions:

1. Define inscription.
2. Explain what it reveals.
3. Give examples such as Ashokan inscriptions or land grants.
4. Mention limitations.
5. Conclude with evidence-based value.

# Unit 2M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating all mahajanapadas as monarchies | Some were ganas or sanghas. |
| Explaining Magadha only by rulers | Geography and resources mattered. |
| Reading prashastis literally | They are praise texts and need comparison. |
| Ignoring farmers | Agrarian surplus supported states and towns. |
| Treating land grants as simple charity | They also involved rights, revenue, and power. |
