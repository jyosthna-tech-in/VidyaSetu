# Chapter 1: Bricks, Beads and Bones
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies the Harappan civilisation through archaeological evidence. It explains how historians reconstruct ancient urban life from material remains such as bricks, seals, beads, pottery, bones, weights, drains, houses, granaries, and burial sites.

The main skill in this chapter is evidence-based thinking. Since Harappan script has not been fully deciphered, historians depend heavily on material culture and settlement patterns.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- locate the Harappan civilisation in time and space;
- explain features of Harappan urban planning;
- describe craft production, trade, agriculture, and subsistence;
- understand seals, weights, beads, and pottery as historical evidence;
- explain burial practices and social differences;
- describe the decline and transformation of Harappan cities;
- understand how archaeologists interpret material remains;
- write answers using evidence rather than general claims.

# Unit 1A: Time and Spread

The Harappan civilisation developed in the northwestern part of the Indian subcontinent. Important sites include Harappa, Mohenjodaro, Dholavira, Kalibangan, Lothal, Rakhigarhi, Banawali, and Chanhudaro.

Broad phases:

- Early Harappan: village cultures and regional development;
- Mature Harappan: urban civilisation with planned cities;
- Late Harappan: decline of urban features and regional transformation.

The Mature Harappan phase is known for cities, craft specialisation, trade, standardisation, and drainage systems.

# Unit 1B: Urban Planning

Harappan cities show careful planning.

Features:

- grid-like street patterns in many sites;
- baked brick construction;
- standardised brick ratios;
- planned drainage;
- wells;
- bathing areas;
- division into citadel and lower town in some cities;
- public and domestic architecture.

Urban planning suggests coordination, technical skill, and organised labour.

# Unit 1C: Drainage and Sanitation

Mohenjodaro is famous for its drainage system. Many houses were connected to street drains. Drains were covered and had inspection holes.

This shows:

- concern for cleanliness;
- technical planning;
- civic organisation;
- integration of private houses with public infrastructure.

Drainage is one of the strongest signs of urban sophistication.

# Unit 1D: Subsistence

Harappans used diverse food resources.

Evidence from seeds and bones suggests:

- wheat;
- barley;
- pulses;
- sesame;
- dates;
- cattle;
- sheep;
- goats;
- buffalo;
- fish in some regions.

Agriculture depended on local ecology. Floodplains, rainfall, and irrigation possibilities varied by region.

# Unit 1E: Craft Production

Harappans produced many specialised goods.

Crafts included:

- bead-making;
- shell-working;
- pottery;
- metalworking;
- stone tools;
- seal carving;
- faience objects.

Chanhudaro is often associated with craft production. Lothal is linked with bead-making and trade.

## Beads

Beads were made from materials such as carnelian, lapis lazuli, shell, steatite, and terracotta. Their production required skill, drilling, polishing, heating, and shaping.

Beads show both technology and trade links.

# Unit 1F: Trade and Exchange

Harappans traded within the subcontinent and with regions beyond.

Evidence includes:

- seals;
- weights;
- raw materials from distant regions;
- Mesopotamian references to Meluhha;
- similar artefacts found across sites;
- dock-like structure at Lothal debated by archaeologists.

Standardised weights suggest regulated exchange.

# Unit 1G: Seals and Script

Harappan seals often contain animal motifs and short inscriptions. The script remains undeciphered.

Seals may have been used for:

- trade;
- identification;
- property marking;
- administrative control;
- ritual or symbolic purposes.

Because the script cannot be read securely, interpretations remain cautious.

# Unit 1H: Social Differences

Archaeologists study social differences through:

- house size;
- burial goods;
- craft specialisation;
- access to luxury items;
- settlement layout;
- public architecture.

Harappan society shows differences, but evidence for kings or palaces is not clear. Unlike Egypt or Mesopotamia, obvious royal tombs or grand statues are absent.

# Unit 1I: Burial Practices

Burials provide clues about beliefs and social practices.

Some graves contain pottery and ornaments, while others are simpler. This may indicate social difference, ritual variation, or personal identity.

However, burials must be interpreted carefully. Grave goods do not automatically prove wealth or rank.

# Unit 1J: Religion and Symbols

Harappan religious life is difficult to reconstruct.

Possible clues:

- terracotta figurines;
- seals with animal motifs;
- fire altars at some sites;
- sacred trees or animals in imagery;
- Great Bath at Mohenjodaro, possibly ritual in function.

Interpretations are debated because written texts are unavailable.

# Unit 1K: Decline and Transformation

After about 1900 BCE, many urban features declined.

Changes include:

- decline of long-distance trade;
- fewer seals and weights;
- reduced urban planning;
- population movement;
- regional cultures becoming more visible;
- changes in craft production.

Possible causes:

- climate change;
- river shifts;
- reduced trade;
- ecological stress;
- overuse of resources;
- gradual regional transformation.

The decline was not a sudden disappearance of people. It was a transformation of urban civilisation into later regional cultures.

# Unit 1L: How Archaeologists Work

Archaeologists use:

- excavation;
- stratigraphy;
- pottery classification;
- radiocarbon dating;
- artefact analysis;
- botanical and faunal remains;
- settlement surveys;
- comparison across sites.

They build interpretations from patterns, not isolated objects.

# Unit 1M: Answer-Writing Framework

For urban planning:

1. Mention planned streets and layout.
2. Explain citadel and lower town where relevant.
3. Add drainage, wells, and brick standardisation.
4. Use Mohenjodaro or Dholavira as example.
5. Conclude with civic organisation.

For evidence:

1. Name the material evidence.
2. Explain what it reveals.
3. Mention limits of interpretation.
4. Avoid unsupported claims.

# Unit 1N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying Harappan decline was sudden | It was gradual and regionally varied. |
| Claiming script is fully read | It remains undeciphered. |
| Assuming every large building is a palace | Interpretations need evidence. |
| Ignoring small artefacts | Beads, seals, weights, and bones are crucial. |
| Treating Harappan society as completely equal | Differences existed, though kingship evidence is unclear. |
