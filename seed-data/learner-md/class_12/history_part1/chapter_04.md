## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies religious and philosophical developments from about 600 BCE onward, including Buddhism, Jainism, other traditions, stupas, sculpture, and sacred architecture. It connects ideas with material remains such as stupas, inscriptions, railings, gateways, and images.

The chapter shows that beliefs are not only found in texts. They are also expressed in buildings, rituals, patronage, pilgrimage, and visual art.

# Unit 4A: Religious Context

From about the sixth century BCE, many thinkers questioned ritual, sacrifice, social hierarchy, and the meaning of life. Cities, trade, kingdoms, and social change created new spaces for debate.

Groups such as Buddhists, Jainas, Ajivikas, and others developed teachings about suffering, karma, rebirth, discipline, and liberation.

# Unit 4B: Buddhism

Buddhism began with Siddhartha Gautama, later known as the Buddha. He renounced royal life, searched for truth, attained enlightenment, and taught a path to end suffering.

Core ideas:

- life involves suffering;
- suffering has causes;
- suffering can end;
- there is a path leading to its end.

The path emphasised ethical conduct, meditation, and wisdom.

# Unit 4C: The Sangha

The sangha was the community of monks and later nuns who followed Buddhist discipline.

Importance:

- preserved teachings;
- organised monastic life;
- spread Buddhism;
- received donations;
- connected lay supporters with religious institutions.

The sangha depended on lay patronage for food, robes, monasteries, and stupas.

# Unit 4D: Jainism

Jainism is associated with a line of tirthankaras, with Mahavira as the twenty-fourth tirthankara.

Core ideas:

- non-violence;
- truth;
- non-stealing;
- celibacy;
- non-possession;
- karma and liberation;
- strict discipline.

Jainism placed strong emphasis on ahimsa and self-control.

# Unit 4E: Popular Religious Practices

Alongside formal teachings, many local and popular practices continued.

These included worship of:

- trees;
- serpents;
- yakshas and yakshis;
- fertility symbols;
- local deities.

Religious traditions often interacted with local beliefs.

# Unit 4F: Stupas

Stupas were sacred Buddhist structures. They often contained relics or symbolised the Buddha's presence.

Main parts:

- anda: dome;
- harmika: square railing on top;
- chhatri: umbrella-like structure;
- railing: enclosure;
- gateways: decorated entrances;
- circumambulatory path: path for ritual walking.

Stupas were places of worship, memory, and community gathering.

# Unit 4G: Sanchi

Sanchi is one of the most important Buddhist sites. Its Great Stupa shows long-term patronage, artistic work, and religious devotion.

Important features:

- large dome;
- carved gateways;
- railings;
- narrative reliefs;
- inscriptions naming donors;
- symbols representing the Buddha.

Sanchi helps historians understand Buddhism as lived religion, not only doctrine.

# Unit 4H: Symbols of the Buddha

Early Buddhist art often represented the Buddha through symbols rather than human form.

Common symbols:

- empty throne;
- footprints;
- wheel;
- Bodhi tree;
- stupa.

These symbols represented events and qualities associated with the Buddha.

# Unit 4I: Sculpture and Narratives

Stupa gateways and railings include carvings of stories, animals, trees, worshippers, and symbolic scenes.

These carvings reveal:

- religious stories;
- artistic styles;
- patronage;
- popular beliefs;
- social life;
- clothing and ornaments;
- interaction of local and Buddhist symbols.

# Unit 4J: Patronage

Religious buildings were supported by many donors.

Donors included:

- rulers;
- merchants;
- guilds;
- monks;
- nuns;
- ordinary men and women.

Inscriptions show that religious art was not produced only by kings. Many social groups participated.

# Unit 4K: Buildings as Historical Evidence

Buildings and monuments reveal:

- technology;
- labour organisation;
- religious practice;
- artistic style;
- patronage networks;
- pilgrimage;
- political support;
- regional connections.

They must be studied with inscriptions, texts, and archaeological context.

# Unit 4L: Religious Change Over Time

Religious traditions changed over time. Buddhist art moved from symbolic representation to anthropomorphic images of the Buddha. New devotional forms emerged. Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain traditions interacted with local practices.

This shows that religions are historical, dynamic, and socially embedded.

# Unit 4N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating religion only as belief | Buildings, rituals, donations, and art also matter. |
| Saying only kings built stupas | Many donors supported them. |
| Ignoring inscriptions | Donor records are crucial evidence. |
| Assuming Buddha was always shown in human form | Early art often used symbols. |
| Treating traditions as isolated | Buddhism, Jainism, and local practices interacted. |
