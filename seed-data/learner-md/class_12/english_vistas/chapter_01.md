# Chapter 1: The Third Level

## 1. Big Picture

The story is about Charley, an ordinary young man who claims to have found a third level at Grand Central Station, even though the station officially has only two levels. This third level seems to lead into the year 1894, a period Charley imagines as peaceful, simple, and secure.

The story deliberately keeps the reader uncertain. Was the third level real? Was it a psychological escape? Was it fantasy? The answer is not fixed. The value of the story lies in the tension between reality and imagination.

## 2. Plot Summary

Charley lives in the modern world, which is full of hurry, insecurity, war memories, and stress. One day, while trying to take the subway from Grand Central Station, he gets lost in a strange corridor and reaches what he believes is a third level of the station.

Everything there looks old-fashioned: clothing, lighting, ticket counters, newspapers, and currency. Charley realises that he has reached the year 1894. He wants to buy tickets to Galesburg, Illinois, a town he associates with peace and an ideal past. However, his modern money is not accepted, so he leaves.

Later, he buys old currency and tries to find the third level again, but fails. His wife Louisa worries about him. Charley's psychiatrist friend, Sam, first interprets the third level as a waking-dream wish fulfilment. But later Sam disappears, and Charley finds a first-day cover with a letter from Sam written from 1894. The letter says Sam has found the third level and invites Charley and Louisa to keep looking for it.

The ending blurs fantasy and reality. The psychiatrist who explained the third level as escapism seems to have escaped through it himself.

## 4. Charley as Narrator

Charley presents himself as ordinary and reasonable. This is important because he wants the reader to believe his impossible experience.

Yet he may also be unreliable because:

1. He is emotionally drawn to the past.
2. He wants escape.
3. His experience is impossible by normal logic.
4. His description mixes real details with fantasy.

The story works because Charley sounds sincere even when his claim is unbelievable.

## 5. Meaning of the Third Level

The third level can be understood in three ways.

| Interpretation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Literal fantasy | It may be a real time-travel passage to 1894. |
| Psychological escape | It may be Charley's wish fulfilment caused by modern stress. |
| Symbol | It represents the human desire to escape from insecurity into a simpler world. |

For exam answers, the safest interpretation is: the third level is both a fantasy device and a symbol of escapism.

## 6. Theme of Escapism

Escapism means mentally or physically avoiding unpleasant reality by moving into imagination, nostalgia, hobbies, or fantasy.

Charley wants to escape:

1. Modern insecurity.
2. Fear and worry.
3. War-haunted civilisation.
4. Urban confusion.
5. Pressure of everyday life.

His stamp collection also functions as a refuge. It connects him to an older world and gives him comfort.

## 7. The Past as an Ideal World

Charley imagines 1894 as:

1. Peaceful.
2. Slower.
3. Friendly.
4. Less complicated.
5. Free from modern wars.

Galesburg becomes a symbol of this ideal past. It is not just a town; it is Charley's dream of security.

Important exam point: the past in the story is romanticised. The story shows Charley's longing, not necessarily historical truth.

## 8. Fantasy and Reality

The story mixes realistic details with impossible events.

| Realistic Elements | Fantasy Elements |
| --- | --- |
| Grand Central Station | A hidden third level leading to 1894 |
| Subway confusion | Time travel |
| Stamp collecting | A first-day cover from the past |
| Psychiatrist's explanation | Psychiatrist disappearing into the past |
| Old currency dealers | Sam's letter from 1894 |

This mixture makes the story interesting because readers cannot fully dismiss or fully prove Charley's experience.

## 9. Role of Sam

Sam is important because he first gives a rational explanation: Charley is using fantasy to escape. However, Sam later disappears and apparently sends a letter from the past.

This reversal creates irony:

1. The psychiatrist becomes the escapist.
2. The rational explainer enters the fantasy.
3. Charley's story becomes more believable.

Sam's letter is the strongest evidence within the story that the third level may be real.

## 11. Important Questions and Answer Guides

### Was the third level a medium of escape for Charley?

Yes. The third level represents Charley's desire to escape the insecurity and stress of the modern world. His psychiatrist calls it wish fulfilment, and Charley's attraction to 1894 shows his longing for a peaceful past. Even his stamp collection suggests his need for refuge.

### What do we infer from Sam's letter?

Sam's letter suggests that the third level may actually exist within the story's fantasy world. It also shows that Sam, despite being a psychiatrist, shares the same desire for escape. His move to Galesburg proves that modern anxiety affects even those who claim to understand it rationally.

### Is there an intersection of time and space in the story?

Yes. Grand Central Station becomes a point where present space and past time appear to meet. The third level connects modern New York with 1894, creating an intersection of time and space.

### Why does Charley want to go to Galesburg?

Galesburg represents peace, simplicity, community, and freedom from modern anxieties. Charley imagines it as a place where life is slower and safer.

## 12. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |

| Calling stamp collecting useless detail | It supports the theme of escape into the past. |

## 16. Final Learning Takeaway

The story is not asking us only whether the third level is real. It asks why people want such a third level to exist. Charley's fantasy reveals a universal desire to escape fear, speed, and uncertainty. The ending makes the fantasy tempting by suggesting that even the rational psychiatrist chooses the imagined peace of the past.
