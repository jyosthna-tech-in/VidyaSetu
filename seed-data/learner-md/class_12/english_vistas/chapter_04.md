## 1. Big Picture

"The Enemy" is set during wartime. Dr Sadao Hoki, a Japanese surgeon, discovers a wounded American prisoner of war near his home. As a Japanese citizen, he is expected to treat the man as an enemy. As a doctor, he is bound to save life. This conflict between patriotism and medical duty drives the story.

The story is not simply about saving an enemy soldier. It is about the difficult choices human beings face when social identity, national loyalty, fear, and conscience collide.

## 2. Plot Summary

Dr Sadao Hoki is a skilled Japanese surgeon who has studied in America. He lives on the Japanese coast with his wife Hana. One day, they find a badly wounded American prisoner of war washed ashore near their house.

Sadao and Hana know that helping the man is dangerous. If they shelter an enemy, they may be punished. If they hand him over, he may die. Sadao's medical training and conscience do not allow him to abandon a wounded man, so he takes him inside and operates on him.

The servants are horrified that an enemy is being kept in the house. They leave because they believe Sadao and Hana are betraying their country. Hana, despite fear and disgust, helps Sadao because she understands his duty as a doctor.

Sadao later tells the old General about the prisoner. The General, who depends on Sadao's medical skill, promises to send assassins to kill the prisoner quietly. The assassins never come because the General forgets, being absorbed in his own illness and safety.

Unable to bear the danger any longer, Sadao helps the American escape by giving him a boat, food, clothing, and instructions. Later, when the General realises the prisoner escaped, he protects himself by claiming that he forgot only because of illness. Sadao remains safe. At the end, Sadao wonders why he could not kill the man, revealing the story's central moral question.

## 4. Dr Sadao's Character

Sadao is complex because he is not shown as sentimentally loving the enemy. He has absorbed national prejudice and feels the American is his enemy. Yet his professional ethics are stronger than hatred.

### Key Traits

1. Highly skilled surgeon.
2. Disciplined and controlled.
3. Patriotic but morally independent.
4. Influenced by Japanese tradition.
5. Trained in Western medicine.
6. Capable of compassion under pressure.
7. Courageous enough to act according to conscience.

### His Central Conflict

```text
Duty as Japanese citizen
vs
Duty as doctor and human being
```

He resolves the conflict by saving the man first and later arranging his escape.

## 5. Hana's Role

Hana is frightened by the risk of sheltering an enemy, but she does not abandon Sadao. Her courage is quieter than Sadao's but equally important.

She:

1. Helps carry the wounded man.
2. Assists during the operation.
3. Handles household pressure after servants leave.
4. Feels fear but does not become cruel.
5. Understands that Sadao's duty as a doctor is unavoidable.

Hana's sympathy grows from seeing the prisoner as helpless rather than as an abstract enemy.

## 6. The American Prisoner

The prisoner is young, wounded, weak, and dependent on Sadao's help. His role is mainly to test the humanity of others.

He is called an enemy by nationality, but his suffering makes him human before he is political.

Important point: the story makes readers ask whether a helpless wounded person can remain only an "enemy".

## 7. The Servants

The servants strongly object to keeping the American in the house. They represent:

1. Fear of punishment.
2. Social prejudice.
3. Wartime hatred.
4. Conventional patriotism.
5. Suspicion of foreignness.

Their reaction increases the pressure on Sadao and Hana. It also shows that moral courage can isolate people from their own community.

## 8. The General

The General is powerful but morally weak. He does not act from human kindness. He protects Sadao because he needs him as a surgeon.

### What His Behaviour Shows

| Action/Attitude | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Promises to kill the prisoner secretly | Wants convenience, not justice. |
| Forgets the promise | Self-absorbed and careless. |
| Depends on Sadao | Values people according to usefulness. |
| Protects himself at the end | More concerned with reputation than duty. |

The General's behaviour contrasts with Sadao's conscience.

## 9. Central Themes

### Humanity Beyond National Boundaries

The story shows that war divides people into "us" and "enemy", but suffering reveals a deeper human connection.

### Professional Ethics

Sadao saves the prisoner because a doctor's duty is to preserve life. His medical ethics override political hostility.

### Moral Courage

True courage is not only fighting an enemy. It is doing what is right when society, state, and fear push in another direction.

### Prejudice and War

War encourages people to reduce others to labels. The story challenges this by presenting the enemy as a wounded human being.

### Self-Interest of Power

The General's actions show how powerful people often protect themselves first, even while speaking of patriotism.

## 10. Title Meaning

The title "The Enemy" is deliberately ironic.

On the surface, the enemy is the American prisoner. But the story makes us question who or what the real enemy is.

Possible meanings:

1. The American soldier as wartime enemy.
2. Hatred as the enemy of humanity.
3. Prejudice as the enemy of moral judgement.
4. Fear as the enemy of conscience.
5. War itself as the enemy of human values.

## 11. Important Conflicts

| Conflict | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Doctor vs patriot | Sadao must choose between saving life and obeying wartime loyalty. |
| Humanity vs prejudice | The prisoner is an enemy, but also a suffering human. |
| Individual conscience vs society | Sadao and Hana act differently from servants' expectations. |
| Duty vs safety | Helping the prisoner endangers the household. |

## 12. Why Sadao Saves the Prisoner

Sadao saves him because:

1. The man is badly wounded and helpless.
2. Sadao is a trained doctor.
3. His hands act almost automatically according to medical duty.
4. He cannot let a man die when he has the skill to save him.
5. His humanity is stronger than wartime hatred.

This does not mean Sadao becomes politically sympathetic to the enemy. It means he refuses to abandon medical ethics.

## 13. Why Hana Becomes Sympathetic

Hana is afraid, but she sees the prisoner's suffering directly. She also sees Sadao's struggle and understands that he cannot behave like an ordinary citizen when a life is in danger.

Her sympathy comes from:

1. Human pity.
2. Shared responsibility with Sadao.
3. Seeing the prisoner as wounded and dependent.
4. Moral strength under domestic and social pressure.

## 14. Why the Prisoner Is Reluctant to Leave

The prisoner knows he is unsafe, but leaving is frightening because:

1. He is still physically weak.
2. He has nowhere secure to go.
3. He depends on Sadao's medical care.
4. Outside the house he may be captured or killed.
5. The house, though dangerous, has become temporary shelter.

His reluctance is realistic, not selfish.

## 15. The Ending

Sadao helps the prisoner escape by arranging a boat and supplies. Later he sees no signal from the island, suggesting the prisoner has escaped safely.

The final question in Sadao's mind is important: he wonders why he could not kill the man. This shows that even after all his training, nationalism, and prejudice, he cannot deny the human value of the life he saved.

## 16. Important Questions and Answer Guides

### What made Sadao help the enemy soldier?

Sadao helped the enemy soldier because he was a doctor before he was an agent of war. The man was wounded and helpless, and Sadao's medical ethics would not allow him to let a patient die. His decision shows that humanity can rise above national hatred.

### What made Hana sympathetic?

Hana's sympathy came from direct contact with suffering and from her understanding of Sadao's duty. Although she feared danger and social disgrace, she helped because the wounded man was helpless and because her husband could not abandon his professional responsibility.

### What explains the General's attitude?

The General's attitude is mainly self-absorbed. He does not protect Sadao out of moral concern; he needs Sadao for his own medical treatment. His failure to send assassins and his later excuse show selfishness, not true patriotism or human compassion.

### How does the story rise above narrow prejudice?

The story rises above prejudice by showing that human suffering is more important than labels of nationality, race, or enemy status. Sadao and Hana are afraid and prejudiced, but they still save a life. Their action proves that compassion can survive even in wartime.

## 22. Final Learning Takeaway

"The Enemy" shows that the hardest moral choices often appear when labels tell us one thing and conscience tells us another. Sadao is expected to treat the American as an enemy, but his training and humanity force him to treat him as a patient. The story's deepest message is that compassion is not weakness; it is the courage to remain human when hatred is easier.
