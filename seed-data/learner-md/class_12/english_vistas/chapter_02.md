## 1. Big Picture

"The Tiger King" is a satire about the Maharaja of Pratibandapuram, whose life becomes dominated by a prophecy that he will be killed by a tiger. Instead of accepting fate with humility, he tries to defeat the prophecy by killing tigers. His obsession turns into cruelty, vanity, administrative absurdity, and ecological destruction.

The story is humorous on the surface, but its criticism is serious. It attacks:

1. Pride of rulers.
2. Blind belief in prophecy.
3. Misuse of political power.
4. Fear-driven obedience of officials.
5. Hunting and destruction of wildlife.
6. The illusion that humans can fully control fate.

## 2. Plot Summary

At the Maharaja's birth, astrologers predict that he will grow into a powerful ruler but will die because of a tiger. The child is connected with tigers from the beginning, and later he becomes determined to kill one hundred tigers to prove the prophecy wrong.

As king, he bans tiger hunting by others and reserves the right to kill all tigers in his kingdom. When tigers become scarce, he marries into a royal family whose territory has many tigers so that he can continue hunting. His ministers and officials obey him out of fear.

By the time he reaches ninety-nine tigers, the hundredth tiger becomes a crisis. A tiger is arranged for him, and the Maharaja believes he has killed it. In reality, the tiger survives the shot and is killed secretly by officials to avoid angering the king.

Later, the Maharaja buys a wooden toy tiger for his son. A tiny sliver from the toy injures him, leading to infection and death. Ironically, the king who tried to defeat the prophecy by killing real tigers dies because of a wooden tiger.

## 4. The Maharaja's Character

The Maharaja is:

1. Brave in a narrow sense.
2. Proud and self-important.
3. Obsessed with control.
4. Cruel toward animals.
5. Unreasonable in decision-making.
6. Surrounded by fearful subordinates.
7. Ultimately powerless before fate.

He appears powerful, but the story shows that power without wisdom becomes ridiculous.

## 5. Satire in the Story

Satire uses humour, exaggeration, irony, and absurdity to criticise human flaws or social systems.

In this story, satire targets kingship and absolute power.

| Satirical Target | How the Story Criticises It |
| --- | --- |
| Royal pride | The king believes he can defeat fate by killing tigers. |
| Blind authority | Officials obey irrational orders. |
| Colonial politics | A British officer's wish for a hunting photograph becomes a political issue. |
| Superstition | A prophecy controls the ruler's entire life. |
| Hunting culture | Tigers are killed for ego, not necessity. |
| Administrative fear | Ministers act to protect themselves, not truth. |

## 6. Dramatic Irony

Dramatic irony occurs when the reader knows something that a character does not.

In the story:

1. The Maharaja thinks he has killed the hundredth tiger.
2. The reader learns that the tiger did not die from his shot.
3. Officials secretly kill the tiger to protect themselves.
4. The Maharaja believes he has defeated the prophecy.
5. The reader sees that his confidence is false.

The final irony is stronger: the king dies from a wooden tiger, not a real one.

## 7. Theme of Fate

The Maharaja tries to control fate through violence. But the prophecy is fulfilled in an unexpected way. The story suggests that human arrogance cannot completely master destiny.

Important point: the story does not ask us to blindly believe in prophecy. It shows the foolishness of living under fear and pride.

## 8. Ecological Message

The story criticises the killing of animals for sport, ego, and prestige.

The Maharaja does not hunt tigers because they threaten people. He hunts them because of personal fear and pride. This makes the story relevant to ecological thinking:

1. Wildlife should not be sacrificed to human vanity.
2. Power should protect nature, not destroy it.
3. Hunting for prestige reflects moral emptiness.
4. Human arrogance damages the natural world.

## 9. Role of the Dewan and Officials

The Dewan and officials represent people who serve power without moral courage.

They are not loyal in a noble sense. They are afraid. Their actions show how authoritarian systems produce false obedience, flattery, and dishonesty.

The handling of the hundredth tiger shows this clearly. Instead of telling the truth, officials hide it because truth is dangerous under a vain ruler.

## 10. Humour and Absurdity

The story's humour comes from exaggerated situations:

1. A baby connected to prophecy.
2. A king turning his life into a tiger-counting mission.
3. Marriage being used as a hunting strategy.
4. Officials staging the hundredth tiger.
5. A wooden toy tiger fulfilling the prophecy.

This humour is not meaningless. It exposes the absurdity of power when guided by fear and ego.

## 11. Important Questions and Answer Guides

### How is the story a satire on those in power?

The story satirises rulers who misuse authority for personal obsession. The Maharaja uses state power to hunt tigers, control officials, and protect his ego. His ministers obey him out of fear, even when his actions are irrational. Through exaggeration and irony, the story criticises arrogant authority.

### What is the role of dramatic irony?

Dramatic irony appears when the Maharaja believes he has killed the hundredth tiger, while the reader knows he has not. This makes his confidence look foolish. The final death caused by a wooden tiger deepens the irony because he is destroyed by the very symbol he tried to conquer.

### What is the author's comment on killing innocent animals?

The author indirectly criticises hunting as cruel and selfish. The Maharaja kills tigers not for public safety but for personal fear and pride. The story shows that animals become victims of human vanity and power.

### Were the Maharaja's servants sincere?

The servants and officials are driven more by fear than sincerity. They obey the king because disobedience may cost them their positions or safety. Their behaviour reflects the weakness of people working under absolute power.

## 17. Final Learning Takeaway

"The Tiger King" uses comedy to expose serious problems. The Maharaja's obsession with killing tigers makes him look powerful, but actually reveals fear, vanity, and foolishness. The ending shows that fate cannot be controlled by violence, and nature should not be destroyed for human ego.
