# Chapter 8: International Trade
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography India

## Chapter at a Glance

International trade connects India with the global economy. India exports and imports goods and services based on resource availability, technology, labour skills, demand, and global market conditions. Trade patterns have changed over time from primary goods toward manufactured goods and services, especially software and business services.

This chapter explains India's foreign trade, composition of exports and imports, direction of trade, ports, and the role of trade in development.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define international trade;
- explain importance of trade for India;
- distinguish export and import;
- understand changes in composition of trade;
- identify major export and import categories;
- explain direction of India's trade;
- describe the role of sea ports;
- understand trade balance and trade deficit;
- connect trade with globalisation and economic development.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| International trade | Exchange of goods and services between countries. |
| Export | Goods or services sold to other countries. |
| Import | Goods or services purchased from other countries. |
| Balance of trade | Value of exports of goods minus imports of goods. |
| Trade deficit | Imports exceed exports. |
| Trade surplus | Exports exceed imports. |
| Direction of trade | Countries or regions with which a country trades. |
| Composition of trade | Types of goods and services traded. |
| Port | Gateway handling overseas trade. |

# Unit 8A: Importance of International Trade

International trade helps India:

- earn foreign exchange;
- import essential raw materials and technology;
- export surplus goods and services;
- expand markets;
- support industries;
- create employment;
- strengthen global relations;
- improve consumer choice.

Trade also exposes domestic producers to global competition.

# Unit 8B: Composition of Exports

India's export basket has diversified over time.

Major categories:

- engineering goods;
- petroleum products;
- gems and jewellery;
- textiles and garments;
- chemicals;
- pharmaceuticals;
- agricultural products;
- marine products;
- software and business services.

Services exports, especially information technology, are a major strength.

# Unit 8C: Composition of Imports

Major imports include:

- crude petroleum and petroleum products;
- machinery;
- electronic goods;
- gold and precious stones;
- chemicals;
- fertilisers;
- edible oils;
- coal;
- defence equipment.

High petroleum import bill is a major reason for trade deficit.

# Unit 8D: Direction of Trade

Direction of trade means the countries or regions with which India trades.

India trades with:

- United States;
- European Union countries;
- China;
- United Arab Emirates;
- Saudi Arabia;
- Southeast Asian countries;
- African countries;
- neighbouring countries.

Direction changes over time due to policy, prices, demand, geopolitics, and trade agreements.

# Unit 8E: Balance of Trade

Balance of trade is:

`Balance of trade = exports of goods - imports of goods`

If imports exceed exports, there is trade deficit.

India often has a merchandise trade deficit because imports of petroleum, gold, electronics, and machinery are high.

Services exports help reduce overall external imbalance.

# Unit 8F: Trade Liberalisation

Trade liberalisation means reducing restrictions on imports and exports.

Effects:

- greater competition;
- wider choice;
- export opportunities;
- integration with global markets;
- pressure on inefficient producers;
- need for quality improvement.

Trade liberalisation must be supported by infrastructure, skills, technology, and fair policy.

# Unit 8G: Sea Ports

Sea ports handle most of India's overseas merchandise trade.

Functions:

- loading and unloading cargo;
- container handling;
- storage;
- customs clearance;
- ship services;
- link with hinterland;
- support for export-import industries.

Ports are connected to hinterland through roads, railways, pipelines, and inland container depots.

# Unit 8H: Major Port Functions

Different ports specialise in different cargo.

Cargo types:

- petroleum;
- coal;
- iron ore;
- containers;
- fertilisers;
- food grains;
- chemicals;
- automobiles.

Efficient ports reduce trade cost and improve competitiveness.

# Unit 8I: Inland Connectivity and Trade

Ports are useful only when connected to production and consumption centres.

Important support systems:

- highways;
- rail corridors;
- warehouses;
- cold chains;
- logistics parks;
- customs systems;
- digital documentation.

Weak logistics can reduce export competitiveness even when production is strong.

# Unit 8J: Trade and Development

Trade supports development by:

- creating jobs;
- encouraging specialisation;
- bringing technology;
- earning foreign exchange;
- supporting manufacturing and services;
- improving infrastructure.

But trade can also create:

- dependence on imports;
- vulnerability to global price shocks;
- pressure on small producers;
- regional concentration of benefits.

# Unit 8L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Confusing direction and composition | Direction is trade partners; composition is traded items. |
| Saying imports are always harmful | Imports may provide fuel, technology, and essential inputs. |
| Ignoring services | India's services exports are very important. |
| Treating ports as isolated | Ports depend on hinterland connectivity. |
| Confusing trade deficit and fiscal deficit | Trade deficit is external goods trade gap. |
