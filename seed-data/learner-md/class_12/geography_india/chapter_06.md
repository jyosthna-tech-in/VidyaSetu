# Chapter 6: Planning and Sustainable Development in Indian Context
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography India

## Chapter at a Glance

Planning means making deliberate choices to use resources and guide development. In India, planning has been used to reduce regional inequality, improve livelihoods, and develop backward areas. Sustainable development adds another requirement: development should meet present needs without damaging the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

This chapter explains planning, target area planning, target group planning, hill area development, drought-prone area development, and sustainable development through examples.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define planning;
- distinguish sectoral and regional planning;
- explain target area and target group approaches;
- understand why regional imbalance occurs;
- describe hill area development;
- describe drought-prone area development;
- explain sustainable development;
- connect development with ecology and social justice;
- write India-specific planning answers.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Planning | Deliberate process of resource use and development decision-making. |
| Sectoral planning | Planning focused on one sector, such as agriculture or power. |
| Regional planning | Planning focused on development of a region. |
| Target area planning | Planning focused on specific backward or problem regions. |
| Target group planning | Planning focused on specific disadvantaged groups. |
| Sustainable development | Development that meets present needs without harming future generations. |
| Regional imbalance | Uneven development across regions. |
| Watershed development | Planning based on drainage unit to conserve soil and water. |

# Unit 6A: Meaning of Planning

Planning is a process of deciding:

- what development is needed;
- where resources should be used;
- which groups need support;
- how goals will be achieved;
- how benefits will be distributed.

Planning is important because resources are limited and development is uneven.

# Unit 6B: Sectoral Planning

Sectoral planning focuses on one sector of the economy.

Examples:

- agriculture planning;
- industrial planning;
- transport planning;
- power planning;
- education planning;
- health planning.

It improves performance of a sector but may ignore regional differences if used alone.

# Unit 6C: Regional Planning

Regional planning focuses on a specific region and tries to integrate different sectors.

It considers:

- local resources;
- population;
- environment;
- infrastructure;
- culture;
- employment;
- regional problems.

Regional planning is useful for backward, hilly, tribal, drought-prone, and border regions.

# Unit 6D: Target Area Planning

Target area planning focuses on areas with specific problems.

Examples:

- hill area development;
- drought-prone area programme;
- desert development;
- command area development;
- tribal area development.

This approach recognises that the same policy cannot solve all regional problems.

# Unit 6E: Target Group Planning

Target group planning focuses on disadvantaged groups.

Examples:

- small farmers;
- landless labourers;
- scheduled castes;
- scheduled tribes;
- women;
- rural artisans;
- urban poor.

It aims to ensure that development benefits reach people who are often excluded.

# Unit 6F: Hill Area Development

Hill areas face special challenges:

- steep slopes;
- soil erosion;
- landslides;
- limited flat land;
- poor transport;
- fragile ecology;
- scattered settlements;
- limited industry.

Development strategies:

- horticulture;
- terrace farming;
- afforestation;
- eco-tourism;
- small hydel power;
- road connectivity with slope care;
- local crafts;
- watershed management.

# Unit 6G: Drought-Prone Area Development

Drought-prone regions face low and uncertain rainfall.

Problems:

- crop failure;
- water scarcity;
- fodder shortage;
- migration;
- livestock stress;
- land degradation.

Strategies:

- water harvesting;
- drought-resistant crops;
- pasture development;
- afforestation;
- tank renovation;
- soil moisture conservation;
- watershed development;
- livestock support.

# Unit 6H: Sustainable Development

Sustainable development means meeting present needs without reducing future generations' ability to meet their needs.

It requires balance among:

- economic growth;
- social justice;
- environmental protection.

Development that destroys forests, water, soil, and local livelihoods cannot be sustainable.

# Unit 6I: Why Sustainability Matters in India

India faces:

- large population pressure;
- poverty and inequality;
- water scarcity;
- land degradation;
- air and water pollution;
- biodiversity loss;
- climate risks;
- regional imbalance.

Sustainable development is necessary because environmental damage hurts poor and vulnerable groups most.

# Unit 6J: Case-Based Planning Logic

For any region, planning should ask:

1. What are the natural resources?
2. What are the environmental limits?
3. What are the livelihood needs?
4. Which groups are vulnerable?
5. What infrastructure is missing?
6. What local skills exist?
7. What development will be sustainable?

# Unit 6K: Integrated Development

Integrated development means combining sectors instead of treating them separately.

Example for a drought-prone area:

- water harvesting;
- crop diversification;
- livestock care;
- rural roads;
- credit;
- market access;
- skill development;
- women's participation.

This creates stronger results than one isolated scheme.

# Unit 6L: Planning and Participation

People's participation improves planning because local communities understand local problems.

Participation helps:

- identify real needs;
- reduce conflict;
- improve maintenance;
- include women and marginal groups;
- protect local ecology;
- increase accountability.

# Unit 6M: Answer-Writing Framework

For planning types:

1. Define planning.
2. Explain sectoral planning.
3. Explain regional planning.
4. Add target area and target group approaches.
5. Give Indian examples.

For sustainable development:

1. Define sustainable development.
2. Mention economic, social, and environmental balance.
3. Explain why India needs it.
4. Add examples such as watershed development or hill planning.
5. Conclude with future generations.

# Unit 6N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating planning as only economic growth | Planning also includes equity, region, and environment. |
| Confusing sectoral and regional planning | Sectoral focuses on sector; regional focuses on area. |
| Ignoring target groups | Development may bypass vulnerable communities. |
| Equating sustainable development with no development | It means balanced development, not stopping development. |
| Forgetting local participation | Sustainable planning needs community involvement. |
