# Chapter 1: Population - Distribution, Density, Growth and Composition
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography India

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies India's population pattern: where people live, how densely they are distributed, how population grows, and how it is composed by age, sex, literacy, occupation, and rural-urban residence. India has one of the world's largest populations, but it is not evenly spread across the country.

Population geography in India must be read with maps. Plains, river valleys, fertile agricultural regions, industrial areas, and urban corridors are densely populated, while mountains, deserts, forests, and remote regions are more sparsely populated.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain distribution of population in India;
- define and calculate population density;
- identify high-density and low-density regions;
- explain physical, economic, and historical factors affecting distribution;
- understand population growth and its phases;
- explain population composition by age, sex, literacy, occupation, and residence;
- interpret sex ratio and literacy patterns;
- connect population patterns with development challenges.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Population distribution | Spatial spread of people across regions. |
| Population density | Number of persons per square kilometre. |
| Population growth | Change in population over time. |
| Decadal growth rate | Percentage change in population over ten years. |
| Sex ratio | Number of females per thousand males. |
| Literacy rate | Percentage of literate population in a specified age group. |
| Occupational structure | Distribution of workers across economic sectors. |
| Rural population | People living in villages and rural settlements. |

# Unit 1A: Distribution of Population

India's population is unevenly distributed.

High population concentration is found in:

- Uttar Pradesh;
- Bihar;
- West Bengal;
- Maharashtra;
- Tamil Nadu;
- Kerala;
- Punjab-Haryana plains;
- major urban regions.

Low population concentration is found in:

- Himalayan states;
- desert regions of Rajasthan;
- forested tribal areas;
- parts of the northeast;
- islands with limited area and ecological constraints.

# Unit 1B: Factors Affecting Distribution

## Physical Factors

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Relief | Plains attract dense population; mountains discourage dense settlement. |
| Climate | Moderate and monsoon climates support agriculture and settlement. |
| Soil | Fertile alluvial soils support dense population. |
| Water | Rivers, groundwater, and rainfall support farming and towns. |
| Vegetation | Dense forests may limit settlement and infrastructure. |

## Economic Factors

Population concentrates where livelihoods are available.

Examples:

- agricultural regions attract rural population;
- industrial belts attract workers;
- cities attract migrants due to jobs and services;
- transport corridors encourage settlement.

## Historical and Social Factors

Old river valley civilisations, trade routes, religious centres, colonial ports, and administrative towns shaped population patterns.

# Unit 1C: Population Density

Population density is:

`Population density = total population / total area`

It helps compare population pressure across states.

High density often occurs where fertile land, rainfall, irrigation, jobs, and urban services exist.

Low density may result from harsh terrain, aridity, dense forests, poor connectivity, or ecological fragility.

# Unit 1D: Population Growth

Population growth in India has passed through different phases:

1. slow growth due to high birth and death rates;
2. rapid growth after mortality declined due to better health care, sanitation, and food supply;
3. slowing growth as birth rates decline;
4. movement toward demographic transition with regional variation.

India's growth rate has declined in recent decades, but absolute population increase remains large because the base population is very high.

# Unit 1E: Components of Growth

Population growth depends on:

- births;
- deaths;
- migration.

Natural growth is births minus deaths. In India, national population growth is mainly shaped by natural increase, while migration is important for regional redistribution.

# Unit 1F: Age Composition

Age composition shows proportion of children, working-age people, and elderly.

Categories:

- young population: 0-14 years;
- working-age population: 15-59 years;
- elderly population: 60 years and above.

A large working-age population can create demographic dividend if education, health, and employment are strong.

# Unit 1G: Sex Ratio

Sex ratio is number of females per thousand males.

`Sex ratio = (female population / male population) x 1000`

Low sex ratio may reflect gender discrimination, poor health access, migration, and social attitudes.

Improving sex ratio requires:

- gender equality;
- female education;
- health care;
- safety;
- legal enforcement;
- social change.

# Unit 1H: Literacy

Literacy improves:

- employment opportunities;
- health awareness;
- political participation;
- gender equality;
- population stabilisation;
- social mobility.

Regional and gender gaps in literacy are important development concerns.

# Unit 1I: Occupational Structure

Occupational structure shows how workers are distributed across sectors.

| Sector | Examples |
|---|---|
| Primary | Agriculture, forestry, fishing, mining |
| Secondary | Manufacturing, construction |
| Tertiary | Trade, transport, services |

India has seen growth of secondary and tertiary sectors, but agriculture still supports a large share of population.

# Unit 1J: Rural-Urban Composition

India remains significantly rural, but urban population is rising.

Urbanisation is driven by:

- industrialisation;
- services;
- education;
- health facilities;
- migration;
- transport connectivity.

Urban growth creates opportunities but also challenges such as housing shortage, congestion, pollution, and informal settlements.

# Unit 1L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Confusing population size and density | Density relates population to area. |
| Saying all large states have high density | Area matters; density can be low in large states. |
| Ignoring physical factors | Relief, climate, soil, and water strongly matter. |
| Treating growth rate and absolute growth as same | Growth rate may fall while absolute increase remains large. |
| Ignoring gender in population composition | Sex ratio is a major indicator. |
