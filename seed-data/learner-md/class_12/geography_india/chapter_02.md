# Chapter 2: Human Settlements
### NCERT Class 12 - Geography India

## Chapter at a Glance

Human settlements are places where people live, build houses, organise social life, and carry out economic activities. Settlements may be rural or urban, compact or dispersed, planned or unplanned. In India, settlement patterns reflect relief, water, land use, caste and community history, transport, security, and economic change.

This chapter explains rural settlement types, urban settlements, town classification, functions of towns, and settlement problems.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- define human settlement;
- distinguish rural and urban settlements;
- explain compact, semi-compact, hamleted, and dispersed settlements;
- identify factors affecting rural settlement patterns;
- classify towns by size and function;
- explain urbanisation in India;
- describe problems of rural and urban settlements;
- write answers using examples from Indian regions.

# Unit 2A: Meaning of Human Settlement

A human settlement is a place where people live. It includes houses, paths, fields, wells, shops, institutions, and social spaces.

Settlements are shaped by:

- landforms;
- water supply;
- climate;
- soil;
- economy;
- security;
- transport;
- social organisation;
- history.

# Unit 2B: Rural and Urban Settlements

## Rural Settlements

Rural settlements are closely linked with agriculture, fishing, forestry, mining, or other primary activities.

Features:

- smaller population;
- close relation with land;
- lower building density;
- community-based life;
- dependence on local environment.

## Urban Settlements

Urban settlements are linked with manufacturing, trade, transport, administration, education, health, and services.

Features:

- larger population;
- high density;
- non-primary occupations;
- complex infrastructure;
- wider social diversity.

# Unit 2C: Compact Settlements

In compact settlements, houses are close together. Such settlements are common in fertile plains and river valleys.

Reasons:

- fertile agricultural land;
- water availability;
- security needs;
- social cohesion;
- flat land;
- common facilities.

Examples are common in northern plains.

# Unit 2D: Semi-Compact Settlements

Semi-compact settlements are intermediate. Houses are not as tightly clustered as compact settlements but not fully scattered.

They may develop when:

- population grows outward from a compact village;
- land conditions vary;
- social groups expand into nearby areas.

# Unit 2E: Hamleted Settlements

Hamleted settlements are divided into separate clusters. Each cluster may be based on caste, community, tribe, occupation, or landholding.

They are common where social segregation or physical barriers divide the settlement.

# Unit 2F: Dispersed Settlements

Dispersed settlements have isolated houses or small groups spread across the landscape.

Common conditions:

- hilly terrain;
- forests;
- desert areas;
- large farms;
- pastoral areas;
- fragmented resources.

Such settlements may have weaker access to schools, health care, and roads.

# Unit 2G: Factors Affecting Rural Settlement Patterns

| Factor | Influence |
|---|---|
| Relief | Plains favour compact settlements; hills favour dispersed settlements. |
| Water | Settlements cluster near wells, rivers, tanks, and springs. |
| Soil | Fertile soil supports dense villages. |
| Security | Historical need for safety encouraged compact settlement. |
| Social structure | Caste and community patterns shape layout. |
| Land ownership | Large farms may lead to dispersed houses. |
| Transport | Roads influence linear settlements. |

# Unit 2H: Urban Settlements in India

Urban settlements grow due to:

- administration;
- trade;
- industry;
- transport;
- education;
- defence;
- pilgrimage;
- tourism;
- services.

Urban growth is uneven. Large metropolitan cities attract migrants, capital, and services, while many small towns grow slowly.

# Unit 2I: Functional Classification of Towns

| Town type | Main function |
|---|---|
| Administrative | Government offices and administration |
| Industrial | Manufacturing and processing |
| Transport | Junctions, ports, railway centres |
| Commercial | Trade and markets |
| Mining | Mineral extraction centres |
| Cantonment | Military function |
| Educational | Universities and institutions |
| Religious | Pilgrimage and sacred functions |
| Tourist | Recreation and heritage |

Many towns are multifunctional.

# Unit 2J: Urbanisation Problems

Urbanisation creates opportunities but also challenges.

Problems:

- housing shortage;
- slums;
- traffic congestion;
- water scarcity;
- waste disposal;
- air pollution;
- unemployment;
- pressure on health and education;
- social inequality.

Urban planning is needed to manage growth.

# Unit 2K: Rural Settlement Problems

Problems include:

- poor road connectivity;
- limited health facilities;
- limited educational access;
- drinking water problems;
- sanitation issues;
- seasonal unemployment;
- vulnerability to floods or droughts;
- out-migration.

Improving rural settlements requires infrastructure, livelihood, health, education, and digital connectivity.

# Unit 2M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Defining settlement as houses only | Include people, functions, and infrastructure. |
| Equating rural with backward | Rural settlements are occupation-based, not simply backward. |
| Saying all villages are compact | India has compact, semi-compact, hamleted, and dispersed forms. |
| Ignoring social factors | Caste, community, and history shape settlements. |
| Treating every town as industrial | Towns have varied functions. |
