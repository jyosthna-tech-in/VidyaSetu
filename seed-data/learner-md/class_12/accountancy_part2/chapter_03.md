# Unit 3A: Meaning of Financial Statements

Financial statements are formal annual reports through which company management communicates financial information to shareholders and other users.

They normally include:

1. Balance sheet.
2. Statement of profit and loss.
3. Cash flow statement.
4. Notes to accounts.

## Users of Financial Statements

| User | What They Need |
|---|---|
| Shareholders | Profitability, dividend potential, financial strength. |
| Lenders | Solvency and repayment capacity. |
| Management | Performance review and planning. |
| Government | Tax and regulatory compliance. |
| Employees | Stability and ability to pay wages or benefits. |
| Suppliers | Creditworthiness. |
| Customers | Continuity and reliability. |

# Unit 3B: Nature of Financial Statements

Financial statements are:

1. Based on recorded facts.
2. Influenced by accounting concepts and conventions.
3. Prepared for a period or date.
4. Expressed mainly in money terms.
5. Summarised, not fully detailed.
6. Intended for general decision-making.

## Recorded Facts

Transactions are recorded based on evidence such as invoices, receipts, contracts, and bank records.

## Accounting Assumptions

Financial statements rely on assumptions such as:

1. Going concern.
2. Accrual.
3. Consistency.
4. Materiality.

## Judgement

Some figures require judgement, such as depreciation, provision for doubtful debts, useful life of assets, and valuation of inventory.

# Unit 3C: Objectives of Financial Statements

Financial statements aim to:

1. Provide information about financial position.
2. Provide information about profitability.
3. Show changes in financial resources.
4. Help users assess solvency and liquidity.
5. Help investors and creditors make decisions.
6. Support comparison over time.
7. Show stewardship of management.
8. Provide data for analysis and planning.

# Unit 3D: Balance Sheet Structure

A company balance sheet is usually presented under equity and liabilities on one side and assets on the other.

## Equity and Liabilities

| Main Head | Examples |
|---|---|
| Shareholders' funds | Share capital, reserves and surplus. |
| Share application money pending allotment | Application money not yet allotted and not refundable immediately, if applicable. |
| Non-current liabilities | Long-term borrowings, deferred tax liabilities, long-term provisions. |
| Current liabilities | Short-term borrowings, trade payables, other current liabilities, short-term provisions. |

## Assets

| Main Head | Examples |
|---|---|
| Non-current assets | Fixed assets, non-current investments, long-term loans and advances, other non-current assets. |
| Current assets | Current investments, inventories, trade receivables, cash and cash equivalents, short-term loans and advances, other current assets. |

# Unit 3E: Shareholders' Funds

Shareholders' funds represent owners' claim.

## Components

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Share capital | Paid-up equity and preference share capital. |
| Reserves and surplus | Retained earnings, securities premium, capital reserve, general reserve, surplus in statement of profit and loss. |

## Share Capital Presentation

Share capital may include:

1. Authorised capital.
2. Issued capital.
3. Subscribed capital.
4. Called-up capital.
5. Paid-up capital.
6. Calls unpaid, if any.
7. Forfeited shares amount, if applicable.

# Unit 3F: Non-Current Liabilities

Non-current liabilities are obligations not expected to be settled within the operating cycle or twelve months.

Examples:

1. Long-term borrowings.
2. Debentures.
3. Long-term loans.
4. Deferred tax liabilities.
5. Long-term provisions.

## Current Maturity

If part of a long-term borrowing is due within twelve months, that part is often shown under current liabilities as current maturities.

# Unit 3G: Current Liabilities

Current liabilities are obligations expected to be settled within the operating cycle or twelve months.

Examples:

1. Short-term borrowings.
2. Trade payables.
3. Outstanding expenses.
4. Current maturities of long-term debt.
5. Interest accrued but not due.
6. Short-term provisions.

# Unit 3H: Non-Current Assets

Non-current assets are held for long-term use or benefit.

## Main Categories

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Property, plant and equipment | Land, building, plant, machinery, furniture. |
| Intangible assets | Goodwill, patents, trademarks, software. |
| Capital work-in-progress | Assets under construction. |
| Non-current investments | Long-term investments. |
| Long-term loans and advances | Loans not recoverable within twelve months. |

# Unit 3I: Current Assets

Current assets are expected to be realised or consumed within the operating cycle or twelve months.

Examples:

1. Inventories.
2. Trade receivables.
3. Cash and cash equivalents.
4. Short-term investments.
5. Short-term loans and advances.
6. Prepaid expenses.

## Trade Receivables

Trade receivables arise from normal credit sales or services. They are shown after deducting provision for doubtful debts if required.

# Unit 3J: Statement of Profit and Loss

The statement of profit and loss reports performance for a period.

## Basic Structure

| Item | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Revenue from operations | Main operating revenue such as sales or service revenue. |
| Other income | Non-operating income such as interest, dividend, or profit on sale of asset. |
| Total revenue | Revenue from operations plus other income. |
| Expenses | Costs incurred to earn revenue. |
| Profit before tax | Profit before income tax. |
| Tax expense | Current tax and deferred tax. |
| Profit after tax | Final profit available for appropriation. |

## Common Expense Heads

1. Cost of materials consumed.
2. Purchases of stock-in-trade.
3. Changes in inventories.
4. Employee benefit expense.
5. Finance costs.
6. Depreciation and amortisation.
7. Other expenses.

# Unit 3K: Notes to Accounts

Notes give details behind figures in the main statements. They may explain:

1. Accounting policies.
2. Share capital details.
3. Reserves movement.
4. Borrowing details.
5. Fixed asset schedule.
6. Contingent liabilities.
7. Commitments.
8. Related party information, if applicable.

Notes are essential because financial statements are summarised.

# Unit 3L: Uses and Importance

Financial statements help users:

1. Assess profitability.
2. Assess solvency.
3. Assess liquidity.
4. Compare performance over years.
5. Compare with other companies.
6. Judge efficiency of management.
7. Plan investments or lending.
8. Understand resource use.

# Unit 3M: Limitations

| Limitation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Historical cost | Many values are based on past cost, not current market value. |
| Monetary focus | Non-monetary factors such as employee skill and brand strength may not be fully shown. |
| Estimates | Depreciation, provisions, and valuation involve judgement. |
| Window dressing | Management may present figures in a more favourable way within rules. |
| Inflation effect | Price-level changes may reduce comparability. |
| Aggregation | Detailed information is summarised. |
| Not a forecast | Statements show past position and performance, not guaranteed future results. |

# Unit 3N: Classification Practice Guide

| Item | Usual Classification |
|---|---|
| Equity share capital | Shareholders' funds. |
| Securities premium | Reserves and surplus. |
| Debentures repayable after five years | Non-current liabilities. |
| Bank overdraft repayable on demand | Current liabilities. |
| Trade payables | Current liabilities. |
| Building | Non-current asset. |
| Inventory | Current asset. |
| Cash at bank | Current asset. |
| Preliminary expenses not written off | Usually deducted or treated as per current reporting requirements; do not show as ordinary asset unless format asks. |

## Final Revision

Company financial statements are structured summaries. The main exam challenge is classification. Always decide whether an item belongs to equity, non-current liability, current liability, non-current asset, or current asset before placing it in the statement.
