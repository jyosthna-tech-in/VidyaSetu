# Unit 6A: Meaning and Purpose

A cash flow statement shows cash inflows and outflows during an accounting period. It explains why cash and cash equivalents increased or decreased.

Profit and cash are different. A company can earn profit but still face cash shortage if customers do not pay quickly or inventory absorbs cash.

## Objectives

The cash flow statement helps users:

1. Assess cash-generating ability.
2. Understand cash needs.
3. Evaluate liquidity.
4. Compare operating performance with cash performance.
5. See how investing and financing decisions affected cash.
6. Judge ability to pay dividends, interest, and debt.

# Unit 6C: Cash and Cash Equivalents

Cash includes:

1. Cash in hand.
2. Demand deposits with banks.

Cash equivalents are short-term, highly liquid investments that can be quickly converted into known cash amounts.

Examples may include:

1. Treasury bills.
2. Commercial paper.
3. Short-term money market instruments.

## Not Usually Cash Equivalents

1. Long-term investments.
2. Shares held for investment.
3. Inventory.
4. Trade receivables.

# Unit 6D: Cash Flows

Cash flow means movement of cash or cash equivalents. Increase or decrease within cash and cash equivalents is not treated as cash flow for statement classification.

Example: Cash deposited into bank changes form from cash in hand to bank balance. It does not increase total cash and cash equivalents.

## Non-Cash Transactions

Non-cash transactions are excluded from cash flow statement but may be disclosed separately.

Examples:

1. Issue of shares to purchase machinery.
2. Conversion of debentures into equity shares.
3. Bonus issue of shares.

# Unit 6E: Classification of Activities

## Operating Activities

Operating activities are principal revenue-producing activities.

Examples:

1. Cash received from customers.
2. Cash paid to suppliers.
3. Cash paid to employees.
4. Cash paid for operating expenses.
5. Cash generated from services.

For a financial enterprise, interest and dividend may be operating because they relate to main revenue activity.

## Investing Activities

Investing activities relate to long-term assets and investments.

Examples:

1. Purchase of machinery.
2. Sale of building.
3. Purchase of long-term investments.
4. Sale of long-term investments.
5. Interest received, for non-financial enterprise.
6. Dividend received, for non-financial enterprise.

## Financing Activities

Financing activities change owners' capital and borrowings.

Examples:

1. Issue of shares for cash.
2. Buy-back of shares.
3. Issue of debentures for cash.
4. Redemption of debentures.
5. Long-term loan taken.
6. Long-term loan repaid.
7. Interest paid, for non-financial enterprise.
8. Dividend paid.

# Unit 6F: Treatment of Specific Items

| Item | Usual Classification for Non-Financial Enterprise |
|---|---|
| Interest received | Investing activity. |
| Dividend received | Investing activity. |
| Interest paid | Financing activity. |
| Dividend paid | Financing activity. |
| Income tax paid | Operating activity unless specifically linked to investing or financing. |
| Purchase of fixed asset | Investing activity. |
| Sale of fixed asset | Investing activity. |
| Issue of shares | Financing activity. |
| Redemption of debentures | Financing activity. |

# Unit 6G: Cash from Operating Activities - Indirect Method

Indirect method starts with profit and adjusts it to cash from operations.

## Broad Format

Profit before tax and extraordinary items

Add non-cash and non-operating expenses:

1. Depreciation.
2. Amortisation.
3. Loss on sale of asset.
4. Finance cost, if treated as financing.

Less non-operating incomes:

1. Profit on sale of asset.
2. Interest income, if investing.
3. Dividend income, if investing.

Result: Operating profit before working capital changes.

Then adjust current assets and current liabilities.

## Working Capital Adjustment Rules

| Change | Effect on Cash from Operations |
|---|---|
| Increase in current asset | Subtract. |
| Decrease in current asset | Add. |
| Increase in current liability | Add. |
| Decrease in current liability | Subtract. |

## Why These Rules Work

Increase in current asset means cash is tied up. Example: receivables increased because sales were recorded but cash not received.

Increase in current liability means expense or purchase has not yet been paid, so cash is conserved.

# Unit 6H: Cash from Operating Activities - Direct Method

Direct method shows major cash receipts and payments.

## Typical Format

| Cash Inflows | Cash Outflows |
|---|---|
| Cash received from customers | Cash paid to suppliers |
| Cash received for services | Cash paid to employees |
|  | Cash paid for operating expenses |
|  | Income tax paid |

Net cash from operating activities = Total operating cash inflows - Total operating cash outflows

## Useful Calculations

Cash received from customers = Revenue from operations + Opening trade receivables - Closing trade receivables

Cash paid to suppliers = Purchases + Opening trade payables - Closing trade payables, adjusted for inventory movement if purchases are not directly given.

# Unit 6I: Cash from Investing Activities

Investing activities usually involve long-term assets and investments.

## Inflows

1. Sale of fixed assets.
2. Sale of long-term investments.
3. Interest received, for non-financial enterprise.
4. Dividend received, for non-financial enterprise.

## Outflows

1. Purchase of fixed assets.
2. Purchase of long-term investments.
3. Loans and advances given, if investment nature.

## Fixed Asset Account Method

To find purchase or sale when not directly given, prepare asset account:

Opening balance + Purchases - Book value of asset sold - Depreciation = Closing balance

Rearrange to find missing amount.

# Unit 6J: Cash from Financing Activities

Financing activities involve owners' funds and borrowings.

## Inflows

1. Issue of shares for cash.
2. Issue of debentures for cash.
3. Long-term loan taken.

## Outflows

1. Redemption of shares or debentures.
2. Repayment of loans.
3. Interest paid, for non-financial enterprise.
4. Dividend paid.

## Share Capital Note

If share capital increases due to bonus issue, there is no cash flow. If shares are issued for cash, it is financing inflow.

# Unit 6L: Worked Mini Case

Profit before tax is Rs. 2,00,000. Depreciation is Rs. 40,000. Profit on sale of machinery is Rs. 10,000. Trade receivables increased by Rs. 30,000. Inventory decreased by Rs. 20,000. Trade payables increased by Rs. 15,000. Tax paid is Rs. 25,000.

## Operating Cash Flow

| Item | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Profit before tax | Rs. 2,00,000 |
| Add depreciation | Rs. 40,000 |
| Less profit on sale of machinery | Rs. 10,000 |
| Operating profit before working capital changes | Rs. 2,30,000 |
| Less increase in trade receivables | Rs. 30,000 |
| Add decrease in inventory | Rs. 20,000 |
| Add increase in trade payables | Rs. 15,000 |
| Cash generated from operations | Rs. 2,35,000 |
| Less tax paid | Rs. 25,000 |
| Cash from operating activities | Rs. 2,10,000 |

# Unit 6M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---|
| Treating profit as cash | Adjust profit for non-cash items and working capital. |
| Adding increase in receivables | Subtract increase in current assets. |
| Subtracting increase in payables | Add increase in current liabilities. |
| Including bonus issue as cash inflow | Bonus issue is non-cash. |
| Treating asset purchase as operating | Fixed asset purchase is investing. |
| Treating loan repayment as investing | Loan repayment is financing. |
| Forgetting tax paid | Deduct tax paid in operating unless specifically classified elsewhere. |
| Not reconciling closing cash | Always match closing cash and cash equivalents. |

## Practice Set

1. Classify: issue of shares, purchase of machinery, cash received from customers, interest paid, sale of investment, dividend paid.
2. Profit before tax is Rs. 1,50,000, depreciation Rs. 20,000, loss on sale of asset Rs. 5,000, receivables increase Rs. 12,000, payables decrease Rs. 8,000. Calculate operating cash before tax.
3. Opening machinery is Rs. 4,00,000, closing machinery Rs. 5,20,000, depreciation Rs. 40,000, machinery sold at book value Rs. 30,000. Find machinery purchased.
4. Opening cash is Rs. 60,000. Operating cash inflow is Rs. 1,20,000, investing cash outflow is Rs. 90,000, financing cash inflow is Rs. 50,000. Find closing cash.
5. Explain why conversion of debentures into shares is not included as cash flow.

## Final Revision

Cash flow statement converts accounting movement into cash movement. Classify first, calculate second, reconcile last. Operating activities explain business cash, investing activities explain long-term asset and investment cash, and financing activities explain owner and lender cash.
