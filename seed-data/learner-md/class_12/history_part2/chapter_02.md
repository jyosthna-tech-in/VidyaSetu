# Chapter 2: Bhakti-Sufi Traditions
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies devotional and mystical traditions in medieval India, especially Bhakti and Sufi movements. These traditions shaped religious life, poetry, music, community practices, and social criticism. They often questioned ritual hierarchy and emphasised personal devotion, love, surrender, and remembrance of the divine.

The chapter is not about one uniform movement. Bhakti and Sufi traditions were diverse, regional, multilingual, and socially layered.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain the meaning of Bhakti and Sufism;
- describe Alvar and Nayanar traditions;
- understand Virashaiva/Lingayat ideas;
- explain major features of Sufi orders;
- describe khanqahs, silsilas, and pirs;
- analyse teachings of Kabir, Guru Nanak, and Mirabai;
- understand devotional poetry as historical evidence;
- explain interaction between religion, society, language, and power.

# Unit 2A: Meaning of Bhakti

Bhakti means loving devotion to a chosen deity. It emphasises emotional relationship between devotee and divine.

Bhakti could be:

- saguna: devotion to a deity with form;
- nirguna: devotion to a formless divine.

Bhakti often used regional languages and songs, making religious expression accessible to wider groups.

# Unit 2B: Alvars and Nayanars

Alvars were devotees of Vishnu. Nayanars were devotees of Shiva. They composed Tamil hymns full of emotional devotion.

Features:

- intense personal devotion;
- use of Tamil language;
- pilgrimage to sacred sites;
- temple-centred worship;
- criticism of rival traditions in some compositions;
- inclusion of devotees from varied social backgrounds.

# Unit 2C: Temples and Bhakti

Temples became important centres of worship, economy, and culture.

They supported:

- rituals;
- festivals;
- music;
- dance;
- land grants;
- craft production;
- community identity.

Bhakti poetry and temple worship often reinforced each other.

# Unit 2D: Virashaiva and Lingayat Traditions

The Virashaiva or Lingayat tradition developed in Karnataka. It is associated with Basavanna and others.

Important ideas:

- devotion to Shiva;
- wearing the linga as personal symbol;
- critique of caste hierarchy;
- questioning of ritualism;
- emphasis on work and devotion;
- criticism of some Brahmanical practices.

This tradition shows how devotion could become social criticism.

# Unit 2E: Sufism

Sufism is a mystical tradition within Islam. It stresses inner love of God, discipline, remembrance, and guidance by a spiritual teacher.

Sufis often used poetry, music, and metaphor to express longing for divine union.

Important elements:

- pir or sheikh;
- murid or disciple;
- silsila;
- khanqah;
- sama or devotional listening in some traditions;
- service to people.

# Unit 2F: Sufi Orders

Sufi orders or silsilas spread across regions.

Important orders in India included:

- Chishti;
- Suhrawardi;
- Qadiri;
- Naqshbandi.

The Chishtis became especially influential in north India. Their khanqahs welcomed people from different backgrounds.

# Unit 2G: Khanqah

A khanqah was a Sufi centre where disciples and visitors gathered.

Functions:

- spiritual teaching;
- prayer and meditation;
- food distribution;
- lodging for travellers;
- community gathering;
- meeting point across social groups.

Khanqahs helped build networks of devotion and support.

# Unit 2H: Sufi Shrines and Ziyarat

After a Sufi saint's death, tombs often became pilgrimage centres.

Ziyarat to shrines expressed devotion and hope for blessing. Shrines became spaces where people from varied communities gathered.

Music, offerings, and festivals helped create shared devotional culture.

# Unit 2I: Kabir

Kabir is associated with nirguna devotion. His verses criticised empty ritual, caste pride, and religious divisions.

Key ideas:

- divine is formless;
- truth is found through inner experience;
- ritual without understanding is useless;
- social hierarchy is questioned;
- Hindu-Muslim divisions are criticised.

Kabir's language drew from everyday speech, making his message powerful and accessible.

# Unit 2J: Guru Nanak

Guru Nanak taught devotion to one divine reality, ethical living, honest work, and sharing.

Important ideas:

- remembrance of the divine name;
- equality;
- rejection of empty ritual;
- community life;
- moral conduct.

The Sikh tradition developed through the teachings of Guru Nanak and later Gurus.

# Unit 2K: Mirabai

Mirabai was a devotee of Krishna. Her poetry expresses intense personal devotion and rejection of social constraints.

She is significant because:

- she challenged elite family expectations;
- she placed devotion above social status;
- her songs express longing and surrender;
- she became a symbol of spiritual defiance.

# Unit 2L: Language and Devotion

Bhakti and Sufi traditions used regional languages, dialects, and oral performance.

This made ideas accessible beyond Sanskrit, Persian, or elite circles.

Songs, poems, and stories circulated through:

- singing;
- gatherings;
- pilgrimage;
- oral memory;
- manuscripts;
- later compilations.

# Unit 2M: Evidence and Interpretation

Historians study:

- devotional poems;
- hagiographies;
- inscriptions;
- temple records;
- Sufi malfuzat and letters;
- architecture;
- oral traditions.

These materials must be read carefully because devotional biographies may include miracles and idealised portraits.

# Unit 2N: Major Themes

## Devotion and Equality

Many traditions challenged social pride and emphasised devotion over birth.

## Regional Languages

Religious expression moved into people's languages.

## Community Spaces

Temples, khanqahs, and shrines became social spaces.

## Critique of Ritual

Some saints criticised ritual without inner devotion.

## Diversity

There was no single Bhakti or Sufi pattern. Traditions varied by region and community.

# Unit 2P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating Bhakti as one uniform movement | It was diverse and regional. |
| Confusing Alvars and Nayanars | Alvars were Vaishnava; Nayanars were Shaiva. |
| Saying all Bhakti rejected temples | Some traditions were temple-centred, others critical. |
| Reducing Sufism to shrine worship | It includes discipline, teaching, and spiritual lineages. |
| Ignoring language | Regional languages were central to spread. |
