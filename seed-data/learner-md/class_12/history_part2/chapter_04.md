# Chapter 4: Peasants, Zamindars and the State
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies agrarian society under the Mughals, mainly through the Ain-i Akbari and other evidence. It examines peasants, zamindars, land revenue, village communities, agriculture, caste, gender, and the relationship between the state and countryside.

The central idea is that the Mughal Empire depended on agrarian surplus. To understand Mughal power, we must understand villages, fields, peasants, zamindars, and revenue systems.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain the importance of agriculture in Mughal India;
- describe peasants and village communities;
- understand zamindars and their role;
- explain land revenue system;
- understand the Ain-i Akbari as evidence;
- describe crops, irrigation, and rural production;
- explain caste, gender, and labour in villages;
- analyse state-society relations in the countryside.

# Unit 4A: Agriculture and Mughal Power

The Mughal Empire depended heavily on land revenue. Agriculture produced the surplus that supported the state, army, nobles, towns, and court.

Therefore, rulers needed:

- measurement of land;
- assessment of crops;
- revenue officials;
- cooperation or control of zamindars;
- stable cultivation;
- peasant production.

# Unit 4B: Peasants

Peasants were not all alike.

Differences existed in:

- size of holdings;
- caste status;
- access to cattle and tools;
- ability to pay revenue;
- dependence on labour;
- regional ecology.

Some peasants were relatively prosperous, while others were poor and vulnerable.

# Unit 4C: Village Community

Villages were units of production and social life.

They included:

- cultivators;
- artisans;
- labourers;
- pastoralists;
- village headmen;
- accountants;
- service groups.

Village communities were not always harmonious. They had hierarchy, caste divisions, gender inequality, and conflicts over resources.

# Unit 4D: Caste and Rural Society

Caste shaped occupation, status, marriage, and social relations in many villages.

Dominant groups often controlled land and local power. Lower-status groups provided labour and services.

But caste patterns varied by region and changed over time.

# Unit 4E: Women in Agrarian Society

Women contributed significantly to agriculture and household production.

Their work included:

- sowing;
- weeding;
- harvesting;
- processing grain;
- animal care;
- craft work;
- domestic labour.

Despite their labour, property rights and public authority were often limited by patriarchy.

# Unit 4F: Zamindars

Zamindars were local landed intermediaries. They had social and political power in rural areas.

Roles:

- collecting revenue;
- maintaining local influence;
- controlling land and labour;
- supporting or resisting the state;
- acting as rural elites.

Zamindars were not simply revenue collectors. They had roots in local society and could mobilise peasants.

# Unit 4G: Land Revenue System

Land revenue was the main income of the Mughal state.

Revenue process involved:

- measuring land;
- classifying crops;
- estimating produce;
- fixing state demand;
- collecting revenue in cash or kind depending on region and system.

The state wanted stable revenue, while peasants wanted manageable demands.

# Unit 4H: Jama and Hasil

Jama means assessed revenue demand.

Hasil means actual collection.

The difference between jama and hasil shows that official assessment did not always match real collection. Crop failure, resistance, corruption, or local conditions could affect hasil.

# Unit 4I: Ain-i Akbari

The Ain-i Akbari was compiled by Abu'l Fazl as part of Akbar's courtly and administrative project.

It provides information about:

- administration;
- revenue;
- provinces;
- crops;
- prices;
- officials;
- ranks;
- imperial ideology.

Limitations:

- it reflects official viewpoint;
- it presents ideal order;
- regional variations may be simplified;
- actual practice could differ.

# Unit 4J: Crops and Production

Mughal India produced diverse crops.

Food crops:

- rice;
- wheat;
- millets;
- pulses.

Cash crops:

- cotton;
- sugarcane;
- indigo;
- oilseeds.

Agriculture depended on monsoon, irrigation, soil, labour, and market demand.

# Unit 4K: Irrigation and Technology

Irrigation methods included wells, tanks, canals, and water-lifting devices. Irrigation expanded cultivation and supported high-value crops.

Technology was not uniform. Local ecology shaped agricultural practices.

# Unit 4L: State and Rural Conflict

The relationship between state, zamindars, and peasants could be cooperative or conflictual.

Conflict could arise due to:

- high revenue demand;
- crop failure;
- zamindar resistance;
- peasant flight;
- local rebellion;
- jagirdar pressure.

The Mughal state needed local cooperation to collect revenue effectively.

# Unit 4M: Answer-Writing Framework

For zamindars:

1. Define zamindars.
2. Explain revenue role.
3. Add local social power.
4. Mention relation with peasants and state.
5. Conclude that they were intermediaries and rural elites.

For Ain-i Akbari:

1. Identify the text and compiler.
2. Explain what information it gives.
3. Mention its administrative purpose.
4. Add limitations as official ideal.
5. Conclude with careful historical use.

# Unit 4N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating peasants as one uniform group | Peasants differed by wealth, caste, and region. |
| Calling zamindars only tax collectors | They were local power holders. |
| Taking Ain-i Akbari as exact reality | It is official and idealised in parts. |
| Ignoring women | Women contributed significantly to agrarian work. |
| Confusing jama and hasil | Jama is assessed demand; hasil is actual collection. |
