## 1. Big Picture

This chapter explains how the Mughal Empire represented itself through chronicles, court culture, paintings, ceremonies, and administration. Mughal rulers did not only conquer territories; they also created an image of legitimate, just, divinely favoured kingship.

The main sources are court chronicles such as the Akbar Nama, Ain-i Akbari, Badshah Nama, and other Persian texts. These sources are valuable because they provide information about administration, court life, ideology, and political events. But they must be read carefully because they were written by court historians who usually supported the emperor's viewpoint.

## 2. Timeline of Major Mughal Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Key Point |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Babur | 1526-1530 | Founder of Mughal rule in India; Timurid background. |
| Humayun | 1530-1540, 1555-1556 | Lost empire to Sher Shah Sur; later regained it. |
| Akbar | 1556-1605 | Expanded and consolidated empire; major administrative and ideological reforms. |
| Jahangir | 1605-1627 | Continued Mughal traditions; known for justice symbolism and court culture. |
| Shah Jahan | 1628-1658 | Height of architectural and courtly splendour. |
| Aurangzeb | 1658-1707 | Expanded territory but faced growing tensions; after his death Mughal power declined. |
| Bahadur Shah Zafar II | Deposed in 1857 | Last Mughal ruler; removed by the British after the Revolt of 1857. |

## 3. Who Were the Mughals?

The word "Mughal" comes from "Mongol", but the rulers themselves preferred to be known as Timurids because they traced descent from Timur on the paternal side. Babur was also linked to the line of Genghis Khan through his mother.

The Mughal Empire was built through:

1. Military conquest.
2. Political alliances.
3. Administrative integration.
4. Accommodation of local elites.
5. Revenue and military systems.
6. Courtly culture and imperial ideology.

## 4. Why Chronicles Matter

Chronicles are continuous historical accounts arranged around time, rulers, and major events.

Mughal chronicles are important because they:

1. Record events of the emperor's reign.
2. Describe court rituals and hierarchy.
3. Provide information about administration.
4. Present the emperor's ideals.
5. Help modern historians reconstruct Mughal political culture.
6. Reveal how imperial authority was justified.

## 5. Limits of Court Chronicles

Court chronicles are rich sources, but they are not neutral.

| Strength | Limitation |
| --- | --- |
| Provide detailed information about court, administration, and events. | Often glorify the emperor. |
| Written by educated insiders close to power. | Focus more on rulers than ordinary people. |
| Preserve names, dates, ranks, institutions, and ceremonies. | May ignore failure, resistance, or dissent. |
| Include ideological concepts of kingship. | Present the official view of empire. |

Historical skill: use chronicles as evidence, but always ask who wrote them, for whom, and why.

## 6. Persian as the Language of Empire

Mughal court chronicles were mainly written in Persian.

### Why Persian Became Important

1. It was already a major courtly language under the Delhi Sultans.
2. It carried prestige in Central Asian and Iranian cultural worlds.
3. Akbar promoted Persian as the language of administration and empire.
4. Migrants from Iran and Central Asia strengthened Persian literary culture.
5. It helped create a shared elite language across the empire.

### Effects

Persian influenced:

1. Administrative records.
2. Court literature.
3. Regional languages.
4. Indo-Persian culture.
5. The development of Urdu through interaction with Hindavi.

## 7. Translation Projects

The Mughal court sponsored translations to bring different knowledge traditions into Persian.

Examples:

| Text/Tradition | Mughal Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Babur's memoirs | Translated from Turkish into Persian as Babur Nama. |
| Mahabharata | Translated into Persian as Razmnama. |
| Sanskrit and other Indian works | Translated to make knowledge available within court culture. |

These projects supported Akbar's wider interest in cultural dialogue and imperial integration.

## 8. Manuscript Production

Books in Mughal India were manuscripts, meaning they were handwritten.

### Kitabkhana

The imperial kitabkhana was both a library and a workshop where manuscripts were stored, copied, illustrated, and produced.

### People Involved

| Specialist | Work |
| --- | --- |
| Paper makers | Prepared paper folios. |
| Calligraphers/scribes | Copied the text beautifully. |
| Gilders | Decorated pages with gold and ornament. |
| Painters | Created illustrations. |
| Bookbinders | Bound folios into volumes. |
| Supervisors | Checked quality and coordination. |

Manuscripts were valuable objects combining knowledge, beauty, craftsmanship, and imperial patronage.

## 9. Calligraphy and Painting

### Calligraphy

Calligraphy was the art of beautiful handwriting. It was highly valued in Mughal culture. Nastaliq was a major style, known for its flowing form.

### Painting

Mughal manuscripts often contained miniature paintings. These paintings did more than decorate books. They communicated ideas about kingship, hierarchy, justice, victory, and imperial power.

### Tension with Orthodoxy

Some religious scholars objected to the depiction of living beings. Mughal emperors and court thinkers such as Abu'l Fazl defended painting as a refined art that helped understanding and representation.

## 10. Abu'l Fazl and the Akbar Nama

Abu'l Fazl was one of Akbar's closest advisers and the author of the Akbar Nama.

### Why Abu'l Fazl Was Important

1. He was learned in many intellectual traditions.
2. He supported Akbar's policies.
3. He opposed narrow religious orthodoxy.
4. He shaped the official image of Akbar's rule.
5. He described the empire in political, administrative, social, and cultural terms.

### Structure of Akbar Nama

| Part | Content |
| --- | --- |
| Book 1 | History before and early part of Akbar's life. |
| Book 2 | Chronological account of Akbar's reign. |
| Book 3 | Ain-i Akbari: administrative, social, cultural, and statistical account of the empire. |

## 11. Badshah Nama

The Badshah Nama is a major chronicle of Shah Jahan's reign. It is important for its rich account of court life, ceremonies, imperial events, and paintings.

Like other Mughal chronicles, it presents imperial power in a grand and ordered form.

## 12. Mughal Ideology of Kingship

Mughal kingship was represented through several ideas.

| Idea | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Divine light | The emperor was imagined as receiving light or favour from God. |
| Sulh-i kul | Peace with all; principle of universal harmony and tolerance. |
| Justice | The emperor was expected to protect weak and strong alike. |
| Hierarchy | Court rituals ranked everyone according to status. |
| Sovereignty | The emperor was the centre of political authority. |
| Universal rule | The emperor was presented as ruler of a diverse population. |

## 13. Divine Light

Court writers and painters represented the emperor as possessing divine light or radiance. This did not simply mean personal glory. It helped justify the emperor's authority as sacred and legitimate.

In paintings, halos around emperors visually expressed this idea.

## 14. Sulh-i Kul

Sulh-i kul means peace with all.

Under Akbar, it became a major principle of governance. It suggested that the emperor should not rule only for one religious group but should maintain harmony among all communities.

### Examples Linked to This Idea

1. Abolition of pilgrimage tax.
2. Abolition of jizya under Akbar.
3. Patronage and grants to different religious institutions.
4. Interfaith discussions at the ibadat khana.
5. Inclusion of different groups in imperial service.

## 15. Justice as an Ideal

Justice was presented as a central virtue of kingship. The emperor was expected to ensure order and protect subjects.

Mughal paintings used symbols such as the lion and lamb/goat peacefully together to represent a realm where the strong and weak could live in harmony.

Jahangir's chain of justice also symbolised the idea that subjects could appeal directly to the ruler.

## 16. The Mughal Court

The court was the visible centre of Mughal power.

It was where:

1. Nobles attended the emperor.
2. Rank and status were displayed.
3. Gifts were exchanged.
4. Political relationships were formed.
5. Imperial ceremonies were performed.
6. The emperor's authority was publicly staged.

Court etiquette mattered because small gestures showed hierarchy and loyalty.

## 17. Capitals and Imperial Space

Mughal capitals shifted over time.

| Capital/Place | Significance |
| --- | --- |
| Agra | Important early Mughal capital. |
| Fatehpur Sikri | Akbar's planned capital; linked with imperial vision and Sufi association. |
| Lahore | Important during campaigns and north-western concerns. |
| Shahjahanabad/Delhi | Shah Jahan's grand capital. |

Capital cities were not only administrative centres. They displayed imperial power through forts, palaces, mosques, markets, and ceremonial spaces.

## 18. Imperial Officials and Administration

The Mughal Empire depended on officials at central and provincial levels.

### Central Officials

| Official | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Wazir/Diwan | Finance and revenue administration. |
| Mir bakhshi | Military pay, ranks, and appointments. |
| Sadr-us-sudur | Grants and religious/legal appointments. |
| Qazi | Judicial function. |

### Provincial Administration

| Unit/Official | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Suba | Province. |
| Subadar | Provincial governor. |
| Diwan | Provincial finance officer. |
| Faujdar | Military commander in districts. |
| Pargana officers | Local revenue and legal administration. |
| Qanungo | Keeper of revenue records. |
| Chaudhuri | Revenue collection role. |

## 19. Mansabdari System

The mansabdari system ranked imperial officials and military commanders.

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Mansab | Rank or position. |
| Zat | Personal rank/status of the officer. |
| Sawar | Number of cavalrymen the officer was expected to maintain. |

This system linked status, salary, military obligation, and imperial service.

## 20. Nobility

The Mughal nobility was diverse. It included Turanis, Iranis, Afghans, Rajputs, Indian Muslims, and other groups.

This diversity helped the empire integrate different regions and warrior elites, but it also required careful balancing by the emperor.

## 21. Relations Beyond Frontiers

The Mughals interacted with other powers such as:

1. Safavids of Iran.
2. Uzbeks of Central Asia.
3. Ottomans.
4. Portuguese and Jesuit missionaries.

Qandahar was especially important because it lay between Mughal and Safavid zones of influence.

## 22. Jesuits at the Mughal Court

Jesuit missionaries visited Akbar's court and recorded their impressions. Their accounts are useful sources, but they must be read critically because they interpreted Akbar's interest in Christianity through their own hopes and expectations.

Akbar invited discussions among Muslims, Hindus, Jainas, Parsis, Christians, and others. These debates reflected his interest in religious and philosophical dialogue.

## 23. How to Use Sources in This Chapter

When analysing a source, ask:

1. Who wrote or created it?
2. Was the creator connected to the court?
3. What was the purpose?
4. What does it reveal?
5. What does it hide or exaggerate?
6. How does it support imperial ideology?

## 24. High-Yield Comparisons

### Chronicle vs Modern History

| Chronicle | Modern History |
| --- | --- |
| Often written by court insiders. | Written through critical study of multiple sources. |
| May glorify ruler. | Tries to evaluate evidence critically. |
| Focuses on emperor and court. | May include ordinary people, economy, gender, regions, and resistance. |
| Preserves valuable details. | Interprets details in wider context. |

### Written Text vs Painting

| Written Chronicle | Painting |
| --- | --- |
| Gives narrative and explanation. | Gives visual representation. |
| Records events, policies, ranks. | Shows hierarchy, symbols, gestures, and splendour. |
| Can explain ideology directly. | Communicates ideology through image and composition. |

## 25. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Treating chronicles as fully objective | They are valuable but court-centred and ideological. |
| Forgetting Persian's importance | Persian was central to Mughal administration and culture. |
| Ignoring manuscript production | Manuscripts were collaborative works of many specialists. |
| Thinking paintings were only decorative | They communicated power, justice, and imperial ideas. |
| Confusing Akbar Nama and Ain-i Akbari | Ain-i Akbari is the third book of Akbar Nama. |
| Treating sulh-i kul as only religious tolerance | It was also a principle of governance over a diverse empire. |

## 26. Exam Answer Frames

### Mughal Chronicles as Sources

Mughal chronicles are important sources because they record events, court rituals, administrative arrangements, imperial ideology, and the emperor's relationship with nobles and subjects. However, they were written by court historians and often glorified the ruler. Therefore, historians use them critically, comparing them with paintings, administrative records, regional sources, and other evidence.

### Production of Manuscripts

The production of Mughal manuscripts took place in the imperial kitabkhana. It involved paper makers, calligraphers, gilders, painters, binders, and supervisors. The manuscript was both a text and an art object. Its beauty reflected imperial patronage, while its content preserved and promoted the emperor's political vision.

### Sulh-i Kul

Sulh-i kul means peace with all. Under Akbar, it became a principle of governance that aimed to maintain harmony among different religious and social groups. It supported the inclusion of diverse communities in the empire and helped present the emperor as a just ruler above sectarian divisions.

## 29. Final Learning Takeaway

The Mughal Empire was built not only by armies and revenue systems but also by words, images, rituals, and symbols. Chronicles and paintings helped present the emperor as divinely favoured, just, powerful, and central to a diverse empire. To study them well, treat them both as sources of information and as instruments of imperial ideology.
