# Chapter 1: Through the Eyes of Travellers
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies medieval India through accounts of travellers such as Al-Biruni, Ibn Battuta, and Francois Bernier. Travellers observed society, politics, religion, cities, trade, caste, gender, and everyday life. Their writings are valuable, but they must be read critically because travellers brought their own assumptions, purposes, and comparisons.

The chapter's key skill is perspective analysis: what did a traveller notice, why did he notice it, and what might he misunderstand?

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- identify major travellers and their contexts;
- explain why travellers came to India;
- describe Al-Biruni's method and observations;
- describe Ibn Battuta's account of travel, cities, and communication;
- describe Bernier's comparison between India and Europe;
- explain travellers' views on caste, women, cities, and state;
- evaluate strengths and limitations of travel accounts;
- write answers that avoid treating travellers as neutral cameras.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Travel account | Written description by a traveller about places and people. |
| Ethnography | Description of people's customs, society, and culture. |
| Comparative method | Understanding one society by comparing it with another. |

# Unit 1A: Why Travel Accounts Matter

Travel accounts help historians understand how outsiders viewed India. They describe things that local texts may ignore because they seem ordinary to insiders.

They can reveal:

- urban life;
- social customs;
- trade routes;
- court culture;
- religious practices;
- gender norms;
- communication systems;
- comparisons across cultures.

But they also contain bias, exaggeration, and misunderstanding.

# Unit 1B: Al-Biruni

Al-Biruni came from Central Asia and wrote about India in the 11th century. He was a scholar of mathematics, astronomy, languages, and comparative religion.

He learned Sanskrit and studied Indian texts. His work tried to explain Indian society and thought to readers outside India.

## Al-Biruni's Method

He:

- studied Sanskrit texts;
- compared Indian ideas with Greek and Islamic thought;
- tried to understand categories used by Indians;
- noted differences in language, religion, and custom.

His method was scholarly, but he still viewed India through comparative distance.

# Unit 1C: Al-Biruni on Caste

Al-Biruni wrote about caste and social separation. He found caste exclusiveness striking.

His account is useful because it records social hierarchy, but it may overstate rigidity by relying heavily on Brahmanical texts and elite explanations.

Historians compare his account with inscriptions, regional practices, and other evidence.

# Unit 1D: Ibn Battuta

Ibn Battuta was a Moroccan traveller who visited India in the 14th century during the Delhi Sultanate. He travelled widely and served for a time in the court of Muhammad bin Tughlaq.

His account describes:

- long-distance travel;
- roads and dangers;
- cities;
- markets;
- court life;
- postal communication;
- social customs.

# Unit 1E: Ibn Battuta on Cities and Communication

Ibn Battuta was impressed by Indian cities, especially their size, markets, and prosperity.

He also described the postal system, including horse relays and runners. This shows the importance of communication for administration and trade.

His account highlights India as connected, mobile, and commercially active.

# Unit 1F: Ibn Battuta on Travel Risks

Travel was exciting but dangerous. Travellers faced:

- robbers;
- harsh terrain;
- disease;
- political conflict;
- unfamiliar languages;
- dependence on patrons.

Caravans, inns, and state protection helped long-distance travel.

# Unit 1G: Francois Bernier

Francois Bernier was a French traveller and physician who visited Mughal India in the 17th century. He compared India with Europe, often criticising Mughal institutions.

He wrote about:

- Mughal court;
- land revenue;
- peasants;
- cities;
- artisans;
- social inequality;
- imperial power.

# Unit 1H: Bernier's Interpretation

Bernier argued that lack of private property in land harmed agriculture and society. He compared Mughal India unfavourably with Europe.

Historians read Bernier carefully because:

- he had European assumptions;
- he wrote for European readers;
- he sometimes generalised from limited observation;
- he misunderstood some Indian institutions.

Still, his account is valuable for studying Mughal society and European perceptions.

# Unit 1I: Travellers on Women

Travellers wrote about practices such as seclusion, labour, slavery, and elite women. Their observations varied by region and class.

Important caution:

Women were not one uniform group. Elite women, rural women, enslaved women, artisans, and workers had different lives.

Traveller accounts often noticed unusual or elite practices more than ordinary routines.

# Unit 1J: Comparing Travellers

| Aspect | Al-Biruni | Ibn Battuta | Bernier |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main identity | Scholar | Traveller and court official | Physician and political observer |
| Method | Textual and comparative | Experience-based travel narrative | Comparative political critique |
| Main interest | Religion, knowledge, caste | Cities, routes, court, customs | State, land, society |
| Limitation | Elite textual dependence | Wonder and exaggeration possible | European bias |

# Unit 1K: Using Travel Accounts Critically

Ask these questions:

1. Who wrote the account?
2. Why was it written?
3. Who was the audience?
4. What did the traveller directly observe?
5. What did he hear from others?
6. What assumptions shaped his judgement?
7. What other evidence can confirm or challenge it?

# Unit 1L: Answer-Writing Framework

For travel accounts:

1. Introduce travel accounts as historical evidence.
2. Give examples of travellers.
3. Explain what they reveal.
4. Add limitations and bias.
5. Conclude with critical use.

For comparing travellers:

1. State their background.
2. Mention period and region.
3. Explain main observations.
4. Compare methods.
5. Mention limitations.

# Unit 1M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating travellers as fully objective | They had assumptions and audiences. |
| Mixing up travellers' centuries | Al-Biruni, Ibn Battuta, and Bernier belong to different periods. |
| Ignoring comparison | Travellers often compared India with their own societies. |
| Using one traveller to describe all India | Their observations were limited by route, class, and access. |
| Ignoring local evidence | Travel accounts need comparison with other evidence. |

## Final Revision

- Traveller accounts reveal how outsiders viewed India.
- Al-Biruni was scholarly and text-focused.
- Ibn Battuta described travel, cities, and court life.

- Travel accounts are valuable but biased.
- Historians must compare them with other evidence.
