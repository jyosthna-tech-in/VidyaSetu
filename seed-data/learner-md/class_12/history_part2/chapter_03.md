# Chapter 3: An Imperial Capital - Vijayanagara
### NCERT Class 12 - History Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter studies Vijayanagara, one of the most important imperial cities of medieval South India. The city, located near present-day Hampi, is studied through ruins, inscriptions, travellers' accounts, architecture, and landscape features.

Vijayanagara was not only a political capital. It was a sacred, military, commercial, and ceremonial centre shaped by rulers, traders, temples, water systems, and urban planning.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain the rise of Vijayanagara;
- describe its location and strategic advantages;
- understand the role of rulers, nayakas, and temples;
- describe the sacred centre and royal centre;
- explain water management and fortifications;
- analyse Mahanavami Dibba and ceremonial spaces;
- understand how historians reconstruct the city from ruins;
- write answers linking architecture, power, and ritual.

# Unit 3A: Location and Landscape

Vijayanagara was located in a rocky landscape near the Tungabhadra River. The terrain offered natural defence. The river and tanks supported water supply.

Location advantages:

- rocky hills for defence;
- river water;
- fertile zones nearby;
- trade route connections;
- sacred associations with older traditions.

# Unit 3B: Rise of the Empire

The Vijayanagara Empire emerged in the 14th century. It became a powerful kingdom in South India and competed with neighbouring states, including the Bahmani Sultanate and later Deccan Sultanates.

Its rulers controlled resources through military power, alliances, tribute, and regional chiefs.

# Unit 3C: Political Structure

The empire included central authority and local power holders.

Important groups:

- rayas;
- nayakas;
- military chiefs;
- temple authorities;
- merchants;
- agricultural communities.

Nayakas controlled territories and armies, but their loyalty could shift. This made the empire strong but also politically complex.

# Unit 3D: Fortifications

Vijayanagara had multiple lines of fortification. Walls enclosed not only the urban core but also agricultural fields, water structures, and settlements.

This shows that defence included protection of food supply and people, not only palace areas.

# Unit 3E: Water Management

The region had seasonal rainfall, so water management was essential.

Features:

- tanks;
- canals;
- channels;
- reservoirs;
- use of Tungabhadra River;
- water storage for agriculture and city life.

Water systems show engineering skill and adaptation to environment.

# Unit 3F: Sacred Centre

The sacred centre included important temples and religious landscapes.

Temples were not only worship spaces. They were also:

- landholders;
- employers;
- centres of festivals;
- artistic patrons;
- economic institutions;
- symbols of royal legitimacy.

The Virupaksha temple is an important example.

# Unit 3G: Royal Centre

The royal centre contained structures associated with rulers, ceremonies, and elite life.

Important features:

- audience halls;
- platforms;
- enclosures;
- palatial remains;
- ceremonial spaces;
- water structures.

Because many buildings were made partly of perishable materials, only stone foundations and structures remain in many cases.

# Unit 3H: Mahanavami Dibba

Mahanavami Dibba is a large ceremonial platform. It is associated with royal rituals, processions, and public display of power.

It shows how kingship was performed through:

- ceremonies;
- military display;
- gifts;
- processions;
- ritual authority;
- visibility before elites and subjects.

# Unit 3I: Bazaars and Trade

Vijayanagara was known for markets and trade. Travellers described wealth, horses, gems, textiles, spices, and busy bazaars.

Trade linked the city with:

- local agricultural areas;
- coastal ports;
- horse trade networks;
- merchants from different regions.

Urban prosperity depended on both political power and commercial exchange.

# Unit 3J: Architecture and Power

Architecture expressed authority.

Examples:

- massive walls showed strength;
- temples linked kings with sacred power;
- ceremonial platforms displayed sovereignty;
- planned spaces organised movement and hierarchy.

Buildings were political statements, not only functional structures.

# Unit 3K: Destruction and Rediscovery

The city suffered major destruction after the Battle of Talikota in 1565, though the empire continued in different forms for some time.

Later, the ruins were studied by surveyors, archaeologists, epigraphists, and historians.

Reconstructing Vijayanagara requires combining:

- ruins;
- inscriptions;
- maps;
- travellers' accounts;
- local traditions;
- architectural study.

# Unit 3M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Treating Vijayanagara only as ruins | It was a living capital with trade, ritual, and politics. |
| Ignoring landscape | Rocky terrain and river shaped the city. |
| Saying temples were only religious | They were also economic and political institutions. |
| Treating fort walls as palace walls only | They enclosed fields and settlements too. |
| Ignoring evidence limits | Many structures survive only partly. |
