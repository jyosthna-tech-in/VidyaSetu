# Unit 10A: Biotechnology in Agriculture

Agriculture faces challenges such as pests, diseases, limited land, environmental stress, and nutrient deficiency. Biotechnology can help by introducing useful traits.

## Possible Benefits

1. Pest resistance.
2. Reduced pesticide use.
3. Improved nutritional quality.
4. Tolerance to environmental stress.
5. Higher yield potential.
6. Better post-harvest quality.

## Caution

Biotechnology is not a replacement for good farming practices. It must be used with ecological awareness, regulation, and farmer education.

# Unit 10B: Genetically Modified Crops

Genetically modified crops carry introduced genes that provide desired traits.

## Desired Traits

| Trait | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Insect resistance | Reduce crop loss and pesticide use. |
| Herbicide tolerance | Help weed management. |
| Disease resistance | Reduce pathogen damage. |
| Delayed ripening | Improve shelf life. |
| Nutritional enhancement | Improve food quality. |
| Stress tolerance | Help survival under drought, salinity, or temperature stress. |

# Unit 10C: Bt Crops

Bt crops contain genes from Bacillus thuringiensis. These genes code for insecticidal proteins.

## How Bt Toxin Works

1. Bt toxin is produced as an inactive protoxin.
2. Insect larva eats plant tissue containing protoxin.
3. Alkaline gut of insect activates the toxin.
4. Activated toxin binds to gut epithelial cells.
5. Pores form in gut cells.
6. Insect stops feeding and dies.

## Examples

1. Bt cotton.
2. Bt corn.
3. Bt brinjal in some regulatory contexts.

## Specificity

Different Cry proteins target different insect groups. This specificity is useful because it can reduce harm to non-target organisms when properly managed.

# Unit 10D: RNA Interference

RNA interference is a gene silencing mechanism.

## Principle

Double-stranded RNA triggers degradation or blocking of complementary mRNA. If mRNA is destroyed, protein is not produced.

## Use in Pest Resistance

If a plant produces RNA matching an essential pest gene, the pest may ingest it. The RNA silences that gene in pest, reducing pest survival or reproduction.

# Unit 10E: Biotechnology in Medicine

Biotechnology helps produce medicines that are pure, specific, and scalable.

## Applications

1. Recombinant insulin.
2. Growth hormone.
3. Vaccines.
4. Monoclonal antibodies.
5. Gene therapy.
6. Molecular diagnosis.

# Unit 10F: Recombinant Insulin

Insulin is a hormone used to treat diabetes. Earlier, insulin was extracted from animals. Recombinant DNA technology allows production of human insulin in microbes.

## Human Insulin Structure

Insulin has A and B chains linked by disulfide bonds.

## Production Logic

1. DNA sequences for A and B chains are prepared.
2. They are inserted into microbial cells.
3. Microbes produce chains.
4. Chains are purified.
5. Chains are combined to form active insulin.

## Advantage

Recombinant insulin is human-compatible and can be produced in large quantities.

# Unit 10G: Gene Therapy

Gene therapy attempts to treat disease by correcting or compensating for defective gene function.

## Basic Steps

1. Identify defective gene.
2. Prepare functional gene copy or corrective approach.
3. Deliver it into target cells.
4. Allow expression or correction.
5. Monitor effect and safety.

## Example Principle

For a disorder caused by lack of functional enzyme, gene therapy may aim to introduce a functional gene so cells produce the enzyme.

## Limitations

1. Delivery to correct cells is difficult.
2. Long-term expression may be challenging.
3. Immune response can occur.
4. Ethical and safety review is essential.

# Unit 10H: Molecular Diagnosis

Molecular diagnosis detects disease at DNA, RNA, or protein level.

## Why It Is Useful

1. Early detection.
2. High specificity.
3. Detection before symptoms in some cases.
4. Identification of pathogen or mutation.

## PCR

PCR amplifies small amounts of DNA or RNA-derived DNA, making detection possible even when pathogen amount is low.

## DNA or RNA Probes

Probes are labelled sequences that bind complementary sequences. If binding occurs, target sequence is present.

## ELISA

ELISA detects antigens or antibodies using enzyme-linked reactions. It is widely used in diagnosis of infections and immune responses.

# Unit 10I: Transgenic Animals

Transgenic animals carry foreign genes.

## Uses

1. Study gene function.
2. Model human diseases.
3. Test vaccine safety.
4. Test drug effects.
5. Produce biological products.
6. Study normal physiology and development.

## Examples of Disease Models

Transgenic animals can model cancer, cystic fibrosis, rheumatoid arthritis, and other disorders, helping researchers study disease mechanisms.

# Unit 10J: Ethical Issues

Biotechnology raises ethical questions.

## Concerns

1. Environmental effects of GM organisms.
2. Effect on non-target organisms.
3. Gene flow to wild relatives.
4. Food safety.
5. Allergies or toxicity.
6. Farmer dependence and patents.
7. Animal welfare.
8. Human genetic intervention ethics.

## Biosafety

Biosafety includes risk assessment, controlled testing, monitoring, and regulation before release or use.

# Unit 10K: Regulation

Regulatory bodies review genetically modified organisms and biotechnology products for safety and responsible use.

They consider:

1. Environmental safety.
2. Food and feed safety.
3. Human health.
4. Labelling and traceability.
5. Field trial data.
6. Ethical guidelines.

# Unit 10L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Bt toxin is active inside bacteria exactly as in insect | It is produced as inactive protoxin and activated in insect gut. |
| All GM crops are the same | Traits differ by inserted gene and purpose. |
| RNA interference changes the DNA sequence | It silences gene expression at RNA level. |
| Recombinant insulin is extracted from animals | It is produced using genetically engineered cells. |
| Gene therapy is simple permanent cure for all genetic diseases | It is complex, disease-specific, and needs safety review. |
| Molecular diagnosis always detects symptoms | It detects molecular markers; symptoms may or may not be present. |
