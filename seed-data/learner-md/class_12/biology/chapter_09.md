# Unit 9A: Meaning of Biotechnology

Biotechnology broadly means using living organisms or their components to make useful products.

Traditional biotechnology includes:

1. Curd making.
2. Bread making.
3. Fermentation.
4. Cheese production.

Modern biotechnology includes:

1. Recombinant DNA technology.
2. Genetic engineering.
3. Tissue culture.
4. Production of therapeutic proteins.

# Unit 9B: Principles of Modern Biotechnology

Modern biotechnology mainly depends on:

1. Genetic engineering.
2. Maintenance of sterile conditions for growth of desired cells.

Genetic engineering changes the genetic material of an organism by introducing, removing, or modifying DNA.

## Two Core Abilities

| Ability | Meaning |
|---|---|
| DNA manipulation | Cut and join DNA precisely. |
| DNA transfer | Insert recombinant DNA into host cell. |

# Unit 9C: Recombinant DNA Technology

Recombinant DNA technology creates new DNA combinations.

## Basic Steps

1. Identify gene of interest.
2. Isolate DNA.
3. Cut DNA with restriction enzyme.
4. Cut vector with same enzyme.
5. Join gene and vector using ligase.
6. Introduce recombinant vector into host.
7. Select transformed host cells.
8. Multiply host cells.
9. Express desired gene.
10. Purify product.

# Unit 9D: Restriction Enzymes

Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific recognition sequences.

## Recognition Sequence

Many restriction enzymes recognise palindromic DNA sequences. A palindromic sequence reads the same in both strands when read in the same direction.

## Sticky Ends

Some enzymes cut DNA unevenly, producing sticky ends. Sticky ends help foreign DNA and vector DNA join because their bases can pair.

## Blunt Ends

Some enzymes cut straight across, producing blunt ends.

## Role in Recombinant DNA

If foreign DNA and vector are cut with the same restriction enzyme, they have compatible ends and can be joined by DNA ligase.

# Unit 9E: DNA Ligase

DNA ligase joins DNA fragments by forming phosphodiester bonds.

In recombinant DNA work, ligase seals the gene of interest into the vector.

# Unit 9F: Cloning Vectors

A vector carries foreign DNA into host cells.

## Features of a Good Vector

| Feature | Importance |
|---|---|
| Origin of replication | Allows vector to replicate in host. |
| Selectable marker | Helps identify transformed cells. |
| Cloning site | Place where foreign DNA can be inserted. |
| Small size | Easier to handle and transfer. |
| High copy number | Produces many copies of inserted DNA. |

## Plasmids

Plasmids are common vectors in bacteria. They are small circular DNA molecules separate from bacterial chromosome.

## Bacteriophages

Bacteriophages can also be used as vectors because they infect bacteria and can deliver DNA.

# Unit 9G: Selectable Markers

Selectable markers help identify cells that have taken up the vector.

Example: Antibiotic resistance genes. If vector carries resistance gene, transformed cells survive on medium with that antibiotic.

## Insertional Inactivation

If foreign DNA is inserted within a marker gene, it may disrupt that gene. This helps distinguish recombinant colonies from non-recombinant colonies.

# Unit 9H: Gel Electrophoresis

Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size.

## Principle

DNA is negatively charged, so it moves toward positive electrode in an electric field.

Smaller fragments move faster and farther through gel.

## Steps

1. Load DNA fragments into wells.
2. Apply electric field.
3. DNA fragments separate by size.
4. Stain or visualise DNA bands.
5. Cut desired band if needed.

# Unit 9I: PCR

PCR amplifies DNA, making millions of copies of a specific segment.

## Requirements

1. Template DNA.
2. Primers.
3. DNA polymerase.
4. Nucleotides.
5. Buffer and suitable conditions.

## Steps

| Step | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Denaturation | DNA strands separate at high temperature. |
| Annealing | Primers bind to complementary sequences. |
| Extension | DNA polymerase extends primers to make new DNA. |

Heat-stable DNA polymerase is used because PCR involves repeated heating.

# Unit 9J: Introducing DNA into Host Cells

Foreign DNA must enter host cells to replicate or express.

## Methods

1. Chemical treatment of bacterial cells.
2. Microinjection into animal cells.
3. Gene gun for plant cells.
4. Disarmed pathogen-based vectors.
5. Electroporation, where electric pulse increases membrane permeability.

# Unit 9K: Obtaining the Foreign Gene Product

After transformation, host cells are grown so the inserted gene can express.

If the gene codes for a protein, the host may produce that protein.

## Culture Conditions

The culture must provide:

1. Nutrients.
2. Correct temperature.
3. Correct pH.
4. Oxygen level if required.
5. Sterile conditions.

# Unit 9L: Bioreactors

Bioreactors are vessels used to grow cells in controlled conditions at large scale.

## Stirred-Tank Bioreactor

It maintains:

1. Mixing.
2. Aeration.
3. Temperature.
4. pH.
5. Nutrient supply.
6. Foam control.

## Why Bioreactors Are Needed

Laboratory cultures are too small for commercial production. Bioreactors allow large-scale growth and product formation.

# Unit 9M: Downstream Processing

Downstream processing includes all steps after product formation.

## Steps

1. Separation.
2. Purification.
3. Concentration.
4. Formulation.
5. Quality testing.
6. Packaging.

The product must be safe, pure, stable, and effective before use.

# Unit 9O: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Restriction enzymes join DNA | They cut DNA; ligase joins DNA. |
| Any vector can be used | A vector needs origin, marker, and cloning site. |
| PCR needs living cells | PCR can amplify DNA in vitro. |
| Larger DNA fragments move farther in gel | Smaller fragments move farther. |
| Transformed cells are always recombinant | Some may carry vector without insert; selection is needed. |
| Product is ready immediately after cell growth | Downstream processing is required. |
