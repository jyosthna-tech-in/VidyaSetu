# Unit 4A: Mendel's Experimental Approach

Gregor Mendel used garden pea because it had clear contrasting traits, short life cycle, many seeds, and controllable pollination.

## Why Pea Plant Was Useful

1. Many true-breeding varieties.
2. Contrasting traits such as tall/dwarf and round/wrinkled seeds.
3. Self-pollination possible.
4. Cross-pollination can be controlled.
5. Large number of offspring.

## Mendel's Method

1. He selected true-breeding parents.
2. He crossed parents with contrasting traits.
3. He observed F1 generation.
4. He selfed F1 plants to obtain F2 generation.
5. He counted offspring and expressed results as ratios.

# Unit 4B: Basic Genetic Vocabulary

| Concept | Example |
|---|---|
| Trait | Plant height. |
| Character | Tallness or dwarfness as expression of height. |
| Alleles | T and t for plant height. |
| Homozygous dominant | TT. |
| Homozygous recessive | tt. |
| Heterozygous | Tt. |
| Phenotype | Tall plant. |
| Genotype | TT or Tt. |

## Dominance

In simple dominance, one allele expresses itself in heterozygous condition. In Tt pea plants, tallness appears because T is dominant over t.

# Unit 4C: Monohybrid Cross

A monohybrid cross studies inheritance of one trait.

Example:

Parents: TT x tt

Gametes: T and t

F1: all Tt, all tall.

Selfing F1:

Tt x Tt

| Gametes | T | t |
|---|---|---|
| T | TT | Tt |
| t | Tt | tt |

## F2 Ratios

| Type | Ratio |
|---|---|
| Genotypic ratio | 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt |
| Phenotypic ratio | 3 tall : 1 dwarf |

# Unit 4D: Law of Segregation

Law of segregation states that the two alleles of a gene separate during gamete formation, so each gamete receives only one allele.

This law is also called purity of gametes.

## Why It Matters

It explains how recessive traits disappear in F1 but reappear in F2.

# Unit 4E: Test Cross

A test cross determines whether a dominant-looking individual is homozygous or heterozygous.

It is crossed with homozygous recessive individual.

## Example

If tall plant is TT:

TT x tt -> all Tt, all tall.

If tall plant is Tt:

Tt x tt -> 1 tall : 1 dwarf.

# Unit 4F: Dihybrid Cross

A dihybrid cross studies two traits together.

Example:

Round yellow seeds x wrinkled green seeds.

RRYY x rryy -> F1 all RrYy, round yellow.

Selfing F1 gives F2 phenotypic ratio:

9 round yellow : 3 round green : 3 wrinkled yellow : 1 wrinkled green.

# Unit 4G: Law of Independent Assortment

This law states that alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation if genes are on different chromosomes or far apart on the same chromosome.

It explains the 9:3:3:1 ratio in a classic dihybrid cross.

## Limitation

Genes located close together on the same chromosome may show linkage and may not assort independently.

# Unit 4H: Incomplete Dominance

In incomplete dominance, heterozygous phenotype is intermediate between two homozygous phenotypes.

Example:

Red flower x white flower -> pink flower in F1.

F2 ratio often becomes:

1 red : 2 pink : 1 white.

Here phenotypic ratio equals genotypic ratio.

# Unit 4I: Codominance and Multiple Alleles

In codominance, both alleles express fully in heterozygous condition.

## ABO Blood Group

ABO blood group is controlled by three alleles: IA, IB, and i.

IA and IB are codominant. Both are dominant over i.

| Genotype | Blood Group |
|---|---|
| IAIA or IAi | A |
| IBIB or IBi | B |
| IAIB | AB |
| ii | O |

This is also an example of multiple alleles because more than two alleles exist in the population, though one individual carries only two.

# Unit 4J: Pleiotropy and Polygenic Inheritance

## Pleiotropy

One gene affects multiple traits.

Example: A gene affecting haemoglobin may influence red blood cell shape and oxygen transport.

## Polygenic Inheritance

Many genes together influence one trait.

Examples:

1. Human skin colour.
2. Human height.
3. Many quantitative traits.

Polygenic traits often show continuous variation.

# Unit 4K: Chromosomal Theory of Inheritance

The chromosomal theory states that genes are located on chromosomes and chromosomes segregate and assort during meiosis.

## Parallels Between Genes and Chromosomes

| Genes | Chromosomes |
|---|---|
| Occur in pairs. | Homologous chromosomes occur in pairs. |
| Alleles segregate during gamete formation. | Homologous chromosomes separate during meiosis. |
| Different genes may assort independently. | Different chromosome pairs assort independently. |

# Unit 4L: Linkage and Recombination

Linkage is the tendency of genes on the same chromosome to be inherited together.

Recombination produces new allele combinations through crossing over.

## Relationship

| Distance Between Genes | Linkage | Recombination |
|---|---|---|
| Close | Strong | Low |
| Far apart | Weak | Higher |

Recombination frequency helps in chromosome mapping.

# Unit 4M: Sex Determination

## Humans

Females are XX and males are XY.

Eggs carry X chromosome. Sperms carry either X or Y.

XX -> female.

XY -> male.

Therefore, male gamete determines sex of child in humans.

## Birds

In birds, males are ZZ and females are ZW. Female gamete determines sex.

## Honey Bees

Honey bees show haplodiploidy:

1. Fertilised eggs become diploid females.
2. Unfertilised eggs become haploid males.

# Unit 4N: Pedigree Analysis

Pedigree analysis studies inheritance of traits in families using symbols.

## Uses

1. Track genetic disorders.
2. Identify carriers.
3. Predict inheritance risk.
4. Study dominant, recessive, and sex-linked patterns.

## Pattern Clues

| Pattern | Clue |
|---|---|
| Autosomal dominant | Trait often appears in every generation. |
| Autosomal recessive | Trait may skip generations; affected child may have unaffected carrier parents. |
| X-linked recessive | More common in males; carrier females may pass it to sons. |

# Unit 4O: Genetic Disorders

## Haemophilia

Haemophilia is an X-linked recessive disorder in which blood clotting is defective. It is more common in males.

## Sickle-Cell Anaemia

Sickle-cell anaemia is caused by a point mutation affecting haemoglobin. Red blood cells may become sickle-shaped under low oxygen.

It is inherited as an autosomal recessive disorder.

## Phenylketonuria

An autosomal recessive disorder in which phenylalanine metabolism is affected, leading to harmful accumulation if untreated.

## Down Syndrome

Down syndrome results from trisomy of chromosome 21. Affected individuals have an extra copy of chromosome 21.

## Turner Syndrome

Turner syndrome occurs in females with only one X chromosome, represented as XO.

## Klinefelter Syndrome

Klinefelter syndrome occurs in males with XXY chromosome constitution.

# Unit 4P: Mutation

Mutation is a sudden heritable change in genetic material.

## Types

1. Gene mutation.
2. Chromosomal mutation.
3. Genomic mutation, such as change in chromosome number.

## Importance

Mutation creates variation. Some mutations are harmful, some neutral, and rarely some are beneficial in a particular environment.

# Unit 4Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Dominant means more common | Dominant means expressed in heterozygous condition, not necessarily common. |
| Recessive traits disappear permanently | Recessive alleles can be hidden in heterozygotes and reappear. |
| Phenotype always reveals genotype | Dominant phenotype may be homozygous or heterozygous. |
| Independent assortment applies to all genes | Linked genes may not assort independently. |
| Sex of child is determined by mother in humans | Sperm carrying X or Y determines sex in humans. |
| Mutation is always harmful | Effects may be harmful, neutral, or sometimes beneficial. |

Inheritance questions are solved by tracking alleles. First identify the inheritance pattern, then write parental genotypes, gametes, combinations, and ratios. Variation arises through recombination, mutation, and chromosome behaviour during meiosis.
