# Unit 11A: Organism and Environment

An organism lives in an environment made of biotic and abiotic factors.

## Abiotic Factors

1. Temperature.
2. Water.
3. Light.
4. Soil.
5. Salinity.
6. pH.
7. Humidity.

Each factor can affect survival, growth, reproduction, and distribution of organisms.

# Unit 11B: Responses to Abiotic Factors

Organisms respond to environmental change in different ways.

| Response | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Regulate | Maintain internal conditions despite external change. |
| Conform | Internal conditions change with external conditions. |
| Migrate | Move temporarily to favourable habitat. |
| Suspend | Enter dormant state during unfavourable conditions. |

## Examples

1. Birds migrate to avoid harsh seasons.
2. Some animals hibernate in winter.
3. Some animals aestivate in summer.
4. Seeds and spores remain dormant until conditions improve.

# Unit 11C: Adaptations

Adaptations are features that help organisms survive and reproduce in their environment.

## Desert Adaptations

1. Thick cuticle in plants.
2. Reduced leaves or spines.
3. CAM photosynthesis in some plants.
4. Water storage tissues.
5. Burrowing or nocturnal habits in animals.

## Aquatic Adaptations

1. Streamlined body in fish.
2. Gills for respiration.
3. Buoyancy adaptations.
4. Osmoregulation in freshwater and marine organisms.

## High Altitude Adaptations

At high altitude, low oxygen creates physiological stress. Humans may respond with increased breathing rate, increased red blood cell production, and acclimatisation.

# Unit 11D: Population Attributes

A population has attributes that an individual does not.

## Main Attributes

| Attribute | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Population density | Size of population per unit area or volume. |
| Birth rate | Number of births per unit population per unit time. |
| Death rate | Number of deaths per unit population per unit time. |
| Sex ratio | Proportion of males and females. |
| Age structure | Distribution of individuals among age groups. |

## Population Density Formula

Change in population size depends on births, deaths, immigration, and emigration.

Population change = Births + Immigration - Deaths - Emigration

# Unit 11E: Age Pyramids

Age pyramids show proportions of pre-reproductive, reproductive, and post-reproductive individuals.

| Shape | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Expanding | Large young population; growing population. |
| Stable | Balanced age groups; stable population. |
| Declining | Fewer young individuals; decreasing population. |

# Unit 11F: Population Growth

Population growth depends on resources and environmental resistance.

## Exponential Growth

Occurs when resources are unlimited.

Equation: dN/dt = rN

N is population size and r is intrinsic rate of natural increase.

## Logistic Growth

Occurs when resources are limited. Growth slows near carrying capacity.

Equation: dN/dt = rN((K - N)/K)

K is carrying capacity.

## Growth Curve

| Growth Type | Curve Shape |
|---|---|
| Exponential | J-shaped. |
| Logistic | S-shaped. |

# Unit 11G: Life History Variation

Organisms differ in life history strategies.

Some produce many small offspring with little care. Others produce fewer offspring with more care.

## Examples of Variation

1. Number of offspring.
2. Size of offspring.
3. Age at reproduction.
4. Frequency of reproduction.
5. Parental care.

Life history traits are shaped by natural selection.

# Unit 11H: Population Interactions

Species interact in different ways.

## Interaction Symbols

| Interaction | Species A | Species B |
|---|---|---|
| Mutualism | + | + |
| Competition | - | - |
| Predation | + | - |
| Parasitism | + | - |
| Commensalism | + | 0 |
| Amensalism | - | 0 |

+ means benefit, - means harm, and 0 means no major effect.

# Unit 11I: Predation

Predation occurs when one organism kills and eats another.

## Importance

1. Transfers energy.
2. Controls prey population.
3. Maintains species diversity.
4. Drives evolution of defence and hunting adaptations.

## Plant Defence Against Herbivory

1. Thorns.
2. Spines.
3. Toxic chemicals.
4. Bad taste.
5. Tough leaves.

# Unit 11J: Competition

Competition occurs when organisms use the same limited resource.

## Types

1. Intraspecific competition: within same species.
2. Interspecific competition: between different species.

## Competitive Exclusion

Two closely related species competing for the same limiting resource may not coexist indefinitely in the same niche.

## Resource Partitioning

Species may coexist by using resources differently, such as feeding at different times or in different places.

# Unit 11K: Parasitism

A parasite lives in or on host and derives nutrition at host's expense.

## Adaptations of Parasites

1. Attachment organs.
2. High reproductive capacity.
3. Loss of unnecessary organs.
4. Complex life cycles.

## Types

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Ectoparasite | Lives on body surface. |
| Endoparasite | Lives inside host body. |
| Brood parasite | Lays eggs in nest of another species. |

# Unit 11L: Mutualism and Commensalism

## Mutualism

Both species benefit.

Examples:

1. Lichens: algae and fungi.
2. Mycorrhiza: fungi and plant roots.
3. Pollination interactions.

## Commensalism

One species benefits and the other is not significantly affected.

Examples:

1. Orchid growing on mango tree.
2. Barnacles on whale.
3. Cattle egret near grazing cattle.

# Unit 11M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Population density always means number per area | It may also use biomass, percentage cover, or relative abundance. |
| Exponential growth is permanent in nature | Resources become limiting, so logistic growth is more realistic. |
| Carrying capacity is fixed forever | It can change with environment and resources. |
| Predation is always harmful for ecosystem | Predation can maintain balance and diversity. |
| Competition occurs only between different species | It can occur within same species too. |
| Commensalism and mutualism are the same | In mutualism both benefit; in commensalism one benefits and other is neutral. |
