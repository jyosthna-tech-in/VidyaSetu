## 1. Big Picture

Flowering plants reproduce sexually through flowers. The flower is not only an attractive structure; biologically, it is the site where male and female reproductive structures form, gametes develop, fertilisation occurs, and seeds and fruits begin their development.

The chapter moves through four major stages:

```text
Flower formation
-> pre-fertilisation structures and events
-> double fertilisation
-> post-fertilisation structures and events
```

The main learning skill is sequence. Most answers become easy when you can place each term in the correct order.

## 2. Core Flow of the Chapter

```text
Stamen forms microspores
-> microspores become pollen grains
-> pistil contains ovules
-> megaspore forms embryo sac
-> pollen reaches stigma by pollination
-> pollen tube grows through style
-> two male gametes enter embryo sac
-> syngamy forms zygote
-> triple fusion forms primary endosperm nucleus
-> embryo and endosperm develop
-> ovule becomes seed
-> ovary becomes fruit
```

## 3. Flower as a Reproductive Organ

The flower is the reproductive structure of angiosperms.

| Floral Part | Reproductive Role |
| --- | --- |
| Stamen | Male reproductive organ; produces pollen grains. |
| Anther | Part of stamen where microspores and pollen grains form. |
| Filament | Stalk that supports the anther. |
| Pistil/Carpel | Female reproductive organ. |
| Stigma | Receives pollen grains. |
| Style | Path through which pollen tube grows. |
| Ovary | Contains ovules. |
| Ovule | Contains embryo sac; later becomes seed. |

## 4. Stamen, Anther, and Microsporangium

### Structure of Stamen

A typical stamen has:

1. Filament.
2. Anther.

The anther is usually bilobed and each lobe has two thecae. A typical anther is therefore described as:

```text
bilobed + dithecous + tetrasporangiate
```

This means it has two lobes, two thecae, and four microsporangia.

### Wall Layers of Microsporangium

From outside to inside:

| Layer | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Epidermis | Protective outer layer. |
| Endothecium | Helps anther dehiscence. |
| Middle layers | Temporary nutritive/supportive layers. |
| Tapetum | Nourishes developing pollen grains and contributes materials for pollen wall formation. |

### Sporogenous Tissue

Cells inside the microsporangium form sporogenous tissue. These cells give rise to microspore mother cells.

## 5. Microsporogenesis

### Meaning

Microsporogenesis is the process by which microspore mother cells undergo meiosis to form haploid microspores.

### Flowchart

```text
Sporogenous tissue
-> microspore mother cell
-> meiosis
-> tetrad of four haploid microspores
-> individual microspores separate
-> pollen grains
```

### Important Point

The microspore mother cell is diploid. Microspores are haploid because meiosis reduces chromosome number.

## 6. Pollen Grain

Pollen grain represents the male gametophyte.

### Wall Layers

| Layer | Nature | Function |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Exine | Outer, tough layer made of sporopollenin | Protects pollen from harsh conditions. |
| Intine | Inner, thin layer | Forms pollen tube during germination. |

### Sporopollenin

Sporopollenin is one of the most resistant biological materials. It helps pollen survive heat, drying, acids, alkalis, and enzymes.

### Germ Pores

Germ pores are regions where exine is absent or thin. Pollen tube emerges through a germ pore.

### Two-Celled and Three-Celled Pollen

In many angiosperms, pollen is shed at the two-celled stage:

| Cell | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Vegetative cell | Larger cell; helps pollen tube growth. |
| Generative cell | Divides to form two male gametes. |

In some plants, pollen is shed at the three-celled stage, already containing two male gametes.

## 7. Pistil, Ovule, and Embryo Sac

### Structure of Pistil

| Part | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Stigma | Receives pollen. |
| Style | Provides passage for pollen tube. |
| Ovary | Contains ovules. |

### Types of Gynoecium

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Monocarpellary | One carpel/pistil. |
| Multicarpellary | More than one carpel. |
| Syncarpous | Carpels fused. |
| Apocarpous | Carpels free. |

## 8. Structure of Ovule

An ovule is also called a megasporangium.

| Part | Function |
| --- | --- |
| Funicle | Stalk attaching ovule to placenta. |
| Hilum | Junction between ovule and funicle. |
| Integuments | Protective layers around nucellus. |
| Micropyle | Small opening left by integuments; pollen tube usually enters here. |
| Nucellus | Nutritive tissue containing embryo sac. |
| Chalaza | Basal region opposite micropyle. |
| Embryo sac | Female gametophyte. |

## 9. Megasporogenesis

### Meaning

Megasporogenesis is the process by which the megaspore mother cell undergoes meiosis to form haploid megaspores.

### Flowchart

```text
Megaspore mother cell in nucellus
-> meiosis
-> four haploid megaspores
-> three degenerate
-> one functional megaspore
-> embryo sac
```

## 10. Development of Embryo Sac

In most flowering plants, the embryo sac develops from a single functional megaspore. This is called monosporic development.

### Nuclear Divisions

```text
Functional megaspore
-> 2-nucleate stage
-> 4-nucleate stage
-> 8-nucleate stage
-> mature embryo sac
```

### Mature Embryo Sac

A typical embryo sac is:

```text
7-celled and 8-nucleate
```

| Region | Cells/Nuclei | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Micropylar end | Egg apparatus: 1 egg cell + 2 synergids | Egg participates in syngamy; synergids guide pollen tube. |
| Centre | Central cell with 2 polar nuclei | Forms primary endosperm nucleus after triple fusion. |
| Chalazal end | 3 antipodal cells | Usually nutritive/supportive, often degenerate later. |

### Filiform Apparatus

Synergids have a special structure called filiform apparatus. It helps guide the pollen tube into the embryo sac.

## 11. Pollination

### Meaning

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma.

Pollination is necessary because male and female gametes in flowering plants are non-motile. Pollen must reach the stigma before fertilisation can occur.

## 12. Types of Pollination

| Type | Meaning | Genetic Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Autogamy | Pollen transfer from anther to stigma of the same flower. | Genetically self-pollination. |
| Geitonogamy | Pollen transfer from one flower to another flower on the same plant. | Functionally cross-pollination but genetically similar to self-pollination. |
| Xenogamy | Pollen transfer from one plant to another plant of the same species. | True cross-pollination; brings genetic variation. |

## 13. Chasmogamous and Cleistogamous Flowers

| Flower Type | Meaning | Pollination |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chasmogamous | Flowers open and expose anthers and stigma. | Can allow cross-pollination. |
| Cleistogamous | Flowers do not open. | Assured self-pollination; no cross-pollination. |

### Advantage of Cleistogamy

It ensures seed formation even when pollinators are absent.

### Disadvantage

It reduces genetic variation because it promotes self-pollination.

## 14. Agents of Pollination

### Abiotic Agents

| Agent | Adaptations |
| --- | --- |
| Wind | Light, non-sticky pollen; exposed stamens; large feathery stigma; many pollen grains. |
| Water | Pollen protected from wetting; occurs in some aquatic plants such as Vallisneria and seagrasses. |

### Biotic Agents

Animals such as bees, butterflies, moths, birds, bats, beetles, and other insects may pollinate flowers.

Animal-pollinated flowers often have:

1. Bright colours.
2. Fragrance.
3. Nectar.
4. Sticky pollen.
5. Floral rewards.

## 15. Outbreeding Devices

Plants often avoid excessive self-pollination because it can reduce genetic variation and may cause inbreeding depression.

Common outbreeding devices:

| Device | How It Prevents Selfing |
| --- | --- |
| Pollen release and stigma receptivity at different times | Same flower cannot self-pollinate easily. |
| Anther and stigma placed at different positions | Pollen does not easily reach stigma of same flower. |
| Self-incompatibility | Genetic mechanism rejects self-pollen. |
| Production of unisexual flowers | Male and female functions are separated. |

If male and female flowers are on different plants, self-pollination is prevented completely.

## 16. Pollen-Pistil Interaction

Pollen-pistil interaction includes all events from pollen landing on stigma to pollen tube entry into ovule.

### Flowchart

```text
Pollen lands on stigma
-> stigma recognises compatible or incompatible pollen
-> compatible pollen germinates
-> pollen tube grows through style
-> generative cell forms two male gametes if not already formed
-> pollen tube enters ovule through micropyle
-> pollen tube enters synergid
-> male gametes are released
```

### Importance

This interaction ensures that only compatible pollen can complete fertilisation.

## 17. Artificial Hybridisation

Artificial hybridisation is a crop improvement method in which desired pollen is used to fertilise selected female parent flowers.

### Steps

1. Select parents with desired traits.
2. Remove anthers from bisexual flower before pollen release. This is emasculation.
3. Cover the flower with a bag to prevent unwanted pollination. This is bagging.
4. Dust mature stigma with desired pollen.
5. Rebag the flower until fruit develops.

### If Female Parent Has Unisexual Female Flowers

Emasculation is not required because anthers are absent. Bagging is still required.

## 18. Double Fertilisation

Double fertilisation is a unique feature of angiosperms.

Two male gametes enter the embryo sac:

| Male Gamete | Fusion | Product |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First male gamete | Fuses with egg cell | Zygote (2n) |
| Second male gamete | Fuses with two polar nuclei | Primary endosperm nucleus (3n) |

### Syngamy

Fusion of one male gamete with egg cell is called syngamy. It forms the diploid zygote.

### Triple Fusion

Fusion of the second male gamete with two polar nuclei is called triple fusion because three haploid nuclei are involved.

### Double Fertilisation

Because syngamy and triple fusion occur in the same embryo sac, the process is called double fertilisation.

## 19. Post-Fertilisation Events

After fertilisation:

| Structure Before Fertilisation | Structure After Fertilisation |
| --- | --- |
| Zygote | Embryo |
| Primary endosperm nucleus | Endosperm |
| Ovule | Seed |
| Integuments | Seed coats |
| Ovary | Fruit |
| Ovary wall | Pericarp |

## 20. Endosperm

Endosperm is nutritive tissue that supports embryo development.

### Development

The primary endosperm nucleus divides repeatedly. In many plants, free nuclear division occurs first, followed by cell wall formation.

### Coconut Example

In coconut, the liquid endosperm contains free nuclei, while the white kernel is cellular endosperm.

## 21. Embryo

The zygote develops into an embryo after some endosperm has formed, so that nutrition is available.

### Dicot Embryo

| Part | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Two cotyledons | Store food or absorb food from endosperm. |
| Embryonal axis | Main axis of embryo. |
| Plumule | Future shoot. |
| Radicle | Future root. |

### Monocot Embryo

Monocot embryos have one cotyledon. In grasses, the single cotyledon is called scutellum.

## 22. Seed

A seed is a fertilised ovule.

### Parts

1. Seed coat.
2. Cotyledon or cotyledons.
3. Embryonal axis.
4. Stored food, if present.

### Albuminous and Non-Albuminous Seeds

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Albuminous | Endosperm remains in mature seed. | Wheat, maize, barley, castor. |
| Non-albuminous | Endosperm is consumed during embryo development. | Pea, groundnut. |

### Perisperm

Persistent nucellus in seed is called perisperm. Examples include black pepper and beet.

## 23. Fruit

Fruit develops from the ovary after fertilisation. The ovary wall becomes the pericarp.

### True Fruit and False Fruit

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| True fruit | Develops only from ovary. | Mango, tomato. |
| False fruit | Other floral parts also contribute. | Apple, strawberry. |

## 24. Seed Dormancy and Importance of Seeds

### Seed Dormancy

Seed dormancy is a temporary inactive state in which seeds do not germinate even under favourable conditions. It helps seeds survive unfavourable periods.

### Importance of Seeds

1. Protect embryo with seed coat.
2. Provide stored food for seedling.
3. Help dispersal to new habitats.
4. Allow survival during unfavourable conditions.
5. Generate genetic variation because they are products of sexual reproduction.
6. Form the basis of agriculture.

## 25. Apomixis

### Meaning

Apomixis is seed formation without fertilisation. It is a form of asexual reproduction that imitates sexual reproduction.

### Importance

Apomixis is important in hybrid seed production because it can preserve desirable hybrid traits generation after generation.

### Examples

Apomixis occurs in some members of Asteraceae and grasses. It is also seen in plants such as Citrus and mango through special embryo-forming mechanisms.

## 26. Polyembryony

Polyembryony is the occurrence of more than one embryo in a seed.

Example: Citrus often shows polyembryony.

## 27. High-Yield Comparisons

### Microsporogenesis vs Megasporogenesis

| Basis | Microsporogenesis | Megasporogenesis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Site | Microsporangium of anther | Nucellus of ovule |
| Mother cell | Microspore mother cell | Megaspore mother cell |
| Division | Meiosis | Meiosis |
| Product | Four microspores | Four megaspores |
| Functional cells | Usually all four may form pollen | Usually only one megaspore is functional |
| Gametophyte formed | Male gametophyte | Female gametophyte |

### Pollen Grain vs Embryo Sac

| Basis | Pollen Grain | Embryo Sac |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gametophyte | Male gametophyte | Female gametophyte |
| Location | Released from anther | Inside ovule |
| Gametes | Produces two male gametes | Contains egg cell |
| Transport | Reaches stigma by pollination | Remains inside ovule |

## 28. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Saying pollination is fertilisation | Pollination is pollen transfer; fertilisation is gamete fusion. |
| Confusing geitonogamy with true cross-pollination | Geitonogamy uses another flower but same plant, so genetically it resembles self-pollination. |
| Saying embryo sac is 8-celled | Typical embryo sac is 7-celled and 8-nucleate. |
| Forgetting triple fusion | Triple fusion forms primary endosperm nucleus. |
| Saying ovary becomes seed | Ovule becomes seed; ovary becomes fruit. |
| Treating apomixis as normal sexual reproduction | Apomixis forms seed without fertilisation. |

## 29. Exam Answer Frames

### Double Fertilisation

Double fertilisation is a characteristic feature of angiosperms. After pollination, the pollen tube enters the embryo sac and releases two male gametes. One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form the diploid zygote; this is syngamy. The other male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei to form the triploid primary endosperm nucleus; this is triple fusion. Since two fusions occur in the same embryo sac, the process is called double fertilisation.

### Pollination

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma. It may be autogamy, geitonogamy, or xenogamy depending on the source of pollen. Pollination is essential because male gametes are non-motile and must be brought near the female gametophyte for fertilisation.

### Embryo Sac

The typical angiosperm embryo sac is 7-celled and 8-nucleate. It has an egg apparatus at the micropylar end, three antipodal cells at the chalazal end, and a central cell with two polar nuclei. The egg participates in syngamy, synergids guide pollen tube entry, and the polar nuclei participate in triple fusion.

## 32. Final Learning Takeaway

Sexual reproduction in flowering plants is a coordinated sequence. The anther produces pollen, the ovule produces the embryo sac, pollination brings pollen to stigma, pollen tube delivers male gametes, double fertilisation creates both zygote and endosperm, and post-fertilisation changes convert ovule into seed and ovary into fruit. Master the sequence and the chapter becomes highly predictable.
