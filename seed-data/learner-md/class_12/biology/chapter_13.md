# Unit 13A: Meaning of Biodiversity

Biodiversity is the variety and variability of living organisms. It includes all plants, animals, microbes, genes, populations, communities, and ecosystems.

## Three Levels

| Level | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Genetic diversity | Variation within a species. | Different rice varieties. |
| Species diversity | Variety of species in an area. | More species in rainforest than a small plantation. |
| Ecosystem diversity | Variety of habitats and ecosystems. | Forests, deserts, wetlands, grasslands. |

# Unit 13B: Genetic Diversity

Genetic diversity helps species adapt to changing environments.

If a species has high genetic variation, some individuals may survive disease, climate stress, or environmental change.

## Importance

1. Supports adaptation.
2. Helps breeding programmes.
3. Maintains disease resistance.
4. Reduces risk from environmental change.

# Unit 13C: Species Diversity

Species diversity includes species richness and relative abundance.

An area with many species and balanced abundance has higher diversity than an area dominated by one species.

## Example

A tropical rainforest has more species diversity than a monoculture crop field.

# Unit 13D: Ecosystem Diversity

Ecosystem diversity refers to variety of ecosystems in a region.

India has high ecosystem diversity because it contains:

1. Himalayas.
2. Deserts.
3. Grasslands.
4. Wetlands.
5. Tropical forests.
6. Mangroves.
7. Coastal ecosystems.
8. Islands.

# Unit 13E: How Many Species Are There?

Only a fraction of Earth's species have been scientifically described. Many microbes, insects, fungi, and deep-sea organisms remain undocumented.

Species richness is not evenly distributed. Some groups such as insects are extremely diverse.

# Unit 13F: Patterns of Biodiversity

## Latitudinal Gradient

Species diversity generally increases from poles to tropics.

Possible reasons:

1. Tropical regions had longer evolutionary time.
2. Tropical climate is more stable.
3. More solar energy supports higher productivity.
4. Complex habitats create more niches.

## Species-Area Relationship

Larger areas usually contain more species.

Relationship: log S = log C + Z log A

S is species richness, A is area, C is constant, and Z is slope.

## Interpretation

As area increases, number of species increases, but not endlessly at same rate.

# Unit 13G: Importance of Species Diversity

Diverse ecosystems are often more stable and productive.

## Benefits

1. Better ecosystem stability.
2. More efficient resource use.
3. Resistance to invasion.
4. Greater resilience after disturbance.
5. Continuation of ecosystem services.

## Rivet Analogy

Species in an ecosystem can be compared to rivets in an airplane. Losing a few may not immediately collapse the system, but losing many, especially key species, can be dangerous.

# Unit 13H: Value of Biodiversity

Biodiversity has many values.

| Value | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direct economic value | Food, medicine, timber, fibre, fuel. |
| Indirect value | Pollination, climate regulation, water purification, nutrient cycling. |
| Ethical value | Other species have a right to exist. |
| Aesthetic value | Beauty, recreation, inspiration. |
| Scientific value | Research, education, genetic information. |

# Unit 13I: Loss of Biodiversity

Biodiversity loss means reduction in genetic variety, species number, or ecosystem variety.

## Consequences

1. Reduced ecosystem stability.
2. Loss of food and medicinal resources.
3. Reduced pollination.
4. Disturbed nutrient cycles.
5. Increased vulnerability to environmental change.
6. Loss of cultural and aesthetic value.

# Unit 13J: Causes of Biodiversity Loss

## Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

This is the most serious threat. Forests, wetlands, and grasslands are converted for agriculture, urbanisation, mining, roads, and industry.

Fragmentation divides large habitats into smaller patches, isolating populations.

## Over-Exploitation

Excessive hunting, fishing, logging, or harvesting can reduce populations and cause extinction.

## Alien Species Invasion

Alien species introduced intentionally or accidentally may become invasive and harm native species.

Examples:

1. Water hyacinth in water bodies.
2. Lantana in forests.
3. Nile perch introduction affecting cichlid fishes in Lake Victoria.

## Co-Extinctions

When one species becomes extinct, dependent species may also disappear.

Examples:

1. Host fish extinction may affect parasites.
2. Plant extinction may affect specific pollinators.

# Unit 13K: In Situ Conservation

In situ conservation protects species in their natural habitat.

## Examples

1. Biosphere reserves.
2. National parks.
3. Wildlife sanctuaries.
4. Reserved forests.
5. Sacred groves.

## Advantages

1. Protects entire ecosystem.
2. Allows natural evolution.
3. Conserves many species together.
4. Protects interactions among organisms.

# Unit 13L: Biodiversity Hotspots

Hotspots are regions with high endemism and high threat.

Conserving hotspots protects many unique species in limited areas.

## Criteria

A hotspot generally has:

1. High number of endemic species.
2. Significant habitat loss or threat.

# Unit 13M: Sacred Groves

Sacred groves are forest patches protected by local communities due to cultural or religious beliefs.

## Importance

1. Protect rare species.
2. Conserve local biodiversity.
3. Preserve water and soil.
4. Show community-based conservation.

# Unit 13N: Ex Situ Conservation

Ex situ conservation protects species outside natural habitat.

## Examples

1. Botanical gardens.
2. Zoological parks.
3. Seed banks.
4. Tissue culture collections.
5. Cryopreservation of gametes or embryos.

## When Useful

1. Species is critically endangered.
2. Natural habitat is severely damaged.
3. Breeding support is needed.
4. Seeds or genetic material need long-term storage.

# Unit 13O: In Situ vs Ex Situ Conservation

| Basis | In Situ | Ex Situ |
|---|---|---|
| Place | Natural habitat. | Outside natural habitat. |
| Protects | Ecosystem and interactions. | Selected species or genetic material. |
| Evolution | Natural processes continue. | More controlled conditions. |
| Examples | National parks, sanctuaries, sacred groves. | Zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks. |
| Best for | Whole habitat conservation. | Emergency rescue and controlled breeding. |

# Unit 13P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Biodiversity means only number of species | It includes genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity. |
| Hotspots are simply beautiful places | They are regions with high endemism and high threat. |
| Ex situ is always better | In situ protects natural interactions and ecosystems. |
| Alien species are always harmless | Some become invasive and damage native biodiversity. |
| Extinction affects only one species | Co-extinctions and ecosystem effects can follow. |
| Conservation is only for animals | It includes plants, microbes, genes, habitats, and ecosystems. |
