## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Ecosystem | Functional unit of organisms and physical environment interacting together. |
| Producer | Autotroph that converts inorganic substances into organic matter. |
| Consumer | Heterotroph that depends directly or indirectly on producers. |
| Decomposer | Organism that breaks down dead organic matter. |
| Productivity | Rate of biomass production. |
| GPP | Gross primary productivity, total organic matter produced by photosynthesis. |
| NPP | Net primary productivity, biomass available to consumers after plant respiration. |
| Decomposition | Breakdown of complex organic matter into simpler substances. |
| Food chain | Linear feeding relationship. |
| Food web | Network of interconnected food chains. |
| Trophic level | Feeding position in food chain. |
| Ecological pyramid | Graphical representation of number, biomass, or energy at trophic levels. |
| Succession | Gradual change in species composition over time. |

# Unit 12A: Ecosystem Structure

An ecosystem has biotic and abiotic components.

## Abiotic Components

1. Light.
2. Temperature.
3. Water.
4. Soil.
5. Air.
6. Minerals.
7. pH.

## Biotic Components

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| Producers | Make food through photosynthesis or chemosynthesis. |
| Consumers | Eat producers or other consumers. |
| Decomposers | Break down dead organic matter. |

# Unit 12B: Stratification

Stratification means vertical distribution of species in layers.

Example in forest:

1. Tall trees.
2. Shrubs.
3. Herbs.
4. Ground layer.
5. Soil organisms.

Stratification helps organisms use light, space, and resources differently.

# Unit 12C: Productivity

Productivity is rate of production of biomass.

## Gross Primary Productivity

GPP is total rate of organic matter production by producers during photosynthesis.

## Net Primary Productivity

NPP is biomass available to consumers.

NPP = GPP - Respiration loss

## Secondary Productivity

Secondary productivity is rate of biomass formation by consumers.

## Factors Affecting Productivity

1. Light.
2. Temperature.
3. Water.
4. Nutrients.
5. Photosynthetic capacity.
6. Type of ecosystem.

# Unit 12D: Decomposition

Decomposition is breakdown of dead organic matter, called detritus.

## Steps

| Step | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Fragmentation | Detritivores break detritus into smaller particles. |
| Leaching | Water-soluble nutrients move into soil. |
| Catabolism | Enzymes degrade complex organic matter. |
| Humification | Formation of dark, resistant humus. |
| Mineralisation | Release of inorganic nutrients. |

## Factors Affecting Decomposition

Decomposition is faster when:

1. Detritus is rich in nitrogen and sugars.
2. Temperature is warm.
3. Moisture is adequate.
4. Oxygen is available.

It is slower when detritus contains lignin and chitin or conditions are cold, dry, or anaerobic.

# Unit 12E: Energy Flow

Energy enters most ecosystems through sunlight captured by producers.

Energy flows:

Sun -> producers -> herbivores -> carnivores -> decomposers

Energy flow is unidirectional. It does not cycle because energy is lost as heat at each transfer.

## Ten Per Cent Law

Only about ten per cent of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next trophic level. The rest is used in metabolism or lost as heat.

# Unit 12F: Food Chains and Food Webs

## Grazing Food Chain

Starts with living green plants.

Example:

Grass -> deer -> tiger

## Detritus Food Chain

Starts with dead organic matter.

Example:

Dead leaves -> decomposers/detritivores -> predators

## Food Web

A food web is a network of food chains. It provides stability because organisms may have alternate food options.

# Unit 12G: Ecological Pyramids

Ecological pyramids represent trophic structure.

## Types

| Pyramid | Shows |
|---|---|
| Pyramid of number | Number of individuals at each trophic level. |
| Pyramid of biomass | Biomass at each trophic level. |
| Pyramid of energy | Energy flow at each trophic level. |

## Important Points

1. Pyramid of energy is always upright.
2. Pyramid of number may be upright or inverted.
3. Pyramid of biomass may be upright or inverted, especially in aquatic ecosystems.

## Limitation

Ecological pyramids often do not represent food webs well and usually ignore decomposers.

# Unit 12H: Ecological Succession

Succession is gradual and predictable change in community composition over time.

## Stages

1. Pioneer species colonise.
2. Soil or habitat conditions change.
3. New species replace earlier species.
4. Community becomes more complex.
5. A relatively stable climax community may develop.

## Primary Succession

Begins in lifeless areas with no soil, such as bare rock.

## Secondary Succession

Begins in areas where life existed before but was disturbed, such as abandoned fields or burned forests.

Secondary succession is faster because soil and some organisms may remain.

# Unit 12I: Succession on Bare Rock

Typical sequence:

1. Lichens.
2. Mosses.
3. Herbs.
4. Shrubs.
5. Forest.

Lichens help weather rock and create soil, allowing later plants to grow.

# Unit 12J: Succession in Water

Hydrarch succession begins in water and may gradually lead toward a terrestrial community.

Typical sequence:

1. Phytoplankton stage.
2. Submerged plant stage.
3. Floating plant stage.
4. Reed-swamp stage.
5. Marsh-meadow stage.
6. Shrub stage.
7. Forest stage.

# Unit 12K: Nutrient Cycling

Nutrients move between organisms and environment in biogeochemical cycles.

## Carbon Cycle

Carbon moves through:

1. Photosynthesis.
2. Feeding.
3. Respiration.
4. Decomposition.
5. Combustion.
6. Ocean-atmosphere exchange.

## Phosphorus Cycle

Phosphorus cycle is sedimentary. Main reservoir is rocks.

Phosphorus enters soil through weathering, moves into plants, passes through food chains, and returns through decomposition and excretion.

## Carbon vs Phosphorus

| Carbon Cycle | Phosphorus Cycle |
|---|---|
| Main reservoir includes atmosphere and oceans. | Main reservoir is rocks. |
| Has gaseous phase. | No major gaseous phase. |
| Strongly linked to photosynthesis and respiration. | Strongly linked to sediment and soil. |

# Unit 12L: Ecosystem Services

Ecosystems provide services that support life and human society.

Examples:

1. Oxygen production.
2. Pollination.
3. Soil formation.
4. Water purification.
5. Climate regulation.
6. Nutrient cycling.
7. Flood control.
8. Aesthetic and cultural value.

# Unit 12M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Energy cycles like nutrients | Energy flows one way; nutrients cycle. |
| GPP and NPP are same | NPP = GPP - respiration loss. |
| Decomposition only means rotting | It includes fragmentation, leaching, catabolism, humification, and mineralisation. |
| Pyramid of energy can be inverted | It is always upright. |
| Succession is random | It is directional and influenced by environment. |
| Secondary succession starts from bare rock | Primary succession starts from bare areas without soil. |

## Final Revision

An ecosystem is about function: producers capture energy, consumers transfer it, decomposers recycle nutrients, and communities change through succession. Energy flow and nutrient cycling are the two central threads of this chapter.
