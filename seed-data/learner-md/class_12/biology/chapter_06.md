# Unit 6A: Origin of Life

The origin of life is studied through ideas about chemical evolution. Early Earth had conditions different from today, with gases, high temperature, lightning, and volcanic activity.

## Chemical Evolution

Chemical evolution suggests:

1. Simple inorganic molecules formed organic molecules.
2. Organic molecules accumulated.
3. Larger molecules such as proteins and nucleic acids formed.
4. Molecular systems capable of self-replication and metabolism emerged.
5. Cellular life developed later.

## Important Point

Evolution of life is different from origin of life. Origin asks how life first appeared; evolution asks how life diversified after it appeared.

# Unit 6B: Evolution of Life Forms

Evolution means change in living forms over time. It explains why organisms show both unity and diversity.

All organisms share basic features such as cellular organisation, genetic material, and metabolism. Yet they differ greatly due to adaptation and descent with modification.

# Unit 6C: Evidences for Evolution

## Fossil Evidence

Fossils show organisms that lived in the past. Fossil layers help reconstruct sequence of life forms.

## Comparative Anatomy

Comparing organs reveals homology and analogy.

### Homologous Organs

Homologous organs have similar basic structure but may perform different functions.

Examples:

1. Forelimbs of human, whale, bat, and horse.
2. Thorn of Bougainvillea and tendril of Cucurbita as modified stems.

Homology indicates common ancestry and divergent evolution.

### Analogous Organs

Analogous organs perform similar function but have different origin and structure.

Examples:

1. Wings of bird and insect.
2. Flippers of penguin and dolphin.
3. Sweet potato root and potato stem as food storage structures.

Analogy indicates convergent evolution.

## Embryological Evidence

Similarities in early embryonic stages of related organisms suggest common ancestry, though interpretations must be careful and evidence-based.

## Molecular Evidence

DNA, RNA, and protein similarities show evolutionary relationships. Closely related organisms have more similar sequences.

# Unit 6D: Adaptive Radiation

Adaptive radiation is evolution of multiple species from a common ancestor into different ecological roles.

## Examples

1. Darwin's finches in Galapagos Islands.
2. Australian marsupials.

In adaptive radiation, different environments favour different traits.

# Unit 6E: Darwin's Natural Selection

Darwin proposed that natural selection drives adaptation.

## Main Points

1. Organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
2. Individuals vary.
3. Some variations are heritable.
4. There is struggle for existence.
5. Individuals with favourable traits survive and reproduce more.
6. Favourable traits become more common over generations.

## Fitness

Fitness means reproductive success, not physical strength alone. A trait is favourable if it helps an organism survive and reproduce in a particular environment.

# Unit 6F: Examples of Natural Selection

## Industrial Melanism

In polluted environments, darker moths were better camouflaged and survived more. In cleaner environments, lighter moths had advantage.

This shows selection depends on environment.

## Antibiotic Resistance

When bacteria are exposed to antibiotics, resistant variants survive and reproduce. Over time, resistant strains become common.

This shows evolution can occur rapidly in microorganisms.

# Unit 6G: Hardy-Weinberg Principle

Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele frequencies in a population remain constant from generation to generation if no evolutionary forces act.

This is genetic equilibrium.

## Conditions

1. No mutation.
2. No migration.
3. No natural selection.
4. Random mating.
5. Very large population.

## Equation

For two alleles p and q:

p + q = 1

p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1

Here p2 represents homozygous dominant frequency, 2pq heterozygous frequency, and q2 homozygous recessive frequency.

# Unit 6H: Factors Affecting Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Evolution occurs when equilibrium is disturbed.

## Factors

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Mutation | Creates new genetic variation. |
| Recombination | Creates new allele combinations. |
| Gene flow | Adds or removes alleles through migration. |
| Genetic drift | Random allele frequency change in small populations. |
| Natural selection | Favourable alleles become more common. |

# Unit 6I: Genetic Drift and Founder Effect

Genetic drift is random change in allele frequency. It is strongest in small populations.

Founder effect occurs when a small group separates and starts a new population. The new population may have allele frequencies different from original population.

## Importance

Genetic drift can reduce variation and may contribute to speciation.

# Unit 6J: Natural Selection Patterns

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Stabilising selection | Favours average phenotype. |
| Directional selection | Favours one extreme phenotype. |
| Disruptive selection | Favours both extremes over intermediate phenotype. |

# Unit 6K: Speciation

Speciation is formation of new species.

## Steps Often Involved

1. Population becomes separated.
2. Gene flow reduces or stops.
3. Mutation, recombination, drift, and selection create differences.
4. Reproductive isolation develops.
5. New species forms.

## Isolation Types

1. Geographical isolation.
2. Reproductive isolation.
3. Behavioural isolation.
4. Seasonal isolation.

# Unit 6L: Human Evolution

Human evolution is reconstructed from fossils, comparative anatomy, molecular data, and tools.

## Broad Trends

1. Increased brain size.
2. Bipedal locomotion.
3. Changes in jaw and teeth.
4. Tool use.
5. Development of culture and language.

## Important Point

Humans did not evolve from modern apes. Humans and modern apes share common ancestors.

# Unit 6M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Evolution means progress toward perfection | Evolution means change and adaptation, not guaranteed perfection. |
| Individuals evolve during life | Populations evolve over generations. |
| Acquired characters are always inherited | Heritable genetic changes matter in evolution. |
| Homologous means same function | Homologous means same basic origin; function may differ. |
| Analogous means common ancestry | Analogous organs show similar function, not necessarily common ancestry. |
| Fitness means physical strength | Fitness means reproductive success. |

## Final Revision

Evolution is best understood at population level. Variation arises, selection and other forces act, allele frequencies change, and over long periods new species may form. Evidence from fossils, anatomy, molecules, and biogeography supports this explanation.
