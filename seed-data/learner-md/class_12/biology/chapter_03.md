# Unit 3A: Meaning of Reproductive Health

Reproductive health is not limited to the absence of disease in reproductive organs. It includes a person's ability to have a responsible, satisfying, and safe reproductive life.

It includes:

1. Normal structure and function of reproductive organs.
2. Emotional maturity.
3. Responsible sexual behaviour.
4. Freedom from reproductive tract infections.
5. Access to accurate information.
6. Access to safe medical care.
7. Respect for individual dignity and informed choice.

## Why It Is a Social Issue

Reproduction affects families, population size, maternal health, child health, gender equality, and public health. Therefore, reproductive health programmes are not only medical programmes; they are also educational and social programmes.

# Unit 3B: Reproductive Health Problems and Strategies

Common reproductive health problems include:

1. Lack of accurate knowledge.
2. Myths and stigma around reproductive issues.
3. Early marriage and early pregnancy.
4. Maternal and infant mortality.
5. Sexually transmitted infections.
6. Unsafe abortions.
7. Infertility.
8. Population pressure.
9. Gender discrimination.

## Strategies

| Strategy | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Sex education | Build correct knowledge and remove myths. |
| Counselling | Help adolescents and couples make informed choices. |
| Maternal healthcare | Reduce pregnancy and childbirth risks. |
| Child immunisation | Improve child survival. |
| Contraceptive access | Support planned families. |
| STI diagnosis and treatment | Reduce infection spread and complications. |
| Legal safeguards | Prevent misuse of medical technologies and unsafe practices. |

# Unit 3C: Population Explosion

Population explosion means a rapid increase in population. It occurs when birth rate remains high while death rate falls due to better healthcare, nutrition, sanitation, and disease control.

## Causes

1. Decline in death rate.
2. Decline in maternal and infant mortality.
3. Increase in life expectancy.
4. Lack of contraceptive awareness.
5. Social preference for large families in some communities.
6. Early marriage.

## Consequences

| Area | Effect |
|---|---|
| Food | More demand and pressure on supply. |
| Housing | Overcrowding and urban stress. |
| Education | More pressure on schools. |
| Healthcare | Greater burden on hospitals and clinics. |
| Employment | Competition for jobs. |
| Environment | More resource use and waste. |

## Population Stabilisation

Population stabilisation aims to balance birth rate and death rate so population growth becomes manageable.

Important measures:

1. Raising marriage age.
2. Encouraging small family norms.
3. Increasing contraceptive awareness.
4. Improving female education.
5. Providing reproductive healthcare.
6. Reducing infant mortality so families do not feel pressure to have many children.

# Unit 3D: Birth Control and Contraception

An ideal contraceptive should be:

1. User-friendly.
2. Easily available.
3. Effective.
4. Reversible, where possible.
5. Affordable.
6. With minimal side effects.
7. Not interfering greatly with normal sexual life.

## Main Categories

| Method | Main Action |
|---|---|
| Natural methods | Avoid sperm-ovum meeting without devices or drugs. |
| Barrier methods | Physically block sperm. |
| IUDs | Create uterine conditions unfavourable for fertilisation or implantation. |
| Hormonal methods | Prevent ovulation or implantation and alter cervical mucus. |
| Surgical methods | Permanently block gamete transport. |

# Unit 3E: Natural Methods

Natural methods avoid fertilisation by timing or behaviour.

## Periodic Abstinence

Couple avoids intercourse during fertile period around ovulation.

Limitation: Menstrual cycles vary, so failure risk is higher than many other methods.

## Coitus Interruptus

Male withdraws penis before ejaculation.

Limitation: It is unreliable because sperm may be present in secretions before ejaculation and timing can fail.

## Lactational Amenorrhea

Breastfeeding can suppress ovulation for a limited period after childbirth, especially during exclusive breastfeeding.

Limitation: Effective mainly up to about six months after childbirth and only under specific conditions.

# Unit 3F: Barrier Methods

Barrier methods prevent sperm from entering the female reproductive tract or meeting the ovum.

## Examples

1. Male condom.
2. Female condom.
3. Diaphragm.
4. Cervical cap.
5. Vault.

## Advantages

1. Many are easy to use.
2. Condoms also reduce risk of STIs.
3. No major hormonal side effects.
4. Can be used when needed.

## Limitations

1. Must be used correctly every time.
2. Failure can occur if torn, slipped, or used incorrectly.
3. Some methods may need medical fitting.

# Unit 3G: Intrauterine Devices

IUDs are inserted into the uterus by trained medical personnel.

## Types

| Type | Examples | Main Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Non-medicated IUD | Lippes loop | Increases phagocytosis of sperm in uterus. |
| Copper-releasing IUD | CuT, Cu7, Multiload 375 | Copper ions suppress sperm motility and fertilising ability. |
| Hormone-releasing IUD | LNG-20, Progestasert | Makes uterus unsuitable for implantation and cervix hostile to sperm. |

## Advantages

1. Long acting.
2. Effective.
3. Does not require daily action.
4. Reversible after removal.

## Caution

IUDs should be inserted and removed by trained healthcare workers. They may not be suitable for every person.

# Unit 3H: Oral, Injectable, and Implant Methods

Hormonal methods mainly use progestogens alone or in combination with estrogen.

## Actions

1. Prevent ovulation.
2. Change cervical mucus to reduce sperm entry.
3. Make endometrium less suitable for implantation.

## Oral Pills

Oral pills are taken daily in a prescribed schedule. Missing pills can reduce effectiveness.

## Emergency Contraception

Emergency contraceptive pills may be used after unprotected intercourse or contraceptive failure, within the advised time window. They are for emergencies, not routine contraception.

## Implants and Injectables

These release hormones over time and reduce the need for daily intake.

# Unit 3I: Surgical Methods

Surgical contraception is also called sterilisation. It is usually permanent.

## Vasectomy

In males, vas deferens is cut or blocked. Sperm transport is prevented.

## Tubectomy

In females, fallopian tubes are cut or blocked. Ovum and sperm cannot meet.

## Important Point

Surgical methods do not usually affect sexual desire or hormone production. They prevent gamete transport.

# Unit 3J: Medical Termination of Pregnancy

Medical termination of pregnancy means intentional termination of pregnancy under legal and medical conditions.

## Situations Where It May Be Considered

1. Pregnancy may endanger the mother's life.
2. Foetus has severe abnormalities.
3. Pregnancy results from sexual assault.
4. Contraceptive failure, under applicable legal provisions.

## Why Safe Medical Care Matters

Unsafe termination can cause severe bleeding, infection, infertility, and death. MTP should only be done by qualified medical professionals under legal guidelines.

## Ethical Caution

MTP must not be misused for sex selection. Sex determination and sex-selective abortion are illegal and harmful to society.

# Unit 3K: Sexually Transmitted Infections

STIs spread mainly through sexual contact. Some can also spread through infected blood, shared needles, or from mother to child.

## Examples

1. Gonorrhoea.
2. Syphilis.
3. Genital herpes.
4. Chlamydiasis.
5. Genital warts.
6. Trichomoniasis.
7. Hepatitis B.
8. HIV infection.

## Common Symptoms

1. Itching.
2. Genital discharge.
3. Pain during urination.
4. Sores or ulcers.
5. Swelling.
6. Lower abdominal pain.

Some infections may have no obvious symptoms, especially in early stages.

## Prevention

1. Avoid multiple sexual partners.
2. Use condoms correctly.
3. Avoid sharing needles.
4. Seek early diagnosis and treatment.
5. Ensure safe blood transfusion.
6. Complete prescribed treatment.

# Unit 3L: Infertility

Infertility means inability to conceive despite unprotected sexual life over a significant period. It can be due to male factors, female factors, both, or unexplained causes.

## Possible Causes

| Male Factors | Female Factors |
|---|---|
| Low sperm count | Ovulation problems |
| Poor sperm motility | Blocked fallopian tubes |
| Abnormal sperm structure | Endometriosis |
| Hormonal imbalance | Uterine abnormalities |
| Infection | Cervical mucus problems |

# Unit 3M: Assisted Reproductive Technologies

ART helps couples conceive when natural conception is difficult.

## Main Techniques

| Technique | Meaning |
|---|---|
| IVF | Fertilisation outside body in laboratory conditions. |
| Embryo transfer | Transfer of embryo into female reproductive tract. |
| ZIFT | Zygote or early embryo transferred into fallopian tube. |
| IUT | Embryo with more cells transferred into uterus. |
| GIFT | Transfer of ovum from donor into fallopian tube. |
| ICSI | Sperm injected directly into ovum in laboratory. |
| AI | Artificial insemination using semen from husband or donor. |
|
## IVF and Embryo Transfer

In IVF, ova and sperm are collected and fertilised outside the body. The resulting embryo is transferred at a suitable stage.

## Important Caution

ART requires expert medical supervision, ethical responsibility, and emotional counselling.

# Unit 3N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Reproductive health means only absence of disease | It includes emotional, behavioural, and social well-being too. |
| Condoms only prevent pregnancy | Condoms also reduce STI risk. |
| Surgical sterilisation stops hormone production | It blocks gamete transport, not hormone secretion. |
| MTP and contraception are the same | Contraception prevents pregnancy; MTP terminates an existing pregnancy under conditions. |
| Infertility is always due to female factors | Male factors, female factors, both, or unexplained factors may be involved. |
| Emergency pills are routine pills | They are for emergency situations. |
