# Chapter 6: The Crisis Of Democratic Order

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies one of the most serious crises in Indian democracy: the Emergency of 1975-1977. The chapter does not only ask what happened. It asks why democratic institutions became vulnerable, how civil liberties were suspended, how opposition politics changed, and what lessons Indian democracy learned after the Emergency.

The Emergency was declared on 25 June 1975 on the ground of internal disturbance. It ended with the 1977 Lok Sabha election, in which the Congress was defeated for the first time at the national level.

Read this chapter through one central question: how can a democratic Constitution give extraordinary powers to a government, and how can those powers be misused?

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

The Emergency of 1975-1977 showed how economic crisis, mass protests, leadership conflict, court confrontation, and concentration of power could suspend democratic freedoms, but the 1977 election also showed the resilience of Indian democracy.

## 2. Why This Chapter Matters

This chapter matters because it tests the limits of democracy.

It asks:

- Can elected governments become authoritarian?
- Can constitutional powers be misused?
- How important are courts, press, opposition, and civil liberties?
- What should be the balance between protest and government order?
- How can voters correct democratic breakdown?

The Emergency is therefore both a warning and a lesson.

## 3. Background To Emergency

After 1971, Indira Gandhi was extremely popular.

Congress had won a major election victory, and the Bangladesh war had increased her stature.

But beneath this popularity, serious tensions were growing:

- Economic crisis.
- Rising prices.
- Food shortage.
- Unemployment.
- Student unrest.
- Labour unrest.
- Opposition mobilisation.
- Conflict with the judiciary.
- Personalisation of power.

## 4. Personalisation Of Politics

Opposition parties argued that government authority was turning into personal authority.

They felt politics was becoming too centred on Indira Gandhi.

The Congress, meanwhile, argued that courts and opposition forces were obstructing pro-poor measures.

This produced a bitter and polarised political atmosphere.

## 5. Economic Context

The Congress had campaigned in 1971 with the slogan Garibi Hatao.

But after 1971-1972, social and economic conditions did not improve much.

Major problems included:

- Burden of Bangladesh refugees.
- Cost of war with Pakistan.
- US aid stoppage after the war.
- Oil price rise in the international market.
- High inflation.
- Low industrial growth.
- High unemployment.
- Failed monsoons.
- Decline in foodgrain output.
- Salary freeze for government employees.

These conditions produced widespread dissatisfaction.

## 6. Inflation

Prices rose sharply.

The raw chapter gives two important figures:

- Prices rose by 23 percent in 1973.
- Prices rose by 30 percent in 1974.

Such inflation hurt ordinary people badly, especially the poor and salaried employees.

## 7. Agricultural Crisis

The monsoons failed in 1972-1973.

Agricultural productivity fell sharply.

Foodgrain output declined by 8 percent.

This made the promise of Garibi Hatao appear distant from everyday reality.

## 8. Opposition Mobilisation

In this atmosphere, non-Congress parties organised protests more effectively.

Student unrest that had existed since the late 1960s became stronger.

Labour unrest also grew.

Marxist-Leninist and Naxalite groups rejected parliamentary politics and used armed methods in some areas.

## 9. Naxalite Movement

Naxalite groups believed in armed struggle to overthrow the existing political and economic system.

They were especially active in West Bengal.

The state government used strong measures to suppress them.

The movement formed part of the wider atmosphere of unrest.

## 10. Gujarat Movement

In January 1974, students in Gujarat began an agitation against:

- Rising prices.
- Foodgrain prices.
- Cooking oil prices.
- Essential commodity shortages.
- Corruption in high places.

The movement spread and was joined by major opposition parties.

President's Rule was imposed in Gujarat.

Opposition parties demanded fresh elections.

## 11. Morarji Desai's Fast

Morarji Desai announced that he would go on an indefinite fast if fresh elections were not held in Gujarat.

Under pressure, assembly elections were held in June 1975.

Congress was defeated in Gujarat.

This defeat showed that anti-Congress mobilisation had electoral power.

## 12. Bihar Movement

In March 1974, students in Bihar protested against:

- Rising prices.
- Food scarcity.
- Unemployment.
- Corruption.

They invited Jayaprakash Narayan, known as JP, to lead the movement.

He agreed on the condition that the movement would remain non-violent and would not remain limited to Bihar.

## 13. Jayaprakash Narayan

JP had a long political history.

He had been:

- A socialist leader.
- A hero of the 1942 Quit India movement.
- A public figure who later moved away from active party politics.
- Associated with Gandhian social work and Bhoodan.

During the Bihar movement, he became the symbol of opposition to Indira Gandhi.

## 14. Total Revolution

JP called for Total Revolution.

This meant transformation in:

- Social life.
- Economic structure.
- Political institutions.
- Governance.
- Public morality.

He believed true democracy required deep change beyond elections.

## 15. National Spread Of The Bihar Movement

The Bihar movement gradually gained national importance.

People from many walks of life joined.

Opposition parties supported JP and projected him as an alternative moral leader to Indira Gandhi.

Indira Gandhi saw the movement as personally directed against her.

## 16. Railway Strike Of 1974

In May 1974, railway employees launched a nationwide strike.

The strike was led by George Fernandes through the National Coordination Committee for Railwaymen's Struggle.

The demands related to:

- Bonus.
- Service conditions.
- Workers' rights.

The railways were India's largest public sector undertaking, so the strike threatened to paralyse the economy.

## 17. Government Response To Railway Strike

The government declared the strike illegal.

It arrested many leaders and used strong measures, including deployment of the territorial army to protect railway tracks.

The strike ended after about twenty days without settlement.

The event added to the atmosphere of labour unrest and confrontation.

## 18. Conflict With Judiciary

The government and judiciary clashed repeatedly.

Three major constitutional questions were involved:

- Can Parliament abridge Fundamental Rights?
- Can Parliament amend the Constitution to curtail the right to property?
- Can Parliament abridge Fundamental Rights to give effect to Directive Principles?

The Supreme Court placed limits on Parliament's amending power.

## 19. Kesavananda Bharati Case

The conflict culminated in the Kesavananda Bharati case.

The Supreme Court held that Parliament could amend the Constitution, but it could not alter its basic structure.

This basic structure doctrine became one of the most important constitutional ideas in India.

## 20. Appointment Of A. N. Ray

After the Kesavananda Bharati decision, a vacancy arose for Chief Justice of India.

The normal practice was to appoint the senior-most judge.

In 1973, the government appointed Justice A. N. Ray as Chief Justice, superseding three senior judges.

This became controversial because the superseded judges had ruled against the government's position.

## 21. Committed Judiciary And Bureaucracy

Some people close to the Prime Minister spoke of the need for a judiciary and bureaucracy committed to the vision of the executive and legislature.

This raised concerns about institutional independence.

In democracy, judges and civil servants are expected to follow the Constitution and law, not personal loyalty to a ruling party.

## 22. Allahabad High Court Judgment

On 12 June 1975, Justice Jagmohan Lal Sinha of the Allahabad High Court declared Indira Gandhi's 1971 election invalid.

The case had been filed by Raj Narain, who had contested against her.

The allegation was that she had used the services of government servants in her election campaign.

## 23. Meaning Of The Judgment

The judgment meant that legally Indira Gandhi was no longer an MP.

Therefore, she could not remain Prime Minister unless she was elected again within six months.

This created a direct threat to her position.

## 24. Supreme Court Partial Stay

On 24 June 1975, the Supreme Court granted a partial stay.

Until the appeal was decided:

- She could remain an MP.
- She could not take part in Lok Sabha proceedings.

This did not fully remove the political crisis.

## 25. Ramlila Ground Rally

On 25 June 1975, opposition parties led by JP held a massive rally at Delhi's Ramlila grounds.

JP announced nationwide satyagraha demanding Indira Gandhi's resignation.

He also asked the army, police, and government employees not to obey illegal and immoral orders.

The government saw this as a serious challenge to state authority.

## 26. Declaration Of Emergency

On 25 June 1975, the government declared a state of Emergency under Article 352.

The stated ground was threat of internal disturbance.

President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed issued the proclamation on the Prime Minister's recommendation.

## 27. What Article 352 Allowed

Article 352 allowed Emergency on grounds such as:

- External threat.
- Internal disturbance, as it then stood.

Once Emergency was proclaimed:

- Federal distribution of powers was practically suspended.
- Power became concentrated in the Union government.
- Fundamental Rights could be restricted or suspended.

## 28. Night Of 25 June 1975

After the proclamation:

- Electricity to major newspaper offices was disconnected.
- Opposition leaders and workers were arrested.
- The Cabinet was informed at a special meeting at 6 a.m. on 26 June, after the key actions had already happened.

This sequence became controversial.

## 29. What Emergency Meant In Practice

Emergency meant:

- Strikes were banned.
- Public protests were disallowed.
- Opposition leaders were jailed.
- Press freedom was suspended.
- Fundamental Rights were curtailed.
- Preventive detention was widely used.
- Courts became less accessible for detained citizens.

The political atmosphere became quiet but tense.

## 30. Press Censorship

Newspapers were required to get prior approval before publishing material.

This is called press censorship.

Some newspapers protested by leaving blank spaces where reports or editorials had been censored.

Later, even blank spaces were banned.

## 31. Bans On Organisations

The government banned organisations such as:

- Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh.
- Jamaat-e-Islami.

The stated concern was social and communal disharmony.

## 32. Suspension Of Fundamental Rights

During Emergency, several Fundamental Rights were suspended or restricted.

Most importantly, citizens could not easily approach courts to restore their rights.

This deeply affected civil liberties.

## 33. Preventive Detention

Preventive detention allows the state to arrest people not because they have committed an offence, but because they are suspected of possibly committing one.

During Emergency, this power was used on a large scale.

Political workers, activists, and critics were detained.

## 34. Habeas Corpus Problem

Many arrested persons tried to file habeas corpus petitions.

Habeas corpus asks the court to examine whether detention is lawful.

The government argued that during Emergency, it did not even have to inform detained persons of the grounds of arrest.

## 35. Supreme Court Judgment On Detention

Several High Courts allowed habeas corpus petitions despite Emergency.

In April 1976, the Supreme Court overruled the High Courts and accepted the government's position.

This effectively closed the doors of the judiciary for detained citizens.

It is regarded as one of the most controversial judgments of the Supreme Court.

## 36. Resistance To Emergency

Resistance existed, though open defiance was rare.

Some political workers went underground and organised protests.

Some newspapers resisted censorship.

Some magazines chose to close rather than submit.

Underground newsletters and leaflets circulated.

Some journalists were arrested.

## 37. Writers And Protest

Some writers returned state honours in protest.

Examples include:

- Kannada writer Shivarama Karanth, who returned Padma Bhushan.
- Hindi writer Phanishwarnath Renu, who returned Padma Shri.

These acts symbolised moral opposition to suspension of democracy.

## 38. Constitutional Amendments During Emergency

Parliament made several changes during Emergency.

One amendment declared that elections of the Prime Minister, President, and Vice-President could not be challenged in court.

This was linked to the Allahabad High Court judgment.

## 39. 42nd Amendment

The 42nd Amendment made wide-ranging changes to the Constitution.

One major change was extending the term of legislatures from five years to six years.

During Emergency, elections could also be postponed by one year.

This meant elections due after 1971 could be delayed.

## 40. Shah Commission

After the Emergency, the Janata government appointed the Shah Commission in 1977.

It investigated Emergency excesses.

Its findings showed that the administration and police became vulnerable to political pressure and that extraordinary powers were misused.

The Commission became important for documenting the institutional damage of the Emergency.

## 41. Lessons Of The Emergency

The Emergency gave several lessons.

First, it showed that democracy in India could be weakened by constitutional means if safeguards were insufficient.

Second, it showed that it is difficult to permanently destroy democracy in India because elections restored democratic functioning.

Third, it increased awareness of civil liberties.

Fourth, it showed the importance of independent courts, media, opposition, and administration.

## 42. Constitutional Safeguards After Emergency

After the Emergency, ambiguities in Emergency provisions were corrected.

Now, internal Emergency can be proclaimed only on the ground of armed rebellion.

Also, advice to the President to proclaim Emergency must be given in writing by the Union Cabinet.

These safeguards were meant to prevent a repeat of 1975.

## 43. Protest And Democracy

The Emergency raised a difficult question:

How should a democracy balance routine government functioning with continuous protest?

Citizens have a right to protest.

But protests can also disrupt governance.

The chapter does not give a simple answer. It asks learners to think about limits, rights, and responsibilities.

## 44. Institution-based Democracy And Mass Participation

There was a tension between:

- Institution-based democracy: elections, legislatures, courts, and procedures.
- Popular participation: marches, strikes, satyagraha, and mass movements.

The Emergency emerged partly from failure to manage this tension democratically.

## 45. Politics After Emergency

In January 1977, after eighteen months of Emergency, the government announced elections.

Opposition leaders and activists were released.

Elections were held in March 1977.

The election became a referendum on Emergency.

## 46. Formation Of Janata Party

Major opposition parties came together to form the Janata Party.

The new party accepted JP's leadership.

The aim was to prevent division of non-Congress votes.

Some Congress leaders opposed to Emergency also joined.

## 47. Congress For Democracy

Jagjivan Ram and some other Congress leaders left Congress and formed Congress for Democracy.

This party later merged with the Janata Party.

Its formation further weakened Congress before the 1977 election.

## 48. Janata Campaign

Janata Party made the election a referendum on Emergency.

Its campaign focused on:

- Arrests.
- Press censorship.
- Suspension of rights.
- Excesses by police and administration.
- Non-democratic character of Emergency rule.

The slogan save democracy captured the mood.

## 49. 1977 Election Results

For the first time since independence, Congress was defeated in a Lok Sabha election.

Results:

- Congress won 154 seats.
- Congress vote share fell below 35 percent.
- Janata Party and allies won 330 out of 542 seats.
- Janata Party alone won 295 seats.

Indira Gandhi lost from Rae Bareli.

Sanjay Gandhi lost from Amethi.

## 50. Regional Pattern Of 1977

Congress did not lose equally everywhere.

It was badly defeated in north India.

Congress lost every seat in:

- Bihar.
- Uttar Pradesh.
- Delhi.
- Haryana.
- Punjab.

It won only one seat each in Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh.

But Congress retained many seats in Maharashtra, Gujarat, Orissa, and the southern states.

## 51. Why North India Voted Differently

The Emergency affected regions differently.

In north India, anger was stronger because of:

- Forced relocation.
- Forced sterilisations.
- Stronger repression.
- Long-term changes in caste politics.
- Movement of middle castes away from Congress.

Janata became a platform for many of these groups.

## 52. Janata Government

After the 1977 election, Janata Party formed the first non-Congress government at the Centre.

There was competition among three leaders:

- Morarji Desai.
- Charan Singh.
- Jagjivan Ram.

Morarji Desai became Prime Minister.

## 53. Problems Of Janata Government

The Janata Party was not cohesive.

Its problems included:

- Internal factions.
- Leadership rivalry.
- Lack of common programme.
- Differences among former parties.
- Difficulty governing after unity based mainly on anti-Emergency sentiment.

The opposition to Emergency kept them united only for a while.

## 54. Collapse Of Janata Government

Morarji Desai's government lost majority in less than 28 months.

Charan Singh then formed a government with Congress support.

Congress withdrew support, and Charan Singh's government lasted about four months.

Fresh Lok Sabha elections were held in January 1980.

## 55. 1980 Election

In 1980, the Janata Party suffered a major defeat.

Congress led by Indira Gandhi won 353 seats and returned to power.

This showed that voters punish governments perceived as unstable and quarrelsome.

## 56. Legacy For Party System

The Emergency and post-Emergency period changed the party system.

Key changes included:

- Congress lost its old umbrella character.
- Non-Congressism became more important.
- Opposition parties learned to unite.
- Backward caste politics gained strength.
- Mandal Commission was later appointed by Janata government.
- Coalition possibilities increased.

## 57. OBC Politics

The 1977 election also reflected a shift among backward castes in north India.

Many of these groups moved away from Congress and supported Janata.

After 1977, non-Congress state governments in north India included important backward caste leaders.

The issue of OBC reservation became more visible, leading to the appointment of the Mandal Commission.

## 58. Constitutional Crisis And Political Crisis

The Emergency was a constitutional crisis because it involved:

- Parliament versus judiciary.
- Emergency powers.
- Suspension of rights.
- Constitutional amendments.

It was also a political crisis because:

- A government with majority suspended democratic processes.
- Opposition leaders were jailed.
- State power was used against dissent.
- Institutions were pressured.

The political crisis was deeper than the legal one.

## 59. Police And Administration

Emergency rule was implemented through police and administration.

The Shah Commission found that these institutions became vulnerable to political pressure.

This is important because even good constitutional rules fail if institutions do not act independently and lawfully.

## 60. Civil Liberties After Emergency

After Emergency, courts became more active in protecting civil liberties.

Civil liberties organisations also became more visible.

The experience made citizens more aware of the importance of rights.

## 66. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

What reasons did the government give for declaring Emergency in 1975?

Answer:

The government argued that there was a threat of internal disturbance and that the political situation had become a grave crisis. It pointed to opposition protests, JP's call for satyagraha, unrest, strikes, and threats to government functioning.

### Question 2

What were the effects of Emergency on press freedom?

Answer:

Press freedom was suspended through censorship. Newspapers had to get prior approval before publication. Some newspapers left blank spaces to show censored material, but later even this form of protest was banned. Journalists were also arrested for opposing Emergency.

### Question 3

Why was the 1977 election important?

Answer:

The 1977 election was important because it became a referendum on Emergency. For the first time since independence, Congress was defeated at the national level. The Janata Party formed the first non-Congress government at the Centre. The result showed that voters could punish anti-democratic rule.

### Question 4

Why did the Janata government fall?

Answer:

The Janata government fell because the party lacked unity, leadership consensus, and a common programme. It had been united mainly by opposition to Emergency. Once in power, internal rivalries among leaders such as Morarji Desai, Charan Singh, and others weakened the government.

## 67. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain the background factors that led to the Emergency.

Answer:

The Emergency was preceded by economic crisis, political unrest, and institutional conflict. After 1971, inflation rose sharply, food output declined, unemployment grew, and the government froze salaries. The Gujarat and Bihar student movements mobilised public anger against corruption and price rise. JP's call for Total Revolution gave the opposition a national symbol. The railway strike of 1974 added to labour unrest. At the same time, the government was in conflict with the judiciary over constitutional amendments and Fundamental Rights. The Allahabad High Court judgment of 12 June 1975 invalidating Indira Gandhi's election created an immediate political crisis. These factors together formed the background to the proclamation of Emergency.

### Question 2

What were the main consequences of Emergency?

Answer:

Emergency concentrated power in the Union government and suspended normal democratic politics. Opposition leaders and activists were arrested. Strikes and protests were banned. Press censorship was imposed. Fundamental Rights were suspended or restricted, and citizens could not effectively use courts to challenge detention. Preventive detention was used widely. Organisations such as RSS and Jamaat-e-Islami were banned. Constitutional amendments, including the 42nd Amendment, were passed. The police and administration became vulnerable to political pressure. However, there were also acts of resistance through underground work, press protest, and writers returning awards.

### Question 3

What lessons did Indian democracy learn from the Emergency?

Answer:

The Emergency showed both the weakness and strength of Indian democracy. It showed that constitutional powers could be misused and that civil liberties, press freedom, courts, and opposition parties are essential for democracy. It also showed that democracy was difficult to destroy permanently in India because elections were held in 1977 and voters defeated the ruling party. After Emergency, safeguards were introduced: internal Emergency can now be proclaimed only on the ground of armed rebellion, and written advice of the Union Cabinet is required. Courts and civil liberties organisations also became more alert to rights violations.

### Question 4

Explain the 1977 election and its impact on Indian politics.

Answer:

The 1977 election was held after eighteen months of Emergency. The opposition parties formed the Janata Party and made the election a referendum on Emergency. Congress was defeated for the first time in a Lok Sabha election, winning only 154 seats and less than 35 percent votes. Janata Party and allies won 330 of 542 seats. The anti-Congress wave was strongest in north India, where Emergency excesses such as forced relocation and sterilisations had greater impact. The election brought the first non-Congress government to power and proved that voters could restore democracy through elections.

## 68. Passage-based Practice

### Passage Theme

The 1977 election made India look close to a two-party system because Congress faced a united Janata alternative.

### Question 1

What made the party system look like a two-party system in 1977?

Answer:

Most major opposition parties united under the Janata Party, while Congress stood on the other side. This created a clear Congress versus Janata contest in many regions.

### Question 2

Why say this if more than two parties existed?

Answer:

Many parties existed, but the main electoral competition was organised into two broad sides: Congress and the Janata-led opposition.

### Question 3

What caused later splits?

Answer:

Congress and Janata both contained many factions, leaders, and social groups. Internal rivalries, ideological differences, and leadership conflicts caused splits after the election.

## 69. Case-based Practice

Read the situation:

A government faces protests, court judgments against its leader, and strong opposition mobilisation. It uses constitutional emergency powers to arrest opponents, censor newspapers, and restrict rights.

### Question 1

Which Indian event does this resemble?

Answer:

It resembles the Emergency of 1975-1977.

### Question 2

Which freedoms are most at risk?

Answer:

Civil liberties, press freedom, right to protest, and access to courts are most at risk.

### Question 3

What democratic correction occurred later?

Answer:

The 1977 election allowed voters to defeat the ruling party and restore normal democratic politics.

## 70. Exam Writing Tips

- Do not reduce Emergency to one cause; include economic, political, judicial, and immediate electoral factors.
- Use exact dates: 12 June 1975, 24 June 1975, 25 June 1975.
- Mention Article 352 and internal disturbance.
- Explain censorship and preventive detention clearly.
- In rights answers, include habeas corpus.
- In lessons answers, mention armed rebellion and written Cabinet advice.
- In 1977 answers, include Janata Party, JP, Congress defeat, and regional pattern.
- In legacy answers, connect Emergency to party system, civil liberties, OBC politics, and institutional safeguards.

## 73. Final Takeaway

The Emergency showed that democracy can be weakened even by an elected government using constitutional powers. It exposed the danger of concentrated authority, weak institutional independence, censorship, preventive detention, and suspended rights. But the 1977 election also showed that Indian voters could restore democracy. The chapter's core lesson is that democracy requires not only elections but also civil liberties, independent institutions, accountable power, and active citizens.
