# Chapter 4: India's External Relations

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how independent India dealt with the world during the early decades after 1947. It connects foreign policy with domestic politics. India did not make external policy in isolation; it had to respond to the Cold War, decolonisation, poverty, Partition, security threats, and the need for development.

The main foreign policy idea in this chapter is non-alignment. But the chapter is not only about non-alignment. It also covers wars with China and Pakistan, the birth of Bangladesh, nuclear policy, and the way international events affected Indian politics at home.

Read this chapter through one central question: how did India try to protect sovereignty, territorial integrity, peace, and development in a divided and conflict-ridden world?

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

India's early foreign policy tried to preserve sovereignty, territorial integrity, peace, and development through non-alignment, but conflicts with China and Pakistan forced India to combine idealism with security concerns.

## 2. Why Foreign Policy Mattered After Independence

Foreign policy mattered because independence was incomplete if India could not decide its own relations with other countries.

A colony does not control its external relations. A sovereign state does.

This is why Nehru saw foreign relations as a test of independence.

## 3. International Context After 1947

India became independent in a difficult international context.

The world had recently experienced the Second World War.

Major developments included:

- Reconstruction after war.
- Creation of the United Nations.
- Beginning of the Cold War.
- Rise of nuclear weapons.
- Collapse of colonial empires.
- Emergence of newly independent countries.
- Division of the world into two military and ideological blocs.

India had to frame its policy in this unstable setting.

## 4. Domestic Context After 1947

India also faced domestic pressures that shaped foreign policy.

These included:

- Partition.
- Poverty.
- Refugee rehabilitation.
- Need for economic development.
- Integration of territory.
- Border disputes left by the British.
- Need for national security.

Foreign policy was therefore shaped by both international and domestic conditions.

## 5. Article 51

Article 51 of the Constitution contains Directive Principles related to international peace and security.

It asks the state to work for:

- International peace and security.
- Just and honourable relations among nations.
- Respect for international law and treaty obligations.
- Settlement of international disputes through arbitration.

These principles influenced India's stated foreign policy goals.

## 6. Main Objectives Of Nehru's Foreign Policy

Jawaharlal Nehru was Prime Minister and also Foreign Minister.

He shaped foreign policy from 1946 to 1964.

The three major objectives of his foreign policy were:

- Preserve hard-earned sovereignty.
- Protect territorial integrity.
- Promote rapid economic development.

He believed non-alignment would help achieve these objectives.

## 7. Nehru's Role

Nehru had deep influence over foreign policy because:

- He was Prime Minister.
- He handled the foreign affairs portfolio.
- He had strong international interests.
- He had moral authority from the freedom struggle.
- He saw India as a voice of newly independent countries.

However, there were other views within India. Some parties and leaders wanted closer ties with the US-led bloc.

## 8. Alternative Views In India

Not everyone agreed fully with Nehru's foreign policy.

Some believed India should be closer to the US-led bloc because it claimed to defend democracy.

Leaders and groups critical of communism, including Dr. Ambedkar, the Bharatiya Jana Sangh, and later the Swatantra Party, were more favourable to a pro-US orientation.

But Nehru had enough political space to shape policy around non-alignment.

## 9. Meaning Of Non-alignment

Non-alignment meant India would not join either Cold War military bloc.

It did not mean:

- Isolation.
- Neutrality on every issue.
- Refusal to take moral positions.
- Equal silence toward all conflicts.

It meant India would judge issues independently and avoid military entanglement.

## 10. Why India Chose Non-alignment

India chose non-alignment because:

- It wanted to protect sovereignty.
- It wanted freedom of decision-making.
- It needed aid from both blocs.
- It wanted peace for development.
- It opposed colonialism and racism.
- It did not want to become a pawn in superpower rivalry.
- It wanted leadership among newly independent countries.

Non-alignment was therefore both idealistic and practical.

## 11. Cold War Blocs

The Cold War world was divided into two major blocs.

| Bloc | Led by | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Western bloc | United States | Capitalist, anti-communist, NATO |
| Socialist bloc | Soviet Union | Communist, planned economy, Warsaw Pact |

Many countries joined one bloc to receive security, aid, or diplomatic support.

India chose not to join either.

## 12. Non-alignment And Military Alliances

India stayed away from military alliances such as:

- NATO.
- Warsaw Pact.

India believed military alliances increased global tension and reduced the independence of smaller countries.

## 13. Non-alignment Was A Balancing Act

Non-alignment was difficult because each bloc suspected India of leaning toward the other.

India sometimes took positions that seemed uneven.

For example:

- India strongly criticised Britain's attack on Egypt during the Suez crisis in 1956.
- India did not publicly condemn the Soviet invasion of Hungary in the same way.

This created criticism, but India still tried to maintain an independent path.

## 14. Benefits Of Non-alignment

Non-alignment gave India several benefits:

- It preserved policy independence.
- It allowed aid from both blocs.
- It reduced pressure to join wars.
- It gave India moral standing among newly independent countries.
- It supported peace as a condition for development.

## 15. Limits Of Non-alignment

Non-alignment also had limits:

- It could not prevent wars with neighbours.
- It sometimes appeared inconsistent.
- It could not remove dependence on major powers.
- It was tested whenever India faced security crises.

The chapter shows both strengths and limits.

## 16. India And The United States

The United States was uneasy with India's non-alignment.

The US preferred countries to take a clear side in the Cold War.

US discomfort increased because:

- India refused military alliances.
- India developed relations with the Soviet Union.
- Pakistan joined US-led military alliances.
- India pursued planned development and import substitution.

## 17. India And The Soviet Union

India developed close ties with the Soviet Union over time.

The Soviet Union supported India's development through aid and cooperation in key areas.

But India officially maintained non-alignment rather than joining the Soviet bloc.

The relationship became especially important during the 1971 crisis.

## 18. Pakistan And US Alliances

Pakistan joined US-led military arrangements during the Cold War.

This affected India because US military support to Pakistan created security concerns.

Pakistan also became important in US-China diplomacy later.

## 19. Planned Development And Foreign Policy

India's economic strategy also affected foreign relations.

The strategy of planned development emphasised:

- Import substitution.
- Domestic industrial base.
- Public sector growth.
- Self-reliance.

This limited India's economic interaction with the outside world compared with export-oriented strategies.

## 20. Afro-Asian Unity

Nehru believed India should play a major role in Asian and African affairs.

India supported:

- Decolonisation.
- Anti-racism.
- Freedom struggles.
- Asian and African solidarity.
- Opposition to apartheid in South Africa.

This gave India a voice beyond its material power.

## 21. Asian Relations Conference

India convened the Asian Relations Conference in March 1947, even before independence.

This showed India's early commitment to Asian solidarity.

It also reflected the idea that the Indian national movement was connected to global anti-colonial struggles.

## 22. Indonesia's Freedom

India supported Indonesia's struggle against Dutch colonial rule.

In 1949, India convened an international conference in support of Indonesian independence.

This was an important example of India's anti-colonial foreign policy.

## 23. Bandung Conference

The Afro-Asian Conference was held at Bandung in Indonesia in 1955.

It brought together newly independent and anti-colonial countries.

The Bandung Conference marked a high point in India's engagement with Asian and African nations.

It later helped lead to the Non-Aligned Movement.

## 24. Non-Aligned Movement

The first NAM summit was held in Belgrade in September 1961.

Important leaders associated with NAM included:

- Nehru of India.
- Nasser of Egypt.
- Tito of Yugoslavia.
- Sukarno of Indonesia.
- Nkrumah of Ghana.

NAM gave newly independent countries a platform outside Cold War blocs.

## 25. India And China: Early Friendship

India's early relationship with China began on a friendly note.

After the Chinese Revolution in 1949, India was among the first countries to recognise the communist government of China.

Nehru believed China was also emerging from Western domination and deserved recognition.

## 26. Patel's Concern About China

Some leaders, including Vallabhbhai Patel, worried about possible Chinese aggression in the future.

Nehru considered such an attack highly unlikely.

For a long time, the India-China border was guarded mainly by paramilitary forces, not the regular army.

This later became part of criticism against Nehru's assessment.

## 27. Panchsheel

Panchsheel means the Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence.

They were jointly stated by India and China on 29 April 1954.

The broad principles included:

- Respect for territorial integrity and sovereignty.
- Non-aggression.
- Non-interference.
- Equality and mutual benefit.
- Peaceful coexistence.

Panchsheel symbolised early India-China friendship.

## 28. Tibet Issue

Tibet was a major issue between India and China.

Historically, Tibet had a special position between India and China.

In 1950, China took control of Tibet.

India initially tried to avoid direct confrontation with China over this issue.

In the 1954 agreement, India accepted China's claim over Tibet through the principle of respecting territorial sovereignty.

## 29. Dalai Lama And Asylum

In 1958, there was an armed uprising in Tibet against Chinese control.

China suppressed the uprising.

In 1959, the Dalai Lama crossed into India and sought asylum.

India granted asylum.

China protested strongly and accused India of allowing anti-China activities from Indian territory.

## 30. Tibetan Refugees In India

Many Tibetans came to India as refugees.

Large Tibetan settlements developed in places such as Delhi and Dharamshala.

Dharamshala in Himachal Pradesh became the home of the Dalai Lama in India.

Many Indian political parties, including the Socialist Party and Jana Sangh, supported the Tibetan cause.

## 31. Border Dispute With China

The India-China border dispute had two major sectors:

| Sector | Area | Issue |
|---|---|---|
| Western sector | Aksai Chin in Ladakh | China occupied and built a road |
| Eastern sector | NEFA, later Arunachal Pradesh | China claimed much of the area |

India argued that the boundary was settled during colonial times.

China argued that colonial decisions did not apply.

## 32. Aksai Chin

Between 1957 and 1959, China occupied Aksai Chin and built a strategic road there.

This was a serious security issue because the area connected Chinese-controlled regions.

India's discovery of the road deepened mistrust.

## 33. Talks Failed

India and China exchanged letters and held talks.

Nehru and Chinese leaders could not resolve the border dispute.

Small border skirmishes occurred before the full war.

## 34. China War Of 1962

In October 1962, China launched a swift and large attack on both disputed sectors.

The attack happened when the world's attention was focused on the Cuban Missile Crisis between the superpowers.

The first attack lasted about a week.

The second wave came in November.

## 35. Course Of The 1962 War

In the eastern sector, Chinese forces captured key areas and advanced close to the entry point of the Assam plains.

In the western sector in Ladakh, Indian forces resisted and blocked some Chinese advances.

China later declared a unilateral ceasefire and withdrew to positions held before the invasion in the eastern sector.

## 36. Consequences Of The 1962 War

The war had major consequences:

- India's international image suffered.
- India experienced a sense of national humiliation.
- Nationalism intensified.
- India sought military assistance from the US and Britain.
- The Soviet Union remained neutral during the conflict.
- Defence preparedness became a major concern.
- Nehru's leadership was criticised.
- Defence Minister V. K. Krishna Menon resigned.
- A no-confidence motion was debated in the Lok Sabha.
- Congress lost some important by-elections.

The political mood of the country changed.

## 37. V. K. Krishna Menon

V. K. Krishna Menon was close to Nehru and served as Defence Minister.

After the 1962 war, he faced strong criticism for lack of defence preparedness.

He had to leave the cabinet.

His resignation showed how a foreign policy and security failure can produce domestic political consequences.

## 38. CPI Split And China War

The China war affected the Communist Party of India.

The conflict, combined with the growing rift between China and the Soviet Union, sharpened ideological differences inside the CPI.

In 1964, the party split.

- The pro-Soviet faction remained CPI.
- The other faction formed CPI(M).

Some leaders later associated with CPI(M) were arrested in the wake of the China war for being pro-China.

## 39. Northeast After 1962

The China war made Indian leaders more aware of the strategic importance and underdevelopment of the Northeast.

The region faced problems of:

- Isolation.
- Weak development.
- Integration.
- Security vulnerability.

After the war, reorganisation began. Nagaland received statehood, and Manipur and Tripura, though Union Territories at the time, received elected legislative assemblies.

## 40. India-China Relations After 1962

It took more than a decade for India and China to restore normal relations.

Full diplomatic relations were restored in 1976.

Atal Bihari Vajpayee, then External Affairs Minister, visited China in 1979.

Later, Rajiv Gandhi became the first Prime Minister after Nehru to visit China.

Over time, trade became an important part of relations.

## 41. India And Pakistan: Early Conflict

India's conflict with Pakistan began soon after Partition.

The main issue was Jammu and Kashmir.

A proxy war between Indian and Pakistani forces broke out in 1947, but it did not become a full war at that time.

The matter was referred to the United Nations.

## 42. Cooperation Despite Conflict

India and Pakistan also cooperated in some areas.

For example:

- Both governments worked to restore women abducted during Partition.
- They negotiated river water sharing.

This shows that conflict and cooperation can exist together in foreign relations.

## 43. Indus Waters Treaty

The Indus Waters Treaty was signed in 1960 by Nehru and General Ayub Khan.

It was mediated by the World Bank.

The treaty settled a long-term river water dispute.

Despite many ups and downs in India-Pakistan relations, the treaty has continued to function.

## 44. 1965 War With Pakistan

A more serious armed conflict began in 1965.

Lal Bahadur Shastri was Prime Minister at that time.

In April 1965, Pakistan launched attacks in the Rann of Kutch area of Gujarat.

This was followed by a larger offensive in Jammu and Kashmir in August-September.

## 45. Pakistan's Expectation In 1965

Pakistan expected local support in Jammu and Kashmir.

That support did not materialise as expected.

To reduce pressure on the Kashmir front, Shastri ordered Indian troops to launch a counter-offensive on the Punjab border.

Indian forces reached close to Lahore.

## 46. Tashkent Agreement

The 1965 war ended with United Nations intervention.

In January 1966, Lal Bahadur Shastri and General Ayub Khan signed the Tashkent Agreement.

The agreement was brokered by the Soviet Union.

It restored peace after the war.

## 47. Economic Impact Of The 1965 War

The 1965 war added to India's difficult economic situation.

India already faced development challenges, and war diverted resources toward defence.

The war showed that foreign conflict could disrupt domestic planning.

## 48. Bangladesh Crisis

In 1970, Pakistan held general elections.

The result exposed the deep divide between West Pakistan and East Pakistan.

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto's party performed strongly in West Pakistan.

The Awami League, led by Sheikh Mujib-ur Rahman, swept East Pakistan.

The Bengali population of East Pakistan had voted against years of political and economic discrimination.

## 49. Refusal To Accept The Verdict

The rulers of Pakistan did not accept the democratic verdict.

They were also unwilling to accept the Awami League's demand for a federation.

In early 1971, the Pakistani army arrested Sheikh Mujib and unleashed violence on the people of East Pakistan.

This led to a liberation struggle for Bangladesh.

## 50. Refugee Crisis In India

Throughout 1971, about 80 lakh refugees fled East Pakistan and entered India.

This created a huge burden for India.

The refugee crisis made the Bangladesh question both a humanitarian and security issue for India.

## 51. India's Support For Bangladesh

India gave moral and material support to the Bangladesh liberation struggle.

Pakistan accused India of trying to break it up.

India argued that the crisis was created by Pakistan's refusal to accept democracy and its repression in East Pakistan.

## 52. US-China-Pakistan Axis

During the Bangladesh crisis, Pakistan received support from the US and China.

US-China rapprochement was beginning in the late 1960s and early 1970s.

Henry Kissinger secretly visited China via Pakistan in July 1971.

This made Pakistan strategically important to the US.

## 53. Indo-Soviet Treaty Of 1971

India signed a 20-year Treaty of Peace and Friendship with the Soviet Union in August 1971.

The treaty assured India of Soviet support if India faced attack.

Critics asked whether this violated non-alignment.

India's position was that the treaty was a strategic necessity in a hostile regional and global environment.

## 54. Was The 1971 Treaty Against Non-alignment?

The treaty did not formally make India a member of the Soviet bloc.

However, it did show a pro-Soviet tilt at a critical moment.

A balanced answer should say:

- India remained officially non-aligned.
- The treaty reduced India's distance from the Soviet Union.
- It was shaped by the US-China-Pakistan alignment.
- It reflected the pressure of national security.

## 55. December 1971 War

Full-scale war between India and Pakistan began in December 1971.

Pakistan attacked Indian areas in Punjab and Rajasthan and moved on the Jammu and Kashmir front.

India responded with air force, navy, and army operations on both western and eastern fronts.

## 56. Eastern Front In 1971

In East Pakistan, the Indian army advanced rapidly with support from the local population.

Within about ten days, Indian forces surrounded Dhaka from three sides.

About 90,000 Pakistani soldiers surrendered.

Bangladesh emerged as an independent country.

## 57. End Of The 1971 War

After Bangladesh became free, India declared a unilateral ceasefire.

The Shimla Agreement was signed by Indira Gandhi and Zulfikar Ali Bhutto on 3 July 1972.

The agreement formalised the return of peace.

## 58. Political Impact Of The 1971 Victory

The 1971 victory led to national celebration in India.

It increased India's military prestige.

It greatly increased Indira Gandhi's popularity.

Congress also performed strongly in later state assembly elections.

This shows how foreign policy success can shape domestic politics.

## 59. Defence And Planning

Wars affected India's development planning.

After 1962, India had to modernise its military.

The Department of Defence Production was set up in November 1962.

The Department of Defence Supplies was set up in November 1965.

Defence expenditure increased sharply, and resources were diverted from development.

## 60. Plan Disruption

The Third Five Year Plan covered 1961-1966.

It was affected by war and economic crisis.

The Fourth Plan could not begin in 1966 as expected.

Three Annual Plans followed, and the Fourth Plan began only in 1969.

This links external conflict with economic planning.

## 61. Kargil Confrontation

In 1999, forces claiming to be Mujahideens occupied several points on the Indian side of the Line of Control in areas such as Mashkoh, Dras, Kaksar, and Batalik.

India suspected involvement of the Pakistan Army.

Indian forces responded, and the conflict continued during May and June 1999.

By 26 July 1999, India had recovered control of many points.

## 62. Why Kargil Was Significant

Kargil was significant because it happened only one year after India and Pakistan demonstrated nuclear capability in 1998.

The conflict remained limited to the Kargil region, but it attracted global attention because both countries were now nuclear-capable.

In Pakistan, the conflict became controversial and was followed by the army takeover under General Pervez Musharraf.

## 63. India's Nuclear Programme

India's nuclear programme began in the late 1940s under the guidance of Homi J. Bhabha.

Nehru believed science and technology were essential for building a modern India.

India initially emphasised peaceful uses of atomic energy.

Nehru opposed nuclear weapons and called for comprehensive nuclear disarmament.

## 64. Nuclear Weapons And The Cold War

Despite calls for disarmament, nuclear arsenals continued to grow.

The five nuclear weapon powers were:

- United States.
- Soviet Union.
- United Kingdom.
- France.
- China.

These were also the permanent members of the United Nations Security Council.

## 65. China's 1964 Nuclear Test

China conducted nuclear tests in October 1964.

This affected India's security thinking.

After the 1962 war, China's nuclear capability increased pressure on India to consider its own strategic options.

## 66. Nuclear Non-proliferation Treaty

The Nuclear Non-proliferation Treaty was created in 1968.

India considered it discriminatory because it allowed existing nuclear weapon states to keep their status while asking others not to develop nuclear weapons.

India refused to sign it.

## 67. India's 1974 Nuclear Test

India conducted its first nuclear explosion in May 1974.

India described it as a peaceful nuclear explosion.

India argued that it remained committed to peaceful uses of nuclear energy.

## 68. Domestic Context Of 1974

The 1974 test occurred during a difficult domestic period.

India faced:

- Economic stress.
- Oil shock after the 1973 Arab-Israel war.
- High inflation.
- Political agitations.
- A nationwide railway strike.

This context matters because nuclear policy and domestic politics were happening at the same time.

## 69. CTBT And 1998 Tests

India opposed the indefinite extension of the NPT in 1995.

India also refused to sign the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty.

In May 1998, India conducted a series of nuclear tests.

Pakistan soon followed with its own tests.

The international community criticised both countries and sanctions were imposed, though later many were waived.

## 70. India's Nuclear Doctrine

India's declared nuclear doctrine includes:

- Credible minimum nuclear deterrence.
- No first use.
- Commitment to global, verifiable, and non-discriminatory nuclear disarmament.

This means India wants enough nuclear capability for deterrence but claims it will not use nuclear weapons first.

## 71. Consensus In Foreign Policy

Indian political parties have often disagreed on details of foreign policy.

However, there has been broad agreement on:

- National integration.
- Protection of international boundaries.
- National interest.
- Sovereignty.
- Security.

This is why foreign policy has usually played a limited role in party competition compared with domestic issues.

## 72. Change After 1977

The Janata Party government came to power in 1977.

It announced that it would follow genuine non-alignment.

This meant it wanted to correct what it saw as a pro-Soviet tilt in earlier policy.

Since then, both Congress and non-Congress governments have tried to improve relations with China and the United States.

## 73. Change After 1990

After 1990, the global context changed.

The Soviet Union declined and later collapsed.

Russia remained an important friend of India, but it no longer had the same global pre-eminence.

The international system became more influenced by economic interests.

India's foreign policy shifted toward closer ties with the United States while maintaining other partnerships.

## 74. India-Pakistan Relations After 1990

Kashmir remained the main issue between India and Pakistan.

At the same time, both sides made efforts to restore normal relations through:

- Cultural exchanges.
- Movement of citizens.
- Transport links.
- Economic cooperation.
- Dialogue.

Train and bus services between the two countries became symbols of these efforts.

Kargil showed that peace efforts remained fragile.

## 75. Foreign Policy And National Interest

Foreign policy is shaped by national interest.

National interest may include:

- Security.
- Development.
- Trade.
- Regional influence.
- Energy.
- Technology.
- Protection of citizens.
- International reputation.

India's foreign policy changed over time because the meaning of national interest changed with global conditions.

## 76. Peace And War: Was Foreign Policy A Failure?

India's foreign policy was built around peace and cooperation, yet India fought wars in 1962, 1965, and 1971.

This does not automatically mean foreign policy failed.

A balanced answer should say:

- India preferred peace and non-alignment.
- Border disputes and neighbour conflicts created security crises.
- International alignments shaped wars.
- Weakness in defence preparation contributed to 1962.
- The 1971 war was linked to humanitarian crisis and Pakistan's internal repression.

Thus wars resulted from both policy limits and external circumstances.

## 77. Domestic Impact Of External Relations

External relations affected domestic politics in several ways:

- 1962 weakened Nehru's prestige.
- Krishna Menon resigned after the China war.
- No-confidence motion was debated after 1962.
- CPI split in 1964 partly due to China-Soviet differences and the China war.
- 1965 added economic pressure.
- 1971 increased Indira Gandhi's popularity.
- Defence spending affected planning.

Foreign policy and domestic politics were deeply connected.

## 79. Comparing 1965 And 1971 Wars

| Main trigger | Pakistan attacks in Rann of Kutch and Jammu and Kashmir | Bangladesh crisis and Pakistani military repression |

| Risk avoided | Loss of independent judgment | Entanglement in superpower rivalry |

## 81. Article 51 And Real Events

Article 51 promoted peace, law, and arbitration.

India tried to follow these through:

- Non-alignment.
- UN peacekeeping.
- Support for arbitration.
- Indus Waters Treaty.
- Afro-Asian cooperation.

But real events such as China war and Pakistan wars showed that principles had to operate in a world of conflict.

## 82. Common Misunderstandings

| Misunderstanding | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Non-alignment means neutrality on every issue | It means independent judgment and distance from military blocs |
| India never leaned toward any bloc | India officially remained non-aligned but sometimes tilted, especially toward the Soviet Union in 1971 |
| Panchsheel prevented conflict with China | Panchsheel symbolised friendship but did not resolve border and Tibet issues |
| India and Pakistan only fought and never cooperated | They cooperated on abducted women and river waters |
| The 1971 war was only an India-Pakistan war | It was also linked to Pakistan's internal crisis and Bangladesh's liberation |
| India's nuclear policy was always weapon-focused | It began with emphasis on peaceful atomic energy and disarmament |
| Foreign policy has no domestic impact | Wars affected leadership, parties, planning, and public opinion |

## 86. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

Why did Nehru regard foreign relations as an indicator of independence?

Answer:

Nehru believed that a country is truly independent only when it controls its own external relations. A colony may have local administration, but if another power controls its foreign relations, its sovereignty is incomplete. Therefore, independent foreign policy was a test of India's freedom.

### Question 2

Why did India stay away from Cold War military blocs?

Answer:

India stayed away because it wanted to preserve sovereignty, avoid superpower rivalry, receive aid from both blocs, focus on development, and take independent positions. Joining a bloc would have reduced India's freedom of judgment and could have dragged it into conflicts unrelated to its interests.

### Question 3

How did the 1962 war affect Indian politics?

Answer:

The 1962 war damaged India's image and created a sense of national humiliation. Nehru was criticised for misjudging China and neglecting defence preparedness. Defence Minister V. K. Krishna Menon resigned. A no-confidence motion was debated in the Lok Sabha. The war also contributed to changes in the Northeast and sharpened divisions in the Communist Party.

### Question 4

Why was the 1971 war important?

Answer:

The 1971 war led to the creation of Bangladesh. It was caused by Pakistan's refusal to accept the democratic verdict in East Pakistan and its military repression there. India faced a refugee crisis of about 80 lakh people and supported Bangladesh's liberation struggle. The war ended with the surrender of about 90,000 Pakistani troops and greatly increased India's prestige.

## 87. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain the policy of non-alignment and assess its importance for India.

Answer:

Non-alignment meant that India refused to join either the US-led or Soviet-led military bloc during the Cold War. It allowed India to take independent positions, protect sovereignty, focus on development, and receive aid from both blocs. It also gave India leadership among newly independent countries in Asia and Africa. Non-alignment was important because India did not want to become a tool of superpower rivalry. However, it faced limits. India sometimes appeared inconsistent, as in its different responses to Suez and Hungary in 1956. Security crises, especially 1962 and 1971, also tested non-alignment. Still, it remained the core of India's early foreign policy.

### Question 2

Describe India's relations with China from early friendship to the 1962 war.

Answer:

India began its relations with China on a friendly note. It recognised the communist government in 1949 and supported China's place in international forums. In 1954, India and China stated the Panchsheel principles. However, tensions grew over Tibet after China took control in 1950 and India gave asylum to the Dalai Lama in 1959. A border dispute also emerged over Aksai Chin in the west and NEFA in the east. China occupied Aksai Chin and built a road there. Talks failed, and in October 1962 China attacked India. The war shocked India, damaged Nehru's prestige, caused Krishna Menon's resignation, and changed India's defence and political outlook.

### Question 3

Explain the Bangladesh crisis and the 1971 India-Pakistan war.

Answer:

In Pakistan's 1970 election, the Awami League led by Sheikh Mujib-ur Rahman won a sweeping mandate in East Pakistan. The rulers of Pakistan refused to accept this democratic verdict and arrested Sheikh Mujib. The Pakistani army then used violence against the people of East Pakistan, leading to a liberation struggle for Bangladesh. About 80 lakh refugees entered India, creating a major humanitarian and security crisis. India supported Bangladesh's struggle. Because Pakistan had support from the US and China, India signed a Treaty of Peace and Friendship with the Soviet Union in August 1971. War broke out in December 1971. Indian forces advanced rapidly in East Pakistan, Dhaka was surrounded, and about 90,000 Pakistani soldiers surrendered. Bangladesh became independent, and the Shimla Agreement of 1972 restored peace.

### Question 4

Explain India's nuclear policy from Nehru to the 1998 tests.

Answer:

India's nuclear programme began in the late 1940s under Homi J. Bhabha. Nehru supported atomic energy for peaceful development and opposed nuclear weapons. He called for global nuclear disarmament. After China tested nuclear weapons in 1964, India's security concerns increased. India rejected the 1968 NPT because it considered the treaty discriminatory. In May 1974, India conducted its first nuclear explosion and described it as peaceful. India later opposed the indefinite extension of the NPT and refused to sign the CTBT. In May 1998, India conducted nuclear tests, demonstrating military nuclear capability. Pakistan soon followed. India's doctrine later stressed credible minimum deterrence, no first use, and support for non-discriminatory disarmament.

## 88. Passage-based Practice

Nehru explained non-alignment as avoiding military blocs while maintaining friendly relations with all countries and judging issues independently.

### Question 1

Why did Nehru want India to stay away from military blocs?

He wanted India to stay away because military blocs would reduce India's freedom of decision-making and entangle India in superpower rivalry. India needed peace and independence to focus on development and protect sovereignty.

### Question 2

Did the Indo-Soviet Treaty of 1971 violate non-alignment?

Answer:

It did not formally make India a member of the Soviet bloc, so India remained officially non-aligned. However, it did show a clear pro-Soviet tilt during the Bangladesh crisis. A balanced answer should explain that the treaty was shaped by security needs and the US-China-Pakistan alignment.

### Question 3

Would non-alignment have been unnecessary without military blocs?

Answer:

Military blocs were the immediate context of non-alignment, but the broader idea of independent foreign policy would still matter. Even without blocs, a country needs freedom to judge issues according to its national interest and principles.

## 93. Final Takeaway

India's external relations were built on non-alignment, peace, sovereignty, and development, but the realities of China, Pakistan, nuclear weapons, and Cold War alignments forced India to make difficult strategic choices. The chapter's central lesson is that foreign policy is never separate from domestic politics: wars, treaties, alliances, refugees, defence spending, and leadership choices all reshape politics inside the country.
