# Chapter 3: Politics Of Planned Development

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains the third major challenge before independent India: economic development. After studying nation-building and democracy, this chapter asks how a poor, unequal, newly independent country tried to improve the well-being of its people.

The chapter is called "Politics of Planned Development" because development is not only an economic matter. Every development decision affects different groups differently. A steel plant, a dam, a land reform, a tariff policy, or a Five Year Plan can create benefits for some people and costs for others.

The central idea is simple but powerful: development involves choices, and choices involve politics.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Independent India adopted planned development because leaders wanted economic growth with social justice, but the chosen strategy created political debates over agriculture, industry, state control, private enterprise, displacement, and equality.

## 2. Why Development Was The Third Challenge

The first two challenges after independence were:

- Nation-building.
- Establishing democracy.

The third challenge was development.

Freedom had little meaning if most citizens remained poor, hungry, illiterate, unemployed, or socially excluded. The new state had to improve people's material lives.

## 3. Development As Welfare

Development was not understood only as higher production.

It was also connected to:

- Poverty reduction.
- Employment.
- Food security.
- Land reform.
- Education.
- Health.
- Irrigation.
- Industrial growth.
- Social justice.
- Reduction of inequality.

This wider meaning made development politically important.

## 4. Why Development Is Political

Development decisions are political because they decide:

- Who gets land.
- Who loses land.
- Which region gets investment.
- Which industry receives protection.
- Which class receives subsidy.
- How much the state controls the economy.
- Whether agriculture or industry gets priority.
- Whether present growth harms future generations.

These cannot be answered by technical experts alone. They require public debate, elected representatives, and political judgment.

## 5. The Orissa Steel Plant Example

The chapter uses the example of iron ore and steel investment in Orissa, now Odisha.

The state had valuable iron ore reserves, and the government hoped steel investment would bring:

- Capital.
- Employment.
- Industrial growth.
- Revenue.
- Modern infrastructure.

But tribal communities feared:

- Displacement.
- Loss of homes.
- Loss of livelihood.
- Loss of forests.
- Cultural disruption.

Environmentalists feared pollution and ecological damage.

This example shows why development is contested.

## 6. Different Meanings Of Development

Development means different things to different groups.

| Group | What development may mean to them |
|---|---|
| Industrialist | Permission, land, minerals, investment, profit |
| State government | Jobs, tax revenue, growth, prestige |
| Urban consumer | More steel, better infrastructure, lower prices |
| Adivasi community | Protection of land, forests, culture, and livelihood |
| Environmentalist | Ecological protection and sustainable use of resources |
| Central government | National growth and investment confidence |

There is no single automatic answer to whose view should prevail.

## 7. Development Requires Political Judgment

Experts can provide information.

For example:

- Economists can estimate growth.
- Engineers can estimate technical feasibility.
- Environmental experts can estimate ecological damage.
- Social scientists can study displacement.

But the final decision must balance values: growth, justice, livelihood, environment, and future costs.

That balance is political.

## 8. Growth And Justice

Most leaders agreed that development should include:

- Economic growth.
- Social and economic justice.

Growth meant increasing production, income, industry, infrastructure, and national capacity.

Justice meant reducing poverty, inequality, exploitation, and concentration of wealth.

The hard question was how to achieve both together.

## 9. Why Private Actors Alone Were Not Trusted

After independence, most leaders believed development could not be left entirely to businessmen, industrialists, and farmers.

Reasons included:

- Poverty was too deep.
- Private capital was limited.
- Infrastructure required huge investment.
- Social justice would not happen automatically.
- Regional balance needed public planning.
- Colonial rule had left an underdeveloped economy.

This created support for state-led planning.

## 10. The Big Development Questions

India had to answer several major questions.

- Should the state plan the whole economy?
- Should government run key industries?
- Should agriculture receive first priority?
- Should heavy industry receive first priority?
- Should land reforms be radical or moderate?
- Should private enterprise be controlled or encouraged?
- How should growth and justice be balanced?
- How should resources be distributed across regions?

These questions shaped the politics of development.

## 11. Modernisation

In the early years, many people equated development with modernisation.

Modernisation was often understood as becoming like the industrialised countries of the West.

It was associated with:

- Industrial growth.
- Scientific thinking.
- Urbanisation.
- Material progress.
- Breakdown of old social structures.
- Capitalism and liberalism in the Western model.

But India also considered other routes to modernity.

## 12. Two Models Before India

At independence, India had two broad development models before it.

| Model | Main features |
|---|---|
| Liberal-capitalist model | Private enterprise, market competition, limited state intervention, Western example |
| Socialist model | State control, planning, public sector, redistribution, Soviet example |

India did not fully copy either model. It adopted a mixed economy with planning.

## 13. Why The Soviet Model Was Attractive

Many Indian leaders were impressed by the Soviet Union because it had shown rapid industrial growth through planning.

Supporters included:

- Communist leaders.
- Socialist leaders.
- Many within Congress.
- Nehru and other planners.

They believed that state planning could help a poor country develop faster and reduce inequality.

## 14. Why American-style Capitalism Had Fewer Supporters

Few Indian leaders supported a fully American-style capitalist model in the early years.

Reasons included:

- Fear of inequality.
- Colonial experience of exploitative commercial policy.
- Limited private capital in India.
- Need for public infrastructure.
- Commitment to social justice from the national movement.

The state was expected to play a major role.

## 15. Left And Right

In economic politics:

| Position | Broad meaning |
|---|---|
| Left | Favours state control, redistribution, regulation, workers, peasants, and social equality |
| Right | Favours free competition, market economy, private enterprise, and limited state intervention |

In India, the Congress of the early years was not simply left or right. It contained both tendencies and tried to balance them.

## 16. Planning As Consensus

Despite disagreements, there was wide agreement that India needed planning.

This agreement existed among:

- Congress leaders.
- Socialists.
- Communists.
- Many economists.
- Some industrialists.
- Many administrators.

The debate was not whether to plan, but what kind of planning and with what priorities.

## 17. Planning Commission

The Planning Commission was set up in March 1950 by a Government of India resolution.

It was not created by the Constitution.

It had an advisory role.

Its recommendations became effective only when approved by the Union Cabinet.

The Prime Minister was its Chairperson.

## 18. Why The Planning Commission Was Powerful

Even though it was advisory, the Planning Commission became very influential because it helped decide:

- National development strategy.
- Five Year Plan priorities.
- Allocation of resources.
- Role of public sector.
- Balance between agriculture and industry.
- Long-term economic direction.

It became the central machinery of planned development.

## 19. Planning And Directive Principles

The Planning Commission's goals were connected to the Directive Principles of State Policy.

Important ideas included:

- Welfare of the people.
- Social, economic, and political justice.
- Right to adequate means of livelihood.
- Distribution of material resources for the common good.
- Prevention of concentration of wealth and means of production.

This shows that planning was linked to constitutional ideals.

## 20. NITI Aayog

The Planning Commission was replaced by NITI Aayog on 1 January 2015.

This later change is useful for understanding that institutions of development policy can change over time.

For this chapter, however, the main focus remains the Planning Commission and early Five Year Plans.

## 21. Global Support For Planning

Planning was popular internationally in the 1940s and 1950s.

Reasons included:

- The Great Depression had weakened faith in unregulated markets.
- Japan and Germany had to rebuild after war.
- The Soviet Union had shown rapid growth through planning.
- Newly independent countries wanted state-led development.

India's planning choice was part of this wider global mood.

## 22. Bombay Plan

The Bombay Plan was prepared in 1944 by leading Indian industrialists.

It proposed a planned economy and supported major state initiatives in industrial and economic investment.

This is important because it shows that planning was not supported only by socialists or communists. Even some big business groups accepted that the state had to lead development.

## 23. Why The Bombay Plan Is Important

The Bombay Plan matters because it challenges a simple assumption:

Assumption: Business groups always oppose planning.

Reality: In India, some leading industrialists supported planning because they knew that large infrastructure, capital goods, and industrial foundations required state support.

## 24. Mixed Economy

India adopted a mixed economy.

This meant:

- The public sector would control key industries and infrastructure.
- The private sector would also operate.
- The state would regulate and guide economic activity.
- Planning would set priorities.

This was a middle path between full capitalism and full socialism.

## 25. Five Year Plans

India adopted Five Year Plans, inspired partly by the Soviet experience.

A Five Year Plan allowed the government to think beyond one-year budgets.

It helped the state decide:

- Where to invest.
- Which sectors to prioritise.
- How to build infrastructure.
- How to link present spending with long-term goals.

## 26. Plan And Non-plan Expenditure

During the planning period, government expenditure was often divided into:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Plan expenditure | Spending linked to Five Year Plan priorities |
| Non-plan expenditure | Routine annual spending |

This distinction helped planners focus on long-term development works.

## 27. First Five Year Plan

The First Five Year Plan covered 1951-1956.

It sought to get the economy out of the cycle of poverty.

Its main focus was agriculture, irrigation, dams, and rural development.

This was necessary because Partition had damaged the agricultural sector and food security was a serious concern.

## 28. K. N. Raj And "Hasten Slowly"

K. N. Raj was a young economist involved in drafting the First Plan.

He argued that India should "hasten slowly" in the first two decades.

The meaning was that extremely rapid change could endanger democracy in a poor and newly independent country.

This idea supported a cautious development strategy.

## 29. Agriculture In The First Plan

The First Plan gave priority to agriculture because:

- Most people lived in villages.
- Food production was essential.
- Partition had disrupted agriculture.
- Rural poverty was severe.
- Land distribution was unequal.
- Irrigation was weak.

The plan invested in dams and irrigation, including large works such as Bhakra Nangal.

## 30. Land Reforms

The First Plan identified land distribution as a major obstacle to agricultural growth.

Land reforms aimed to change unfair patterns of land ownership and tenancy.

The broad goals were:

- Reduce landlord domination.
- Give security to tenants.
- Improve agricultural productivity.
- Promote rural justice.
- Reduce extreme inequality.

Implementation, however, varied across states and was often limited.

## 31. Savings And National Income

Planners wanted to raise national income.

To increase national income, investment had to rise.

To increase investment, savings had to rise.

But this was difficult because most people were poor and already spent very little.

This explains why development was not just a matter of desire. It required resources.

## 32. First Plan Achievements

The First Plan helped:

- Stabilise the economy after Partition.
- Emphasise agriculture.
- Expand irrigation.
- Begin large public works.
- Build confidence in planning.
- Connect development with poverty reduction.

It was cautious and rural-oriented.

## 33. First Plan Limitations

The First Plan was limited because:

- Poverty remained widespread.
- Land reform was uneven.
- Industrial capacity remained weak.
- Savings rose less than expected.
- Rural inequalities were not fully removed.

Still, it created a foundation for future planning.

## 34. Second Five Year Plan

The Second Five Year Plan began in 1956.

It focused on rapid industrialisation, especially heavy industries.

It was shaped by P. C. Mahalanobis and a team of planners and economists.

## 35. P. C. Mahalanobis

P. C. Mahalanobis was a scientist and statistician of international reputation.

He founded the Indian Statistical Institute and became the architect of the Second Plan.

He supported rapid industrialisation and a strong public sector.

## 36. Heavy Industry

Heavy industries include sectors such as:

- Steel.
- Machinery.
- Electricity.
- Railways.
- Heavy engineering.
- Communications infrastructure.

The idea was that heavy industry would create the base for long-term economic independence.

## 37. Socialist Pattern Of Society

Before the Second Plan was finalised, the Congress session at Avadi declared the goal of a socialist pattern of society.

This did not mean India became a fully socialist state.

It meant Congress accepted a larger role for the state, public sector, regulation, and social justice.

The Second Plan reflected this orientation.

## 38. Import Substitution

The government imposed tariffs and protected domestic industries from foreign competition.

This was part of import substitution.

The goal was to produce within India goods that were earlier imported.

This would reduce dependence on foreign countries and encourage Indian industry.

## 39. Public Sector In The Second Plan

Many major industries required huge capital and long-term investment.

Private Indian capital was not strong enough to build all of them.

Therefore, industries such as steel, electricity, railways, machinery, and communication expanded through the public sector.

## 40. Why The Second Plan Was A Turning Point

The Second Plan marked a turning point because it shifted emphasis from agriculture-first caution to heavy industrial transformation.

It aimed to change the structure of the economy.

Instead of only improving existing production, it wanted to build industrial capacity for the future.

## 41. Comparing The First And Second Plans

| Point | First Five Year Plan | Second Five Year Plan |
|---|---|---|
| Period | 1951-1956 | Began in 1956 |
| Main focus | Agriculture, irrigation, rural development | Heavy industry and public sector |
| Main thinker | K. N. Raj associated with cautious approach | P. C. Mahalanobis associated with industrialisation |
| Strategy | Stabilise economy and reduce poverty cycle | Structural transformation |
| Mood | Hasten slowly | Rapid industrial push |
| Key concern | Food, agriculture, land reforms | Steel, machinery, electricity, industrial base |

## 42. The Agriculture Versus Industry Debate

One of the biggest debates was whether India should give more priority to agriculture or industry.

Arguments for agriculture:

- Most people depended on agriculture.
- Food security was urgent.
- Rural poverty was widespread.
- Land reform was necessary.

Arguments for industry:

- Industrial growth was needed for long-term power.
- Heavy industry could reduce dependence on imports.
- Modern infrastructure required industrial capacity.
- Public sector industries could create national strength.

The challenge was balance.

## 43. Criticism Of Industrial Priority

Critics argued that the Second and Third Plan strategies had an urban bias.

They believed industry received too much attention while agriculture was neglected.

This created danger of food shortage.

Some critics wanted agriculture-related industries rather than heavy industries to receive priority.

## 44. Technological Backwardness

India was technologically backward in many sectors.

To build modern industry, India had to import technology from the global market.

This required foreign exchange, which was scarce.

Thus, rapid industrialisation created pressure on India's external resources.

## 45. Food Shortage Problem

If too much investment went to industry and too little to agriculture, food production could become insufficient.

Food shortage would hurt the poor most.

This is why planners found it difficult to balance industry and agriculture.

## 46. Third Five Year Plan

The Third Five Year Plan began in 1961.

It was not significantly different from the Second Plan in its broad industrial orientation.

However, the early 1960s brought serious pressures, including economic stress and later war-related costs.

## 47. Plan Holiday

The Fourth Five Year Plan was due to begin in 1966.

But India faced acute economic crisis.

The government therefore introduced a plan holiday, meaning regular Five Year Planning was suspended for a period and annual plans were used instead.

This showed the limits of the early planning model.

## 48. Achievements Of Planned Development

Planned development achieved several things:

- Built basic industrial infrastructure.
- Expanded the public sector.
- Created dams and irrigation works.
- Created a framework for long-term development.
- Established institutions for economic planning.
- Promoted scientific and technical capacity.
- Encouraged self-reliance.
- Linked development to social justice goals.

These were important foundations.

## 49. Limitations Of Planned Development

The limitations were also serious:

- Poverty remained widespread.
- Land reforms were limited.
- Inequality continued.
- Agriculture did not grow fast enough.
- Industrial growth depended on imported technology.
- Bureaucratic controls increased.
- Private sector incentives and state control coexisted uneasily.
- Regional imbalances remained.
- Food shortages created pressure.

This is why the chapter says success was more limited in development than in early democracy.

## 50. The Congress Contradiction

The chapter includes a passage about contradictions inside Congress policy.

On one side, Congress endorsed socialist principles:

- State ownership.
- Regulation.
- Control over key sectors.
- Reduction of wealth concentration.

On the other side, the Congress government also gave incentives to private investment to increase production.

This contradiction reflected the mixed economy and the coalition-like nature of Congress.

## 51. Why Congress Pursued Contradictory Policies

Congress pursued contradictory policies because it included different social groups and ideological tendencies.

It had to satisfy:

- Socialists.
- Business groups.
- Rural elites.
- Workers.
- Farmers.
- Planners.
- Regional leaders.

The result was a development policy that tried to combine state control with private incentives.

## 52. Political Implications Of The Contradiction

The contradiction had several political implications:

- Left parties criticised Congress for being too friendly to private capital.
- Right groups criticised Congress for excessive state control.
- Business groups supported some state investment but disliked controls.
- Farmers demanded more attention to agriculture.
- Congress could remain dominant by balancing all sides.

This is why development policy cannot be separated from party politics.

## 53. Charan Singh And Farmers

Charan Singh is associated with farmers and agrarian interests.

His politics stressed the importance of agriculture and rural India.

This is useful for the chapter's matching exercise and for understanding criticism of heavy industrial bias.

## 54. Verghese Kurien And Milk Cooperatives

Verghese Kurien is associated with milk cooperatives.

He played a central role in India's cooperative dairy movement.

The broader lesson is that development could also occur through cooperative institutions, not only through heavy industry.

## 55. Bihar Famine And Zoning

The exercise links the Bihar famine with zoning.

Zoning refers to food management arrangements that restricted or organised movement and distribution across zones during food scarcity.

This reminds learners that food security and agricultural policy were central development concerns.

## 56. Development And Democracy

K. N. Raj's caution matters because development policy can affect democracy.

If change is too fast, unequal, or coercive, people may resist.

If development is too slow, citizens may lose faith in democracy.

Thus planners had to consider political stability as well as economic growth.

## 57. Development And Displacement

Large development works can displace people.

Displacement creates questions:

- Who pays the cost of development?
- Are affected communities compensated fairly?
- Are livelihoods restored?
- Are cultural and ecological losses counted?
- Do affected people have a say?

The Orissa example shows that these questions remain relevant.

## 58. Development And Environment

Industrialisation can create pollution, deforestation, and ecological damage.

Environmental concerns force society to ask whether growth today is harming future generations.

This makes development a question of sustainability.

## 59. Development And Adivasi Communities

Adivasi regions often contain forests, minerals, and land needed for industrial expansion.

This creates a conflict between resource extraction and community rights.

For strong answers, mention that development debates must include people whose lives are directly affected.

## 60. Why Experts Alone Cannot Decide

Experts can measure costs and benefits, but they cannot decide values on behalf of the people.

For example, an economist may calculate output, but a displaced family measures loss in terms of home, culture, land, and belonging.

Therefore, democratic politics is necessary in development decisions.

## 61. Self-reliance

Planned development aimed to reduce dependence on foreign countries.

Self-reliance meant:

- Building domestic industry.
- Producing key goods within India.
- Reducing imports where possible.
- Developing public sector capacity.
- Building scientific and technological institutions.

This was connected to national sovereignty.

## 62. Import Substitution: Strengths And Limits

Import substitution helped Indian industry grow under protection.

But it also had limits:

- It could reduce competition.
- It could create inefficient industries.
- It required continued state support.
- It could limit export growth.

Still, in the early years, leaders saw it as necessary for building domestic capacity.

## 63. Public Sector: Strengths And Limits

The public sector helped build industries that private capital could not build easily.

It also allowed the state to pursue national goals beyond profit.

But public sector expansion could also create:

- Bureaucratic delays.
- Inefficiency.
- Political interference.
- Heavy fiscal burden.

Good answers should balance both sides.

## 64. Land Reform: Promise And Limits

Land reform promised rural justice and agricultural growth.

Its limits came from:

- Resistance by landowners.
- Legal loopholes.
- Uneven implementation across states.
- Administrative weakness.
- Political influence of rural elites.

This shows how development policy depends on power relations.

## 65. Planning And Federalism

Planning affected centre-state relations.

The central government and Planning Commission influenced resource allocation, but states implemented many development policies.

This created both cooperation and tension.

Development planning was therefore also part of Indian federal politics.

## 66. Why Development Strategy Changed Later

The early planning strategy faced increasing criticism because:

- Growth was slower than expected.
- Food shortages persisted.
- Bureaucratic controls expanded.
- Private investment faced restrictions.
- Public sector performance was uneven.
- Foreign exchange pressures increased.
- Global and domestic conditions changed.

Later governments moved away from the earlier planning model.

## 71. Multiple Choice Practice

### Question 1

Which statement about the Bombay Plan is incorrect?

Answer:

The incorrect statement is that it supported state ownership of industry in a fully socialist sense.

The Bombay Plan was prepared by industrialists and supported planning and state initiative, but it did not demand full socialist state ownership of all industry.

### Question 2

Which idea did not form part of the early phase of India's development policy?

Answer:

Liberalisation did not form part of the early development policy. The early phase emphasised planning, self-sufficiency, and state intervention.

### Question 3

The idea of planning in India drew from which influences?

Answer:

Planning drew from several influences, including the Bombay Plan, Soviet experience, Gandhian ideas in some debates, and demands from social groups. The strongest institutional influence came from planning ideas and the Soviet experience, but Indian planning was shaped by multiple streams.

## 72. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

Why is development considered a political issue?

Answer:

Development is political because it affects different groups differently. A factory or dam may create jobs and growth, but it may also displace local people or damage the environment. Deciding how to balance growth, justice, livelihood, and ecology requires political judgment and democratic approval, not only expert advice.

### Question 2

Why did India adopt planning after independence?

Answer:

India adopted planning because leaders believed development could not be left to private actors alone. Poverty, inequality, weak industry, food shortages, and limited capital required state intervention. Planning was also supported by global experience, the Soviet model, the Bombay Plan, and the national movement's commitment to growth with justice.

### Question 3

What was the role of the Planning Commission?

Answer:

The Planning Commission prepared development plans and advised the government on priorities, resource allocation, and long-term strategy. It was set up in March 1950 by a government resolution, was not a constitutional body, and its recommendations became effective only after approval by the Union Cabinet.

### Question 4

What was the major thrust of the First Five Year Plan?

Answer:

The First Five Year Plan focused mainly on agriculture, irrigation, dams, land reforms, and getting the economy out of the cycle of poverty. It gave priority to rural recovery because agriculture had been badly affected by Partition and food security was urgent.

### Question 5

How was the Second Plan different from the First Plan?

Answer:

The Second Plan focused on heavy industry and rapid industrialisation, while the First Plan focused on agriculture and rural development. The Second Plan was shaped by P. C. Mahalanobis and reflected the Congress goal of a socialist pattern of society. It expanded public sector industries such as steel, machinery, railways, electricity, and communication.

## 73. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain the major debates about development at the time of independence.

Answer:

At independence, India had to decide what kind of development it wanted. Some leaders emphasised industrialisation and modern heavy industry, while others stressed agriculture, rural poverty, and land reform. There was also a debate between liberal-capitalist and socialist models. Most leaders agreed that growth and social justice were both necessary and that the state must play a major role. However, they disagreed about how much control the state should have, how much space private enterprise should receive, and whether agriculture or industry should be the priority. These debates showed that development was a political matter involving different social interests.

### Question 2

Describe the First and Second Five Year Plans and explain their differences.

Answer:

The First Five Year Plan, covering 1951-1956, focused mainly on agriculture, irrigation, dams, and land reforms. It aimed to overcome poverty and stabilise the economy after Partition. K. N. Raj argued for a cautious approach, saying India should hasten slowly. The Second Five Year Plan, launched in 1956, focused on heavy industries and public sector expansion. It was shaped by P. C. Mahalanobis and reflected the goal of a socialist pattern of society. The First Plan was rural and agricultural in emphasis, while the Second Plan aimed at structural transformation through industrialisation.

### Question 3

Assess the achievements and limitations of planned development.

Answer:

Planned development created a framework for long-term economic policy, built public sector industries, expanded irrigation and dams, encouraged self-reliance, promoted scientific capacity, and connected development with social justice. However, it had serious limitations. Poverty and inequality remained, land reforms were uneven, agriculture was sometimes neglected, food shortages persisted, industrialisation required imported technology, and bureaucratic controls grew. The strategy also created tension between socialist goals and incentives to private investment. Therefore, planned development laid important foundations but did not solve India's development problems fully.

## 74. Passage-based Practice

### Passage Theme

Francine Frankel points to a contradiction inside early Congress development policy: socialist principles existed alongside incentives for private investment.

### Question 1

What contradiction is being described?

Answer:

The contradiction is that Congress endorsed state ownership, regulation, and control over key sectors to reduce concentration of wealth, but the Congress government also encouraged private investment to increase production. This meant socialist language and liberal economic incentives operated together.

### Question 2

What were the political implications?

Answer:

The contradiction allowed Congress to balance different groups, but it also invited criticism from both left and right. Left parties felt Congress was too friendly to private capital, while right groups felt it used too much state control. The contradiction reflected Congress's broad coalition character.

### Question 3

Was this related to the nature of opposition parties?

Answer:

Yes. Since opposition parties criticised Congress from different ideological directions, Congress tried to occupy the middle ground. Its broad policy allowed it to absorb some socialist demands while also keeping private enterprise active.

## 75. Case-based Practice

Read the situation:

A government wants to build a steel plant in a mineral-rich tribal region. Industrialists support the plan. Urban consumers want cheaper steel. Local Adivasi communities fear displacement. Environmental groups fear pollution.

### Question 1

Why is this a political issue?

Answer:

It is political because the decision affects different groups differently and requires balancing growth, jobs, livelihood, rights, and environment.

### Question 2

Can experts alone decide the issue?

Answer:

No. Experts can provide information, but elected representatives and affected people must participate because the decision involves values and social costs.

### Question 3

What would a democratic approach require?

Answer:

A democratic approach would require consultation with affected communities, environmental assessment, fair compensation, livelihood protection, transparent decision-making, and public accountability.

## 76. Exam Writing Tips

- Start answers by saying development involves choices and politics.
- Use the Orissa example to show conflicting interests.
- Do not write that planning was only a socialist idea; mention the Bombay Plan.
- Mention that the Planning Commission was not a constitutional body.
- Use K. N. Raj for the First Plan and P. C. Mahalanobis for the Second Plan.
- In comparison answers, write agriculture for the First Plan and heavy industry for the Second Plan.
- Use the phrase "growth with justice" to capture early development goals.
- Mention the contradiction between state control and private investment when answering passage questions.

## 77. High-value Facts To Remember

- Planning Commission was set up in March 1950.
- It was created by a government resolution, not by the Constitution.
- The Prime Minister was its Chairperson.
- Bombay Plan was prepared in 1944 by leading industrialists.
- First Five Year Plan covered 1951-1956.
- First Plan focused on agriculture, irrigation, dams, and land reforms.
- K. N. Raj argued that India should hasten slowly.
- Second Five Year Plan began in 1956.
- P. C. Mahalanobis was the architect of the Second Plan.
- Avadi session of Congress declared socialist pattern of society.
- Second Plan focused on heavy industries and public sector.
- Third Plan began in 1961.
- Fourth Plan was postponed in 1966 and plan holiday followed.
- NITI Aayog replaced Planning Commission on 1 January 2015.

## 78. One-page Revision Sheet

Development after independence meant economic growth with social and economic justice.

Development is political because different groups define it differently and bear different costs.

The Orissa steel example shows conflict among government, industry, Adivasi communities, environmental concerns, and national growth.

India considered liberal-capitalist and socialist models but chose a mixed economy.

Planning became widely accepted because private actors alone could not solve poverty, inequality, and underdevelopment.

The Planning Commission was set up in March 1950 and became the key institution of planned development.

The Bombay Plan showed that even some industrialists supported planning and state initiative.

The First Five Year Plan focused on agriculture, irrigation, dams, land reforms, and poverty.

The Second Five Year Plan focused on heavy industry, public sector, and structural transformation.

Planned development built important foundations but faced limitations such as food shortages, uneven land reforms, technological dependence, and urban bias.

## 79. Final Takeaway

The politics of planned development shows that economic policy is never neutral. India's early planners tried to combine growth, justice, democracy, and self-reliance through state-led planning. Their strategy built major foundations, but it also created conflicts over agriculture, industry, private enterprise, public sector, displacement, and inequality. The chapter's central lesson is that development is a political choice about the kind of society a country wants to build.
