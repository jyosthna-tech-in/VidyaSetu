# Chapter 8: Recent Developments In Indian Politics

## Learner Orientation

This chapter gives a broad view of Indian politics after 1989. It studies the decline of Congress dominance, coalition politics, the Mandal issue, the rise of OBC and Dalit politics, the growth of BJP, the Ram Janmabhoomi movement, new economic reforms, and the emergence of a new political consensus.

The chapter is not a simple list of recent events. It explains how the party system changed. India moved from Congress dominance to a multi-party and coalition era. Later, the BJP gained single-party majorities in 2014 and 2019, but coalition politics and state-level parties remained important.

Read this chapter as the final stage of the book's story: from one-party dominance to competitive multi-party politics with new social groups, new economic policies, and new ideological debates.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

After 1989, Indian politics moved from Congress dominance to a more competitive party system shaped by coalitions, OBC assertion, Dalit politics, BJP's rise, new economic reforms, regional parties, and a new political consensus.

## 2. Why 1989 Matters

The 1989 election is a turning point because it marked the end of the Congress system.

Congress had won 415 seats in 1984.

In 1989, it was reduced to 197 seats.

Congress remained important after 1989, but it no longer held the central position it had enjoyed earlier.

## 3. Five Major Developments At The End Of The 1980s

The chapter identifies five developments that shaped politics after the 1980s:

- Defeat of Congress in the 1989 election.
- Rise of the Mandal issue.
- New economic reforms.
- Ram Janmabhoomi movement and Ayodhya dispute.
- Assassination of Rajiv Gandhi in May 1991 and change in Congress leadership.

These developments reshaped the party system, social representation, economic policy, and political debate.

## 4. Congress From 1984 To 1989

Rajiv Gandhi became Prime Minister after Indira Gandhi's assassination in 1984.

Congress won a massive victory in the 1984 Lok Sabha election.

But by 1989, Congress support declined sharply.

The 1989 defeat showed that Congress could no longer dominate the party system in the old way.

## 5. Congress After 1989

Congress came back to power after the 1991 election, but the old centrality was gone.

It remained a major national party and formed governments later, especially under UPA in 2004 and 2009.

But after 1989, it had to operate in a more competitive and coalition-oriented system.

## 6. The Mandal Issue

The Mandal issue emerged when the National Front government decided in 1990 to implement Mandal Commission recommendations.

The recommendation concerned reservation in central government jobs for Other Backward Classes.

The decision sparked intense support and opposition.

It changed the nature of representation in Indian politics.

## 7. New Economic Reforms

Economic policy changed sharply around 1991.

These changes are called:

- New economic reforms.
- Structural adjustment programme.
- New economic policy.

The reforms moved India away from the earlier heavily planned and regulated economic model.

They were criticised by many movements and organisations, but governments of different parties continued them.

## 8. Ram Janmabhoomi Movement

The Ram Janmabhoomi movement and Ayodhya dispute became central to national politics.

It transformed debates over secularism, democracy, religious identity, law, and political mobilisation.

The textbook chapter takes the story up to the Supreme Court judgment of 9 November 2019 and the subsequent legal resolution.

## 9. Assassination Of Rajiv Gandhi

Rajiv Gandhi was assassinated in May 1991 during an election campaign in Tamil Nadu.

He was killed by a Sri Lankan Tamil linked to the LTTE.

After his death, Congress chose P. V. Narasimha Rao as Prime Minister.

Rao's government became associated with the 1991 economic reforms.

## 10. Era Of Coalitions

The 1989 election defeated Congress but did not give a majority to any other party.

Congress was the largest party but did not have a majority and chose to sit in opposition.

The National Front formed government with support from:

- BJP.
- Left Front.

Both supported the government from outside.

This began a new phase of coalition politics at the Centre.

## 11. National Front Government

The National Front was an alliance led by Janata Dal and included regional parties.

V. P. Singh became Prime Minister.

The unusual feature was that two ideologically opposite forces, BJP and the Left, supported it because both wanted to keep Congress out of power.

## 12. Decline Of Congress Dominance

The decline of Congress did not mean another single party immediately replaced it.

Instead, several parties rose at the same time.

The party system became fragmented and competitive.

This created space for regional parties, caste-based parties, and coalition governments.

## 13. Multi-party System After 1989

India always had many parties, but after 1989 the distribution of votes and seats changed.

No one or two parties automatically captured most seats.

From 1989 until 2014, no single party secured a clear majority in Lok Sabha.

This produced an era of minority and coalition governments.

## 14. Regional Parties In National Politics

Regional parties became crucial because no national party could form government alone for a long period.

They influenced:

- Government formation.
- Cabinet composition.
- Policy priorities.
- Centre-state relations.
- National alliances.

State-level parties became national power brokers.

## 15. United Front Government

In 1996, the United Front government came to power.

It included Janata Dal and several regional parties.

Unlike 1989, BJP did not support it.

Congress supported the United Front from outside because Congress and the Left wanted to keep BJP out of power.

This showed how unstable political alignments had become.

## 16. BJP In 1996

BJP emerged as the largest party in the 1996 election and was invited to form government.

However, most other parties opposed its policies.

It could not secure a majority in Lok Sabha.

This showed that being the largest party was not enough in coalition politics.

## 17. NDA Governments

BJP later came to power by leading coalitions.

It led government:

- From May 1998 to June 1999.
- Again after the October 1999 election.

Atal Bihari Vajpayee was Prime Minister.

The NDA government formed in 1999 completed its full term.

## 18. UPA Governments

In 2004, NDA was defeated.

Congress entered coalition politics in a major way and led the United Progressive Alliance.

UPA received support from Left Front parties.

In 2009, Congress improved its seat count from 145 to 206, and Manmohan Singh became Prime Minister for a second term.

## 19. 2014 And 2019

In 2014, BJP under Narendra Modi won 282 seats on its own.

It was the first single-party majority after 30 years.

Even with its own majority, BJP formed the NDA government with allies.

In 2019, BJP won 303 seats.

The chapter still treats coalition politics as relevant because alliances and state-level parties remained significant.

## 21. Why Coalitions Emerged

Coalitions emerged because:

- Congress lost its umbrella character.
- Regional parties became stronger.
- OBC and Dalit parties grew.
- Voters became more willing to support state-level parties.
- No single party could win a majority for many years.
- Social diversity was expressed through multiple parties.

Coalition politics reflected the diversity of Indian society.

## 22. Coalition Politics: Strengths

Coalition politics can strengthen democracy because:

- More groups get representation.
- Regional voices reach national government.
- Power is shared.
- Governments must negotiate.
- National parties cannot ignore states.

It can make democracy more inclusive.

## 23. Coalition Politics: Limits

Coalition politics can also create problems:

- Instability.
- Policy compromise.
- Smaller parties may gain disproportionate influence.
- Governments may avoid difficult decisions.
- Ideologically different parties may join only for power.

Good answers should mention both sides.

## 24. Political Rise Of OBCs

OBC means Other Backward Classes.

These are communities other than Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes that suffer from social and educational backwardness.

They are often called backward castes.

Their political rise became one of the most important developments after 1989.

## 25. OBCs And Decline Of Congress

Many backward caste groups moved away from Congress over time.

This created space for non-Congress parties.

The Janata Party in 1977 and later Janata Dal mobilised significant OBC support.

This background prepared the ground for Mandal politics.

## 26. Reservations In Southern States

Reservations for OBCs existed in several southern states since the 1960s or earlier.

But similar policies were not widely implemented in north India at the national level.

This regional difference became important when Mandal recommendations were implemented nationally.

## 27. Karpoori Thakur

Karpoori Thakur, Chief Minister of Bihar, was a pioneer in OBC reservation politics.

His government introduced reservation policies for OBCs in Bihar.

This helped bring backward caste claims into mainstream politics.

## 28. Mandal Commission

The central government appointed the Second Backward Classes Commission in 1978.

It became popularly known as the Mandal Commission after its chairperson B. P. Mandal.

It studied social and educational backwardness and recommended ways to identify and support backward classes.

## 29. Mandal Commission Recommendations

The Commission gave its report in 1980.

It recommended that backward classes should be understood largely as backward castes.

It found that these groups had low presence in education and public employment.

It recommended 27 percent reservation in central government jobs and educational institutions for OBCs.

It also suggested other measures such as land reform.

## 30. Implementation In 1990

In August 1990, the National Front government decided to implement the Mandal recommendation for reservation in central government jobs.

This triggered intense debate and violent anti-Mandal protests, especially in parts of north India.

Supporters saw it as social justice.

Opponents saw it as unfair or divisive.

## 31. Indra Sawhney Case

The decision was challenged in the Supreme Court.

The case became known as the Indra Sawhney case.

In November 1992, the Supreme Court upheld the government's decision.

Over time, most major parties accepted the policy of OBC reservation.

## 32. Mandalisation

Mandalisation refers to the transformation of politics after Mandal.

It meant:

- OBC identity became politically visible.
- Backward caste leaders gained influence.
- Parties demanded greater OBC representation.
- Social justice became a national political issue.
- Caste-based political mobilisation expanded.

## 33. Rise Of Dalit Politics

The 1980s also saw the rise of Dalit political organisation.

In 1978, BAMCEF was formed.

It stood for political power to the Bahujan: SC, ST, OBC, and minorities.

From this stream came DS-4 and later BSP under Kanshi Ram.

## 34. Kanshi Ram

Kanshi Ram built Bahujan politics around the idea that socially oppressed and backward groups form a majority.

He saw political power as the key to social equality.

He founded BAMCEF, DS-4, and later Bahujan Samaj Party in 1984.

## 35. Bahujan Samaj Party

BSP began as a small party supported mainly by Dalit voters in Punjab, Haryana, and Uttar Pradesh.

In the 1989 and 1991 elections, it achieved a breakthrough in Uttar Pradesh.

It became the first major party supported mainly by Dalit voters to achieve this kind of political success.

Over time, BSP expanded support to other social groups while retaining a strong Dalit base.

## 36. OBC Politics And Dalit Politics

OBC politics and Dalit politics sometimes developed independently and sometimes competed with each other.

Both changed representation by bringing socially marginal groups into political power.

They also changed party strategies, alliances, and leadership patterns.

## 37. Communalism, Secularism, And Democracy

Another long-term development was the rise of politics based on religious identity.

This produced debates about:

- Secularism.
- Minority rights.
- Gender justice.
- Personal law.
- Cultural nationalism.
- Constitutional equality.

BJP's rise is studied in this context.

## 38. From Jana Sangh To BJP

The Bharatiya Jana Sangh had merged into the Janata Party after the Emergency.

After the Janata Party broke up, former Jana Sangh supporters formed the Bharatiya Janata Party in 1980.

Initially, BJP adopted Gandhian socialism along with cultural nationalism.

It did not perform well in the 1984 election.

## 39. BJP After 1986

After 1986, BJP emphasised nationalism more strongly.

It also used Hindutva for political mobilisation.

This shift helped BJP consolidate its political identity and grow in the 1990s.

## 40. Hindutva

Hindutva was popularised by Vinayak Damodar Savarkar.

It understands Indian nationhood through the idea that India is both fatherland and holy land for Hindus.

Supporters of Hindutva argue that a strong nation can be built on a united national culture.

Critics argue that it creates tension with secular and plural ideas of citizenship.

## 41. Shah Bano Case

In 1985, Shah Bano, a divorced Muslim woman aged 62, filed a case for maintenance from her former husband.

The Supreme Court ruled in her favour.

Some orthodox Muslim leaders saw the judgment as interference in Muslim Personal Law.

## 42. Muslim Women Act, 1986

The government passed the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986.

This law nullified the effect of the Supreme Court judgment in the Shah Bano case.

The decision was criticised by:

- Women's organisations.
- Many Muslim groups.
- Intellectuals.
- BJP, which called it minority appeasement.

The case raised the tension between personal law, gender justice, and secularism.

## 43. Ayodhya Issue

The Ayodhya issue involved legal, religious, cultural, and political claims over the Ram Janmabhoomi site.

The dispute was connected to claims about the birthplace of Shri Ram and the ownership of the site.

It became one of the most important political issues of the period.

## 44. Long History Of The Dispute

The chapter notes that the dispute had a long history beginning in the sixteenth century.

A three-dome structure was built at the site in 1528.

The site later became the subject of legal disputes.

The structure was sealed in 1949 after court proceedings began.

In 1986, a district court ordered the unlocking of the structure, allowing worship.

## 45. 1992 Demolition

In 1992, the structure at the disputed site was demolished.

Many critics saw this as a major challenge to Indian democracy and secularism.

The event intensified debates over religious mobilisation and constitutional order.

## 46. Supreme Court Judgment Of 2019

On 9 November 2019, a five-judge constitutional bench of the Supreme Court delivered its judgment on the Ayodhya dispute.

The judgment allotted the disputed site for construction of a Ram temple through a trust.

It also directed that suitable land be allotted for construction of a mosque to the Sunni Central Waqf Board.

The textbook presents this as resolution through legal and democratic procedures.

## 47. Ayodhya And Democratic Resolution

The chapter's key lesson is that conflicts in a plural society should be handled through:

- Law.
- Courts.
- Mediation attempts.
- Public procedures.
- Constitutional values.

Even sensitive conflicts require due process rather than violence.

## 48. Emergence Of New Consensus

After 1989, politics became highly competitive.

At the same time, a broad consensus emerged among major parties on several issues.

This is important because parties competed intensely, but still accepted some common policy directions.

## 49. Element 1: New Economic Policies

Most major political parties accepted new economic policies after 1991.

Many groups and movements opposed these policies, but governments led by different parties continued them.

Supporters believed reforms would make India more prosperous and globally powerful.

Critics worried about inequality, displacement, workers' rights, and social security.

## 50. Element 2: Backward Caste Claims

Most parties accepted the political and social claims of backward castes.

There was broad support for reservation in education and employment.

Parties also accepted that OBCs should receive a share in political power.

This was a major change from earlier elite-dominated politics.

## 51. Element 3: Role Of State-level Parties

State-level parties became central to national governance.

The line between national and state-level parties became less rigid.

Regional parties shared power at the Centre and shaped national policy.

## 52. Element 4: Pragmatic Alliances

Coalition politics increased pragmatic alliances.

Parties often joined alliances for power sharing even when they did not fully agree ideologically.

For example, many NDA allies did not share the full Hindutva ideology of BJP but joined the coalition.

This shows a shift from ideology-only alliances to power-sharing arrangements.

## 53. Popular Movements And New Issues

Even as parties operated within consensus, popular movements raised issues such as:

- Poverty.
- Displacement.
- Minimum wages.
- Livelihood.
- Social security.
- Justice.
- Gender.
- Caste.
- Regional rights.

These movements reminded the state of responsibilities that electoral parties might neglect.

## 54. BJP And Congress Competition

After 1989, BJP and Congress became the two largest poles of national competition.

However, India did not become a simple two-party system because regional parties and social justice parties remained powerful.

Coalitions led by BJP or Congress often needed other parties.

## 55. Lok Sabha Elections From 2004 To 2019

The chapter covers the period through 2019.

Key points:

- 2004: NDA defeated; UPA came to power with Congress leadership.
- 2009: UPA returned; Congress increased seats from 145 to 206.
- 2014: BJP won 282 seats and led NDA.
- 2019: BJP won 303 seats and again led NDA.

## 59. Main Issues After 1989

Major issues after 1989 included:

- Coalition politics.
- Decline of Congress dominance.
- Rise of BJP.
- Mandal and OBC reservation.
- Dalit political mobilisation.
- New economic reforms.
- Ram Janmabhoomi movement.
- Role of regional parties.
- New political consensus.

These issues produced changing alliances such as National Front, United Front, NDA, and UPA.

## 60. Why Ideology Became Less Decisive In Alliances

In coalition politics, parties often joined or supported governments for strategic reasons.

Examples:

- BJP and Left both supported National Front in 1989 to keep Congress out.
- Congress supported United Front in 1996 to keep BJP out.
- NDA included parties that did not fully share BJP's ideology.

This supports the view that alliances were often pragmatic rather than purely ideological.

## 61. But Ideology Did Not Disappear

It would be wrong to say ideology disappeared completely.

Ideology remained important in:

- Mandal debates.
- Secularism debates.
- Hindutva politics.
- Economic reforms.
- Social justice politics.
- Dalit and OBC mobilisation.

Coalition politics reduced ideological purity in alliances, but ideological conflicts continued.

## 62. Congress Continued To Matter

Even after decline of dominance, Congress continued to influence politics.

Reasons:

- It remained a national party.
- It formed government in 1991.
- It led UPA in 2004 and 2009.
- It remained a major pole of competition.
- Many regional and national parties positioned themselves for or against Congress.

Decline of dominance did not mean disappearance.

## 63. BJP As A Significant Force

BJP became significant through several stages:

- Jana Sangh tradition.
- Merger into Janata Party after Emergency.
- Formation of BJP in 1980.
- Initial Gandhian socialism and cultural nationalism.
- Weak performance in 1984.
- Shift after 1986 toward nationalism and Hindutva.
- Growth through Ayodhya and anti-Congress politics.
- Largest party in 1996.
- NDA governments in 1998 and 1999.
- Single-party majorities in 2014 and 2019.

## 64. Advantages Of Multi-party System

India's multi-party system has several advantages:

- Represents social diversity.
- Gives space to regional parties.
- Allows Dalit and OBC representation.
- Prevents monopoly of one party.
- Encourages negotiation.
- Makes national politics more inclusive.

This is useful for essay answers defending India's party system.

## 65. Challenges Of Multi-party System

Challenges include:

- Fragmentation.
- Unstable governments.
- Opportunistic alliances.
- Policy compromise.
- Difficulty aggregating many interests.
- Leadership conflicts.

The party system must balance representation with governability.

## 66. Party System And Representation

The chapter's final passage asks whether parties can accommodate and aggregate diverse interests.

Accommodation means giving groups space and recognition.

Aggregation means combining many demands into a workable policy programme.

Modern Indian parties face the challenge of doing both.

## 67. Why Accommodation And Aggregation Matter

If parties fail to accommodate interests, groups may feel excluded.

If parties fail to aggregate interests, politics becomes fragmented and unstable.

A good party system must both represent diversity and create governable choices.

## 71. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

Why did coalition politics emerge after 1989?

Answer:

Coalition politics emerged because Congress lost its old dominance and no other party immediately replaced it. Regional parties, caste-based parties, OBC and Dalit parties, and BJP all gained strength. From 1989 to 2014, no single party won a clear majority, so governments required support from multiple parties.

### Question 2

What was the Mandal Commission and why was it important?

Answer:

The Mandal Commission was the Second Backward Classes Commission appointed in 1978 under B. P. Mandal. It studied social and educational backwardness and recommended 27 percent reservation for OBCs in central government jobs and educational institutions. Its implementation in 1990 made OBC identity and representation central to national politics.

### Question 3

How did BSP change Indian politics?

Answer:

BSP brought Dalit political assertion into mainstream electoral politics. Under Kanshi Ram, it built politics around the Bahujan idea, meaning SC, ST, OBC, and minorities as a majority. It achieved electoral breakthroughs in Uttar Pradesh in 1989 and 1991 and showed that a party based mainly on Dalit support could become a major political force.

### Question 4

What are the four elements of the new consensus?

Answer:

The four elements are broad acceptance of new economic policies, acceptance of backward caste claims and reservations, acceptance of state-level parties in national governance, and pragmatic alliances that prioritise power sharing over strict ideological agreement.

## 72. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain the main developments in Indian politics after 1989.

Answer:

After 1989, Indian politics changed in several ways. Congress dominance declined, and the 1989 election marked the end of the Congress system. Coalition politics began because no single party could form government alone for many years. The Mandal issue brought OBC reservation and backward caste representation to national politics. Dalit politics grew through BAMCEF, DS-4, and BSP. BJP emerged as a major force through nationalism, Hindutva, and the Ayodhya movement. New economic reforms changed India's development strategy from 1991 onward. Regional parties became crucial in national governance. Together, these developments created a more competitive and fragmented party system.

### Question 2

Assess the significance of Mandal politics.

Answer:

Mandal politics was significant because it transformed social representation in Indian politics. The Mandal Commission recommended 27 percent reservation for OBCs, and the National Front government implemented this in 1990. The decision created intense protests and support, and the Supreme Court upheld it in the Indra Sawhney case in 1992. Mandalisation made OBC identity politically visible, encouraged backward caste leadership, and forced major parties to accept social justice claims. It changed politics from elite-dominated competition to broader participation by backward castes.

### Question 3

Explain the rise of BJP as a major political force.

Answer:

BJP emerged from the Jana Sangh tradition after the Janata Party broke up. It was formed in 1980 and initially adopted Gandhian socialism along with cultural nationalism, but it did poorly in 1984. After 1986, it emphasised nationalism and Hindutva more strongly. The Shah Bano case allowed it to criticise Congress on minority appeasement, and the Ayodhya movement became a major mobilisation issue. BJP became the largest party in 1996, led coalition governments in 1998 and 1999, and later won single-party majorities in 2014 and 2019 under Narendra Modi.

### Question 4

Explain the new political consensus after 1989.

Answer:

After 1989, intense political competition existed alongside a broad consensus among major parties. Most parties accepted new economic policies, even though many movements criticised them. Parties accepted backward caste claims and supported reservation for OBCs. State-level parties became accepted participants in national governance. Coalition politics also made pragmatic alliances common, even among parties with ideological differences. This consensus did not remove conflict, but it created a shared framework within which parties competed.

## 74. Case-based Practice

Read the situation:

A national election weakens a dominant party but gives no other party a majority. Several regional and caste-based parties become important. A coalition forms with outside support from ideologically different parties.

### Question 1

## 75. Exam Writing Tips

- Do not write that Congress ended after 1989; write that Congress dominance ended.
- In coalition answers, mention National Front, United Front, NDA, and UPA.
- In Mandal answers, include 1978, 1980, 1990, 1992, and 27 percent reservation.
- In BJP answers, connect Jana Sangh, BJP formation in 1980, Hindutva, Ayodhya, and coalition governments.
- In Ayodhya answers, write carefully and mention legal resolution through the Supreme Court judgment of 9 November 2019.
- In consensus answers, include all four elements.
- Use "representation plus governability" as a strong phrase for party system essays.
