# Chapter 5: Challenges To And Restoration Of The Congress System

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how Congress dominance was challenged in the 1960s and how Indira Gandhi restored Congress dominance in a new form. The chapter continues the story from the era of one-party dominance, but the Congress of the early 1970s was not the same as the old broad Congress system of the 1950s.

The central movement of the chapter is:

- Nehru's death created the challenge of succession.
- Shastri's death created a second succession crisis.
- The 1967 election weakened Congress dominance.
- Opposition parties used non-Congressism and coalitions.
- Congress split in 1969 between the Syndicate and Indira Gandhi.
- Indira Gandhi used left-leaning policies and Garibi Hatao to build a new support base.
- Congress dominance returned after 1971, but the party became more centralised and leader-dependent.

Read this chapter as a story of transition: from the old Congress system to the Indira Gandhi-led new Congress.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

Congress dominance weakened after Nehru and the 1967 elections, but Indira Gandhi restored it by defeating the Syndicate, splitting the party, using a pro-poor ideological appeal, and building a new leader-centred Congress.

## 2. Why This Chapter Matters

The chapter matters because it shows that dominance in democracy is never permanent.

The Congress that looked unbeatable in the 1950s faced:

- Leadership crises.
- Economic crisis.
- Opposition unity.
- Regional challenges.
- Internal factional conflict.
- Electoral setbacks.

Yet it returned to dominance under Indira Gandhi. The return, however, changed the nature of the party system.

## 3. From Congress System To Crisis

In the early years, Congress dominated because it was broad, inclusive, and rooted in the freedom movement.

By the 1960s, this system came under pressure.

The party could no longer easily accommodate all differences inside itself. Opposition parties became more organised. Regional and social groups began asserting themselves.

## 4. The Challenge Of Political Succession

Jawaharlal Nehru died in May 1964.

He had been India's Prime Minister since independence and had unmatched authority.

His death raised two questions:

- After Nehru, who?
- After Nehru, what?

The second question was deeper. It asked whether Indian democracy would survive without Nehru's leadership.

## 5. Why Succession Was A Test Of Democracy

Many newly independent countries failed to manage peaceful leadership change.

Some turned to:

- Military rule.
- One-party authoritarianism.
- Personal rule.
- Political instability.

India's ability to choose a new leader peacefully became an important test of democratic maturity.

## 6. The Dangerous Decade

The 1960s were sometimes called the dangerous decade.

Observers feared that unresolved problems could threaten India's democracy:

- Poverty.
- Inequality.
- Communal tensions.
- Regional divisions.
- Food shortage.
- Economic crisis.
- Leadership uncertainty.

These fears did not destroy democracy, but they shaped political anxiety.

## 7. From Nehru To Shastri

After Nehru's death, K. Kamaraj, the Congress president, consulted party leaders and Congress MPs.

There was consensus in favour of Lal Bahadur Shastri.

Shastri was chosen unanimously as leader of the Congress parliamentary party and became Prime Minister.

This peaceful succession proved many critics wrong.

## 8. Lal Bahadur Shastri

Shastri was a non-controversial leader from Uttar Pradesh.

He had served in Nehru's cabinet and was respected for:

- Simplicity.
- Moral integrity.
- Commitment to principles.
- Administrative experience.

Earlier, he had resigned as Railway Minister after a major railway accident, accepting moral responsibility.

## 9. Challenges Before Shastri

Shastri served as Prime Minister from 1964 to 1966.

His short term faced two major challenges:

- Food crisis caused by drought and failed monsoons.
- War with Pakistan in 1965.

India was also still recovering from the economic effects of the 1962 war with China.

## 10. Jai Jawan Jai Kisan

Shastri's slogan "Jai Jawan Jai Kisan" captured the two urgent needs of the time.

| Phrase | Meaning in context |
|---|---|
| Jai Jawan | Support for soldiers during war |
| Jai Kisan | Support for farmers during food crisis |

The slogan connected national security with food security.

## 11. Shastri's Death

Shastri died suddenly on 11 January 1966 in Tashkent.

He was there after signing an agreement with Pakistan's President Ayub Khan to end the 1965 war.

His death created a second leadership crisis within two years.

## 12. From Shastri To Indira Gandhi

After Shastri's death, Congress faced a contest between:

- Morarji Desai.
- Indira Gandhi.

Morarji Desai was experienced and had served as Chief Minister of Bombay state and as a central minister.

Indira Gandhi was Nehru's daughter, had served as Congress President, and had been Information and Broadcasting Minister in Shastri's cabinet.

## 13. Secret Ballot In Congress

This time the succession was not unanimous.

The contest was settled by a secret ballot among Congress MPs.

Indira Gandhi defeated Morarji Desai by winning support from more than two-thirds of Congress MPs.

This peaceful but competitive leadership change again showed democratic maturity.

## 14. Why Senior Leaders Backed Indira Gandhi

Many senior Congress leaders supported Indira Gandhi because they believed she lacked administrative experience and would depend on them.

They expected to guide or control her.

This expectation later proved wrong.

## 15. Indira Gandhi's Early Difficulty

Indira Gandhi became Prime Minister in 1966.

She had to quickly:

- Handle economic crisis.
- Prepare for the 1967 election.
- Deal with senior Congress leaders.
- Establish her own authority.
- Respond to public unrest.

She was initially seen by some as politically inexperienced.

## 16. Why 1967 Was A Landmark

The fourth general election of 1967 was a landmark because it sharply weakened Congress dominance.

For the first time:

- Congress faced serious challenge at national and state levels.
- Opposition parties were more united.
- Congress lost power in many states.
- Many senior Congress leaders lost elections.
- Coalition governments became common in states.

The election showed that Congress could be defeated.

## 17. Context Of The 1967 Election

The years before the 1967 election were marked by:

- Deaths of two Prime Ministers.
- A new Prime Minister with limited experience.
- Failed monsoons.
- Widespread drought.
- Decline in agricultural production.
- Food shortage.
- Foreign exchange crisis.
- Drop in industrial production.
- Military expenditure after wars.
- Price rise.
- Unemployment.
- Public protests.

This created strong anti-Congress feeling.

## 18. Devaluation Of The Rupee

One of Indira Gandhi's early decisions was devaluation of the rupee.

Before devaluation, one US dollar cost less than Rs. 5.

After devaluation, it cost more than Rs. 7.

Many saw this as done under pressure from the United States.

The decision added to political criticism.

## 19. Public Protest

People protested against:

- Price rise.
- Food scarcity.
- Unemployment.
- Corruption.
- Economic hardship.

Bandhs and hartals became frequent.

The government often treated these protests as law and order problems, which increased public bitterness.

## 20. Non-Congressism

Opposition parties realised that divided votes helped Congress win.

Ram Manohar Lohia gave a name to the strategy of anti-Congress unity: non-Congressism.

The idea was that all non-Congress parties should unite or coordinate to defeat Congress.

## 21. Lohia's Argument

Lohia argued that Congress rule had become undemocratic and anti-poor.

Therefore, he believed opposition unity was necessary to reclaim democracy.

This brought together parties with very different ideologies.

## 22. Electoral Verdict Of 1967

In 1967, Congress retained power at the Centre, but with its lowest seats and vote share since 1952.

Many cabinet ministers lost.

Prominent defeated Congress leaders included:

- Kamaraj in Tamil Nadu.
- S. K. Patil in Maharashtra.
- Atulya Ghosh in West Bengal.
- K. B. Sahay in Bihar.

The result was described by observers as a political earthquake.

## 23. State-level Setback

Congress lost majority in seven states.

In two more states, defections prevented it from forming government.

The nine states where Congress lost power included:

- Punjab.
- Haryana.
- Uttar Pradesh.
- Madhya Pradesh.
- Bihar.
- West Bengal.
- Orissa.
- Madras.
- Kerala.

This was a major blow to the Congress system.

## 24. DMK Victory In Madras

In Madras state, now Tamil Nadu, the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam won a clear majority.

The DMK had led anti-Hindi agitations against the imposition of Hindi as official language.

This was the first time a non-Congress party secured a majority on its own in a state.

## 25. Rise Of Coalitions

After 1967, no single party had a clear majority in several states.

Non-Congress parties came together to form coalition governments.

These joint legislative parties were called Samyukt Vidhayak Dal, or SVD.

## 26. SVD Governments

SVD governments were often ideologically diverse.

For example, in Bihar, the coalition included:

- Socialist parties.
- CPI on the left.
- Jana Sangh on the right.

In Punjab, the Popular United Front included rival Akali groups, communist parties, socialists, Republican Party, and Jana Sangh.

These coalitions showed the practical power of non-Congressism.

## 27. Defection

Defection means an elected representative leaves the party on whose ticket they won and joins another party.

After 1967, defections played an important role in making and unmaking governments.

Breakaway Congress legislators helped install non-Congress governments in Haryana, Madhya Pradesh, and Uttar Pradesh.

## 28. Aya Ram, Gaya Ram

The phrase Aya Ram, Gaya Ram became popular after Gaya Lal, an MLA from Haryana, changed parties three times in a fortnight in 1967.

The phrase came to symbolise frequent floor crossing by legislators.

It became a sign of unstable and opportunistic politics.

Later, the Constitution was amended to reduce defections.

## 29. Limits Of Non-Congress Coalitions

Many non-Congress coalition governments did not last long.

Reasons included:

- Ideological differences.
- Personal ambition.
- Defections.
- Lack of common programme.
- Weak coordination.
- Congress attempts to regain power.

This meant Congress was weakened but not yet replaced by a stable alternative.

## 30. Split In The Congress

After the 1967 election, the real challenge to Indira Gandhi came from inside Congress.

The party retained power at the Centre, but its authority was weakened.

Internal conflict between Indira Gandhi and senior party leaders intensified.

## 31. The Syndicate

The Syndicate was the informal name for a group of powerful Congress organisation leaders.

It included leaders such as:

- K. Kamaraj.
- S. K. Patil.
- S. Nijalingappa.
- N. Sanjeeva Reddy.
- Atulya Ghosh.

The Syndicate controlled the party organisation and had influence over leadership and policy.

## 32. Why The Syndicate Was Powerful

The Syndicate was powerful because:

- Its leaders controlled state units.
- They had organisational experience.
- They influenced candidate selection.
- They had helped choose Shastri.
- They had helped Indira Gandhi become Prime Minister.
- They expected her to follow their advice.

## 33. Indira Gandhi Versus Syndicate

Indira Gandhi wanted to assert her independence.

She gradually sidelined the Syndicate by:

- Building her own advisory circle.
- Taking bold policy positions.
- Appealing directly to the people.
- Using ideological language.
- Presenting herself as pro-poor.

She converted a power struggle into an ideological struggle.

## 34. Ten Point Programme

In May 1967, the Congress Working Committee adopted a Ten Point Programme.

It included:

- Social control of banks.
- Nationalisation of general insurance.
- Ceiling on urban property and income.
- Public distribution of food grains.
- Land reforms.
- House sites for the rural poor.

The programme gave government policy a left orientation.

## 35. Why The Ten Point Programme Mattered

The Ten Point Programme helped Indira Gandhi:

- Present herself as pro-poor.
- Distinguish herself from conservative Congress leaders.
- Gain support from left-leaning groups.
- Shift attention from internal party power to social policy.
- Build a new popular appeal.

The Syndicate formally approved it but had reservations.

## 36. Presidential Election Of 1969

The conflict became open in 1969 after President Zakir Hussain died.

The Syndicate nominated N. Sanjeeva Reddy as the official Congress candidate.

Indira Gandhi opposed this choice.

She encouraged Vice-President V. V. Giri to contest as an independent candidate.

## 37. Bank Nationalisation

During this conflict, Indira Gandhi announced the nationalisation of fourteen major private banks.

This policy strengthened her pro-poor and socialist image.

It also sharpened differences with Morarji Desai, who was Deputy Prime Minister and Finance Minister.

Desai eventually left the government.

## 38. Privy Purse

Privy purse was the allowance promised to former princes after princely states joined India.

At the time of accession, these privileges were accepted as part of integration.

Later, many argued that hereditary privileges contradicted equality and social justice.

Indira Gandhi supported abolition of privy purses.

## 39. Debate On Privy Purse

Supporters of abolition argued:

- Hereditary privilege violated equality.
- Public money should not support former rulers.
- Democratic India should not preserve princely status.

Opponents such as Morarji Desai argued:

- Abolition was a breach of faith.
- The promises made during accession should be respected.

The issue became important in the 1971 election.

## 40. Conscience Vote

The Congress president S. Nijalingappa issued a whip asking Congress MPs and MLAs to vote for Sanjeeva Reddy.

Indira Gandhi called for a conscience vote.

This meant members should vote according to personal judgment rather than party direction.

V. V. Giri won the election.

The official Congress candidate lost.

## 41. Formal Split In Congress

The defeat of Sanjeeva Reddy formalised the Congress split.

The Congress president expelled Indira Gandhi from the party.

Indira Gandhi claimed that her group was the real Congress.

By November 1969, two groups had emerged:

| Group | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Congress(O) | Congress Organisation, led by Syndicate leaders |
| Congress(R) | Congress Requisitionists, led by Indira Gandhi |

They were also called Old Congress and New Congress.

## 42. Ideological Framing Of The Split

Indira Gandhi presented the split as a battle between:

- Socialists and conservatives.
- Pro-poor and pro-rich.
- New politics and old organisation.

This framing helped her appeal to voters beyond Congress organisation networks.

## 43. Minority Government After Split

After the split, Indira Gandhi's government became a minority government.

It survived with issue-based support from other parties, including:

- Communist Party of India.
- DMK.

Indira Gandhi wanted a fresh mandate to end this dependence.

## 44. Dissolution Of Lok Sabha

In December 1970, Indira Gandhi recommended dissolution of the Lok Sabha.

The fifth general election was held in February 1971.

This was a bold move because Congress(R) was new and organisationally weaker than Congress(O).

## 45. The 1971 Contest

The 1971 contest seemed difficult for Indira Gandhi.

Congress(O) had many old organisation leaders.

Major non-communist, non-Congress opposition parties formed the Grand Alliance.

Congress(R) had an alliance with CPI.

## 46. Grand Alliance

The Grand Alliance included:

- Samyukta Socialist Party.
- Praja Socialist Party.
- Bharatiya Jana Sangh.
- Swatantra Party.
- Bharatiya Kranti Dal.

These parties had different ideologies but joined together against Indira Gandhi.

## 47. Indira Hatao Versus Garibi Hatao

Indira Gandhi argued that the opposition had only one common slogan: Indira Hatao, meaning remove Indira.

She offered a positive slogan: Garibi Hatao, meaning remove poverty.

This contrast was politically powerful.

## 48. Meaning Of Garibi Hatao

Garibi Hatao was not only a slogan.

It represented a strategy to build support among:

- Landless labourers.
- Dalits.
- Adivasis.
- Minorities.
- Women.
- Unemployed youth.
- The poor and disadvantaged.

It helped Indira Gandhi create an independent nationwide support base.

## 49. Policies Linked To Garibi Hatao

Indira Gandhi focused on:

- Public sector growth.
- Land ceiling.
- Ceiling on urban property.
- Reduction of income and opportunity disparities.
- Abolition of princely privileges.
- Pro-poor image of the state.

These policies connected ideology with electoral strategy.

## 50. 1971 Election Results

The 1971 results were dramatic.

The Congress(R)-CPI alliance won:

- 375 Lok Sabha seats.
- 48.4 percent votes.

Indira Gandhi's Congress(R) alone won:

- 352 seats.
- About 44 percent of popular votes.

Congress(O) won only 16 seats.

The Grand Alliance won fewer than 60 seats combined.

## 51. Meaning Of The 1971 Verdict

The 1971 election established Indira Gandhi's Congress(R) as the real Congress in public perception.

It also restored Congress dominance at the Centre.

The old organisation leaders lost power and prestige.

## 52. Bangladesh War And Popularity

Soon after the 1971 election, the Bangladesh crisis and India-Pakistan war occurred.

India's victory and the creation of Bangladesh greatly increased Indira Gandhi's popularity.

She was seen as:

- Protector of the poor.
- Strong nationalist leader.
- Decisive Prime Minister.

## 53. 1972 State Elections

After the 1971 victory and Bangladesh war, Congress swept through state assembly elections in 1972.

Congress returned to power in almost all states.

With this, the dominance of Congress appeared restored.

## 54. Was The Old Congress System Restored?

This is the central analytical question.

The answer is no, not fully.

Indira Gandhi restored Congress dominance, but not the old Congress system.

The new Congress was different from the old Congress.

## 55. Old Congress Versus New Congress

| Feature | Old Congress system | New Congress under Indira Gandhi |
|---|---|---|
| Organisation | Strong, broad, faction-based | Weaker organisation |
| Leadership | Collective and negotiated | Centralised around supreme leader |
| Social base | Broad umbrella across many groups | More focused on poor, women, Dalits, Adivasis, minorities |
| Internal factions | Many factions accommodated conflict | Fewer autonomous factions |
| Method of appeal | Party network and freedom movement legacy | Direct popular appeal and slogans |
| Capacity to absorb conflict | High | Reduced |

## 56. Leader-centred Politics

The new Congress relied heavily on Indira Gandhi's personal popularity.

This gave it strong electoral appeal but weakened internal party democracy.

Decision-making became more centralised.

## 57. Shrinking Democratic Space

The chapter warns that even as Congress became popular, spaces for democratic expression narrowed.

Popular unrest around development and deprivation continued.

The concentration of authority around one leader later contributed to the crisis described in the next chapter.

## 58. Why Congress Dominance Returned

Congress dominance returned because:

- Indira Gandhi defeated the Syndicate.
- She gained control over the party.
- She used pro-poor slogans effectively.
- The Grand Alliance lacked a coherent programme.
- Congress(R) won decisively in 1971.
- Bangladesh war increased her popularity.
- Congress swept state elections in 1972.

## 59. Why The Restoration Was Different

The restoration was different because:

- Congress was no longer an umbrella party in the old sense.
- It depended more on one leader.
- Organisation became weaker.
- Internal dissent had less space.
- Ideological appeal replaced broad internal accommodation.

The Congress system was restored by changing its nature.

## 68. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

What was non-Congressism?

Answer:

Non-Congressism was the strategy of bringing together non-Congress parties to defeat Congress. Ram Manohar Lohia gave this strategy its name. The idea was that opposition votes were divided, helping Congress win, so parties with different ideologies should coordinate against Congress.

### Question 2

What was the Syndicate?

Answer:

The Syndicate was an informal group of powerful Congress organisation leaders such as K. Kamaraj, S. K. Patil, S. Nijalingappa, N. Sanjeeva Reddy, and Atulya Ghosh. It influenced party organisation, leadership selection, and policy. It helped Indira Gandhi become Prime Minister but later became her main internal rival.

### Question 3

What led to the formal split of Congress in 1969?

Answer:

The immediate issue was the 1969 presidential election. The Syndicate supported N. Sanjeeva Reddy as the official Congress candidate, while Indira Gandhi supported V. V. Giri and called for a conscience vote. Giri won, the official candidate lost, and Congress split into Congress(O) and Congress(R).

### Question 4

Why did the Grand Alliance fail in 1971?

Answer:

The Grand Alliance failed because it united mainly around opposition to Indira Gandhi and lacked a coherent programme. Indira Gandhi offered a positive and popular slogan, Garibi Hatao, and presented herself as pro-poor. Congress(R) gained a strong popular mandate.

## 69. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain why the 1967 election was a turning point in Indian politics.

Answer:

The 1967 election was a turning point because it seriously challenged Congress dominance for the first time. Congress retained power at the Centre but with its lowest seats and vote share since 1952. It lost power in many states, and several senior Congress leaders were defeated. Opposition parties used non-Congressism to reduce vote division. Coalition governments and SVD governments emerged in states. Defections also became important in making and unmaking governments. The election proved that Congress could be defeated and opened a new phase of competitive politics.

### Question 2

Explain the conflict between Indira Gandhi and the Syndicate.

Answer:

The Syndicate was a group of powerful Congress organisation leaders who expected Indira Gandhi to depend on them after becoming Prime Minister. Indira Gandhi instead tried to assert her independence. She built her own advisory circle, sidelined senior leaders, and gave government policy a left orientation through the Ten Point Programme. The conflict became open during the 1969 presidential election when the Syndicate supported N. Sanjeeva Reddy and Indira Gandhi supported V. V. Giri. Giri's victory and Reddy's defeat led to the formal Congress split. Indira Gandhi presented the split as an ideological battle between pro-poor and conservative forces.

### Question 3

How did Indira Gandhi restore Congress dominance?

Answer:

Indira Gandhi restored Congress dominance by turning an internal power struggle into a popular ideological campaign. She supported policies such as bank nationalisation, abolition of privy purse, land reforms, and public sector expansion. She used the slogan Garibi Hatao to build support among poor and disadvantaged groups. In the 1971 election, Congress(R) defeated the Grand Alliance and Congress(O), winning 352 seats on its own. The Bangladesh war later increased her popularity, and Congress swept state elections in 1972. Thus Congress dominance was restored, but in a more centralised and leader-centred form.

### Question 4

Was the Congress system restored after 1971?

Answer:

Congress dominance was restored after 1971, but the old Congress system was not fully restored. The old Congress was a broad umbrella organisation with strong factions and capacity to accommodate diverse interests. The new Congress under Indira Gandhi depended heavily on her personal popularity, had a weaker organisation, and had less room for internal factions. It built support among the poor, women, Dalits, Adivasis, minorities, and unemployed youth, but it could not absorb all social tensions like the old Congress. Therefore, Indira Gandhi restored the Congress system by changing its nature.

## 70. Passage-based Practice

### Passage Theme

Sudipta Kaviraj argues that Indira Gandhi changed Congress from a federal, democratic, ideological organisation into a centralised and leader-centred party.

### Question 1

What is the difference between Nehru's and Indira Gandhi's strategies?

Answer:

Nehru led a broad Congress that accommodated factions, ideologies, and regional leaders. Indira Gandhi relied more on centralised leadership, personal popularity, slogans, and direct appeal to voters.

### Question 2

Why might the author say Congress as an organisation died in the 1970s?

Answer:

The author means that Congress lost its earlier organisational depth, internal democracy, and ability to accommodate many interests. It became more dependent on one leader and electoral slogans.

### Question 3

How did this affect other parties?

Answer:

Other parties also adjusted to a more competitive and slogan-driven political environment. They formed alliances against Congress and focused more on electoral strategy, social groups, and leadership appeals.

## 71. Case-based Practice

A dominant party loses several state elections but keeps national power. Opposition parties with different ideologies unite only to defeat it. Inside the ruling party, senior organisation leaders clash with the Prime Minister.

### Question 1

Which period does this resemble?

Answer:

It resembles Indian politics after the 1967 elections.

### Question 2

What strategy did opposition parties use?

Answer:

They used non-Congressism.

### Question 3

What internal conflict followed?

Answer:

The conflict between Indira Gandhi and the Congress Syndicate followed, leading to the 1969 split.

## 72. Exam Writing Tips

- Do not write that Congress lost the Centre in 1967; it did not.
- Use "political earthquake" only with explanation, not as a substitute for facts.
- Mention non-Congressism when explaining opposition unity.
- Mention SVD governments when explaining coalitions after 1967.
- Use Aya Ram, Gaya Ram for defection.
- In the 1969 split answer, include Sanjeeva Reddy, V. V. Giri, conscience vote, and expulsion of Indira Gandhi.
- In 1971 answers, contrast Indira Hatao with Garibi Hatao.
- In restoration answers, clearly distinguish dominance from old-style Congress system.

- Nehru died in May 1964.

## 74. One-page Revision Sheet

After Nehru's death, India managed peaceful succession through Shastri.

After Shastri's death, Indira Gandhi defeated Morarji Desai in a secret ballot among Congress MPs.

The 1967 election weakened Congress dominance and created coalition governments in many states.

Non-Congressism brought ideologically different opposition parties together against Congress.

Defections destabilised governments and produced the phrase Aya Ram, Gaya Ram.

Inside Congress, Indira Gandhi clashed with the Syndicate, an informal group of powerful organisation leaders.

The Ten Point Programme and bank nationalisation helped Indira Gandhi present a left-leaning, pro-poor image.

The 1969 presidential election led to Congress split into Congress(O) and Congress(R).

In 1971, Indira Gandhi defeated the Grand Alliance with the slogan Garibi Hatao.

The Bangladesh war and 1972 state elections restored Congress dominance.

But the restored Congress was leader-centred and more centralised, not the old broad Congress system.

## 75. Final Takeaway

The Congress system was challenged by succession crises, economic hardship, opposition unity, state-level coalitions, defections, and internal conflict. Indira Gandhi restored Congress dominance through bold policies, ideological positioning, and direct appeal to the poor. But this restoration changed Congress from a broad, faction-accommodating organisation into a more centralised, leader-dependent party. That change explains why the next chapter moves toward the crisis of democratic order.
