# Chapter 2: Era Of One-party Dominance

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains how democratic politics began in independent India and why the Congress party dominated the first phase of elections. The main point is not that India had only one party. India had many parties, competitive elections, opposition leaders, and ideological debates. The unusual feature was that the Congress won election after election within a democratic system.

Read this chapter as the story of two things happening at the same time:

- India successfully created a system of free and fair elections.
- The Congress became dominant because of its freedom movement legacy, organisation, leadership, and broad social base.

The chapter also explains why opposition parties still mattered even when they were electorally weak.

## 1. The Chapter In One Sentence

India created a working democracy through free and fair elections, but the Congress dominated early politics because it inherited the freedom struggle, had a nationwide organisation, and functioned as a broad coalition inside one party.

## 2. Why Democracy Was A Challenge

After independence, India faced Partition, poverty, illiteracy, refugee crises, princely state integration, and regional diversity.

Many newly independent countries used such problems as reasons to delay or suspend democracy.

Their argument was simple:

- Unity must come first.
- Democracy may create conflict.
- Elections may divide people.
- Strong rule is needed for development.

India chose a different route.

## 3. India's Democratic Choice

India's leaders chose democracy because the freedom struggle had already trained people in public debate, mass mobilisation, protest, representation, and political participation.

They did not see politics as a disease. They saw democratic politics as the method through which social conflicts could be handled peacefully.

This was a major decision. India did not wait to become rich or fully literate before giving people the vote.

## 4. Politics As Problem-solving

In a democracy, different groups have different interests.

Examples:

- Farmers may want cheap credit and higher crop prices.
- Industrial workers may want better wages.
- Business groups may want fewer controls.
- Linguistic groups may want recognition.
- Minorities may want security.
- Poor citizens may want welfare.

Politics is the process through which such differences are represented, negotiated, and converted into public decisions.

## 5. Constitution And Elections

The Constitution was:

- Adopted on 26 November 1949.
- Signed on 24 January 1950.
- Put into effect on 26 January 1950.

Once the Constitution came into force, India needed its first democratically elected government.

Rules existed on paper. Now institutions had to make democracy work in practice.

## 6. Election Commission Of India

The Election Commission of India was set up in January 1950.

Sukumar Sen became the first Chief Election Commissioner.

His responsibility was enormous: conduct the first general election in a newly independent, poor, highly diverse, and mostly illiterate country.

## 7. Why The First Election Was Difficult

The first general election required:

- Drawing constituency boundaries.
- Preparing electoral rolls.
- Training polling staff.
- Creating ballot systems.
- Printing materials.
- Transporting election equipment.
- Ensuring secrecy of voting.
- Preventing unfair practices.
- Conducting simultaneous elections to the Lok Sabha and state assemblies.

No election on this scale had ever been conducted before.

## 8. Delimitation

Delimitation means drawing the boundaries of electoral constituencies.

This matters because every voter must belong to a constituency, and each constituency must elect a representative.

Without fair delimitation, elections can become unequal or confusing.

## 9. Electoral Rolls

Electoral rolls are lists of eligible voters.

Preparing them was difficult because India had a huge population and weak administrative records.

A major gender problem appeared when many women's names were recorded not as independent citizens but as "wife of" or "daughter of" someone.

The Election Commission refused to accept this. It ordered revision where possible and deletion where necessary.

This was important because democracy required women to be recognised as citizens in their own right.

## 10. Size Of The First General Election

The first general election involved:

- About 17 crore eligible voters.
- About 489 Lok Sabha seats.
- About 3,200 MLA seats.
- More than 3 lakh officers and polling staff.
- Only about 15 percent literate voters.

This was an experiment of historic scale.

## 11. Universal Adult Franchise

Universal adult franchise means every adult citizen has the right to vote regardless of caste, class, gender, religion, wealth, education, or social status.

India adopted universal adult franchise from the start.

This was bold because many countries had introduced voting rights gradually. Some advanced democracies had taken a long time to give equal voting rights to women.

## 12. Why Universal Adult Franchise Was A Big Step

Universal adult franchise was significant because it:

- Treated poor citizens as equal political members.
- Gave women the same political status as men.
- Gave Dalits, Adivasis, minorities, and marginal groups equal voting rights.
- Rejected the idea that literacy is necessary for political judgment.
- Made democracy a mass practice from the beginning.

Critics called it risky. The first election proved them wrong.

## 13. Voting Method In The First Election

In the first general election, each candidate had a separate ballot box with their election symbol.

The voter received a blank ballot paper and dropped it into the box of the chosen candidate.

Symbols were crucial because most voters could not read.

About 20 lakh steel boxes were used.

## 14. Later Voting Methods

After the first two elections, the system changed.

Instead of separate boxes for each candidate, a ballot paper carried names and symbols of all candidates. Voters marked their choice on the ballot paper.

This paper ballot method continued for many years.

By 2004, the entire country had shifted to Electronic Voting Machines.

## 15. Timing Of The First Election

The first general election was expected earlier, but it had to be postponed because preparations took time.

It was held from October 1951 to February 1952.

It is usually called the 1952 election because most voting took place in January 1952.

## 16. Features Of The First Election

The first election had several important features:

- It was competitive.
- On average, more than four candidates contested each seat.
- More than half the eligible voters voted.
- The results were accepted as fair even by losing parties.
- It proved that poor and illiterate citizens could participate meaningfully.

## 17. Why The First Election Was A Landmark

The first general election changed global thinking about democracy.

Before this, many believed democracy could work only in rich and literate societies.

India showed that democratic elections could be held in a poor, diverse, newly independent country.

This made the 1952 election a landmark in the history of democracy.

## 18. Critics Of Indian Democracy

Some observers believed universal adult franchise in India would fail.

Their doubts were based on:

- Poverty.
- Illiteracy.
- Size of the population.
- Social hierarchy.
- Administrative weakness.
- Recent Partition violence.

The success of the election challenged these assumptions.

## 19. Congress Victory In 1952

The Congress was expected to win the first election.

It had several advantages:

- It inherited the legacy of the national movement.
- It had a nationwide organisation.
- It had local-level networks.
- Its leaders were well known.
- Jawaharlal Nehru was extremely popular.
- Many freedom movement leaders contested as Congress candidates.

The size of the victory, however, surprised many.

## 20. Results Of The First Lok Sabha Election

In the first Lok Sabha election:

- Total Lok Sabha seats: 489.
- Congress won: 364.
- Communist Party of India won: 16.

The Congress finished far ahead of all rivals.

State assembly elections were held along with Lok Sabha elections. Congress won most states and formed governments even where it initially lacked a majority in some cases.

## 21. Congress In 1957 And 1962

The second general election was held in 1957.

The third general election was held in 1962.

In both elections, the Congress maintained dominance. It won about three-fourths of Lok Sabha seats.

No opposition party came close to its strength.

## 22. Congress At The State Level

The Congress also dominated state politics.

In most states, it won majority support and formed governments.

Important exceptions appeared, especially Kerala in 1957.

But overall, during the first decade and more after independence, Congress controlled both national and most state governments.

## 23. Vote Share And Seat Share

The Congress victory was strengthened by the electoral system.

In 1952:

- Congress received about 45 percent of the total votes.
- Congress won about 74 percent of Lok Sabha seats.

This difference between vote share and seat share is very important.

## 24. First-past-the-post System

India follows the first-past-the-post system for Lok Sabha and state assembly elections.

In this system, the candidate who gets more votes than every other candidate in a constituency wins.

The winning candidate does not need more than 50 percent of votes.

If opposition votes are divided among many parties and candidates, a party with a plurality can win many seats.

## 25. How The System Helped Congress

Non-Congress votes were often divided among different opposition parties and independent candidates.

This helped Congress win many seats even when its vote share was below 50 percent.

The system rewarded Congress because it was the largest party across many constituencies.

## 26. Important Exam Distinction

Congress dominance does not mean Congress got all votes.

It means Congress converted a strong vote share, national spread, and divided opposition into a much larger seat share.

This distinction prevents shallow answers.

## 27. Communist Victory In Kerala

In March 1957, the Communist Party of India won the largest number of seats in the Kerala assembly.

It won 60 out of 126 seats and had support from five independents.

E. M. S. Namboodiripad became Chief Minister.

This was historic because it was the first time a Communist government came to power anywhere in the world through democratic elections.

## 28. Kerala And Article 356

The Communist government in Kerala promised radical and progressive measures.

Opposition groups, including the Congress, began a movement against it.

In 1959, the Congress government at the Centre dismissed the Kerala government under Article 356.

This decision became controversial and is often cited as an early example of misuse of emergency powers.

## 29. Why Kerala Matters

Kerala matters because it shows three things:

- Congress dominance was not total.
- Opposition parties could win power through elections.
- Centre-state relations could become politically controversial.

It also shows that democratic competition existed in the era of Congress dominance.

## 30. Nature Of Congress Dominance

One-party dominance can happen in two ways.

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Non-democratic dominance | Other parties are banned, suppressed, or made powerless by law or force |
| Democratic dominance | Many parties compete freely, but one party repeatedly wins |

India's Congress dominance belonged to the second type.

This is why the Indian case was different from one-party regimes where democracy was compromised.

## 31. Comparison With Other Countries

In some countries, one-party dominance was created by law, military control, or authoritarian rule.

In India, opposition parties were allowed to contest.

Elections were competitive.

The Congress still won repeatedly.

This is the key reason why the chapter treats India's one-party dominance as unusual but democratic.

## 32. Reasons For Congress Dominance

Congress dominance had several causes:

- Legacy of the freedom struggle.
- Strong national organisation.
- Popular leaders.
- Nationwide presence.
- Local networks.
- Early start compared to other parties.
- Ability to include many social groups.
- Ability to absorb different ideologies.
- Weak and divided opposition.
- Electoral advantage under first-past-the-post.

No single reason is enough. Good answers combine these factors.

## 33. Freedom Struggle Legacy

The Congress was widely seen as the party that had led the struggle for independence.

Many citizens associated it with sacrifice, patriotism, national unity, and anti-colonial struggle.

This gave the party moral legitimacy in the early years.

## 34. Organisational Advantage

Congress had a network across the country before many other parties were even formed.

It had:

- Provincial units.
- Local committees.
- Experienced organisers.
- Known leaders.
- Links with social groups.
- Campaign experience from the freedom movement.

This gave it a strong advantage in elections.

## 35. Nehru's Leadership

Jawaharlal Nehru was the most popular national leader of the time.

He toured the country and led the Congress campaign.

His appeal helped Congress connect national goals with electoral politics.

But Congress dominance cannot be explained only by Nehru. It also rested on organisation, history, and coalition-building.

## 36. Congress As A Social Coalition

The Congress brought together many social groups.

These included:

- Peasants.
- Industrialists.
- Workers.
- Landowners.
- Middle classes.
- Urban groups.
- Rural groups.
- Upper castes.
- Lower castes.
- Religious minorities.
- Regional elites.

This made Congress a broad social coalition.

## 37. Why The Social Coalition Was Powerful

A party that represents only one group can be strong in one area but weak elsewhere.

Congress represented many groups across many regions.

This helped it become a national party rather than a narrow regional or class party.

## 38. Congress As An Ideological Coalition

Congress also contained different ideological tendencies.

It had space for:

- Conservatives.
- Radicals.
- Socialists.
- Moderates.
- Gandhian thinkers.
- Secular nationalists.
- Pro-business voices.
- Pro-peasant voices.
- Left-leaning and centrist leaders.

This made it an ideological coalition.

## 39. Congress As A Platform

During the freedom movement, the Congress functioned like a platform where different groups could work together against colonial rule.

Some organisations and tendencies had their own views but operated within or near the Congress.

After independence, this platform-like nature helped Congress absorb social conflict inside itself.

## 40. Factions Inside Congress

A faction is a group within a party.

Factions may be based on:

- Ideology.
- Region.
- Caste.
- Personal loyalty.
- Leadership ambition.
- Policy preferences.

Congress had many factions in its state units and national organisation.

## 41. Why Factions Did Not Always Weaken Congress

Usually factions are seen as a sign of weakness.

In the Congress system, they often strengthened the party.

They allowed leaders with different ambitions and ideas to remain inside the party instead of forming separate parties.

Political competition happened inside Congress as well as between Congress and other parties.

## 42. Congress As Ruling Party And Opposition

Because Congress contained many factions and ideological tendencies, it often acted like both government and internal opposition.

A policy could be challenged from within the party.

Different Congress groups could influence decisions.

This is why early Indian politics is often called the Congress system.

## 43. Meaning Of Congress System

The Congress system refers to a party system in which:

- Congress was the dominant ruling party.
- Opposition parties existed but were weak.
- Many social and ideological conflicts were absorbed inside Congress.
- Factions inside Congress functioned like internal opposition.
- Other parties often tried to influence Congress from outside.

It was not a one-party state. It was a multi-party democracy dominated by one very broad party.

## 44. Opposition Parties In The 1950s

India had many opposition parties even during Congress dominance.

Important opposition parties included:

- Communist Party of India.
- Socialist Party and later socialist groups.
- Bharatiya Jana Sangh.
- Swatantra Party.
- Regional and smaller parties.

Most won few seats, but they mattered politically.

## 45. Why Opposition Parties Mattered

Opposition parties performed important democratic functions:

- Criticised government policies.
- Offered alternative ideas.
- Represented ideological currents.
- Kept democratic alternatives alive.
- Prevented resentment from becoming anti-democratic.
- Influenced factions inside Congress.
- Trained future leaders.

Weak seat strength did not mean political irrelevance.

## 46. Socialist Party

The Socialist Party originated from the Congress Socialist Party formed in 1934.

Its leaders wanted a more radical and egalitarian Congress.

In 1948, Congress changed its rules to prevent dual party membership. This forced socialists to form a separate party.

## 47. Socialist Ideology

The Socialist Party believed in democratic socialism.

It criticised Congress for:

- Favouring capitalists.
- Favouring landlords.
- Ignoring workers.
- Ignoring peasants.
- Not going far enough in redistribution.

But the socialists faced a problem when Congress itself declared the goal of a socialist pattern of society in 1955.

This made it harder for them to appear as a distinct alternative.

## 48. Socialist Leaders

Important socialist leaders included:

- Jayaprakash Narayan.
- Achyut Patwardhan.
- Asoka Mehta.
- Acharya Narendra Dev.
- Rammanohar Lohia.
- S. M. Joshi.

The socialist tradition later influenced several parties in contemporary Indian politics.

## 49. Communist Party Of India

Communist groups emerged in India in the early twentieth century, influenced by the Russian Revolution.

From 1935, many communists worked from within the Congress framework.

After independence, the CPI debated whether India's freedom was genuine or only a transfer of power that preserved old class structures.

## 50. CPI And Elections

Soon after independence, the CPI initially supported violent uprisings in places such as Telangana.

This line failed to win broad support and was suppressed.

In 1951, the CPI abandoned the path of violent revolution and decided to participate in elections.

In the first general election, it won 16 Lok Sabha seats and became the largest opposition party.

## 51. CPI Support Base

The CPI had stronger support in:

- Andhra Pradesh.
- West Bengal.
- Bihar.
- Kerala.

Important leaders included:

- A. K. Gopalan.
- S. A. Dange.
- E. M. S. Namboodiripad.
- P. C. Joshi.
- Ajay Ghosh.
- P. Sundarayya.

## 52. CPI Split

The Communist Party split in 1964.

The split was influenced by ideological differences connected to the Soviet Union and China.

The pro-Soviet faction remained CPI.

The other faction formed CPI(M).

This split later shaped left politics in India.

## 53. Bharatiya Jana Sangh

The Bharatiya Jana Sangh was formed in 1951.

Shyama Prasad Mukherjee was its founder-president.

Its ideological roots were linked to the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh and the Hindu Mahasabha.

## 54. Jana Sangh Ideology

The Jana Sangh emphasised:

- One country, one culture, and one nation.
- Indian culture and traditions as the basis of modernity.
- Akhand Bharat, or reunification of India and Pakistan.
- Replacing English with Hindi as the official language.
- Opposition to special concessions for religious and cultural minorities.
- Development of nuclear weapons, especially after China's 1964 test.

It remained electorally small in the 1950s but later became historically important.

## 55. Jana Sangh Electoral Performance

In the 1950s, the Jana Sangh was on the margins of electoral politics.

It won:

- 3 Lok Sabha seats in 1952.
- 4 Lok Sabha seats in 1957.

Its early support came mainly from urban areas in Hindi-speaking states such as Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Delhi, and Uttar Pradesh.

The Bharatiya Janata Party traces its roots to the Bharatiya Jana Sangh.

## 56. Swatantra Party

The Swatantra Party represented a different kind of opposition.

It opposed excessive state control over the economy and criticised centralised planning.

Its leaders included C. Rajagopalachari, Minoo Masani, and others.

It supported freer private enterprise and was critical of heavy government regulation.

## 57. Swatantra Party Ideology

The Swatantra Party favoured:

- Economy free from excessive state control.
- Private enterprise.
- Less centralised planning.
- Less government interference in business.
- Greater space for market competition.

It was therefore located on the right side of the economic debate.

This is why the exercise answer links Minoo Masani with the Swatantra Party and identifies economy free from state control as one of its guiding principles.

## 58. Left And Right In This Chapter

The chapter uses left and right mainly to describe views on social change and the role of the state.

| Position | Broad meaning |
|---|---|
| Left | Supports state intervention, redistribution, workers, peasants, and social equality |
| Right | Supports market freedom, private enterprise, and limited state control |

These terms are not insults. They help classify political ideas.

## 59. Where Congress Stood

Congress was difficult to classify as simply left or right.

It contained left, right, and centrist tendencies.

It supported planning and public sector growth, but it also accommodated private business and rural elites.

This is why Congress is best understood as a broad centrist coalition.

## 60. Role Of Personal Respect

Early Indian politics had strong ideological differences, but many leaders maintained personal respect for opponents.

Examples:

- Dr. B. R. Ambedkar served in Nehru's first cabinet.
- Shyama Prasad Mukherjee also served in Nehru's first cabinet.
- Nehru invited socialist leaders to cooperate.

This helped create a democratic political culture.

## 61. Decline Of Personal Respect Over Time

As party competition became sharper, personal relationships between ruling and opposition leaders became more tense.

The chapter suggests that early democratic politics had a special quality: intense disagreement could exist with some level of respect.

This is useful for reflective exam answers.

## 63. Why The Congress System Was Stable

The Congress system was stable because:

- Congress had national legitimacy.
- It could absorb internal criticism.
- It represented many social interests.
- It had leaders at national and regional levels.
- Opposition was fragmented.
- Voters trusted Congress after independence.
- Electoral rules rewarded the largest party.

This stability helped the new republic survive its early years.

## 64. Why The Congress System Was Not Permanent

Congress dominance was not permanent because:

- Its ability to accommodate all groups declined.
- Regional leaders and interests became stronger.
- Opposition parties gained experience.
- Social conflicts became harder to manage inside one party.
- New political issues emerged.
- Voters gradually became more willing to try alternatives.

Later chapters explain this decline.

## 65. One-party Dominance Is Not One-party Rule

This distinction is essential.

| One-party dominance | One-party rule |
|---|---|
| Other parties are allowed | Other parties may be banned or suppressed |
| Elections are competitive | Elections may be controlled |
| Voters can choose alternatives | Real choice may be absent |
| Example: Congress dominance in early India | Example: authoritarian one-party states |

India had one-party dominance, not one-party rule.

## 66. Why Opposition Was Weak

Opposition parties were weak because:

- Many were newly formed.
- They lacked nationwide organisation.
- They had limited resources.
- Their support was concentrated in pockets.
- Their votes were divided.
- Congress absorbed many of their ideas.
- Congress had the freedom movement legacy.

Still, they contributed to democratic life.

## 67. Opposition As Pressure From The Margins

Opposition parties were often far from actual power.

But they influenced politics by:

- Criticising Congress.
- Shaping public debate.
- Encouraging Congress factions.
- Making voters aware of alternatives.
- Bringing ideological questions into Parliament.

This pressure helped prevent democracy from becoming empty.

## 68. Congress And Candidate Selection

Because Congress was dominant, choosing a Congress candidate often became politically important.

Internal competition for tickets could be intense.

This again shows that political competition happened inside Congress, not only between parties.

## 69. The Tandon Episode

The chapter refers to internal conflict inside Congress, including the election of P. D. Tandon as Congress president against Nehru's wishes.

This shows that even Nehru did not control Congress completely.

Congress had powerful internal groups, leaders, and ideological tensions.

## 70. Ambedkar's Warning About Hero-worship

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar warned that hero-worship in politics could lead to degradation and dictatorship.

This warning matters because early India had popular leaders, especially Nehru.

Democracy required citizens to respect institutions, not blindly follow individuals.

## 71. Democracy And Literacy

The first election proved that literacy is not the same as political intelligence.

Illiterate voters could still:

- Understand their interests.
- Recognise symbols.
- Evaluate leaders.
- Participate in voting.
- Accept election results.

This remains one of the strongest democratic lessons of the chapter.

## 72. Gender And Electoral Identity

The deletion or correction of women's names in electoral rolls showed a deeper social problem.

Women were often recognised through male relatives instead of as independent citizens.

The Election Commission's response affirmed the principle that each voter has an individual political identity.

## 73. Election Symbols

Election symbols helped voters identify candidates and parties.

They were especially important in a context where literacy rates were low.

Symbols made the ballot more accessible.

## 74. Free And Fair Elections

The first election was considered free and fair because:

- It was organised by an independent Election Commission.
- Many parties and candidates contested.
- Voters participated in large numbers.
- Losers accepted the results.
- Administrative procedures were carefully followed.

This created trust in democratic institutions.

## 75. Why Acceptance By Losers Matters

An election becomes stable when winners accept responsibility and losers accept the legitimacy of the process.

In 1952, losing candidates and parties accepted the results as fair.

This strengthened democracy because it showed that political conflict could be settled through elections rather than violence.

## 76. The First Election And World Democracy

India's first election expanded the meaning of democracy globally.

It showed that democracy was not only for rich Western countries.

A newly independent Asian country with poverty, diversity, and low literacy could also conduct mass elections.

## 77. Congress Victory: Strength And Limitation

Congress dominance gave India stable government in the early years.

But dominance also had limitations.

If one party becomes too powerful, there is a risk that:

- Opposition may become weak.
- Internal party conflicts may replace open public debate.
- Citizens may not see realistic alternatives.
- Ruling leaders may become complacent.

The chapter therefore treats Congress dominance as both a strength and a challenge.

## 78. Kerala Dismissal As A Warning

The dismissal of the Communist government in Kerala under Article 356 warned that central power could be used against elected state governments.

This event is important for understanding:

- Centre-state relations.
- Emergency provisions.
- Limits of Congress tolerance.
- Tension between democratic mandate and central authority.

## 79. Why The Congress Could Absorb Opposition Ideas

Because Congress was ideologically broad, it could adopt ideas from different sides.

For example:

- Social welfare language from the left.
- Planning and development.
- National unity from centralist voices.
- Accommodation of private enterprise.
- Gandhian concerns.

This made it difficult for opposition parties to create sharp alternatives.

## 80. Coalition Inside A Party

In later decades, India saw coalitions of different parties.

In the early decades, Congress itself functioned like a coalition inside one party.

This means groups with different views stayed within Congress and negotiated power internally.

## 86. Short Answer Practice

### Question 1

Why was the first general election a difficult exercise?

Answer:

The first general election was difficult because India had a huge electorate, low literacy, poor infrastructure, and great social diversity. Electoral rolls had to be prepared, constituencies had to be delimited, election symbols and ballot boxes had to be arranged, and polling staff had to be trained. About 17 crore eligible voters participated in an election of a scale never seen before.

### Question 2

Why was universal adult franchise considered bold in India?

Answer:

It was considered bold because India adopted equal voting rights despite poverty, low literacy, caste hierarchy, gender inequality, and recent Partition violence. Many countries had introduced voting rights gradually, but India gave all adult citizens equal political status from the beginning.

### Question 3

Why did Congress dominate the first three general elections?

Answer:

Congress dominated because it inherited the legacy of the freedom struggle, had a nationwide organisation, possessed popular leaders such as Nehru, included many social groups and ideologies, and benefited from divided opposition votes under the first-past-the-post system.

### Question 4

Why did opposition parties matter even when they won few seats?

Answer:

Opposition parties mattered because they criticised government policies, offered alternatives, represented ideological diversity, influenced factions inside Congress, trained future leaders, and kept democratic competition alive.

## 87. Long Answer Practice

### Question 1

Explain the nature of Congress dominance in the early years after independence.

Answer:

Congress dominance in the early years was democratic rather than authoritarian. Many parties contested elections, and elections were free and fair, but Congress won repeatedly. Its dominance came from its freedom struggle legacy, national organisation, popular leadership, and ability to include many social groups and ideological tendencies. The first-past-the-post system also helped it win a larger share of seats than votes. Congress functioned like a broad coalition, and many political conflicts were managed through factions inside the party. Therefore, India had one-party dominance, not one-party rule.

### Question 2

Explain the meaning of Congress as a social and ideological coalition.

Answer:

Congress was a social coalition because it included peasants, workers, industrialists, landowners, urban groups, rural groups, upper castes, lower castes, minorities, and regional elites. It was an ideological coalition because it contained conservatives, radicals, socialists, moderates, Gandhian thinkers, and centrists. This broadness came from the national movement, where Congress had acted as a platform for many groups. After independence, this helped Congress dominate because many interests preferred to fight for influence inside Congress rather than leave it.

### Question 3

Why was the first general election of 1952 a landmark in world democracy?

Answer:

The first general election was a landmark because it showed that democracy could work in a poor, diverse, newly independent, and largely illiterate country. About 17 crore eligible voters were included, and elections were held for the Lok Sabha and state assemblies. The Election Commission used symbols, ballot boxes, trained staff, and careful procedures. More than half the eligible voters participated, and the results were accepted as fair. This disproved the belief that democracy was possible only in wealthy and literate societies.

## 88. Passage-based Practice

### Passage Theme

Rajni Kothari argued that Congress should not become a narrow, disciplined party because its broad and eclectic character allowed it to perform many roles in Indian politics.

### Question 1

Why might a narrow Congress have been unsuitable in early India?

Answer:

A narrow Congress would have excluded many groups and ideologies that had participated in the freedom movement. Early India needed a broad party that could absorb conflicts among regions, classes, castes, and ideological groups. A tightly disciplined party with one ideology might have created more opposition outside the party and made democratic management harder.

### Question 2

Give examples of Congress's eclectic role.

Answer:

Congress included left and right tendencies, leaders from different regions and communities, pro-planning voices, private enterprise supporters, socialists, Gandhian groups, and moderates. It also accommodated internal factions and served as a platform for many interests.

### Question 3

Why might Gandhi's view of continuing Congress as a movement be called romantic?

Answer:

It may be called romantic because after independence, India needed organised parties, elections, government responsibility, and policy decisions. A freedom movement could not simply continue in the same form when Congress had to govern a democratic state.

Is this automatically undemocratic?

No. It is not automatically undemocratic if elections are free and fair, opposition parties can contest, and voters have real choice.

It resembles the era of Congress dominance in the first phase of Indian democracy.

## 93. Final Takeaway

The era of one-party dominance was not the absence of democracy. It was the first form Indian democracy took after independence: competitive elections, a dominant Congress, weak but important opposition parties, and a political system where many conflicts were managed inside a broad national party. The chapter's central lesson is that democracy in India began with one-party dominance, but not with one-party dictatorship.
