# Chapter 8: Social Movements

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies social movements as organised collective efforts to bring about or resist social change. It connects many earlier themes: caste, gender, class, tribe, ecology, democracy, labour, globalisation, and identity.

The central idea is that the rights people enjoy today did not simply appear. Many rights were won through struggle. Social movements change laws, institutions, values, identities, and everyday life. They also change the people who participate in them.

## 1. Why Social Movements Matter

Many rights now considered normal were won through movements.

Examples include:

- Eight-hour workday.
- Weekly rest.
- Equal pay demands.
- Social security.
- Pension.
- Voting rights.
- Anti-colonial independence.
- Civil rights.
- Anti-apartheid rights.
- Women's rights.
- Dalit rights.
- Environmental protections.

Social movements remind us that society changes through struggle.

## 2. Right To Vote As A Movement Outcome

Universal adult franchise means every adult has the right to vote.

In many countries, this right was not given to everyone at first. Property-owning men were often privileged. Workers, women, and poor people had to struggle for political rights.

Chartism in Britain demanded parliamentary representation, voting rights, and the ability to stand for election without property. Women's suffrage movements fought for women's right to vote.

India adopted universal adult franchise after independence, making it a radical democratic step.

## 3. What Is A Social Movement?

A social movement is a sustained collective effort by people to bring about or prevent change.

It usually has:

- Shared goals.
- Some organisation.
- Leadership or coordination.
- Collective identity.
- Ideology.
- Repeated action over time.
- Methods of mobilisation.
- A public issue.

It is more than a one-time protest.

## 4. Social Movement Versus Spontaneous Protest

Spontaneous protests may be sudden and disorganised.

A social movement is sustained and organised.

For example:

- A one-day crowd reaction may be protest.
- A long campaign with meetings, slogans, leaders, networks, and demands is a movement.

Social movements may include protest, but they also include education, organisation, lobbying, cultural work, and negotiation.

## 5. Features Of Social Movements

Social movements usually include:

- Sustained collective action.
- Shared objectives.
- Shared ideology.
- Some organisational structure.
- Leadership or coordination.
- Public claims.
- Mobilisation of supporters.
- Opposition to existing power or demand for reform.
- Symbols, songs, slogans, or rituals.
- Strategies of protest and negotiation.

These features may change during the movement's life.

## 6. Movements And The State

Social movements often direct demands toward the state.

They may demand:

- New laws.
- Change in policy.
- Implementation of rights.
- Recognition of identity.
- Compensation.
- Protection from violence.
- Access to resources.
- Environmental regulation.

Movements may also challenge social groups, religious authorities, corporations, landlords, or patriarchal family structures.

## 7. Counter Movements

Social change often produces opposition.

Examples:

- When Raja Ram Mohun Roy campaigned against sati, defenders of sati organised against reform.
- When reformers demanded women's education, some groups opposed it.
- When widow remarriage was supported, reformers faced boycott.
- When lower caste children entered schools, some privileged caste families withdrew their children.
- Reservation policies have often faced opposition from counter movements.

Counter movements show that social change threatens existing privileges.

## 8. Protest Repertoires

Movements develop distinct methods of protest.

Examples:

- Processions.
- Picketing.
- Fasts.
- Strikes.
- Street theatre.
- Songs.
- Poetry.
- Black cloth.
- Candle marches.
- Slogans.
- Petitions.
- Public meetings.
- Court cases.
- Media campaigns.
- Boycotts.

Gandhi used innovative methods such as ahimsa, satyagraha, the charkha, and the Salt March.

## 9. Gandhi's Repertoire Of Satyagraha

Gandhi transformed everyday items into symbols of resistance.

Examples:

- Khadi represented support for Indian cotton growers, spinners, and weavers.
- The charkha symbolised self-reliance and opposition to colonial industrial exploitation.
- Salt represented a basic necessity unjustly taxed by the colonial state.

These symbols made political struggle understandable to ordinary people.

## 10. Social Change And Social Movements

Social change is broad, continuous, and ongoing.

Social movements are organised efforts directed toward specific goals.

Examples:

- Westernisation is a broad social change.
- Nineteenth century reform movements were social movements.
- Urbanisation is a broad social change.
- A housing rights campaign is a social movement.

The distinction matters because social movements are intentional and organised, while social change can occur through many processes.

## 11. Why Sociology Studies Social Movements

Sociology has always studied social movements because they reveal:

- Conflict.
- Power.
- Inequality.
- Social order.
- Collective identity.
- State-society relations.
- Change in values.
- Mobilisation of people.

The French Revolution, Industrial Revolution, labour protests, and poor people's struggles shaped early sociological thought.

## 12. Durkheim And Social Order

Emile Durkheim was concerned with how society holds together.

Social movements were often seen by elites as disorderly threats. Durkheim's interest in social integration reflected anxieties about rapid social change.

## 13. Marxist And Historical Perspectives

Scholars influenced by Marx saw collective action differently.

They argued that crowds and mobs were not simply irrational. Poor people often had a moral economy, meaning shared ideas about fairness and justice.

When people protest against hunger, wages, taxes, or exploitation, they act from a sense of moral violation.

## 14. Types Of Social Movements

Social movements are often classified into:

- Redemptive or transformatory.
- Reformist.
- Revolutionary.

These categories help analysis, but real movements may combine elements of all three.

## 15. Redemptive Movements

Redemptive movements aim to change personal consciousness, behaviour, and social practices of members.

Example:

- Narayana Guru's work among the Ezhava community in Kerala encouraged changes in social practices and self-respect.

## 16. Reformist Movements

Reformist movements seek gradual change within existing social and political arrangements.

Examples:

- Movement for linguistic reorganisation of states.
- Right to Information campaign.
- Campaigns for legal reform.

Reformist movements do not usually aim to overthrow the whole system.

## 17. Revolutionary Movements

Revolutionary movements aim to radically transform social relations.

They may seek control of state power or destruction of oppressive structures.

Examples:

- Bolshevik Revolution in Russia.
- Naxalite movement in India.

Revolutionary movements often arise where people believe gradual reform cannot remove oppression.

## 18. Movements Change Over Time

A movement may begin with revolutionary goals but later become reformist. A movement may begin as mass protest and later become institutionalised as an organisation.

Social scientists call this development toward social movement organisations.

Movements are dynamic, not fixed.

## 19. Interpretation Of Movements

How a movement is described depends on perspective.

The uprising of 1857 was called a mutiny by British rulers, but many Indian nationalists called it the first war of independence.

This shows that movements are interpreted through power and ideology.

## 20. Old Social Movements

Old social movements were often class-based and focused on economic inequality and power.

Examples:

- Working class movements.
- Peasant movements.
- Trade union movements.
- Socialist movements.
- Anti-colonial national movements.

They were often linked with political parties.

## 21. New Social Movements

New social movements are often organised around:

- Environment.
- Gender.
- Identity.
- Human rights.
- Tribal rights.
- Cultural recognition.
- Peace.
- Democracy.
- Quality of life.

They often cut across class boundaries.

Examples:

- Women's movement.
- Environmental movements.
- Tribal rights movements.
- Dalit assertion.
- Regional movements.

## 22. Old And New Movements In India

In India, the distinction between old and new movements is difficult.

For example, the Chipko movement involved:

- Ecology.
- Livelihood.
- Gender.
- Local autonomy.
- State policy.
- Class inequality.

Similarly, tribal movements involve identity, ecology, land, and economic rights together.

Indian movements often combine class, identity, culture, and environment.

## 23. Civil Society And Non-Party Politics

When people feel political parties do not represent them, they may join movements outside formal party politics.

Civil society includes:

- Trade unions.
- Women's groups.
- Environmental groups.
- Tribal organisations.
- Dalit organisations.
- Human rights groups.
- Non-governmental organisations.
- Campaign networks.

These groups pressure the state from outside formal electoral politics.

## 24. Ecological Movements

Ecological movements challenge models of development that damage the environment and displace people.

They raise questions about:

- Forests.
- Water.
- Dams.
- Pollution.
- Livelihood.
- Displacement.
- Sustainability.
- Rights of local communities.

The chapter uses Chipko as a key example.

## 25. Chipko Movement

The Chipko movement took place in the Himalayan foothills.

Villagers, including many women, hugged trees to prevent contractors from cutting forests.

Their concerns included:

- Firewood.
- Fodder.
- Daily forest needs.
- Floods and landslides.
- Livelihood.
- Ecological balance.
- Opposition to distant state decisions.

## 26. Red And Green Issues

Chipko combined red and green issues.

Red issues refer to social and economic inequality, such as poor villagers versus commercial contractors and state revenue interests.

Green issues refer to ecological sustainability, such as protection of forests, prevention of floods, and environmental balance.

For villagers, economy and ecology were connected. Their survival depended on forests.

## 27. Peasant Movements

Peasant movements or agrarian struggles have a long history.

Examples include:

- Indigo revolt in Bengal.
- Deccan riots against moneylenders.
- Champaran Satyagraha.
- Bardoli Satyagraha.
- Tebhaga movement.
- Telangana movement.

These movements addressed issues such as rent, taxes, indigo cultivation, moneylenders, sharecropping, feudal oppression, and land rights.

## 28. Kisan Sabhas

Peasant organisations emerged in the twentieth century.

Important organisations included:

- Bihar Provincial Kisan Sabha.
- All India Kisan Sabha.

They demanded freedom from economic exploitation for peasants, workers, and other oppressed classes.

## 29. Tebhaga And Telangana

The Tebhaga movement in Bengal demanded that sharecroppers receive two-thirds of the produce rather than the customary half.

The Telangana movement challenged feudal conditions in the princely state of Hyderabad and was led by communist mobilisation.

Both show how agrarian movements challenged unequal land and labour relations.

## 30. New Farmers' Movements

New farmers' movements emerged in the 1970s in regions such as Punjab and Tamil Nadu.

They were often:

- Regional.
- Non-party.
- Led by market-oriented farmers.
- Focused on price and policy issues.

Demands included:

- Remunerative prices.
- Better procurement.
- Lower input costs.
- Opposition to taxation.
- Loan relief.

Methods included blocking roads and railways and refusing entry to officials or politicians.

## 31. Workers' Movements

Factory production began in colonial India in the nineteenth century.

Early labour conditions were harsh because the colonial state did not strongly regulate wages or working conditions.

Workers protested through strikes even before strong trade unions developed.

## 32. Trade Union Development

Important developments included:

- Early strikes in Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, and Ahmedabad.
- First trade union in Madras in 1918.
- Textile Labour Association founded by Gandhi.
- All India Trade Union Congress formed in 1920.
- Indian National Trade Union Congress formed in 1947.

Trade unions later split along ideological and party lines.

## 33. Worker Struggles And Civil Liberties

The 1974 railway workers' strike showed intense confrontation between the state and trade unions.

Worker movements were not only about wages. They were also connected with civil liberties, political rights, and dignity.

## 34. Dalit Movement

Dalit movements cannot be explained only by economic exploitation.

They are struggles for:

- Recognition as equal human beings.
- Self-respect.
- Dignity.
- End of untouchability.
- Cultural assertion.
- Political representation.
- Social equality.
- Space for self-determination.

The word Dalit means broken or oppressed, but in political use it rejects pollution and caste hierarchy.

## 35. Diversity Of Dalit Movements

There is no single unified Dalit movement.

Examples include:

- Satnami movement of Chamars in Chhattisgarh.
- Adi Dharma movement in Punjab.
- Mahar movement in Maharashtra.
- Jatav mobilisation in Agra.
- Anti-Brahmin movement in South India.

All assert dignity and equality, but they differ in ideology, region, and strategy.

## 36. Dalit Literature

Dalit literature is an important part of Dalit assertion.

It uses images, language, and experiences rooted in Dalit life.

It challenges mainstream writing that may hide oppression behind polished language.

Dalit literature gives a call for social and cultural revolt.

## 37. Dalit Movements And Movement Types

Dalit movements may include:

- Reformist elements, such as legal rights and reservation.
- Redemptive elements, such as self-respect and religious conversion.
- Revolutionary elements, such as abolishing caste oppression.

This shows that movement types often overlap.

## 38. Backward Class Movements

Backward class movements emerged in colonial and post-colonial contexts.

The colonial state often distributed opportunities through caste categories, encouraging caste-based organisation.

Groups in similar social positions formed wider political identities. This is called horizontal stretch.

Backward class organisations demanded:

- Representation.
- Education.
- Jobs.
- Reservation.
- Political recognition.

This also shows secularisation of caste: caste became a basis for political mobilisation rather than only ritual hierarchy.

## 39. Tribal Movements

Tribal movements differ across regions, but many share concerns about:

- Land alienation.
- Forest rights.
- Cultural identity.
- Autonomy.
- Exploitation by outsiders.
- Displacement by development.
- Mining and industrialisation.

The chapter gives Jharkhand as an important example.

## 40. Jharkhand Movement

Jharkhand became a separate state in 2000 after a long history of resistance.

Birsa Munda became an important icon of the movement.

The movement drew strength from:

- Shared adivasi identity.
- Memory of resistance.
- Writing by literate adivasis.
- Cultural documentation.
- Opposition to dikus, or outsiders seen as exploiters.
- Land alienation.
- Mining and industrial projects benefiting outsiders.

The movement combined identity, economic justice, and control over resources.

## 41. Tribal Movements In The North East

Tribal movements in the North East developed in relation to state formation after independence.

Many tribes had distinct identities, institutions, and traditions of autonomy. Incorporation into larger administrative structures created anxiety.

Earlier phases sometimes involved secessionist tendencies. Later phases often focused on autonomy within the Indian constitutional framework.

These movements show the importance of ethnicity, culture, autonomy, and political recognition.

## 42. Women's Movement

The women's movement in India has a long history.

It includes:

- Nineteenth century reform movements.
- Early women's organisations.
- Women's participation in nationalist struggles.
- Women's participation in peasant and tribal struggles.
- Post-1970s campaigns against violence.
- Campaigns for legal reform, land rights, employment rights, and dignity.

## 43. Early Women's Organisations

Important organisations included:

- Women's India Association.
- All India Women's Conference.
- National Council for Women in India.

Many began with welfare and education concerns but later connected women's rights with national freedom and political equality.

## 44. Women In Agrarian And Tribal Struggles

Women participated in:

- Tebhaga movement.
- Telangana armed struggle.
- Warli tribal revolt.
- Civil disobedience actions.

This challenges the idea that only educated middle class women participated in social movements.

## 45. Women's Movement After 1947

After independence, some activists entered nation-building work, while Partition trauma also affected organising.

In the mid-1970s, the women's movement revived with new campaigns and strategies.

Issues included:

- Violence against women.
- Dowry.
- Sexual harassment.
- Land rights.
- Employment.
- Legal reform.
- Representation.
- Recognition of both parents in official forms.

## 46. Differences Among Women

All women face gender disadvantage, but not all women experience it in the same way.

Differences arise from:

- Caste.
- Class.
- Tribe.
- Region.
- Religion.
- Education.
- Rural or urban location.

The concerns of a middle class woman may differ from those of a peasant woman or Dalit woman.

## 47. Gender-Just Society

A gender-just society allows both women and men to live without restrictive gender domination.

It recognises that patriarchy harms women directly but also restricts men by forcing them into narrow ideas of masculinity.

Gender justice requires:

- Education.
- Legal rights.
- Safety.
- Economic opportunity.
- Land and property rights.
- Political representation.
- Freedom from violence.
- Improved sex ratio.

## 48. Movements And The Individual-Society Relationship

Social movements show the dialectical relationship between individuals and society.

Individuals join together to change social conditions. In doing so, they change society and also change themselves.

Movements produce:

- New identities.
- New confidence.
- New rights.
- New laws.
- New public debates.
- New forms of organisation.

## 49. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Social movements are only protests

Movements include protest, but also organisation, education, negotiation, legal action, cultural work, and public debate.

### Misunderstanding 2: Social movements are always disorderly

Movements often arise because existing institutions ignore injustice.

### Misunderstanding 3: Old and new movements are easy to separate in India

Indian movements often combine class, caste, gender, ecology, livelihood, and identity.

### Misunderstanding 4: Women's movements are only middle class

Women have participated in peasant, tribal, labour, nationalist, and anti-dowry struggles.

## 50. Model Answer: Features Of Social Movements

A social movement is a sustained collective effort to bring about or prevent social change. It is different from spontaneous protest because it has some organisation, shared goals, ideology, leadership, and continuity over time. Social movements often make demands on the state or challenge powerful social groups. They use methods such as meetings, processions, strikes, petitions, songs, symbols, media campaigns, and court cases. Movements may face counter movements from groups defending the status quo. They are important because they help create rights, challenge inequality, and transform society.

## 51. Model Answer: Old And New Social Movements In India

Old social movements were often class-based and linked with political parties or trade unions. They focused on wages, land, power, and economic inequality. New social movements often focus on environment, identity, gender, culture, rights, and quality of life. In India, however, the distinction is difficult because many movements combine these concerns. The Chipko movement involved ecology, livelihood, gender, and regional autonomy. Tribal movements involve identity, forest rights, land, displacement, and economic justice. Dalit movements combine recognition, dignity, caste annihilation, representation, and material equality. Therefore, Indian social movements often blur the line between old and new.

## 53. Quick Revision

- Social movements are sustained collective efforts for or against change.
- They require organisation, shared goals, ideology, and continuity.
- Rights such as voting, labour protections, and equality were won through movements.
- Social movements are different from broad social change.
- Movements can be redemptive, reformist, or revolutionary.
- Old movements focused mainly on class and power; new movements often focus on identity, environment, gender, and rights.
- In India, movements often combine old and new issues.
- Chipko combined livelihood, ecology, gender, and local autonomy.
- Peasant and worker movements challenged exploitation.
- Dalit movements seek dignity, equality, and annihilation of caste.
- Tribal movements involve land, forest, autonomy, and identity.
- Women's movements have addressed reform, nationalism, violence, land, employment, and gender justice.
