# Chapter 5: Change and Development in Industrial Society

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies work, industry, employment, and labour in modern India. It does not treat industry only as factories and machines. It studies industry as a social world involving workers, managers, caste, gender, class, migration, unions, technology, working conditions, and globalisation.

The chapter's central idea is that the way people work is deeply connected to who they are in society. Jobs are shaped by education, caste, gender, region, family networks, state policy, markets, and technology. Industrial change therefore produces social change.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Explain how work shapes social identity.
- Describe sociological images of industrial society.
- Explain alienation and division of labour.
- Understand the specific character of industrialisation in India.
- Distinguish organised and unorganised sectors.
- Analyse the effects of liberalisation on employment.
- Explain how people find jobs through networks, contractors, and jobbers.
- Describe how work is controlled in factories and offices.
- Explain outsourcing, contract labour, and just-in-time production.
- Understand working conditions in mining, factories, services, and home-based work.
- Explain the importance of strikes and trade unions.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Industrial society | Society where machine production, factories, services, and wage work become important | It changes social relations and identities |
| Division of labour | Specialisation of tasks among different workers | Industrial work often breaks production into small repetitive tasks |
| Alienation | Condition in which workers feel separated from the product, process, and meaning of their work | Marx used it to analyse industrial labour |
| Organised sector | Registered units with formal rules, wages, and benefits | Only a small share of Indian workers are in this sector |
| Unorganised sector | Informal work with weak regulation and little security | Most Indian workers are located here |
| Liberalisation | Policy shift reducing state controls and opening markets to private and foreign capital | It changed Indian industry and employment patterns |
| Outsourcing | Giving work to outside firms, contractors, or households | It reduces costs but often increases worker insecurity |
| Contract labour | Workers hired through contractors or temporary arrangements | It reduces job security and benefits |

## 1. Work And Social Identity

Work is not only a way to earn money. It shapes:

- Social status.
- Lifestyle.
- Place of residence.
- Family expectations.
- Education choices.
- Friendships.
- Political awareness.
- Class position.
- Gender roles.

For example, people working in the same city may live very different lives. Film stars, factory owners, casual workers, technicians, drivers, and street vendors may all be part of the same urban economy but have very different incomes, neighbourhoods, diets, leisure, and opportunities.

## 2. Industry As A Social World

The chapter uses examples such as the film industry to show that every industry has:

- Workers.
- Owners.
- Unions.
- Contractors.
- Distributors.
- Marketing networks.
- Consumers.
- Work hierarchies.
- Wage differences.
- Safety concerns.

Even industries associated with glamour depend on labour. Dancers, stunt artists, extras, technicians, drivers, costume workers, and cleaners all form part of the industrial process.

## 3. Social Institutions Shape Work

Work is shaped by social institutions such as:

- Caste.
- Kinship.
- Gender.
- Region.
- Class.
- Education.
- Labour law.
- State policy.

Examples:

- Women are more common in nursing and teaching because society associates women with care.
- Caste and class influence access to high-paying professions.
- Migrant networks help people find jobs.
- Family contacts help self-employed workers find clients.
- Gender stereotypes influence who is seen as suitable for certain work.

## 4. Images Of Industrial Society

Classical sociologists studied industrialisation because it transformed society.

Industrialisation was associated with:

- Urbanisation.
- Factory work.
- Wage labour.
- Loss of face-to-face rural relations.
- Anonymous professional relations.
- Division of labour.
- Machine discipline.
- Repetitive work.
- New class relations.

Thinkers such as Karl Marx, Max Weber, and Emile Durkheim tried to understand these changes.

## 5. Division Of Labour

Industrial production often divides work into small specialised tasks.

A worker may produce only one part of a product and may never see the final product.

Effects include:

- Greater efficiency.
- Repetition.
- Loss of control over the full process.
- Dependence on machines and managers.
- Reduced creative satisfaction.

## 6. Alienation

Karl Marx used the idea of alienation to describe workers' separation from meaningful work.

Workers may feel alienated because:

- They do not own what they produce.
- They do not control the production process.
- Work is repetitive and exhausting.
- They work only to survive.
- Technology may replace them.
- The product belongs to the employer.

Alienation shows that industrial work can create wealth while making workers feel powerless.

## 7. Industrialisation And Equality

Industrialisation can reduce some older inequalities. For example, caste distinctions may matter less in trains, buses, factories, cyber cafes, and urban workplaces.

But older inequalities may continue in new forms.

Examples:

- Upper caste men may dominate well-paying professions.
- Women may be paid less than men.
- Migrants may receive insecure jobs.
- Lower caste groups may remain concentrated in low-status work.

Thus industrialisation does not automatically create equality.

## 8. Specific Character Of Indian Industrialisation

India's industrialisation is different from the western model.

In many developed countries:

- Most people work in services.
- A smaller share works in industry.
- Very few work in agriculture.

In India:

- A large share of workers remains in agriculture.
- The service sector contributes a major share to national income.
- Manufacturing employs fewer people than expected.
- Informal work dominates.

This creates a serious problem: the sector employing many people may generate relatively low income.

## 9. Employment By Sector

The chapter notes that a large share of Indian workers were still in the primary sector, especially agriculture and mining.

The important sociological point is not only the exact percentage. The key issue is mismatch:

- Agriculture employs many people.
- Agriculture contributes a declining share to income.
- Services contribute high income but do not employ everyone.
- Secure industrial employment remains limited.

This produces inequality and underemployment.

## 10. Employment Status

Indian workers may be:

- Self-employed.
- Regular salaried workers.
- Casual labourers.

A large share of Indian workers are self-employed. Many others are casual workers. Only a smaller share has regular salaried work.

This matters because regular jobs usually provide:

- Stable income.
- Benefits.
- Social security.
- Legal protection.
- Pension or provident fund.
- Clearer work rules.

Most workers do not have these advantages.

## 11. Organised And Unorganised Sectors

The organised sector includes registered units, often employing ten or more workers throughout the year, with formal rules and legal benefits.

The unorganised sector includes small, informal, unregistered, or weakly regulated work.

Most Indian workers are in the unorganised sector.

## 12. Social Implications Of A Small Organised Sector

The small size of the organised sector has many consequences.

### Limited secure employment

Few workers have stable jobs with benefits.

### Dependence on personal relations

In small workplaces, employers' personal likes and dislikes may determine wages, promotions, and job security.

### Weak unionisation

Informal workers often lack unions and collective bargaining power.

### Old-age insecurity

Workers without pensions often depend on children or family support.

### Limited cross-regional exposure

Large workplaces can bring people from different backgrounds together. Small informal workplaces may remain socially narrow.

## 13. Government Employment

Government employment has historically been important in India because it provides:

- Security.
- Regular salary.
- Benefits.
- Prestige.
- Mobility across region, caste, and religion.

Public sector workplaces such as Bhilai Steel Plant brought people from different parts of India together, creating new social environments.

## 14. Liberalisation And Indian Industry

Since the 1990s, India followed liberalisation policies.

These included:

- Encouraging private investment.
- Encouraging foreign investment.
- Reducing licensing requirements.
- Opening sectors such as telecom, power, and civil aviation.
- Making foreign products more available.
- Selling state shares in public sector companies.

These policies changed industrial ownership and employment.

## 15. Multinational Companies And Indian Firms

Liberalisation led to:

- Foreign companies entering India.
- Indian companies being bought by multinationals.
- Indian companies expanding abroad.
- Stronger competition.
- Greater advertising.
- New consumer goods.

The example of Parle drinks being bought by Coca Cola shows how a global firm can reshape markets through capital and advertising.

## 16. Disinvestment And Worker Insecurity

Disinvestment means the government sells its share in public sector companies.

Workers may fear disinvestment because:

- Jobs may be cut.
- Voluntary retirement may be pushed.
- Work may be outsourced.
- Benefits may decline.
- Private owners may prioritise profit.

The case of Modern Foods shows how privatisation can lead to large-scale retirement or job loss.

## 17. Outsourcing

Outsourcing means transferring work to outside firms, contractors, or households.

Companies outsource:

- Cleaning.
- Security.
- Manufacturing of parts.
- Packaging.
- Data processing.
- Customer service.
- Home-based production.

### Why companies outsource

- To reduce costs.
- To avoid permanent employees.
- To reduce union strength.
- To increase flexibility.
- To shift risk to smaller firms or workers.

### Effects on workers

- Lower wages.
- Poor conditions.
- Less job security.
- Difficult unionisation.
- More contract labour.

## 18. Land Acquisition For Industry

Industrial development often requires land. This may displace farmers, especially adivasis and other rural communities.

Problems include:

- Low compensation.
- Loss of livelihood.
- Pollution.
- Casualisation of displaced workers.
- Movement to urban footpaths or informal settlements.
- Conflict between development and rights.

This connects industrial society to rural society and social movements.

## 19. How People Find Jobs

Only a small percentage of people find jobs through advertisements or employment exchanges.

Many depend on:

- Relatives.
- Friends.
- Caste networks.
- Regional networks.
- Contractors.
- Former employers.
- Personal reputation.
- Mobile phone contacts.

Self-employed workers such as plumbers, electricians, carpenters, private tutors, architects, and photographers often rely on personal contacts.

## 20. Jobbers And Mistris

In earlier textile mills, workers often got jobs through jobbers or mistris.

Mistris were workers who had the backing of owners. They recruited people from their own region or community and controlled access to jobs.

This shows how industrial work could still be organised through caste, kinship, and regional ties.

## 21. Badli Workers And Contract Work

Badli workers substitute for permanent workers who are absent.

Many badli workers may work for years in the same company but never receive permanent status.

This is a form of insecurity within the organised sector itself.

Contract work allows employers to use labour without giving workers full benefits.

## 22. Contractors And Debt

Contractors recruit labour for:

- Construction.
- Brick kilns.
- Road work.
- Industrial sites.
- Seasonal work.

They may give advance money to workers. This advance becomes debt. Workers may then work without wages until the advance is repaid.

This resembles older debt-based labour, but workers are not tied to a single landlord in exactly the same way. They may move, but their freedom is limited by debt and lack of alternatives.

## 23. How Work Is Controlled

Managers try to increase production in two main ways:

- Extend working hours.
- Increase output within the same time.

Technology can increase productivity, but it can also make workers work at the pace of machines.

## 24. Machinery And Labour

Machines can reduce human effort, but they can also replace workers or intensify work.

Marx and Gandhi both worried about machinery when it displaced people or concentrated wealth.

Gandhi did not oppose tools as such. He opposed the craze for machinery that saved labour for a few while throwing many people out of work.

His support for the spinning wheel was linked with decentralised work, self-reliance, and opposition to exploitation.

## 25. Assembly Line And Just-In-Time Production

Modern factories may use assembly lines where products move continuously and workers perform fixed tasks.

Just-in-time production means parts arrive exactly when needed, reducing storage costs.

For companies, this saves money. For workers, it creates pressure because delays in supply can disrupt targets and increase stress.

## 26. IT Work And Time Pressure

Software and IT workers are often seen as skilled, creative, and middle class. But their work can also involve strict deadlines, long hours, and intense monitoring.

Reasons include:

- Global clients in different time zones.
- Underestimated timelines.
- Outsourced assignments.
- Delivery deadlines.
- Peer pressure.
- Flexible timing that becomes long working time.

This shows that even knowledge work can involve labour control.

## 27. Deskilling

Harry Braverman argued that machines can deskill workers.

Deskilling means that skills once held by workers are transferred to machines or management systems.

Examples:

- Computer software replacing manual drafting skills.
- Assembly lines reducing craft knowledge.
- Automated systems standardising service work.

However, new technologies may also create new skills. The question is who controls those skills and who benefits from them.

## 28. Working Conditions

Workers produce goods and services that others consume, but they often work in difficult conditions.

The government has laws regulating:

- Hours of work.
- Overtime.
- Safety.
- Health.
- Compensation.

However, enforcement is weak in many small workplaces, mines, quarries, and contract arrangements.

## 29. Mining

Mining is dangerous work.

Workers may face:

- Flooding.
- Fire.
- Roof collapse.
- Gas emissions.
- Poor ventilation.
- Blasting injuries.
- Falling objects.
- Heat and rain in open mines.
- Diseases such as tuberculosis and silicosis.

Subcontracting often allows employers to avoid responsibility for accidents and benefits.

## 30. Migrant Workers In Industry

Many industrial and service workers are migrants.

Examples include young women in fish processing plants along the coast, often from Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Kerala.

Migrant workers may experience:

- Crowded housing.
- Long hours.
- Isolation.
- Lack of family support.
- Low bargaining power.
- Some economic independence.

For young women, migration can create both vulnerability and autonomy.

## 31. Home-Based Work

Home-based work includes:

- Lace.
- Zari.
- Brocade.
- Carpets.
- Bidis.
- Agarbattis.
- Embroidery.

This work is often done by women and children.

An agent supplies raw material and collects finished products. Workers are paid piece-rate.

## 32. Bidi Production Chain

Bidi work shows how home-based labour is connected to a larger market.

The chain includes:

1. Collection of tendu leaves.
2. Sale to forest department or contractor.
3. Auction to bidi manufacturers.
4. Supply of leaves and tobacco to contractors.
5. Distribution to home-based workers.
6. Rolling bidis at home.
7. Collection by contractor.
8. Roasting and branding by manufacturer.
9. Distribution to wholesalers.
10. Sale through local shops.

The final product passes through many hands, but home workers receive very low payment.

## 33. Why Home-Based Work Is Invisible

Home-based workers are often invisible because:

- They work inside homes.
- They are counted as housewives rather than workers.
- They lack formal contracts.
- They are paid by piece.
- Their work is mediated by contractors.
- They are difficult to unionise.

This invisibility is gendered because women do much of this work.

## 34. Strikes

A strike is a collective refusal to work.

Workers may strike for:

- Better wages.
- Recognition of unions.
- Safer conditions.
- Job security.
- Benefits.
- Opposition to unfair dismissals.

Strikes are difficult because workers lose wages and employers may use replacement labour.

## 35. Lockouts

A lockout occurs when management shuts the gate and prevents workers from working.

It is a weapon used by employers in industrial conflict.

Both strikes and lockouts show that industrial relations involve power struggles.

## 36. Bombay Textile Strike Of 1982

The Bombay textile strike was led by Dr. Datta Samant and involved nearly a quarter million workers and their families.

Workers demanded:

- Better wages.
- Right to form their own union.

The strike lasted nearly two years. Many workers eventually returned because they were desperate. Around one lakh workers lost jobs.

Consequences included:

- Workers returning to villages.
- Workers shifting to casual labour.
- Movement to powerloom centres such as Bhiwandi, Malegaon, and Ichalkaranji.
- Decline of old textile mill employment.
- Conflict over mill land and the future of Mumbai.

## 37. Trade Unions

Trade unions are organisations of workers.

They help workers:

- Bargain collectively.
- Demand wages.
- Demand safety.
- Resist unfair dismissal.
- Challenge contract work.
- Participate in industrial politics.

However, union power is weaker in small firms, informal work, and outsourced production.

## 38. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Industrialisation removes caste and gender inequality

Industrial workplaces may reduce some older barriers, but caste, gender, and class continue to shape job access and wages.

### Misunderstanding 2: Liberalisation always creates better jobs

Liberalisation creates new opportunities for some, but it also increases outsourcing, contract work, insecurity, and inequality.

### Misunderstanding 3: Home-based work is not real work

Home-based work is part of the economy and produces goods for markets, but it is underpaid and invisible.

### Misunderstanding 4: Technology only helps workers

Technology can reduce drudgery, but it can also intensify work, deskill workers, or replace them.

## 39. Model Answer: Organised And Unorganised Sector

The organised sector consists of registered units with formal rules, regular wages, and benefits such as pension, safety provisions, and legal protection. The unorganised sector includes informal, small, or weakly regulated work where workers often lack security and benefits. In India, most workers are in the unorganised sector. This has important social implications. Workers depend on personal relations, have little job security, and are less likely to be unionised. The small size of the organised sector also explains the high demand for government jobs, which provide stability and benefits.

## 40. Model Answer: Liberalisation And Employment

Liberalisation has changed employment patterns in India by encouraging private and foreign investment, reducing state control, and increasing competition. It has created new jobs in sectors such as telecom, services, retail, IT, and business process outsourcing. However, it has also increased contract labour, outsourcing, disinvestment, and job insecurity. Permanent employment has declined in many sectors, while informal and casual work has expanded. Therefore, liberalisation has produced both opportunity and insecurity, depending on class, education, skill, and access to networks.
