# Chapter 6: Globalisation and Social Change

## Learner Orientation

This chapter explains globalisation as one of the most important processes shaping contemporary society. It studies how global connections affect local lives, work, agriculture, media, culture, consumption, politics, and identity.

The main point is that globalisation is not only about foreign products or world trade. It is about the stretching and intensification of social, economic, political, and cultural relationships across the world. It affects everyone, but not everyone in the same way.

## What You Should Be Able To Do After This Chapter

- Define globalisation sociologically.
- Use sociological imagination to connect local lives with global processes.
- Explain why global interconnections are not completely new.
- Identify what is distinctive about contemporary globalisation.
- Distinguish globalisation from liberalisation.
- Explain transnational corporations, electronic economy, knowledge economy, and global finance.
- Understand the role of communication technologies.
- Analyse the new international division of labour.
- Explain the political dimensions of globalisation.
- Understand cultural homogenisation and glocalisation.
- Analyse the impact of globalisation on consumption, corporate culture, indigenous knowledge, and marginal livelihoods.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |

| Liberalisation | Policy shift that opens the economy by reducing state controls and encouraging market forces | In India, liberalisation since 1991 helped intensify globalisation |
| Sociological imagination | Ability to connect personal life with larger social processes | Helps link a village farmer, call centre worker, or consumer to global systems |
| Transnational corporation | Company that produces or markets goods and services in more than one country | Major driver of economic globalisation |

## 1. Why Globalisation Matters

No discussion of social change in the twenty-first century can ignore globalisation.

It affects:

- Work.
- Agriculture.
- Media.
- Education.
- Consumption.
- Migration.
- Finance.
- Politics.
- Culture.
- Gender relations.
- Youth aspirations.
- Local markets.
- Traditional occupations.

Globalisation connects everyday life with large-scale policies and institutions.

## 2. Sociological Imagination And Globalisation

Sociological imagination helps us connect personal experiences with larger public processes.

Examples:

- A farmer in a remote village may be affected by World Trade Organization rules.
- A young person in a city may find work in a call centre serving foreign clients.
- A local fruit seller may sell imported apples.
- A handloom weaver may lose work because of cheaper machine-made or imported goods.
- A family may change food habits because of global brands.
- A student may attend online classes through a mobile phone.

Globalisation connects the micro and the macro, the local and the global.

## 3. Globalisation Affects People Unequally

Globalisation creates opportunities for some and hardship for others.

Some may gain:

- New jobs.
- New products.
- Better communication.
- International markets.
- Access to information.
- Global education.

Others may lose:

- Livelihood.
- Market protection.
- Traditional occupations.
- Bargaining power.
- Cultural security.
- Access to local resources.

The impact depends on class, region, gender, caste, skill, and access to capital.

## 4. Examples Of Unequal Impact

The chapter gives examples of groups harmed by global market changes:

- Women silk spinners in Bihar lost jobs when cheaper Chinese and Korean silk yarn entered markets.
- Fish workers were affected by large fishing vessels entering Indian waters.
- Women gum collectors in Gujarat lost employment due to cheaper imported gum.
- Rag pickers in cities were affected by imported waste paper.
- Traditional entertainers lost audiences because of television and radio.

These examples show that consumers may gain cheaper products while workers lose livelihoods.

## 5. Are Global Connections New?

Global connections are not entirely new.

India has long been connected with the world through:

- Trade routes.
- Migration.
- Religious travel.
- Conquest.
- Scholarship.
- Language exchange.
- Cultural influence.

The Silk Route connected India with China, Persia, Egypt, Rome, and other regions.

People came to India as traders, conquerors, migrants, scholars, and settlers.

## 6. Early Global Interactions

Indian history includes long-distance connections.

Examples:

- The Silk Route connected civilisations.
- Sanskrit scholars and Buddhist travellers moved across Asia.
- Languages across Asia show influence from India and other regions.
- Communities often remember ancestors migrating from other places.

This means India was never fully isolated.

## 7. Colonialism And Global Connection

Colonialism was also a global system.

Modern capitalism required:

- Raw materials.
- Markets.
- Labour.
- Capital.
- Energy.
- Transport networks.

British colonialism connected India to global capitalism in an unequal way.

Examples:

- Indian raw materials served British industries.
- Indian labourers were sent overseas as indentured workers.
- British manufactured goods entered Indian markets.
- Colonial cities linked India to the world economy.

## 8. Independent India And The World

Independent India also had a global outlook.

The nationalist movement supported:

- Anti-colonial struggles.
- International solidarity.
- Global peace.
- Development cooperation.

Indians continued to migrate, study, trade, and work abroad.

Foreign firms operated in India, and India imported and exported goods and technologies.

So the question is not whether India had global links before. The question is what is distinctive about contemporary globalisation.

## 9. What Is Distinctive About Contemporary Globalisation?

Contemporary globalisation is distinctive because of its speed, scale, and intensity.

It is driven by:

- Information technology.
- Communication networks.
- Global finance.
- Transnational corporations.
- Liberalisation policies.
- New labour arrangements.
- Global media.
- International institutions.
- Rapid transport.
- Market integration.

Social and economic relations stretch across the world more intensely than before.

## 10. Definition Of Globalisation

Globalisation refers to the growing interdependence between different people, regions, and countries as social and economic relationships stretch worldwide.

It includes:

- Economic processes.
- Political processes.
- Cultural processes.
- Technological processes.
- Social consequences.

It is not only an economic issue.

## 11. Globalisation And Liberalisation

Globalisation and liberalisation are related but not identical.

### Globalisation

Refers to worldwide interdependence and flows of capital, goods, people, information, media, and culture.

### Liberalisation

Refers to policy decisions that open the economy by reducing state restrictions on trade, finance, investment, and industry.

In India, liberalisation after 1991 made the economy more open to global markets.

## 12. Economic Policy Of Liberalisation

Since 1991, India introduced economic reforms in:

- Agriculture.
- Industry.
- Trade.
- Foreign investment.
- Technology.
- Public sector.
- Finance.

The assumption was that integration into the global market would benefit the economy.

But these reforms also involved structural adjustment, often requiring cuts in state spending on social sectors such as health, education, and welfare.

## 13. International Institutions

Globalisation has increased the influence of institutions such as:

- International Monetary Fund.
- World Bank.
- World Trade Organization.

These institutions affect national policy through loans, trade rules, and conditions.

This raises questions about democracy and sovereignty: who decides economic policy, elected governments or international institutions?

## 14. Transnational Corporations

Transnational corporations operate in more than one country.

They may:

- Produce goods in one country.
- Assemble in another.
- Market globally.
- Shift production to cheaper locations.
- Use global supply chains.

Examples include Coca Cola, General Motors, Colgate-Palmolive, Kodak, Mitsubishi, and many others. Some Indian companies have also become transnational.

## 15. Electronic Economy

The electronic economy allows banks, investors, companies, and fund managers to move money across countries instantly.

This is possible because of:

- Computers.
- Internet.
- Financial software.
- Global communication networks.
- Stock exchanges.

This creates opportunities for investment, but also risks. Money can enter and leave markets very quickly, causing sudden rises and falls.

## 16. Knowledge Economy

The knowledge economy is based less on physical production and more on information, design, technology, media, services, marketing, and symbolic products.

Examples include:

- Software.
- Media and entertainment.
- Internet services.
- Event management.
- Financial services.
- Professional consulting.
- Design.
- Advertising.

The growth of such work changes education, skills, urban life, and class aspirations.

## 17. Globalisation Of Finance

Financial markets are globally integrated. Large amounts of capital are traded across cities such as New York, Tokyo, London, and Mumbai.

Finance operates 24 hours across time zones.

This makes national economies more connected, but also more vulnerable to global shocks.

## 18. Global Communication

Communication technology has transformed globalisation.

Examples:

- Internet.
- Mobile phones.
- Satellite television.
- Email.
- Video calls.
- Digital platforms.
- Online banking.
- Social media.

Communication compresses time and space. People can interact instantly across long distances.

## 19. Digital Divide

Not everyone has equal access to digital communication.

The digital divide refers to inequalities in access to:

- Internet.
- Devices.
- Electricity.
- Digital literacy.
- Language skills.
- Affordable data.
- Safe online spaces.

The digital divide follows class, gender, region, language, and rural-urban inequalities.

## 20. Telecommunications Expansion In India

India's telecom growth has been dramatic.

At independence, telephone access was extremely limited. By the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, public call offices, mobile phones, and digital networks spread widely.

Telephones and mobile phones serve not only commercial purposes but also social and cultural needs:

- Keeping contact with family.
- Maintaining migrant links.
- Managing emergencies.
- Supporting education.
- Coordinating work.
- Maintaining emotional ties.

## 21. COVID-19 And Online Education

During 2020-21, many children used mobile phones for online classes.

This change is sociologically important because it showed:

- Digital technology can support education.
- Poor households may lack devices or data.
- Gender inequality may affect who gets the phone.
- Teachers and students need digital skills.
- Home space affects learning.
- Education became linked to household resources.

Thus technology does not affect all learners equally.

## 22. Globalisation And Labour

Globalisation has reorganised labour across countries.

A new international division of labour has emerged in which routine manufacturing and service work may move to countries where labour is cheaper.

Production can be broken into parts across many countries.

## 23. Nike Example

Nike grew as an importer and then became a transnational corporation.

Production shifted from:

- Japan.
- South Korea.
- Thailand.
- Indonesia.
- India and other places.

The shift followed labour costs. If labour becomes cheaper elsewhere, production may move again.

This makes workers vulnerable because jobs can be relocated.

## 24. Fordism And Post-Fordism

Fordism refers to mass production in centralised factories, with standardised products and assembly lines.

Post-Fordism refers to flexible production in dispersed locations.

Post-Fordism includes:

- Outsourcing.
- Contract work.
- Global supply chains.
- Smaller production units.
- Flexible labour.
- Rapid market response.

Flexibility benefits producers, but it often makes workers insecure.

## 25. Globalisation And Employment

Globalisation has created new jobs for some urban middle class youth.

Examples:

- Call centres.
- Business process outsourcing.
- Software.
- Retail.
- Restaurants.
- Shopping malls.
- Sales work.
- Digital services.

But employment growth has not always matched economic growth. Many jobs are insecure, temporary, or low paid.

## 26. Political Changes

Globalisation has political dimensions.

The collapse of the socialist world strengthened neo-liberal economic policies.

Neo-liberalism generally supports:

- Free markets.
- Reduced state regulation.
- Reduced subsidies.
- Privatisation.
- Free enterprise.
- Global trade.

But critics argue that markets alone do not ensure fairness. This is why the idea of inclusive globalisation is important.

## 27. Regional And International Organisations

Globalisation has increased the role of regional and international bodies.

Examples:

- European Union.
- Association of Southeast Asian Nations.
- South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation.
- World Trade Organization.
- International governmental organisations.
- International non-governmental organisations.

These organisations address issues that cross national borders.

## 28. IGOs And INGOs

Intergovernmental organisations are created by governments. They regulate or coordinate transnational issues.

International non-governmental organisations are independent organisations that work on issues such as environment, health, rights, and humanitarian aid.

Examples include:

- Greenpeace.
- Red Cross.
- Amnesty International.
- Doctors Without Borders.

## 29. Globalisation And Culture

Globalisation affects culture through:

- Media.
- Food.
- Clothing.
- Music.
- Film.
- Language.
- Consumption.
- Advertising.
- Corporate work culture.
- Youth identity.

Some fear that globalisation will make all cultures similar. Others argue that global influences are adapted locally.

## 30. Homogenisation

Homogenisation means cultures becoming similar.

Concerns include:

- Loss of local languages.
- Decline of traditional arts.
- Spread of global brands.
- Standardised consumer culture.
- Western-style media influence.
- Similar shopping malls and lifestyles.

But cultures rarely change by simple replacement.

## 31. Glocalisation

Glocalisation means mixing global and local elements.

Examples:

- Global television channels using Indian languages.
- Fast food chains modifying menus for Indian consumers.
- Music mixing bhangra, pop, film, and global beats.
- Hinglish advertising.
- Global products marketed through local festivals.

Glocalisation may be creative, but it may also be a market strategy.

## 32. Gender And Culture

Defenders of fixed tradition sometimes use culture to justify gender inequality.

Examples may include:

- Restrictions on women's education.
- Restrictions on women's mobility.
- Exclusion from public life.
- Defence of patriarchal customs.

Globalisation may be blamed for cultural decline, but not every defence of tradition is democratic. A democratic culture must allow women and men equal dignity and freedom.

## 33. Culture Of Consumption

Globalisation has promoted a culture of consumption.

This includes:

- Shopping malls.
- Multiplexes.
- Fast food.
- Fashion.
- Beauty industries.
- Tourism.
- Game shows.
- Advertising.
- Branded goods.

Shopping becomes not only buying goods but also leisure, identity, and status.

The meaning of spending changes. Saving may no longer be presented as the main virtue; consumption becomes a sign of success.

## 34. Corporate Culture

Corporate culture refers to management strategies that create a shared organisational identity.

It may include:

- Company rituals.
- Team events.
- Motivational language.
- Dress codes.
- Performance targets.
- Loyalty-building exercises.
- Branding of workplace identity.

It is meant to increase productivity and competitiveness. But it can also hide stress, long hours, and pressure.

## 35. New Professional Classes

Globalisation has created upwardly mobile professionals in cities.

They work in:

- Software.
- Multinational banks.
- Chartered accountancy firms.
- Stock markets.
- Travel.
- Fashion.
- Entertainment.
- Media.
- Corporate services.

They may earn high salaries and become major consumers, but their work often involves stress and long hours.

## 36. Threats To Indigenous Traditions And Knowledge

Globalisation can threaten traditional occupations and knowledge systems.

Affected groups include:

- Weavers.
- Folk performers.
- Traditional theatre groups.
- Craft workers.
- Indigenous healers.
- Agricultural knowledge holders.
- Communities using local seeds and medicinal plants.

Some companies have attempted to patent knowledge related to Tulsi, Haldi, Rudraksha, or Basmati rice. This raises questions about intellectual property and community knowledge.

## 37. Traditional Entertainers

Traditional performers may lose livelihood when television, radio, and digital media reach villages.

People may watch performances without paying, or prefer mass media entertainment.

This shows that cultural change can affect economic survival.

## 38. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Globalisation is completely new

India has had global connections for centuries. What is new is the speed, scale, and intensity of contemporary globalisation.

### Misunderstanding 2: Globalisation benefits everyone

It creates opportunities for some but destroys livelihoods for others.

### Misunderstanding 3: Globalisation is only economic

It is also political, cultural, technological, and social.

### Misunderstanding 4: Global culture simply replaces local culture

Local cultures adapt, resist, mix, and reinterpret global forms.

## 39. Model Answer: Distinctive Features Of A Globalised Economy

A globalised economy is marked by worldwide interdependence, transnational corporations, electronic finance, global markets, rapid communication, and a new international division of labour. Production may be spread across countries, with design, manufacturing, marketing, and data processing taking place in different locations. Money can move instantly through digital networks. The knowledge economy, based on information and services, becomes more important. In India, liberalisation after 1991 opened the economy to foreign investment, trade, and competition. These changes created new opportunities but also increased insecurity for many workers and producers.

## 40. Model Answer: Glocalisation

Glocalisation refers to the mixing of global and local culture. It happens when global products, media, or companies adapt to local traditions, languages, tastes, and markets. For example, foreign television channels use Indian languages, fast food chains modify menus for Indian consumers, and global music styles mix with local forms. Glocalisation may reflect genuine cultural creativity, but it may also be a market strategy used by companies to sell more effectively. Therefore, glocalisation shows that culture under globalisation is neither purely global nor purely local.
