# Chapter 4: Change and Development in Rural Society

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies rural India as a changing social world. It focuses on land, agriculture, caste, class, labour, land reforms, the Green Revolution, migration, globalisation, and agrarian distress.

The chapter's central idea is that agriculture is not only an economic activity. It is also connected to social power, caste hierarchy, gender relations, cultural life, and state policy. Rural change is therefore never only about crops or technology. It is about people, property, labour, inequality, and development.

## 1. Rural India And Agriculture

India is urbanising, but a large share of its people still live in rural areas. Rural life is closely connected to agriculture and related occupations.

Agriculture matters because:

- It is a major source of livelihood.
- Land is a major form of property.
- Land gives social status.
- Land shapes political power.
- Agricultural seasons shape festivals.
- Rural culture is linked to the agrarian way of life.

Examples of harvest festivals include Pongal, Bihu, Baisakhi, and Ugadi. These show the connection between agriculture and culture.

## 2. The Rural Is Not Only Agriculture

Rural society includes many non-farm occupations.

Traditional rural occupations included:

- Potters.
- Carpenters.
- Weavers.
- Ironsmiths.
- Goldsmiths.
- Storytellers.
- Priests.
- Astrologers.
- Oil pressers.
- Water distributors.

Many of these occupations were historically connected to caste. Some declined during colonial rule and modern industrial growth.

Today rural non-farm work includes:

- Government service.
- Teaching.
- Postal work.
- Factory work.
- Army service.
- Transport.
- Shops.
- Construction.
- Rural services.

This means rural India is diverse and changing.

## 3. Land As A Productive Resource

Land is the most important productive resource in rural areas.

Access to land determines:

- Whether a household cultivates.
- Whether it works as labour.
- Whether it can produce surplus.
- Whether it has social status.
- Whether it can influence local politics.
- Whether it can access credit and markets.

Because land is unequally distributed, rural society is unequal.

## 4. Agrarian Structure

Agrarian structure means the pattern of landholding and agricultural production.

It includes:

- Who owns land.
- How much land different groups own.
- Who leases land.
- Who works as labour.
- Who controls irrigation.
- Who controls credit.
- Who decides crop choices.
- Who benefits from agricultural profits.

Agrarian structure is the basis of rural class structure.

## 5. Rural Classes

Different rural classes include:

- Large landowners.
- Medium farmers.
- Small farmers.
- Marginal farmers.
- Tenants.
- Sharecroppers.
- Landless agricultural labourers.
- Rural artisans.
- Migrant workers.

Large and medium farmers may earn from cultivation. Small and marginal farmers often struggle. Landless labourers depend on wage work and face insecurity.

## 6. Underemployment

Many agricultural labourers do not get work throughout the year. They may work only during sowing, transplanting, harvesting, or other peak seasons.

This is called underemployment.

Underemployment leads to:

- Low income.
- Debt.
- Seasonal migration.
- Dependence on landlords.
- Poor bargaining power.

## 7. Tenants And Sharecroppers

Tenants cultivate land leased from landowners.

Their income is lower because they must pay rent. In some cases, rent may be a large share of the crop.

Tenants face:

- Insecure rights.
- Fear of eviction.
- Limited incentive to invest in land improvement.
- Dependence on landowners.

## 8. Caste And Class In Rural India

Rural class relations are closely connected with caste.

In many regions:

- Dominant landowning groups belong to middle or upper castes.
- Marginal farmers and landless labourers often belong to Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, or Other Backward Classes.
- Former untouchable castes were often denied land ownership.
- Lower caste groups supplied much of the agricultural labour.

However, caste and class do not overlap perfectly everywhere. For example, Brahmins may be high in ritual status but not always major landowners.

## 9. Dominant Caste

M.N. Srinivas used the term dominant caste for a caste that has:

- Large numbers in a region.
- Land ownership.
- Economic power.
- Political influence.
- Social status.

Examples include:

- Jats and Rajputs in parts of Uttar Pradesh.
- Vokkaligas and Lingayats in Karnataka.
- Kammas and Reddis in Andhra Pradesh.
- Jat Sikhs in Punjab.

Dominant castes often control local institutions and labour relations.

## 10. Proprietary Caste And Rural Power

In many regions, one or two caste groups control most land and resources. These groups can command labour, influence panchayats, control credit, and shape village decisions.

This makes rural inequality both economic and social.

The poor may face not only low wages but also caste humiliation and dependence.

## 11. Begar And Hereditary Labour

Begar means unpaid or forced labour.

In many regions, lower caste groups historically had to provide labour to landlords or village elites.

Even where such practices are legally abolished, similar relations may survive through debt, dependence, threats, or social pressure.

## 12. Colonial Agrarian Structure

British colonial rule changed land relations.

In some areas, the British ruled through zamindars. Zamindars gained strong property rights and extracted revenue from cultivators.

Heavy land revenue caused:

- Peasant distress.
- Stagnation in agriculture.
- Famines and insecurity.
- Extraction by intermediaries.

In other areas, the raiyatwari system made cultivators or local landholders responsible for paying revenue directly to the state.

These different systems shaped later regional differences in agrarian structure.

## 13. Land Reforms After Independence

After independence, Indian policymakers saw land reform as necessary for:

- Agricultural growth.
- Reduction of poverty.
- Social justice.
- Weakening of landlords.
- Empowerment of cultivators.

Major land reforms included:

- Abolition of zamindari.
- Tenancy regulation or abolition.
- Land ceiling laws.
- Redistribution of surplus land.

## 14. Abolition Of Zamindari

Abolition of zamindari removed intermediaries between cultivators and the state.

This was one of the more effective land reforms because it weakened many zamindars and strengthened actual landholders.

However, its effects varied across regions.

## 15. Tenancy Reform

Tenancy reforms aimed to:

- Protect tenants from eviction.
- Regulate rents.
- Give tenants ownership rights in some cases.
- Reduce exploitation.

Implementation was often weak. West Bengal and Kerala are important examples where tenancy reforms had stronger effects.

## 16. Land Ceiling Laws

Land ceiling laws placed an upper limit on how much land a family could own.

Surplus land was supposed to be taken by the state and redistributed to landless households, especially Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes.

In practice, many landowners avoided ceiling laws through loopholes such as:

- Benami transfers.
- Dividing land among relatives.
- False records.
- Legal delay.
- Political influence.

Therefore, land reforms did not eliminate rural inequality.

## 17. Why Land Reforms Matter

Land reforms matter for both productivity and justice.

They can:

- Reduce inequality.
- Give cultivators security.
- Encourage investment in land.
- Reduce dependence on landlords.
- Improve bargaining power.
- Support rural democracy.

Weak land reforms leave rural society unequal and limit agricultural development.

## 18. Green Revolution

The Green Revolution was a state-supported agricultural modernisation programme introduced mainly in the 1960s and 1970s.

It involved:

- High-yielding variety seeds.
- Irrigation.
- Chemical fertilisers.
- Pesticides.
- New machinery.
- Credit.
- Scientific farming practices.

It was introduced mainly in regions with assured irrigation, such as Punjab, western Uttar Pradesh, coastal Andhra Pradesh, and parts of Tamil Nadu.

## 19. Positive Effects Of The Green Revolution

The Green Revolution:

- Increased agricultural productivity.
- Increased foodgrain production.
- Helped India move toward food self-sufficiency.
- Raised income for some farmers.
- Encouraged market-oriented agriculture.
- Increased use of technology in farming.

It is considered a major achievement in agricultural policy.

## 20. Unequal Effects Of The Green Revolution

The benefits were not equally distributed.

Medium and large farmers benefited more because they had:

- Land.
- Capital.
- Irrigation.
- Credit.
- Ability to buy fertilisers and pesticides.
- Access to information.

Small farmers often could not afford the new inputs.

Tenant cultivators were sometimes displaced because landowners took back land to cultivate directly once farming became more profitable.

## 21. Commercialisation Of Agriculture

Commercialisation means growing crops mainly for sale in the market.

This increased after the Green Revolution.

Effects included:

- Greater market dependence.
- Cash income for successful farmers.
- Demand for wage labour in some regions.
- Shift from subsistence crops to market crops.
- Exposure to price fluctuations.
- Growing importance of traders, banks, and input dealers.

## 22. Environmental Effects

The Green Revolution also had environmental effects.

These included:

- Declining groundwater.
- Soil degradation.
- Chemical pollution.
- Loss of biodiversity.
- Dependence on hybrid seeds.
- Reduced use of local seed varieties.
- Pesticide-related health risks.

Many farmers' movements and scientists now stress sustainable and organic alternatives.

## 23. Changing Rural Labour Relations

After independence, especially in Green Revolution regions, rural labour relations changed.

Important changes included:

- More use of wage labour.
- Shift from payment in grain to payment in cash.
- Weakening of hereditary labour ties.
- Decline of some bonded labour relations.
- Rise of free wage labourers.

Sociologist Jan Breman described this as a shift from patronage to exploitation.

## 24. Patronage To Exploitation

In earlier patron-client relations, labourers were dependent on landlords but sometimes received limited protection, credit, or customary support.

As agriculture became commercialised, landlords became more profit-oriented. Old bonds weakened, but workers did not necessarily become secure.

They became wage labourers with fewer obligations to landlords but also fewer protections.

This is why freedom in labour markets can coexist with insecurity.

## 25. Capitalist Agriculture

Capitalist agriculture involves:

- Production for market.
- Use of wage labour.
- Investment in technology.
- Profit orientation.
- Separation of workers from land.

Some scholars see Green Revolution regions as moving toward capitalist agriculture. However, caste, family labour, tenancy, and local power continue to shape agriculture, so the process is uneven.

## 26. Rural Development And Infrastructure

The state invested in:

- Irrigation.
- Roads.
- Electricity.
- Credit.
- Cooperatives.
- Agricultural extension.
- Rural development programmes.

These changed the rural economy and linked villages more closely with markets and towns.

## 27. New Rural Elites

In prosperous agricultural regions, some dominant caste farmers became wealthy and diversified into:

- Trade.
- Transport.
- Agro-processing.
- Industry.
- Education.
- Real estate.
- Local politics.

Their children often entered higher education and white-collar jobs.

This produced rural-urban elites who were economically and politically powerful.

## 28. Regional Differences

Rural change is uneven across India.

In some regions, strong agriculture, irrigation, and education produced new elites.

In other regions, lack of land reforms and weak development kept agrarian inequality largely intact.

Kerala followed a different pattern, with land reforms, political mobilisation, education, and migration to Gulf countries shaping rural society.

## 29. Circulation Of Labour

Commercialisation and rural inequality increased seasonal migration.

Workers migrate from poorer regions to:

- Farms in Punjab and Haryana.
- Brick kilns in Uttar Pradesh.
- Construction sites in Delhi, Bengaluru, and other cities.
- Sugarcane fields.
- Urban informal work.

Jan Breman called such workers footloose labour, but this does not mean they are free in a strong sense. They are often insecure and poorly paid.

## 30. Migrant Labour Conditions

Migrant workers may face:

- Low wages.
- No job security.
- Long working hours.
- Poor housing.
- Debt to contractors.
- Lack of legal protection.
- Separation from family.
- Vulnerability to exploitation.

Employers may prefer migrants because they are easier to control and can be paid less.

## 31. Feminisation Of Agricultural Labour

When men migrate for work, women often take on more agricultural tasks in the village.

This is called feminisation of agricultural labour.

But women usually face:

- Lower wages than men.
- Little recognition as farmers.
- Limited land ownership.
- Heavy domestic work.
- Limited access to credit and technology.
- Social restrictions.

Women may work on land but not own it. This is a major gender inequality in rural society.

## 32. Liberalisation And Rural Society

Since the late 1980s and especially after 1991, liberalisation has affected agriculture.

Important changes include:

- Opening Indian markets to imports.
- Greater role of the World Trade Organization.
- Decline of some state support.
- Greater exposure to global prices.
- Entry of multinational companies.
- Increased dependence on commercial seeds, fertilisers, and pesticides.

These changes affect farmers differently depending on land, capital, crop, region, and market access.

## 33. Contract Farming

In contract farming, farmers grow crops for companies under an agreement.

The company may provide:

- Seeds.
- Inputs.
- Technical advice.
- Working capital.
- Guaranteed purchase.

The farmer grows crops such as tomatoes, potatoes, flowers, grapes, cotton, oilseeds, or gherkins.

### Possible benefits

- Assured buyer.
- Access to inputs.
- Market link.
- Potential income.

### Possible risks

- Dependence on company.
- Shift away from food grains.
- High input use.
- Ecological damage.
- Loss of local knowledge.
- Price and quality disputes.
- Exclusion of small farmers.

## 34. Multinational Companies And Inputs

Multinational and private companies increasingly sell:

- Seeds.
- Fertilisers.
- Pesticides.
- Herbicides.
- Agricultural technologies.

As state agricultural extension has weakened, company agents may become the main advisers to farmers. But they also have a commercial interest in selling products.

This can increase farmer dependence and debt.

## 35. Agrarian Distress

Agrarian distress refers to crisis in rural agriculture.

Causes include:

- High input costs.
- Declining subsidies.
- Debt.
- Unstable crop prices.
- Crop failure.
- Dependence on cash crops.
- Natural disasters.
- Market competition.
- Lack of extension support.
- Withdrawal of state support.
- Individualisation of risk.

## 36. Farmer Suicides

The chapter links farmer suicides with agrarian distress.

Many suicides involve marginal or small farmers who borrow heavily to invest in high-cost cultivation but face crop failure, low prices, or debt.

This shows that farmer suicide is not only an individual tragedy. It is connected to structural changes in agriculture, markets, credit, state policy, and rural society.

## 37. State Schemes

The chapter refers to schemes intended to support farmers and rural India, such as:

- Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana.
- Gram Uday se Bharat Uday Abhiyan.
- National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture.
- Kisan Credit Card.

Such schemes aim to reduce risk, improve access to credit, and support rural development, though their effectiveness depends on implementation.

## 38. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Rural society is static

Rural India is changing through markets, migration, education, technology, state policy, and globalisation.

### Misunderstanding 2: Green Revolution benefited everyone equally

It increased productivity, but larger farmers benefited more and inequalities deepened in some regions.

### Misunderstanding 3: Land reform solved rural inequality

Land reforms were uneven and often weakly implemented.

### Misunderstanding 4: Migrant labour is fully free

Migrant workers may not be tied to one landlord, but they face debt, insecurity, and exploitation.

### Misunderstanding 5: Women are not farmers

Women do large amounts of agricultural work but are often denied land titles and recognition.

## 39. Model Answer: Caste And Class In Rural India

Caste and class are closely related in rural India because land ownership is often distributed along caste lines. Dominant caste groups usually own much of the land and control local political power. Many marginal farmers and landless agricultural labourers belong to Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, or Other Backward Classes. Former untouchable castes were often denied land ownership and supplied labour to landowners. However, caste and class do not overlap perfectly in every region. For example, some high ritual status groups may not be major landowners. Still, the broad link between caste, land, labour, and power is central to understanding rural inequality.

## 40. Model Answer: Green Revolution

The Green Revolution was a programme of agricultural modernisation based on high-yielding seeds, fertilisers, pesticides, irrigation, and technology. It increased agricultural productivity and helped India become more self-sufficient in foodgrain production. However, its benefits were uneven. Medium and large farmers benefited more because they could afford inputs and had access to irrigation and credit. Small farmers and tenants often gained less, and some tenants were displaced. The Green Revolution also increased dependence on chemical inputs and contributed to environmental problems such as groundwater depletion and soil degradation. Therefore, it was both a technological achievement and a source of new social and ecological concerns.
