# Chapter 7: Mass Media and Communications

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies mass media as a social institution. It looks at newspapers, radio, television, film, advertisements, internet, mobile phones, and digital communication. The chapter does not treat media only as entertainment. It studies media as a field shaped by the state, market, technology, language, class, audience, and democracy.

The main idea is that media and society influence each other. Media is shaped by the society in which it operates, but media also shapes public opinion, national identity, consumer culture, political debate, everyday habits, and social relationships.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Mass media | Communication forms that reach large audiences, such as television, newspapers, radio, film, internet, and magazines | Media is central to contemporary public life |
| Mass communication | Communication from organised producers to large audiences | Requires technology, capital, and management |
| Print media | Newspapers, magazines, books, pamphlets, and other printed forms | Played a major role in reform and nationalism |
| Public broadcasting | Broadcasting intended to serve public interest, often supported by the state | All India Radio and Doordarshan were shaped by development goals |
| Commercialisation | Shift toward advertising, profit, ratings, and consumer markets | Changed media content and priorities |
| Infotainment | Mix of information and entertainment | Used to attract audiences but may weaken serious public debate |
| Media conglomerate | Large company owning many media platforms | Concentrates media power |
| Localisation | Adapting media content to local languages, cultures, and audiences | Helped global and national media reach Indian audiences |
| Digital divide | Unequal access to digital technology and media | Shapes who can use new communication forms |
| Audience segmentation | Dividing audiences by interest, language, age, class, region, or lifestyle | Drives specialised media content |

## 1. What Is Mass Media?

Mass media includes:

- Television.
- Newspapers.
- Radio.
- Films.
- Magazines.
- Advertisements.
- Video games.
- CDs.
- Internet.
- Mobile communication.
- Digital platforms.

It is called mass media because it reaches large audiences.

Mass communication is different from face-to-face communication because it requires:

- Large-scale technology.
- Formal organisation.
- Capital investment.
- Professional workers.
- Production schedules.
- Distribution systems.
- Management.

## 2. Media In Everyday Life

Media is part of ordinary life.

People use media to:

- Read news.
- Watch entertainment.
- Follow sports.
- Contact family.
- Search for jobs.
- Check exam results.
- Advertise services.
- Learn.
- Share opinions.
- Participate in public debate.

Mobile phones have changed the daily lives of students, workers, migrants, service providers, and families.

## 3. Media And Society: A Dialectical Relationship

The relationship between media and society is dialectical. This means both influence each other.

Society shapes media through:

- State policy.
- Market demand.
- Language diversity.
- Technology.
- Class and literacy.
- Cultural expectations.
- Political conflicts.

Media shapes society through:

- News.
- Public opinion.
- Advertising.
- Consumer desires.
- Political awareness.
- National identity.
- Social reform.
- Entertainment.
- Debate.

## 4. Why Sociology Studies Media

Sociology studies media because media reveals:

- Power.
- Inequality.
- Culture.
- Technology.
- State control.
- Market control.
- Public communication.
- Audience behaviour.
- Social change.

Media is not neutral. What is shown, who is shown, how it is shown, and what is ignored are sociological questions.

## 5. Digital Divide

Different groups do not have equal access to media.

Access depends on:

- Income.
- Education.
- Language.
- Region.
- Gender.
- Electricity.
- Internet connection.
- Device ownership.
- Digital skills.

The digital divide means that new media may expand opportunity for some while excluding others.

## 6. Beginnings Of Modern Mass Media

The first modern mass media institution was the printing press.

Modern printing developed in Europe with Johann Gutenberg in the fifteenth century.

At first, printing was often used for religious books. Later, with industrialisation, transport, literacy, and technology, newspapers began to reach mass audiences.

## 7. Print And Nationalism

Print helped create a sense of shared belonging.

People who never met each other could read or hear the same news. This helped create the feeling of being part of a larger community.

Benedict Anderson called the nation an imagined community because members of a nation imagine themselves as connected even if they do not personally know one another.

## 8. Print In Colonial India

Print media was central to:

- Social reform.
- Public debate.
- Anti-colonial opinion.
- Nationalist mobilisation.
- Spread of political ideas.
- Criticism of colonial policies.

Newspapers and journals allowed reformers and nationalists to debate issues such as caste, gender, education, rights, and colonial rule.

## 9. Colonial Control Of Media

Under British rule, newspapers, films, and radio were monitored.

Radio was state-owned, so nationalist voices could not easily use it.

Newspapers and magazines had limited circulation because literacy was low, but their influence was larger than circulation figures because news was read aloud and passed by word of mouth.

The colonial state imposed censorship when nationalist opinion became threatening.

## 10. Early Indian Newspapers

Important early publications included:

- Sambad Kaumudi.
- Mirat-ul-Akbar.
- Bombay Samachar.
- Shome Prakash.
- The Times of India.
- The Pioneer.
- The Madras Mail.
- The Statesman.
- The Civil and Military Gazette.

These publications show the growth of a public sphere in colonial India.

## 11. Media After Independence

After independence, the media was expected to support nation-building.

Jawaharlal Nehru called on media to act as a watchdog of democracy.

Media was expected to:

- Spread development information.
- Promote scientific temper.
- Support national integration.
- Challenge oppressive practices.
- Fight untouchability.
- Oppose child marriage.
- Promote modern education.
- Inform citizens about state programmes.

## 12. Films Division And Development Communication

The Films Division produced newsreels and documentaries that were shown before films in cinema halls.

These films documented development efforts such as:

- Dams.
- Factories.
- Agriculture.
- Scientific work.
- Public health.
- National unity.

This reflected the early state's development vision.

## 13. Radio In India

Radio broadcasting began with amateur clubs in cities such as Kolkata and Chennai.

By independence, India had only a few radio stations, mainly in major cities.

After independence, All India Radio expanded across the country and became a major public broadcasting institution.

## 14. All India Radio And Development

All India Radio broadcast:

- News.
- Current affairs.
- Development programmes.
- Agricultural information.
- Development conversations.
- Educational content.
- Music and entertainment.

Radio was important because it could reach people who were not literate.

During the Green Revolution, AIR broadcast information about high-yielding varieties and farming methods.

## 15. Vividh Bharati

Vividh Bharati became popular because it broadcast Hindi film songs and entertainment programmes.

It also carried sponsored programmes and advertisements, showing the beginning of commercial elements within public broadcasting.

## 16. Television In India

Television began experimentally in India in 1959, partly to promote rural development.

The Satellite Instructional Television Experiment, known as SITE, broadcast educational programmes to rural viewers in the mid-1970s.

Doordarshan later expanded to major cities and then nationwide.

## 17. Doordarshan And Nation-Building

Early television programming included:

- News.
- Children's programmes.
- Women's programmes.
- Farmers' programmes.
- Educational programmes.
- Entertainment.

The state used television to promote development and national unity.

## 18. Commercialisation Of Television

Television became more commercial with:

- Sponsored programmes.
- Advertisements.
- Urban consumer audiences.
- Entertainment serials.
- Colour broadcasting.
- National network expansion.

The 1982 Asian Games helped popularise colour television.

## 19. Hum Log And Entertainment-Education

Hum Log was India's first long-running soap opera.

It combined entertainment with social messages such as:

- Gender equality.
- Small family norm.
- National integration.
- Social responsibility.

The programme showed that entertainment media could also teach and influence social attitudes.

## 20. Epics And Television Publics

Television broadcasts of Ramayana and Mahabharata became extremely popular.

They created shared viewing experiences across regions and households.

They also showed the power of television to shape national cultural imagination.

## 21. Print Media After Independence

After independence, newspapers were seen as part of nation-building and public service.

Journalism was often understood as a calling connected with:

- Patriotism.
- Social reform.
- Public service.
- Democratic debate.

Over time, newspapers became more professional and commercially organised.

## 22. Emergency And Media Freedom

The Emergency of 1975 was a major challenge to press freedom because censorship was imposed.

The restoration of democracy in 1977 restored media freedom.

This history shows why free media is essential to democracy.

## 23. Newspaper Expansion

Indian newspapers expanded significantly after independence.

Expansion occurred because of:

- Rising literacy.
- Better technology.
- Regional language growth.
- Lower prices.
- Multiple editions.
- Wider distribution.
- Advertising revenue.
- Local news networks.

Language newspapers became especially important.

## 24. Technology And Newspaper Production

New technology changed newspaper production.

Earlier tools included:

- Notebook.
- Pen.
- Typewriter.
- Telephone.

New tools include:

- Digital recorder.
- Laptop.
- Mobile phone.
- Internet.
- Digital camera.
- News software.
- Local area networks.

Technology increased speed and allowed late deadlines, district editions, and rapid photo transmission.

## 25. Newspapers As Consumer Products

Commercial competition changed newspapers.

Newspapers began to:

- Segment audiences.
- Add supplements.
- Expand sports, entertainment, lifestyle, and gossip pages.
- Depend more on advertisements.
- Reduce prices to increase circulation.
- Use infotainment.

This raises questions about whether public service journalism is weakened by market pressure.

## 26. Infotainment

Infotainment combines information and entertainment.

Examples:

- News presented dramatically.
- Entertainment-style political coverage.
- Celebrity-heavy news.
- Crime shows with suspense.
- Lifestyle pages presented as news.

Infotainment can attract audiences, but it may reduce attention to serious issues.

## 27. Globalisation And Television

In 1991 India had mainly state-controlled television. By the late 1990s, private satellite channels multiplied rapidly.

Factors included:

- Gulf War coverage popularising CNN.
- Arrival of Star TV.
- Zee TV in Hindi.
- Cable networks.
- Private investment.
- Advertising markets.
- Liberalisation.

Television became more competitive and market-oriented.

## 28. Cable Television

Cable television grew quickly in cities.

Video cassette recorders and apartment cable networks gave viewers alternatives to Doordarshan.

Cable operators expanded rapidly, offering films, music, foreign programmes, and private channels.

## 29. Localisation Of Global Media

Foreign networks realised that Indian audiences preferred familiar languages and cultural references.

Localisation included:

- Hindi programming.
- Regional language channels.
- Dubbing.
- Indian hosts.
- Local music.
- Indian festivals.
- Hinglish slogans.

This is similar to glocalisation studied in the previous chapter.

## 30. Television News And Competition

News channels compete for viewership.

Competition can lead to:

- Faster reporting.
- Live coverage.
- Public debate.
- More regional voices.

But it can also lead to:

- Sensationalism.
- Invasion of privacy.
- Emotional spectacle.
- Neglect of complex issues.
- Race for exclusive visuals.

The rescue coverage of a child trapped in a borewell is an example of media turning a tragedy into continuous national spectacle.

## 31. Reality Shows And Soap Operas

Soap operas are serialised stories without a fixed ending until they are taken off air. Viewers become familiar with characters and histories.

Reality shows, game shows, talk shows, and comedy shows have become popular.

Examples include:

- Kaun Banega Crorepati.
- Indian Idol.
- Bigg Boss.

Many formats are adapted from global models but localised for Indian audiences.

## 32. FM Radio

Privately owned FM radio stations began expanding from 2002.

FM stations often focus on:

- Music.
- Entertainment.
- Local events.
- Youth audiences.
- Advertising.
- Celebrity anchors.

Private FM channels are usually not allowed to broadcast independent political news bulletins, so many specialise in music and lifestyle content.

## 33. Media Conglomerates

Many FM channels are owned by larger media groups.

Media conglomerates can own:

- Newspapers.
- Radio channels.
- Television channels.
- Digital platforms.
- Advertising networks.

This concentration raises questions about diversity of voices.

## 34. Community And Local Radio

Local radio has great potential because it can serve specific communities, languages, and local needs.

A village-level station can broadcast:

- Local songs.
- Public interest messages.
- Missing person alerts.
- Health information.
- Agriculture information.
- Local news.

This shows that media does not always need to be only national or corporate. It can be deeply local.

## 35. Media, State, And Market

Media is shaped by both state and market.

The state can:

- Own broadcasting institutions.
- Regulate licenses.
- Censor in exceptional or authoritarian moments.
- Promote development communication.
- Make broadcasting policy.

The market can:

- Shape content through advertising.
- Reward high ratings.
- Promote consumer culture.
- Encourage sensationalism.
- Support entertainment-heavy programming.

A democratic media system must balance freedom, responsibility, diversity, and public interest.

## 36. Media And Democracy

Media supports democracy by:

- Informing citizens.
- Exposing corruption.
- Giving voice to grievances.
- Enabling public debate.
- Questioning power.
- Connecting regions.
- Reporting social issues.

But media can also weaken democracy if it:

- Spreads misinformation.
- Ignores marginalised groups.
- Serves only advertisers.
- Sensationalises suffering.
- Concentrates ownership.
- Reduces politics to spectacle.

## 37. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Media only reflects society

Media also shapes society by influencing public opinion, consumer desires, political debate, and cultural identity.

### Misunderstanding 2: More channels always mean more diversity

Many channels may still be controlled by a few owners or driven by similar commercial priorities.

### Misunderstanding 3: Entertainment has no social effect

Entertainment can shape gender norms, family ideals, consumption, aspirations, and political imagination.

### Misunderstanding 4: Digital media is equally available to all

Access depends on money, devices, literacy, language, electricity, and social freedom.

## 38. Model Answer: Changes In Television

Television in India began as a state-led medium for development and education. Early Doordarshan programming included news, farmers' programmes, women's programmes, children's programmes, and educational content. Programmes such as Hum Log combined entertainment with social messages. Colour television and national network expansion increased television's reach. After liberalisation, private satellite channels, cable networks, and global media companies transformed television. Entertainment, advertising, reality shows, regional channels, and 24-hour news became central. This expansion democratised access to information in some ways, but it also increased commercialisation, sensationalism, and competition for ratings.

## 39. Model Answer: Newspaper Change

Newspapers in India were once closely linked with social reform, nationalism, and public service. After independence, they continued to contribute to democratic debate and nation-building. With technological change, newspapers became faster and more localised, using digital tools, multiple editions, and better networks. At the same time, commercial competition made newspapers increasingly dependent on advertising and audience segmentation. Infotainment, lifestyle pages, and consumer content expanded. Therefore, newspapers have grown in reach and technology, but they also face pressure to balance public service with market demands.
