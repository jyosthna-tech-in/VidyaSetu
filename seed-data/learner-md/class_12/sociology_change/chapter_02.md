# Chapter 2: Cultural Change

## Learner Orientation

This chapter studies how Indian society changed culturally under colonial conditions and after. The previous chapter focused on structural change, such as cities, industries, land, and law. This chapter turns to culture: values, norms, lifestyles, customs, education, reform, gender ideas, caste practices, religion, language, art, and everyday behaviour.

The chapter has two major parts:

- Social reform movements of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
- Four sociological processes: sanskritisation, westernisation, modernisation, and secularisation.

The chapter's main message is that cultural change in India was not a simple replacement of tradition by modernity. It was a complex process of debate, reinterpretation, imitation, resistance, reform, and conflict.

- Define sanskritisation and explain its limits.

- Analyse how caste, gender, class, region, and religion shaped cultural change differently.
- Critically examine whether cultural change always produces equality.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Cultural change | Change in norms, values, lifestyles, beliefs, practices, and meanings | The chapter studies changes in ways of life and thought |
| Social reform movement | Organised effort to change social practices seen as unjust or harmful | Reformers challenged sati, child marriage, caste discrimination, and restrictions on widows and women |
| Sanskritisation | Process by which a lower caste, tribe, or group adopts customs and lifestyle of a higher caste | Shows status mobility within caste hierarchy, but also has serious limits |
| Westernisation | Changes caused by long contact with western culture and British rule | Includes changes in technology, institutions, values, dress, education, art, and lifestyle |
| Modernisation | Movement toward modern values such as science, individual choice, achievement, bureaucracy, and universalism | Helps analyse social change beyond outward western habits |
| Secularisation | Decline or transformation of religious influence in social life | In India it often means religion and caste changing their social role rather than disappearing |
| Secularisation of caste | Shift of caste from ritual hierarchy toward political and interest-based mobilisation | Important for understanding caste associations and caste politics |
| Gendered change | Change that affects men and women differently | Many processes of cultural change have different consequences for women |

## 1. Culture And Social Structure

Social structure means the continuing arrangement of persons in relationships defined or controlled by institutions.

Culture means socially established norms, values, meanings, symbols, and patterns of behaviour.

The two cannot be completely separated.

Examples:

- English education changed institutions, but also changed values and aspirations.
- Urban life changed residence patterns, but also changed clothing, speech, leisure, and social interaction.
- Social reform changed laws, but also challenged ideas about gender, caste, marriage, and religion.

## 2. Colonial Context Of Cultural Change

Colonialism brought new institutions and ideas into Indian society. These included:

- Western education.
- Printing press.
- Newspapers.
- Railways.
- Telegraph.
- Public associations.
- Legal reform.
- Liberal ideas.
- Ideas of equality, rights, and freedom.

But colonialism was contradictory. Indians read about liberty and equality while living under foreign rule. This contradiction forced Indians to rethink both tradition and modernity.

## 3. Social Reform Movements

Social reform movements emerged in the nineteenth century as organised attempts to change practices considered unjust.

Major issues included:

- Sati.
- Child marriage.
- Ban on widow remarriage.
- Restrictions on women's education.
- Caste discrimination.
- Untouchability.
- Purdah.
- Polygamy.
- Lack of access to education for oppressed groups.

These issues were not entirely new. Earlier traditions such as Buddhism, Bhakti, and Sufi movements had also challenged social exclusion. What was new in the nineteenth century was the colonial-modern context: print, public debate, new associations, western liberal ideas, and reinterpretation of older texts.

## 4. The Modern Framework Of Reform

Sociologist Satish Saberwal identified three important aspects of the modern framework of change:

- Modes of communication.
- Forms of organisation.
- Nature of ideas.

### Modes of communication

New technologies helped ideas move faster.

Examples:

- Printing press.
- Newspapers.
- Journals.
- Telegraph.
- Railways.
- Steamships.
- Public lectures.
- Translations across Indian languages.

Reformers from different regions could learn from one another.

### Forms of organisation

Modern associations became important.

Examples:

- Brahmo Samaj.
- Arya Samaj.
- All-India Muslim Ladies Conference.
- Reform associations.
- Public meetings.
- Educational societies.

These organisations created a public arena for debate.

### Nature of ideas

Reformers used a mix of:

- Western liberal ideas.
- Humanitarian arguments.
- Religious reinterpretation.
- Scriptural debate.
- Ideas of national progress.
- Ideas of education and reason.

This combination made reform intellectually complex.

## 5. Reformers And The Mix Of Ideas

Social reformers did not all argue in the same way. Many combined modern and traditional arguments.

Examples:

- Raja Ram Mohun Roy criticised sati using humanitarian reasoning as well as Hindu texts.
- Ranade defended widow remarriage through scriptural interpretation.
- Sir Syed Ahmed Khan emphasised free inquiry within Islam and the compatibility of reason and faith.
- Kandukuri Viresalingam drew on both Indian logic and modern science.
- Jotiba Phule argued against caste and opened a school for women in Pune.
- Pandita Ramabai challenged gender oppression and worked for women's education.

## 6. Female Education As A Reform Issue

Female education became a central question.

Reformers argued that:

- Society could not progress if women remained uneducated.
- Women needed education for self-respect and social participation.
- Educated women could become better citizens and contributors to the nation.

But there was disagreement. Some argued that educated women existed in ancient India. Others said education had always been limited to privileged groups. These debates show that tradition itself was being reinterpreted.

## 7. Caste And Reform

Reform movements differed in their approach to caste.

Some reformers focused mainly on issues faced by upper caste, middle class women and men.

Others placed caste oppression at the centre. For example, Jotiba Phule saw caste as a system of domination, not merely a set of social customs.

Different reformers disagreed on whether social evils were:

- A decline from true religion.
- Misinterpretation of tradition.
- An intrinsic part of caste and religious hierarchy.
- A result of ignorance.
- A result of power relations.

This makes reform movements important for sociology because they reveal conflict within society.

## 8. Reform Within Muslim Communities

Muslim reformers debated issues such as:

- Polygamy.
- Purdah.
- Women's education.
- Religious interpretation.
- Social modernisation.

For example, Jahanara Shah Nawas criticised the misuse of polygamy and argued that educated women should help end such practices. The debate around such resolutions shows that reform involved conflict within communities, not only conflict between colonisers and Indians.

## 9. Opposition To Reform

Reform movements faced resistance.

Examples:

- Dharma Sabha opposed the Brahmo Samaj's campaign against sati.
- Opponents of women's education claimed it would destroy society.
- Reformers supporting widow remarriage were often socially boycotted.
- Oppressed caste children entering schools sometimes led privileged caste families to withdraw their children.

This shows that social change is contested. Reform is not only an idea; it challenges power.

## 10. Four Processes Of Cultural Change

The chapter studies four important processes:

- Sanskritisation.
- Westernisation.
- Modernisation.
- Secularisation.

These processes can overlap. A person may be modern in one respect and traditional in another. A family may adopt western furniture but retain conservative gender roles. A caste group may use modern politics to defend caste identity.

## 11. Sanskritisation

The term sanskritisation was introduced by M.N. Srinivas.

It refers to a process in which a lower caste, tribe, or social group adopts the customs, rituals, beliefs, and lifestyle of a higher caste, especially a twice-born caste.

Examples of adoption may include:

- Vegetarianism.
- Wearing sacred symbols.
- Performing certain rituals.
- Claiming higher status.
- Changing marriage practices.
- Adopting upper caste customs.
- Using Sanskritic names or vocabulary.

The aim is usually to improve status in the local caste hierarchy.

## 12. Conditions For Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation often becomes possible when a group has:

- Improved economic position.
- Improved political power.
- Higher collective self-consciousness.
- Contact with religious centres or sects.
- A desire for upward status mobility.

However, the process is not easy. Dominant castes may punish groups that try to imitate them.

## 13. Regional Variation In Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation did not operate the same way everywhere.

In regions where Sanskritic castes were dominant, the wider region could become more Sanskritised.

In regions where non-Sanskritic groups were dominant, a different cultural influence could prevail. This has been called de-Sanskritisation.

In Punjab, for example, Sanskritic influence was historically less dominant than Persian influence for long periods.

## 14. Gender And Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation can affect men and women differently.

For men, it may sometimes mean improved public status.

For women, it may mean:

- Greater seclusion.
- More restrictions on mobility.
- Dowry replacing bride-price.
- Stronger control over sexuality.
- More pressure to follow upper caste gender norms.

Therefore, status mobility for a group may not mean freedom for everyone within that group.

## 15. Criticisms Of Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation has been criticised for several reasons.

### It exaggerates mobility

It may improve the position of some groups, but it does not change the caste structure itself.

This means it may produce positional change, not structural change.

### It accepts upper caste norms as superior

The concept may appear to treat upper caste customs as desirable and lower caste customs as inferior.

### It can reproduce inequality

By adopting purity and pollution ideas, groups may reinforce the same hierarchy that oppressed them.

### It can harm women

Adoption of upper caste practices may increase restrictions on women.

### It erodes Dalit and oppressed caste cultural traditions

Skills, labour, ecological knowledge, craft identities, and local cultural forms may be treated as shameful instead of valuable.

## 16. Anti-Caste Assertion

Not all upward mobility took the path of sanskritisation. Anti-Brahmin movements, Backward Classes movements, and Dalit movements often rejected the idea that oppressed groups should imitate upper castes.

Dalit assertion placed pride in Dalit identity, culture, and struggle.

This shows that cultural change can also involve resistance, not only imitation.

## 17. Westernisation

M.N. Srinivas defined westernisation as changes brought about in Indian society and culture as a result of over 150 years of British rule.

Westernisation includes changes in:

- Technology.
- Institutions.
- Education.
- Ideology.
- Values.
- Dress.
- Food.
- Art.
- Literature.
- Household objects.
- Architecture.
- Ways of thinking.

## 18. Types Of Westernisation

Westernisation occurred at different levels.

### Elite westernisation

Some Indians adopted western education, lifestyles, and intellectual patterns. Many early reformers belonged to this group.

### General spread of western cultural traits

Many households adopted objects and habits such as:

- Dining tables.
- Sofas.
- Refrigerators.
- Television.
- Western-style clothing.
- New food habits.
- Modern medicine.
- New technologies.

### Western influence on art and literature

Artists and writers responded to the colonial encounter. Ravi Varma's paintings, for example, combined western techniques such as perspective and oil painting with Indian themes.

## 19. Westernisation Is Not The Same As Modernity

Westernisation may involve external forms, such as dress or furniture, without democratic values.

Examples:

- A person may wear western clothes but hold caste prejudices.
- A family may use modern technology but restrict women's freedom.
- Female foeticide combines modern technology with patriarchal discrimination.

Therefore, westernisation does not automatically mean equality, democracy, or rationality.

## 20. Modernisation

Modernisation is broader than westernisation.

Modernisation is associated with:

- Scientific attitude.
- Universal rather than purely local outlook.
- Individual choice.
- Achievement rather than birth-based status.
- Bureaucratic organisation.
- Rational planning.
- Technology.
- Separation of work from family.
- Education.
- Mobility.

Modernisation theory often assumed that societies should follow the path taken by western Europe and North America. But India's modernisation occurred in a colonial context, so it cannot be understood as a simple copy of the west.

## 21. Modernity And Tradition

Modernity did not simply destroy tradition. It also led to rethinking tradition.

Examples:

- Reformers reinterpreted religious texts.
- Nationalists used modern ideas while defending Indian heritage.
- Social groups combined traditional clothing with western accessories.
- Families adopted modern education while retaining caste marriage preferences.

Tradition is not fixed. It is constantly reworked.

## 22. Secularisation

In western sociology, secularisation often means decline in religious influence.

Indicators may include:

- Lower participation in religious organisations.
- Decline in religious belief.
- Reduced social power of religious institutions.

But in India the situation is more complex. Religion has not simply disappeared. Instead, religious practices have changed in their social meaning.

## 23. Secular Dimensions Of Ritual

Rituals may have non-religious functions.

They can provide:

- Occasions for social gathering.
- Opportunities to display wealth.
- Public status.
- Family prestige.
- Social networking.
- Political visibility.

For example, weddings may show family status through cars, jewellery, clothing, guest lists, and the presence of important persons.

## 24. Secularisation Of Caste

Traditional caste operated within a religious framework of purity and pollution.

In contemporary India, caste also operates as:

- Political identity.
- Voting bloc.
- Pressure group.
- Association.
- Basis for reservation demands.
- Social network.
- Marriage preference.

This shift is called the secularisation of caste.

Caste has not disappeared. It has changed form.

## 25. Caste And Politics

Rajni Kothari argued that asking whether caste is disappearing is less useful than asking how caste is changing under modern politics.

Politics uses existing social identities. Since caste is one of the major ways people are organised in Indian society, political actors mobilise caste identities.

This does not mean politics is outside society. It means politics works through social structures.

## 26. Cultural Change Is Uneven

Different groups experience cultural change differently.

The impact varies by:

- Caste.
- Class.
- Gender.
- Region.
- Religion.
- Language.
- Rural or urban location.
- Access to education.
- Access to wealth.

For example:

- English may be a path to mobility for some and a mark of exclusion for others.
- Western education may empower women, but may also be limited by patriarchal family structures.
- Modern politics may weaken ritual hierarchy but strengthen caste as political identity.

## 27. Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Tradition is fixed

Tradition is constantly interpreted, defended, criticised, and changed.

### Misunderstanding 2: Modernity always means equality

Modern technology and modern institutions can coexist with caste, patriarchy, and prejudice.

### Misunderstanding 3: Westernisation and modernisation are identical

Westernisation refers to western influence. Modernisation refers to broader processes such as rationality, science, achievement, and institutional change.

### Misunderstanding 4: Sanskritisation removes caste inequality

Sanskritisation may improve status for some, but it does not abolish caste hierarchy.

## 29. Model Answer: Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation is a concept developed by M.N. Srinivas to describe the process by which a lower caste, tribe, or other group adopts the customs, rituals, beliefs, and lifestyle of a higher caste in order to improve its status in the local hierarchy. It may involve vegetarianism, ritual practices, Sanskritic names, or upper caste marriage customs. The process often requires improved economic or political position. However, it has been criticised because it exaggerates social mobility and does not change the caste structure. It accepts upper caste norms as superior and may reproduce ideas of purity and pollution. It can also harm women by increasing restrictions such as seclusion or dowry. Therefore, sanskritisation may produce positional mobility for some groups but does not ensure social equality.

## 30. Model Answer: Westernisation And Modernisation

Westernisation refers to changes in Indian society caused by long contact with western culture and British rule. It includes changes in technology, education, institutions, dress, food, art, and values. Modernisation is broader. It refers to processes such as scientific thinking, individual choice, achievement-based status, bureaucracy, and rational organisation. A person may be westernised in dress but not modern in values. For example, someone may wear western clothes while holding caste or gender prejudices. Therefore, westernisation and modernisation overlap but are not identical.
