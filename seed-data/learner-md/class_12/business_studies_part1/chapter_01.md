# Unit 1A: Concept of Management

Management is needed wherever people work together toward a goal. A school, hospital, shop, factory, government office, sports team, and large corporation all require management.

Management can be understood as a process of getting things done through people with the aim of achieving organisational goals effectively and efficiently.

## Important Elements of the Definition

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Process | Management is a series of continuous functions, not a one-time action. |
| Getting things done | Managers do not perform every task personally; they guide people and resources. |
| Through people | Human effort is central to management. |
| Goals | Management is purposeful and goal-oriented. |
| Effectively | The correct task must be completed. |
| Efficiently | The task must be completed with minimum waste. |

# Unit 1B: Effectiveness and Efficiency

Effectiveness and efficiency are related but not identical.

## Effectiveness

Effectiveness means achieving the desired result. If a company planned to produce 5,000 units and actually produced 5,000 units, it was effective.

## Efficiency

Efficiency means using resources wisely. If the company produced 5,000 units but used excessive labour, electricity, and raw materials, it was effective but not efficient.

## Comparison

| Basis | Effectiveness | Efficiency |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | End result. | Cost and resource use. |
| Main question | Was the goal achieved? | Was the goal achieved with minimum waste? |
| Example | Completing production target. | Completing production target at planned cost. |
| Risk if ignored | Goals remain incomplete. | Costs rise and profits fall. |

## Best Managerial Outcome

The best situation is high effectiveness and high efficiency. A manager should not only complete the right task but also use time, money, material, and people carefully.

# Unit 1C: Characteristics of Management

## 1. Management Is Goal-Oriented

Every organisation exists for a purpose. Management directs individual and group effort toward that purpose.

Examples:

1. A business may aim for profit and growth.
2. A hospital may aim for patient care.
3. A school may aim for quality education.

## 2. Management Is All-Pervasive

Management is needed in all types of organisations:

1. Business and non-business organisations.
2. Large and small organisations.
3. Manufacturing and service organisations.
4. Local, national, and global organisations.

## 3. Management Is Multidimensional

Management involves three dimensions:

| Dimension | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Management of work | Tasks must be completed. |
| Management of people | Employees must be guided and motivated. |
| Management of operations | Inputs must be converted into useful outputs. |

## 4. Management Is a Continuous Process

Management does not stop after one decision. Managers keep planning, organising, staffing, directing, and controlling.

## 5. Management Is a Group Process

Organisations need people with different skills. Management coordinates their efforts.

## 6. Management Is Dynamic

Business conditions change. Management must respond to changes in technology, competition, laws, customer tastes, and resources.

## 7. Management Is Intangible

Management cannot be seen directly. Its presence is felt through order, discipline, results, employee morale, and goal achievement.

# Unit 1D: Objectives of Management

Management objectives can be grouped into organisational, social, and personal objectives.

## Organisational Objectives

| Objective | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Survival | Business must earn enough revenue to cover costs and continue. |
| Profit | Business must earn surplus for reward, risk, and growth. |
| Growth | Business should expand sales, production, market share, or capability. |

## Social Objectives

Management must consider society. Social objectives include:

1. Providing quality goods and services.
2. Creating employment.
3. Using environmentally responsible methods.
4. Paying fair wages.
5. Following laws.
6. Contributing to community welfare.

## Personal Objectives

Employees also have goals. Management should help satisfy:

1. Fair pay.
2. Safe working conditions.
3. Recognition.
4. Career growth.
5. Respect and belonging.

## Balancing Objectives

Good management balances organisational survival, social responsibility, and employee satisfaction. Ignoring any one of them creates long-term problems.

# Unit 1E: Importance of Management

## 1. Management Helps Achieve Group Goals

Management gives common direction to individual efforts.

## 2. Management Increases Efficiency

It reduces waste of time, money, material, and effort.

## 3. Management Creates Dynamic Organisation

It helps organisations adapt to change.

## 4. Management Helps Achieve Personal Objectives

Employees work better when their needs and goals are recognised.

## 5. Management Helps Development of Society

Efficient organisations create goods, services, employment, innovation, and social value.

# Unit 1F: Nature of Management as Art

Management is an art because it involves practical application of knowledge and personal skill.

## Features of Art in Management

| Feature of Art | Management Connection |
|---|---|
| Existence of theoretical knowledge | Management has principles and theories. |
| Personalised application | Managers apply knowledge differently. |
| Based on practice and creativity | Better management improves with experience and judgement. |

## Example

Two managers may use the same principle of motivation, but one may use recognition, another may use career growth. The application depends on people and situation.

# Unit 1G: Nature of Management as Science

Management is a science because it has a systematic body of knowledge based on observation and analysis.

## Features of Science in Management

| Feature of Science | Management Connection |
|---|---|
| Systematised knowledge | Management has organised concepts and principles. |
| Principles based on observation | Many principles arise from research and experience. |
| Cause and effect relationship | Some relationships can be predicted, such as motivation improving performance. |
| Universal but flexible principles | Principles apply widely but need situational adjustment. |

## Management Is Not an Exact Science

Management deals with human beings. Human behaviour is complex, so management principles are not as exact as laws of physics or chemistry.

# Unit 1H: Nature of Management as Profession

Management has some features of a profession, but not all features are fully compulsory.

## Professional Features

| Feature | Management Position |
|---|---|
| Well-defined body of knowledge | Management has theory, principles, and specialised education. |
| Restricted entry | Entry is not fully restricted; many managers may not have formal management degrees. |
| Professional association | Management associations exist. |
| Ethical code | Ethical conduct is expected. |
| Service motive | Managers should serve organisation and society, not only personal gain. |

## Conclusion

Management is moving toward professionalisation, but it is not a fully restricted profession like medicine or law.

# Unit 1I: Levels of Management

## Top Management

Top management includes chairperson, managing director, chief executive officer, board members, and senior executives.

Main functions:

1. Set overall goals.
2. Frame policies.
3. Make long-term plans.
4. Coordinate departments.
5. Represent organisation externally.
6. Take major decisions.

## Middle Management

Middle management includes departmental heads and divisional managers.

Main functions:

1. Implement plans of top management.
2. Interpret policies.
3. Assign work to lower levels.
4. Motivate and guide supervisors.
5. Coordinate within departments.
6. Report performance to top management.

## Supervisory or Operational Management

This level includes supervisors, foremen, section officers, and team leaders.

Main functions:

1. Direct workers.
2. Maintain quality and discipline.
3. Arrange materials and tools.
4. Solve day-to-day problems.
5. Report worker performance.
6. Ensure work is completed on time.

# Unit 1J: Functions of Management

## Planning

Planning means deciding in advance what is to be done, how it is to be done, when it is to be done, and who will do it.

## Organising

Organising means arranging tasks, resources, authority, and responsibility so plans can be implemented.

## Staffing

Staffing means recruiting, selecting, training, developing, and retaining suitable people.

## Directing

Directing means instructing, guiding, motivating, leading, supervising, and communicating with people.

## Controlling

Controlling means setting standards, measuring actual performance, comparing both, and taking corrective action.

## Function Flow

| Function | Output |
|---|---|
| Planning | Goals and action path. |
| Organising | Structure and roles. |
| Staffing | Right people in roles. |
| Directing | People take action. |
| Controlling | Performance stays on track. |

# Unit 1K: Coordination - Essence of Management

Coordination is the process of integrating individual and group efforts toward common goals.

It is called the essence of management because every management function requires coordination.

## Features of Coordination

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Integrates group effort | Brings different activities together. |
| Ensures unity of action | Everyone moves toward common goal. |
| Continuous | Needed at every stage. |
| All-pervasive | Needed at all levels and departments. |
| Responsibility of all managers | Not limited to one department. |
| Deliberate function | Managers must consciously coordinate. |

## Why Coordination Is Needed

1. Growth in size of organisation.
2. Functional differentiation.
3. Specialisation.
4. Interdependence of tasks.
5. Need to avoid duplication and conflict.

# Unit 1L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Management means giving orders only | It includes planning, organising, staffing, directing, controlling, and coordination. |
| Effectiveness and efficiency are same | Effectiveness is goal achievement; efficiency is minimum resource use. |
| Management is needed only in business | It is needed in all organisations. |
| Coordination is a separate one-time function | It is continuous and present in all functions. |
| Top managers do only controlling | Top managers mainly set goals, policies, and direction. |

## Case Analysis Toolkit

Business Studies case questions usually do not ask for direct definitions. They describe a manager's action and expect you to identify the concept.

| Case Signal | Concept to Identify | Explanation to Add |
|---|---|---|
| Target achieved but cost was very high | Effective but not efficient | Goal was achieved, but resources were not used economically. |
| Cost was low but target was missed | Efficient but not effective | Resources were saved, but desired result was not achieved. |
| Different departments work toward one common goal | Coordination | Efforts are integrated to avoid conflict and duplication. |
| Senior managers decide company policies | Top management | They set overall direction and long-term objectives. |
| Department heads convert policies into action | Middle management | They link top plans with operating work. |
| Supervisors guide workers daily | Supervisory management | They handle direct execution and worker performance. |
| Manager plans, hires, motivates, and checks results | Management process | Shows interrelated functions of management. |

## Effectiveness and Efficiency Matrix

| Situation | Effectiveness | Efficiency | Managerial Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goal achieved at planned cost | High | High | Strong management. |
| Goal achieved with waste | High | Low | Needs cost control and process improvement. |
| Goal missed despite low cost | Low | High | Needs better goal focus and execution. |
| Goal missed with high cost | Low | Low | Poor management; urgent corrective action needed. |

## Coordination Across Management Functions

Coordination is not a separate corner of management. It appears inside every function.

| Function | How Coordination Appears |
|---|---|
| Planning | Plans of departments must support common organisational goals. |
| Organising | Duties and authority must fit together without duplication. |
| Staffing | People must be placed where their skills match departmental needs. |
| Directing | Instructions, motivation, and leadership must align individual effort. |
| Controlling | Deviations in one department must be corrected in relation to other departments. |

## Answer Writing Framework

For a 3 to 4 mark answer:

1. Name the concept.
2. Define it in one clear sentence.
3. Explain two relevant points from the case.
4. Add the business effect, such as better coordination, efficiency, morale, or goal achievement.

For a 5 to 6 mark answer:

1. Start with the concept and definition.
2. Explain features or importance in separate points.
3. Link each point to the case.
4. End with why the concept improves organisational performance.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A production manager reaches the monthly output target by using extra shifts, emergency purchases, and overtime labour. The output is completed, but cost rises sharply.

Answer logic:

1. The manager is effective because the target was achieved.
2. The manager is not efficient because resources were used at a higher cost.
3. Management requires both effectiveness and efficiency.
4. Corrective steps may include better scheduling, reliable power backup, improved inventory planning, and cost monitoring.

## Final Revision

Management is a purposeful, continuous, group-based process. Its quality is seen in whether goals are achieved, resources are used wisely, people are developed, society is respected, and all departments work in coordination.
