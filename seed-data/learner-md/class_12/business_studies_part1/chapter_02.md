# Unit 2A: Meaning of Principles of Management

Principles of management are broad and general guidelines for managerial decision-making and behaviour.

They are not rigid formulas. They guide managers, but managers must adapt them to situation.

## Why Principles Are Needed

Managers face complex problems involving people, resources, time, and uncertainty. Principles help them avoid random decisions and use tested guidance.

# Unit 2B: Nature of Management Principles

## 1. Universal Application

Management principles can be applied in different organisations, but the degree of application may differ.

## 2. General Guidelines

They guide thinking and action. They do not provide exact ready-made solutions.

## 3. Formed by Practice and Experimentation

Principles arise from managerial experience, observation, and analysis.

## 4. Flexible

They can be modified according to situation.

## 5. Mainly Behavioural

They deal with human behaviour, so results may vary.

## 6. Cause and Effect Relationship

They indicate likely consequences. For example, fair remuneration usually improves employee satisfaction.

## 7. Contingent

Application depends on conditions such as size, culture, technology, and people.

# Unit 2D: Fayol's Principles of Management

Henry Fayol developed fourteen principles based on his management experience.

## 1. Division of Work

Work should be divided into specialised tasks. Specialisation increases efficiency and skill.

Example: In a garment factory, cutting, stitching, finishing, and packing may be handled by specialised workers.

## 2. Authority and Responsibility

Authority is the right to give orders. Responsibility is the obligation to perform work.

There should be balance between authority and responsibility. Giving responsibility without authority creates frustration. Giving authority without responsibility creates misuse.

## 3. Discipline

Discipline means obedience to rules and agreements.

It requires:

1. Good supervision.
2. Clear rules.
3. Fair penalties.
4. Respectful conduct.

## 4. Unity of Command

One employee should receive orders from one superior only.

If an employee receives orders from two bosses, confusion, conflict, and divided loyalty may result.

## 5. Unity of Direction

Activities with same objective should have one head and one plan.

Example: All marketing campaigns for a product should follow one coordinated plan.

## 6. Subordination of Individual Interest to General Interest

Organisational interest should take priority over personal interest.

Managers must ensure that personal goals do not damage organisational goals.

## 7. Remuneration of Employees

Pay should be fair to both employees and organisation.

Fair remuneration improves motivation, loyalty, and productivity.

## 8. Centralisation and Decentralisation

Centralisation means concentration of decision-making authority at top level. Decentralisation means sharing authority with lower levels.

The right balance depends on organisation size, manager capability, and situation.

## 9. Scalar Chain

Scalar chain is the formal line of authority from top to bottom.

Communication usually follows this chain. However, a gang plank may be used for urgent communication between employees at same level with proper permission.

## 10. Order

There should be a place for everything and everyone.

Material order means proper arrangement of goods and tools. Social order means right person in right job.

## 11. Equity

Managers should treat employees with fairness, kindness, and justice.

Equity does not mean absence of discipline. It means discipline should be fair and unbiased.

## 12. Stability of Personnel

Employee turnover should be minimised. Stable employees gain experience and contribute better.

Frequent transfers or exits reduce efficiency.

## 13. Initiative

Employees should be encouraged to suggest and execute plans within limits of authority.

Initiative improves involvement and creativity.

## 14. Esprit de Corps

Esprit de corps means team spirit and unity.

Managers should promote harmony, cooperation, and belonging.

# Unit 2E: Taylor's Scientific Management

F.W. Taylor focused on improving efficiency through scientific study of work.

Scientific management means knowing exactly what is to be done and ensuring it is done in the best and least costly way.

## Taylor's Main Concern

Taylor wanted to replace guesswork with scientific analysis.

# Unit 2F: Principles of Scientific Management

## 1. Science, Not Rule of Thumb

Work methods should be based on scientific study, not trial and error.

## 2. Harmony, Not Discord

Management and workers should cooperate. Conflict reduces productivity.

## 3. Cooperation, Not Individualism

Managers and workers should work as partners. Management should share gains and workers should support improvement.

## 4. Development of Each Person to Greatest Efficiency

Workers should be scientifically selected, trained, and developed.

# Unit 2G: Techniques of Scientific Management

## Functional Foremanship

Taylor suggested dividing supervision among specialists.

| Role | Function |
|---|---|

| Instruction card clerk | Prepares instructions. |

## Production In-Charge Roles

| Role | Function |
|---|---|
| Speed boss | Ensures timely work speed. |
| Gang boss | Arranges machines, tools, and materials. |
| Repair boss | Maintains machines. |

## Standardisation and Simplification

Standardisation sets uniform standards for:

1. Materials.
2. Tools.
3. Methods.
4. Working conditions.
5. Product quality.

Simplification eliminates unnecessary variety and reduces cost.

## Method Study

Method study finds the best way of doing a task.

Goal: reduce cost and improve efficiency.

## Motion Study

Motion study analyses movements of workers and machines to remove unnecessary motions.

Goal: save time and reduce fatigue.

## Time Study

Time study determines standard time required to perform a task.

Uses:

1. Set labour standards.
2. Plan production.
3. Decide fair work target.

## Fatigue Study

Fatigue study determines rest intervals needed to maintain efficiency.

## Differential Piece Wage System

Efficient workers are paid at a higher rate per unit. Inefficient workers receive lower rate.

Purpose:

1. Reward efficiency.
2. Motivate workers.
3. Increase output.

# Unit 2H: Fayol vs Taylor

| Basis | Fayol | Taylor |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | General administration. | Shop-floor efficiency. |
| Level | Top and middle management. | Supervisory and worker level. |
| Perspective | Manager's perspective. | Engineer's perspective. |
| Main contribution | Fourteen principles. | Scientific management techniques. |
| Scope | Wider. | More specific. |
| Aim | Improve overall management. | Improve productivity and work methods. |

# Unit 2I: Case Identification Guide

| Case Clue | Likely Principle |
|---|---|
| Employee gets orders from two bosses | Unity of command violated. |
| Similar activities have different plans | Unity of direction violated. |
| Worker has duty but no authority | Authority-responsibility imbalance. |
| Employee suggestions are ignored | Initiative not encouraged. |
| Frequent employee turnover | Stability of personnel violated. |
| Workers and management are in conflict | Harmony or cooperation principle violated. |
| Work method based on guesswork | Science, not rule of thumb violated. |
| Unnecessary worker movements | Motion study needed. |

# Unit 2J: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Principles are rigid rules | They are flexible guidelines. |
| Unity of command and unity of direction are same | Command concerns one boss; direction concerns one plan for same objective. |
| Authority and responsibility can be separated fully | They should be balanced. |
| Fayol and Taylor gave identical ideas | Fayol focused on administration; Taylor focused on scientific work methods. |
| Differential piece wage means equal wages | It pays higher rate to efficient workers. |
| Scalar chain can never be bypassed | Gang plank may be used for urgent communication with permission. |

## Principle Diagnosis Toolkit

Use this table when a case asks you to identify a principle.

| Case Detail | Principle or Technique | Why |
|---|---|---|
| One employee reports to two bosses | Unity of command | Orders from multiple superiors create confusion. |
| Two departments run separate plans for the same product launch | Unity of direction | Same objective should have one head and one plan. |
| Manager gives targets but no authority | Authority and responsibility | Responsibility must be matched with authority. |
| Employees are punished differently for same error | Equity | Fair and just treatment is missing. |
| Workers leave frequently | Stability of personnel | High turnover reduces efficiency. |
| Management ignores worker suggestions | Initiative | Employees should be encouraged to contribute ideas. |
| Departments compete instead of cooperate | Esprit de corps | Team spirit is weak. |
| Work is done by old habit without analysis | Science, not rule of thumb | Scientific methods should replace guesswork. |
| Worker movements are studied and reduced | Motion study | Unnecessary motions are eliminated. |
| Standard time for a task is calculated | Time study | Time standard is being fixed. |
| Rest pauses are fixed scientifically | Fatigue study | Fatigue is studied to maintain efficiency. |

## Deep Distinctions Learners Often Miss

| Pair | Difference |
|---|---|
| Unity of command vs unity of direction | Command concerns one subordinate and one boss; direction concerns one plan for one objective. |
| Discipline vs equity | Discipline focuses on obedience to rules; equity focuses on fairness in treatment. |
| Centralisation vs decentralisation | Centralisation keeps authority at top; decentralisation disperses authority downward. |
| Scalar chain vs gang plank | Scalar chain is formal authority line; gang plank is permitted shortcut for urgent communication. |
| Method study vs motion study | Method study finds best way to do work; motion study removes wasteful body and machine movements. |
| Time study vs fatigue study | Time study fixes standard time; fatigue study fixes rest intervals. |

## Taylor Technique Selection Guide

| Managerial Problem | Best Technique |
|---|---|
| Workers use different tools and output quality varies | Standardisation. |
| Too many varieties increase cost | Simplification. |
| No one knows fair daily target | Time study. |
| Workers waste effort in bending, lifting, or searching | Motion study. |
| Workers slow down after long hours | Fatigue study. |
| Efficient and inefficient workers receive same reward | Differential piece wage system. |
| Supervisor is overloaded with many specialised duties | Functional foremanship. |

## Case Answer Framework

When answering a principle-based case:

1. Identify the principle or technique.
2. Quote the case clue briefly in your own words.
3. Explain the principle.
4. State the likely effect of following or violating it.
5. If asked, suggest correction.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A worker in production receives an instruction from the production manager to complete a batch by evening. At the same time, the quality manager orders him to stop work and change the method. The worker becomes confused and output is delayed.

Answer:

1. The principle violated is unity of command.
2. According to this principle, one employee should receive orders from one superior only.
3. Multiple orders create confusion, conflict, and delay.
4. The correction is to define authority clearly and route quality instructions through the proper reporting relationship, or create an agreed procedure for quality intervention.

## Final Revision

Principles of management are practical guides. Fayol tells us how to administer organisations; Taylor tells us how to improve work scientifically. In case questions, identify the clue, connect it to a principle, and explain the effect on efficiency, discipline, or coordination.
