# Unit 4A: Meaning of Planning

Planning is deciding in advance what is to be done and how it is to be done. It bridges the gap between where the organisation is and where it wants to go.

Planning involves:

1. Setting objectives.
2. Choosing a course of action.
3. Estimating future conditions.
4. Allocating resources.
5. Deciding standards for performance.

# Unit 4B: Features of Planning

## 1. Planning Focuses on Objectives

Planning begins with goals. If there is no objective, planning has no direction.

## 2. Planning Is Primary Function

Planning comes before organising, staffing, directing, and controlling. Other functions depend on the plan.

## 3. Planning Is Pervasive

Planning is done at all levels. Top managers plan for the whole organisation, while supervisors plan daily work.

## 4. Planning Is Continuous

Plans are made, implemented, reviewed, and revised. New situations require new plans.

## 5. Planning Is Futuristic

Planning deals with future action. It requires forecasting and assumptions.

## 6. Planning Involves Decision-Making

Planning means choosing the best alternative among available options.

## 7. Planning Is a Mental Exercise

Planning requires thinking, analysis, imagination, judgement, and foresight.

# Unit 4C: Importance of Planning

## 1. Provides Direction

Planning tells everyone what the organisation wants to achieve.

## 2. Reduces Risk of Uncertainty

Planning cannot remove uncertainty, but it helps managers prepare for likely changes.

## 3. Reduces Overlapping and Wasteful Activities

Clear plans prevent duplication and confusion.

## 4. Promotes Innovative Ideas

Planning requires managers to think of better ways to work.

## 5. Facilitates Decision-Making

Plans provide criteria for choosing among alternatives.

## 6. Establishes Standards for Controlling

Control compares actual performance with planned standards. Without planning, control has no base.

# Unit 4D: Limitations of Planning

## 1. Planning May Create Rigidity

Employees may follow plans blindly even when conditions change.

## 2. Planning May Not Work in Dynamic Environment

Rapid changes in technology, competition, policy, or customer taste can make plans outdated.

## 3. Planning Reduces Creativity if Misused

Strict plans may discourage initiative if managers do not allow flexibility.

## 4. Planning Is Costly

Planning needs time, experts, research, and information.

## 5. Planning Is Time-Consuming

Detailed planning may delay action in urgent situations.

## 6. Planning Does Not Guarantee Success

A plan is only a guide. Poor implementation or unexpected events can still cause failure.

## 7. Planning May Create False Sense of Security

Managers may assume plans will work automatically and stop monitoring the environment.

# Unit 4E: Planning Process

## Step 1: Setting Objectives

The organisation must decide what it wants to achieve.

Example: Increase sales by 15 per cent in one year.

## Step 2: Developing Premises

Premises are assumptions about future conditions such as demand, competition, prices, and government policy.

## Step 3: Identifying Alternative Courses of Action

Managers list possible ways to achieve objectives.

## Step 4: Evaluating Alternatives

Each alternative is judged for cost, benefit, risk, feasibility, and time.

## Step 5: Selecting the Best Alternative

The most suitable plan is chosen. Sometimes a combination of alternatives is selected.

## Step 6: Implementing the Plan

The plan is put into action through organising, staffing, directing, and resource allocation.

## Step 7: Follow-Up

Progress is monitored. If needed, corrective action is taken.

# Unit 4F: Types of Plans

Plans may be single-use or standing.

## Single-Use Plans

Single-use plans are made for one-time situations.

Examples:

1. Programme for opening a new branch.
2. Budget for a one-time campaign.

## Standing Plans

Standing plans are used repeatedly for recurring situations.

Examples:

1. Policy.
2. Procedure.
3. Method.
4. Rule.

# Unit 4G: Objectives

Objectives are desired results.

## Features of Good Objectives

1. Clear.
2. Measurable.
3. Time-bound.
4. Realistic.
5. Linked to organisational purpose.

Example: Increase market share from 10 per cent to 12 per cent within twelve months.

# Unit 4H: Strategy

Strategy is a comprehensive plan for achieving major objectives under competitive conditions.

It considers:

1. Long-term goals.
2. Resources.
3. Competitors.
4. Market conditions.
5. Environmental opportunities and threats.

Example: A company may use low-cost strategy, premium quality strategy, or digital expansion strategy.

# Unit 4I: Policy

Policy is a general guideline for decision-making.

Example: A company may have a policy to replace defective products within seven days.

Policies allow discretion. They guide decisions but do not give every step.

# Unit 4J: Procedure

Procedure is a sequence of steps for completing work.

Example: Recruitment procedure may include application screening, test, interview, reference check, and appointment.

Procedures reduce confusion and ensure consistency.

# Unit 4K: Method

Method is the prescribed way of doing a specific step.

Example: A company may prescribe a particular method for packaging fragile items.

Procedure gives the sequence. Method explains how a step is performed.

# Unit 4L: Rule

Rule is a specific statement that tells what must or must not be done.

Example: "No smoking inside factory premises."

Rules allow no discretion. They must be followed exactly.

# Unit 4M: Programme

Programme is a detailed plan including objectives, policies, procedures, rules, tasks, resources, and time schedule.

Example: Employee training programme for new sales staff.

# Unit 4N: Budget

Budget is a quantified plan.

Examples:

1. Sales budget.
2. Cash budget.
3. Production budget.
4. Advertising budget.

Budgets help planning and controlling because they provide numerical standards.

# Unit 4O: Plan Type Comparison

| Type | Nature | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | End result | Increase sales by 10 per cent. |
| Strategy | Broad competitive plan | Enter online market. |
| Policy | Decision guideline | Credit allowed only to approved customers. |
| Procedure | Step sequence | Steps for handling customer complaint. |
| Method | Way to perform step | Use barcode scanning for inventory. |
| Rule | Must or must not do | Safety helmet compulsory. |
| Programme | Detailed action package | Training programme. |
| Budget | Numerical plan | Monthly sales budget. |

# Unit 4P: Planning and Controlling

Planning and controlling are closely linked.

Planning sets standards. Controlling measures whether standards are achieved.

Without planning, there is no standard for control. Without control, planning cannot be checked and improved.

# Unit 4Q: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Planning removes all risk | It reduces risk but cannot eliminate uncertainty. |
| Planning is only for top management | It is done at every level. |
| Rule and policy are same | Policy allows discretion; rule does not. |
| Procedure and method are same | Procedure gives sequence; method gives way of doing a step. |
| Budget is only financial | It can be financial or quantitative, such as units, hours, or output. |
| Planning guarantees success | Implementation and changing conditions also matter. |

## Plan Identification Toolkit

| Case Clue | Type of Plan | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Increase sales by 20 per cent in one year" | Objective | It states measurable result. |
| "Enter rural markets through low-price packs" | Strategy | It is a broad competitive action path. |
| "Credit will be given only to regular customers" | Policy | It guides decisions with discretion. |
| "Complaint must move from help desk to service head to refund team" | Procedure | It gives sequence of steps. |

## Planning Quality Checklist

A high-quality plan should be:

1. Linked to clear objectives.
2. Based on realistic premises.
3. Flexible enough to handle change.
4. Supported by resources.
5. Communicated to those who implement it.
6. Measurable through standards.
7. Time-bound.
8. Consistent with other departmental plans.

## Premises in Detail

Premises are assumptions about the future. They are not guesses made casually; they are reasoned expectations.

| Premise Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Internal premise | Availability of skilled workers, machine capacity, cash position. |
| External premise | Demand, inflation, competition, law, technology, supplier reliability. |
| Controllable premise | Company policy, internal budget, work schedule. |
| Non-controllable premise | Government policy, natural events, competitor action. |

## Why Planning Can Fail Despite Good Intentions

| Failure Reason | Learner Explanation |
|---|---|
| Weak premises | Plan was based on wrong assumptions. |
| Poor communication | People did not understand what to do. |
| No flexibility | Plan could not adjust to new conditions. |
| Lack of resources | Plan was unrealistic for available funds or people. |
| No follow-up | Deviations were not identified in time. |

## High-Quality Example

Case: A company fixes a goal to increase online sales by 30 per cent. It assumes internet demand will keep rising, compares website upgrade, app launch, and marketplace selling, then chooses app launch with a monthly digital advertising budget.

Answer:

1. The case shows planning process.
2. The goal is the objective.
3. Rising internet demand is a premise.
4. Website upgrade, app launch, and marketplace selling are alternatives.
5. App launch is the selected alternative.
6. Digital advertising budget is a quantified plan supporting implementation.

## Final Revision

Planning is the thinking function of management. It gives direction, prepares the organisation for future action, and creates standards for control. The chapter becomes easy when you can identify each type of plan from a case clue.
