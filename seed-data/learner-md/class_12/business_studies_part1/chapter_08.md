# Unit 8A: Meaning of Controlling

Controlling is the process of comparing actual performance with planned standards and taking corrective action when needed.

Control does not mean only checking mistakes. It means ensuring that work moves toward organisational goals.

## Example

If a sales target is Rs. 10,00,000 but actual sales are Rs. 8,50,000, controlling requires identifying the gap, analysing reasons, and taking corrective action.

# Unit 8B: Nature of Controlling

## 1. Goal-Oriented

Control exists to achieve planned goals.

## 2. Pervasive

Control is needed at all levels and in all departments.

## 3. Continuous

Performance must be monitored regularly.

## 4. Both Backward-Looking and Forward-Looking

It looks backward by comparing actual performance with past standards. It is forward-looking because corrective action improves future performance.

## 5. Action-Oriented

Control is useful only when it leads to corrective action.

# Unit 8C: Importance of Controlling

## 1. Accomplishing Organisational Goals

Controlling keeps activities aligned with objectives.

## 2. Judging Accuracy of Standards

Control reveals whether standards are realistic.

## 3. Making Efficient Use of Resources

It reduces waste and misuse of resources.

## 4. Improving Employee Motivation

Clear standards and fair evaluation guide employees.

## 5. Ensuring Order and Discipline

Employees know that performance is monitored.

## 6. Facilitating Coordination

Control integrates efforts across departments by focusing on common standards.

# Unit 8D: Relationship Between Planning and Controlling

Planning and controlling are inseparable.

## Planning Without Controlling

Plans remain statements of intention if performance is not checked.

## Controlling Without Planning

Control is impossible without standards. Planning provides those standards.

## Relationship Table

| Planning | Controlling |
|---|---|
| Sets objectives and standards. | Measures achievement of standards. |
| Looks ahead. | Checks results and improves future action. |
| First function. | Follows implementation. |
| Decides what should happen. | Checks what actually happened. |

## Key Line

Planning is prescriptive; controlling is evaluative.

# Unit 8E: Controlling Process

## Step 1: Setting Performance Standards

Standards are benchmarks for performance.

They may be:

1. Quantitative, such as units, cost, time, profit, or sales.
2. Qualitative, such as customer satisfaction or employee morale.

## Step 2: Measuring Actual Performance

Actual performance is measured through reports, observation, accounting data, surveys, or digital systems.

## Step 3: Comparing Actual Performance With Standards

Actual results are compared with standards to find deviations.

Deviation = Actual performance - Standard performance

The meaning of a deviation depends on context. Higher cost than standard is unfavourable. Higher sales than standard is favourable.

## Step 4: Analysing Deviations

Managers identify causes of deviations.

Possible causes:

1. Unrealistic standards.
2. Poor training.
3. Lack of resources.
4. Machine breakdown.
5. Weak supervision.
6. Market change.
7. Employee absenteeism.

## Step 5: Taking Corrective Action

Corrective action depends on cause.

| Cause | Possible Corrective Action |
|---|---|
| Poor training | Arrange training. |
| Machine breakdown | Repair or replace machine. |
| Unrealistic standard | Revise standard. |
| Low motivation | Improve incentives and leadership. |
| Poor material quality | Change supplier or inspect input. |
| Weak sales | Improve promotion, pricing, or sales effort. |

# Unit 8F: Critical Point Control

Managers cannot control every small detail with equal attention. Critical point control means focusing on key areas that have major impact on success.

Examples:

1. Cash flow in a small business.
2. Defect rate in manufacturing.
3. Delivery time in e-commerce.
4. Customer complaints in service business.

# Unit 8G: Management by Exception

Management by exception means managers should focus attention on significant deviations and ignore minor variations.

## Benefits

1. Saves managerial time.
2. Focuses on major problems.
3. Improves efficiency.
4. Allows lower-level managers to handle routine matters.

## Example

If cost exceeds standard by 1 per cent, supervisor may handle it. If cost exceeds standard by 15 per cent, senior manager should investigate.

# Unit 8H: Techniques of Managerial Control

## Budgetary Control

Budgetary control uses budgets as standards and compares actual results with budgeted results.

Examples:

1. Sales budget.
2. Production budget.
3. Cash budget.
4. Expense budget.

## Ratio Analysis

Ratios help control liquidity, profitability, efficiency, and solvency.

## Responsibility Accounting

Performance is evaluated according to areas of responsibility.

Types of responsibility centres:

1. Cost centre.
2. Revenue centre.
3. Profit centre.
4. Investment centre.

## Management Audit

Management audit evaluates overall quality and effectiveness of management.

## Network Techniques

Network techniques help plan and control complex tasks by showing sequence and timing of activities.

# Unit 8I: Corrective Action Guide

| Deviation | Possible Question to Ask |
|---|---|
| Low sales | Is demand low, price high, product weak, or sales effort poor? |
| High cost | Are materials costly, wastage high, or labour inefficient? |
| Late delivery | Is scheduling poor or supplier unreliable? |
| Poor quality | Is training weak, machine faulty, or input defective? |
| High absenteeism | Are working conditions, morale, or supervision poor? |

# Unit 8J: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Controlling means only finding faults | It aims to improve performance through corrective action. |
| Planning and controlling are unrelated | Planning sets standards; controlling checks them. |
| Every deviation needs top management attention | Only significant deviations need higher-level attention. |
| Standards must always be quantitative | Some standards are qualitative. |
| Corrective action is same for every deviation | Action depends on cause. |
| Control is only backward-looking | It also improves future action. |

## Control Diagnosis Toolkit

| Case Detail | Control Step |
|---|---|
| Target sales are fixed at Rs. 50,00,000 | Setting standards. |
| Actual sales report shows Rs. 42,00,000 | Measuring performance. |
| Sales shortfall is Rs. 8,00,000 | Comparing actual with standard. |
| Shortfall is due to weak distribution | Analysing deviation. |
| Company appoints new distributors | Corrective action. |

## Deviation Interpretation Table

| Standard | Actual | Deviation Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Cost Rs. 10 per unit | Cost Rs. 12 per unit | Unfavourable cost deviation. |
| Sales Rs. 5,00,000 | Sales Rs. 6,00,000 | Favourable sales deviation. |
| Defect rate 2 per cent | Defect rate 6 per cent | Unfavourable quality deviation. |
| Delivery time 2 days | Delivery time 5 days | Unfavourable service deviation. |
| Customer rating 4.5 | Customer rating 4.1 | Possible qualitative concern. |

## Choosing Corrective Action

| Root Cause | Weak Correction | Strong Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Workers lack skill | Scold workers | Provide training and supervision. |
| Machine breaks often | Push workers harder | Repair, maintain, or replace machine. |
| Raw material is defective | Increase inspection only | Improve supplier quality and incoming checks. |
| Standard is unrealistic | Blame employees | Revise standard after analysis. |
| Sales territory is too large | Demand more visits | Redesign territories or add sales staff. |

## Management by Exception in Practice

Managers should define tolerance limits.

Example:

1. Sales deviation up to 2 per cent may be handled by sales supervisor.
2. Deviation between 2 and 8 per cent may go to regional manager.
3. Deviation above 8 per cent may require top management review.

This saves time and ensures major issues receive attention.

## Case Answer Framework

1. Identify the control step or principle.
2. Define it.
3. Mention standard and actual performance if given.
4. Calculate or describe deviation.
5. Analyse likely cause.
6. Recommend corrective action.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A company sets a standard defect rate of 1 per cent. Actual defect rate becomes 5 per cent. Investigation shows new workers were placed directly on machines without training.

Answer:

1. The controlling process shows comparison, deviation analysis, and corrective action.
2. Standard defect rate was 1 per cent; actual was 5 per cent.
3. The deviation is unfavourable and significant.
4. Cause is lack of training.
5. Corrective action should include training, closer supervision, and quality checks for new workers.

## Final Revision

Controlling closes the management loop. Planning decides standards; directing and organising create action; controlling checks results and corrects deviations. In case answers, always mention standard, actual performance, deviation, cause, and corrective action.
