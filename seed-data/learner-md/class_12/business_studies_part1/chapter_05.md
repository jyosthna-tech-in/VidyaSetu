# Unit 5A: Meaning of Organising

Organising is the process of identifying and grouping work, defining and delegating authority, and establishing relationships so people can work together effectively.

Planning decides what is to be done. Organising decides how the work will be divided and coordinated.

## Simple Example

If a school holds an annual function, work must be divided into stage arrangement, invitations, seating, rehearsals, finance, discipline, and hospitality. Each task needs people, authority, responsibility, and coordination. This is organising.

# Unit 5B: Importance of Organising

## 1. Benefits of Specialisation

Work is divided into specific jobs. People become skilled in their tasks.

## 2. Clarity in Working Relationships

Organising defines who reports to whom and who is responsible for what.

## 3. Optimum Use of Resources

Resources are allocated properly and duplication is reduced.

## 4. Adaptation to Change

A clear structure helps the organisation adjust when work expands or changes.

## 5. Effective Administration

Authority and responsibility become clear, making control easier.

## 6. Development of Personnel

Delegation gives employees chance to learn and grow.

## 7. Expansion and Growth

Good structure supports opening new branches, products, and departments.

# Unit 5C: Organising Process

## Step 1: Identification and Division of Work

Total work is identified and divided into smaller tasks.

Example: In a manufacturing firm, work may include purchasing, production, quality checking, sales, finance, and customer support.

## Step 2: Departmentalisation

Similar tasks are grouped together.

Example: All finance-related work may be grouped under finance department.

## Step 3: Assignment of Duties

Work is assigned to people according to skill, ability, and job position.

## Step 4: Establishing Reporting Relationships

Authority relationships are created so employees know:

1. From whom they receive orders.
2. To whom they report.
3. Who can make which decisions.

## Process Table

| Step | Main Question |
|---|---|
| Identify work | What work must be done? |
| Group work | Which tasks belong together? |
| Assign duties | Who will do each task? |
| Define authority | Who reports to whom? |

# Unit 5D: Organisational Structure

Organisational structure is the framework within which managerial and operating tasks are performed.

It shows:

1. Job positions.
2. Departments.
3. Authority relationships.
4. Communication lines.
5. Responsibility areas.

## Span of Management

Span of management means number of subordinates directly reporting to a manager.

| Wide Span | Narrow Span |
|---|---|
| More subordinates under one manager. | Fewer subordinates under one manager. |
| Fewer levels. | More levels. |
| Suitable when work is routine and employees are trained. | Suitable when work is complex or close supervision is needed. |

# Unit 5E: Functional Structure

Functional structure groups activities according to major functions.

Common departments:

1. Production.
2. Marketing.
3. Finance.
4. Human resources.
5. Research and development.

## Advantages

1. Occupational specialisation.
2. Better control within departments.
3. Less duplication.
4. Easier training of specialists.
5. Economies of scale.

## Limitations

1. Departmental conflicts may arise.
2. Managers may focus on department goals over organisational goals.
3. Coordination may become difficult.
4. Responsibility for final product may be unclear.

## Suitable When

Functional structure is suitable when:

1. Organisation produces limited products.
2. Operations are stable.
3. Functional specialisation is important.
4. Scale is large enough for separate departments.

# Unit 5F: Divisional Structure

Divisional structure groups activities by product, region, customer group, or business division.

Each division may have its own production, marketing, finance, and personnel functions.

## Advantages

1. Product focus.
2. Clear profit responsibility.
3. Faster decision-making within division.
4. Easier performance measurement.
5. Supports growth and diversification.

## Limitations

1. Duplication of resources across divisions.
2. Higher cost.
3. Conflict between divisions.
4. Managers may focus on divisional goals over organisational goals.

## Suitable When

Divisional structure is suitable when:

1. Organisation has many products.
2. Product lines are diverse.
3. Each division needs separate attention.
4. Profitability of each product line must be measured.

# Unit 5G: Functional vs Divisional Structure

| Basis | Functional Structure | Divisional Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Grouping | By function. | By product, region, or division. |
| Best for | Single or limited product firms. | Multi-product firms. |
| Specialisation | Functional specialisation. | Product or market specialisation. |
| Cost | Lower duplication. | Higher duplication. |
| Responsibility | Department responsibility. | Product or division responsibility. |
| Coordination | Across functions may be difficult. | Within division easier, across divisions harder. |

# Unit 5H: Formal Organisation

Formal organisation is deliberately created by management.

## Features

1. Official authority relationships.
2. Clear rules and procedures.
3. Defined roles.
4. Focus on work.
5. Stable structure.

## Advantages

1. Clarity of authority.
2. Avoids duplication.
3. Easier coordination.
4. Helps achieve organisational goals.

## Limitations

1. May be rigid.
2. May ignore human relationships.
3. Slow communication through official channels.

# Unit 5I: Informal Organisation

Informal organisation arises naturally from social interaction among employees.

## Features

1. Develops spontaneously.
2. Based on friendship, trust, and common interest.
3. Has no fixed structure.
4. Communication is fast.
5. Can influence attitudes and behaviour.

## Advantages

1. Fast communication.
2. Emotional support.
3. Better cooperation.
4. Helps managers understand employee feelings.

## Limitations

1. May spread rumours.
2. May resist change.
3. May create group pressure.
4. May conflict with formal goals.

# Unit 5J: Delegation

Delegation means transfer of authority from superior to subordinate for completing assigned work.

Delegation does not mean superior escapes responsibility. The superior remains accountable for final result.

## Elements of Delegation

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Authority | Right to command and make decisions. |
| Responsibility | Duty to perform assigned work. |
| Accountability | Answerability for outcome. |

## Flow

Authority flows downward. Responsibility flows upward because subordinate is responsible to superior. Accountability cannot be delegated completely.

# Unit 5K: Importance of Delegation

## 1. Effective Management

Managers can focus on important work by assigning routine work.

## 2. Employee Development

Subordinates learn decision-making and responsibility.

## 3. Motivation

Authority and trust improve confidence and morale.

## 4. Growth

Delegation prepares future managers and supports expansion.

## 5. Better Coordination

Clear authority and responsibility improve workflow.

# Unit 5L: Decentralisation

Decentralisation is systematic delegation of authority throughout the organisation.

Delegation is between superior and subordinate. Decentralisation is an organisational policy of distributing decision-making power.

## Importance

1. Develops initiative among subordinates.
2. Develops managerial talent.
3. Quick decisions.
4. Relief to top management.
5. Facilitates growth.
6. Better control through clear responsibility.

# Unit 5M: Delegation vs Decentralisation

| Basis | Delegation | Decentralisation |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Transfer of authority from superior to subordinate. | Systematic dispersal of authority to lower levels. |
| Nature | Compulsory for management. | Optional policy choice. |
| Scope | Narrow. | Wider. |
| Purpose | Reduce manager's burden and get work done. | Increase participation and speed decisions. |
| Control | Superior retains control. | Top management retains overall control but lower levels decide more. |

# Unit 5N: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Organising is same as planning | Planning decides goals; organising arranges resources to achieve them. |
| Functional structure is always best | It suits limited product firms; divisional suits diversified firms. |
| Informal organisation is always harmful | It can help communication and morale if guided well. |
| Delegation removes superior's accountability | Final accountability remains with superior. |
| Delegation and decentralisation are same | Decentralisation is wider and systematic. |

## Structure Selection Toolkit

| Business Situation | Better Structure | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| One major product, large scale operations | Functional structure | Specialisation by production, finance, marketing, and personnel is efficient. |
| Many unrelated products | Divisional structure | Each product line needs separate focus and profit responsibility. |
| Same product sold in many regions with similar operations | Functional or regional variant, depending on scale | Choose based on whether function or geography creates more complexity. |
| Rapid product diversification | Divisional structure | Faster product-level decisions and accountability. |
| Small firm with simple operations | Simple functional grouping | Divisional structure may be too costly. |

## Delegation Quality Checklist

Delegation works well only when:

1. Duties are clear.
2. Authority is sufficient.
3. Expected results are measurable.
4. Subordinate is competent or trained.
5. Superior trusts the subordinate.
6. Reporting system is clear.
7. Accountability is understood.
8. Interference is avoided after delegation.

## Why Delegation Fails

| Failure Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Superior does not trust employees | Authority is not actually transferred. |
| Subordinate fears criticism | Initiative remains low. |
| Duties are unclear | Subordinate does not know expected result. |
| Authority is inadequate | Subordinate cannot make needed decisions. |
| No feedback system | Superior cannot monitor progress. |

## Decentralisation Depth

Decentralisation is more than occasional delegation. It is a deliberate philosophy of sharing decision-making power.

| Indicator of High Decentralisation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Branch managers decide local pricing within limits | Authority is pushed closer to market. |
| Department heads approve routine expenses | Top management is freed from minor decisions. |
| Product managers control product strategy | Divisional accountability improves. |
| Lower managers evaluate local suppliers | Decision speed increases. |

## Formal and Informal Organisation: Case Use

| Case Clue | Organisation Type |
|---|---|
| Official reporting line, job description, authority chart | Formal organisation. |
| Employees form friendship group and share information quickly | Informal organisation. |
| Rumour spreads before official notice | Informal organisation. |
| Work flows according to rules and procedures | Formal organisation. |

## Case Answer Framework

1. Identify whether the case concerns structure, formal/informal organisation, delegation, or decentralisation.
2. Define the concept.
3. Explain the case clue.
4. State one advantage or risk.
5. Suggest managerial action if the case shows a problem.

## High-Quality Example

Case: A company producing shoes, watches, and packaged foods uses one common production manager and one common marketing manager for all products. Managers complain that product decisions are slow and no one is clearly responsible for profit of each product.

Answer:

1. The company should consider divisional structure.
2. Divisional structure groups work by product line.
3. Shoes, watches, and packaged foods are diverse products needing separate attention.
4. Divisional structure gives product focus and clear profit responsibility.
5. The limitation is possible duplication of resources, so common services may still be shared where economical.

## Final Revision

Organising gives shape to plans. It divides work, groups tasks, assigns duties, and creates authority relationships. The most tested areas are functional versus divisional structure, formal versus informal organisation, and delegation versus decentralisation.
