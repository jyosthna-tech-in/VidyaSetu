## 1. Big Picture

A not-for-profit organisation is formed to provide service rather than to earn profit for owners. Examples include clubs, schools, charitable hospitals, libraries, sports associations, cultural societies, trade unions, and religious institutions.

These organisations may not sell goods like a business concern, but they still receive money, spend money, buy assets, owe liabilities, collect subscriptions, receive donations, and hold funds for specific purposes. Because of this, they need proper accounting records.

The chapter teaches how to convert cash-based records into final accounts that show:

| Question | Statement That Answers It |
| --- | --- |
| How much cash came in and went out? | Receipts and Payments Account |
| Did the organisation earn a surplus or suffer a deficit for the year? | Income and Expenditure Account |
| What is the financial position at year-end? | Balance Sheet |

The major challenge in this chapter is not memorising formats. The real skill is adjustment: deciding whether an item belongs to the current year, whether it is capital or revenue, and whether it affects cash, income, expense, asset, liability, or fund.

## 2. Nature of Not-for-Profit Organisations

### Meaning

A not-for-profit organisation is an entity whose main objective is to provide service to members or society. It does not exist to distribute profit among owners.

### Common Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Service objective | The organisation works for education, welfare, recreation, charity, health, culture, sports, or similar purposes. |
| No profit distribution | Any surplus is kept in the organisation and used for its objectives. It is not distributed like business profit. |
| Managed by trustees or committee | A managing committee, trustees, or elected members control the funds and activities. |
| Separate records are needed | Even without profit motive, accountability is necessary because funds belong to members, donors, or the public purpose. |
| Capital fund instead of capital account | There is no owner capital account. The accumulated fund is usually called Capital Fund or General Fund. |
| Main incomes are recurring and non-recurring | Subscriptions, donations, grants, entrance fees, interest, and proceeds from activities are common sources. |

## 3. Why Accounting Is Needed

Not-for-profit organisations need accounting for five practical reasons:

1. To record all receipts and payments.
2. To prevent misuse of funds.
3. To know whether regular income is enough to cover regular expenses.
4. To report to members, donors, government authorities, and regulators.
5. To show assets, liabilities, and funds at the end of the accounting year.

Learner shortcut: even when there is no profit motive, there is still accountability.

## 4. Important Accounting Terms

| Term | Meaning | Exam Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Subscription | Periodic amount paid by members, usually annually. | Treated as revenue income for the year to which it relates. |
| Donation | Gift received in cash or kind. | Treatment depends on whether it is general or specific. |
| Legacy | Amount received through a will. | Usually capitalised unless it is small and recurring. |
| Entrance fee | Amount paid by a new member at admission. | Treatment depends on instructions; may be capitalised or treated as income. |
| Life membership fee | One-time fee for lifetime membership. | Usually capital receipt; shown as a liability/fund and transferred gradually if required. |
| Capital fund | Accumulated fund of the organisation. | Similar to owner capital in a business balance sheet. |
| Specific fund | Fund created for a particular purpose. | Shown as liability until used for that purpose. |
| Surplus | Excess of income over expenditure. | Added to capital fund. |
| Deficit | Excess of expenditure over income. | Deducted from capital fund. |
| Receipts and Payments Account | Summary of cash and bank transactions. | Includes capital and revenue items, whether current-year or not. |
| Income and Expenditure Account | Nominal account showing current-year income and expenses. | Prepared on accrual basis. |

## 5. Receipts and Payments Account

### Meaning

Receipts and Payments Account is a summarised cash book. It records all cash and bank receipts on one side and all cash and bank payments on the other side.

### Key Characteristics

| Point | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Nature | Real account because it records cash and bank. |
| Basis | Cash basis. Only actual receipts and actual payments are recorded. |
| Opening balance | Usually cash in hand and cash at bank. If bank overdraft exists, it appears on the payments/credit side as opening overdraft. |
| Closing balance | Cash and bank balance at the end. If payments exceed receipts, bank overdraft may appear on the receipts/debit side as closing overdraft. |
| Capital and revenue distinction | Not made. Both are recorded. |
| Current-year distinction | Not made. Past, current, and future year amounts are all recorded if cash is received or paid during the year. |
| Non-cash items | Not recorded. Depreciation, accrued income, outstanding expenses, and provisions are excluded. |

### Format

| Receipts | Amount | Payments | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Balance b/d: Cash | xxx | Bank overdraft b/d | xxx |
| Balance b/d: Bank | xxx | Salaries | xxx |
| Subscriptions received | xxx | Rent paid | xxx |
| Donations received | xxx | Purchase of furniture | xxx |
| Sale of old asset | xxx | Sports material purchased | xxx |
| Interest received | xxx | Investments purchased | xxx |
| Bank overdraft c/d | xxx | Balance c/d: Cash | xxx |
|  |  | Balance c/d: Bank | xxx |

### How to Prepare It

1. Start with opening cash and bank balances.
2. Record every cash or bank receipt, no matter whether it is capital or revenue.
3. Record every cash or bank payment, no matter whether it is capital or revenue.
4. Do not include non-cash adjustments.
5. Balance the account to find closing cash/bank or overdraft.

### Common Mistake

Do not remove advance subscription or outstanding subscription while preparing Receipts and Payments Account. If cash was received, record it. Adjustments are made later in Income and Expenditure Account and Balance Sheet.

## 6. Income and Expenditure Account

### Meaning

Income and Expenditure Account is prepared to find the surplus or deficit of a not-for-profit organisation for a particular year.

It is similar to a Profit and Loss Account, but it uses the terms surplus and deficit instead of net profit and net loss.

### Key Characteristics

| Point | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Nature | Nominal account. |
| Basis | Accrual basis. |
| Records | Only revenue incomes and revenue expenses of the current year. |
| Excludes | Capital receipts, capital payments, and items not related to the current year. |
| Includes non-cash items | Depreciation, outstanding expenses, accrued income, prepaid expenses, and provisions are adjusted. |
| Result | Surplus or deficit. |

### Format

| Expenditure | Amount | Income | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Salaries | xxx | Subscriptions | xxx |
| Rent | xxx | General donations | xxx |
| Depreciation | xxx | Interest on investments | xxx |
| Printing and stationery consumed | xxx | Entrance fee, if treated as revenue | xxx |
| Repairs | xxx | Profit from activity | xxx |
| Deficit transferred to Capital Fund | xxx | Surplus transferred to Capital Fund | xxx |

### How to Prepare It from Receipts and Payments Account

1. Take only revenue receipts and revenue payments.
2. Exclude opening and closing cash/bank balances.
3. Exclude capital receipts and capital payments.
4. Adjust incomes for outstanding and advance amounts.
5. Adjust expenses for outstanding and prepaid amounts.
6. Add non-cash expenses such as depreciation.
7. Find surplus or deficit.

## 7. Difference Between Receipts and Payments Account and Income and Expenditure Account

| Basis | Receipts and Payments Account | Income and Expenditure Account |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nature | Real account | Nominal account |
| Basis | Cash basis | Accrual basis |
| Opening balance | Has opening cash/bank balance | Has no opening balance |
| Closing result | Shows closing cash/bank balance or overdraft | Shows surplus or deficit |
| Capital items | Included | Excluded |
| Revenue items | Included if cash is received or paid | Included only if related to current year |
| Past/future year items | Included if paid or received during the year | Excluded |
| Non-cash items | Excluded | Included |
| Purpose | Shows cash movement | Shows operating result |

## 8. Balance Sheet

### Meaning

The Balance Sheet shows the financial position of the organisation on the last day of the accounting year. It lists assets, liabilities, and funds.

### Format

| Liabilities | Amount | Assets | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Capital Fund opening balance | xxx | Cash in hand | xxx |
| Add: Surplus | xxx | Cash at bank | xxx |
| Less: Deficit | xxx | Outstanding subscriptions | xxx |
| Specific funds | xxx | Accrued income | xxx |
| Outstanding expenses | xxx | Prepaid expenses | xxx |
| Subscriptions received in advance | xxx | Furniture | xxx |
| Creditors | xxx | Building | xxx |
|  |  | Investments | xxx |

### Capital Fund

Capital Fund is found by preparing an opening Balance Sheet if it is not directly given.

Formula:

```text
Opening Capital Fund = Opening Assets - Opening Liabilities
```

At year-end:

```text
Closing Capital Fund = Opening Capital Fund + Surplus - Deficit + Capitalised Receipts
```

## 9. Adjustment of Subscription

Subscription is the most important recurring income in this chapter. The amount shown in Income and Expenditure Account must relate only to the current year.

### Formula

```text
Subscription for current year
= Subscription received during the year
+ Outstanding subscription at the end
+ Advance subscription at the beginning
- Outstanding subscription at the beginning
- Advance subscription at the end
```

### Why This Works

| Adjustment | Reason |
| --- | --- |
| Add outstanding at end | Earned this year but not yet received. |
| Add advance at beginning | Received last year but belongs to this year. |
| Less outstanding at beginning | Received this year but belonged to last year. |
| Less advance at end | Received this year but belongs to next year. |

### Balance Sheet Treatment

| Item | Balance Sheet Side |
| --- | --- |
| Outstanding subscription at end | Asset |
| Subscription received in advance at end | Liability |

## 10. Adjustment of Expenses

Expenses in Income and Expenditure Account must also relate only to the current year.

### Formula

```text
Expense for current year
= Amount paid during the year
+ Outstanding expense at the end
+ Prepaid expense at the beginning
- Outstanding expense at the beginning
- Prepaid expense at the end
```

### Balance Sheet Treatment

| Item | Balance Sheet Side |
| --- | --- |
| Outstanding expense at end | Liability |
| Prepaid expense at end | Asset |

## 11. Treatment of Special Items

### Donations

| Type | Meaning | Treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| General donation | Donation without specific purpose | Usually income in Income and Expenditure Account |
| Specific donation | Donation for a named purpose such as building, prize, library, or tournament | Capitalised and shown as liability/fund |

### Legacy

Legacy means amount received through a will. Since it is usually non-recurring, it is normally treated as capital receipt and added to Capital Fund, unless instructions say otherwise.

### Entrance Fee

Entrance fee is paid by new members. Its treatment depends on the question:

| Instruction | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Treat as revenue | Credit to Income and Expenditure Account |
| Treat as capital | Add to Capital Fund |
| Capitalise a percentage | Capitalise that percentage and treat the remaining part as revenue |
| No instruction | Follow the policy used in the question or the common convention expected by the examiner |

### Life Membership Fee

Life membership fee is normally capital in nature because it is a one-time receipt for many years of membership. It may be shown as a liability or separate fund. If the question asks for annual transfer, only that portion is credited to Income and Expenditure Account.

### Sale of Old Asset

Receipts and Payments Account records the cash received from sale.

Income and Expenditure Account records profit or loss on sale, if relevant.

Balance Sheet removes the asset at book value.

### Depreciation

Depreciation is not recorded in Receipts and Payments Account because no cash is paid. It is recorded in Income and Expenditure Account as an expense and deducted from the asset in the Balance Sheet.

### Specific Funds

Examples: tournament fund, prize fund, building fund, library fund.

Rules:

1. Income related to a specific fund is added to that fund.
2. Expenses related to that fund are deducted from that fund.
3. The balance appears on the liabilities side of the Balance Sheet.
4. If fund expenses exceed the fund balance, the excess may be charged to Income and Expenditure Account if instructed or logically required.

## 12. Consumable Items

Items like stationery, medicines, sports material, and printing material may be consumed during the year. The amount charged to Income and Expenditure Account should be the material actually consumed, not simply the amount purchased.

Formula:

```text
Material consumed
= Opening stock
+ Purchases during the year
- Closing stock
```

If creditors or cash purchases are involved, first find total purchases, then calculate consumption.

## 13. Step-by-Step Final Accounts Method

### Step 1: Prepare Receipts and Payments Account

Use only cash and bank information. Do not adjust for outstanding, prepaid, accrued, depreciation, or capital/revenue distinction at this stage.

### Step 2: Classify Items

Mark each item as:

| Mark | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Revenue income | Goes to Income and Expenditure Account income side |
| Revenue expense | Goes to Income and Expenditure Account expenditure side |
| Capital receipt | Goes to Balance Sheet or Capital Fund |
| Capital payment | Becomes asset or reduces liability |
| Adjustment | Affects Income and Expenditure Account and Balance Sheet |

### Step 3: Prepare Income and Expenditure Account

Take only current-year revenue items after all adjustments.

### Step 4: Prepare Balance Sheet

Show assets and liabilities at year-end. Transfer surplus or deficit to Capital Fund.

## 14. Mini Worked Example: Subscription

During the year, subscription received was Rs. 1,20,000.

Additional information:

| Item | Amount |
| --- | ---: |
| Outstanding subscription at beginning | Rs. 8,000 |
| Outstanding subscription at end | Rs. 12,000 |
| Subscription received in advance at beginning | Rs. 5,000 |
| Subscription received in advance at end | Rs. 7,000 |

Calculation:

```text
Subscription for current year
= 1,20,000 + 12,000 + 5,000 - 8,000 - 7,000
= Rs. 1,22,000
```

Treatment:

| Statement | Amount |
| --- | ---: |
| Income and Expenditure Account income side | Rs. 1,22,000 |
| Balance Sheet asset: outstanding subscription | Rs. 12,000 |
| Balance Sheet liability: subscription received in advance | Rs. 7,000 |

## 15. Mini Worked Example: Expenses

Rent paid during the year was Rs. 48,000.

Additional information:

| Item | Amount |
| --- | ---: |
| Outstanding rent at beginning | Rs. 3,000 |
| Outstanding rent at end | Rs. 5,000 |
| Prepaid rent at beginning | Rs. 2,000 |
| Prepaid rent at end | Rs. 4,000 |

Calculation:

```text
Rent for current year
= 48,000 + 5,000 + 2,000 - 3,000 - 4,000
= Rs. 48,000
```

The paid amount and current-year expense can sometimes be the same after adjustments, but do not assume that. Always calculate.

## 16. Exam Answer Frames

### Short Answer: Receipts and Payments Account

Receipts and Payments Account is a summary of cash and bank transactions of a not-for-profit organisation. It is prepared on cash basis and records all receipts and payments, whether capital or revenue and whether related to past, current, or future years. Its balance shows closing cash/bank balance or overdraft.

### Short Answer: Income and Expenditure Account

Income and Expenditure Account is prepared to find the surplus or deficit of a not-for-profit organisation for an accounting year. It is prepared on accrual basis and includes only revenue incomes and expenses relating to the current year. Capital items and unrelated-year items are excluded.

### Long Answer: Final Accounts Process

To prepare final accounts of a not-for-profit organisation, first prepare or analyse the Receipts and Payments Account to identify actual cash inflows and outflows. Then classify each item as revenue or capital. Revenue items related to the current year are adjusted for outstanding, prepaid, accrued, and advance amounts and transferred to Income and Expenditure Account. Capital items, assets, liabilities, specific funds, and closing balances are shown in the Balance Sheet. The surplus is added to Capital Fund and the deficit is deducted from Capital Fund.

## 17. High-Yield Distinctions

| Pair | Difference |
| --- | --- |
| Surplus vs Profit | Surplus belongs to a service organisation and is used for its objectives; profit belongs to a business and may be distributed to owners. |
| Capital Fund vs Capital Account | Capital Fund is accumulated organisational fund; Capital Account belongs to an owner or partner. |
| Specific donation vs General donation | Specific donation is used only for a named purpose; general donation supports normal activities. |
| Outstanding income vs Accrued income | Both mean income earned but not received; in this chapter the idea is usually applied to subscriptions and interest. |
| Prepaid expense vs Outstanding expense | Prepaid is paid before it becomes due; outstanding is due but unpaid. |

## 18. Common Errors and Corrections

| Error | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Treating all receipts as income | Only revenue receipts related to the current year become income. |
| Recording depreciation in Receipts and Payments Account | Depreciation is non-cash; record it in Income and Expenditure Account. |
| Ignoring advance subscription | Advance subscription at end is a liability, not current-year income. |
| Treating purchase of furniture as expense | Furniture is an asset, not revenue expenditure. |
| Forgetting opening Capital Fund | If not given, prepare opening Balance Sheet using opening assets and liabilities. |
| Charging specific fund expenses to Income and Expenditure Account directly | First adjust them against the related fund. |

## 19. Practice Questions

### Concept Questions

1. What is a not-for-profit organisation? Give three examples.
2. Why do not-for-profit organisations prepare accounts even though they do not aim to earn profit?
3. Distinguish between Receipts and Payments Account and Income and Expenditure Account.
4. What is Capital Fund? How is it calculated?
5. Explain the treatment of specific donations.
6. Why is depreciation not shown in Receipts and Payments Account?
7. What is the difference between subscription received and subscription earned?

### Calculation Practice

1. Subscription received during the year is Rs. 80,000. Outstanding subscription at the beginning is Rs. 6,000 and at the end is Rs. 9,000. Advance subscription at the beginning is Rs. 4,000 and at the end is Rs. 3,000. Calculate subscription income.
2. Salaries paid during the year are Rs. 72,000. Salary outstanding at the beginning is Rs. 5,000 and at the end is Rs. 8,000. Salary prepaid at the beginning is Rs. 2,000 and at the end is Rs. 1,000. Calculate salary expense.
3. Opening stock of stationery is Rs. 3,000, stationery purchased is Rs. 14,000, and closing stock is Rs. 2,500. Calculate stationery consumed.
4. Opening assets are Rs. 4,00,000 and opening liabilities are Rs. 90,000. Find opening Capital Fund.

## 21. Final Learning Takeaway

This chapter is about converting a cash summary into meaningful financial statements. Receipts and Payments Account tells what happened to cash. Income and Expenditure Account tells whether the organisation had a surplus or deficit for the current year. Balance Sheet tells what the organisation owns, owes, and has accumulated as funds. Once you classify each item correctly, the chapter becomes a structured adjustment exercise rather than a memory exercise.
