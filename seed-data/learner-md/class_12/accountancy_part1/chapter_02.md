## 1. Big Picture

A sole proprietor owns and controls a business alone. When a business needs more capital, more skills, more risk-sharing, or more managerial support, two or more persons may join together and form a partnership.

In partnership accounting, the major issue is not only calculating profit. The major issue is deciding how that profit is shared and how partner-related items are recorded.

This chapter teaches:

1. What a partnership is.
2. What a partnership deed is.
3. What happens when the deed is silent.
4. How fixed and fluctuating capital accounts are maintained.
5. How Profit and Loss Appropriation Account distributes profit.
6. How to calculate interest on capital and interest on drawings.
7. How salary, commission, and guarantee of profit are treated.
8. How to make past adjustments when errors are discovered later.

## 2. Meaning of Partnership

Partnership is a relationship between persons who agree to share the profits of a business carried on by all or any one of them acting for all.

This definition contains four important ideas:

| Idea | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Agreement | Partnership is created by agreement, not merely by joint ownership. |
| Business | The activity must be business; charitable activity alone is not partnership. |
| Profit sharing | Partners agree to share profits. Loss sharing is normally implied unless agreed otherwise. |
| Mutual agency | Each partner is both an agent and a principal: a partner can bind the firm, and the firm can bind the partner. |

## 3. Essential Features of Partnership

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Two or more persons | At least two persons are needed to form a partnership. |
| Agreement | The relationship arises from agreement, oral or written. |
| Lawful business | The firm must carry on lawful business. |
| Sharing of profit | Partners must agree to share business profit. |
| Mutual agency | Business may be carried on by all partners or by one acting for all. |
| Unlimited liability | Partners are personally liable for firm debts, unless the structure is a limited liability partnership. |
| No separate legal entity under ordinary partnership | The firm and partners are not fully separate in the way a company is separate from shareholders. |

## 4. Important Terms

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Partner | A person who has entered into partnership with others. |
| Firm | Collective name for all partners. |
| Firm name | Name under which partnership business is carried on. |
| Partnership deed | Written agreement containing the terms and conditions of partnership. |
| Profit sharing ratio | Ratio in which partners divide profit and loss. |
| Capital | Amount invested by a partner in the firm. |
| Drawings | Cash or goods withdrawn by a partner for personal use. |
| Interest on capital | Return allowed to partners on capital contributed, if agreed. |
| Interest on drawings | Charge imposed on partners for using firm funds personally, if agreed. |
| Partner salary/commission | Appropriation of profit allowed to a partner for service, if agreed. |

## 5. Partnership Deed

### Meaning

A partnership deed is a written document that contains the terms of agreement among partners.

### Why It Matters

The deed reduces disputes because it states what partners have already agreed. In accounting problems, the deed tells you how to treat items such as interest on capital, interest on drawings, salary, commission, profit sharing ratio, and admission or retirement terms.

### Usual Contents of a Partnership Deed

| Clause | What It Decides |
| --- | --- |
| Name and address of firm | Identity of the business |
| Names and addresses of partners | Identity of partners |
| Nature of business | Type of activity carried on |
| Capital contribution | Amount contributed by each partner |
| Profit sharing ratio | How profit/loss will be divided |
| Interest on capital | Whether partners receive interest on capital and at what rate |
| Interest on drawings | Whether drawings are charged with interest and at what rate |
| Partner salary/commission | Whether any partner receives remuneration |
| Partner loan | Rate of interest on loans advanced by partners |
| Admission/retirement/death terms | How reconstitution will be handled |
| Dissolution terms | How accounts will be settled if the firm closes |

## 6. Rules When Partnership Deed Is Silent

If the partnership deed does not mention a matter, the Indian Partnership Act rules apply.

| Matter | Rule When Deed Is Silent |
| --- | --- |
| Profit sharing | Profits and losses are shared equally. |
| Interest on capital | Not allowed. |
| Interest on drawings | Not charged. |
| Partner salary/commission | Not allowed. |
| Interest on partner loan | Allowed at 6% per annum. |
| Remuneration for extra work | Not allowed unless agreed. |

### Exam Warning

Do not assume that higher capital means higher profit share. If the deed is silent, profit is shared equally even if capitals are unequal.

## 7. Profit and Loss Appropriation Account

### Meaning

Profit and Loss Appropriation Account shows how the net profit of a partnership firm is appropriated among partners after partner-related adjustments.

It is prepared after the Profit and Loss Account.

### Why It Is Needed

Profit and Loss Account calculates business profit from operations. Profit and Loss Appropriation Account distributes that profit among partners according to agreement.

### Format

| Debit Side | Amount | Credit Side | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Interest on capital | xxx | Net profit transferred from Profit and Loss Account | xxx |
| Partner salary | xxx | Interest on drawings | xxx |
| Partner commission | xxx |  |  |
| Profit transferred to partners' capital/current accounts | xxx |  |  |

If there is a loss, the account may start with net loss on the debit side and then transfer loss to partners.

## 8. Journal Entries for Profit Appropriation

### Transfer of Net Profit

```text
Profit and Loss A/c Dr.
    To Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c
```

### Interest on Capital Allowed

```text
Interest on Capital A/c Dr.
    To Partners' Capital/Current A/cs
```

Transfer to appropriation:

```text
Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c Dr.
    To Interest on Capital A/c
```

### Interest on Drawings Charged

```text
Partners' Capital/Current A/cs Dr.
    To Interest on Drawings A/c
```

Transfer to appropriation:

```text
Interest on Drawings A/c Dr.
    To Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c
```

### Partner Salary or Commission Allowed

```text
Salary/Commission to Partner A/c Dr.
    To Partner's Capital/Current A/c
```

Transfer to appropriation:

```text
Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c Dr.
    To Salary/Commission to Partner A/c
```

### Share of Final Profit

```text
Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c Dr.
    To Partners' Capital/Current A/cs
```

### Share of Final Loss

```text
Partners' Capital/Current A/cs Dr.
    To Profit and Loss Appropriation A/c
```

## 9. Methods of Maintaining Partners' Capital Accounts

There are two methods:

1. Fixed capital method.
2. Fluctuating capital method.

## 10. Fixed Capital Method

Under this method, each partner has two accounts:

| Account | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Partner's Capital Account | Records permanent capital introduced and permanent capital withdrawn. |
| Partner's Current Account | Records drawings, interest on drawings, interest on capital, salary, commission, and share of profit/loss. |

### Key Point

Capital Account remains fixed unless there is additional capital or permanent withdrawal of capital. Current Account may show debit or credit balance.

### Capital Account Format

| Debit | Amount | Credit | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Bank: permanent withdrawal | xxx | Balance b/d | xxx |
| Balance c/d | xxx | Bank: additional capital | xxx |

### Current Account Format

| Debit | Amount | Credit | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Drawings | xxx | Balance b/d | xxx |
| Interest on drawings | xxx | Interest on capital | xxx |
| Share of loss | xxx | Salary/commission | xxx |
| Balance c/d | xxx | Share of profit | xxx |

## 11. Fluctuating Capital Method

Under this method, only one capital account is maintained for each partner. All partner-related items are recorded in the Capital Account itself.

### Items Recorded

| Debit Side | Credit Side |
| --- | --- |
| Drawings | Opening capital |
| Interest on drawings | Additional capital |
| Share of loss | Interest on capital |
| Withdrawal of capital | Salary/commission |
| Closing balance | Share of profit |

### Key Point

In the absence of instructions, capital accounts are usually prepared by the fluctuating capital method.

## 12. Difference Between Fixed and Fluctuating Capital Methods

| Basis | Fixed Capital Method | Fluctuating Capital Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Number of accounts | Capital Account and Current Account | Only Capital Account |
| Capital balance | Normally remains fixed | Changes frequently |
| Drawings | Recorded in Current Account | Recorded in Capital Account |
| Interest on capital | Recorded in Current Account | Recorded in Capital Account |
| Share of profit/loss | Recorded in Current Account | Recorded in Capital Account |
| Default method if no instruction | Usually not assumed unless specified | Usually assumed |

## 13. Interest on Capital

### Meaning

Interest on capital is the amount allowed to partners for using their capital in the firm.

### Conditions

1. It is allowed only if the partnership deed provides for it.
2. It is an appropriation of profit, not a charge against profit, unless the deed specifically says it is a charge.
3. If profit is insufficient, interest may be restricted to available profit unless the agreement says otherwise.
4. If the firm has a loss, interest on capital is usually not allowed unless it is treated as a charge.

### Basic Formula

```text
Interest on capital = Capital x Rate x Time / 100
```

If capital changes during the year, calculate interest separately for each period.

### Example

A partner has capital of Rs. 2,00,000 for 12 months. Interest is allowed at 8% per annum.

```text
Interest = 2,00,000 x 8/100 = Rs. 16,000
```

If Rs. 50,000 additional capital is introduced after 6 months:

```text
Interest = 2,00,000 x 8/100 + 50,000 x 8/100 x 6/12
= 16,000 + 2,000
= Rs. 18,000
```

## 14. Interest on Drawings

### Meaning

Interest on drawings is charged to partners when they withdraw cash or goods for personal use.

### Why It Is Charged

Drawings reduce the funds available to the firm. Interest on drawings compensates the firm for that early withdrawal.

### Formula for a Single Drawing

```text
Interest on drawings = Drawings x Rate x Time / 100
```

Time is counted from the date of drawing to the end of the accounting year.

### When Drawings Are Made Regularly

If equal drawings are made every month:

| Timing of Monthly Drawings | Average Period |
| --- | --- |
| Beginning of each month | 6.5 months |
| Middle of each month | 6 months |
| End of each month | 5.5 months |

Formula:

```text
Interest = Total drawings x Rate x Average period / 100 / 12
```

### Example

A partner withdraws Rs. 5,000 at the beginning of every month. Rate is 12% per annum.

```text
Total drawings = 5,000 x 12 = Rs. 60,000
Average period = 6.5 months
Interest = 60,000 x 12/100 x 6.5/12
= Rs. 3,900
```

## 15. Partner Salary and Commission

Partner salary and commission are allowed only if the partnership deed provides for them.

They are appropriations of profit, not normal employee expenses, because partners are owners of the firm.

### Treatment

| Item | Partner's Account | Profit and Loss Appropriation Account |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Salary allowed | Credited | Debited |
| Commission allowed | Credited | Debited |

### Commission Calculation Types

| Type | Formula Idea |
| --- | --- |
| Commission as percentage of sales | Sales x Rate |
| Commission as percentage of profit before commission | Profit before commission x Rate |
| Commission as percentage of profit after commission | Profit before commission x Rate / (100 + Rate) |

Example: Profit before commission is Rs. 1,10,000. Commission is 10% of profit after commission.

```text
Commission = 1,10,000 x 10 / 110 = Rs. 10,000
```

## 16. Guarantee of Profit

Sometimes one partner is guaranteed a minimum profit. If the partner's actual share is less than the guaranteed amount, the deficiency is borne by other partners according to the agreement.

### Steps

1. Distribute profit normally in the profit sharing ratio.
2. Compare guaranteed partner's share with guaranteed amount.
3. Find deficiency.
4. Deduct deficiency from the partner or partners who agreed to bear it.
5. Credit the guaranteed partner with the guaranteed amount.

### Example

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. Total profit is Rs. 60,000. C is guaranteed Rs. 15,000.

Normal share of C:

```text
60,000 x 1/6 = Rs. 10,000
```

Deficiency:

```text
15,000 - 10,000 = Rs. 5,000
```

If A and B bear deficiency in their profit ratio 3:2:

| Partner | Normal Share | Deficiency Adjustment | Final Share |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| A | Rs. 30,000 | Less Rs. 3,000 | Rs. 27,000 |
| B | Rs. 20,000 | Less Rs. 2,000 | Rs. 18,000 |
| C | Rs. 10,000 | Add Rs. 5,000 | Rs. 15,000 |

## 17. Past Adjustments

### Meaning

Past adjustment is needed when profit has already been distributed but later it is discovered that some item was wrongly omitted, wrongly included, or wrongly calculated.

Examples:

1. Interest on capital was omitted.
2. Interest on drawings was not charged.
3. Salary to a partner was forgotten.
4. Profit was shared in the wrong ratio.

### Efficient Method

Instead of reopening all accounts, prepare an adjustment statement and pass one journal entry.

### Steps

1. Find what each partner should have received or been charged.
2. Find what each partner actually received or was charged.
3. Compare correct position with actual position.
4. Debit partners who received excess benefit.
5. Credit partners who received less benefit.

### Adjustment Entry

```text
Over-credited Partner's Capital/Current A/c Dr.
    To Under-credited Partner's Capital/Current A/c
```

Use the exact partner names and amounts from the adjustment statement.

## 18. Mini Worked Example: Profit Distribution

A and B are partners sharing profits equally. Net profit before appropriations is Rs. 1,00,000. The deed provides:

| Item | A | B |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Interest on capital | Rs. 10,000 | Rs. 8,000 |
| Salary | Rs. 12,000 | Nil |
| Interest on drawings | Rs. 2,000 | Rs. 1,000 |

Profit and Loss Appropriation Account:

| Debit | Amount | Credit | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Interest on capital: A | 10,000 | Net profit | 1,00,000 |
| Interest on capital: B | 8,000 | Interest on drawings: A | 2,000 |
| Salary to A | 12,000 | Interest on drawings: B | 1,000 |
| Profit to A | 36,500 |  |  |
| Profit to B | 36,500 |  |  |
| Total | 1,03,000 | Total | 1,03,000 |

Explanation:

```text
Total amount available for appropriation = 1,00,000 + 3,000 = Rs. 1,03,000
Specific appropriations = 10,000 + 8,000 + 12,000 = Rs. 30,000
Balance profit = 1,03,000 - 30,000 = Rs. 73,000
Each partner's share = Rs. 36,500
```

## 19. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Thinking |
| --- | --- |
| Allowing interest on capital when deed is silent | Interest on capital is not allowed unless agreed. |
| Charging interest on drawings automatically | It is charged only if agreed. |
| Sharing profit according to capital when deed is silent | Profit is shared equally when deed is silent. |
| Treating partner salary as employee salary | Partner salary is an appropriation of profit. |
| Recording all items in Capital Account under fixed capital method | Use Current Account for routine adjustments. |
| Forgetting time in interest calculations | Capital and drawings must be adjusted for the period involved. |
| Allowing full interest on capital when profit is insufficient | Restrict to available profit unless deed makes it a charge. |

## 20. Exam Answer Frames

### Partnership Deed

A partnership deed is a written agreement among partners containing the terms and conditions of partnership. It usually states capital contribution, profit sharing ratio, interest on capital, interest on drawings, partner salary, partner commission, loan interest, and rules for admission, retirement, death, and dissolution. It is important because it guides accounting treatment and reduces disputes.

### Profit and Loss Appropriation Account

Profit and Loss Appropriation Account is prepared by a partnership firm to show the distribution of net profit among partners. It records partner-related appropriations such as interest on capital, interest on drawings, salary, commission, and final share of profit or loss. It is prepared after the Profit and Loss Account.

### Fixed and Fluctuating Capital

Under the fixed capital method, each partner has a Capital Account and a Current Account. The Capital Account remains fixed unless capital is introduced or withdrawn permanently, while routine adjustments are recorded in the Current Account. Under the fluctuating capital method, only one Capital Account is maintained for each partner and all adjustments are recorded in it, so the balance keeps changing.

## 23. Final Learning Takeaway

Partnership accounting is agreement-driven. First read the partnership deed or identify that it is silent. Then decide which items are allowed, how they are calculated, and where they are recorded. Once this logic is clear, capital accounts and Profit and Loss Appropriation Account become a sequence of fair distribution rather than a set of disconnected formats.
