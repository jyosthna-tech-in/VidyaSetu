# Chapter 4: Reconstitution of a Partnership Firm - Retirement or Death of a Partner

## Chapter Identity

| Item | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Class | 12 |
| Subject | Accountancy Part 1 |
| Chapter | Reconstitution of a Partnership Firm - Retirement or Death of a Partner |
| Core Skill | Calculating dues of a retiring or deceased partner and adjusting the accounts of continuing partners |
| Main Idea | Retirement or death changes the profit-sharing relationship, so the firm must settle the outgoing partner's rights fairly. |

## 1. Big Picture

When a partner retires or dies, the old partnership agreement comes to an end. The business may continue, but it continues with changed partners and changed terms. This is called reconstitution of partnership.

The retiring partner, or the legal representatives of a deceased partner, must receive what is due from the firm. That amount is not just the capital balance. It may include share of goodwill, share of reserves, share of revaluation profit or loss, interest on capital, salary or commission up to the date, and share of profit up to the date of retirement or death.

The chapter is mainly about one question:

```text
What is the amount payable to the outgoing partner, and how should the continuing partners adjust it?
```

## 2. Retirement and Death: Meaning

### Retirement

Retirement means a partner leaves the firm while the remaining partners continue the business.

A partner may retire:

1. With the consent of all partners.
2. According to an agreement.
3. By giving notice, if the partnership is at will.

### Death

Death of a partner also causes reconstitution. The deceased partner cannot continue in the firm, so the amount due is transferred to the deceased partner's executor or legal representative.

### Similarity Between Retirement and Death

The accounting treatment is mostly the same. In both cases, the outgoing partner is entitled to a fair settlement.

### Difference

| Retirement | Death |
| --- | --- |
| Amount is payable to the retiring partner. | Amount is payable to executor/legal representative. |
| Date is usually agreed. | Date is the date of death. |
| Retiring partner may negotiate settlement directly. | Legal representative receives the settlement. |

## 3. Accounting Matters to Be Settled

At the time of retirement or death, the following matters are usually handled:

1. New profit sharing ratio of continuing partners.
2. Gaining ratio of continuing partners.
3. Treatment of goodwill.
4. Revaluation of assets and liabilities.
5. Treatment of unrecorded assets and liabilities.
6. Distribution of accumulated profits, reserves, and losses.
7. Share of profit or loss up to the date of retirement/death.
8. Interest on capital, salary, or commission up to the date, if applicable.
9. Adjustment of continuing partners' capitals, if required.
10. Settlement of the amount due to the retiring/deceased partner.

## 4. New Profit Sharing Ratio

### Meaning

New profit sharing ratio is the ratio in which the remaining partners share future profits after the outgoing partner leaves.

### How to Find It

There are three common situations.

| Situation | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Question gives new ratio directly | Use the given ratio. |
| Remaining partners acquire outgoing partner's share in a given ratio | Add acquired share to each continuing partner's old share. |
| No information is given | Continuing partners continue in their old ratio among themselves. |

### Example: No Information Given

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. B retires. No new ratio is given.

Remaining partners are A and C. Their old shares were 3:1 among themselves, so the new ratio is:

```text
A : C = 3 : 1
```

### Example: Share Acquired in Given Ratio

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. C retires. C's share is acquired by A and B equally.

C's old share:

```text
1/6
```

Each gets:

```text
1/6 x 1/2 = 1/12
```

New shares:

```text
A = 3/6 + 1/12 = 6/12 + 1/12 = 7/12
B = 2/6 + 1/12 = 4/12 + 1/12 = 5/12
```

New ratio:

```text
A : B = 7 : 5
```

## 5. Gaining Ratio

### Meaning

Gaining ratio is the ratio in which continuing partners gain the outgoing partner's share of profit.

Formula:

```text
Gaining share = New share - Old share
```

Gaining ratio is calculated using the gaining shares of the continuing partners.

### Why It Matters

The retiring or deceased partner is compensated for goodwill by the partners who gain. Therefore, goodwill adjustment is usually made in the gaining ratio.

### Example

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. C retires. A and B's new ratio is 3:2.

Old shares:

```text
A = 3/6
B = 2/6
C = 1/6
```

New shares:

```text
A = 3/5
B = 2/5
```

Gaining shares:

```text
A = 3/5 - 3/6 = 18/30 - 15/30 = 3/30
B = 2/5 - 2/6 = 12/30 - 10/30 = 2/30
```

Gaining ratio:

```text
A : B = 3 : 2
```

## 6. Goodwill on Retirement or Death

### Why Goodwill Is Adjusted

Goodwill is built by all partners, including the outgoing partner. When a partner leaves, that partner gives up the right to future profits. The continuing partners gain that share. Therefore, the outgoing partner should be compensated for their share of goodwill.

### Basic Rule

```text
Outgoing partner's share of goodwill = Total goodwill x Outgoing partner's old share
```

The partners who gain compensate the outgoing partner in their gaining ratio.

### Journal Entry When Goodwill Is Not Raised in Books

```text
Continuing Partners' Capital A/cs Dr.
    To Retiring/Deceased Partner's Capital A/c
```

Debit continuing partners in gaining ratio. Credit outgoing partner with their share of goodwill.

### Example

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. C retires. Goodwill is valued at Rs. 60,000. A and B gain in 3:2.

C's share of goodwill:

```text
60,000 x 1/6 = Rs. 10,000
```

Adjustment:

| Partner | Ratio | Debit |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| A | 3/5 | Rs. 6,000 |
| B | 2/5 | Rs. 4,000 |
| C | Credit | Rs. 10,000 |

Entry:

```text
A's Capital A/c Dr. 6,000
B's Capital A/c Dr. 4,000
    To C's Capital A/c 10,000
```

## 7. When a Continuing Partner Sacrifices

Sometimes one continuing partner may gain while another continuing partner sacrifices. In that case, the gaining partner compensates both:

1. The outgoing partner for the share given up.
2. The continuing partner who has sacrificed.

Use:

```text
Gain = New share - Old share
Sacrifice = Old share - New share
```

Only gaining partners are debited. Partners who sacrifice are credited.

## 8. Existing Goodwill in the Books

If goodwill already appears in the Balance Sheet, it should generally be written off among all partners in the old profit sharing ratio before fresh goodwill adjustment.

Entry:

```text
All Partners' Capital A/cs Dr.
    To Goodwill A/c
```

After this, adjust the outgoing partner's share of current goodwill through continuing partners' capital accounts.

### Reason

Existing goodwill belongs to all old partners. Therefore, writing it off affects all partners in the old ratio.

## 9. Revaluation of Assets and Liabilities

### Why Revaluation Is Needed

At retirement or death, assets and liabilities may not be shown at current values. Since the outgoing partner helped create the position of the firm, they should share profit or loss from revaluation.

### Revaluation Account

Revaluation Account records changes in assets and liabilities and unrecorded assets/liabilities.

### Entries

#### Increase in Asset

```text
Asset A/c Dr.
    To Revaluation A/c
```

#### Decrease in Asset

```text
Revaluation A/c Dr.
    To Asset A/c
```

#### Increase in Liability

```text
Revaluation A/c Dr.
    To Liability A/c
```

#### Decrease in Liability

```text
Liability A/c Dr.
    To Revaluation A/c
```

#### Unrecorded Asset Brought In

```text
Asset A/c Dr.
    To Revaluation A/c
```

#### Unrecorded Liability Brought In

```text
Revaluation A/c Dr.
    To Liability A/c
```

#### Profit on Revaluation

```text
Revaluation A/c Dr.
    To All Partners' Capital A/cs
```

Credit all partners in old profit sharing ratio.

#### Loss on Revaluation

```text
All Partners' Capital A/cs Dr.
    To Revaluation A/c
```

Debit all partners in old profit sharing ratio.

### Why Old Ratio Is Used

Revaluation profit or loss belongs to the period before retirement/death. Therefore, it is shared by all old partners, including the outgoing partner, in the old profit sharing ratio.

## 10. Accumulated Profits, Reserves, and Losses

### Accumulated Profits and Reserves

Examples:

1. General reserve.
2. Workmen compensation reserve, if no liability exists.
3. Credit balance of Profit and Loss Account.

Entry:

```text
Reserve/Profit and Loss A/c Dr.
    To All Partners' Capital A/cs
```

Credit all partners in old ratio.

### Accumulated Losses

Examples:

1. Debit balance of Profit and Loss Account.
2. Deferred revenue expenditure.
3. Advertisement suspense.

Entry:

```text
All Partners' Capital A/cs Dr.
    To Accumulated Loss A/c
```

Debit all partners in old ratio.

## 11. Workmen Compensation Reserve

This item is often tested because it depends on the liability.

| Situation | Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| No claim/liability exists | Distribute full reserve among old partners in old ratio. |
| Claim equals reserve | Transfer reserve to liability; no distribution. |
| Claim is less than reserve | Transfer claim to liability and distribute excess reserve among old partners. |
| Claim is more than reserve | Transfer full reserve to liability and debit excess through Revaluation Account. |

## 12. Share of Profit up to Date of Retirement or Death

If a partner leaves during the year, their share of profit up to that date must be calculated.

### Common Methods

| Method | Formula Idea |
| --- | --- |
| Time basis | Previous year's profit or estimated profit x period completed x outgoing partner's share |
| Sales/turnover basis | Profit estimated using sales up to retirement/death |

### Time Basis Example

Previous year's profit = Rs. 1,20,000. Partner retires after 3 months. Partner's share = 1/4.

```text
Estimated profit for 3 months = 1,20,000 x 3/12 = Rs. 30,000
Retiring partner's share = 30,000 x 1/4 = Rs. 7,500
```

Entry:

```text
Profit and Loss Suspense A/c Dr.
    To Retiring Partner's Capital A/c
```

If there is estimated loss:

```text
Retiring Partner's Capital A/c Dr.
    To Profit and Loss Suspense A/c
```

## 13. Amount Due to Retiring Partner

The retiring partner's capital account is adjusted to find the final amount due.

### Credits to Retiring Partner's Capital Account

1. Opening capital balance.
2. Share of goodwill.
3. Share of revaluation profit.
4. Share of accumulated profits/reserves.
5. Interest on capital up to retirement, if allowed.
6. Salary or commission due, if allowed.
7. Share of profit up to date of retirement.

### Debits to Retiring Partner's Capital Account

1. Drawings.
2. Interest on drawings.
3. Share of revaluation loss.
4. Share of accumulated losses.
5. Share of loss up to date of retirement.
6. Any liability personally taken over.

The balancing figure is the amount payable.

## 14. Settlement of Retiring Partner's Dues

### Full Payment Immediately

```text
Retiring Partner's Capital A/c Dr.
    To Bank A/c
```

### Part Payment and Balance as Loan

```text
Retiring Partner's Capital A/c Dr.
    To Bank A/c
    To Retiring Partner's Loan A/c
```

### Transfer Entire Amount to Loan

```text
Retiring Partner's Capital A/c Dr.
    To Retiring Partner's Loan A/c
```

### Instalment Payment with Interest

Interest:

```text
Interest A/c Dr.
    To Retiring Partner's Loan A/c
```

Payment:

```text
Retiring Partner's Loan A/c Dr.
    To Bank A/c
```

## 15. Settlement on Death

In case of death, the final balance of the deceased partner's capital account is transferred to Executor's Account.

Entry:

```text
Deceased Partner's Capital A/c Dr.
    To Executor's A/c
```

Payment to executor:

```text
Executor's A/c Dr.
    To Bank A/c
```

If the amount remains unpaid, Executor's Account appears as a liability.

## 16. Adjustment of Continuing Partners' Capitals

Sometimes the question asks that continuing partners' capitals should be adjusted in the new profit sharing ratio.

### Steps

1. Prepare adjusted capital accounts after goodwill, revaluation, reserves, losses, and settlement entries.
2. Find total capital of continuing partners.
3. Divide total capital in the new profit sharing ratio.
4. Compare required capital with actual capital.
5. If actual capital is more than required, partner withdraws excess.
6. If actual capital is less than required, partner brings in cash.

### If Total Capital Is Based on One Partner's Capital

Sometimes the question says capital of the new firm should be based on a particular partner's capital. In that case:

```text
Total capital = Given partner's adjusted capital / Given partner's share
```

Then divide total capital in the new ratio.

## 17. Full Accounting Sequence

Use this order in exam problems:

1. Calculate new profit sharing ratio.
2. Calculate gaining ratio.
3. Write off old goodwill, if any.
4. Adjust current goodwill.
5. Prepare Revaluation Account.
6. Transfer revaluation profit/loss to old partners.
7. Distribute accumulated reserves/profits and losses.
8. Calculate outgoing partner's share of profit/loss up to date.
9. Prepare partners' capital accounts.
10. Settle outgoing partner's balance.
11. Adjust continuing partners' capitals, if required.
12. Prepare the new Balance Sheet.

## 18. Mini Worked Example

A, B, and C share profits in 3:2:1. C retires. A and B will share future profits in 3:2. Goodwill is valued at Rs. 90,000. General reserve is Rs. 30,000. Revaluation profit is Rs. 12,000.

### Goodwill

C's share:

```text
90,000 x 1/6 = Rs. 15,000
```

A and B gain in 3:2, so:

| Partner | Debit |
| --- | ---: |
| A | Rs. 9,000 |
| B | Rs. 6,000 |
| C | Credit Rs. 15,000 |

### General Reserve

Distributed in old ratio 3:2:1:

| Partner | Credit |
| --- | ---: |
| A | Rs. 15,000 |
| B | Rs. 10,000 |
| C | Rs. 5,000 |

### Revaluation Profit

Distributed in old ratio 3:2:1:

| Partner | Credit |
| --- | ---: |
| A | Rs. 6,000 |
| B | Rs. 4,000 |
| C | Rs. 2,000 |

C receives credit for goodwill, reserve, and revaluation because all three relate to the period when C was a partner.

## 19. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Thinking |
| --- | --- |
| Sharing goodwill in old ratio | Outgoing partner's share of goodwill is compensated by gaining partners in gaining ratio. |
| Sharing revaluation in new ratio | Revaluation belongs to old period, so use old ratio. |
| Ignoring existing goodwill | Existing goodwill should generally be written off in old ratio before fresh adjustment. |
| Treating reserves as belonging only to continuing partners | Reserves belong to all old partners. |
| Confusing gaining ratio with new ratio | Gaining ratio is new share minus old share. |
| Crediting deceased partner directly with payment | Transfer balance to Executor's Account first. |
| Forgetting profit up to date of death | The deceased partner is entitled to profit up to the date of death if applicable. |

## 20. Exam Answer Frames

### Retirement of a Partner

Retirement of a partner leads to reconstitution of the partnership firm. The old agreement comes to an end and the continuing partners carry on the business under changed terms. The retiring partner is entitled to the balance of capital after adjustments for goodwill, revaluation of assets and liabilities, accumulated profits and losses, drawings, interest, salary or commission, and share of profit up to the date of retirement.

### Gaining Ratio

Gaining ratio is the ratio in which continuing partners acquire the outgoing partner's share of profit. It is calculated by subtracting each continuing partner's old share from the new share. It is important because the continuing partners who gain must compensate the retiring or deceased partner for goodwill.

### Revaluation Account

Revaluation Account is prepared at the time of retirement or death of a partner to record changes in the values of assets and liabilities and to bring unrecorded assets or liabilities into the books. The resulting profit or loss is transferred to all partners' capital accounts in their old profit sharing ratio.

## 23. Final Learning Takeaway

Retirement or death questions become manageable when you separate past rights from future rights. Anything earned or suffered before retirement belongs to all old partners in the old ratio. Anything gained after the outgoing partner leaves belongs to the continuing partners, so goodwill compensation is based on the gaining ratio. The final capital balance of the outgoing partner is the amount that must be settled.
