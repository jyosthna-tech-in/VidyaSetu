## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains what happens when a physical process or chemical reaction becomes balanced without stopping.

The central idea is:

**At equilibrium, the forward and reverse processes continue, but they occur at equal rates, so the measurable properties of the system remain constant.**

This is why equilibrium is called **dynamic equilibrium**, not static equilibrium.

The chapter has two large parts:

1. Chemical equilibrium: equilibrium in reversible reactions, equilibrium constants, reaction quotient, Le Chatelier's principle, and industrial applications.
2. Ionic equilibrium: acids, bases, pH, ionization constants, buffers, salt hydrolysis, solubility product, and common ion effect.

If you understand the logic of equilibrium once, the entire chapter becomes connected. The same idea explains vapour pressure, saturated solutions, ammonia synthesis, weak acids, buffer solutions, and precipitation of salts.

# 1. The Meaning of Dynamic Equilibrium

## The Basic Idea

Equilibrium does not mean that nothing is happening.

It means:

- the forward process continues,
- the reverse process continues,
- both occur at equal rates,
- there is no net change in measurable properties.

For example:

`H2O(l) <=> H2O(vapour)`

In a closed vessel, water molecules continue to evaporate and water vapour molecules continue to condense. At equilibrium, the number of molecules leaving the liquid becomes equal to the number returning to it.

The vapour pressure becomes constant, but molecular motion has not stopped.

---

## Static Versus Dynamic Equilibrium

| Static Equilibrium | Dynamic Equilibrium |
| --- | --- |
| No activity continues. | Opposite processes continue. |
| Common in mechanical stillness. | Common in chemical and physical processes. |
| Nothing changes even microscopically. | Microscopic change continues, macroscopic properties stay constant. |

Chemical equilibrium is always dynamic.

---

## Conditions for Equilibrium

Equilibrium is usually possible when:

- the system is closed,
- temperature is fixed,
- the process is reversible,
- enough time is allowed for forward and reverse rates to become equal.

Open systems usually do not reach equilibrium because matter can escape.

Example: water in an open watch glass evaporates completely because vapour molecules disperse into the room and do not return at the same rate.

---

# 2. Physical Equilibrium

Physical equilibrium involves physical changes such as phase change or dissolution. No new chemical substance is formed.

---

## 2.1 Solid-Liquid Equilibrium

Example:

`H2O(s) <=> H2O(l)`

Ice and water can remain in equilibrium at 273 K and 1 atm pressure.

At this point:

- ice melts into water,
- water freezes into ice,
- both processes occur at the same rate,
- mass of ice and water remains constant,
- temperature remains constant.

The normal melting point or freezing point of a pure substance is the temperature at which its solid and liquid phases are in equilibrium at 1 atm pressure.

---

## 2.2 Liquid-Vapour Equilibrium

Example:

`H2O(l) <=> H2O(g)`

In a closed container:

- liquid evaporates,
- vapour condenses,
- vapour pressure rises at first,
- after equilibrium, vapour pressure becomes constant.

The equilibrium vapour pressure of a liquid is the pressure exerted by its vapour at equilibrium at a given temperature.

Important points:

- vapour pressure increases with temperature,
- liquids with higher vapour pressure are more volatile,
- more volatile liquids usually have lower boiling points.

At 1.013 bar, the temperature at which liquid and vapour are in equilibrium is called the normal boiling point.

---

## 2.3 Solid-Vapour Equilibrium

Some solids sublime directly into vapour.

Examples:

`I2(s) <=> I2(g)`

`Camphor(s) <=> Camphor(g)`

`NH4Cl(s) <=> NH4Cl(g)`

If iodine crystals are placed in a closed vessel, violet vapour appears. After some time, the colour intensity becomes constant because sublimation and deposition occur at equal rates.

---

## 2.4 Dissolution Equilibrium of Solids in Liquids

Example:

`Sugar(s) <=> Sugar(aq)`

A saturated solution contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at a given temperature.

In a saturated solution:

- solute particles dissolve from solid into solution,
- solute particles crystallise from solution into solid,
- both rates become equal,
- concentration of solute remains constant.

Solubility is constant at a fixed temperature.

---

## 2.5 Dissolution Equilibrium of Gases in Liquids

Example:

`CO2(g) <=> CO2(aq)`

Soda water contains carbon dioxide dissolved under pressure. When the bottle is opened, pressure decreases and some dissolved `CO2` escapes as bubbles.

This is explained by Henry's law:

**At a fixed temperature, the amount of gas dissolved in a given mass of solvent is proportional to the pressure of the gas above the solvent.**

Important:

- higher gas pressure increases solubility of gas,
- higher temperature usually decreases solubility of gas,
- open soda becomes flat because `CO2` escapes.

---

## 2.6 General Characteristics of Physical Equilibrium

Physical equilibria share these features:

1. Equilibrium is possible only in a closed system at fixed temperature.
2. Opposite processes occur at equal rates.
3. The state is dynamic but stable.
4. Measurable properties remain constant.
5. Each physical equilibrium has a constant parameter at a given temperature.

| Physical Process | Constant Quantity at Equilibrium |
| --- | --- |
| Liquid-vapour | Vapour pressure |
| Solid-liquid | Melting/freezing point at fixed pressure |
| Solid in liquid | Solubility |
| Gas in liquid | Ratio of dissolved gas to gas pressure |

# 3. Chemical Equilibrium

## Definition

Chemical equilibrium is the state in a reversible chemical reaction where:

- forward reaction rate equals reverse reaction rate,
- concentrations of reactants and products remain constant,
- both reactants and products are present,
- the reaction does not stop at the molecular level.

Example:

`H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)`

If we start with `H2` and `I2`, `HI` forms. If we start with `HI`, it decomposes into `H2` and `I2`. At equilibrium, the same mixture can be reached from either direction if total atoms and conditions are the same.

---

## Dynamic Nature Using Ammonia

Haber's ammonia equilibrium:

`N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)`

Even at equilibrium, the reaction does not stop. Experiments using deuterium show that hydrogen and deuterium atoms continue to exchange among molecules. This proves that forward and reverse reactions continue.

---

## Extent of Reaction at Equilibrium

Different reactions reach different kinds of equilibrium.

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Nearly complete reaction | Products dominate; reactants left in very small amount. |
| Barely proceeding reaction | Reactants dominate; only small product amount forms. |
| Intermediate reaction | Reactants and products are both present in comparable amounts. |

The extent of a reaction depends on:

- concentration,
- temperature,
- pressure for gases,
- nature of reactants,
- reaction conditions.

---

# 4. Law of Chemical Equilibrium

## General Reaction

For:

`aA + bB <=> cC + dD`

The equilibrium constant in concentration form is:

`K_c = ([C]^c [D]^d) / ([A]^a [B]^b)`

Here:

- `[A]`, `[B]`, `[C]`, `[D]` are equilibrium molar concentrations,
- powers are stoichiometric coefficients from the balanced equation,
- `K_c` is constant at a fixed temperature.

This is also called the law of mass action.

---

## Example: Hydrogen Iodide

For:

`H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)`

`K_c = [HI]^2 / ([H2][I2])`

The square on `[HI]` comes from the coefficient 2 in the balanced equation.

---

## Important Rules for Equilibrium Constant

### Rule 1: Reverse reaction

If a reaction is reversed, the new equilibrium constant is the reciprocal.

If:

`H2 + I2 <=> 2HI`

has equilibrium constant `K_c`, then:

`2HI <=> H2 + I2`

has:

`K'_c = 1 / K_c`

---

### Rule 2: Multiplying the equation

If the entire balanced equation is multiplied by `n`, the new equilibrium constant becomes:

`K_new = (K_original)^n`

This is why the exact balanced equation must be specified when quoting `K`.

---

### Rule 3: Pure solids and pure liquids

Pure solids and pure liquids do not appear in equilibrium constant expressions.

Reason:

Their concentrations remain constant because density and molar mass are fixed.

Example:

`CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`

`K_c = [CO2]`

`K_p = p_CO2`

The solids `CaCO3` and `CaO` are not written in the expression.

---

# 5. Homogeneous and Heterogeneous Equilibria

## Homogeneous Equilibrium

All reactants and products are in the same phase.

Examples:

`N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)`

`Fe3+(aq) + SCN-(aq) <=> [Fe(SCN)]2+(aq)`

For homogeneous equilibria, include all reacting species except pure solvent when it is present in very large amount.

---

## Heterogeneous Equilibrium

More than one phase is present.

Examples:

`H2O(l) <=> H2O(g)`

`CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`

`Ni(s) + 4CO(g) <=> Ni(CO)4(g)`

For:

`Ni(s) + 4CO(g) <=> Ni(CO)4(g)`

`K_c = [Ni(CO)4] / [CO]^4`

The pure solid `Ni` is omitted.

---

## Warning

Pure solids and pure liquids must be present for the heterogeneous equilibrium to exist, even though they are not written in the `K` expression.

For example, in:

`CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`

both `CaCO3` and `CaO` must be present at equilibrium for `p_CO2` to have the equilibrium value.

---

# 6. Equilibrium Constant for Gases: `K_p`

For gases, equilibrium can be expressed using partial pressures.

For:

`aA(g) + bB(g) <=> cC(g) + dD(g)`

`K_p = (p_C^c p_D^d) / (p_A^a p_B^b)`

where `p_A`, `p_B`, `p_C`, `p_D` are equilibrium partial pressures.

---

## Relation Between `K_p` and `K_c`

Using the ideal gas relation:

`p = cRT`

The relation is:

`K_p = K_c(RT)^Delta n`

where:

`Delta n = moles of gaseous products - moles of gaseous reactants`

Only gaseous species are counted.

---

## How to Use `Delta n`

| Reaction | `Delta n` | Result |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| `H2 + I2 <=> 2HI` | `2 - 2 = 0` | `K_p = K_c` |
| `N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3` | `2 - 4 = -2` | `K_p = K_c(RT)^-2` |
| `PCl5 <=> PCl3 + Cl2` | `2 - 1 = 1` | `K_p = K_c(RT)` |

Use `R = 0.0831 bar L mol^-1 K^-1` when pressure is in bar.

---

# 7. What the Magnitude of `K` Tells Us

The equilibrium constant tells the extent of the reaction, not its speed.

| Value of `K` | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `K > 10^3` | Products predominate; reaction proceeds nearly to completion. |
| `K < 10^-3` | Reactants predominate; reaction hardly proceeds. |
| `10^-3 <= K <= 10^3` | Appreciable amounts of both reactants and products are present. |

Examples:

- Very large `K`: formation of water or hydrogen chloride.
- Very small `K`: decomposition of water at ordinary conditions.
- Intermediate `K`: formation of hydrogen iodide from hydrogen and iodine.

Important:

**A large `K` does not mean the reaction is fast.**

It only means the equilibrium mixture contains more products.

---

# 8. Reaction Quotient `Q`

## Meaning

The reaction quotient has the same expression as `K`, but it is calculated using concentrations or pressures at any moment, not necessarily equilibrium values.

For:

`aA + bB <=> cC + dD`

`Q_c = ([C]^c [D]^d) / ([A]^a [B]^b)`

using current concentrations.

---

## Comparing `Q` and `K`

| Comparison | Meaning | Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `Q < K` | Too many reactants or too few products. | Forward reaction is favoured. |
| `Q > K` | Too many products or too few reactants. | Reverse reaction is favoured. |
| `Q = K` | Composition is already at equilibrium. | No net shift. |

This is one of the most useful tools in the chapter.

---

## Example Logic

For:

`H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)`

If:

`K_c = 57`

and current:

`Q_c = 8`

then:

`Q_c < K_c`

So the mixture must form more `HI`. The reaction moves forward.

---

# 9. ICE Table Method for Equilibrium Calculations

Many equilibrium numericals use an ICE table.

ICE means:

- Initial concentration,
- Change,
- Equilibrium concentration.

---

## General Method

1. Write the balanced equation.
2. Write initial concentrations or pressures.
3. Let the change be `x`.
4. Use stoichiometry to fill the change row.
5. Write equilibrium values in terms of `x`.
6. Substitute into `K`.
7. Solve for `x`.
8. Reject impossible roots.

---

## Example Pattern

For:

`PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)`

If initial `PCl5 = 3.0 M`:

| Species | Initial | Change | Equilibrium |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| `PCl5` | `3.0` | `-x` | `3.0 - x` |
| `PCl3` | `0` | `+x` | `x` |
| `Cl2` | `0` | `+x` | `x` |

Then:

`K_c = [PCl3][Cl2] / [PCl5]`

`K_c = x^2 / (3.0 - x)`

This becomes an algebra problem.

---

## Common ICE Table Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Forgetting stoichiometric coefficients | Use `2x`, `3x`, etc. when coefficients demand it. |
| Using initial values in `K` | `K` must use equilibrium values. |
| Keeping impossible root | Concentration cannot be negative or exceed the possible initial amount. |
| Omitting units in setup | Concentrations are usually in `mol L^-1`; pressures in bar/atm. |

---

# 10. Equilibrium Constant and Gibbs Energy

## Thermodynamic Connection

Equilibrium is also connected to Gibbs energy.

The relation is:

`Delta G = Delta G^0 + RT ln Q`

At equilibrium:

`Delta G = 0`

and:

`Q = K`

So:

`0 = Delta G^0 + RT ln K`

`Delta G^0 = -RT ln K`

or:

`K = e^(-Delta G^0 / RT)`

---

## What This Means

| Condition | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Delta G < 0` | Forward reaction is spontaneous at that moment. |
| `Delta G > 0` | Reverse reaction is favoured at that moment. |
| `Delta G = 0` | System is at equilibrium. |
| `Delta G^0 < 0` | `K > 1`; products are favoured under standard conditions. |
| `Delta G^0 > 0` | `K < 1`; reactants are favoured under standard conditions. |

This section connects equilibrium with thermodynamics.

---

# 11. Le Chatelier's Principle

## Statement

If a system at equilibrium is disturbed by a change in concentration, pressure, volume, or temperature, it shifts in a direction that reduces or counteracts the effect of the disturbance.

This principle applies to both physical and chemical equilibrium.

---

## 11.1 Effect of Concentration

If a reactant is added, the system consumes it by shifting forward.

If a product is added, the system consumes it by shifting backward.

If a reactant is removed, the system shifts backward to replenish it.

If a product is removed, the system shifts forward to replenish it.

---

## Concentration Rule

| Change | Shift |
| --- | --- |
| Add reactant | Forward |
| Remove reactant | Backward |
| Add product | Backward |
| Remove product | Forward |

---

## Example: Haber Process

`N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)`

If `NH3` is removed by liquefaction, the equilibrium shifts forward to produce more ammonia.

This is industrially useful because continuous removal of product increases yield.

---

## Example: Calcium Oxide Production

`CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`

If `CO2` is continuously removed from the kiln, the reaction shifts forward and more `CaO` forms.

---

## 11.2 Effect of Pressure and Volume

Pressure affects only equilibria involving gases.

Increasing pressure by decreasing volume shifts equilibrium toward the side with fewer moles of gas.

Decreasing pressure by increasing volume shifts equilibrium toward the side with more moles of gas.

If both sides have the same number of moles of gas, pressure change has no effect on equilibrium composition.

---

## Pressure Rule

| Change | Shift |
| --- | --- |
| Increase pressure | Toward fewer gas moles |
| Decrease pressure | Toward more gas moles |
| Equal gas moles both sides | No shift |

---

## Examples

For:

`N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)`

Gas moles:

- left: 4
- right: 2

Increasing pressure shifts forward and favours ammonia.

For:

`C(s) + CO2(g) <=> 2CO(g)`

Gas moles:

- left: 1
- right: 2

Increasing pressure shifts backward.

---

## 11.3 Effect of Inert Gas

If an inert gas is added at constant volume:

- total pressure increases,
- partial pressures of reacting gases do not change,
- equilibrium is not disturbed.

The equilibrium changes only if partial pressures or concentrations of reacting species change.

---

## 11.4 Effect of Temperature

Temperature is special because it changes the value of the equilibrium constant.

For an exothermic reaction:

- heat is treated as a product,
- increasing temperature shifts equilibrium backward,
- `K` decreases as temperature increases.

For an endothermic reaction:

- heat is treated as a reactant,
- increasing temperature shifts equilibrium forward,
- `K` increases as temperature increases.

---

## Temperature Rule

| Reaction Type | Increase Temperature | Decrease Temperature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Exothermic | Shifts backward | Shifts forward |
| Endothermic | Shifts forward | Shifts backward |

---

## Example: Ammonia Synthesis

`N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)`

`Delta H = -92.38 kJ mol^-1`

The reaction is exothermic.

Low temperature favours ammonia yield, but very low temperature makes the reaction too slow. Industrial conditions therefore use a compromise: about 500 degree C, high pressure, and iron catalyst.

---

## Example: Nitrogen Dioxide Equilibrium

`2NO2(g) <=> N2O4(g)`

`NO2` is brown, while `N2O4` is colourless.

Heating favours the endothermic direction and increases brown `NO2`. Cooling favours colourless `N2O4`.

---

## 11.5 Effect of Catalyst

A catalyst:

- lowers activation energy,
- speeds up both forward and reverse reactions,
- helps equilibrium be reached faster,
- does not change equilibrium composition,
- does not change `K`.

A catalyst does not appear in the equilibrium constant expression.

---

## Industrial Importance of Catalysts

In the Haber process, iron catalyst allows ammonia formation at a useful rate under conditions where yield is still reasonable.

In the contact process:

`2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)`

`V2O5` or platinum catalyst is used because the uncatalysed reaction is too slow even though the equilibrium constant is large.

# 12. Ionic Equilibrium

## Meaning

Ionic equilibrium is equilibrium involving ions in aqueous solution.

It is important for:

- acids,
- bases,
- salts,
- pH,
- buffers,
- solubility,
- precipitation.

---

## Electrolytes

Electrolytes conduct electricity in aqueous solution because they produce ions.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strong electrolyte | Almost completely ionized. | `NaCl`, `HCl`, `NaOH` |
| Weak electrolyte | Partially ionized; equilibrium exists between ions and unionized molecules. | `CH3COOH`, `NH3` |

---

## Dissociation Versus Ionization

| Term | Strict Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Dissociation | Separation of ions that already exist in a solid ionic compound, such as `NaCl`. |
| Ionization | Formation of ions from neutral molecules, such as `HCl` or `CH3COOH`. |

In this chapter, the terms are often used interchangeably.

---

# 13. Acids and Bases

## 13.1 Arrhenius Concept

Arrhenius acid:

**A substance that gives `H+` or `H3O+` ions in water.**

Example:

`HX(aq) + H2O(l) -> H3O+(aq) + X-(aq)`

Arrhenius base:

**A substance that gives `OH-` ions in water.**

Example:

`MOH(aq) -> M+(aq) + OH-(aq)`

Limitation:

- applies mainly to aqueous solutions,
- cannot explain bases like `NH3`, which do not contain `OH-`.

---

## 13.2 Bronsted-Lowry Concept

Bronsted-Lowry acid:

**Proton donor.**

Bronsted-Lowry base:

**Proton acceptor.**

Example:

`NH3(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)`

Here:

- `H2O` donates a proton, so it acts as an acid,
- `NH3` accepts a proton, so it acts as a base.

---

## Conjugate Acid-Base Pair

A conjugate acid-base pair differs by one proton.

| Acid | Conjugate Base |
| --- | --- |
| `HF` | `F-` |
| `H2SO4` | `HSO4-` |
| `HCO3-` | `CO3^2-` |
| `H2O` | `OH-` |

| Base | Conjugate Acid |
| --- | --- |
| `NH3` | `NH4+` |
| `NH2-` | `NH3` |
| `HCOO-` | `HCOOH` |

Rule:

- conjugate base has one less proton,
- conjugate acid has one extra proton.

---

## Amphiprotic Species

Some species can act as both acid and base.

Examples:

| Species | Conjugate Acid | Conjugate Base |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `H2O` | `H3O+` | `OH-` |
| `HCO3-` | `H2CO3` | `CO3^2-` |
| `HSO4-` | `H2SO4` | `SO4^2-` |
| `NH3` | `NH4+` | `NH2-` |

---

## 13.3 Lewis Concept

Lewis acid:

**Electron pair acceptor.**

Lewis base:

**Electron pair donor.**

Example:

`BF3 + :NH3 -> BF3:NH3`

Here:

- `BF3` accepts an electron pair, so it is a Lewis acid,
- `NH3` donates a lone pair, so it is a Lewis base.

Examples:

| Lewis Acid | Why |
| --- | --- |
| `H+` | Accepts an electron pair. |
| `BF3` | Electron deficient boron. |
| `AlCl3` | Electron deficient aluminium. |
| `Mg2+`, `Co3+` | Metal ions can accept electron pairs. |

| Lewis Base | Why |
| --- | --- |
| `OH-` | Donates lone pair. |
| `F-` | Donates lone pair. |
| `H2O` | Donates lone pair from oxygen. |
| `NH3` | Donates lone pair from nitrogen. |

---

# 14. Strong and Weak Acids/Bases

## Strong Acids

Strong acids are almost completely ionized in water.

Examples:

- `HClO4`
- `HCl`
- `HBr`
- `HI`
- `HNO3`
- `H2SO4` first ionization strongly

Strong acid means good proton donor.

Strong acids have very weak conjugate bases.

---

## Weak Acids

Weak acids are only partially ionized in water.

Examples:

- `CH3COOH`
- `HF`
- `HNO2`
- `HCN`
- phenol

In a weak acid solution, most molecules remain unionized.

---

## Strong Bases

Strong bases are almost completely ionized in water.

Examples:

- `LiOH`
- `NaOH`
- `KOH`
- `CsOH`
- `Ba(OH)2`

---

## Weak Bases

Weak bases ionize only partially.

Examples:

- `NH3`
- aniline
- pyridine
- urea
- amines

---

## Strength and Conjugate Relationship

| If Acid/Base Is | Its Conjugate Is |
| --- | --- |
| Strong acid | Very weak base |
| Weak acid | Relatively stronger base |
| Strong base | Very weak acid |
| Weak base | Relatively stronger acid |

Equilibrium favours formation of the weaker acid and weaker base.

# 15. Ionic Product of Water and pH

## Auto-Ionization of Water

Water can act as both acid and base:

`H2O(l) + H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)`

The ionic product of water is:

`K_w = [H+][OH-]`

At 298 K:

`K_w = 1.0 x 10^-14`

In pure water:

`[H+] = [OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-7 M`

---

## Acidic, Basic, Neutral

| Solution | Ion Relation | pH at 298 K |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acidic | `[H+] > [OH-]` | `pH < 7` |
| Neutral | `[H+] = [OH-]` | `pH = 7` |
| Basic | `[H+] < [OH-]` | `pH > 7` |

---

## pH Definition

For dilute solutions:

`pH = -log[H+]`

Similarly:

`pOH = -log[OH-]`

At 298 K:

`pH + pOH = 14`

---

## Logarithmic Meaning

pH is logarithmic.

So:

- if `[H+]` increases 10 times, pH decreases by 1,
- if `[H+]` decreases 100 times, pH increases by 2.

This is why a small pH change can mean a large change in acidity.

---

## pH Examples

| Substance | Approximate pH |
| --- | ---: |
| Gastric juice | 1.2 |
| Lemon juice | 2.2 |
| Vinegar/soft drinks | about 3 |
| Tomato juice | 4.2 |
| Black coffee | 5 |
| Milk | 6.8 |
| Human blood | 7.4 |
| Sea water/egg white | 7.8 |
| Milk of magnesia | 10 |
| 0.1 M NaOH | 13 |

# 16. Weak Acid Ionization

## General Weak Acid

For weak acid `HX`:

`HX(aq) + H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + X-(aq)`

The acid ionization constant is:

`K_a = [H+][X-] / [HX]`

Larger `K_a` means stronger acid.

Also:

`pK_a = -log K_a`

Lower `pK_a` means stronger acid.

---

## Degree of Ionization

If initial concentration is `c` and degree of ionization is `alpha`:

| Species | Equilibrium Concentration |
| --- | --- |
| `HX` | `c(1 - alpha)` |
| `H+` | `c alpha` |
| `X-` | `c alpha` |

Then:

`K_a = c alpha^2 / (1 - alpha)`

For very weak acids where `alpha` is small:

`K_a approximately c alpha^2`

so:

`alpha approximately sqrt(K_a / c)`

---

## Percent Dissociation

`Percent dissociation = ([acid dissociated] / [initial acid]) x 100`

For a monoprotic weak acid:

`Percent dissociation = alpha x 100`

---

## Weak Acid Problem Method

1. Write the ionization equation.
2. Build the ICE table.
3. Substitute in `K_a`.
4. Use approximation only when justified.
5. Find `[H+]`.
6. Calculate pH.

---

## Example Pattern

For `0.08 M HOCl`, if `K_a = 2.5 x 10^-5`:

`HOCl <=> H+ + OCl-`

Let `[H+] = x`.

`K_a = x^2 / (0.08 - x)`

If `x << 0.08`:

`x^2 / 0.08 = 2.5 x 10^-5`

`x = 1.41 x 10^-3 M`

`pH = -log(1.41 x 10^-3) = 2.85`

---

# 17. Weak Base Ionization

## General Weak Base

For weak base `MOH`:

`MOH(aq) <=> M+(aq) + OH-(aq)`

`K_b = [M+][OH-] / [MOH]`

For ammonia:

`NH3(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)`

`K_b = [NH4+][OH-] / [NH3]`

---

## Degree of Ionization of Weak Base

If initial concentration is `c` and degree of ionization is `alpha`:

`K_b = c alpha^2 / (1 - alpha)`

For small `alpha`:

`alpha approximately sqrt(K_b / c)`

---

## Weak Base Problem Method

1. Write base ionization equation.
2. Find `[OH-]` from `K_b`.
3. Calculate `pOH = -log[OH-]`.
4. Use `pH = 14 - pOH` at 298 K.

---

## Example Pattern: Ammonia

For `0.05 M NH3`, if `K_b = 1.77 x 10^-5`:

`NH3 + H2O <=> NH4+ + OH-`

Approximation:

`alpha = sqrt(K_b / c)`

`alpha = sqrt((1.77 x 10^-5) / 0.05)`

`alpha approximately 0.018`

`[OH-] = c alpha = 0.05 x 0.018 = 9.0 x 10^-4 M`

Then:

`[H+] = K_w / [OH-]`

and pH is basic.

---

# 18. Relation Between `K_a` and `K_b`

For a conjugate acid-base pair:

`K_a x K_b = K_w`

At 298 K:

`K_a x K_b = 1.0 x 10^-14`

Taking negative logarithm:

`pK_a + pK_b = pK_w = 14`

---

## How to Use This

If you know `K_b` of `NH3`, you can find `K_a` of `NH4+`:

`K_a = K_w / K_b`

Similarly, if you know `K_a` of `CH3COOH`, you can find `K_b` of `CH3COO-`:

`K_b = K_w / K_a`

---

# 19. Polyprotic Acids and Acid Strength

## Polyprotic Acids

Polyprotic acids contain more than one ionizable proton.

Examples:

- `H2SO4`
- `H2CO3`
- `H2S`
- `H3PO4`
- oxalic acid

For a diprotic acid `H2X`:

First ionization:

`H2X <=> H+ + HX-`

`K_a1 = [H+][HX-] / [H2X]`

Second ionization:

`HX- <=> H+ + X^2-`

`K_a2 = [H+][X^2-] / [HX-]`

Usually:

`K_a1 > K_a2 > K_a3`

Reason:

After losing one proton, the species becomes negatively charged. Removing another positively charged proton from a negative ion is more difficult because of electrostatic attraction.

---

## Factors Affecting Acid Strength

Acid strength depends mainly on:

- strength of the `H-A` bond,
- polarity of the `H-A` bond,
- stability of the conjugate base,
- solvation of ions.

---

## Same Group Trend

Down a group, bond strength decreases as atom size increases. Acid strength increases.

Example:

`HF < HCl < HBr < HI`

Acid strength increases from `HF` to `HI`.

Reason:

`H-I` bond is weaker than `H-F`, so `HI` donates proton more easily.

---

## Same Period Trend

Across a period, electronegativity becomes more important. Greater polarity of `H-A` bond increases acidity.

Example:

`CH4 < NH3 < H2O < HF`

Acid strength increases toward `HF`.

---

# 20. Common Ion Effect in Acid-Base Equilibrium

## Meaning

Common ion effect is the shift in equilibrium caused by adding an ion already present in the equilibrium.

It is a direct application of Le Chatelier's principle.

---

## Example: Acetic Acid and Acetate

`CH3COOH(aq) <=> H+(aq) + CH3COO-(aq)`

If sodium acetate is added, it provides `CH3COO-`, a common ion.

The equilibrium shifts backward:

`H+ + CH3COO- -> CH3COOH`

So:

- ionization of acetic acid decreases,
- `[H+]` decreases,
- pH increases.

---

## Adding Strong Acid

If `H+` is added to acetic acid equilibrium:

`CH3COOH <=> H+ + CH3COO-`

the equilibrium also shifts backward, reducing ionization.

---

## Why It Matters

Common ion effect explains:

- buffer action,
- reduced ionization of weak acids/bases,
- reduced solubility of sparingly soluble salts,
- selective precipitation in qualitative analysis.

---

# 21. Salt Hydrolysis

## Meaning

Salt hydrolysis is the reaction of ions of a salt with water to produce acidic or basic solutions.

Not all salts hydrolyse.

---

## Salts of Strong Acid and Strong Base

Example:

`NaCl`

`Na+` comes from strong base `NaOH`.

`Cl-` comes from strong acid `HCl`.

Neither ion hydrolyses significantly.

Result:

`pH = 7` at 298 K.

---

## Salts of Weak Acid and Strong Base

Example:

`CH3COONa`

`CH3COONa -> CH3COO- + Na+`

Acetate ion hydrolyses:

`CH3COO- + H2O <=> CH3COOH + OH-`

Result:

- `OH-` increases,
- solution becomes basic,
- `pH > 7`.

---

## Salts of Strong Acid and Weak Base

Example:

`NH4Cl`

`NH4Cl -> NH4+ + Cl-`

Ammonium ion hydrolyses:

`NH4+ + H2O <=> NH4OH + H+`

Result:

- `H+` increases,
- solution becomes acidic,
- `pH < 7`.

---

## Salts of Weak Acid and Weak Base

Example:

`CH3COONH4`

Both ions can hydrolyse.

For such salts, the pH depends on `pK_a` of the weak acid and `pK_b` of the weak base:

`pH = 7 + 1/2(pK_a - pK_b)`

If:

- `pK_a > pK_b`, solution is slightly basic,
- `pK_a < pK_b`, solution is slightly acidic,
- `pK_a = pK_b`, solution is nearly neutral.

Example:

For ammonium acetate:

`pK_a` of acetic acid is about 4.76.

`pK_b` of ammonium hydroxide is about 4.75.

`pH = 7 + 1/2(4.76 - 4.75) = 7.005`

Nearly neutral.

---

# 22. Buffer Solutions

## Meaning

A buffer solution resists change in pH when:

- small amount of acid is added,
- small amount of base is added,
- the solution is diluted.

Buffers are important in:

- blood,
- urine,
- biochemical reactions,
- medicines,
- cosmetics,
- titrations.

---

## Types of Buffer

| Buffer Type | Composition | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acidic buffer | Weak acid + salt of that acid with strong base | `CH3COOH + CH3COONa` |
| Basic buffer | Weak base + salt of that base with strong acid | `NH4OH + NH4Cl` |

---

## Acidic Buffer Equation

For weak acid `HA` and its salt containing `A-`:

`HA <=> H+ + A-`

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation is:

`pH = pK_a + log([A-] / [HA])`

In practical salt-acid form:

`pH = pK_a + log([salt] / [acid])`

---

## Basic Buffer Equation

For weak base `B` and conjugate acid `BH+`:

`B + H2O <=> BH+ + OH-`

`pOH = pK_b + log([BH+] / [B])`

At 298 K:

`pH = 14 - pOH`

or:

`pH = 14 - pK_b + log([B] / [BH+])`

For the ammonium buffer:

`NH4OH + NH4Cl`

equal concentrations give pH around 9.25.

---

## Why Buffer pH Is Not Much Affected by Dilution

In the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, pH depends on the ratio:

`[salt] / [acid]`

or:

`[base] / [conjugate acid]`

On dilution, both concentrations decrease by the same factor, so the ratio remains almost unchanged.

---

## How Buffers Work

For acidic buffer `CH3COOH + CH3COONa`:

- added `H+` is consumed by `CH3COO-`,
- added `OH-` is neutralised by `CH3COOH`,
- pH changes only slightly.

For basic buffer `NH4OH + NH4Cl`:

- added `H+` is consumed by weak base,
- added `OH-` is consumed by `NH4+`,
- pH remains nearly constant.

---

# 23. Solubility Equilibria of Sparingly Soluble Salts

## Solubility

Solubility is the amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a particular temperature.

Salts may be classified as:

| Category | Solubility |
| --- | --- |
| Soluble | Greater than `0.1 M` |
| Slightly soluble | Between `0.01 M` and `0.1 M` |
| Sparingly soluble | Less than `0.01 M` |

---

## Why Some Salts Dissolve Poorly

Dissolution depends on the balance between:

- lattice enthalpy: energy needed to separate ions in the crystal,
- solvation enthalpy: energy released when ions are solvated by water.

If solvation enthalpy is not enough to overcome lattice enthalpy, solubility is low.

---

## Solubility Product Constant

For:

`MxXy(s) <=> x M^p+(aq) + y X^q-(aq)`

The solubility product is:

`K_sp = [M^p+]^x [X^q-]^y`

Pure solid is omitted.

---

## Relation Between `K_sp` and Solubility

If molar solubility is `S`:

`[M^p+] = xS`

`[X^q-] = yS`

So:

`K_sp = (xS)^x (yS)^y`

This formula must be used carefully with stoichiometric coefficients.

---

## Example: `A2X3`

For:

`A2X3(s) <=> 2A^3+(aq) + 3X^2-(aq)`

If solubility is `S`:

`[A^3+] = 2S`

`[X^2-] = 3S`

Therefore:

`K_sp = [A^3+]^2 [X^2-]^3`

`K_sp = (2S)^2(3S)^3`

`K_sp = 108S^5`

---

# 24. Ionic Product `Q_sp` and Precipitation

`Q_sp` has the same expression as `K_sp`, but uses current ion concentrations.

| Comparison | Result |
| --- | --- |
| `Q_sp < K_sp` | Solution is unsaturated; more salt can dissolve. |
| `Q_sp = K_sp` | Solution is saturated; equilibrium exists. |
| `Q_sp > K_sp` | Precipitation occurs until `Q_sp` becomes `K_sp`. |

---

## Common Ion Effect on Solubility

Adding a common ion decreases solubility.

Example:

For:

`AgCl(s) <=> Ag+(aq) + Cl-(aq)`

Adding `NaCl` increases `[Cl-]`.

The equilibrium shifts backward and `AgCl` precipitates.

---

## Practical Uses

Common ion effect is used in:

- purification of salts,
- qualitative analysis,
- gravimetric estimation,
- selective precipitation of ions.

Example:

Passing `HCl` gas through saturated `NaCl` solution increases chloride ion concentration and precipitates pure sodium chloride.

---

# 25. Numerical Strategy Board

## Strategy 1: Writing `K_c`

1. Balance the equation.
2. Put products in numerator.
3. Put reactants in denominator.
4. Raise each concentration to its coefficient.
5. Omit pure solids and pure liquids.

Example:

`2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)`

`K_c = [SO3]^2 / ([SO2]^2[O2])`

---

## Strategy 2: Using `K_p = K_c(RT)^Delta n`

1. Count gaseous product moles.
2. Count gaseous reactant moles.
3. Find `Delta n`.
4. Substitute into formula.

Example:

`2NOCl(g) <=> 2NO(g) + Cl2(g)`

`Delta n = 3 - 2 = 1`

`K_p = K_c(RT)`

---

## Strategy 3: Direction from `Q`

1. Write `Q` expression.
2. Substitute current concentrations.
3. Compare with `K`.

| If | Then |
| --- | --- |
| `Q < K` | Reaction moves forward. |
| `Q > K` | Reaction moves backward. |
| `Q = K` | Already at equilibrium. |

---

## Strategy 4: pH of Strong Acid or Base

For strong acid:

`[H+] = acid concentration x number of acidic protons released`

`pH = -log[H+]`

For strong base:

`[OH-] = base concentration x number of hydroxide ions released`

`pOH = -log[OH-]`

`pH = 14 - pOH`

---

## Strategy 5: pH of Weak Acid

For weak acid `HA`:

`K_a = [H+][A-] / [HA]`

Use ICE table:

`K_a = x^2 / (c - x)`

If weak:

`x approximately sqrt(K_a c)`

Then:

`pH = -log x`

---

## Strategy 6: pH of Weak Base

For weak base:

`K_b = [conjugate acid][OH-] / [base]`

Use:

`K_b = x^2 / (c - x)`

Find:

`[OH-] = x`

Then:

`pOH = -log x`

`pH = 14 - pOH`

---

## Strategy 7: Buffer pH

For acidic buffer:

`pH = pK_a + log([salt] / [acid])`

For basic buffer:

`pOH = pK_b + log([salt] / [base])`

Then:

`pH = 14 - pOH`

---

## Strategy 8: Solubility Product

1. Write dissolution equation.
2. Let molar solubility be `S`.
3. Write ion concentrations using coefficients.
4. Substitute into `K_sp`.
5. Solve for `S`.

Example:

`AgCl(s) <=> Ag+ + Cl-`

`K_sp = S^2`

So:

`S = sqrt(K_sp)`

For:

`CaF2(s) <=> Ca2+ + 2F-`

`K_sp = (S)(2S)^2 = 4S^3`

---

# 26. Common Mistakes to Avoid

## Mistake 1: Thinking equilibrium means reaction has stopped

Correct idea:

The reaction continues in both directions at equal rates.

---

## Mistake 2: Using initial concentrations in `K`

Correct idea:

`K` uses equilibrium concentrations. Use `Q` for non-equilibrium concentrations.

---

## Mistake 3: Including solids in `K`

Correct idea:

Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted.

---

## Mistake 4: Forgetting powers in `K`

Correct idea:

Use stoichiometric coefficients as powers.

---

## Mistake 5: Confusing `K` with rate

Correct idea:

`K` tells final composition tendency, not speed.

---

## Mistake 6: Saying catalyst increases yield

Correct idea:

Catalyst helps reach equilibrium faster but does not change equilibrium composition.

---

## Mistake 7: Applying pressure rule to liquids and solids

Correct idea:

Pressure changes matter mainly for gaseous equilibria.

---

## Mistake 8: Forgetting that temperature changes `K`

Correct idea:

Concentration and pressure shifts change composition but not `K`; temperature changes `K`.

---

## Mistake 9: Treating weak acid as fully ionized

Correct idea:

Weak acids and weak bases require equilibrium calculations.

---

## Mistake 10: Comparing `K_sp` values blindly

Correct idea:

Compare solubility after considering stoichiometry. A salt with smaller `K_sp` is not always less soluble if formulas differ.

# 27. High-Quality Answer Frameworks

## Framework A: Dynamic Equilibrium

Use this structure:

1. Define dynamic equilibrium.
2. Say forward and reverse rates are equal.
3. Say measurable properties remain constant.
4. Give one example.
5. Emphasise that reaction does not stop.

### Model Answer

Dynamic equilibrium is the state in which forward and reverse processes continue at equal rates. Because the two rates are equal, there is no net change in measurable properties such as concentration, pressure, or amount of phases. For example, in a closed vessel containing water and water vapour, evaporation and condensation continue, but vapour pressure remains constant. Hence equilibrium is dynamic, not static.

---

## Framework B: Equilibrium Constant Expression

Use this structure:

1. Write the balanced equation.

3. Raise each term to its coefficient.
4. Omit pure solids and liquids.
5. Mention fixed temperature.

### Model Answer

The concentrations used must be equilibrium concentrations and the exponents are the stoichiometric coefficients. Pure solids and pure liquids are omitted because their concentrations remain constant. The value of `K_c` is constant at a fixed temperature.

## Framework C: Le Chatelier's Principle

Use this structure:

1. State principle.
2. Identify disturbance.
3. Decide how system can oppose it.
4. Predict shift.
5. Connect to yield if needed.

### Model Answer

Le Chatelier's principle states that when a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts in a direction that reduces the effect of the disturbance. If pressure is increased for a gaseous equilibrium, the system shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas. In the Haber process, `N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3`, the product side has fewer gas moles, so increasing pressure favours ammonia formation.

Use this structure:

# 28. Practice Questions With Short Guidance

## Question: Why can pure solids and liquids be omitted from equilibrium expressions?

Because their concentrations remain constant at a fixed temperature. The concentration of a pure solid or liquid depends on density and molar mass, not on the amount present. Therefore, these constant terms are included in the equilibrium constant and are not written separately.

---

## Question: What happens when volume is increased for liquid-vapour equilibrium?

Initially, vapour pressure decreases because the same vapour occupies a larger volume. Evaporation becomes faster than condensation until more liquid evaporates. Finally, at the same temperature, vapour pressure returns to the original equilibrium value if liquid remains.

---

## Question: Why does removal of ammonia increase ammonia yield in Haber process?

For `N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3`, removing `NH3` decreases product concentration and makes `Q < K`. The system shifts forward to form more ammonia until equilibrium is restored.

---

## Question: Why does common ion reduce solubility?

For a salt equilibrium, adding one ion already present increases its concentration. The ionic product becomes larger than allowed by `K_sp`, so the equilibrium shifts backward and some salt precipitates.

---

## Question: Why is `HF` weaker than `HI` even though fluorine is more electronegative?

Down the halogen group, bond strength is more important than polarity. The `H-F` bond is very strong, so `HF` does not release `H+` easily. The `H-I` bond is much weaker, so `HI` is a stronger acid.

---

# 30. One-Page Exam Revision

Equilibrium is a dynamic state in which forward and reverse processes occur at equal rates, so measurable properties remain constant. Physical equilibria include solid-liquid, liquid-vapour, solid-vapour, and dissolution equilibria. Chemical equilibrium occurs in reversible reactions and is represented by an equilibrium constant. For `aA + bB <=> cC + dD`, `K_c = [C]^c[D]^d/[A]^a[B]^b`. For gases, `K_p = K_c(RT)^Delta n`. Pure solids and liquids are omitted.

The magnitude of `K` tells the extent of reaction. Large `K` means products dominate; small `K` means reactants dominate. The reaction quotient `Q` predicts direction: `Q < K` means forward, `Q > K` means reverse, and `Q = K` means equilibrium. Gibbs energy connects thermodynamics with equilibrium through `Delta G = Delta G^0 + RT ln Q` and `Delta G^0 = -RT ln K`.

Le Chatelier's principle says that a system at equilibrium shifts to oppose a disturbance. Concentration changes are opposed by consuming or replenishing the changed species. Pressure changes affect gaseous equilibria and favour the side with fewer or more gas moles. Temperature changes the value of `K`: exothermic reactions are favoured by lower temperature, endothermic reactions by higher temperature. Catalysts speed up attainment of equilibrium but do not change equilibrium composition.

Ionic equilibrium deals with acids, bases, salts, and sparingly soluble salts. `K_w = [H+][OH-] = 10^-14` at 298 K, and `pH = -log[H+]`. Weak acids and bases are described by `K_a` and `K_b`, with `K_aK_b = K_w` for conjugate pairs. Common ion effect suppresses ionization or solubility. Buffers resist pH change and are described by Henderson-Hasselbalch equations. Salt hydrolysis explains acidic, basic, or neutral salt solutions. Solubility product `K_sp` decides whether a sparingly soluble salt dissolves or precipitates.

---

## Best Final Line for Answers

Equilibrium is a balance of rates, not a halt of reaction; its constant composition can be measured by `K`, disturbed predictably by Le Chatelier's principle, and applied to acids, bases, buffers, and solubility through ionic equilibrium.
