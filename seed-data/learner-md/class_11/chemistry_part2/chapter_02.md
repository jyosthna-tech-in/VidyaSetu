# Chapter 2: Redox Reactions
### NCERT Class 11 - Chemistry Part 2

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter teaches one of the most important patterns in chemistry:

**Oxidation and reduction always occur together.**

A reaction in which one species loses electrons and another species gains electrons is called a redox reaction. The name redox comes from **reduction + oxidation**.

The chapter begins with older classical meanings of oxidation and reduction, then improves the idea using electron transfer, then gives the most useful method for covalent compounds: oxidation number.

The chapter also teaches how to:

- identify oxidising and reducing agents,
- assign oxidation numbers,
- classify redox reactions,
- balance redox equations,
- understand redox titrations,
- connect redox reactions to electrode processes and galvanic cells.

Redox reactions are not just a classroom topic. They explain burning of fuels, extraction of metals, batteries, corrosion, bleaching, respiration, photosynthesis, and many industrial processes.

# 1. Why Redox Reactions Matter

Chemistry studies matter and the transformation of one kind of matter into another. Many important transformations are redox reactions.

Redox reactions are involved in:

- burning of fuels,
- respiration,
- photosynthesis,
- corrosion of metals,
- extraction of metals,
- batteries,
- manufacture of caustic soda,
- electrochemical processes,
- bleaching,
- environmental chemistry,
- ozone-related reactions,
- hydrogen as a fuel.

The chapter's key message is:

**Where there is oxidation, there is always reduction.**

You cannot have one without the other because electrons lost by one species must be gained by another.

---

# 2. Classical Idea of Oxidation and Reduction

The meanings of oxidation and reduction developed gradually.

At first, oxidation was connected only with oxygen. Later, the definition was expanded to include hydrogen, electronegative elements, and electropositive elements.

---

## 2.1 Oxidation as Addition of Oxygen

Originally, oxidation meant addition of oxygen to an element or compound.

Examples:

`2Mg(s) + O2(g) -> 2MgO(s)`

Magnesium is oxidised because oxygen is added to it.

`S(s) + O2(g) -> SO2(g)`

Sulphur is oxidised because oxygen is added to it.

`CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l)`

Carbon in methane is oxidised because oxygen is added and hydrogen is effectively removed.

---

## 2.2 Oxidation as Removal of Hydrogen

Oxidation also includes removal of hydrogen from a substance.

Example:

`2H2S(g) + O2(g) -> 2S(s) + 2H2O(l)`

Hydrogen sulphide is oxidised to sulphur because hydrogen is removed from sulphur.

---

## 2.3 Oxidation as Addition of Electronegative Element

The definition was expanded beyond oxygen. Addition of a more electronegative element also counts as oxidation.

Examples:

`Mg(s) + F2(g) -> MgF2(s)`

`Mg(s) + Cl2(g) -> MgCl2(s)`

`Mg(s) + S(s) -> MgS(s)`

Magnesium is oxidised because fluorine, chlorine, and sulphur are more electronegative than magnesium.

---

## 2.4 Oxidation as Removal of Electropositive Element

Oxidation can also mean removal of an electropositive element.

Example from the chapter:

`2K4[Fe(CN)6](aq) + H2O2(aq) -> 2K3[Fe(CN)6](aq) + 2KOH(aq)`

Potassium ferrocyanide changes to potassium ferricyanide. The removal of electropositive potassium is interpreted as oxidation.

---

## Classical Definition of Oxidation

Oxidation is:

- addition of oxygen,
- addition of electronegative element,
- removal of hydrogen,
- removal of electropositive element.

---

## 2.5 Classical Reduction

Reduction was first understood as removal of oxygen.

Example:

`2HgO(s) -> 2Hg(l) + O2(g)`

Mercury oxide is reduced because oxygen is removed.

Reduction also includes:

- removal of electronegative element,
- addition of hydrogen,
- addition of electropositive element.

Examples:

`2FeCl3(aq) + H2(g) -> 2FeCl2(aq) + 2HCl(aq)`

Ferric chloride is reduced because chlorine is removed.

`CH2=CH2(g) + H2(g) -> CH3-CH3(g)`

Ethene is reduced because hydrogen is added.

---

## Classical Definition of Reduction

Reduction is:

- removal of oxygen,
- removal of electronegative element,
- addition of hydrogen,
- addition of electropositive element.

---

## Why Classical Definitions Are Not Enough

Classical definitions are useful, but limited.

They work well for reactions involving:

- oxygen,
- hydrogen,
- strongly electronegative elements,
- strongly electropositive elements.

But many reactions do not clearly involve oxygen or hydrogen. Also, many covalent reactions involve electron shifts rather than complete electron transfer.

This is why the electron-transfer definition and oxidation-number method are needed.

---

# 3. Oxidation and Reduction Occur Together

In every redox reaction:

- one species is oxidised,
- another species is reduced.

If one species loses something, another gains something. If one species loses electrons, another gains electrons.

This is why oxidation and reduction are inseparable.

---

## Example: Hydrogen Sulphide and Chlorine

`H2S(g) + Cl2(g) -> 2HCl(g) + S(s)`

`H2S` is oxidised because hydrogen is removed from sulphur.

`Cl2` is reduced because hydrogen is added to chlorine.

---

## Example: Thermite-Type Reaction

`3Fe3O4(s) + 8Al(s) -> 9Fe(s) + 4Al2O3(s)`

Aluminium is oxidised because oxygen is added to it.

Iron oxide is reduced because oxygen is removed from it.

---

## Example: Sodium Hydride Formation

`2Na(s) + H2(g) -> 2NaH(s)`

This looks unusual if you only think in terms of oxygen. But it is redox:

- sodium becomes `Na+`, so sodium is oxidised,
- hydrogen becomes `H-`, so hydrogen is reduced.

This example leads naturally to the electron-transfer definition.

---

# 4. Redox in Terms of Electron Transfer

## Core Definitions

| Process | Electron Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Oxidation | Loss of electrons |
| Reduction | Gain of electrons |
| Oxidising agent | Electron acceptor |
| Reducing agent | Electron donor |

Memory aid:

**OIL RIG**

- Oxidation Is Loss
- Reduction Is Gain

---

## Example: Sodium Chloride Formation

Overall reaction:

`2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2NaCl(s)`

This can be split into half reactions.

Oxidation half reaction:

`2Na(s) -> 2Na+(g) + 2e-`

Reduction half reaction:

`Cl2(g) + 2e- -> 2Cl-(g)`

Overall:

`2Na(s) + Cl2(g) -> 2Na+Cl-(s)`

Sodium loses electrons, so sodium is oxidised.

Chlorine gains electrons, so chlorine is reduced.

---

## Half Reactions

A half reaction shows only one side of the electron-transfer process.

| Half Reaction Type | What It Shows |
| --- | --- |
| Oxidation half reaction | Electrons produced on product side. |
| Reduction half reaction | Electrons consumed on reactant side. |

Examples:

Oxidation:

`Zn(s) -> Zn2+(aq) + 2e-`

Reduction:

`Cu2+(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)`

---

## Oxidising and Reducing Agents

This is a common source of confusion.

| Species | What It Does | What Happens to It |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Oxidising agent | Oxidises another species by accepting electrons. | Gets reduced. |
| Reducing agent | Reduces another species by donating electrons. | Gets oxidised. |

In:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

- `Zn` loses electrons, so it is oxidised.
- `Zn` donates electrons to `Cu2+`, so it is the reducing agent.
- `Cu2+` gains electrons, so it is reduced.
- `Cu2+` accepts electrons from `Zn`, so it is the oxidising agent.

---

# 5. Competitive Electron Transfer

## Zinc and Copper Ions

When a zinc strip is placed in copper nitrate or copper sulphate solution:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

Observations:

- reddish-brown copper deposits on zinc,
- blue colour of `Cu2+` solution fades,
- zinc goes into solution as `Zn2+`.

Meaning:

- zinc has stronger tendency to lose electrons than copper,
- copper ions accept electrons and form copper metal.

---

## Copper and Zinc Ions

If copper metal is placed in zinc sulphate solution, no visible reaction occurs.

`Cu(s) + Zn2+(aq) -> Cu2+(aq) + Zn(s)` does not proceed appreciably.

This shows that zinc has a greater electron-releasing tendency than copper.

---

## Copper and Silver Ions

When copper metal is placed in silver nitrate solution:

`Cu(s) + 2Ag+(aq) -> Cu2+(aq) + 2Ag(s)`

Copper is oxidised to `Cu2+`.

Silver ions are reduced to silver metal.

This shows copper has a greater tendency to lose electrons than silver.

---

## Metal Activity Order from Examples

From these observations:

`Zn > Cu > Ag`

This means:

- zinc releases electrons more easily than copper,
- copper releases electrons more easily than silver.

This idea leads to the electrochemical series and galvanic cells.

---

# 6. Oxidation Number

## Why Oxidation Number Is Needed

Some redox reactions do not involve complete electron transfer.

Example:

`2H2(g) + O2(g) -> 2H2O(l)`

Water is covalent. Hydrogen does not fully transfer electrons to oxygen the way sodium transfers electrons to chlorine. But oxygen is more electronegative, so the shared electrons are closer to oxygen.

To track this shift, chemists use oxidation number.

---

## Meaning

Oxidation number is the apparent charge assigned to an atom in a compound or ion, assuming that bonding electrons belong completely to the more electronegative atom.

It is a book-keeping device.

It helps us decide:

- which atom is oxidised,
- which atom is reduced,
- which species is oxidising agent,
- which species is reducing agent.

---

## Oxidation Number Versus Real Charge

Oxidation number is not always a real charge.

For ionic compounds, it may match real ionic charge.

For covalent compounds, it is often only an assigned number.

Example:

In `H2O`, hydrogen is assigned `+1` and oxygen `-2`, but water does not contain free `H+` and `O2-` ions.

---

# 7. Rules for Assigning Oxidation Number

## Rule 1: Free Elements

An element in free or uncombined state has oxidation number `0`.

Examples:

`H2`, `O2`, `Cl2`, `O3`, `P4`, `S8`, `Na`, `Mg`, `Al`

Each atom has oxidation number `0`.

---

## Rule 2: Monoatomic Ions

For a monoatomic ion, oxidation number equals ionic charge.

Examples:

| Ion | Oxidation Number |
| --- | ---: |
| `Na+` | `+1` |
| `Mg2+` | `+2` |
| `Fe3+` | `+3` |
| `Cl-` | `-1` |
| `O2-` | `-2` |

---

## Rule 3: Alkali, Alkaline Earth, and Aluminium

| Element Group | Oxidation Number in Compounds |
| --- | ---: |
| Alkali metals | `+1` |
| Alkaline earth metals | `+2` |
| Aluminium | `+3` |

Examples:

- `Na` in `NaCl` is `+1`.
- `Ca` in `CaCl2` is `+2`.
- `Al` in `Al2O3` is `+3`.

---

## Rule 4: Oxygen

Oxygen is usually `-2`.

Examples:

- `H2O`: oxygen is `-2`
- `CO2`: oxygen is `-2`
- `MgO`: oxygen is `-2`

Exceptions:

| Compound Type | Oxygen Oxidation Number |
| --- | ---: |
| Peroxides, such as `H2O2`, `Na2O2` | `-1` |
| Superoxides, such as `KO2`, `RbO2` | `-1/2` |
| `OF2` | `+2` |
| `O2F2` | `+1` |

Oxygen becomes positive with fluorine because fluorine is more electronegative than oxygen.

---

## Rule 5: Hydrogen

Hydrogen is usually `+1`.

Examples:

- `HCl`: hydrogen is `+1`
- `H2O`: hydrogen is `+1`
- `NH3`: hydrogen is `+1`

Exception:

In metal hydrides, hydrogen is `-1`.

Examples:

- `LiH`
- `NaH`
- `CaH2`

---

## Rule 6: Halogens

Fluorine is always `-1` in its compounds.

Chlorine, bromine, and iodine are usually `-1` as halide ions.

But chlorine, bromine, and iodine can have positive oxidation numbers when combined with oxygen or fluorine.

Examples:

- `Cl-`: chlorine is `-1`
- `HClO`: chlorine is `+1`
- `ClO3-`: chlorine is `+5`
- `ClO4-`: chlorine is `+7`

---

## Rule 7: Sum Rule

For a neutral compound:

**sum of oxidation numbers = 0**

For a polyatomic ion:

**sum of oxidation numbers = charge on the ion**

Examples:

In `CO3^2-`:

Let carbon be `x`.

Oxygen total = `3(-2) = -6`

`x - 6 = -2`

`x = +4`

Carbon is `+4`.

---

# 8. Worked Oxidation Number Examples

## Example 1: `H2SO4`

Let sulphur be `x`.

Hydrogen: `2(+1) = +2`

Oxygen: `4(-2) = -8`

Neutral compound:

`+2 + x - 8 = 0`

`x = +6`

Sulphur is `+6`.

---

## Example 2: `Cr2O7^2-`

Let chromium be `x`.

Oxygen: `7(-2) = -14`

Ion charge = `-2`

`2x - 14 = -2`

`2x = +12`

`x = +6`

Chromium is `+6`.

---

## Example 3: `NO3-`

Let nitrogen be `x`.

Oxygen: `3(-2) = -6`

Ion charge = `-1`

`x - 6 = -1`

`x = +5`

Nitrogen is `+5`.

---

## Example 4: `NaBH4`

Sodium is `+1`.

Hydrogen in borohydride behaves as hydride, so hydrogen is `-1`.

Let boron be `x`.

`+1 + x + 4(-1) = 0`

`x = +3`

Boron is `+3`.

---

## Example 5: `CaO2`

This is a peroxide.

Calcium is `+2`.

Total oxygen oxidation number must be `-2`.

Each oxygen is:

`-1`

---

# 9. Stock Notation

Stock notation shows the oxidation state of a metal using Roman numerals.

Examples:

| Traditional/Formula | Stock Notation Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `FeO` | Iron(II) oxide, `Fe` is `+2` |
| `Fe2O3` | Iron(III) oxide, `Fe` is `+3` |
| `CuI` | Copper(I) iodide, `Cu` is `+1` |
| `CuO` | Copper(II) oxide, `Cu` is `+2` |
| `SnCl2` | Tin(II) chloride, `Sn` is `+2` |
| `SnCl4` | Tin(IV) chloride, `Sn` is `+4` |
| `MnO2` | Manganese(IV) oxide, `Mn` is `+4` |

Stock notation is especially useful for transition metals because they can show multiple oxidation states.

# 10. Redox in Terms of Oxidation Number

## Definitions

| Process | Oxidation Number Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Oxidation | Increase in oxidation number. |
| Reduction | Decrease in oxidation number. |
| Oxidising agent | Species that increases oxidation number of another species; itself gets reduced. |
| Reducing agent | Species that lowers oxidation number of another species; itself gets oxidised. |

---

## Example: Copper Oxide and Copper Sulphide

Reaction:

`2Cu2O(s) + Cu2S(s) -> 6Cu(s) + SO2(g)`

Oxidation states:

- Cu in `Cu2O` is `+1`.
- Cu in `Cu2S` is also `+1`.
- Cu in elemental copper is `0`.
- S in `Cu2S` is `-2`.
- S in `SO2` is `+4`.

Changes:

- copper: `+1 -> 0`, reduced,
- sulphur: `-2 -> +4`, oxidised.

Therefore:

- copper species are reduced,
- sulphur in `Cu2S` is oxidised,
- `Cu2O` acts as oxidising agent,
- sulphur in `Cu2S` acts as reducing agent.

---

# 11. Fractional Oxidation Numbers

Sometimes an element appears to have a fractional oxidation number.

Examples:

- carbon in `C3O2` has average oxidation number `+4/3`,
- bromine in `Br3O8` has average oxidation number `+16/3`,
- sulphur in `S4O6^2-` has average oxidation number `+2.5`.

This does not mean electrons are transferred in fractions.

It means the element exists in different oxidation states at different positions in the structure, and the calculated value is only an average.

---

## Example: Tetrathionate Ion

In `S4O6^2-`, not all sulphur atoms are equivalent.

Two sulphur atoms may have higher oxidation state, while the middle sulphur atoms may have oxidation state zero.

The average becomes fractional, but the real structural picture is not fractional.

---

# 12. Types of Redox Reactions

Redox reactions are classified into four broad types:

1. Combination reactions
2. Decomposition reactions
3. Displacement reactions
4. Disproportionation reactions

---

## 12.1 Combination Reactions

General form:

`A + B -> C`

A combination reaction is redox if at least one reactant is in elemental form and oxidation numbers change.

Examples:

`C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)`

Carbon changes from `0` to `+4`.

Oxygen changes from `0` to `-2`.

`3Mg(s) + N2(g) -> Mg3N2(s)`

Magnesium changes from `0` to `+2`.

Nitrogen changes from `0` to `-3`.

`CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l)`

Carbon changes from `-4` in methane to `+4` in carbon dioxide.

Oxygen changes from `0` to `-2`.

---

## 12.2 Decomposition Reactions

General idea:

A compound breaks into two or more substances.

A decomposition reaction is redox if at least one product is in elemental form and oxidation numbers change.

Examples:

`2H2O(l) -> 2H2(g) + O2(g)`

Hydrogen changes from `+1` to `0`.

Oxygen changes from `-2` to `0`.

`2NaH(s) -> 2Na(s) + H2(g)`

Sodium changes from `+1` to `0`.

Hydrogen changes from `-1` to `0`.

`2KClO3(s) -> 2KCl(s) + 3O2(g)`

Chlorine changes from `+5` to `-1`.

Oxygen changes from `-2` to `0`.

---

## Decomposition Not Always Redox

Example:

`CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g)`

Oxidation numbers:

- calcium remains `+2`,
- carbon remains `+4`,
- oxygen remains `-2`.

No oxidation number changes, so it is not redox.

---

## 12.3 Displacement Reactions

General form:

`X + YZ -> XZ + Y`

One element displaces another from a compound.

Displacement reactions are of two main types:

- metal displacement,
- non-metal displacement.

---

## Metal Displacement

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Example:

`Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) -> ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)`

Ionic form:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

Zinc is oxidised.

Copper ion is reduced.

---

## Hydrogen Displacement from Water

Very reactive metals displace hydrogen from cold water.

Examples:

`2Na(s) + 2H2O(l) -> 2NaOH(aq) + H2(g)`

`Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)`

Less reactive metals such as magnesium and iron react with steam.

`Mg(s) + 2H2O(g) -> Mg(OH)2(s) + H2(g)`

`2Fe(s) + 3H2O(g) -> Fe2O3(s) + 3H2(g)`

---

## Hydrogen Displacement from Acids

Many metals displace hydrogen from acids.

Examples:

`Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)`

`Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)`

`Fe(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> FeCl2(aq) + H2(g)`

The rate of hydrogen evolution reflects metal activity:

`Mg > Zn > Fe`

Very unreactive metals such as silver and gold do not react with hydrochloric acid.

---

## Non-Metal Displacement: Halogens

Halogens show oxidising power:

`F2 > Cl2 > Br2 > I2`

Fluorine is the strongest oxidising agent among halogens.

More reactive halogens displace less reactive halide ions.

Examples:

`Cl2 + 2Br- -> 2Cl- + Br2`

`Br2 + 2I- -> 2Br- + I2`

Fluorine is so reactive that it attacks water and cannot be prepared from `F-` by ordinary chemical oxidants. It is obtained by electrolysis.

---

## 12.4 Disproportionation Reactions

In a disproportionation reaction, the same element in the same substance is both oxidised and reduced.

The element must be in an intermediate oxidation state so that it can move both upward and downward.

---

## Example: Hydrogen Peroxide

`2H2O2(aq) -> 2H2O(l) + O2(g)`

Oxidation state of oxygen:

- in `H2O2`: `-1`,
- in `H2O`: `-2`,
- in `O2`: `0`.

Oxygen is both reduced (`-1 -> -2`) and oxidised (`-1 -> 0`).

So this is disproportionation.

---

## Example: Chlorine in Alkali

`Cl2(g) + 2OH-(aq) -> ClO-(aq) + Cl-(aq) + H2O(l)`

Chlorine changes:

- from `0` in `Cl2`,
- to `+1` in `ClO-`,
- to `-1` in `Cl-`.

Chlorine is both oxidised and reduced.

This reaction is related to formation of household bleaching agents.

---

# 13. Balancing Redox Reactions

Redox equations must be balanced for:

- atoms,
- charge,
- electrons lost and gained.

Two methods are used:

1. Oxidation number method
2. Ion-electron method

---

# 14. Oxidation Number Method

## Steps

1. Write correct formulas for reactants and products.
2. Assign oxidation numbers to all relevant elements.
3. Identify atoms whose oxidation numbers change.
4. Calculate increase and decrease in oxidation number.
5. Make total increase equal to total decrease.
6. Balance remaining atoms other than H and O.
7. Balance O by adding `H2O`.
8. Balance H by adding `H+` in acidic medium.
9. In basic medium, add `OH-` to neutralise `H+`.
10. Check atoms and charges.

---

## Example: Dichromate and Sulphite in Acidic Medium

Skeletal equation:

`Cr2O7^2- + SO3^2- -> Cr3+ + SO4^2-`

Oxidation changes:

- chromium: `+6 -> +3`, reduction,
- sulphur: `+4 -> +6`, oxidation.

Each chromium decreases by 3. Since there are two chromium atoms, total decrease is 6.

Each sulphur increases by 2. Need 3 sulphite ions to give total increase 6.

Balanced ionic equation:

`Cr2O7^2-(aq) + 3SO3^2-(aq) + 8H+(aq) -> 2Cr3+(aq) + 3SO4^2-(aq) + 4H2O(l)`

---

## Example: Permanganate and Bromide in Basic Medium

Skeletal equation:

`MnO4- + Br- -> MnO2 + BrO3-`

Oxidation changes:

- manganese: `+7 -> +4`, reduction by 3,
- bromine: `-1 -> +5`, oxidation by 6.

Two permanganate ions are needed for one bromide ion to balance electron change.

The final balanced equation in basic medium is obtained by balancing O and H using `H2O` and `OH-`.

The essential lesson:

**In basic medium, do not leave `H+` in the final equation. Convert `H+` and `OH-` to water and simplify.**

---

# 15. Ion-Electron Method

This method is also called the half-reaction method.

It is very reliable for ionic redox reactions.

---

## Steps in Acidic Medium

1. Write the skeletal ionic equation.
2. Separate into oxidation and reduction half reactions.
3. Balance atoms other than H and O.
4. Balance oxygen by adding `H2O`.
5. Balance hydrogen by adding `H+`.
6. Balance charge by adding electrons.
7. Multiply half reactions to equalise electrons.
8. Add half reactions and cancel electrons.
9. Check atoms and charges.

---

## Example: Dichromate Oxidising Ferrous Ion

Skeletal equation:

`Fe2+ + Cr2O7^2- -> Fe3+ + Cr3+`

Oxidation half:

`Fe2+ -> Fe3+ + e-`

Reduction half:

`Cr2O7^2- -> Cr3+`

Balance chromium:

`Cr2O7^2- -> 2Cr3+`

Balance oxygen with water:

`Cr2O7^2- -> 2Cr3+ + 7H2O`

Balance hydrogen with `H+`:

`Cr2O7^2- + 14H+ -> 2Cr3+ + 7H2O`

Balance charge with electrons:

Left charge: `-2 + 14 = +12`

Right charge: `+6`

Add 6 electrons to left:

`Cr2O7^2- + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr3+ + 7H2O`

Multiply iron half reaction by 6:

`6Fe2+ -> 6Fe3+ + 6e-`

Add:

`6Fe2+ + Cr2O7^2- + 14H+ -> 6Fe3+ + 2Cr3+ + 7H2O`

---

## Steps in Basic Medium

Use the same steps as acidic medium first. Then:

1. Add equal number of `OH-` ions to both sides to neutralise every `H+`.
2. Combine `H+ + OH-` into `H2O`.
3. Cancel extra water molecules if present on both sides.
4. Check atoms and charges.

---

## Example: Permanganate and Iodide in Basic Medium

Skeletal equation:

`MnO4- + I- -> MnO2 + I2`

Oxidation half:

`2I- -> I2 + 2e-`

Reduction half in basic medium:

`MnO4- + 2H2O + 3e- -> MnO2 + 4OH-`

Equalise electrons:

Oxidation half x 3:

`6I- -> 3I2 + 6e-`

Reduction half x 2:

`2MnO4- + 4H2O + 6e- -> 2MnO2 + 8OH-`

Add:

`6I- + 2MnO4- + 4H2O -> 3I2 + 2MnO2 + 8OH-`

This is the balanced ionic equation.

---

# 16. Redox Titrations

Redox titrations are used to determine the strength of an oxidising or reducing agent.

Like acid-base titrations use pH indicators, redox titrations use colour changes caused by redox reactions.

---

## 16.1 Self-Indicator: Permanganate

`MnO4-` is intensely coloured.

In acidic solution, it acts as an oxidising agent.

During titration, the purple/pink colour disappears as long as reducing agent is present.

The endpoint is reached when the first permanent pale pink colour remains.

This happens because all reductant has been consumed and a tiny excess of permanganate remains.

---

## 16.2 External or Redox Indicator

Dichromate `Cr2O7^2-` is not a self-indicator.

Diphenylamine may be used as an indicator. Near the equivalence point, dichromate oxidises the indicator and produces an intense blue colour.

---

## 16.3 Iodine-Starch Method

Some oxidising agents oxidise iodide ions to iodine.

Example:

`2Cu2+(aq) + 4I-(aq) -> Cu2I2(s) + I2(aq)`

Iodine gives an intense blue colour with starch.

Iodine can be titrated with thiosulphate:

`I2(aq) + 2S2O3^2-(aq) -> 2I-(aq) + S4O6^2-(aq)`

The blue starch-iodine colour disappears when iodine is consumed.

---

# 17. Limitations of Oxidation Number Concept

Oxidation number is very useful, but it is a formal book-keeping concept.

Its limitations:

- it may not represent real charge,
- fractional oxidation numbers are averages,
- covalent bonding often involves electron shift rather than full transfer,
- modern understanding uses electron density changes.

Modern view:

- oxidation means decrease in electron density around an atom,
- reduction means increase in electron density around an atom.

---

# 18. Redox Reactions and Electrode Processes

## Direct Electron Transfer

In:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

if zinc is placed directly in copper sulphate solution, electrons are transferred directly from zinc to copper ions.

Heat is evolved, but electrical energy is not usefully collected.

---

## Indirect Electron Transfer

If zinc and copper systems are separated into two half cells and connected by a wire, electrons flow through the wire.

This produces electric current.

The setup is a galvanic cell.

---

## Daniell Cell

The Daniell cell uses:

- zinc rod in zinc sulphate solution,
- copper rod in copper sulphate solution,
- external wire,
- salt bridge.

Overall reaction:

`Zn(s) + Cu2+(aq) -> Zn2+(aq) + Cu(s)`

Oxidation at zinc electrode:

`Zn(s) -> Zn2+(aq) + 2e-`

Reduction at copper electrode:

`Cu2+(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)`

---

## Role of Salt Bridge

The salt bridge:

- completes the internal circuit,
- allows ions to migrate,
- maintains electrical neutrality,
- prevents direct mixing of the two solutions.

It usually contains an inert electrolyte such as potassium chloride or ammonium nitrate, often in a gel.

---

## Electron Flow and Current

In Daniell cell:

- electrons flow from zinc to copper through the external wire,
- zinc acts as anode,
- copper acts as cathode,
- conventional current direction is opposite to electron flow.

Remember:

Oxidation occurs at anode.

Reduction occurs at cathode.

---

## Redox Couple

A redox couple contains oxidised and reduced forms of the same substance.

Examples:

`Zn2+/Zn`

`Cu2+/Cu`

The oxidised form is written before the reduced form.

The slash or vertical line represents the interface between forms, such as solution/solid.

---

# 19. Standard Electrode Potential

## Meaning

Electrode potential is the potential associated with an electrode process.

Standard electrode potential is measured when:

- concentration of aqueous species is `1 M`,
- gases are at `1 atm` pressure,
- temperature is `298 K`.

The standard hydrogen electrode is assigned:

`E^0 = 0.00 V`

---

## Interpreting `E^0`

The table gives electrode potentials for reduction reactions:

`Oxidised form + ne- -> Reduced form`

Higher positive `E^0` means stronger tendency to get reduced.

So:

- high positive `E^0` means strong oxidising agent,
- highly negative `E^0` means reduced form is a strong reducing agent.

---

## Examples from the Series

| Couple | `E^0` Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `F2/F-` has very high positive value | Fluorine is a very strong oxidising agent. |
| `Ag+/Ag` positive | Silver ion is easily reduced compared with hydrogen ion. |
| `Zn2+/Zn` negative | Zinc metal is a good reducing agent. |
| `Li+/Li` very negative | Lithium metal is a very strong reducing agent. |

---

## Negative and Positive Values

| `E^0` Sign | Interpretation |
| --- | --- |
| Positive `E^0` | Oxidised form has greater tendency to be reduced than `H+`. |
| Negative `E^0` | Reduced form has greater tendency to lose electrons than hydrogen. |

The NCERT note states:

- a negative `E^0` means the redox couple is a stronger reducing agent than `H+/H2`,
- a positive `E^0` means the redox couple is a weaker reducing agent than `H+/H2`.

---

# 20. How to Identify Oxidation, Reduction, Oxidant, Reductant

## Step-by-Step Method

1. Assign oxidation numbers to elements that may change.
2. Compare oxidation numbers before and after reaction.
3. Increase in oxidation number means oxidation.
4. Decrease in oxidation number means reduction.
5. Species oxidised is the reducing agent.
6. Species reduced is the oxidising agent.

---

## Example: `CuO + H2 -> Cu + H2O`

Oxidation numbers:

- Cu in `CuO`: `+2`
- Cu in `Cu`: `0`
- H in `H2`: `0`
- H in `H2O`: `+1`

Changes:

- Cu: `+2 -> 0`, reduced,
- H: `0 -> +1`, oxidised.

Therefore:

- `CuO` is oxidising agent,
- `H2` is reducing agent.

---

## Example: `Fe2O3 + 3CO -> 2Fe + 3CO2`

Oxidation numbers:

- Fe in `Fe2O3`: `+3`
- Fe in `Fe`: `0`
- C in `CO`: `+2`
- C in `CO2`: `+4`

Changes:

- Fe: `+3 -> 0`, reduced,
- C: `+2 -> +4`, oxidised.

Therefore:

- `Fe2O3` is oxidising agent,
- `CO` is reducing agent.

---

## Example: `4NH3 + 5O2 -> 4NO + 6H2O`

Oxidation numbers:

- N in `NH3`: `-3`
- N in `NO`: `+2`
- O in `O2`: `0`
- O in products: `-2`

Changes:

- nitrogen: `-3 -> +2`, oxidised,
- oxygen: `0 -> -2`, reduced.

Therefore:

- `NH3` is reducing agent,
- `O2` is oxidising agent.

---

# 21. High-Quality Answer Frameworks

## Framework A: Proving a Reaction Is Redox

Use this structure:

1. Assign oxidation numbers.
2. Identify increase and decrease.
3. Name oxidised and reduced species.
4. Name oxidising and reducing agents.
5. Conclude that both processes occur simultaneously.

### Model Answer

The reaction is redox because oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously. On assigning oxidation numbers, the element whose oxidation number increases is oxidised, while the element whose oxidation number decreases is reduced. The species oxidised acts as reducing agent because it donates electrons, and the species reduced acts as oxidising agent because it accepts electrons.

---

## Framework B: Classical Versus Electronic Definitions

Use this structure:

1. State classical oxidation and reduction.
2. State electronic definitions.
3. Explain why electronic definition is wider.
4. Give example.

### Model Answer

Classically, oxidation means addition of oxygen or electronegative element, or removal of hydrogen or electropositive element. Reduction means removal of oxygen or electronegative element, or addition of hydrogen or electropositive element. The electronic definition is broader: oxidation is loss of electrons and reduction is gain of electrons. For example, in formation of sodium chloride, sodium loses electrons and chlorine gains electrons, so the reaction is redox even when explained through electron transfer.

---

## Framework C: Oxidation Number

Use this structure:

1. Define oxidation number.
2. Mention it is a formal book-keeping device.
3. Explain increase/decrease.
4. Use example.

### Model Answer

Oxidation number is the apparent charge assigned to an atom in a compound or ion by assuming that bonding electrons belong completely to the more electronegative atom. It is useful for tracking electron shifts in reactions, especially covalent reactions. Increase in oxidation number means oxidation, while decrease in oxidation number means reduction. Thus oxidation numbers help identify oxidant and reductant.

---

## Framework D: Disproportionation

Use this structure:

1. Define disproportionation.
2. Say same element is both oxidised and reduced.
3. Mention intermediate oxidation state.
4. Give example.

### Model Answer

Disproportionation is a redox reaction in which the same element in one oxidation state is simultaneously oxidised and reduced. The reacting species usually contains the element in an intermediate oxidation state, so it can move to both higher and lower states. For example, in `2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2`, oxygen changes from `-1` in hydrogen peroxide to `-2` in water and `0` in oxygen, so it is both reduced and oxidised.

---

## Framework E: Daniell Cell

Use this structure:

1. State overall reaction.
2. Give oxidation and reduction half reactions.
3. Explain electron flow.
4. Explain salt bridge.
5. State redox couples.

### Model Answer

In a Daniell cell, zinc is oxidised and copper ion is reduced. The oxidation half reaction is `Zn -> Zn2+ + 2e-`, and the reduction half reaction is `Cu2+ + 2e- -> Cu`. Electrons flow from zinc electrode to copper electrode through the external wire. The salt bridge completes the internal circuit and maintains electrical neutrality. The two redox couples are `Zn2+/Zn` and `Cu2+/Cu`.

---

# 22. Common Exam Tasks and How to Handle Them

## Task 1: Assign Oxidation Number

Use the rules in this order:

1. Free elements = `0`.
2. Monoatomic ions = ionic charge.
3. Alkali metals = `+1`.
4. Alkaline earth metals = `+2`.
5. Oxygen usually `-2`, with exceptions.
6. Hydrogen usually `+1`, metal hydrides `-1`.
7. Fluorine always `-1`.
8. Sum equals total charge.

---

## Task 2: Identify Oxidant and Reductant

Remember:

- oxidant gets reduced,
- reductant gets oxidised.

Do not identify them only by oxygen/hydrogen words. Use oxidation number change.

---

## Task 3: Balance Redox Equation

For ionic equations, the ion-electron method is usually safer.

In acidic medium:

- balance O with `H2O`,
- balance H with `H+`,
- balance charge with electrons.

In basic medium:

- first balance as acidic,
- add `OH-` to remove `H+`,
- simplify water.

---

## Task 4: Identify Disproportionation

Ask:

- Does the same element appear in both higher and lower oxidation states after reaction?
- Was it in an intermediate oxidation state before reaction?

If yes, it is disproportionation.

---

## Task 5: Interpret Electrode Potentials

Use reduction potential table:

- more positive reduction potential means stronger oxidising tendency of oxidised form,
- more negative reduction potential means stronger reducing tendency of reduced form.

# 23. Common Mistakes to Avoid

## Mistake 1: Saying oxidation always means oxygen addition

Correct idea:

Oxygen addition is only the classical meaning. Oxidation is best understood as electron loss or increase in oxidation number.

---

## Mistake 2: Confusing oxidising agent with oxidised substance

Correct idea:

The oxidising agent causes oxidation but itself gets reduced.

---

## Mistake 3: Confusing reducing agent with reduced substance

Correct idea:

The reducing agent causes reduction but itself gets oxidised.

---

## Mistake 4: Forgetting exceptions for oxygen and hydrogen

Correct idea:

Oxygen is `-1` in peroxides, `-1/2` in superoxides, and positive with fluorine. Hydrogen is `-1` in metal hydrides.

---

## Mistake 5: Treating fractional oxidation number as real

Correct idea:

Fractional oxidation number is usually an average over atoms in different structural environments.

---

## Mistake 6: Leaving electrons in final balanced equation

Correct idea:

Electrons must cancel when oxidation and reduction half reactions are added.

---

## Mistake 7: Leaving `H+` in basic medium

Correct idea:

In basic medium, add `OH-` to neutralise `H+` and simplify water.

---

## Mistake 8: Forgetting charge balance

Correct idea:

A balanced redox equation must balance atoms and total charge.

---

# 25. One-Page Exam Revision

Redox reactions are reactions in which oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously. Classically, oxidation means addition of oxygen/electronegative element or removal of hydrogen/electropositive element. Reduction means removal of oxygen/electronegative element or addition of hydrogen/electropositive element. In electronic terms, oxidation is loss of electrons and reduction is gain of electrons. The oxidising agent accepts electrons and gets reduced, while the reducing agent donates electrons and gets oxidised.

Oxidation number is used to track electron shift, especially in covalent compounds. Increase in oxidation number means oxidation and decrease means reduction. Free elements have oxidation number zero; monoatomic ions have oxidation number equal to their charge; oxygen is usually `-2`; hydrogen is usually `+1`; fluorine is always `-1`; and the sum of oxidation numbers equals the total charge.

Redox reactions are classified as combination, decomposition, displacement, and disproportionation reactions. In disproportionation, the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced. Redox equations are balanced by oxidation number method or ion-electron method. In acidic medium, use `H+` and `H2O`; in basic medium, remove `H+` using `OH-`.

Redox reactions are used in titrations. Permanganate can act as a self-indicator, dichromate may use diphenylamine, and iodine-starch colour change is useful in iodometric methods. Redox also explains electrode processes. In a Daniell cell, zinc is oxidised at the anode and copper ions are reduced at the cathode. Electrons flow through the external wire, while ions move through the salt bridge. Standard electrode potentials compare the tendencies of species to get reduced or oxidised.

---

## Best Final Line for Answers

A redox reaction is best understood as a linked electron-transfer process: the species that loses electrons is oxidised and acts as the reducing agent, while the species that gains electrons is reduced and acts as the oxidising agent.
