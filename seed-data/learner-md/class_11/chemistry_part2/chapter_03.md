This chapter builds the foundation for organic chemistry.

Organic chemistry is mainly the chemistry of carbon compounds. Carbon forms a huge number of compounds because it is tetravalent, forms strong covalent bonds, and can link with itself to form chains, branches, rings, and complex frameworks. This property is called catenation.

The chapter teaches four major skills:

1. How organic molecules are structured and represented.
2. How organic compounds are classified and named.
3. How organic reactions are understood through mechanisms and electronic effects.
4. How organic compounds are purified and analysed.

The central idea is:

**Organic chemistry is not memorising random compounds; it is understanding how structure, bonding, electron movement, functional groups, and reaction conditions control properties and reactions.**

# 1. General Introduction to Organic Chemistry

## Why Carbon Compounds Are Special

Carbon forms an enormous number of compounds because:

- carbon is tetravalent,
- carbon forms strong covalent bonds,
- carbon can bond with other carbon atoms,
- carbon forms chains, branches, and rings,
- carbon bonds with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur, phosphorus, and halogens,
- carbon can form single, double, and triple bonds.

This diversity makes organic chemistry a separate and very large branch of chemistry.

---

## Importance of Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are present in:

- DNA,
- proteins,
- blood,
- muscles,
- skin,
- medicines,
- fuels,
- polymers,
- dyes,
- clothing materials,
- food substances,
- agricultural chemicals.

Organic chemistry is therefore important in biology, medicine, industry, agriculture, environment, and materials science.

---

## Historical Note: Vital Force Theory

Earlier, chemists believed that organic compounds could be formed only by living organisms due to a special "vital force".

This idea was rejected when Wohler prepared urea, an organic compound, from ammonium cyanate, an inorganic compound.

`NH4CNO -> NH2CONH2`

Ammonium cyanate gives urea on heating.

Later, Kolbe synthesised acetic acid and Berthelot synthesised methane, further proving that organic compounds could be prepared in the laboratory.

# 2. Tetravalence and Shapes of Carbon Compounds

## Tetravalence of Carbon

Carbon has four valence electrons and forms four covalent bonds to complete its octet.

This explains why carbon forms compounds such as:

- methane, `CH4`,
- ethane, `C2H6`,
- ethene, `C2H4`,
- ethyne, `C2H2`,
- methanol, `CH3OH`,
- acetic acid, `CH3COOH`.

---

## Hybridisation and Shape

Carbon can use different hybridisations.

| Hybridisation | Example | Shape Around Carbon | Bond Angle | Main Bond Type |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `sp3` | methane, `CH4` | tetrahedral | about 109.5 degrees | single bonds |
| `sp2` | ethene, `C2H4` | trigonal planar | about 120 degrees | double bond present |
| `sp` | ethyne, `C2H2` | linear | 180 degrees | triple bond present |

---

## Bond Length, Strength, and s-Character

Hybridisation affects bond length and bond strength.

| Hybrid Orbital | s-Character | Bond Length Trend | Electronegativity of Carbon |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| `sp3` | 25 percent | longest among these | lowest |
| `sp2` | 33 percent | intermediate | intermediate |
| `sp` | 50 percent | shortest | highest |

More s-character means the hybrid orbital is closer to the nucleus.

Therefore:

- `sp` carbon forms shorter and stronger bonds than `sp2`,
- `sp2` carbon forms shorter and stronger bonds than `sp3`,
- `sp` carbon is more electronegative than `sp2` and `sp3` carbon.

---

## Sigma and Pi Bonds

A single bond is a sigma bond.

A double bond contains:

- one sigma bond,
- one pi bond.

A triple bond contains:

- one sigma bond,
- two pi bonds.

---

## Features of Pi Bonds

Pi bonds form by sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals.

Important consequences:

- atoms around a double bond are generally planar,
- rotation around a double bond is restricted,
- pi electron cloud lies above and below the plane of atoms,
- pi electrons are more exposed and more easily attacked by reagents,
- multiple bonds are reactive centres in many organic reactions.

Example:

In ethene, `H2C=CH2`, the p orbitals on two carbon atoms must remain parallel. Rotation would disturb pi overlap, so free rotation around the double bond is not possible.

# 3. Structural Representation of Organic Compounds

Organic structures can be represented in several ways.

Each representation has a purpose.

---

## 3.1 Complete Structural Formula

Complete structural formula shows all atoms and all bonds.

Example:

Ethane can be represented by showing every C-H and C-C bond.

This is useful for beginners but becomes bulky for larger molecules.

---

## 3.2 Condensed Structural Formula

Condensed formula omits many bond lines but preserves connectivity.

Examples:

| Compound | Condensed Formula |
| --- | --- |
| Ethane | `CH3CH3` |
| Ethene | `CH2=CH2` |
| Ethyne | `HCCH` or `HC≡CH` in printed form |
| Methanol | `CH3OH` |
| Octane | `CH3(CH2)6CH3` |

Condensed formulas are compact but still show the order of atoms.

---

## 3.3 Bond-Line Formula

Bond-line formulas use lines to represent carbon skeletons.

Rules:

- carbon atoms are not written,
- hydrogen atoms attached to carbon are not written,
- each line end or line junction represents a carbon atom,
- enough hydrogens are assumed to complete carbon valency four,
- heteroatoms such as O, N, S, Cl, Br are written explicitly,
- multiple bonds are shown by double or triple lines.

This is the most efficient way to draw organic compounds.

---

## How to Read Bond-Line Structures

At every line end or corner:

1. place a carbon atom,
2. count how many bonds are already shown,
3. add enough hydrogens to make four bonds.

Example:

A line with three connected segments may represent a four-carbon chain.

---

## 3.4 Cyclic Compounds in Bond-Line Formula

Rings are drawn as polygons.

| Ring | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Triangle | Cyclopropane |
| Pentagon | Cyclopentane |
| Hexagon | Cyclohexane |

If a heteroatom or substituent is present, it is written explicitly.

---

## 3.5 Three-Dimensional Representation

Some organic molecules are three-dimensional. A flat drawing may not show true shape.

Wedge-dash notation is used:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Normal line | Bond lies in plane of paper. |
| Solid wedge | Bond projects out of plane toward observer. |
| Dashed wedge | Bond projects behind plane away from observer. |

This is especially important for tetrahedral carbon and stereochemistry.

---

## 3.6 Molecular Models

Three common molecular models are:

| Model | What It Shows Best |
| --- | --- |
| Framework model | Bond pattern and skeleton. |
| Ball-and-stick model | Atoms and bonds clearly. |
| Space-filling model | Relative size and volume occupied by atoms. |

Computer graphics can also be used for molecular modelling.

---

# 4. Classification of Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are classified to manage their huge number.

They can be classified by:

- carbon skeleton,
- ring/open-chain structure,
- functional group.

---

## 4.1 Acyclic or Open-Chain Compounds

These compounds have carbon atoms arranged in open chains.

They may be:

- straight-chain,
- branched-chain.

They are also called aliphatic compounds.

Examples:

- ethane, `CH3CH3`,
- isobutane,
- acetaldehyde,
- acetic acid.

---

## 4.2 Cyclic or Closed-Chain Compounds

These compounds contain rings.

They are divided into:

- alicyclic compounds,
- aromatic compounds.

---

## Alicyclic Compounds

Alicyclic compounds are cyclic compounds with properties similar to aliphatic compounds.

Examples:

- cyclopropane,
- cyclohexane,
- cyclohexene.

If the ring contains only carbon atoms, it is homocyclic.

If the ring contains an atom other than carbon, such as oxygen or nitrogen, it is heterocyclic.

Example:

Tetrahydrofuran contains oxygen in the ring.

---

## Aromatic Compounds

Aromatic compounds are special ring compounds such as benzene and related compounds.

Examples:

- benzene,
- aniline,
- naphthalene,
- toluene.

Aromatic compounds may be:

- benzenoid,
- non-benzenoid,
- heterocyclic aromatic.

Examples of heterocyclic aromatic compounds:

- furan,
- thiophene,
- pyridine.

---

## 4.3 Functional Group Classification

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms that gives an organic compound its characteristic chemical reactions.

Examples:

| Functional Group | Class |
| --- | --- |
| `-OH` | Alcohol |
| `-CHO` | Aldehyde |
| `>C=O` | Ketone |
| `-COOH` | Carboxylic acid |
| `-NH2` | Amine |
| `-X` | Halo compound |
| `-CN` | Nitrile |
| `-NO2` | Nitro compound |

Compounds with the same functional group usually undergo similar chemical reactions.

Example:

`CH3OH`, `CH3CH2OH`, and `(CH3)2CHOH` all contain `-OH`, so they show alcohol-like reactions such as liberation of hydrogen with sodium.

---

## 4.4 Homologous Series

A homologous series is a family of organic compounds:

- with the same functional group,
- represented by a general formula,
- showing similar chemical properties,
- with successive members differing by `-CH2-`.

Examples:

- alkanes,
- alkenes,
- alkynes,
- haloalkanes,
- alcohols,
- aldehydes,
- ketones,
- carboxylic acids,
- amines.

---

# 5. IUPAC Nomenclature

## Why IUPAC Naming Is Needed

Organic chemistry has millions of compounds. Common names are not enough.

IUPAC names are systematic. They allow a learner to infer structure from name and name from structure.

---

## Basic Parts of an IUPAC Name

An IUPAC name generally contains:

1. Root word: number of carbon atoms in parent chain.
2. Primary suffix: type of carbon-carbon bond.
3. Secondary suffix: principal functional group.
4. Prefixes: substituents or secondary functional groups.
5. Locants: numbers showing positions.

---

## Root Words

| Number of Carbon Atoms | Root |
| ---: | --- |
| 1 | meth |
| 2 | eth |
| 3 | prop |
| 4 | but |
| 5 | pent |
| 6 | hex |
| 7 | hept |
| 8 | oct |
| 9 | non |
| 10 | dec |

---

## Primary Suffix

| Bond Type | Suffix |
| --- | --- |
| C-C single bonds only | `-ane` |
| C=C double bond | `-ene` |
| C≡C triple bond | `-yne` |

In ASCII writing, a triple bond may be written as `C#C`.

---

## Naming Straight-Chain Alkanes

Straight-chain saturated hydrocarbons end in `-ane`.

Examples:

| Formula | IUPAC Name |
| --- | --- |
| `CH4` | methane |
| `C2H6` | ethane |
| `C3H8` | propane |
| `C4H10` | butane |
| `C5H12` | pentane |

---

## Alkyl Groups

An alkyl group is formed by removing one hydrogen from an alkane.

| Alkane | Alkyl Group |
| --- | --- |
| methane | methyl, `-CH3` |
| ethane | ethyl, `-C2H5` |
| propane | propyl |
| butane | butyl |

The suffix changes from `-ane` to `-yl`.

---

# 6. Nomenclature of Branched Alkanes

## Rule 1: Choose the Longest Chain

Select the longest continuous carbon chain as the parent chain.

If two chains have equal length, choose the one with more substituents.

---

## Rule 2: Number the Chain

Number from the end nearer to the first substituent.

The aim is to give substituents the lowest possible set of locants.

Example:

`2,5,6-trimethyloctane` is preferred over `3,4,7-trimethyloctane` because `2,5,6` is lower.

---

## Rule 3: Name Substituents

Substituents are named as prefixes.

Example:

`2-methylpentane`

means a five-carbon parent chain with a methyl group at carbon 2.

---

## Rule 4: Use di, tri, tetra for Repeated Substituents

If identical substituents repeat:

- two = di,
- three = tri,
- four = tetra,
- five = penta,
- six = hexa.

Examples:

- `2,4-dimethylpentane`,
- `2,2,4-trimethylpentane`,
- `3-ethyl-4,4-dimethylheptane`.

Numbers are separated by commas. Numbers and words are separated by hyphens.

---

## Rule 5: Alphabetical Order

Different substituents are written alphabetically.

Prefixes such as di, tri, tetra are not considered for alphabetical order.

Example:

`3-ethyl-6-methyloctane`

not:

`6-ethyl-3-methyloctane`

when the locant sets are equivalent, the substituent that comes first alphabetically gets the lower number.

---

## Rule 6: Branched Substituents

If a substituent itself is branched, number the substituent chain from the point of attachment.

The branched substituent name is placed in parentheses.

Example:

`5-(2-ethylbutyl)-3,3-dimethyldecane`

---

## Note on Common Prefixes

For substituent names:

- `iso` and `neo` are treated as part of the name in alphabetical order,
- `sec` and `tert` are not treated as part of the fundamental name for alphabetical order.

---

# 7. Nomenclature of Cyclic Compounds

Saturated monocyclic compounds are named by prefixing `cyclo` to the corresponding alkane.

Examples:

- cyclopropane,
- cyclopentane,
- cyclohexane.

If substituents are present, number the ring to give lowest locants.

Example:

`3-ethyl-1,1-dimethylcyclohexane`

is preferred over a name giving higher or incorrect substituent positions.

---

# 8. Nomenclature of Compounds with Functional Groups

## General Method

1. Identify the principal functional group.
2. Choose the longest chain containing that functional group.
3. Number the chain so the principal functional group gets the lowest possible number.
4. Use suffix for principal functional group.
5. Name other groups as prefixes.
6. Give locants for multiple bonds, functional groups, and substituents.

---

## Priority Order of Some Functional Groups

The NCERT order of decreasing priority includes:

`-COOH > -SO3H > -COOR > -COCl > -CONH2 > -CN > -CHO > >C=O > -OH > -NH2 > C=C > C#C`

Groups such as:

- alkyl `-R`,
- phenyl `C6H5-`,
- halogens,
- `-NO2`,
- alkoxy `-OR`,

are usually written as prefixes.

---

## Example: Ketone and Alcohol

If both keto and hydroxy groups are present, ketone has higher priority than alcohol.

`HOCH2(CH2)3CH2COCH3`

is named as:

`7-hydroxyheptan-2-one`

not:

`2-oxoheptan-7-ol`

because ketone is principal over alcohol.

---

## Example: Double Bond and Halogen

`BrCH2CH=CH2`

is named:

`3-bromoprop-1-ene`

The double bond gets priority in numbering over the bromo prefix.

---

## Multiple Same Functional Groups

If more than one identical functional group is present, use:

- di,
- tri,
- tetra.

Example:

`HOCH2CH2OH`

is:

`ethane-1,2-diol`

The parent alkane name is retained before the suffix when needed.

---

# 9. Nomenclature of Benzene Derivatives

## Monosubstituted Benzene

Examples:

| Compound | Common/IUPAC Style |
| --- | --- |
| `C6H5CH3` | methylbenzene, toluene |
| `C6H5OCH3` | methoxybenzene, anisole |
| `C6H5NH2` | aminobenzene, aniline |
| `C6H5NO2` | nitrobenzene |
| `C6H5Br` | bromobenzene |

---

## Disubstituted Benzene

For two substituents, positions may be:

| Positions | Common Prefix |
| --- | --- |
| 1,2 | ortho or o- |
| 1,3 | meta or m- |
| 1,4 | para or p- |

Examples:

- `1,2-dibromobenzene` = o-dibromobenzene,
- `1,3-dibromobenzene` = m-dibromobenzene,
- `1,4-dibromobenzene` = p-dibromobenzene.

---

## Tri- and Higher Substituted Benzene

For three or more substituents, use numbering and lowest locant rule. The o, m, p system is not used.

Example:

`1-chloro-2,4-dinitrobenzene`

not an o/m/p name.

---

## Phenyl Group

When benzene is a substituent, it is called phenyl:

`C6H5-`

It may also be abbreviated as `Ph-`.

# 10. Isomerism

## Meaning

Isomerism is the phenomenon in which two or more compounds have the same molecular formula but different properties.

Such compounds are called isomers.

---

## Main Types

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Structural isomerism | Same molecular formula but different connectivity. |
| Stereoisomerism | Same connectivity but different spatial arrangement. |

Structural isomerism includes:

- chain isomerism,
- position isomerism,
- functional group isomerism,
- metamerism.

Stereoisomerism includes:

- geometrical isomerism,
- optical isomerism.

---

## 10.1 Chain Isomerism

Same molecular formula, different carbon skeleton.

Example:

`C5H12` has three chain isomers:

- pentane,
- 2-methylbutane,
- 2,2-dimethylpropane.

---

## 10.2 Position Isomerism

Same molecular formula and same functional group, but different position of substituent or functional group.

Example:

`C3H8O` can represent:

- propan-1-ol,
- propan-2-ol.

The `-OH` group is at a different position.

---

## 10.3 Functional Group Isomerism

Same molecular formula, different functional groups.

Example:

`C3H6O` can represent:

- propanal, an aldehyde,
- propanone, a ketone.

---

## 10.4 Metamerism

Metamerism arises due to different alkyl groups on either side of a polyvalent functional group.

Example:

`C4H10O` can represent:

- methoxypropane, `CH3OC3H7`,
- ethoxyethane, `C2H5OC2H5`.

Both are ethers but have different alkyl groups around oxygen.

---

## 10.5 Stereoisomerism

Stereoisomers have the same connectivity but different spatial arrangement.

Types:

- geometrical isomerism,
- optical isomerism.

This chapter introduces the idea; detailed study comes later.

---

# 11. Fundamentals of Organic Reaction Mechanism

## Meaning of Reaction Mechanism

A reaction mechanism explains:

- which bond breaks,
- which bond forms,
- how electrons move,
- what intermediates are formed,
- why the product forms.

Organic reactions usually involve breaking and forming covalent bonds.

---

## Substrate and Reagent

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Substrate | Organic molecule whose carbon framework is being attacked or changed. |
| Reagent | Species that attacks or reacts with the substrate. |

In many reactions, the substrate contains the reactive carbon centre and the reagent brings or takes electrons.

---

# 12. Fission of a Covalent Bond

Covalent bonds can break in two ways:

1. Heterolytic cleavage
2. Homolytic cleavage

---

## 12.1 Heterolytic Cleavage

In heterolytic cleavage, the shared electron pair goes completely to one atom.

This produces ions.

Example:

`CH3-Br -> CH3+ + Br-`

Here, bromine takes the shared electron pair.

The carbon fragment becomes a carbocation.

---

## Carbocation

A carbocation is a positively charged carbon species.

Examples:

- `CH3+`, methyl cation,
- `CH3CH2+`, primary carbocation,
- `(CH3)2CH+`, secondary carbocation,
- `(CH3)3C+`, tertiary carbocation.

Carbocations are electron-deficient and reactive.

They usually have:

- six electrons around positively charged carbon,
- empty p orbital,
- trigonal planar arrangement around the cationic carbon.

---

## Carbanion

A carbanion is a negatively charged carbon species.

Example:

`CH3-`

Carbanions are electron-rich and reactive.

They usually have:

- lone pair on carbon,
- negative charge,
- approximately `sp3` hybridised carbon,
- distorted tetrahedral structure.

---

## 12.2 Homolytic Cleavage

In homolytic cleavage, each bonded atom takes one electron from the shared pair.

This produces free radicals.

Example:

`R-X -> R. + X.`

The dot represents an unpaired electron.

---

## Free Radical

A free radical is a neutral species containing an unpaired electron.

Examples:

- methyl radical, `CH3.`,
- ethyl radical, `CH3CH2.`,
- tert-butyl radical, `(CH3)3C.`

Free radicals are highly reactive.

Radicals are often formed in reactions involving heat, light, or peroxides.

---

## Comparing Intermediates

| Intermediate | Charge | Electron Nature | Formed By |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Carbocation | positive | electron-deficient | heterolysis |
| Carbanion | negative | electron-rich | heterolysis |
| Free radical | neutral | unpaired electron | homolysis |

---

# 13. Nucleophiles and Electrophiles

## Nucleophile

A nucleophile is an electron-rich species that donates an electron pair.

The word means nucleus-seeking.

Examples:

- `OH-`,
- `CN-`,
- `Cl-`,
- `Br-`,
- `NH3`,
- `H2O`,
- carbanions.

Nucleophiles attack electron-deficient centres.

---

## Electrophile

An electrophile is an electron-deficient species that accepts an electron pair.

The word means electron-seeking.

Examples:

- `H+`,
- `NO2+`,
- carbocations,
- carbonyl carbon in `C=O`,
- alkyl halide carbon in `R-X`,
- `BF3`, `AlCl3`.

Electrophiles attack electron-rich centres.

---

## How to Remember

| Species | Gives or Takes Electron Pair? | Attacks |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nucleophile | gives | positive/electron-poor centre |
| Electrophile | takes | negative/electron-rich centre |

---

## Curved Arrow Notation

Curved arrows show movement of electron pairs.

Rules:

- arrow starts where electrons are,
- arrow ends where electrons go,
- full-headed curved arrow = pair of electrons,
- half-headed arrow = one electron movement.

This notation is essential for understanding organic mechanisms.

---

# 14. Electronic Effects in Organic Compounds

Electron displacement can be permanent or temporary.

Permanent effects:

- inductive effect,
- resonance effect,
- hyperconjugation.

Temporary effect:

- electromeric effect.

These effects explain:

- acidity,
- basicity,
- stability of intermediates,
- orientation of reactions,
- relative reactivity.

---

# 15. Inductive Effect

## Meaning

Inductive effect is the permanent displacement of sigma electrons through a chain due to electronegativity difference.

Example:

In chloroethane, `CH3CH2Cl`, chlorine is more electronegative than carbon, so the C-Cl bond is polar.

`C(delta+) - Cl(delta-)`

This effect is transmitted through sigma bonds but decreases rapidly with distance.

---

## Negative Inductive Effect, `-I`

Groups that withdraw electron density show `-I` effect.

Examples:

- `-NO2`,
- `-CN`,
- `-COOH`,
- `-CHO`,
- `-F`,
- `-Cl`,
- `-Br`,
- `-I`.

They make nearby carbon electron-poor.

---

## Positive Inductive Effect, `+I`

Groups that release electron density show `+I` effect.

Examples:

- alkyl groups such as `-CH3`, `-C2H5`,
- metals in organometallic compounds.

Alkyl groups donate electron density relative to hydrogen.

---

## Distance Dependence

Inductive effect decreases as the number of intervening bonds increases.

Example:

In `CH3CH2CH2Br`, the effect of bromine is strongest near the carbon attached to bromine and weakest far away.

---

## Application: Acidity

Electron-withdrawing groups stabilise the conjugate base of an acid and increase acidity.

Example:

`Cl3CCOOH > Cl2CHCOOH > ClCH2COOH > CH3COOH`

More chlorine atoms create stronger `-I` effect, making the acid stronger.

Electron-donating alkyl groups reduce acidity by destabilising the carboxylate ion.

---

# 16. Resonance and Resonance Effect

## Why Resonance Is Needed

Some molecules cannot be represented properly by one Lewis structure.

Example: benzene.

If benzene had alternating single and double bonds, it should have two different C-C bond lengths. Experimentally all C-C bonds in benzene are equal, about 139 pm, intermediate between single and double bond lengths.

This means electrons are delocalised.

---

## Resonance Structures

Resonance structures are hypothetical contributing structures.

They are not rapidly interconverting real structures.

The real molecule is a resonance hybrid.

Important:

- resonance structures have same atom positions,
- only electron positions change,
- resonance hybrid is more stable than any individual structure,
- more stable contributors contribute more.

---

## Example: Nitro Methane

In nitromethane, the two N-O bonds are experimentally equal. This is explained by resonance between structures where the double bond is shown with either oxygen.

The actual structure has delocalised bonding.

---

## Resonance Effect

Resonance effect is polarity produced by interaction of:

- two pi bonds,
- a pi bond and a lone pair,
- a pi bond and an adjacent empty p orbital.

It is transmitted through a conjugated system.

---

## Positive Resonance Effect, `+R`

In `+R` effect, a group donates electrons into a conjugated system.

Examples:

- `-OH`,
- `-OR`,
- `-NH2`,
- `-NHR`,
- `-NR2`,
- halogens.

Example:

In aniline, `-NH2` can donate its lone pair into the benzene ring, increasing electron density at certain positions.

---

## Negative Resonance Effect, `-R`

In `-R` effect, a group withdraws electrons from a conjugated system.

Examples:

- `-NO2`,
- `-CN`,
- `-CHO`,
- `>C=O`,
- `-COOH`.

Example:

In nitrobenzene, `-NO2` withdraws electron density from the ring through resonance.

---

## Conjugated System

A conjugated system has alternate single and multiple bonds or lone pair-pi interaction.

Examples:

- `CH2=CH-CH=CH2`,
- benzene,
- aniline,
- nitrobenzene.

Conjugation allows delocalisation of pi electrons.

---

# 17. Electromeric Effect

## Meaning

Electromeric effect is a temporary effect shown by compounds containing multiple bonds in the presence of an attacking reagent.

It involves complete transfer of pi electrons to one of the atoms of a multiple bond.

The effect disappears when the attacking reagent is removed.

---

## Positive Electromeric Effect, `+E`

In `+E` effect, pi electrons move toward the atom to which the attacking reagent attaches.

---

## Negative Electromeric Effect, `-E`

In `-E` effect, pi electrons move away from the atom to which the attacking reagent attaches.

---

## Electromeric Versus Inductive

| Feature | Inductive Effect | Electromeric Effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nature | Permanent | Temporary |
| Bond involved | Sigma bonds | Pi bonds |
| Needs reagent? | No | Yes |
| Electron shift | Partial | Complete |

If inductive and electromeric effects operate in opposite directions during attack, electromeric effect usually predominates.

---

# 18. Hyperconjugation

## Meaning

Hyperconjugation is delocalisation of sigma electrons, usually C-H sigma electrons, into an adjacent empty p orbital or pi system.

It is a permanent stabilising effect.

It is also called no-bond resonance in some explanations.

---

## Example: Ethyl Carbocation

In `CH3CH2+`, the positively charged carbon has an empty p orbital.

C-H sigma bonds of the adjacent methyl group can overlap with this empty p orbital and donate electron density.

This stabilises the carbocation.

---

## Carbocation Stability

More adjacent C-H sigma bonds means more hyperconjugation and greater stability.

Stability order:

`tertiary carbocation > secondary carbocation > primary carbocation > methyl carbocation`

Example:

`(CH3)3C+` is more stable than `CH3CH2+`, and `CH3+` is least stable because it has no adjacent C-H bond for hyperconjugation.

---

## Hyperconjugation in Alkenes

Alkyl groups attached to a double bond can donate electron density through hyperconjugation.

This helps explain why more substituted alkenes are generally more stable.

---

# 19. Types of Organic Reactions

Organic reactions are broadly classified into:

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Substitution | One atom/group replaces another. |
| Addition | Atoms/groups add across a multiple bond. |
| Elimination | Atoms/groups are removed, often forming a multiple bond. |
| Rearrangement | Carbon skeleton or group positions change within the molecule. |

These are studied in more detail in Hydrocarbons and Class 12 organic chemistry.

# 20. Purification of Organic Compounds

After preparation or extraction, an organic compound is usually impure.

Purification method depends on:

- physical state,
- boiling point,
- melting point,
- solubility,
- volatility,
- sublimability,
- stability on heating,
- nature of impurities.

Purity is often checked by sharp melting point or boiling point. Modern methods also use chromatographic and spectroscopic techniques.

---

## 20.1 Sublimation

Sublimation is used for solids that change directly from solid to vapour on heating.

It separates sublimable compounds from non-sublimable impurities.

Examples:

- camphor,
- naphthalene,
- benzoic acid,
- iodine.

---

## 20.2 Crystallisation

Crystallisation is used to purify solid organic compounds.

Principle:

The compound and impurities have different solubilities in a suitable solvent.

Method:

1. Dissolve impure solid in hot solvent.
2. Filter if insoluble impurities are present.
3. Concentrate solution to near saturation.
4. Cool the solution.
5. Pure crystals separate.
6. Filter and dry crystals.

The remaining liquid is called mother liquor.

Coloured impurities may be removed using activated charcoal.

Repeated crystallisation may be needed if impurities have similar solubility.

---

## 20.3 Simple Distillation

Distillation is used to separate:

- volatile liquid from non-volatile impurity,
- liquids with sufficiently different boiling points.

The lower boiling liquid vaporises first, condenses, and is collected.

Example:

Chloroform, boiling point about 334 K, can be separated from aniline, boiling point about 457 K.

---

## 20.4 Fractional Distillation

Used when liquid components have close boiling points.

A fractionating column is used.

Principle:

Repeated vaporisation and condensation enrich the vapour in the more volatile component.

Each vaporisation-condensation step acts like a theoretical plate.

Application:

Separation of petroleum fractions from crude oil.

---

## 20.5 Distillation Under Reduced Pressure

Used for liquids that:

- have very high boiling points,
- decompose at or below their boiling points.

Reducing external pressure lowers the boiling point.

Example:

Glycerol can be separated from spent lye in soap industry by this method.

---

## 20.6 Steam Distillation

Used for substances that are:

- steam volatile,
- immiscible with water.

Principle:

The mixture boils when:

`p = p1 + p2`

where:

- `p` is atmospheric pressure,
- `p1` is vapour pressure of organic liquid,
- `p2` is vapour pressure of water.

Because the total pressure is the sum, the organic liquid vaporises below its normal boiling point.

Example:

Aniline can be separated from aniline-water mixture by steam distillation.

---

## 20.7 Differential Extraction

Used when an organic compound is dissolved in an aqueous medium but is more soluble in an organic solvent.

Conditions:

- organic solvent and water must be immiscible,
- compound must be more soluble in organic solvent.

Method:

1. Shake aqueous solution with organic solvent in separatory funnel.
2. Allow two layers to separate.
3. Separate organic layer.
4. Remove solvent by evaporation or distillation.

If compound is only slightly soluble in organic solvent, continuous extraction may be used.

---

# 21. Chromatography

## Meaning

Chromatography is used to:

- separate mixtures,
- purify compounds,
- test purity.

It involves a stationary phase and a mobile phase.

Components separate because they interact differently with the two phases.

---

## Stationary and Mobile Phases

| Phase | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Stationary phase | Fixed phase, solid or liquid. |
| Mobile phase | Moving solvent, solvent mixture, or gas. |

Different components move at different rates, causing separation.

---

## 21.1 Adsorption Chromatography

Based on different degrees of adsorption on an adsorbent.

Common adsorbents:

- silica gel,
- alumina.

More strongly adsorbed substances move slowly. Less strongly adsorbed substances move faster.

Types:

- column chromatography,
- thin layer chromatography.

---

## Column Chromatography

In column chromatography:

- adsorbent is packed in a glass column,
- mixture is placed at top,
- eluant flows down,
- components separate according to adsorption strength.

Most strongly adsorbed components remain near the top for longer.

---

## Thin Layer Chromatography, TLC

TLC uses a thin layer of adsorbent on a glass or plastic plate.

Method:

1. Spot mixture near the base line.
2. Place plate in a solvent chamber.
3. Solvent rises by capillary action.
4. Components move different distances.

Retardation factor:

`Rf = distance moved by substance from base line / distance moved by solvent front from base line`

Detection:

- coloured spots are visible,
- colourless spots may be seen under UV light,
- iodine chamber can show brown spots,
- specific spray reagents can be used,
- amino acids may be detected with ninhydrin.

---

## 21.2 Partition Chromatography

Based on continuous differential partitioning between stationary and mobile phases.

Paper chromatography is an example.

In paper chromatography:

- water trapped in paper acts as stationary phase,
- solvent acts as mobile phase,
- components separate due to different partitioning between water and solvent.

The developed paper strip is called a chromatogram.

---

# 22. Qualitative Analysis of Organic Compounds

Qualitative analysis tells which elements are present.

Common elements in organic compounds:

- carbon,
- hydrogen,
- oxygen,
- nitrogen,
- sulphur,
- halogens,
- phosphorus.

---

## 22.1 Detection of Carbon and Hydrogen

The compound is heated with copper(II) oxide.

Carbon is oxidised to carbon dioxide:

`C + 2CuO -> 2Cu + CO2`

Hydrogen is oxidised to water:

`2H + CuO -> Cu + H2O`

Tests:

| Product | Test | Observation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `CO2` | lime water | turns milky due to `CaCO3` |
| `H2O` | anhydrous copper sulphate | white turns blue |

Equations:

`CO2 + Ca(OH)2 -> CaCO3 + H2O`

`CuSO4 + 5H2O -> CuSO4.5H2O`

White anhydrous copper sulphate turns blue hydrated copper sulphate.

---

## 22.2 Lassaigne's Test

Nitrogen, sulphur, halogens, and phosphorus are detected by Lassaigne's test.

Organic compounds contain these elements in covalent form. They are first converted into ionic sodium salts by fusion with sodium metal.

General conversions:

`Na + C + N -> NaCN`

`2Na + S -> Na2S`

`Na + X -> NaX`

where `X = Cl, Br, I`.

The fused mass is extracted with water to obtain sodium fusion extract.

---

## Test for Nitrogen

Sodium fusion extract is boiled with iron(II) sulphate and acidified with concentrated sulphuric acid.

Formation of Prussian blue colour confirms nitrogen.

Key reactions:

`6CN- + Fe2+ -> [Fe(CN)6]^4-`

`3[Fe(CN)6]^4- + 4Fe3+ -> Fe4[Fe(CN)6]3.xH2O`

The blue compound is ferriferrocyanide.

---

## Test for Sulphur

### Lead Acetate Test

Acidify sodium fusion extract with acetic acid and add lead acetate.

Black precipitate confirms sulphur.

`S2- + Pb2+ -> PbS`

Black `PbS` is formed.

### Sodium Nitroprusside Test

Add sodium nitroprusside.

Violet colour confirms sulphur.

`S2- + [Fe(CN)5NO]^2- -> [Fe(CN)5NOS]^4-`

---

## If Nitrogen and Sulphur Are Both Present

During sodium fusion, sodium thiocyanate may form:

`Na + C + N + S -> NaSCN`

With ferric ions, thiocyanate gives blood-red colour:

`Fe3+ + SCN- -> [Fe(SCN)]2+`

If excess sodium is used:

`NaSCN + 2Na -> NaCN + Na2S`

Then usual nitrogen and sulphur tests can be obtained.

---

## Test for Halogens

Sodium fusion extract is acidified with nitric acid and treated with silver nitrate.

`X- + Ag+ -> AgX`

| Halogen | Precipitate | Solubility in Ammonium Hydroxide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chlorine | white `AgCl` | soluble |
| Bromine | pale yellow `AgBr` | sparingly soluble |
| Iodine | yellow `AgI` | insoluble |

Nitric acid is added before silver nitrate to decompose interfering `CN-` and `S2-` ions.

---

## Test for Phosphorus

The compound is heated with sodium peroxide so that phosphorus is oxidised to phosphate.

The solution is boiled with nitric acid and treated with ammonium molybdate.

Yellow colour or precipitate confirms phosphorus.

Key product:

ammonium phosphomolybdate.

---

# 23. Quantitative Analysis

Quantitative analysis determines mass percentage of elements in an organic compound.

This helps calculate empirical and molecular formula.

---

## 23.1 Estimation of Carbon and Hydrogen

A known mass of organic compound is burnt in excess oxygen and copper(II) oxide.

Carbon becomes carbon dioxide.

Hydrogen becomes water.

Water is absorbed in weighed anhydrous calcium chloride tube.

Carbon dioxide is absorbed in weighed potassium hydroxide tube.

Increase in masses gives amounts of `H2O` and `CO2`.

---

## Formulas for Carbon and Hydrogen

If:

- mass of compound = `m g`,
- mass of `CO2` formed = `m2 g`,
- mass of `H2O` formed = `m1 g`,

then:

`%C = (12 x m2 x 100) / (44 x m)`

`%H = (2 x m1 x 100) / (18 x m)`

Reason:

- `44 g CO2` contains `12 g C`,
- `18 g H2O` contains `2 g H`.

---

## 23.2 Estimation of Nitrogen: Dumas Method

In Dumas method, nitrogen-containing organic compound is heated with copper oxide in carbon dioxide atmosphere.

Nitrogen is converted into free nitrogen gas.

Any nitrogen oxides formed are reduced to nitrogen by passing over hot copper.

Carbon dioxide is absorbed by potassium hydroxide.

Nitrogen gas volume is measured.

---

## Dumas Formula

Volume of nitrogen is corrected to STP.

If corrected volume of nitrogen at STP is `V mL` and mass of compound is `m g`:

`%N = (28 x V x 100) / (22400 x m)`

Reason:

`22400 mL N2` at STP weighs `28 g`.

If gas is collected over water:

`actual pressure = atmospheric pressure - aqueous tension`

---

## 23.3 Estimation of Nitrogen: Kjeldahl Method

In Kjeldahl method:

1. The nitrogenous compound is heated with concentrated sulphuric acid.
2. Nitrogen is converted into ammonium sulphate.
3. The mixture is treated with excess sodium hydroxide.
4. Ammonia is liberated.
5. Ammonia is absorbed in known excess standard acid.
6. Unused acid is titrated with standard alkali.
7. Difference gives amount of ammonia and nitrogen.

Key reactions:

`Organic compound + H2SO4 -> (NH4)2SO4`

`(NH4)2SO4 + 2NaOH -> Na2SO4 + 2NH3 + 2H2O`

`2NH3 + H2SO4 -> (NH4)2SO4`

---

## Limitation of Kjeldahl Method

Kjeldahl method is not applicable to compounds containing nitrogen in:

- nitro groups,
- azo groups,
- ring nitrogen such as pyridine.

Reason:

These forms of nitrogen do not convert completely into ammonium sulphate under the method conditions.

---

## 23.4 Estimation of Halogens: Carius Method

A known mass of organic compound is heated with fuming nitric acid and silver nitrate in a sealed Carius tube.

Halogen forms silver halide:

`AgX`

The precipitate is filtered, washed, dried, and weighed.

Formula:

If:

- mass of compound = `m g`,
- mass of `AgX` = `m1 g`,
- atomic mass of halogen = `A`,
- molar mass of `AgX` = `M`,

then:

`% halogen = (A x m1 x 100) / (M x m)`

---

## 23.5 Estimation of Sulphur

The compound is heated with sodium peroxide or fuming nitric acid in a Carius tube.

Sulphur is oxidised to sulphuric acid.

It is precipitated as barium sulphate by adding barium chloride.

If:

- mass of compound = `m g`,
- mass of `BaSO4` = `m1 g`,

then:

`%S = (32 x m1 x 100) / (233 x m)`

Reason:

`233 g BaSO4` contains `32 g S`.

---

## 23.6 Estimation of Phosphorus

The compound is heated with fuming nitric acid. Phosphorus is oxidised to phosphoric acid.

It may be precipitated as:

- ammonium phosphomolybdate, `(NH4)3PO4.12MoO3`,
- or magnesium pyrophosphate, `Mg2P2O7`.

If estimated as ammonium phosphomolybdate:

`%P = (31 x m1 x 100) / (1877 x m)`

If estimated as `Mg2P2O7`:

`%P = (62 x m1 x 100) / (222 x m)`

where:

- `m` = mass of compound,
- `m1` = mass of precipitate.

---

## 23.7 Estimation of Oxygen

Oxygen is usually found by difference:

`%O = 100 - sum of percentages of all other elements`

It can also be estimated directly by converting oxygen into carbon monoxide and then carbon dioxide using red-hot coke and iodine pentoxide, but this is less commonly emphasised at this level.

Modern CHN elemental analysers can determine carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen using very small amounts of sample.

---

# 24. Common Exam Questions and How to Answer

## Question: Why does carbon form many compounds?

Carbon forms many compounds because it is tetravalent, forms strong covalent bonds with carbon and other elements, and shows catenation. It can form straight chains, branched chains, rings, single bonds, double bonds, and triple bonds. This structural variety leads to a huge number of organic compounds.

---

## Question: Why is rotation restricted around a double bond?

A double bond contains one sigma bond and one pi bond. The pi bond is formed by sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals. Rotation around the double bond would disturb this parallel overlap and break the pi bond. Therefore rotation around a carbon-carbon double bond is restricted.

---

## Question: What is a functional group?

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms attached to a carbon chain that determines the characteristic chemical reactions of the compound. For example, `-OH` is the functional group of alcohols, `-CHO` of aldehydes, and `-COOH` of carboxylic acids.

---

## Question: What is the difference between homolytic and heterolytic cleavage?

In homolytic cleavage, each bonded atom takes one electron from the shared pair, forming free radicals. In heterolytic cleavage, one atom takes the entire shared pair, forming ions such as carbocations and carbanions.

---

## Question: Why is nitric acid added before silver nitrate in Lassaigne's halogen test?

Nitric acid decomposes cyanide and sulphide ions that may be present in sodium fusion extract. If not removed, these ions would also form precipitates with silver nitrate and interfere with the halogen test.

---

## Question: Why is acetic acid used instead of sulphuric acid in lead acetate test for sulphur?

Lead acetate test requires formation of black lead sulphide from sulphide ions. Sulphuric acid can itself produce lead sulphate with lead acetate, interfering with the test. Acetic acid acidifies the solution without causing such interference.

---

## Question: Why does organic liquid vaporise below its boiling point in steam distillation?

In steam distillation, the mixture boils when the sum of vapour pressures of water and organic liquid equals atmospheric pressure. Since the organic liquid contributes only part of the total pressure, it vaporises at a temperature below its normal boiling point.

---

# 26. Common Mistakes to Avoid

## Mistake 1: Thinking all carbon compounds are organic

Correct idea:

Most carbon compounds are organic, but compounds such as carbonates, bicarbonates, carbides, cyanides, carbon monoxide, and carbon dioxide are usually treated separately as inorganic compounds.

---

## Mistake 2: Ignoring functional group priority in naming

Correct idea:

The principal functional group decides the suffix and numbering priority.

---

## Mistake 3: Choosing the shorter chain because it looks simpler

Correct idea:

Always choose the longest chain containing the principal functional group and maximum unsaturation where required.

---

## Mistake 4: Confusing electrophile and nucleophile

Correct idea:

Nucleophile donates an electron pair. Electrophile accepts an electron pair.

---

## Mistake 5: Treating resonance structures as real separate molecules

Correct idea:

Resonance structures are hypothetical contributors. The real molecule is a resonance hybrid.

---

## Mistake 6: Confusing electromeric effect with resonance

Correct idea:

Electromeric effect is temporary and occurs only during reagent attack. Resonance is a permanent delocalisation in the ground state.

---

## Mistake 7: Using Kjeldahl method for all nitrogen compounds

Correct idea:

Kjeldahl method does not work properly for nitro, azo, and ring nitrogen compounds like pyridine.

---

## Mistake 8: Forgetting aqueous tension correction in Dumas method

Correct idea:

If nitrogen is collected over water, actual pressure of nitrogen equals atmospheric pressure minus aqueous tension.
