# Part 1: The Physics Answer-Key Mindset

## Core Idea

In physics, an answer key often looks short because it assumes the learner already knows the model. A line such as:

```text
N = 70 x 10 = 700 N; reading is 70 kg
```

is not just a calculation. It hides a complete force diagram:

1. The person is standing on a weighing scale.
2. The scale reads the normal reaction.
3. The person has mass 70 kg.
4. The value of gravitational acceleration used is 10 m s^-2.
5. If there is no vertical acceleration, normal force equals weight.

That is the standard expected in these notes: recover the reasoning behind the compressed answer.

---

## The Standard Five-Step Physics Solution

| Step | Learner Action | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Identify the system | Person, stone, helicopter, coin, disc, bead, or planet |
| 2 | Draw or imagine the forces | Weight, normal reaction, tension, friction, thrust |
| 3 | Choose the law | Newton's laws, conservation of momentum, work-energy theorem, rolling condition |
| 4 | Write the equation with units | `F = ma`, `K = (1/2)mv^2`, `v = R omega` |
| 5 | Interpret the result | Direction, sign, scale reading, whether the object slips or stays |

# Part 2: Units, Dimensions, And Measurement

## Why Dimensional Checking Appears Often

Several recovered fragments are based on one powerful rule:

> Both sides of a physical equation must have the same dimensions.

This is why some formulas can be rejected without solving the full problem.

---

## Trigonometric Functions Must Be Dimensionless

The source gives this idea more than once:

```text
The argument of a trigonometric function must always be dimensionless.
```

That means expressions such as `sin(v)`, `cos(t)`, or `tan(length)` are not physically meaningful unless the argument is a pure number or an angle in radians.

### Learner Explanation

A trigonometric function describes a ratio. For example:

```text
tan theta = opposite side / adjacent side
```

A ratio of two lengths has no unit. Therefore, the expression inside or attached to a trigonometric function cannot carry units like metre, second, or kilogram.

### Exam Use

If a proposed formula says:

```text
tan theta = v
```

then it is wrong because `v` has unit `m s^-1`. But:

```text
tan theta = v / v'
```

can be correct because speed divided by speed is dimensionless.

---

## Correct Relativistic Mass Pattern

The recovered source gives the correct formula:

```text
m = m0 (1 - v^2/c^2)^(-1/2)
```

### Meaning Of Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `m` | relativistic mass |
| `m0` | rest mass |
| `v` | speed of the body |
| `c` | speed of light in vacuum |

### Why The Formula Passes A Dimension Check

The ratio `v^2/c^2` is dimensionless because both `v` and `c` are speeds.

So:

```text
1 - v^2/c^2
```

is also dimensionless, and raising it to a power is allowed. The final unit of `m` comes from `m0`, so the formula gives mass.

---

## Parsec As A Unit Of Length

The source gives:

```text
1 parsec = 3.084 x 10^16 m
```

### Learner Explanation

A parsec is an astronomical distance unit. It is used because distances between stars are too large to handle conveniently in metres.

### What To Remember

| Quantity | Value |
| --- | --- |
| 1 parsec | `3.084 x 10^16 m` |
| Approximation | about `3 x 10^16 m` |

### Common Mistake

Do not treat parsec as a time unit. It is a length unit, even though the word sounds unusual.

---

## Density Of The Sun

The extracted answer notes that the average density of the Sun is in the range of liquids or solids, not ordinary gases.

### Learner Explanation

This does not mean the Sun is a normal solid or liquid. It means that the Sun's huge gravitational attraction compresses its inner material strongly. Because the outer layers press inward due to gravity, the average mass per unit volume becomes large.

### Answer-Writing Frame

```text
The Sun has very high mass. Its own gravity pulls the outer layers inward.
This compression gives it a high average density, even though its material is not a normal solid.
```

# Part 3: Motion In One Dimension

## Position-Time Graphs

The recovered source mentions impossible graphs and warns that an `x-t` plot is not necessarily the actual trajectory.

### Key Principle

An `x-t` graph shows position along one chosen axis as time changes. It does not draw the path of the body in space.

### Example

If a stone falls vertically while we plot height against time, the graph may be a curve. That curve is not the stone's visible path in space; the actual path may be a straight vertical line.

---

## What Makes A Motion Graph Impossible?

| Graph Error | Why It Is Impossible |
| --- | --- |
| One body has two positions at the same time | A single particle cannot be at two different locations at one instant. |
| Velocity has two opposite values at the same time | A single velocity value is defined at a given instant. |
| Speed becomes negative | Speed is a magnitude and cannot be negative. |
| Total path length decreases with time | Distance travelled cannot undo itself. |

---

## Velocity At The Highest Point Of Projectile-Like Motion

The source notes that a pebble or stone at the highest point is not necessarily at rest.

### Learner Explanation

In projectile motion without air resistance:

1. The vertical component of velocity becomes zero at the highest point.
2. The horizontal component remains unchanged.
3. Therefore, the total velocity is not zero unless the motion was purely vertical.
4. Acceleration remains vertically downward due to gravity.

### Strong Answer

```text
At the highest point, only the vertical component of velocity is zero.
The horizontal component remains constant, so the body is still moving.
The acceleration is still g downward.
```

---

## Sign Convention In Motion

The answer fragments use signs such as:

```text
x < 0, v < 0, a > 0
x > 0, v > 0, a < 0
```

### Learner Explanation

The sign of a quantity depends on the chosen positive direction.

| Quantity | What Its Sign Means |
| --- | --- |
| `x > 0` | position is on the positive side of origin |
| `x < 0` | position is on the negative side of origin |
| `v > 0` | motion is along positive direction |
| `v < 0` | motion is along negative direction |
| `a > 0` | acceleration is along positive direction |
| `a < 0` | acceleration is along negative direction |

### Common Mistake

Do not say "negative velocity means slowing down". Negative velocity means motion in the negative direction. A body slows down only when velocity and acceleration have opposite signs.

# Part 4: Vectors And Projectile Reasoning

## Scalar And Vector Distinction

The recovered source lists examples of scalar and vector quantities.

### Scalars

Scalars have magnitude only.

| Scalar | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Volume | amount of space occupied |
| Mass | amount of matter |
| Speed | rate of distance travelled |
| Density | mass per unit volume |
| Number of moles | amount of substance |
| Angular frequency | rate of angular cycle repetition |

### Vectors

Vectors have magnitude and direction.

Examples include displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, and impulse.

---

## Average Speed And Average Velocity

The recovered source states that average speed equals the magnitude of average velocity only for a straight path in one direction.

### Why

```text
average speed = total path length / total time
average velocity = displacement / total time
```

Path length and displacement are equal only when the body moves along a straight line without changing direction.

### Common Mistake

If a body returns to its starting point, displacement is zero, so average velocity is zero. But the average speed is not zero because distance was travelled.

---

## Uniform Circular Motion

The source gives centripetal acceleration:

```text
a = v^2 / R
```

directed toward the centre.

### Learner Explanation

In uniform circular motion:

1. Speed remains constant.
2. Direction of velocity keeps changing.
3. Since velocity changes, acceleration exists.
4. The acceleration points toward the centre.

### Important Distinction

The centripetal force is not a new force. It is the name given to the net inward force required for circular motion. The actual force may be tension, gravity, friction, normal reaction, or a combination.

# Part 5: Newton's Laws And Force Analysis

## No Net Force And The First Law

The source repeatedly uses the idea:

```text
No net force according to the First Law.
```

### Meaning

If the net external force on a body is zero, then:

1. A body at rest remains at rest.
2. A body in motion continues with constant velocity.

### Important

Constant velocity means both constant speed and constant direction. If direction changes, velocity changes, so there must be acceleration and therefore a net force.

---

## Force At An Instant Does Not Depend On Past History

The recovered source says that force at an instant depends on the situation at that instant, not on history.

### Learner Explanation

If a stone is at a particular point and the only force acting on it is gravity, the force is `mg` downward. It does not matter whether the stone was thrown up, dropped, or moving sideways. The present forces decide the present acceleration.

---

## Apparent Weight And Scale Reading

The extracted answer gives the important scale-reading example:

```text
N = 70 x 10 = 700 N; reading is 70 kg
70 x 10 - N = 70 x 5; reading is 35 kg
N - 70 x 10 = 70 x 5; reading is 105 kg
reading would be zero
```

### Physical Setup

A person of mass `70 kg` stands on a weighing scale. The scale reads the normal force exerted by the scale on the person, often displayed as an equivalent mass.

### Case 1: No Vertical Acceleration

```text
N = mg
N = 70 x 10
N = 700 N
```

The scale reading is equivalent to `70 kg`.

### Case 2: Downward Acceleration Of 5 m s^-2

If the person accelerates downward:

```text
mg - N = ma
70 x 10 - N = 70 x 5
N = 350 N
```

The scale reads equivalent to `35 kg`.

### Case 3: Upward Acceleration Of 5 m s^-2

If the person accelerates upward:

```text
N - mg = ma
N - 70 x 10 = 70 x 5
N = 1050 N
```

The scale reads equivalent to `105 kg`.

### Case 4: Free Fall

If the person and scale fall freely together:

```text
mg - N = mg
N = 0
```

The scale reads zero. This is apparent weightlessness.

---

## Pulling And Pushing A Lawn Mower

The source explains why pulling can be easier than pushing.

### Learner Explanation

When you push at a downward angle, part of your applied force increases the normal reaction. Since friction depends on normal reaction, friction increases.

When you pull at an upward angle, part of your applied force reduces the normal reaction. Friction decreases.

### Short Answer

```text
Pulling reduces the normal reaction and hence reduces friction.
Pushing increases the normal reaction and hence increases friction.
Therefore pulling is usually easier.
```

---

## Horse And Cart In Empty Space

The recovered answer says the horse-cart system has no external force in empty space.

### Learner Explanation

The horse pulls the cart and the cart pulls the horse with equal and opposite internal forces. For the combined system of horse plus cart, those forces cancel.

On the ground, the horse pushes the ground backward and the ground applies friction forward. That external friction helps the system move.

### Exam Point

Always define the system before deciding whether a force is internal or external.

## Helicopter Free-Body Example

The recovered source gives:

```text
F = 7.5 x 10^3 N upward
R = 3.25 x 10^4 N upward
```

### Case A: Crew And Passengers As The System

Mass of crew and passengers:

```text
m = 300 kg
```

Acceleration upward:

```text
a = 15 m s^-2
```

Using:

```text
F - mg = ma
```

Substitute:

```text
F - 300 x 10 = 300 x 15
F = 7500 N
F = 7.5 x 10^3 N upward
```

By Newton's third law, the crew and passengers push downward on the floor with the same magnitude.

### Case B: Helicopter Plus Crew And Passengers As The System

Total mass:

```text
m = 1300 kg
```

Using:

```text
R - mg = ma
```

Substitute:

```text
R - 1300 x 10 = 1300 x 15
R = 32500 N
R = 3.25 x 10^4 N upward
```

The air exerts this upward force on the helicopter system. The helicopter exerts an equal downward force on the air.

# Part 6: Momentum, Impulse, Work, And Energy

## Impulse

The source includes impulse results such as:

```text
Impulse = change in momentum
```

### Formula

```text
J = Delta p = F Delta t
```

### Learner Explanation

Impulse measures how much momentum changes during a force acting over time.

If a ball is stopped gently, the time interval is larger, so the force needed is smaller. This is why players move their hands backward while catching a ball.

---

## Conservation Of Momentum

The recovered source uses momentum conservation for collisions and recoil-like situations.

### Principle

If no external force acts on a system:

```text
total initial momentum = total final momentum
```

### Important

Momentum can be conserved even when kinetic energy is not conserved. This is especially important in inelastic collisions.

---

## Work-Energy Theorem

The source states:

```text
Work done by the net force on a body equals change in its kinetic energy.
```

### Formula

```text
W_net = Delta K
```

where:

```text
Delta K = K_final - K_initial
```

### Learner Explanation

If the net work is positive, kinetic energy increases. If the net work is negative, kinetic energy decreases. If net work is zero, kinetic energy remains unchanged.

---

## Conservative Force And Potential Energy

The source notes:

```text
For a conservative force, work done over a path is minus the change in potential energy.
```

### Formula

```text
W = -Delta U
```

### Meaning

If potential energy decreases, the conservative force does positive work. If potential energy increases, the conservative force does negative work.

---

## Inelastic Collision

The recovered answer states that linear momentum is conserved during an inelastic collision, but kinetic energy is not conserved.

### Correct Statement

```text
In an isolated system, total momentum is conserved in both elastic and inelastic collisions.
Kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions.
```

### Common Mistake

Do not say "energy is lost" in an inelastic collision. Total energy is conserved, but some kinetic energy changes into heat, sound, deformation, or internal energy.

# Part 7: Rotation And Rolling

## Centre Of Mass

The source mentions that the centre of mass may lie outside a body.

### Examples

| Body | Where Centre Of Mass May Lie |
| --- | --- |
| Ring | at the empty centre |
| Hollow sphere | at the geometric centre |
| Hollow cylinder | on the axis, possibly in empty space |
| Hollow cube | at geometric centre |

### Learner Explanation

Centre of mass is a mathematical point representing mass distribution. It does not need to be a material point of the body.

---

## Angular Momentum Conservation

The recovered source gives the common angular speed of two discs after contact:

```text
omega = (I1 omega1 + I2 omega2) / (I1 + I2)
```

### When To Use

Use this when:

1. Two rotating bodies interact.
2. External torque about the axis is zero or negligible.
3. They finally rotate together with one common angular speed.

### Why Kinetic Energy May Decrease

Friction between the discs can dissipate mechanical energy as heat, but the torque is internal to the system. Therefore angular momentum remains conserved while rotational kinetic energy may decrease.

---

## Rolling Begins When The Point Of Contact Stops Slipping

The recovered source gives:

```text
v = mu_k g t
omega = omega0 - (mu_k m g R t) / I
```

Rolling begins when:

```text
v = R omega
```

### Learner Explanation

At first, the body spins and slips on the surface. Kinetic friction has two effects:

1. It accelerates the centre of mass forward.
2. It reduces the angular speed.

Pure rolling begins when the bottom point is instantaneously at rest relative to the ground.

---

## Rolling Time For Ring And Disc

For a ring:

```text
I = mR^2
t = omega0 R / (2 mu_k g)
```

For a disc:

```text
I = (1/2)mR^2
t = R omega0 / (3 mu_k g)
```

### Comparison

The disc begins pure rolling earlier than the ring for the same `R`, `omega0`, and `mu_k`.

### Why

The disc has smaller moment of inertia than the ring. Friction changes its angular speed more effectively, so the no-slip condition is reached sooner.

---

## Coin On A Rotating Disc

The source gives the condition:

```text
r <= mu g / omega^2
```

### Meaning

For a coin to rotate with the disc without slipping, friction must provide the required centripetal force.

Required centripetal force:

```text
F_c = m r omega^2
```

Maximum static friction:

```text
f_max = mu m g
```

Condition for no slipping:

```text
m r omega^2 <= mu m g
r <= mu g / omega^2
```

### Learner Interpretation

A coin nearer the centre is safer from slipping because it needs less centripetal force. A coin farther from the centre requires more friction and may slip first.

---

## Bead On A Rotating Circular Wire

The source gives:

```text
cos theta = g / (R omega^2)
```

### Learner Explanation

This result comes from resolving forces on a bead in a rotating circular wire setup. The normal reaction contributes to the centripetal requirement, while weight acts downward.

### Condition

Since:

```text
cos theta <= 1
```

the bead remains at the lowest point unless the angular speed is large enough to allow another equilibrium position.

---

## Centripetal Effect Is Not A Separate Material Force

The source warns:

```text
Do not confuse actual material forces with the effects they produce.
```

### Meaning

For circular motion, the expression:

```text
mv^2/R
```

is not an extra force. It is the required inward net force.

### Example

If a body moves in a vertical circle, tension and weight are real forces. Their resultant provides centripetal acceleration.

At the bottom:

```text
T - mg = mv^2/R
```

At the top, depending on direction:

```text
T + mg = mv^2/R
```

The exact signs depend on which direction is taken as inward.

# Part 8: Gravitation Answer Fragments

The source includes several answer fragments labelled as Chapter 8 questions. These are treated here as a mini-section on gravitation reasoning.

---

## 8.1: Tidal Effect

The recovered answer says:

```text
Tidal effect depends inversely on the cube of distance.
Force depends inversely on the square of distance.
```

### Learner Explanation

Gravitational force between two masses follows:

```text
F proportional to 1/r^2
```

But tidal effect is caused by the difference in gravitational pull across the size of an extended body. Because it depends on how rapidly gravity changes with distance, it varies more sharply:

```text
tidal effect proportional to 1/r^3
```

### Strong Answer

```text
Tidal effect is not simply the gravitational force at one point.
It is the difference in gravitational pull across an extended body.
Since this variation falls as the inverse cube of distance, tidal effects become weak very rapidly with distance.
```

---

## 8.7: Escape Velocity

The source answer gives:

```text
Escape velocity is independent of the mass of the body and the direction of projection.
It depends on the gravitational potential at the point of launch.
```

### Formula Pattern

For a spherical planet, near the surface:

```text
v_e = sqrt(2GM/R)
```

or:

```text
v_e = sqrt(2gR)
```

### What Escape Velocity Depends On

| Factor | Does Escape Velocity Depend On It? | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mass of projected body | No | Mass cancels in energy equation |
| Direction of projection | No, if no atmosphere and same point | It is a required speed, not a direction |
| Planet mass | Yes | Stronger gravity needs greater escape speed |
| Planet radius | Yes | Larger radius changes gravitational potential |
| Height of launch | Slightly | Potential changes with height |
| Latitude | Slightly | Effective gravity changes due to rotation and shape |

### Why Mass Cancels

Escape condition:

```text
(1/2)m v_e^2 = GMm/R
```

Cancel `m`:

```text
(1/2)v_e^2 = GM/R
```

So the escape speed does not depend on the mass of the escaping object.

---

## 8.10 And 8.11: Completing A Hemisphere To A Sphere

The recovered source says:

```text
Complete the hemisphere to a sphere.
At both P and C, potential is constant and hence intensity is zero.
```

### Learner Explanation

This is a symmetry argument. Instead of directly calculating the field of a hemisphere, imagine adding the missing half to form a complete sphere. For a complete spherical shell, gravitational field inside is zero.

### Key Ideas

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Gravitational potential | Work-related scalar quantity |
| Gravitational intensity | Gravitational field; force per unit mass |
| Constant potential | No spatial change in potential |
| Zero intensity | Field is zero where potential has no gradient |

### Answer-Writing Frame

```text
Complete the hemisphere conceptually to form a full spherical shell.
For a complete shell, the potential at interior points is constant.
Since gravitational intensity is related to the spatial change of potential, constant potential gives zero intensity.
```

## 8.19: Energy Answer

The recovered answer gives:

```text
5.9 x 10^9 J
```

### How To Treat This

Because the source gives only the final value, a learner should not memorise the number alone. The important point is that gravitation energy problems usually come from:

```text
U = -GMm/r
```

or a difference:

```text
Delta U = GMm(1/r1 - 1/r2)
```

depending on the exact question.

### Learner Reminder

Always check whether the question asks for:

1. potential energy at a point,
2. change in potential energy,
3. work done by gravity,
4. work done by an external agent,
5. energy required to escape.

These may have different signs.

## 8.23: Gravity Versus Rotation At A Compact Star

The recovered answer gives:

```text
GM/R^2 = 2.3 x 10^12 m s^-2
omega^2 R = 1.1 x 10^6 m s^-2
```

### Meaning Of The Two Quantities

| Quantity | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `GM/R^2` | gravitational acceleration at the surface |
| `omega^2 R` | outward centrifugal-effect acceleration in rotating frame |

### Comparison

The inward gravitational acceleration is much larger than the outward rotational effect:

```text
2.3 x 10^12 >> 1.1 x 10^6
```

Therefore, an object at the equator remains stuck to the star and does not fly off.

### Angular Speed Increase

The source notes that if angular speed increases by about a factor of 2000, the object may fly off.

### Why The Factor Is Large

The rotational term is:

```text
omega^2 R
```

It depends on the square of angular speed. To make the rotational effect comparable to gravity, `omega` must increase a lot.

## 8.25: Height Answer

The recovered answer gives:

```text
495 km
```

### How To Use This

This appears to be the final result of a gravitation height calculation. Since only the final value is present, learn the method rather than the number.

Common height-related gravitation relations include:

```text
g_h = g R^2 / (R + h)^2
```

and, for small heights compared with Earth's radius:

```text
g_h approximately equals g(1 - 2h/R)
```

### Answer-Writing Advice

If a question gives a change in `g` with height, decide whether to use the exact formula or the small-height approximation. Use the approximation only when `h` is much smaller than `R`.

# Part 10: Common Mistakes To Avoid

## Mistake 1: Treating Centripetal Force As An Extra Force

Wrong thinking:

```text
Tension + gravity + centripetal force act on the body.
```

Correct thinking:

```text
Tension and gravity are real forces.
Their inward resultant provides the centripetal requirement mv^2/R.
```

---

## Mistake 2: Ignoring Direction In Newton's Laws

Always choose a positive direction before writing:

```text
F_net = ma
```

For upward acceleration:

```text
N - mg = ma
```

For downward acceleration:

```text
mg - N = ma
```

The same quantities appear, but the equation changes because direction changes.

---

## Mistake 3: Confusing Speed And Velocity

Speed is a scalar. Velocity is a vector.

At the highest point of projectile motion:

```text
vertical velocity = 0
horizontal velocity may not be 0
acceleration = g downward
```

So the body is not necessarily at rest.

---

## Mistake 4: Memorising A Number Without Its Model

The source contains final results such as:

```text
5.9 x 10^9 J
495 km
```

These values are not useful unless the learner knows which formula produced them. Always attach a final value to its physical model.

---

## Mistake 5: Forgetting The System Boundary

Internal and external forces depend on what system you choose.

Example:

| System Chosen | Force Classification |
| --- | --- |
| horse alone | cart's pull on horse is external |
| cart alone | horse's pull on cart is external |
| horse plus cart | horse-cart pull pair is internal |

Momentum and force arguments become much clearer after defining the system.

# Part 13: One-Page Revision Sheet

## Must-Know Ideas

- A physical formula must be dimensionally consistent.
- Trigonometric arguments must be dimensionless.
- A graph of position against time is not necessarily the actual path.
- Speed is scalar; velocity is vector.
- At a projectile's highest point, acceleration is still downward.
- Apparent weight is the normal reaction, not the actual gravitational weight.
- Momentum conservation depends on external force, not on whether the collision is elastic.
- Work done by net force equals change in kinetic energy.
- Centripetal acceleration is an effect of real inward forces.
- Pure rolling begins when `v = R omega`.
- Static friction provides centripetal force for a coin on a rotating disc.
- Escape velocity does not depend on the mass of the projected body.
- Tidal effect falls with distance faster than gravitational force.
