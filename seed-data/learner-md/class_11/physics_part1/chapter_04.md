# Chapter 4 - Laws Of Motion

## 1. Chapter Purpose

The previous chapters described motion. This chapter explains why motion changes.

Kinematics tells us how position, velocity, and acceleration behave. Dynamics connects that motion to force.

The central idea is:

A body accelerates only when a net external force acts on it.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain why force is not needed to maintain uniform motion
- understand inertia
- state and apply Newton's first law
- define momentum
- state and apply Newton's second law
- calculate impulse and change in momentum
- state and correctly interpret Newton's third law
- use conservation of momentum
- draw and use free-body diagrams
- solve equilibrium problems
- use common forces such as weight, normal reaction, tension, spring force, and friction
- distinguish static and kinetic friction
- solve circular motion force problems
- analyze motion on flat and banked roads

## 2. The Central Question

The basic question of this chapter is:

What governs the motion of bodies?

Common experience may suggest that a force is needed to keep a body moving. For example:

- a toy car stops when we stop pulling it
- a rolling ball eventually stops
- a bicycle slows down when pedaling stops

But this conclusion is incomplete.

These bodies stop because friction or resistance opposes their motion.

If there were no friction and no resistance, a moving body would continue moving with constant velocity without any applied force.

## 3. Aristotle's Fallacy

Aristotle believed that an external force is required to keep a body in motion.

This idea seems natural because most everyday motion happens with friction.

Example:

A toy car on the floor stops after being pushed. It appears that a continuous force is needed to keep it moving.

The actual reason it stops is friction.

To keep it moving with constant velocity, the applied force must only balance friction. The net force is then zero.

Thus, the corrected idea is:

Force is not required to maintain uniform motion. Force is required to change motion.

## 4. Galileo And The Law Of Inertia

Galileo studied motion on inclined planes.

He observed:

- a ball rolling down an inclined plane speeds up
- a ball rolling up an inclined plane slows down
- a ball on a horizontal frictionless plane should neither speed up nor slow down

In an ideal frictionless case, a body moving on a horizontal plane would continue with constant velocity forever.

This leads to the law of inertia.

Inertia is the tendency of a body to resist change in its state of rest or uniform motion.

## 5. Inertia

Inertia means resistance to change.

A body at rest tends to remain at rest.

A body in uniform straight-line motion tends to remain in that motion.

Mass is a measure of inertia.

Greater mass means greater resistance to change in motion.

Examples:

- It is harder to push a loaded truck than a small car.
- Passengers jerk backward when a bus suddenly starts.
- Passengers jerk forward when a bus suddenly stops.

## 6. Newton's First Law Of Motion

Newton's first law states:

Every body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled by a net external force to change that state.

In simpler form:

If the net external force on a body is zero, its acceleration is zero.

Mathematically:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=\vec{0}\quad \Rightarrow \quad \vec{a}=\vec{0}
$$

This means:

- if a body is at rest, it remains at rest
- if a body is moving, it continues with constant velocity

provided the net external force is zero.

## 7. Force And Net Force

A force is an interaction that can change the state of motion of a body.

Force is a vector.

If several forces act on a body, their vector sum is the net force.

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2+\vec{F}_3+\cdots
$$

The acceleration of the body is determined by the net external force, not by any one force alone.

Example:

A book at rest on a table has:

- weight downward
- normal reaction upward

The book is at rest because the net force is zero.

The forces are equal and opposite in this case, but they are not a third-law pair because they act on the same body.

## 8. Momentum

Momentum is defined as the product of mass and velocity.

$$
\vec{p}=m\vec{v}
$$

Momentum is a vector because velocity is a vector.

SI unit:

$$
\text{kg m s}^{-1}
$$

Large momentum may result from:

- large mass
- large velocity
- both

Examples:

- A loaded truck moving slowly can have large momentum.
- A bullet has large effect because of high speed.
- A fast cricket ball is harder to stop than a slow one of the same mass.

## 9. Newton's Second Law Of Motion

Newton's second law states:

The rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force.

In SI units, the proportionality constant is 1:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=\frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}
$$

If mass is constant:

$$
\vec{p}=m\vec{v}
$$

so:

$$
\frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}=m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}
$$

Thus:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=m\vec{a}
$$

This is the most commonly used form of Newton's second law.

## 10. Unit Of Force

The SI unit of force is newton.

One newton is the force that gives a mass of 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m s^-2.

$$
1\ \text{N}=1\ \text{kg m s}^{-2}
$$

## 11. Component Form Of Newton's Second Law

Since force and acceleration are vectors:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=m\vec{a}
$$

is equivalent to component equations:

$$
F_x=ma_x
$$

$$
F_y=ma_y
$$

$$
F_z=ma_z
$$

This is very useful in two-dimensional problems.

## 12. Force Is Local And Instantaneous

Acceleration at an instant is caused by the net force at that instant.

A body does not carry force from its past motion.

Example:

If a stone is dropped from a uniformly moving train and air resistance is neglected, the moment it is released the only force on it is gravity. It does not continue to have a horizontal force just because it was moving with the train.

It may have horizontal velocity, but not horizontal acceleration.

## 13. Worked Example: Astronaut Outside Spaceship

An astronaut accidentally separates from a spaceship accelerating in empty interstellar space. Assume no nearby stars exert gravitational force.

Once outside, the astronaut is no longer pushed by the spaceship.

Net force on astronaut:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=\vec{0}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\vec{a}=\vec{0}
$$

The astronaut continues with the velocity he had at the instant of separation.

## 14. Worked Example: Bullet Stopping In Wood

A bullet of mass:

$$
m=0.04\ \text{kg}
$$

enters a wooden block with speed:

$$
u=90\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

and stops after:

$$
s=0.60\ \text{m}
$$

Find average resistive force.

Use:

$$
v^2=u^2+2as
$$

Here:

$$
v=0
$$

So:

$$
0=(90)^2+2a(0.60)
$$

$$
a=-\frac{8100}{1.2}
$$

$$
a=-6750\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

Force:

$$
F=ma
$$

Magnitude:

$$
|F|=0.04(6750)=270\ \text{N}
$$

The force is opposite to the motion of the bullet.

## 15. Impulse

Impulse is force multiplied by time interval.

$$
\vec{J}=\vec{F}\Delta t
$$

For a varying force, impulse is the area under the force-time graph:

$$
\vec{J}=\int \vec{F}\,dt
$$

Impulse equals change in momentum:

$$
\vec{J}=\Delta\vec{p}
$$

Impulse is useful when a large force acts for a short time, such as:

- bat hitting ball
- ball bouncing from wall
- hammer striking nail
- collision between vehicles

## 16. Why A Cricketer Draws Hands Back

When a cricketer catches a fast ball, the ball's momentum must become zero.

The change in momentum is fixed.

Since:

$$
F_{\text{avg}}\Delta t=\Delta p
$$

we have:

$$
F_{\text{avg}}=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}
$$

Increasing the stopping time reduces the average force.

That is why a skilled cricketer moves the hands backward while catching.

## 17. Worked Example: Impulse On A Ball

A ball of mass:

$$
m=0.15\ \text{kg}
$$

comes toward a batsman with speed:

$$
12\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

The batsman hits it back with the same speed in the opposite direction.

Take the direction from batsman to bowler as positive.

Initial velocity:

$$
v_i=-12\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Final velocity:

$$
v_f=+12\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Change in momentum:

$$
\Delta p=m(v_f-v_i)
$$

$$
\Delta p=0.15[12-(-12)]
$$

$$
\Delta p=0.15(24)=3.6\ \text{N s}
$$

Impulse:

$$
\boxed{3.6\ \text{N s}}
$$

in the direction from batsman to bowler.

## 18. Newton's Third Law Of Motion

Newton's third law states:

To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

A clearer physics statement is:

If body A exerts a force on body B, then body B exerts an equal and opposite force on body A.

Mathematically:

$$
\vec{F}_{AB}=-\vec{F}_{BA}
$$

where:

- \(\vec{F}_{AB}\) means force on A by B
- \(\vec{F}_{BA}\) means force on B by A

## 19. Important Points About Third Law

### 19.1 Forces Occur In Pairs

A single isolated force does not exist.

Force is always an interaction between two bodies.

### 19.2 Action And Reaction Are Simultaneous

The two forces occur at the same time.

One does not cause the other after a delay in Newtonian mechanics.

### 19.3 They Act On Different Bodies

This is the most important point.

Action and reaction forces do not cancel when analyzing one body because they act on different bodies.

Example:

Earth pulls a stone downward.

The stone pulls Earth upward with equal force.

These forces act on different bodies.

## 20. Common Third-Law Misconception

For a book resting on a table:

- weight acts on the book by Earth
- normal force acts on the book by table

These two forces are equal and opposite if the book is at rest.

But they are not a third-law pair because both act on the same body, the book.

The third-law pairs are:

- Earth pulls book downward; book pulls Earth upward
- table pushes book upward; book pushes table downward

## 21. Conservation Of Momentum

For an isolated system with no net external force, total momentum remains constant.

$$
\vec{p}_{\text{initial}}=\vec{p}_{\text{final}}
$$

For two bodies A and B:

$$
\vec{p}_A+\vec{p}_B=\vec{p}'_A+\vec{p}'_B
$$

This follows from Newton's second and third laws.

Internal forces cancel in pairs because they are equal and opposite.

External force is required to change total momentum of a system.

## 22. Recoil Of A Gun

Before firing, gun and bullet are at rest.

Total momentum:

$$
0
$$

After firing:

$$
m_b\vec{v}_b+m_g\vec{v}_g=0
$$

So:

$$
\vec{v}_g=-\frac{m_b}{m_g}\vec{v}_b
$$

The gun recoils opposite to the bullet.

## 23. Worked Example: Recoil Speed

A shell of mass:

$$
m_b=0.020\ \text{kg}
$$

is fired from a gun of mass:

$$
m_g=100\ \text{kg}
$$

with shell speed:

$$
v_b=80\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Initial total momentum is zero.

So:

$$
m_bv_b+m_gv_g=0
$$

$$
v_g=-\frac{m_bv_b}{m_g}
$$

$$
v_g=-\frac{0.020(80)}{100}
$$

$$
v_g=-0.016\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

The recoil speed is:

$$
\boxed{0.016\ \text{m s}^{-1}}
$$

opposite to the shell.

## 24. Equilibrium Of A Particle

A particle is in equilibrium when the net external force on it is zero.

$$
\sum \vec{F}=\vec{0}
$$

Then:

$$
\vec{a}=\vec{0}
$$

The particle may be:

- at rest
- moving with constant velocity

For two forces:

$$
\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2=\vec{0}
$$

so:

$$
\vec{F}_1=-\vec{F}_2
$$

For three forces:

$$
\vec{F}_1+\vec{F}_2+\vec{F}_3=\vec{0}
$$

Component form:

$$
\sum F_x=0,\quad \sum F_y=0,\quad \sum F_z=0
$$

## 25. Free-Body Diagram

A free-body diagram is a diagram showing one chosen body or system and all external forces acting on it.

Rules:

1. Choose the system.
2. Draw only that system.
3. Show all forces on the system.
4. Do not show forces exerted by the system on other objects.
5. Resolve forces into components if needed.
6. Apply Newton's second law along each axis.

Most mechanics errors come from drawing forces on the wrong body.

## 26. Worked Example: Suspended Mass With Horizontal Pull

A mass of 6 kg is suspended by a rope. A horizontal force of 50 N is applied at a point on the rope. Take:

$$
g=10\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

Weight:

$$
mg=6(10)=60\ \text{N}
$$

At equilibrium, the upper tension \(T_1\) balances:

- vertical tension \(60\ \text{N}\)
- horizontal force \(50\ \text{N}\)

So:

$$
T_1\cos\theta=60
$$

and:

$$
T_1\sin\theta=50
$$

Divide:

$$
\tan\theta=\frac{50}{60}
$$

$$
\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{5}{6}\right)
$$

$$
\theta\approx40^\circ
$$

## 27. Common Forces In Mechanics

### 27.1 Weight

Weight is gravitational force on a body.

Near Earth:

$$
\vec{W}=m\vec{g}
$$

Magnitude:

$$
W=mg
$$

Direction: vertically downward.

### 27.2 Normal Reaction

Normal reaction is the contact force perpendicular to the surface.

It is not always equal to \(mg\).

It equals \(mg\) only in special cases such as a body at rest on a horizontal surface with no vertical acceleration.

### 27.3 Tension

Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable.

For a light, inextensible string over a smooth pulley, the tension is usually taken to be the same throughout the string.

### 27.4 Spring Force

For small extensions or compressions, spring force is:

$$
\vec{F}=-k\vec{x}
$$

The negative sign shows that spring force is restoring: it acts opposite to displacement from equilibrium.

### 27.5 Friction

Friction is the component of contact force parallel to the contact surface.

It opposes impending or actual relative motion.

## 28. Static Friction

Static friction acts when there is no relative sliding between surfaces.

It is self-adjusting up to a maximum value.

If applied force is small, static friction simply matches it.

The law is:

$$
f_s\le \mu_sN
$$

Maximum static friction:

$$
f_{s,\text{max}}=\mu_sN
$$

where:

- \(\mu_s\) is coefficient of static friction
- \(N\) is normal reaction

Do not write \(f_s=\mu_sN\) unless limiting friction is reached.

## 29. Kinetic Friction

Kinetic friction acts when surfaces slide relative to each other.

Magnitude:

$$
f_k=\mu_kN
$$

where \(\mu_k\) is coefficient of kinetic friction.

Usually:

$$
\mu_k<\mu_s
$$

Kinetic friction opposes actual relative motion.

## 30. Static Friction Can Cause Motion

Friction does not always oppose motion of the body. It opposes relative motion between surfaces.

Example:

A box rests on the floor of an accelerating train. The box accelerates with the train because static friction acts on the box in the forward direction.

Without friction, the train floor would move forward under the box and the box would slide backward relative to the train.

## 31. Maximum Acceleration Without Slipping

A box remains at rest relative to an accelerating train if static friction can provide the required acceleration.

Required force:

$$
ma
$$

Maximum static friction:

$$
\mu_sN=\mu_smg
$$

So:

$$
ma\le \mu_smg
$$

Therefore:

$$
a\le \mu_sg
$$

Maximum acceleration:

$$
a_{\text{max}}=\mu_sg
$$

If:

$$
\mu_s=0.15,\quad g=10\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

then:

$$
a_{\text{max}}=1.5\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

## 32. Angle Of Repose

A block on an inclined plane is just about to slide when:

$$
mg\sin\theta=f_{s,\text{max}}
$$

and:

$$
N=mg\cos\theta
$$

Since:

$$
f_{s,\text{max}}=\mu_sN
$$

we get:

$$
mg\sin\theta=\mu_smg\cos\theta
$$

So:

$$
\mu_s=\tan\theta
$$

At the limiting angle:

$$
\theta_{\text{max}}=\tan^{-1}\mu_s
$$

If:

$$
\theta_{\text{max}}=15^\circ
$$

then:

$$
\mu_s=\tan15^\circ\approx0.27
$$

## 33. Rolling Friction

Rolling friction is usually much smaller than sliding friction.

This is why wheels are useful.

In machines, friction can be reduced by:

- lubricants
- ball bearings
- air cushions

But friction is also essential:

- walking needs friction
- vehicles need friction to accelerate
- brakes need friction to stop motion

## 34. Circular Motion And Centripetal Force

For uniform circular motion:

$$
a_c=\frac{v^2}{R}
$$

By Newton's second law, required inward force is:

$$
F_c=m\frac{v^2}{R}
$$

This is called centripetal force.

Important:

Centripetal force is not a new kind of force. It is the name given to whichever real force provides inward acceleration.

Examples:

| Situation | Force providing centripetal force |
|---|---|
| stone tied to string | tension |
| planet around Sun | gravitational force |
| car turning on flat road | friction |
| electron in atom model | electric force |

## 35. Car On A Level Circular Road

For a car turning on a flat road:

Vertical forces:

$$
N=mg
$$

Friction provides centripetal force:

$$
f_s=\frac{mv^2}{R}
$$

Maximum available static friction:

$$
f_{s,\text{max}}=\mu_sN=\mu_smg
$$

For no slipping:

$$
\frac{mv^2}{R}\le \mu_smg
$$

Cancel \(m\):

$$
v^2\le \mu_sRg
$$

So maximum speed is:

$$
v_{\text{max}}=\sqrt{\mu_sRg}
$$

## 36. Worked Example: Cyclist Turning

A cyclist moves at:

$$
18\ \text{km h}^{-1}
$$

on a level road and takes a circular turn of radius:

$$
R=3\ \text{m}
$$

Coefficient of static friction:

$$
\mu_s=0.1
$$

Convert speed:

$$
18\ \text{km h}^{-1}=5\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Maximum safe speed:

$$
v_{\text{max}}=\sqrt{\mu_sRg}
$$

$$
v_{\text{max}}=\sqrt{0.1(3)(9.8)}
$$

$$
v_{\text{max}}=\sqrt{2.94}\approx1.7\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Since:

$$
5>1.7
$$

the cyclist will slip.

## 37. Banked Road

On a banked road, the normal reaction has a horizontal component that can help provide centripetal force.

If friction is not needed, the ideal speed is:

$$
v_0=\sqrt{Rg\tan\theta}
$$

where:

- \(R\) is radius of turn
- \(\theta\) is banking angle

For a given \(R\) and \(\theta\), driving close to this speed reduces wear of tyres.

With friction, the maximum safe speed is higher than on a flat road.

## 38. Apparent Weight In A Lift

A weighing scale reads the normal force \(N\), not the true gravitational weight \(mg\).

### 38.1 Lift Moving With Constant Velocity

Acceleration:

$$
a=0
$$

So:

$$
N=mg
$$

### 38.2 Lift Accelerating Upward

Take upward positive.

$$
N-mg=ma
$$

So:

$$
N=m(g+a)
$$

Apparent weight increases.

### 38.3 Lift Accelerating Downward

$$
mg-N=ma
$$

So:

$$
N=m(g-a)
$$

Apparent weight decreases.

### 38.4 Free Fall

If:

$$
a=g
$$

then:

$$
N=0
$$

The person feels weightless.

## 39. Solving Mechanics Problems

Use this procedure:

1. Read the problem and identify the body or system.
2. Choose axes.
3. Draw a free-body diagram.
4. Show only external forces on the chosen system.
5. Resolve forces into components.
6. Apply \(\sum F_x=ma_x\) and \(\sum F_y=ma_y\).
7. Use constraints such as same acceleration for connected bodies.
8. Solve algebraically.
9. Check signs and units.

## 40. Worked Example: Block And Floor Yielding

A wooden block of mass 2 kg rests on a floor. Later a 25 kg cylinder is placed on it and the floor yields downward with acceleration:

$$
0.1\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

Take:

$$
g=10\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

### 40.1 Before Floor Yields

Block is at rest.

Weight:

$$
2(10)=20\ \text{N}
$$

Since acceleration is zero:

$$
N=20\ \text{N}
$$

The block pushes the floor downward with:

$$
20\ \text{N}
$$

### 40.2 After Cylinder Is Added

System mass:

$$
2+25=27\ \text{kg}
$$

Weight:

$$
27(10)=270\ \text{N}
$$

Let upward normal force by floor be \(N'\).

The system accelerates downward with:

$$
0.1\ \text{m s}^{-2}
$$

Taking downward positive:

$$
270-N'=27(0.1)
$$

$$
270-N'=2.7
$$

$$
N'=267.3\ \text{N}
$$

So the system pushes the floor downward with:

$$
267.3\ \text{N}
$$

## 41. Common Mistakes

### 41.1 Saying Force Is Needed For Uniform Motion

Net force is needed for acceleration, not for uniform motion.

### 41.2 Treating \(ma\) As An Extra Force

In:

$$
\vec{F}_{\text{net}}=m\vec{a}
$$

\(m\vec{a}\) is not an additional force. It is the result of the net force.

### 41.3 Cancelling Action And Reaction On One Body

Action and reaction forces act on different bodies.

Do not cancel them in the free-body diagram of one body.

### 41.4 Assuming \(N=mg\) Always

Normal reaction equals \(mg\) only in special equilibrium cases.

In an accelerating lift, \(N\) can be greater or less than \(mg\).

### 41.5 Always Writing \(f_s=\mu_sN\)

Static friction is self-adjusting.

Use:

$$
f_s\le \mu_sN
$$

Only at limiting condition:

$$
f_s=\mu_sN
$$

### 41.6 Treating Centripetal Force As A New Force

Centripetal force is the inward net force required for circular motion. It must be supplied by a real interaction such as tension, gravity, friction, or normal force.

## 46. Practice Set D: Momentum

Two bodies collide in an isolated system.

Before collision:

$$
\vec{p}_{\text{total}}=m_1\vec{u}_1+m_2\vec{u}_2
$$

After collision:

$$
\vec{p}_{\text{total}}=m_1\vec{v}_1+m_2\vec{v}_2
$$

Conservation gives:

$$
m_1\vec{u}_1+m_2\vec{u}_2=m_1\vec{v}_1+m_2\vec{v}_2
$$

Use signs carefully in one dimension.

## 47. Practice Set E: Friction

### 47.1 Static Friction Limit

If:

$$
\mu_s=0.4,\quad N=100\ \text{N}
$$

then:

$$
f_{s,\text{max}}=0.4(100)=40\ \text{N}
$$

If the applied force is 25 N, static friction is 25 N.

If the applied force is 45 N, the body begins to slide.

### 47.2 Kinetic Friction

If:

$$
\mu_k=0.3,\quad N=100\ \text{N}
$$

then:

$$
f_k=0.3(100)=30\ \text{N}
$$
