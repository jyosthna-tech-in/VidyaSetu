# Chapter 1 - Units And Measurements

## 1. Chapter Purpose

Physics is a quantitative science. It does not only ask what happens; it asks how much, how fast, how far, how massive, how hot, and how accurately these quantities can be known.

This chapter builds the measurement language used in all later Physics chapters. Every formula in mechanics, heat, waves, electricity, and modern physics depends on units, dimensions, and correct handling of measured numbers.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- explain what measurement means
- distinguish between base and derived quantities
- identify the seven SI base units
- use common non-SI units when needed
- understand significant figures
- round off results correctly
- apply rules of significant figures in arithmetic
- estimate uncertainty in simple measurements
- write dimensional formulae
- check dimensional consistency of equations
- use dimensional analysis to find relations among physical quantities
- understand the limitations of dimensional analysis

## 2. Measurement

Measurement means comparing a physical quantity with a standard quantity of the same kind.

The standard used for comparison is called a unit.

For example:

If the length of a table is measured as 2 m, it means the table is twice the standard unit called metre.

A measurement has two parts:

- numerical value
- unit

Example:

$$
5.0\ \text{m}
$$

Here:

- \(5.0\) is the numerical value
- m is the unit

Writing only "5.0" is incomplete because it does not say what is being measured.

Writing only "m" is incomplete because it does not say how many metres.

## 3. Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is a property that can be measured.

Examples:

- length
- mass
- time
- speed
- force
- energy
- temperature
- electric current

Some physical quantities are chosen as base quantities. Other quantities are obtained from them.

## 4. Base Quantities And Derived Quantities

### 4.1 Base Quantities

Base quantities are independent quantities from which other quantities can be built.

The SI system uses seven base quantities.

| Base quantity | SI unit | Unit symbol | Dimension symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| length | metre | m | \(L\) |
| mass | kilogram | kg | \(M\) |
| time | second | s | \(T\) |
| electric current | ampere | A | \(A\) |
| thermodynamic temperature | kelvin | K | \(K\) |
| amount of substance | mole | mol | mol |
| luminous intensity | candela | cd | cd |

### 4.2 Derived Quantities

Derived quantities are obtained from base quantities.

Examples:

Speed is distance per time:

$$
v=\frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}
$$

Its SI unit is:

$$
\text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Area is length multiplied by length:

$$
A=l \times b
$$

Its SI unit is:

$$
\text{m}^2
$$

Force is mass multiplied by acceleration:

$$
F=ma
$$

Its SI unit is:

$$
\text{kg m s}^{-2}
$$

This unit is also called newton:

$$
1\ \text{N}=1\ \text{kg m s}^{-2}
$$

## 5. System Of Units

A complete set of base units and derived units is called a system of units.

Historically, three systems were commonly used:

| System | Unit of length | Unit of mass | Unit of time |
|---|---|---|---|
| CGS | centimetre | gram | second |
| FPS | foot | pound | second |
| MKS | metre | kilogram | second |

The internationally accepted system is the SI system.

SI stands for Systeme International d'Unites, meaning International System of Units.

## 6. The SI System

The SI system is used because:

- it is internationally accepted
- it is decimal-based
- it makes unit conversion easier
- it gives a standard language for science and engineering

In SI, physical quantities are expressed using standard unit symbols.

Examples:

| Quantity | Correct unit symbol |
|---|---|
| metre | m |
| kilogram | kg |
| second | s |
| ampere | A |
| kelvin | K |
| mole | mol |
| candela | cd |
| newton | N |
| joule | J |
| watt | W |

## 7. Plane Angle And Solid Angle

Apart from the seven base units, two important angle-related quantities are often used.

### 7.1 Plane Angle

Plane angle is defined as:

$$
\theta=\frac{\text{arc length}}{\text{radius}}
$$

The SI unit is radian:

$$
\text{rad}
$$

Since it is length divided by length, it is dimensionless.

### 7.2 Solid Angle

Solid angle is three-dimensional angle.

It is defined using area on a sphere:

$$
\Omega=\frac{\text{area intercepted on sphere}}{\text{radius}^2}
$$

The SI unit is steradian:

$$
\text{sr}
$$

It is also dimensionless.

## 8. Some Common Derived Units

| Quantity | Formula idea | SI unit | Special name |
|---|---|---|---|
| area | length x length | m^2 | none |
| volume | length x length x length | m^3 | none |
| speed | length / time | m s^-1 | none |
| acceleration | speed / time | m s^-2 | none |
| force | mass x acceleration | kg m s^-2 | newton |
| work | force x displacement | kg m^2 s^-2 | joule |
| power | work / time | kg m^2 s^-3 | watt |
| pressure | force / area | kg m^-1 s^-2 | pascal |

These derived units are built from base units.

## 9. Units Outside SI That Are Still Used

Some non-SI units are commonly retained because they are convenient in daily life or special fields.

Examples:

| Quantity | Unit | Relation |
|---|---|---|
| time | minute | \(1\ \text{min}=60\ \text{s}\) |
| time | hour | \(1\ \text{h}=3600\ \text{s}\) |
| time | day | \(1\ \text{d}=86400\ \text{s}\) |
| volume | litre | \(1\ \text{L}=10^{-3}\ \text{m}^3\) |
| mass | tonne | \(1\ \text{t}=10^3\ \text{kg}\) |
| energy | electron volt | \(1\ \text{eV}=1.602\times10^{-19}\ \text{J}\) |

These units should be converted to SI when using standard formulae.

## 10. Scientific Notation

Scientific notation expresses numbers as:

$$
a \times 10^b
$$

where:

$$
1 \le a < 10
$$

and \(b\) is an integer.

Examples:

$$
300000000 = 3.0 \times 10^8
$$

$$
0.000000000106 = 1.06 \times 10^{-10}
$$

Scientific notation is useful because physical quantities may be extremely large or extremely small.

## 11. Order Of Magnitude

The order of magnitude of a quantity is the nearest power of 10 that gives its rough size.

Example:

The diameter of Earth is approximately:

$$
1.28 \times 10^7\ \text{m}
$$

This is of the order:

$$
10^7\ \text{m}
$$

The diameter of a hydrogen atom is approximately:

$$
1.06 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{m}
$$

This is of the order:

$$
10^{-10}\ \text{m}
$$

So Earth is roughly:

$$
10^{17}
$$

times larger in diameter than a hydrogen atom.

## 12. Significant Figures

Every measurement has uncertainty. Significant figures show the precision of a measurement.

Significant figures include:

- all reliably known digits
- the first uncertain digit

Example:

If a pendulum time period is measured as:

$$
1.62\ \text{s}
$$

then 1 and 6 are reliable, and 2 is the first uncertain digit.

So the measurement has 3 significant figures.

## 13. Rules For Counting Significant Figures

### 13.1 Non-Zero Digits

All non-zero digits are significant.

Examples:

| Number | Significant figures |
|---:|---:|
| 237 | 3 |
| 4.86 | 3 |
| 91.2 | 3 |

### 13.2 Zeros Between Non-Zero Digits

Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.

Examples:

| Number | Significant figures |
|---:|---:|
| 205 | 3 |
| 3007 | 4 |
| 4.006 | 4 |

### 13.3 Leading Zeros

Zeros to the left of the first non-zero digit are not significant.

Examples:

| Number | Significant figures |
|---:|---:|
| 0.007 | 1 |
| 0.000603 | 3 |
| 0.02308 | 4 |

Leading zeros only locate the decimal point.

### 13.4 Trailing Zeros Without Decimal Point

Trailing zeros in a number without a decimal point are usually not significant.

Example:

$$
12300
$$

normally has 3 significant figures unless more information is given.

### 13.5 Trailing Zeros With Decimal Point

Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant.

Examples:

| Number | Significant figures |
|---:|---:|
| 3.500 | 4 |
| 0.06900 | 4 |
| 4.700 | 4 |

### 13.6 Scientific Notation Removes Ambiguity

The number:

$$
4700
$$

may be ambiguous.

But:

$$
4.700 \times 10^3
$$

clearly has 4 significant figures.

The power of 10 does not affect the number of significant figures.

## 14. Exact Numbers

Exact numbers have infinite significant figures.

Examples:

- the 2 in circumference \(2\pi r\)
- the 6 in surface area of a cube \(6a^2\)
- the number of oscillations counted in an experiment
- conversion definitions such as \(1\ \text{min}=60\ \text{s}\)

Exact numbers do not limit the significant figures of the final answer.

## 15. Arithmetic With Significant Figures

### 15.1 Multiplication And Division

In multiplication or division, the final answer should have as many significant figures as the measured value with the least significant figures.

Example:

Mass:

$$
4.237\ \text{g}
$$

Volume:

$$
2.51\ \text{cm}^3
$$

Density:

$$
\rho=\frac{4.237}{2.51}=1.6880478...\ \text{g cm}^{-3}
$$

The mass has 4 significant figures.

The volume has 3 significant figures.

So the density must be reported with 3 significant figures:

$$
\rho=1.69\ \text{g cm}^{-3}
$$

### 15.2 Addition And Subtraction

In addition or subtraction, the final answer should have as many decimal places as the value with the least decimal places.

Example:

$$
436.32\ \text{g}+227.2\ \text{g}+0.301\ \text{g}
$$

Ordinary addition gives:

$$
663.821\ \text{g}
$$

The least precise value is:

$$
227.2\ \text{g}
$$

It has only one decimal place.

So the final answer should be:

$$
663.8\ \text{g}
$$

### 15.3 Why Rules Differ

For multiplication and division, significant figures matter.

For addition and subtraction, decimal places matter.

This is because addition and subtraction compare positions of digits directly.

## 16. Rounding Off

Rounding off means removing unnecessary uncertain digits.

### 16.1 Basic Rule

If the digit to be dropped is less than 5, leave the previous digit unchanged.

Example:

$$
1.743 \to 1.74
$$

If the digit to be dropped is more than 5, increase the previous digit by 1.

Example:

$$
2.746 \to 2.75
$$

### 16.2 When The Dropped Digit Is Exactly 5

If the digit to be dropped is exactly 5, use the even-rule convention:

- if the preceding digit is even, leave it unchanged
- if the preceding digit is odd, increase it by 1

Examples:

$$
2.745 \to 2.74
$$

because 4 is even.

$$
2.735 \to 2.74
$$

because 3 is odd.

### 16.3 Intermediate Steps

In long calculations, keep one extra digit during intermediate steps.

Round only at the end.

This reduces rounding error.

Each side of a cube is measured as:

$$
7.203\ \text{m}
$$

This has 4 significant figures.

### 17.1 Surface Area

$$
A=6a^2
$$

$$
A=6(7.203)^2
$$

$$
A=311.299254\ \text{m}^2
$$

Rounded to 4 significant figures:

$$
A=311.3\ \text{m}^2
$$

### 17.2 Volume

Volume:

$$
V=a^3
$$

$$
V=(7.203)^3
$$

$$
V=373.714754\ \text{m}^3
$$

Rounded to 4 significant figures:

$$
V=373.7\ \text{m}^3
$$

## 18. Worked Example: Density

Mass:

$$
5.74\ \text{g}
$$

Volume:

$$
1.2\ \text{cm}^3
$$

Density:

$$
\rho=\frac{m}{V}
$$

$$
\rho=\frac{5.74}{1.2}=4.7833...\ \text{g cm}^{-3}
$$

Mass has 3 significant figures.

Volume has 2 significant figures.

So density should have 2 significant figures:

$$
\rho=4.8\ \text{g cm}^{-3}
$$

## 19. Uncertainty In Measurement

No measurement is perfectly exact.

The uncertainty depends on:

- least count of the measuring instrument
- skill of observation
- experimental conditions
- repeated variation in readings

If a length is measured as:

$$
16.2\ \text{cm}
$$

with uncertainty:

$$
\pm 0.1\ \text{cm}
$$

we write:

$$
l=16.2 \pm 0.1\ \text{cm}
$$

## 20. Absolute Error And Relative Error

Absolute error is the uncertainty in the same unit as the measurement.

Relative error is:

$$
\text{Relative error}=\frac{\text{absolute error}}{\text{measured value}}
$$

Percentage error is:

$$
\text{Percentage error}=\frac{\text{absolute error}}{\text{measured value}}\times100
$$

Example:

If:

$$
m=1.02 \pm 0.01\ \text{g}
$$

then percentage error is:

$$
\frac{0.01}{1.02}\times100 \approx 1\%
$$

If:

$$
m=9.89 \pm 0.01\ \text{g}
$$

then:

$$
\frac{0.01}{9.89}\times100 \approx 0.1\%
$$

The same absolute error can produce different relative errors.

## 21. Combining Uncertainties

For multiplication and division, relative errors add approximately.

If:

$$
Q=ab
$$

then:

$$
\frac{\Delta Q}{Q} \approx \frac{\Delta a}{a}+\frac{\Delta b}{b}
$$

For powers:

$$
Q=a^n
$$

then:

$$
\frac{\Delta Q}{Q} \approx n\frac{\Delta a}{a}
$$

These rules help estimate uncertainty in calculated quantities.

## 22. Example: Area With Uncertainty

Suppose:

$$
l=16.2 \pm 0.1\ \text{cm}
$$

and:

$$
b=10.1 \pm 0.1\ \text{cm}
$$

Area:

$$
A=lb
$$

Numerical value:

$$
A=16.2 \times 10.1=163.62\ \text{cm}^2
$$

Percentage error in length:

$$
\frac{0.1}{16.2}\times100 \approx 0.6\%
$$

Percentage error in breadth:

$$
\frac{0.1}{10.1}\times100 \approx 1.0\%
$$

Total percentage error:

$$
0.6\%+1.0\%=1.6\%
$$

Uncertainty in area:

$$
1.6\%\ \text{of}\ 163.62 \approx 2.6\ \text{cm}^2
$$

So:

$$
A=164 \pm 3\ \text{cm}^2
$$

## 23. Dimensions Of Physical Quantities

Dimensions describe the nature of a physical quantity in terms of base quantities.

The main mechanical dimensions are:

| Quantity | Dimension symbol |
|---|---|
| mass | \(M\) |
| length | \(L\) |
| time | \(T\) |

For many mechanics problems, only \(M\), \(L\), and \(T\) are needed.

Examples:

Length has dimension:

$$
[L]
$$

Mass has dimension:

$$
[M]
$$

Time has dimension:

$$
[T]
$$

## 24. Dimensional Formula

The dimensional formula of a physical quantity shows how it depends on base dimensions.

### 24.1 Area

Area:

$$
A=\text{length}\times\text{length}
$$

So:

$$
[A]=[L^2]
$$

### 24.2 Volume

Volume:

$$
V=\text{length}\times\text{length}\times\text{length}
$$

So:

$$
[V]=[L^3]
$$

### 24.3 Speed

Speed:

$$
v=\frac{\text{length}}{\text{time}}
$$

So:

$$
[v]=[L T^{-1}]
$$

### 24.4 Acceleration

Acceleration:

$$
a=\frac{\text{velocity}}{\text{time}}
$$

So:

$$
[a]=[L T^{-2}]
$$

### 24.5 Force

Force:

$$
F=ma
$$

So:

$$
[F]=[M][L T^{-2}]
$$

Therefore:

$$
[F]=[M L T^{-2}]
$$

### 24.6 Energy

Work or energy:

$$
W=F \times s
$$

So:

$$
[W]=[M L T^{-2}][L]
$$

Therefore:

$$
[W]=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

### 24.7 Power

Power:

$$
P=\frac{W}{t}
$$

So:

$$
[P]=[M L^2 T^{-3}]
$$

### 24.8 Density

Mass density:

$$
\rho=\frac{m}{V}
$$

So:

$$
[\rho]=[M L^{-3}]
$$

## 25. Dimensional Equation

A dimensional equation equates a physical quantity to its dimensional formula.

Examples:

$$
[v]=[M^0 L T^{-1}]
$$

$$
[a]=[M^0 L T^{-2}]
$$

$$
[F]=[M L T^{-2}]
$$

$$
[\rho]=[M L^{-3} T^0]
$$

The powers of missing dimensions are zero.

## 26. Principle Of Homogeneity Of Dimensions

Only quantities with the same dimensions can be added or subtracted.

For example:

- length can be added to length
- time can be added to time
- velocity cannot be added to force
- mass cannot be subtracted from temperature

In a correct physical equation, every term must have the same dimensions.

This is called the principle of homogeneity of dimensions.

## 27. Checking Dimensional Consistency

Consider the equation of motion:

$$
x=x_0+v_0t+\frac{1}{2}at^2
$$

Check each term.

The left side:

$$
[x]=[L]
$$

First term:

$$
[x_0]=[L]
$$

Second term:

$$
[v_0t]=[L T^{-1}][T]=[L]
$$

Third term:

$$
[at^2]=[L T^{-2}][T^2]=[L]
$$

All terms have dimension \([L]\), so the equation is dimensionally consistent.

## 28. Important Warning About Dimensional Consistency

If an equation is dimensionally wrong, it is definitely wrong.

If an equation is dimensionally correct, it may still be physically wrong.

Dimensional analysis cannot find numerical constants such as:

$$
2,\quad \frac{1}{2},\quad \pi
$$

It also cannot identify whether a dimensionless function should be:

$$
\sin x,\quad \cos x,\quad e^x,\quad \log x
$$

The argument of trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions must be dimensionless.

## 29. Worked Example: Checking Energy Equation

Check whether:

$$
\frac{1}{2}mv^2=mgh
$$

is dimensionally correct.

Left side:

$$
[mv^2]=[M][L T^{-1}]^2
$$

$$
[mv^2]=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

Right side:

$$
[mgh]=[M][L T^{-2}][L]
$$

$$
[mgh]=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

Both sides have the same dimensions.

Therefore, the equation is dimensionally correct.

## 30. Worked Example: Ruling Out Wrong Formulae

Kinetic energy has dimensions:

$$
[K]=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

Check the proposed formulae.

### 30.1 \(K=m^2v^3\)

$$
[m^2v^3]=[M^2][L T^{-1}]^3
$$

$$
=[M^2 L^3 T^{-3}]
$$

This is not the dimension of energy.

So it is ruled out.

### 30.2 \(K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2\)

$$
[mv^2]=[M][L T^{-1}]^2=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

This has the correct dimensions.

It cannot be ruled out.

### 30.3 \(K=ma\)

$$
[ma]=[M][L T^{-2}]=[M L T^{-2}]
$$

This is force, not energy.

So it is ruled out.

### 30.4 \(K=\frac{3}{16}mv^2\)

The numerical factor is dimensionless, so:

$$
\left[\frac{3}{16}mv^2\right]=[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

This has the correct dimensions.

It cannot be ruled out by dimensional analysis alone.

### 30.5 \(K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2+ma\)

The first term has dimensions of energy.

The second term has dimensions of force.

Energy and force cannot be added.

So this expression is dimensionally wrong.

## 31. Deducing A Relation By Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis can sometimes help find how one quantity depends on others.

The method works best when:

- the dependent quantity is known
- the relevant independent quantities are known
- the relation is assumed to be a product of powers

## 32. Worked Example: Time Period Of Simple Pendulum

Suppose the time period \(T\) of a simple pendulum depends on:

- length \(l\)
- acceleration due to gravity \(g\)
- mass \(m\)

Assume:

$$
T=k l^x g^y m^z
$$

where \(k\) is dimensionless.

Write dimensions:

$$
[T]=[L]^x[L T^{-2}]^y[M]^z
$$

So:

$$
[T]=[L^{x+y}T^{-2y}M^z]
$$

Compare powers of \(M\), \(L\), and \(T\).

For mass:

$$
z=0
$$

For length:

$$
x+y=0
$$

For time:

$$
-2y=1
$$

So:

$$
y=-\frac{1}{2}
$$

and:

$$
x=\frac{1}{2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
T=k l^{1/2}g^{-1/2}
$$

or:

$$
T=k\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}
$$

Dimensional analysis cannot give \(k\).

Experiment and theory give:

$$
k=2\pi
$$

So:

$$
T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}
$$

## 33. Limitations Of Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is powerful, but limited.

It cannot:

- find dimensionless numerical constants
- decide between formulae with the same dimensions
- derive formulae involving addition of several terms unless the form is already known
- identify exact functions such as sine, cosine, logarithm, or exponential
- distinguish between different physical quantities with the same dimensions

Example:

Work and torque have the same dimensions:

$$
[M L^2 T^{-2}]
$$

But they are physically different quantities.

## 34. Unit Conversion Strategy

When converting units:

1. write the starting value with units
2. multiply by conversion factors equal to 1
3. cancel old units
4. keep the required new unit

Example:

Convert:

$$
18\ \text{km h}^{-1}
$$

to:

$$
\text{m s}^{-1}
$$

Use:

$$
1\ \text{km}=1000\ \text{m}
$$

and:

$$
1\ \text{h}=3600\ \text{s}
$$

Then:

$$
18\ \text{km h}^{-1}=18\times\frac{1000\ \text{m}}{3600\ \text{s}}
$$

$$
=5\ \text{m s}^{-1}
$$

## 40. Practice Set F: Estimation Problems

### 40.1 Diameter Of A Thread

You are given a thread and a metre scale.

Method:

1. Wind the thread tightly around a pencil many times, say 50 turns.
2. Measure the total width of the turns using the metre scale.
3. Divide the total width by 50.

If 50 turns occupy 2.5 cm:

$$
\text{diameter}=\frac{2.5\ \text{cm}}{50}=0.05\ \text{cm}
$$

This improves accuracy because a very small thickness is converted into a larger measurable length.

### 40.2 Thickness Of Hair

A microscope has magnification 100.

The observed width of hair is:

$$
3.5\ \text{mm}
$$

Actual thickness:

$$
\frac{3.5\ \text{mm}}{100}=0.035\ \text{mm}
$$

So:

$$
\boxed{0.035\ \text{mm}}
$$

## 41. Practice Set G: Atomic Scale

The angstrom is commonly used on the atomic scale.

Instead of using the non-ASCII symbol, write:

$$
1\ \text{angstrom}=10^{-10}\ \text{m}
$$

If the size of a hydrogen atom is about:

$$
0.5\ \text{angstrom}
$$

then:

$$
r=0.5\times10^{-10}\ \text{m}
$$

For a rough atomic volume, use:

$$
V=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3
$$

For one mole, multiply by Avogadro number:

$$
N_A=6.022\times10^{23}
$$

This shows how unit conversion and order of magnitude combine in physical estimates.

## 42. Common Mistakes

### 42.1 Writing A Number Without Unit

Wrong:

$$
l=5
$$

Correct:

$$
l=5\ \text{m}
$$

### 42.2 Changing Units And Changing Significant Figures

A change of unit should not change the actual precision.

If:

$$
4.700\ \text{m}
$$

is written as:

$$
4.700\times10^3\ \text{mm}
$$

it still has 4 significant figures.

### 42.3 Using Multiplication Rules For Addition

For addition and subtraction, count decimal places, not significant figures.

### 42.4 Rounding Too Early

In long calculations, rounding too early can change the final answer.

Keep one extra digit during intermediate steps.

### 42.5 Treating Dimensional Correctness As Proof

Dimensional correctness is not proof of physical correctness.

It is only a necessary condition.

## 45. One Page Revision

Measurement:

$$
\text{physical quantity}=\text{numerical value}\times\text{unit}
$$

SI base units:

| Quantity | Unit |
|---|---|
| length | m |
| mass | kg |
| time | s |
| current | A |
| temperature | K |
| amount | mol |
| luminous intensity | cd |

Significant figures:

- non-zero digits are significant
- zeros between non-zero digits are significant
- leading zeros are not significant
- trailing zeros after a decimal are significant
- scientific notation removes ambiguity

Multiplication and division:

- final answer has the same number of significant figures as the least precise measured value

Addition and subtraction:

- final answer has the same number of decimal places as the least precise measured value

Dimensions:

$$
[v]=[L T^{-1}]
$$

$$
[a]=[L T^{-2}]
$$

$$
[F]=[M L T^{-2}]
$$

$$
[E]=[M L^2T^{-2}]
$$

Dimensional consistency:

- every term in a valid physical equation must have the same dimensions
- dimensionally wrong means physically wrong
- dimensionally correct does not guarantee physically correct
