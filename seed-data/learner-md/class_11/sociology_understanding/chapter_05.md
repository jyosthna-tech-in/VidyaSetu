## Chapter Overview

Indian sociology developed in conversation with colonial knowledge, nationalism, social reform, anthropology, and the study of caste, tribe, village, religion, and social change.

This chapter introduces important Indian sociologists who shaped the discipline: G.S. Ghurye, D.P. Mukerji, A.R. Desai, and M.N. Srinivas. Their work gave sociology in India its own concerns and methods.

## 1. Context of Indian Sociology

Indian sociology emerged in a society marked by:

1. Colonial rule.
2. Caste hierarchy.
3. Religious diversity.
4. Tribal communities.
5. Village life.
6. Nationalist struggle.
7. Social reform.
8. Modern education.
9. Urbanisation.
10. Development planning.

Indian sociologists had to understand both tradition and change.

## 2. Colonial Knowledge and Indian Society

British administrators collected information through:

1. Census.
2. Surveys.
3. Gazetteers.
4. Ethnographic accounts.
5. Anthropometric measurement.

This knowledge was often shaped by colonial assumptions. Indian sociologists used, criticised, and reworked these materials.

## 3. G.S. Ghurye

Govind Sadashiv Ghurye was one of the founding figures of Indian sociology.

Major areas of work:

1. Caste.
2. Race.
3. Tribe.
4. Kinship.
5. Family.
6. Marriage.
7. Culture.
8. Religion.
9. Cities.
10. Social integration.

He helped institutionalise sociology at Bombay University and trained many scholars.

## 4. Ghurye on Caste

Ghurye gave a systematic definition of caste.

He identified important features:

1. Segmental division.
2. Hierarchy.
3. Restrictions on social interaction and food.
4. Differential rights and duties.
5. Hereditary occupation.
6. Restrictions on marriage.

His work made caste a central theme in Indian sociology.

## 5. Segmental Division

Segmental division means society is divided into closed caste groups.

Caste membership is decided by birth. A person cannot ordinarily choose or change caste.

This makes caste different from many achieved statuses.

## 6. Hierarchy

Caste groups are ranked higher or lower.

This hierarchy is linked with ideas of purity, pollution, occupation, ritual status, and social distance.

Legal equality has challenged caste hierarchy, but social practices may continue.

## 7. Caste and Race Debate

In colonial anthropology, some scholars argued that caste originated in race.

Ghurye examined such arguments and showed that racial explanations were limited and regionally uneven.

Today, racial theories of caste are not accepted as scientific explanations, but studying the debate helps understand early sociology and anthropology.

## 8. Ghurye on Tribes

Ghurye argued that many tribal communities were not completely isolated from Hindu society.

He described tribes as "backward Hindus", reflecting a nationalist assimilationist view.

This view was debated. Protectionist scholars worried that assimilation would lead to exploitation and loss of tribal culture.

The debate remains relevant in questions of tribal identity, development, displacement, and autonomy.

## 9. D.P. Mukerji

Dhurjati Prasad Mukerji was an influential sociologist, teacher, and public intellectual.

He came to sociology through history and economics.

Major concerns:

1. Indian tradition.
2. Social change.
3. Culture.
4. Music.
5. Marxism as method.
6. Indian modernity.
7. Group life.

## 10. D.P. Mukerji on Tradition

D.P. argued that Indian society is deeply shaped by social traditions.

For him, tradition was not dead repetition. It was living, adaptive, and changing.

He believed Indian sociologists must understand:

1. Sanskritic and classical traditions.
2. Regional languages.
3. Folk practices.
4. Group life.
5. Historical continuity.
6. Internal change.

## 11. Tradition and Change

D.P. Mukerji argued that change in India often occurs within tradition rather than by completely breaking it.

He emphasised:

1. Parampara or succession.
2. Itihas or history.
3. Anubhava or lived experience.
4. Collective experience of groups.
5. Bhakti and Sufi traditions as forces of change.

He criticised blind imitation of Western models.

## 12. A.R. Desai

A.R. Desai used a Marxist approach to study Indian society.

Major themes:

1. Indian nationalism.
2. State and society.
3. Peasant struggles.
4. Class structure.
5. Urbanisation.
6. Development.
7. Social movements.

Desai focused on political economy and class relations.

## 13. Desai on Indian Nationalism

Desai argued that Indian nationalism must be understood in relation to material conditions and class forces under colonialism.

He studied how modern industries, railways, communication, education, and new classes contributed to nationalism.

His approach differed from purely cultural or spiritual explanations.

## 14. M.N. Srinivas

Mysore Narasimhachar Srinivas was central to Indian sociology and social anthropology.

Major contributions:

1. Village studies.
2. Fieldwork method.
3. Sanskritisation.
4. Dominant caste.
5. Social change.
6. Caste in modern India.

He encouraged close empirical study of Indian society.

## 15. Village Studies

Srinivas showed that villages are important sites for studying caste, kinship, power, economy, and change.

Village studies helped sociologists understand:

1. Local caste relations.
2. Land and power.
3. Ritual hierarchy.
4. Factional politics.
5. Social mobility.
6. Rural change after independence.

## 16. Sanskritisation

Sanskritisation is the process by which a lower or middle caste attempts to raise its social status by adopting practices associated with higher castes.

Examples may include changes in:

1. Ritual practices.
2. Food habits.
3. Dress.
4. Marriage customs.
5. Worship patterns.

Sanskritisation is a form of social mobility within the caste system, but it does not abolish caste inequality.

## 17. Dominant Caste

Dominant caste refers to a caste that is powerful in a local or regional setting because of:

1. Numerical strength.
2. Land ownership.
3. Economic resources.
4. Political power.
5. Intermediate or high ritual position.

This concept helped explain rural power in democratic India.

## 18. Comparing Indian Sociologists

| Sociologist | Main Focus | Key Ideas |
|---|---|---|
| G.S. Ghurye | Caste, race, tribe, culture | Features of caste, tribe-Hindu relation |
| D.P. Mukerji | Tradition and change | Living tradition, collective experience |
| A.R. Desai | Political economy | Marxist study of nationalism and state |
| M.N. Srinivas | Village, caste, mobility | Sanskritisation, dominant caste, fieldwork |

## 19. Indian Sociology: Shared Concerns

Indian sociology has repeatedly studied:

1. Caste.
2. Tribe.
3. Village.
4. Kinship.
5. Religion.
6. Class.
7. Nationalism.
8. Development.
9. Social movements.
10. Tradition and modernity.

These themes remain important today.

## 20. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Administrator-anthropologists | Colonial officials who studied Indian society |
| Anthropometry | Measurement of human bodies for racial classification |
| Endogamy | Marriage within a defined group |
| Exogamy | Marriage outside a prohibited group |
| Assimilation | Absorption of one culture into another |
| Sanskritisation | Status mobility through adopting higher-caste practices |
| Dominant caste | Locally powerful caste group |
| Marxist sociology | Analysis focused on class, economy, and power |
| Tradition | Inherited practices and meanings transmitted over time |

## 21. Exam-Oriented Summary

Indian sociology developed through engagement with colonial knowledge, nationalism, caste, tribe, village, and social change. G.S. Ghurye systematised the study of caste and debated tribes and race. D.P. Mukerji stressed Indian tradition as living and changing. A.R. Desai used a Marxist approach to study nationalism, state, and class. M.N. Srinivas advanced village studies and concepts such as Sanskritisation and dominant caste.

These thinkers show that Indian sociology is not a copy of Western sociology. It developed through Indian problems, fieldwork, historical analysis, and debate over tradition and modernity.
