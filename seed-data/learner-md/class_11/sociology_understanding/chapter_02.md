## Chapter Overview

Societies change constantly, but they also seek order. Social order refers to relatively stable patterns that make life predictable. Social change refers to transformations in institutions, relationships, values, technology, economy, and power.

This chapter studies change and order in rural and urban settings. It shows that villages and cities are not separate worlds. They are connected through migration, markets, media, state policy, and development.

## 1. Meaning of Social Change

Social change means significant alteration in social structures, institutions, norms, values, or relationships over time.

Examples:

1. Change in land ownership.
2. Expansion of education.
3. Migration to cities.
4. Decline of some caste restrictions.
5. Growth of new occupations.
6. Changes in family structure.
7. Spread of digital communication.

## 2. Meaning of Social Order

Social order refers to stable arrangements that allow society to function.

It includes:

1. Norms.
2. Institutions.
3. Authority.
4. Routine.
5. Social control.
6. Predictable roles.

Order does not mean equality. An unequal society may also have order, but that order may be unjust.

## 3. Change and Order Together

Change and order are connected.

Too much rigidity can produce conflict. Too much sudden change can produce insecurity.

Society constantly balances:

1. Continuity and transformation.
2. Tradition and modernity.
3. Stability and conflict.
4. Authority and resistance.

## 4. sources of Social Change

Social change may arise from:

1. Technology.
2. Economic change.
3. State policy.
4. Social movements.
5. Education.
6. Migration.
7. War.
8. Environmental crisis.
9. Media.
10. Globalisation.

Different groups experience change differently.

## 5. Rural Society

Rural society is often associated with villages, agriculture, caste relations, land, kinship, and local community.

But villages are not isolated or unchanging.

They are connected to:

1. Cities.
2. Markets.
3. State programmes.
4. Migration.
5. Television and mobile phones.
6. Roads and transport.
7. Schools.
8. Political parties.

## 6. Agrarian Structure

Agrarian structure refers to social relations around land and agriculture.

It includes:

1. Landowners.
2. Tenants.
3. Sharecroppers.
4. Agricultural labourers.
5. Moneylenders.
6. Caste groups.
7. State agencies.

Land ownership often shapes power in rural society.

## 7. Land Reforms

Land reforms after independence aimed to reduce inequality in land relations.

They included:

1. Abolition of intermediaries.
2. Tenancy reforms.
3. Land ceiling laws.
4. Redistribution of surplus land.

Implementation varied across regions.

Land reform changed rural power in some areas but did not eliminate inequality.

## 8. Dominant Caste

M.N. Srinivas used the term dominant caste for caste groups that combine:

1. Numerical strength.
2. Land ownership.
3. Economic power.
4. Political influence.
5. Intermediate or high ritual status.

Dominant castes often shape village politics and local power.

Their dominance may be challenged by lower caste movements, landless labourers, and democratic politics.

## 9. Rural Change

Rural change may result from:

1. Land reforms.
2. Green Revolution.
3. Mechanisation.
4. Migration.
5. Education.
6. Panchayati Raj.
7. Welfare schemes.
8. Caste mobilisation.
9. Rural employment programmes.
10. Market integration.

These changes can empower some groups while displacing or weakening others.

## 10. Rural Conflict

Rural conflict may arise over:

1. Land.
2. Wages.
3. Water.
4. Caste status.
5. Political representation.
6. Access to common resources.
7. Gender rights.
8. Development benefits.

Conflict shows that rural order is not always harmonious.

## 11. Urban Society

Urban society is associated with cities and towns.

Features:

1. Large population.
2. Density.
3. Occupational diversity.
4. Formal institutions.
5. Markets.
6. Migration.
7. Anonymity.
8. Inequality.
9. Infrastructure.

Cities are centres of opportunity and exclusion.

## 12. Urbanisation

Urbanisation means growth of urban population and urban ways of life.

It is caused by:

1. Industrialisation.
2. Migration.
3. Expansion of services.
4. Education.
5. Transport.
6. Administrative growth.
7. Market opportunities.

Urbanisation changes family, work, housing, politics, and identity.

## 13. Migration and the City

People migrate to cities for:

1. Work.
2. Education.
3. Safety.
4. Marriage.
5. Better services.
6. Escape from rural constraints.

Migrants may face:

1. Informal work.
2. Slum housing.
3. Discrimination.
4. Lack of documents.
5. Poor sanitation.
6. Insecurity of livelihood.

## 14. Gated Communities

Gated communities are enclosed urban localities with controlled entry.

They reflect:

1. Class segregation.
2. Desire for security.
3. Privatised services.
4. Separation from poorer neighbourhoods.

They show how urban order may be based on exclusion.

## 15. Gentrification

Gentrification is the transformation of a lower-income urban area into a middle-class or upper-class area.

It may bring:

1. New buildings.
2. Higher rents.
3. Commercial development.
4. Displacement of poorer residents.
5. Change in local culture.

Urban development can therefore benefit some and harm others.

## 16. Ghettoisation

Ghettoisation is the concentration of a particular community in a neighbourhood due to exclusion, fear, discrimination, or collective choice.

It may involve religion, caste, ethnicity, race, or class.

Ghettoisation can provide safety and community support, but it can also deepen segregation.

## 17. Mass Transit and Urban Change

Transport shapes city life.

Affordable and safe public transport affects:

1. Work access.
2. Women's mobility.
3. Class interaction.
4. Housing location.
5. Pollution.
6. Time use.
7. Urban expansion.

Changes in transport can alter the social character of a city.

## 18. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Social change | Transformation in social structure or relations |
| Social order | Stable pattern of social life |
| Rural society | Village and agrarian social life |
| Urbanisation | Growth of cities and urban ways of life |
| Agrarian structure | Social relations around land and agriculture |
| Dominant caste | Numerically and economically powerful caste in a region |
| Gated community | Enclosed urban locality with controlled access |
| Gentrification | Upgrading that often displaces poorer residents |
| Ghettoisation | Concentration of a community in segregated space |
| Mass transit | Large-scale public transport system |

## 19. Exam-Oriented Summary

Social change and social order coexist. Rural society is shaped by land, caste, agriculture, kinship, state policy, and markets. Land reforms, technology, democratic politics, and migration have changed rural power relations. Dominant caste is a key concept for understanding rural power.

Urban society is shaped by migration, density, occupations, housing, transport, and inequality. Cities create opportunity but also exclusion through slums, gated communities, gentrification, and ghettoisation. Social change affects groups differently.
