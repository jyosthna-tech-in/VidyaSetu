## Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Explain the relationship between environment and society.
2. Understand why environmental problems are also social problems.
3. Identify how class, caste, gender, region, and power affect access to resources.
4. Explain resource depletion, pollution, deforestation, and climate concerns.
5. Understand environmental movements and conflicts.
6. Analyse urban environmental issues such as water, land, waste, and housing.

## Chapter Overview

Environment is not outside society. People depend on land, water, forests, air, minerals, animals, and climate. At the same time, social organisation shapes how these resources are used and who benefits from them.

Environmental problems are not only natural events. They are connected with inequality, development models, technology, consumption, state policy, and power.

## 1. Meaning of Environment

Environment refers to the natural and built surroundings in which human beings live.

It includes:

1. Land.
2. Water.
3. Air.
4. Forests.
5. Animals.
6. Climate.
7. Minerals.
8. Settlements.
9. Roads.
10. Built infrastructure.

Sociology studies how society interacts with these surroundings.

## 2. Environment and Society

Society affects the environment through:

1. Agriculture.
2. Industry.
3. Mining.
4. Urbanisation.
5. Transport.
6. Consumption.
7. Waste disposal.
8. Dams and infrastructure.

Environment affects society through:

1. Resources.
2. Livelihoods.
3. Disasters.
4. Disease patterns.
5. Settlement patterns.
6. Migration.

The relation is two-way.

## 3. Ecology

Ecology is the study of relationships among organisms and their environment.

In sociology, ecological thinking helps us understand how human actions disturb or maintain environmental balance.

Human societies are part of ecosystems, not separate from them.

## 4. Environmental Problems as Social Problems

Environmental problems become social because:

1. Different groups use resources differently.
2. Some groups cause more damage than others.
3. Some groups suffer more than others.
4. State policy decides access and control.
5. Markets turn resources into commodities.
6. Development may displace communities.

Example: water scarcity may be caused by rainfall, but distribution depends on class, location, infrastructure, and political power.

## 5. Resource Depletion

Resource depletion means excessive use or exhaustion of natural resources.

Examples:

1. Groundwater decline.
2. Loss of forests.
3. Soil degradation.
4. Overfishing.
5. Mineral exhaustion.
6. Loss of biodiversity.

Depletion is often linked with commercial extraction and unequal consumption.

## 6. Deforestation

Deforestation means loss of forest cover.

Causes:

1. Logging.
2. Mining.
3. Dams.
4. Roads.
5. Commercial agriculture.
6. Urban expansion.
7. Industrial use.

Effects:

1. Loss of biodiversity.
2. Soil erosion.
3. Livelihood loss for forest communities.
4. Climate impact.
5. Increased conflict over resources.

## 7. Pollution

Pollution occurs when harmful substances enter air, water, soil, or living systems.

Types:

1. Air pollution.
2. Water pollution.
3. Soil pollution.
4. Noise pollution.
5. Industrial pollution.
6. Plastic and solid waste pollution.

Pollution affects health unequally. Poor communities often live closer to polluted areas.

## 8. Industrialisation and Environment

Industrialisation can create jobs and goods, but it can also cause:

1. Emissions.
2. Effluents.
3. Toxic waste.
4. Resource extraction.
5. Worker health risks.
6. Displacement.
7. Urban congestion.

Sociology asks who gains and who bears the cost.

## 9. Climate Change

Climate change is linked to greenhouse gas emissions and global patterns of production and consumption.

Social aspects:

1. Richer groups and countries often emit more.
2. Poor communities are more vulnerable.
3. Farmers face rainfall uncertainty.
4. Coastal people face sea-level risks.
5. Climate migration may increase.

Climate change is therefore an inequality issue.

## 10. Water as Contested Resource

Water access depends on:

1. Class.
2. Caste.
3. Gender.
4. Region.
5. Urban planning.
6. State supply.
7. Private purchase power.

Poor households may spend more time and effort collecting water than rich households with piped supply.

Women and girls often bear the burden of water collection.

## 11. Urban Environment

Urban environmental conflicts arise around:

1. Land.
2. Housing.
3. Water.
4. Sanitation.
5. Waste.
6. Air pollution.
7. Transport.
8. Green spaces.

Migrants may settle in informal housing because legal housing is unaffordable. Later, they may be evicted when land becomes valuable.

## 12. Development and Displacement

Development works such as dams, mines, industries, roads, and protected areas may displace people.

Those displaced are often:

1. Adivasi communities.
2. Small farmers.
3. Landless workers.
4. Forest dwellers.
5. Urban poor.

The question is not only compensation. It is also livelihood, culture, dignity, and participation in decision making.

## 13. Environmental Movements

Environmental movements challenge destructive development and demand protection of resources and communities.

They may focus on:

1. Forest rights.
2. Anti-dam struggles.
3. Anti-mining struggles.
4. Pollution control.
5. Water rights.
6. Urban housing rights.
7. Conservation with community participation.

Environmental movements often link ecology with justice.

## 14. Environmental Justice

Environmental justice means fair distribution of environmental benefits and burdens.

It asks:

1. Who gets clean water?
2. Who breathes polluted air?
3. Who is displaced?
4. Who profits from extraction?
5. Who decides land use?
6. Whose knowledge counts?

Environmental justice connects ecology with democracy and equality.

## 15. Sustainable Development

Sustainable development means meeting present needs without destroying the ability of future generations to meet their needs.

It requires:

1. Resource conservation.
2. Reduced inequality.
3. Community participation.
4. Clean technology.
5. Responsible consumption.
6. Protection of biodiversity.
7. Long-term planning.

## 16. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Environment | Natural and built surroundings of social life |
| Ecology | Relations among organisms and environment |
| Deforestation | Loss of forest cover |
| Emissions | Waste gases released by processes |
| Effluents | Liquid waste from industry |
| Aquifer | Underground water-bearing formation |
| Monoculture | Reduction of plant life to a single variety |
| Environmental justice | Fair distribution of environmental benefits and burdens |
| Sustainable development | Development that protects future needs |

## 17. Exam-Oriented Summary

Environment and society are deeply connected. Environmental issues are social because access to resources, exposure to pollution, displacement, and decision-making power are unequally distributed. Water, land, forests, housing, and air are shaped by class, caste, gender, state policy, and markets.

Environmental movements show that ecological protection and social justice are linked. Sustainable development requires conservation, equality, participation, and long-term responsibility.
