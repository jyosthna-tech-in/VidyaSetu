## Chapter Overview

Sociology is often called a child of the age of revolution. It emerged in Europe after major transformations changed society: scientific rationalism, political revolution, industrial capitalism, urbanisation, and modern state formation.

Karl Marx, Emile Durkheim, and Max Weber are central classical sociologists. They gave different but powerful ways of understanding modern society.

## 1. Context of Sociology

Modern sociology emerged from three major transformations:

1. Enlightenment.
2. French Revolution.
3. Industrial Revolution.

These changes transformed knowledge, politics, economy, religion, family, work, and social order.

## 2. Enlightenment

The Enlightenment emphasised reason, science, criticism, and human agency.

It challenged:

1. Religious authority as the only explanation.
2. Tradition as unquestionable.
3. Divine right of rulers.
4. Superstition.

It encouraged the idea that society can be studied rationally and changed by human beings.

## 3. French Revolution

The French Revolution of 1789 transformed political imagination.

It promoted ideals of:

1. Liberty.
2. Equality.
3. Fraternity.
4. Citizenship.
5. Popular sovereignty.

It challenged feudal privilege and raised questions about rights, democracy, and modern state power.

## 4. Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution transformed production and social life.

Major changes:

1. Factory system.
2. Machine production.
3. Urbanisation.
4. Wage labour.
5. Growth of capitalism.
6. Expansion of markets.
7. New class divisions.
8. Harsh working conditions.

Sociology emerged partly to understand these changes.

## 5. Karl Marx

Karl Marx studied capitalism, class, production, and conflict.

Central idea: the economic organisation of society shapes social relations, politics, and ideas.

Marx saw modern society as divided mainly between:

1. Bourgeoisie: owners of means of production.
2. Proletariat: workers who sell labour power.

## 6. Mode of Production

Mode of production refers to the way society organises material production.

It includes:

1. Means of production: land, tools, machines, factories, technology.
2. Relations of production: relations among people in production, such as owners and workers.

Different modes of production create different class relations.

## 7. Class Conflict

Marx argued that class conflict drives historical change.

In capitalism, capitalists seek profit, while workers seek wages and better conditions.

This creates conflict over:

1. Wages.
2. Working hours.
3. Control of labour.
4. Ownership.
5. Surplus value.

## 8. Alienation

Alienation means separation or estrangement.

In capitalism, workers may be alienated from:

1. The product of their labour.
2. The process of work.
3. Other human beings.
4. Their own creative human potential.

Work becomes forced and controlled rather than meaningful self-expression.

## 9. Emile Durkheim

Durkheim wanted sociology to be a scientific discipline.

He argued that society has realities outside individuals that influence their behaviour.

He called these social facts.

## 10. Social Facts

Social facts are ways of acting, thinking, and feeling that exist outside individuals and exercise constraint over them.

Examples:

1. Law.
2. Religion.
3. Moral rules.
4. Language.
5. Currency.
6. Marriage norms.

Durkheim argued that social facts should be studied as things, meaning objectively and systematically.

## 11. Division of Labour

Durkheim studied how division of labour affects social solidarity.

In simple societies, solidarity is based on similarity.

In complex societies, solidarity is based on interdependence.

## 12. Mechanical and Organic Solidarity

| Mechanical Solidarity | Organic Solidarity |
|---|---|
| Based on similarity | Based on difference and interdependence |
| Found in simpler societies | Found in complex modern societies |
| Strong collective conscience | Specialised roles |
| Repressive law is common | Restitutive law is common |

Durkheim saw modern society as needing moral regulation to avoid disorder.

## 13. Max Weber

Max Weber studied social action, meaning, authority, rationalisation, religion, and bureaucracy.

He argued that sociology must understand the meanings people attach to their actions.

## 14. Social Action

Social action is action to which the actor attaches meaning and which takes account of others.

Types of social action:

1. Instrumentally rational action.
2. Value-rational action.
3. Affective action.
4. Traditional action.

Weber focused on understanding subjective meaning.

## 15. Ideal Type

An ideal type is an analytical model that highlights essential features of a social phenomenon.

It is not a moral ideal. It is a tool for comparison.

Example: Weber's ideal type of bureaucracy.

## 16. Bureaucracy

Bureaucracy is a formal organisation based on rules, hierarchy, written documents, office, and specialised roles.

Features:

1. Fixed jurisdiction.
2. Hierarchy of offices.
3. Written rules.
4. Expert training.
5. Full-time officials.
6. Impersonal functioning.
7. Separation of office from person.

Bureaucracy is efficient but can become rigid and dehumanising.

## 17. Authority

Weber identified three types of authority:

1. Traditional authority: based on custom.
2. Charismatic authority: based on extraordinary personal qualities.
3. Legal-rational authority: based on rules and office.

Modern states rely heavily on legal-rational authority.

## 18. Comparing the Three Thinkers

| Thinker | Main Focus | Key Concepts |
|---|---|---|
| Marx | Capitalism and class conflict | Mode of production, class, alienation |
| Durkheim | Social order and social facts | Social fact, solidarity, division of labour |
| Weber | Meaning and rationalisation | Social action, ideal type, bureaucracy, authority |

Together, they shaped the foundations of sociology.

## 19. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Enlightenment | Age of reason and scientific thinking |
| Mode of production | System of production and production relations |
| Alienation | Separation from work, product, others, or self |
| Class conflict | Struggle between classes |
| Social fact | External and constraining social reality |
| Solidarity | Social cohesion |
| Social action | Meaningful action oriented to others |
| Ideal type | Analytical model for comparison |
| Bureaucracy | Rule-bound formal organisation |
| Authority | Legitimate power |

## 20. Exam-Oriented Summary

Sociology emerged in Europe through the Enlightenment, French Revolution, and Industrial Revolution. These transformations created new questions about reason, rights, capitalism, urban life, class, state, and social order.

Marx analysed capitalism through class conflict, mode of production, and alienation. Durkheim established sociology as a science of social facts and studied solidarity. Weber focused on social action, meaning, rationalisation, authority, and bureaucracy.
