## Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning of social structure.
2. Understand social stratification as structured inequality.
3. Identify the difference between ascribed and achieved inequalities.
4. Explain cooperation, competition, and conflict as social processes.
5. Understand how social structure both enables and constrains individuals.
6. Connect caste, class, gender, and power with stratification.

## Chapter Overview

Society is not a random collection of individuals. It has patterned arrangements of roles, statuses, institutions, norms, and power relations. These patterns form social structure.

Social structure creates order, but it also creates inequality. Social stratification is the ranking of groups in a hierarchy. Social processes such as cooperation, competition, and conflict happen within these structures.

## 1. Meaning of Social Structure

Social structure refers to the stable and patterned arrangement of relationships, institutions, roles, norms, and statuses in society.

It includes:

1. Family patterns.
2. Caste and class relations.
3. Gender relations.
4. Political institutions.
5. Economic organisation.
6. Religious institutions.
7. Education systems.
8. Rules of interaction.

Social structure shapes what people can do and what is expected of them.

## 2. Structure and Individual Action

Social structure constrains individuals, but it also enables action.

Examples of constraint:

1. Caste rules can restrict marriage.
2. Poverty can limit education.
3. Gender norms can limit mobility.
4. Class position can limit occupation.

Examples of enabling:

1. Family provides care.
2. School provides learning.
3. Law protects rights.
4. Social movements create opportunities.

Individuals act within structures, and their actions can also change structures.

## 3. Status and Role in Structure

Status is a position in society.

Role is the expected behaviour linked to a status.

Example: the status "student" includes roles such as attending class, studying, asking questions, and following school rules.

Social structure is made up of many linked statuses and roles.

## 4. Institutions and Structure

Institutions provide organised patterns of social life.

Examples:

1. Family organises care and kinship.
2. Economy organises work and resources.
3. Polity organises power.
4. Religion organises sacred meanings.
5. Education organises learning and certification.

Institutions make society durable across generations.

## 5. Social Stratification

Social stratification is the division of society into hierarchical layers.

It means some groups have more access to:

1. Wealth.
2. Power.
3. Prestige.
4. Education.
5. Health.
6. Land.
7. Security.
8. Social honour.

Stratification is not accidental. It is patterned and reproduced.

## 6. Features of Stratification

Social stratification is:

1. Social, not merely individual.
2. Persistent over generations.
3. Supported by beliefs and institutions.
4. Connected with inequality of opportunity.
5. Found in different forms across societies.
6. Often challenged by social movements.

## 7. Caste as Stratification

Caste is a form of stratification based largely on birth.

Features:

1. Hereditary membership.
2. Hierarchy.
3. Endogamy.
4. Restrictions on food and social interaction.
5. Traditional occupation links.
6. Ideas of purity and pollution.

Caste inequality has been legally challenged, but its social effects continue in many forms.

## 8. Class as Stratification

Class is based mainly on economic position.

It may involve:

1. Ownership of property.
2. Income.
3. Occupation.
4. Education.
5. Market position.
6. Life chances.

Class is more open than caste in principle, but class inequality can still be deeply persistent.

## 9. Gender Stratification

Gender stratification refers to unequal power, rights, roles, and opportunities based on gender.

It appears in:

1. Household work.
2. Education.
3. Employment.
4. Wages.
5. Mobility.
6. Inheritance.
7. Political representation.
8. Violence and safety.

Gender inequality is learned and institutionalised, not natural.

## 10. Social Mobility

Social mobility means movement of individuals or groups within a stratification system.

Types:

1. Upward mobility.
2. Downward mobility.
3. Horizontal mobility.
4. Intergenerational mobility.
5. Intragenerational mobility.

Mobility depends on education, economy, law, social networks, discrimination, and historical conditions.

## 11. Social Processes

Social processes are repeated forms of interaction through which society works.

Important social processes:

1. Cooperation.
2. Competition.
3. Conflict.

These processes are not isolated. They often coexist.

## 12. Cooperation

Cooperation means working together toward shared goals.

Examples:

1. Family members sharing work.
2. Farmers using common irrigation.
3. Students preparing a group presentation.
4. Citizens responding to disaster.

Cooperation is necessary for social life.

## 13. Competition

Competition occurs when people or groups struggle for limited rewards under some rules.

Examples:

1. Exams.
2. Job selection.
3. Elections.
4. Market competition.
5. Sports.

Competition can motivate performance, but unequal starting points can make it unfair.

## 14. Conflict

Conflict occurs when groups struggle over power, resources, dignity, values, or identity.

Examples:

1. Labour strikes.
2. Caste movements.
3. Gender rights movements.
4. Land struggles.
5. Ethnic conflict.

Conflict can disrupt order, but it can also produce social change.

## 15. Cooperation and Conflict Together

Societies often contain both cooperation and conflict.

Example: workers and employers cooperate in production, but may conflict over wages.

Example: family members cooperate in care, but may conflict over inheritance or gender roles.

Sociology studies both stability and tension.

## 16. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Social structure | Patterned arrangement of social relations |
| Status | Social position |
| Role | Expected behaviour attached to status |
| Stratification | Structured social inequality |
| Caste | Birth-based hierarchical system |
| Class | Economic grouping based on resources and market position |
| Gender stratification | Inequality based on gender |
| Social mobility | Movement within social hierarchy |
| Cooperation | Working together |
| Competition | Struggle under rules for limited rewards |
| Conflict | Struggle over interests, power, or values |

## 17. Exam-Oriented Summary

Social structure refers to patterned social arrangements that shape individual and group life. Stratification is structured inequality, visible in caste, class, gender, and other hierarchies. It affects life chances, dignity, opportunity, and power.

Social processes such as cooperation, competition, and conflict operate within structures. Cooperation creates social order, competition organises struggles for rewards, and conflict may challenge inequality and produce change.
