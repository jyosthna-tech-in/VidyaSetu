## Big Idea

The first cities did not emerge only because land was fertile. City life required a larger system: productive agriculture, irrigation, trade, craft specialisation, storage, social organisation, administration, rulers, temples, seals, and writing.

Mesopotamia, the land between the Euphrates and Tigris rivers in present-day Iraq, is one of the best examples of early city life. Its cities show how writing and urban institutions grew together. Writing helped people record goods, organise labour, authenticate transactions, preserve stories, teach mathematics, and build scholarly traditions.

## From Hunting And Gathering To City Life

The chapter begins with the long history of early human societies.

Before cities, people:

1. Lived by hunting animals.
2. Gathered plant produce.
3. Used stone tools.
4. Used fire.
5. Developed language.
6. Lived in caves, rock shelters, forests, or temporary shelters.

Over time, major changes prepared the ground for settled life.

## Shift To Agriculture

Around 10,000 years ago, people gradually shifted from nomadic life to settled agriculture.

This happened when people learned:

1. Which plants were useful.
2. Where plants grew.
3. Which seasons produced fruits or grains.
4. How to grow plants intentionally.

### Crops In Different Regions

| Region | Crops mentioned |
|---|---|
| West Asia | Wheat, barley, peas, pulses |
| East and Southeast Asia | Millet, rice |
| Africa | Millet |

## Animal Domestication

Around the same broad period, people domesticated animals.

Examples:

1. Sheep.
2. Goat.
3. Cattle.
4. Pig.
5. Donkey.

Animals gave:

1. Meat.
2. Milk.
3. Wool.
4. Labour.
5. Transport.
6. Manure.

Later, cattle and donkeys were harnessed to ploughs and carts.

## New Technologies Before Cities

Important developments included:

1. Pottery for storing grain and cooking food.
2. Permanent houses.
3. Mortars and pestles for grinding grain.
4. Stone axes and hoes for clearing and digging land.
5. Polished stone tools.
6. Cotton and flax fibres.
7. Woollen textiles.
8. Copper and tin use.
9. Trade in wood, metals, stones, shell, and obsidian.

These changes did not instantly create cities, but they made larger, more complex communities possible.

## Why Trade Became Important

Villages and towns grew as people exchanged goods and ideas.

Goods moved from place to place:

1. Wood.
2. Stones.
3. Precious and semi-precious stones.
4. Metals.
5. Shell.
6. Obsidian.

This movement of goods prepared the ground for:

1. Trade.
2. Transport.
3. Specialised crafts.
4. Administrative control.
5. Small states.

## Reading Timelines

The textbook timeline is not just a list of dates. It teaches how historians organise long processes.

Important rules:

1. Some dates mark events.
2. Some dates mark the beginning of long processes.
3. Some dates are revised when new evidence appears.
4. Historical processes overlap across regions.
5. Blank spaces do not mean nothing happened.
6. Some processes are harder to date than wars or rulers.

Learners should use timelines to compare regions, not memorise dates mechanically.

## Mesopotamia: Location And Name

Mesopotamia means land between rivers.

The word comes from Greek:

1. Mesos means middle.
2. Potamos means river.

Mesopotamia lay between the Euphrates and Tigris rivers.

It is now mostly part of the Republic of Iraq.

## Why Mesopotamia Is Important

Mesopotamian civilisation is known for:

1. Prosperous cities.
2. Long-distance trade.
3. Temples.
4. Writing.
5. Literature.
6. Mathematics.
7. Astronomy.
8. Clay tablets.
9. Administrative records.
10. Urban culture.

Its writing system and literature spread to eastern Mediterranean, northern Syria, and Turkey after 2000 BCE.

## Names Of Regions And Languages

### Sumer And Akkad

In the beginning of recorded history, the land, especially the urbanised south, was called Sumer and Akkad.

### Babylonia

After 2000 BCE, when Babylon became important, southern Mesopotamia was called Babylonia.

### Assyria

From about 1100 BCE, when Assyrians established their kingdom in the north, the region became known as Assyria.

### Languages

| Language | Historical role |
|---|---|
| Sumerian | First known language of the land |
| Akkadian | Replaced Sumerian in everyday use around 2400 BCE |
| Aramaic | Entered from about 1400 BCE and became widely spoken after 1000 BCE |

Akkadian flourished until the time of Alexander, 336-323 BCE, with regional changes.

Aramaic is similar to Hebrew and is still spoken in parts of Iraq.

## Archaeology In Mesopotamia

Archaeology in Mesopotamia began in the 1840s.

Long excavations took place at sites such as:

1. Uruk.
2. Mari.

Historians use many forms of evidence:

1. Buildings.
2. Statues.
3. Ornaments.
4. Graves.
5. Tools.
6. Seals.
7. Written documents.
8. Tablets.
9. Plant remains.
10. Animal remains.

The large number of written documents makes Mesopotamia especially important for studying early city life.

## European Interest And The Bible

Mesopotamia was important to European scholars because it appeared in the Old Testament.

The Book of Genesis referred to Shimar, meaning Sumer, as a land of brick-built cities.

In 1873, a British newspaper funded a British Museum expedition to search for a tablet about the Flood story.

By the 1960s, scholars increasingly understood that Old Testament stories were not necessarily literal historical records. They could preserve memories of major historical changes.

## Flood Traditions

The Bible tells a story of a great Flood and Noah's ark.

Mesopotamian tradition had a similar flood story with a main figure called Ziusudra or Utnapishtim.

The historical value of such stories is not that every detail is literally true. Their value lies in how societies preserved memories of floods, destruction, renewal, gods, kings, and human survival.

## Geography Of Mesopotamia

Iraq has diverse environments.

### Northeast

The northeast has:

1. Green undulating plains.
2. Mountain ranges.
3. Clear streams.
4. Wild flowers.
5. Enough rainfall for crops.

Agriculture began here between 7000 and 6000 BCE.

### North

The north has steppe land.

This area suited animal herding because:

1. Winter rains produced grasses.
2. Sheep and goats could graze.
3. Herding was often better than agriculture.

### East

Tributaries of the Tigris provided routes into the mountains of Iran.

This helped communication and exchange.

### South

The south was desert.

Yet the first cities and writing emerged here.

This was possible because:

1. Euphrates and Tigris carried fertile silt.
2. Floods deposited fine mud.
3. Water could be channelled into fields.
4. Irrigation supported crops even with low rainfall.

## Agriculture In Southern Mesopotamia

Southern Mesopotamia was highly productive despite insufficient rainfall.

Reasons:

1. Rivers brought silt.
2. Channels supplied water.
3. Irrigation supported wheat, barley, peas, and lentils.
4. Date palms provided fruit.
5. Fish were available in rivers.
6. Sheep and goats produced meat, milk, and wool in nearby areas.

However, rural prosperity alone did not create cities. Urbanism required additional conditions.

## Meaning Of Urbanism

Cities are not simply places with many people.

Urbanism begins when an economy develops beyond food production.

Urban economies include:

1. Trade.
2. Manufacturing.
3. Services.
4. Administration.
5. Storage.
6. Distribution.
7. Record keeping.

City people depend on other city and village people. This dependence creates continuous interaction.

## Division Of Labour

Division of labour is a mark of urban life.

Example:

1. A seal carver specialises in carving.
2. The carver needs bronze tools made by another specialist.
3. The bronze tool maker needs copper, tin, and charcoal.
4. Traders or administrators arrange raw materials.
5. Food producers supply grain and other food.
6. Scribes keep records.

No single person produces everything needed.

## Why Cities Need Organisation

Cities need organisation because many activities must be coordinated.

Examples:

1. Grain must be collected from villages.
2. Food must be stored.
3. Workers must be assigned tasks.
4. Raw materials must be imported.
5. Craftspeople need tools and supplies.
6. Temples and palaces must manage labour.
7. Seals and writing must authenticate goods.
8. Records must track rations, taxes, and trade.

Urban economies often require written records because memory alone cannot manage such complexity.

## Movement Of Goods Into Cities

Southern Mesopotamia lacked many raw materials.

It needed:

1. Wood.
2. Stone.
3. Metals.
4. Precious stones.
5. Semi-precious stones.
6. Tin.
7. Copper.

This encouraged long-distance trade.

Water transport on rivers and canals made movement easier than land transport for bulky goods.

## Development Of Writing

Writing in Mesopotamia began around 3200 BCE.

Early writing developed from practical needs:

1. Recording goods.
2. Recording grain.
3. Recording animals.
4. Recording labour.
5. Recording offerings.
6. Recording deliveries.
7. Recording trade.

Writing was first closely connected with administration.

## Clay Tablets

Mesopotamian writing was usually done on clay tablets.

Process:

1. Wet clay was shaped into a tablet.
2. A reed stylus was pressed into clay.
3. The tablet was dried in the sun or baked.
4. Once dry, the record became durable.

Because clay survives well, thousands of tablets have been recovered.

## Cuneiform

Cuneiform means wedge-shaped writing.

By about 2600 BCE, signs became more wedge-shaped because scribes pressed a stylus into clay.

Cuneiform could represent:

1. Words.
2. Syllables.
3. Sounds.
4. Names.
5. Numbers.

Writing was difficult and required training. Scribes became important urban specialists.

## Scribes

Scribes were trained writers.

They worked in:

1. Temples.
2. Palaces.
3. Administrative offices.
4. Trade networks.
5. Schools.

Their work included:

1. Recording rations.
2. Recording offerings.
3. Writing letters.
4. Copying old texts.
5. Keeping accounts.
6. Preserving literature.
7. Training later scribes.

## Writing And City Life

Writing helped cities function.

It allowed:

1. Record keeping.
2. Storage control.
3. Labour management.
4. Tax or tribute tracking.
5. Trade documentation.
6. Legal agreements.
7. Royal inscriptions.
8. Literary preservation.
9. Mathematical learning.
10. Astronomical records.

The chapter's central argument is that writing and city life developed together.

## Temples And Early Cities

Some of the earliest cities developed around temples.

An early temple was considered the house of a god.

This is why it may have resembled a house in plan or function, though often larger and more elaborate.

### Temple Functions

Temples were:

1. Religious centres.
2. Economic centres.
3. Storehouses.
4. Employers of craftspeople.
5. Organisers of labour.
6. Owners of land and herds.
7. Centres of redistribution.

Temple activity brought together priests, workers, scribes, craftspeople, farmers, and rulers.

## Rise Of Rulers

As cities grew, rulers became more important.

Rulers:

1. Organised large building works.
2. Mobilised labour.
3. Defended cities.
4. Led war.
5. Controlled tribute and rations.
6. Commissioned temples and walls.
7. Sent people to fetch raw materials.

Urban institutions depended heavily on central authority.

## Uruk

Uruk was one of the greatest early Mesopotamian cities.

Key points:

1. It grew to about 250 hectares around 3000 BCE.
2. It became about twice as large as Mohenjo-daro would be later.
3. It expanded to about 400 hectares by about 2800 BCE.
4. It had a defensive wall early.
5. It was continuously occupied from about 4200 BCE to about 400 CE.

## Labour At Uruk

War captives and local people were put to work for temples or rulers.

They were paid rations.

Hundreds of ration lists have been found.

The chapter mentions an estimate that one temple required 1,500 men working 10 hours a day for five years.

This shows the scale of labour organisation in early cities.

## Technical Advances At Uruk

Around 3000 BCE, Uruk saw important technical developments:

1. Bronze tools for crafts.
2. Brick columns.
3. Clay cone mosaics for temple walls.
4. Imported stone sculpture.
5. Potter's wheel.

The potter's wheel was especially suited to urban economy because it allowed workshops to produce many similar pots efficiently.

## The Seal As An Urban Artefact

Mesopotamian seals were often cylindrical.

They were:

1. Pierced through the centre.
2. Fitted with a stick.
3. Rolled over wet clay.
4. Used to create a continuous design.

### Uses Of Seals

Seals were used to:

1. Secure cloth packages.
2. Seal pots.
3. Mark ownership.
4. Authenticate letters.
5. Show official position.
6. Show connection with a deity.
7. Mark a city dweller's role in public life.

Some seals carried writing.

## Life In The City

Mesopotamian cities had social inequality.

Evidence from Ur shows that a small ruling elite had great wealth.

Royal graves contained:

1. Jewellery.
2. Gold vessels.
3. Musical instruments.
4. Shell and lapis lazuli inlays.
5. Ceremonial weapons.

Ordinary people are studied through houses, legal texts, streets, burials, and daily objects.

## Family Life

Legal texts show that the nuclear family was common.

Features:

1. Father was head of the family.
2. A married son and his family might live with his parents.
3. Marriage required consent.
4. Gifts were exchanged.
5. Families ate together and made temple offerings.
6. The bride received a share from her father.
7. Sons inherited the father's house, herds, fields, and related property.

## Ur: Ordinary Houses

Ur was one of the earliest cities excavated systematically.

Features:

1. Narrow winding streets.
2. Irregular house plots.
3. Wheeled carts could not reach many houses.
4. Grain and firewood likely arrived by donkey.
5. Absence of clear town planning.
6. No street drains like those at Mohenjo-daro.
7. Drains and clay pipes inside courtyards.
8. Roofs likely sloped inward toward courtyards.
9. Sumps collected water and sewage.
10. Refuse thrown into streets raised street levels.
11. Doorways opened into courtyards, giving privacy.
12. Some burials were under house floors.

## Mari: A Trading Town In A Pastoral Zone

Mari flourished after 2000 BCE.

It stood upstream on the Euphrates, not in the most productive southern plain.

Its importance came from:

1. Trade.
2. River transport.
3. Contact between farmers and herders.
4. Strategic position between south Mesopotamia and uplands of Turkey, Syria, and Lebanon.

## Farmers And Herders Around Mari

Mari's region included both agriculture and animal rearing.

Herders exchanged:

1. Young animals.
2. Cheese.
3. Leather.
4. Meat.

They received:

1. Grain.
2. Metal tools.
3. Other agricultural or craft products.

Manure from flocks was useful for farmers.

## Conflict And Cooperation

Farmers and herders could cooperate, but conflict also occurred.

Possible tensions:

1. Flocks crossing sown fields.
2. Herdsmen raiding stored goods.
3. Settled groups blocking access to river or canal water.
4. Officials watching mobile camps.

Mobile pastoralists were not simply threats. They were also traders, labourers, soldiers, and cultural contributors.

## Pastoral Groups And Mesopotamian Power

Nomadic communities entered Mesopotamian history repeatedly.

Groups mentioned include:

1. Akkadians.
2. Amorites.
3. Assyrians.
4. Aramaeans.

Some entered as herders, harvest workers, or soldiers. Some settled and became powerful.

The kings of Mari were Amorites.

Mesopotamian society became strong partly because it absorbed different peoples and cultures.

## Palace At Mari

The palace of King Zimrilim, 1810-1760 BCE, was a major urban institution.

It was:

1. Residence of the royal family.
2. Administrative centre.
3. Production centre for precious metal ornaments.
4. Place for receiving visitors.
5. Place where food supplies were recorded.

### Palace Features

1. About 260 rooms.
2. Area of about 2.4 hectares.
3. One entrance on the north.
4. Large paved courtyards.
5. Audience hall.
6. Throne room.
7. Storerooms.
8. Kitchens.
9. Scribes' office.
10. Clay tablet storage.

Daily lists show large quantities of flour, bread, meat, fish, fruit, beer, and wine for the royal table.

## Mari And Trade

Mari prospered because it controlled river trade on the Euphrates.

Goods passing through included:

1. Wood.
2. Copper.
3. Tin.
4. Oil.
5. Wine.
6. Grinding stones.
7. Barley.

Boats stopped at Mari for inspection.

Officials could levy a charge of about one-tenth the value of goods before allowing boats to move downstream.

Copper from Alashiya, the island of Cyprus, and tin were especially important because bronze required both copper and tin.

Mari was not militarily very strong, but it was exceptionally prosperous.

## Excavating Mesopotamian Towns

Modern excavations are more careful and precise than older excavations.

Archaeologists now record:

1. Walls.
2. Floors.
3. Pits.
4. Soil colour.
5. Soil texture.
6. Plant remains.
7. Animal remains.
8. Charred bones.
9. Seeds.
10. Fibres.
11. Room use.
12. Roofed and open areas.

## Abu Salabikh

Abu Salabikh was a small town of about 10 hectares around 2500 BCE.

Its population was less than 10,000.

Archaeologists learned about it by:

1. Scraping surface layers.
2. Identifying wall outlines.
3. Excavating selected houses.
4. Sieving soil.
5. Studying plant and animal remains.
6. Studying charred fish bones.
7. Identifying kitchens from plant seeds and fibres in dung-cake fuel.
8. Identifying living rooms by fewer traces.
9. Studying pig teeth found in streets.
10. Using microscopic study of floors.

This shows that urban archaeology depends on small clues.

## Mesopotamian City Culture

Mesopotamians valued city life.

Their poetry and stories remembered cities even after war and destruction.

The Gilgamesh tradition shows this pride clearly.

## Gilgamesh And Uruk

Gilgamesh was believed to have ruled Uruk after Enmerkar.

In the epic tradition:

1. He is a powerful hero.
2. His friend's death shocks him.
3. He searches for immortality.
4. He fails to escape death.
5. He returns to Uruk.
6. He takes consolation in the city wall and foundations.

The point is important: Gilgamesh does not find immortality in family lineage alone. He finds meaning in the city his people built.

This shows how urban achievement became a cultural value.

## Legacy Of Writing

Mesopotamia's greatest legacy includes:

1. Writing.
2. Record keeping.
3. Schools.
4. Mathematics.
5. Astronomy.
6. Time reckoning.
7. Literature.
8. Libraries.
9. Scholarly copying.

Oral stories can preserve memory, but science and mathematics depend heavily on written records that later scholars can read, copy, check, and improve.

## Mathematics

Around 1800 BCE, Mesopotamian tablets included:

1. Multiplication tables.
2. Division tables.
3. Square tables.
4. Square-root tables.
5. Compound interest tables.
6. Practical calculation problems.

The chapter notes that Mesopotamians calculated the square root of 2 with remarkable accuracy.

## Time Reckoning

Mesopotamian influence survives in time divisions used today:

1. Year divided into 12 months.
2. Month divided into four weeks.
3. Day divided into 24 hours.
4. Hour divided into 60 minutes.

These divisions passed through later empires and cultures into the wider world.

## Astronomy

Mesopotamian scholars recorded:

1. Solar eclipses.
2. Lunar eclipses.
3. Year, month, and day of observations.
4. Positions of stars.
5. Positions of constellations.

Such work depended on writing and schools.

## Early Library Of Assurbanipal

The Assyrian empire was at its height between 720 and 610 BCE.

Assurbanipal ruled from 668 to 627 BCE.

He collected a major library at Nineveh.

### What The Library Contained

The library included tablets on:

1. History.
2. Epics.
3. Omens.
4. Astrology.
5. Hymns.
6. Poems.
7. Scholarly texts.

Assurbanipal sent scribes south to locate old tablets.

Even after Sumerian ceased to be spoken around 1800 BCE, it remained taught in schools through sign lists, vocabulary texts, and bilingual tablets.

Assurbanipal's library had about 1,000 texts and about 30,000 tablets, grouped by subject.

## Early Preservation Of The Past

Nabopolassar released Babylonia from Assyrian control in 625 BCE.

Later Babylon became one of the greatest cities of the world.

Features of Babylon:

1. More than 850 hectares.
2. Triple wall.
3. Great palaces.
4. Temples.
5. Ziggurat.
6. Processional way.
7. Active trading houses.
8. Mathematicians and astronomers.

Nabonidus, the last ruler of independent Babylon, showed interest in earlier kings, old statues, old inscriptions, and priestly traditions. His actions show that ancient Mesopotamians themselves preserved and studied their own past.

## Common Misunderstandings

### Misunderstanding 1: Cities grew only because soil was fertile

Correct understanding:

Fertility was necessary but not sufficient. Cities also required trade, labour organisation, craft specialisation, storage, administration, rulers, temples, and writing.

### Misunderstanding 2: Writing began mainly for literature

Correct understanding:

Early writing began largely for practical administration: recording goods, labour, rations, and transactions. Literature came later as writing traditions expanded.

### Misunderstanding 3: Herders were always enemies of cities

Correct understanding:

Herders could create conflict, but they also traded, supplied animals, provided manure, worked as labourers or soldiers, settled, and sometimes became rulers.

### Misunderstanding 4: Ancient stories are useless for history

Correct understanding:

Ancient stories may not be literal records, but they reveal memory, values, fears, religious ideas, and the importance of cities and kingship.

### Misunderstanding 5: Archaeologists only look for large monuments

Correct understanding:

Modern archaeologists study tiny clues such as seeds, bones, floor marks, dung fuel, street refuse, and room traces.

## Exam-Ready Definitions

### Urbanism

Urbanism is a way of life in which people live in towns or cities and depend on specialised occupations, trade, services, administration, and organised exchange beyond food production.

### Cuneiform

Cuneiform is the wedge-shaped writing system of Mesopotamia, made by pressing a stylus into wet clay tablets.

### Scribe

A scribe was a trained writer who kept records, copied texts, wrote letters, and helped temples, palaces, and traders manage information.

### Cylinder Seal

A cylinder seal was a carved stone cylinder rolled over wet clay to create an impression used for ownership, security, authenticity, and public identity.

### Ziggurat

A ziggurat was a stepped temple tower in Mesopotamian cities.

### Division Of Labour

Division of labour means the separation of work into specialised tasks performed by different people.

## Short Answer Bank

### Why did city life begin in Mesopotamia?

City life began because irrigation-based agriculture, river transport, trade, craft specialisation, temple organisation, rulers, and writing developed together in the Euphrates-Tigris region.

### Why was southern Mesopotamia productive despite low rainfall?

The Euphrates and Tigris carried fertile silt. Their waters could be channelled into fields, allowing irrigation agriculture even in a dry region.

### Why did writing become necessary?

Writing became necessary because urban economies required records of goods, rations, labour, trade, storage, offerings, and administrative decisions.

### Why were seals important?

Seals secured goods, authenticated letters, marked ownership, and showed the public role of the owner in city life.

### Why was Mari prosperous?

Mari was located on the Euphrates trade route between southern cities and resource-rich uplands. It collected charges on goods such as wood, copper, tin, oil, wine, and stones.

### What does the Gilgamesh tradition show?

It shows Mesopotamian pride in cities. Gilgamesh fails to gain immortality but finds consolation in Uruk's city wall and the achievement of his people.

## Practice Questions With Answer Cues

### Question 1

Why do we say that natural fertility and high food production were not the only causes of early urbanisation?

Answer cue:

Because cities required trade, crafts, services, social organisation, storage, labour control, rulers, temples, seals, and writing. Food surplus was necessary, but it was not sufficient.

### Question 2

Which were necessary conditions, causes, and outcomes of early urbanisation?

Answer cue:

Highly productive agriculture and water transport were necessary conditions. Lack of metal and stone encouraged trade and helped cause urban connections. Division of labour and use of seals were outcomes and features of urban life. Military power of kings helped organise compulsory labour and large public works.

### Question 3

Why were mobile animal herders not necessarily a threat to town life?

Answer cue:

Herders exchanged animals, cheese, leather, meat, and manure for grain and tools. They could also work as labourers or soldiers and sometimes settled into city society. Conflict existed, but cooperation was also important.

### Question 4

Why would the early temple have been much like a house?

Answer cue:

The temple was understood as the house of the god. It contained offerings, stores, servants, and spaces for ritual and administration, like an enlarged and sacred household.

### Question 5

Which new institutions depended on the initiative of the king?

Answer cue:

Large temples, palaces, city walls, long-distance procurement, compulsory labour, administration, scribal offices, ration distribution, and military organisation depended strongly on royal initiative.

### Question 6

What do ancient stories tell us about Mesopotamian civilisation?

Answer cue:

They reveal beliefs about gods, floods, kingship, mortality, memory, city pride, and the importance of preserving tradition. The Gilgamesh tradition shows deep pride in Uruk as a human achievement.

## Final Takeaway

Mesopotamian city life was built from interdependence. Farmers, herders, traders, craftspeople, scribes, priests, rulers, and labourers all formed one urban system. Writing was not separate from this system; it was one of the tools that made complex city life possible and one of the reasons Mesopotamian knowledge could outlive the cities themselves.
