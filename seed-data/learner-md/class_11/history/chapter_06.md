## Learning Purpose

This chapter studies the displacement of indigenous peoples in North America and Australia under European settler colonialism.

It asks how people who had lived in a land for thousands of years were pushed aside by settlers who claimed land, built states, expanded farming, extracted minerals, and wrote histories that often erased earlier inhabitants.

The chapter is not only about conquest.

It is about conflicting ideas of land, property, civilisation, history, rights, culture, and memory.

It also shows how indigenous peoples later asserted their cultures, land rights, and historical voices.

## Core Learning Outcomes

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning of indigenous peoples.
2. Distinguish between direct imperial control and settler colonialism.
3. Explain how European settlement displaced native peoples in America and Australia.
4. Describe Native American life before large-scale European settlement.
5. Explain differences between European and indigenous ideas of land and nature.
6. Analyse how trade, gifts, commodities, alcohol, and markets shaped early encounters.
7. Explain the meaning of frontier in United States history.
8. Describe how the Gold Rush, railways, farms, and industry expanded settler control.
9. Explain how treaties, reservations, and forced removal dispossessed Native Americans.
10. Understand the Trail of Tears and the Cherokee case.
11. Explain how anthropology first studied native communities through unequal assumptions.
12. Describe the history of Aboriginal peoples and Torres Strait Islanders in Australia.
13. Explain terra nullius and why it mattered.
14. Analyse the White Australia policy and later multiculturalism.
15. Explain the importance of the Mabo case and National Sorry Day.
16. Evaluate why indigenous histories were left out of many settler history books.

## How To Read This Chapter

Read this chapter through four connected questions.

1. How did native peoples live before European settlement expanded?
2. How did Europeans justify taking land?
3. How did settler states build wealth and democracy while excluding native peoples?
4. How did indigenous peoples later recover voice, rights, and recognition?

The chapter repeatedly contrasts two worldviews.

For many indigenous communities, land was sacred, shared, remembered, and lived with.

For settlers, land became property, commodity, farm, mine, railway route, and state territory.

## 1. Modernisation And Colonisation

The chapter begins in the wider context of modernisation.

Modernisation included:

- industrialisation;
- political revolutions;
- transformation from subjects to citizens;
- capitalism;
- railways;
- factories;
- nation-states;
- new forms of imperialism.

But modernisation also had heavy human costs.

European expansion displaced indigenous peoples.

Settler colonies often treated native land as empty or underused.

## 2. Direct Imperial Control And Settler Colonies

Not all colonies were the same.

### Direct Imperial Control

In places such as India, the British East India Company defeated local rulers, annexed territories, collected taxes, built railways, excavated mines, and developed plantations while using existing administrative systems.

### Settler Colonies

In settler colonies, European immigrants moved permanently into the land and became the majority population.

Examples include:

- North America;
- Australia;
- New Zealand;
- South Africa;
- parts of South America.

Settlers built new societies by displacing earlier inhabitants.

## 3. Meaning Of Indigenous Peoples

Indigenous peoples are communities belonging naturally and historically to a place before later conquest, colonisation, or settlement.

The term highlights:

- original connection to land;
- distinct culture;
- language;
- memory;
- community traditions;
- spiritual relationship with place;
- experience of colonisation.

Other terms used in different contexts include Native American, First Nations, Aboriginal peoples, and Torres Strait Islanders.

## 4. Erasure In Settler Histories

Until the mid-twentieth century, many American and Australian textbooks described Europeans as discovering lands that were already inhabited.

Native peoples were often mentioned only as obstacles or enemies.

This erased the fact that native communities had occupied, named, understood, used, and cared for these lands for thousands of years.

Place names such as Ohio, Mississippi, Seattle, Saskatchewan, Wollongong, and Parramatta remind us that indigenous presence shaped the landscape long before settler states.

## 5. Oral History And Native Writing

From the 1960s onward, native peoples were increasingly encouraged to write or dictate their own histories.

This is connected with oral history.

Oral history preserves memories through spoken accounts, songs, stories, ceremonies, and community knowledge.

Museums and galleries later began to include native art and indigenous-curated displays.

The National Museum of the American Indian is important because American Indians themselves helped curate it.

## 6. European Imperialism

From the seventeenth century, France, Holland, and England expanded trade and colonies.

Ireland was also treated almost like an English colony because many landowners were English settlers.

European expansion was driven by profit.

But forms of control differed by region.

### Types Of Colonial Control

| Region | Pattern |
|---|---|
| South Asia | Trading companies became political powers |
| Africa | Coastal trade first, later interior colonisation |
| South Africa | Settler colony pattern |
| North America | Settler expansion and state formation |
| Australia | Settler colony and penal settlement |

## 7. Naming The New World

European naming was part of claiming.

Names such as America, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand came from European exploration, Latin vocabulary, indigenous terms heard by Europeans, or Dutch mapping.

Many places were named "New" versions of European places.

This naming practice symbolised a settler view: the land was being placed into a European mental map.

## 8. North America: Geography And Resources

North America extends from the Arctic Circle to the Tropic of Cancer and from the Pacific Ocean to the Atlantic Ocean.

It includes:

- Rocky Mountains;
- Arizona and Nevada deserts;
- Sierra Nevada mountains;
- Great Plains;
- Great Lakes;
- Mississippi and Ohio valleys;
- Appalachian Mountains;
- Canadian forests.

The continent had oil, gas, minerals, forests, fertile plains, rivers, fish, and wildlife.

These resources later attracted settlers, industries, railways, farms, and mining.

## 9. Native Peoples Of North America

The earliest inhabitants came from Asia across a land bridge near the Bering Straits more than 30,000 years ago.

During the last Ice Age, people moved further south.

Population increased after climate became more stable.

Native communities were diverse.

They spoke many languages and lived in different ecological settings.

They combined:

- hunting;
- fishing;
- gathering;
- gardening;
- maize cultivation;
- village life;
- river-valley settlement;
- seasonal movement;
- craft production.

## 10. Why North America Was Different From Central And South America

In Central and South America, large empires such as the Aztec and Inca existed.

They had urban centres, monumental architecture, tribute systems, and large-scale agriculture.

In much of North America, native peoples did not develop similar empires.

Many lived in smaller communities, bands, and villages.

They produced for need rather than large surplus.

Control of land as private property was not central in the same way.

This difference shaped how Europeans misunderstood and judged them.

## 11. Native Ideas Of Land

Many Native American communities did not think of land as a commodity to be bought and sold.

Land was connected with:

- ancestors;
- stories;
- hunting;
- fishing;
- sacred places;
- seasonal movement;
- memory;
- community use;
- responsibility.

They used land without needing to own it as a market object.

This deeply conflicted with European settler ideas of property.

## 12. Gift Exchange And Alliances

Native communities valued alliances and gift exchange.

Goods could be shared or exchanged as part of friendship, diplomacy, and treaty relationships.

Wampum belts made of coloured shells could record agreements and treaty relationships.

For Europeans, trade goods were commodities for profit.

For native peoples, exchange was often tied to relationship and obligation.

This caused misunderstanding.

## 13. Early Encounters With Europeans

Early European traders in North America often depended on native help.

They traded for:

- fish;
- furs;
- local knowledge;
- hunting skill;
- routes;
- food.

Europeans offered:

- blankets;
- iron vessels;
- guns;
- alcohol;
- manufactured goods.

Alcohol had serious destructive effects because many native communities had not known it earlier.

European traders sometimes used addiction to control trade terms.

## 14. Mutual Perceptions

Eighteenth-century Europeans often defined civilisation through literacy, organised religion, cities, and market production.

Because many native societies did not fit these criteria, Europeans called them uncivilised.

Some Europeans romanticised them as noble and close to nature.

Others dismissed them as primitive.

Native peoples often saw Europeans as greedy, destructive, and confused about land.

The same encounter was understood in very different moral languages.

## 15. Settlers And Forests

Settlers wanted farms.

They looked at forests and imagined cleared fields, fences, crops, roads, and property.

Native peoples read the land differently.

They understood tracks, seasons, animals, water, plants, and sacred places.

For settlers, cutting forests could mean progress.

For native communities, it could mean destruction of a living world.

## 16. The United States And Canada

The United States and Canada became states at the end of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

At first, they occupied only parts of the land they later controlled.

During the next century, both expanded across huge territories.

Expansion took place through:

- purchase;
- war;
- treaties;
- settlement;
- railway building;
- mining;
- forced removal of native peoples.

The consent of native peoples was rarely treated as necessary.

## 17. The Frontier

The frontier was the moving boundary of European settlement in the United States.

For settlers, it meant opportunity, land, freedom, farming, profit, and national expansion.

For Native Americans, it meant dispossession, forced movement, violence, confinement, and destruction of hunting grounds.

This is why the frontier had opposite meanings for settlers and indigenous peoples.

## 18. Land Hunger And Migration

Many European migrants wanted land.

Some were younger sons from Britain and France who would not inherit family property.

Others came from Germany, Sweden, Italy, Poland, and other regions after losing land or seeking better opportunities.

They were attracted by cheap land and the possibility of large farms.

They cleared prairies and forests.

They introduced crops such as rice and cotton for profit.

## 19. Barbed Wire And Farm Expansion

Settlers wanted to protect farms from animals such as wolves and mountain lions.

These animals were hunted severely.

The invention of barbed wire in 1873 helped settlers enclose land.

Enclosure turned open landscapes into private farms and ranches.

This further restricted native movement and animal life.

## 20. Slavery And The Southern United States

The southern climate was hot and plantation owners wanted large-scale labour.

Instead of doing the work themselves, they purchased enslaved Africans.

Even after the slave trade was banned, enslaved people already in the United States and their children remained enslaved.

Northern states, whose economies depended less on plantations, increasingly supported abolition.

The American Civil War from 1861 to 1865 ended slavery legally, but African Americans continued to fight for civil rights into the twentieth century.

## 21. Canada And French Settlers

Canada had a different political problem.

Britain conquered Canada from France in 1763.

French settlers demanded autonomous political status.

In 1867, Canada was organised as a Confederation of autonomous states.

This helped address settler political conflict, but native land rights remained unresolved for much longer.

## 22. Native Peoples Lose Their Land

As United States settlement expanded, native peoples were forced or induced to sign treaties selling land.

Payments were often low.

Settlers and officials sometimes took more land than promised or paid less than agreed.

The legal language of treaty often hid coercion and fraud.

Many settlers argued that native peoples did not deserve land because they did not use it according to European standards of farming, private property, and market production.

## 23. Cherokee Case And John Marshall

The Cherokee people made major efforts to understand and adapt to American ways.

They learned English and developed institutions.

But they were still denied equal rights.

In 1832, Chief Justice John Marshall recognised that the Cherokee were a distinct community with certain sovereignty and that Georgia's laws had no force in their territory.

President Andrew Jackson refused to honour the judgment.

He ordered forced removal.

## 24. Trail Of Tears

The forced removal of the Cherokee became known as the Trail of Tears.

About 15,000 people were forced to move.

More than a quarter died on the journey.

The event shows the gap between legal recognition and settler power.

Even a favourable court judgment could be ignored when state power supported removal.

## 25. Reservations

Native peoples were pushed westward and placed on reservations.

These were often lands with which they had no earlier connection.

Even land promised permanently could be taken again if minerals, gold, lead, or oil were found there.

Different tribes were sometimes forced to share the same land, creating conflict.

Reservations were a method of confinement.

## 26. Resistance

Native peoples did not give up without resistance.

The United States army crushed many rebellions between 1865 and 1890.

In Canada, the Metis resisted between 1869 and 1885.

These struggles show that indigenous peoples actively opposed dispossession, even when military power was unequal.

## 27. Anthropology And Inequality

Anthropology developed in North America from the 1840s in a context of unequal power.

Many anthropologists studied native communities as primitive peoples expected to disappear.

This reflected European assumptions that civilisation meant cities, writing, markets, and settled agriculture.

Later scholarship challenged this view and recognised indigenous cultures as complex, adaptive, and historically rich.

## 28. Chief Seattle And Sacred Land

Chief Seattle's response to land purchase demands expressed a very different relationship to land.

He described land, water, trees, memory, and ancestors as sacred and connected.

The key idea was that air, water, land, and memory cannot be simply bought and sold as property.

The response became important because it showed the moral distance between settler property logic and indigenous ecological-spiritual relationships.

## 29. Gold Rush And Railways

Gold was found in California in the 1840s.

Thousands of Europeans rushed to North America to seek fortune.

The Gold Rush accelerated:

- migration;
- settlement;
- railway construction;
- mining;
- demand for labour;
- displacement of native peoples.

Chinese labourers were recruited to build railways.

The United States transcontinental railway was completed by 1870.

The Canadian railway was completed by 1885.

## 30. Industrial Growth In North America

North American industrialisation differed from Britain's.

In Britain, early industrialisation was strongly linked to coal and cotton textiles.

In North America, industry expanded to build railways, machinery, and tools for large-scale farming.

Industrial towns grew.

Factories multiplied.

By 1890, the United States had become the leading industrial power.

## 31. Bison Extermination

By 1890, bison had almost disappeared.

This destroyed the hunting life of many Native American communities.

Bison extermination was not only environmental destruction.

It was also social and cultural destruction because it undermined food systems, mobility, clothing, tools, and ceremonies.

## 32. Property Rights And Democracy

United States settlers celebrated democracy and property rights.

But these rights were limited.

Voting rights and property protections applied mainly to white men.

Native peoples, African Americans, women, and many others were excluded or subordinated.

This contradiction is central to the chapter.

A settler society could speak of liberty while denying liberty to those it displaced or enslaved.

## 33. Winds Of Change In North America

Improvement began slowly in the twentieth century.

The 1928 Meriam survey exposed poor health and education conditions on reservations.

The Indian Reorganisation Act of 1934 gave native peoples on reservations the right to buy land and take loans.

In 1954, the Declaration of Indian Rights accepted United States citizenship but demanded that reservations and traditions be protected.

In Canada, the government first refused to recognise aboriginal rights in 1969.

Indigenous opposition led to debate and protest.

In 1982, the Canadian Constitution Act accepted existing aboriginal and treaty rights.

## 34. Australia: Deep Human History

Human habitation in Australia is very old.

Aboriginal peoples began living there over 40,000 years ago, possibly earlier.

They came from New Guinea when a land bridge connected the regions.

In Aboriginal traditions, they had always been there.

The past is understood through Dreamtime, a concept in which past and present are not separated in the European historical way.

## 35. Aboriginal Diversity

In the late eighteenth century, Australia had between 350 and 750 native communities.

Each had its own language.

Even today, many Aboriginal languages continue to be spoken.

The Torres Strait Islanders are another indigenous group in the north.

Together, Aboriginal peoples and Torres Strait Islanders form a small percentage of Australia's present population, but their cultural history is immense.

## 36. European Arrival In Australia

Important events include:

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| 1606 | Dutch travellers sight Australia |
| 1642 | Tasman lands on island later named Tasmania |
| 1770 | James Cook reaches Botany Bay |
| 1788 | British penal colony formed and Sydney founded |

British settlement in Australia began nearly three centuries after the first major European encounters in the Americas.

## 37. Convicts And Settlement

Britain used Australia as a penal colony after American independence ended convict transportation to the American colonies.

Many early settlers were convicts deported from England.

After finishing their jail terms, they could live in Australia as free people if they did not return to Britain.

They often took land for cultivation and pushed Aboriginal peoples away.

## 38. Effects On Aboriginal Peoples

Aboriginal peoples suffered from:

- exposure to new diseases;
- loss of land;
- loss of food resources;
- violence;
- forced labour;
- exclusion;
- destruction of sacred places;
- separation of children from families.

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, nearly 90 percent of some native populations died because of disease, loss of resources, and conflict.

## 39. Development Of Australia

Australia's settler economy developed through:

- sheep farms;
- mining stations;
- vineyards;
- wheat farming;
- gold rushes;
- cheap labour;
- European migration.

In 1901, the Federation of Australia was formed with six states.

In 1911, Canberra was established as the capital.

The name Canberra comes from a native word meaning meeting place.

## 40. Labour And Race In Australia

Some Aboriginal people worked on farms under harsh conditions close to slavery.

Chinese immigrants also provided cheap labour in the nineteenth century.

Settler fear of non-white migration led to exclusionary policies.

Until 1974, Australia had a policy designed to keep non-white immigrants out.

This is known as the White Australia policy.

## 41. The Great Australian Silence

In 1968, anthropologist W.E.H. Stanner spoke of the Great Australian Silence.

He meant the silence of historians about Aboriginal peoples.

Australian history had often been written as if it began with Captain Cook's arrival.

This ignored thousands of years of indigenous history.

Henry Reynolds later asked why Australians had not been told this history.

## 42. Multiculturalism

From 1974, multiculturalism became official policy in Australia.

This meant recognising and respecting the cultures of native peoples and immigrants from Europe and Asia.

It encouraged the study of native cultures, art, stories, languages, and histories.

Aboriginal people increasingly wrote their own life histories.

Museums and galleries began to include native art and cultural displays.

## 43. Terra Nullius

Terra nullius means land belonging to nobody.

Australian governments used this idea to justify European takeover.

It denied the historic relationship of Aboriginal peoples to land.

It treated Australia as legally empty before British settlement.

This doctrine was deeply unjust because Aboriginal peoples had occupied, named, used, and held sacred relationships with the land for tens of thousands of years.

## 44. Mabo Case

In 1992, the Australian High Court in the Mabo case declared terra nullius legally invalid.

It recognised that native claims to land existed before 1770.

This was a major turning point.

It did not undo the past, but it legally challenged the foundation of settler land claims.

## 45. Stolen Generations And National Sorry Day

Many children of mixed Aboriginal and European descent were forcibly separated from their native relatives.

This history is remembered as one of the gravest injustices of Australian settler policy.

In 1995, a national inquiry examined the separation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children from families.

On 26 May 1999, National Sorry Day was observed as a public apology for the children taken from their families.

## 46. Competing Ideas Of Civilisation

The chapter repeatedly contrasts European and indigenous ideas.

| European Settler View | Indigenous View |
|---|---|
| Land is property | Land is sacred relationship |
| Nature can be commodified | Nature is part of community and memory |
| Farming for profit shows progress | Taking only what is needed can be responsible |
| Written documents prove ownership | Oral memory and sacred connection carry meaning |
| Market production shows civilisation | Community, gift, and ecological knowledge matter |
| Empty land means land not farmed in European ways | Land can be used without private ownership |

## 47. Capitalism And Mainstream Culture

The chapter asks what mainstream means.

Mainstream culture is often shaped by economic and political power.

Settler states tried to make native peoples join the mainstream by adopting European language, law, schooling, property ideas, and religion.

But indigenous peoples often wanted citizenship without losing land, culture, language, and traditions.

This is a central political issue.

## 57. Textbook Question Guide

### Question 1: Comment on points of difference between native peoples of South and North America.

Native peoples of South and Central America included large imperial societies such as the Aztec and Inca, with cities, tribute, rulers, and more intensive agriculture.

In much of North America, native peoples lived in smaller bands and villages and combined hunting, fishing, gathering, gardening, and maize cultivation.

They did not usually create large kingdoms and empires like those in Central and South America.

Control of land as private property was less central for many North American groups.

European conquest also differed: Spanish and Portuguese empires dominated South America earlier, while North America saw later settler expansion by British, French, Dutch, and other European migrants.

### Question 2: Other than English language, what English economic and social features appear in nineteenth-century USA?

Nineteenth-century USA showed many features associated with English settler life: private property, farms, market production, representative institutions for white men, individual property rights, and capitalist enterprise.

It also developed railways, factories, large-scale farming, and industrial towns.

Settlers brought ideas of improvement through clearing land, fencing farms, and producing for profit.

However, these features existed alongside slavery, exclusion of native peoples, racial discrimination, and unequal citizenship.

### Question 3: What did the frontier mean to Americans?

To European settlers in the USA, the frontier meant opportunity, cheap land, personal freedom, farming, profit, expansion, and national destiny.

It was imagined as a moving boundary where settlers could build new lives.

To Native Americans, however, the frontier meant dispossession, forced movement, broken treaties, loss of hunting grounds, military conflict, reservations, and cultural destruction.

Thus, the frontier was freedom for settlers but confinement for native peoples.

### Question 4: Why was the history of Australian native peoples left out of history books?

Australian native history was left out because settler historians wrote history from a European viewpoint.

They treated Captain Cook's arrival as the beginning of Australian history.

They used the idea of terra nullius to pretend the land belonged to nobody.

Racist assumptions dismissed Aboriginal peoples as primitive or disappearing.

Much indigenous knowledge was oral and not treated as valid by European historians.

Only from the later twentieth century did historians, activists, museums, and native writers begin challenging this silence.

### Question 5: How satisfactory is a museum gallery display in explaining the culture of a people?

A museum gallery can be useful, but it is never complete by itself.

It can show objects, tools, paintings, textiles, weapons, maps, photographs, models, and recorded stories.

It can help visitors see material culture and historical change.

But culture is not only objects.

It includes language, land, memory, ritual, music, law, food, family, grief, humour, and living community practice.

A good gallery must include indigenous voices, oral histories, community interpretation, and the history of colonisation.

If objects are removed from their land and meaning, the display can become misleading.

### Question 6: Imagine an encounter in California in about 1880 between a former African slave, a Chinese labourer, a German Gold Rush migrant, and a Hopi native.

A strong answer should show different experiences of the same place.

The former African slave might speak of legal freedom after the Civil War but continued racial discrimination and the long struggle for civil rights.

The Chinese labourer might speak of building railways, doing hard work for low wages, and facing exclusion from white settlers.

The German migrant might speak of land, gold, farming, and the dream of prosperity in America.

The Hopi native might speak of land loss, broken promises, pressure from settlers, and the sacred relationship between people and place.

Their conversation should show that California and the American West meant opportunity for some, forced labour for others, and dispossession for native peoples.

## 58. High-Quality Answer Frames

### Frame 1: Explain Settler Colonialism

Settler colonialism is a form of colonialism in which migrants from an outside power settle permanently in a territory and build a new society there. Unlike direct rule over an existing population, settler colonialism usually involves taking land, reducing indigenous peoples to a minority, and creating new states based on settler property, law, and culture.

### Frame 2: Explain Native Land Ideas

Many indigenous communities did not see land as a commodity that could be bought and sold. Land was connected with ancestors, sacred places, food, stories, memory, and community responsibility. Europeans often saw land as private property for farming, mining, sale, and profit. This difference produced deep conflict during settlement.

### Frame 3: Explain The Frontier

The frontier was the moving edge of settler expansion in the United States. Settlers saw it as a space of freedom, property, and opportunity. Native peoples experienced it as shrinking land, military violence, forced removal, broken treaties, and confinement to reservations. The same frontier therefore meant expansion for settlers and contraction for indigenous peoples.

### Frame 4: Explain Terra Nullius

Terra nullius means land belonging to nobody. Australian authorities used this doctrine to justify taking land without treaties with Aboriginal peoples. It denied the long historical and sacred relationship of indigenous peoples with the land. The Mabo case in 1992 rejected this doctrine and recognised native land claims before British arrival.

### Frame 5: Explain The Winds Of Change

The winds of change refer to twentieth-century shifts in native rights and recognition. In North America, the Meriam survey, Indian Reorganisation Act, Declaration of Indian Rights, and Canadian Constitution Act helped recognise native rights. In Australia, the Great Australian Silence was challenged, multiculturalism became official policy, terra nullius was rejected in Mabo, and National Sorry Day acknowledged past injustice.

## 61. Advanced Analytical Points

### Democracy And Exclusion Coexisted

The United States celebrated democracy and property rights, but these rights initially protected mainly white men.

Native peoples, enslaved Africans, women, and many immigrants were excluded.

### Property Was A Weapon Of Colonisation

Settlers used private property ideas to claim that land not farmed in European ways was unused.

This justified taking land from people who used it differently.

### Museums Can Heal Or Repeat Erasure

Museums can preserve and explain culture when indigenous people guide interpretation.

But museums can also repeat colonial removal if they display objects without living context or consent.

### The Language Of Discovery Is Political

Calling Australia or America discovered by Europeans ignores the people already living there.

It turns invasion and settlement into heroic exploration.

### Rights Came Through Struggle

Recognition in the twentieth century came because indigenous peoples organised, protested, wrote, spoke, litigated, and preserved culture.

It was not simply granted by settler governments.

## 64. Final Chapter Summary

Displacing Indigenous Peoples studies the history of native peoples in North America and Australia under settler colonialism.

European settlers arrived with ideas of land as property, profit, farm, mine, and state territory.

Indigenous peoples often understood land as sacred, ancestral, shared, and inseparable from community life.

In North America, native communities had lived for thousands of years in diverse societies based on hunting, fishing, gathering, gardening, maize cultivation, crafts, oral memory, and alliances.

European trade first produced cooperation, but markets, alcohol, land hunger, farming, and settlement gradually produced dispossession.

The frontier meant freedom and opportunity for settlers, but forced movement and confinement for native peoples.

The Cherokee case, Trail of Tears, reservations, Gold Rush, railways, bison destruction, and industrial expansion show how settler growth depended on native loss.

In Australia, Aboriginal peoples and Torres Strait Islanders had deep histories, languages, cultures, and sacred relationships with land.

British penal settlement, sheep farms, mining, disease, violence, and land seizure devastated native communities.

The doctrine of terra nullius denied indigenous land rights for generations.

In the twentieth century, indigenous peoples in North America and Australia fought for recognition, cultural survival, land rights, and historical voice.

The Meriam survey, Indian Reorganisation Act, Canadian Constitution Act, Great Australian Silence debate, multiculturalism, Mabo case, and National Sorry Day mark stages in that struggle.

The chapter's key lesson is that modern nations were often built by erasing earlier peoples, but indigenous communities continued to resist, remember, and reclaim their histories.
