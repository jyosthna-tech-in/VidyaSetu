# Chapter 3: Organisation of Data
### NCERT Class 11 Statistics for Economics

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how raw data is arranged into a clear and usable form. After data is collected, it is usually scattered and difficult to understand. Organisation of data means classifying, arranging, and summarising it so that statistical analysis becomes possible.

The main idea is:

> Raw data must be organised before it can be analysed.

## 1. Why Organisation of Data Is Needed

Data collected from a survey or record is often raw and unarranged.

Example:

Marks of 100 students, monthly expenditure of households, prices of vegetables, or incomes of workers may be collected as a long list. A long list does not immediately show:

- Highest value
- Lowest value
- Most common value range
- Number of people in each group
- Pattern or concentration
- Comparisons between groups

Organisation brings order into the data.

Daily-life example:

A shopkeeper separates goods into groups such as grains, spices, soaps, and stationery. This makes it easier to find and sell goods. Similarly, a statistician groups raw data into classes to understand it.

---

## 2. Meaning of Raw Data

Raw data means data in its original, unclassified, and unarranged form.

Example:

```text
45, 72, 38, 91, 60, 45, 78, 52, 38, 66
```

This list gives values, but it does not clearly show the pattern. Once arranged or classified, it becomes easier to interpret.

### Problems With Raw Data

| Problem | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Disorganised | Values are not arranged in order |
| Difficult to compare | Patterns are hidden |
| Time-consuming | Searching for information takes effort |
| Not suitable for analysis | Statistical methods need organised data |
| Hard to summarise | Large raw data cannot be understood quickly |

---

## 3. Meaning of Classification

Classification means arranging data into groups or classes according to common characteristics.

Examples:

| Data | Possible classification |
|---|---|
| Students | Gender, class, marks, stream |
| Households | Income group, family size, location |
| Workers | Occupation, wage group, skill level |
| Villages | District, population size, literacy level |

Classification should be based on the purpose of the study.

If the purpose is to study income inequality, classify households by income. If the purpose is to study regional differences, classify by place.

---

## 4. Objectives of Classification

Classification helps to:

1. Condense large data.
2. Make data simple and clear.
3. Bring similar items together.
4. Enable comparison.
5. Show patterns and relationships.
6. Prepare data for tabulation, diagrams, averages, and other statistical tools.

Classification is not decoration. It is the first step toward meaningful statistical analysis.

---

## 5. Types of Classification

Data may be classified in different ways.

| Type | Basis | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Chronological classification | Time | Population by year |
| Spatial classification | Place | Literacy by state |
| Qualitative classification | Attribute or quality | Population by gender or literacy |
| Quantitative classification | Numerical variable | Students by marks or families by income |

---

## 6. Chronological Classification

Chronological classification arranges data according to time.

Time may be:

- Years
- Months
- Weeks
- Days
- Hours

Example:

| Year | Population |
|---|---|
| 1991 | 81.8 crore |
| 2001 | 102.7 crore |
| 2011 | 121.0 crore |

This type of data is useful for studying trends.

Uses:

- Population growth
- Price changes
- Production over years
- Sales over months
- Imports and exports over time

---

## 7. Spatial Classification

Spatial classification arranges data according to place or geographical location.

Examples:

| Place | Literacy rate |
|---|---|
| State A | 78 percent |
| State B | 84 percent |
| State C | 70 percent |

Uses:

- Compare states
- Compare rural and urban areas
- Compare districts
- Compare countries
- Study regional inequality

---

## 8. Qualitative Classification

Qualitative classification is based on attributes or qualities.

Attributes are characteristics that cannot be measured directly in numbers, though they can be counted after grouping.

Examples:

| Attribute | Classes |
|---|---|
| Gender | Male, female |
| Literacy | Literate, illiterate |
| Employment status | Employed, unemployed |
| Marital status | Married, unmarried |
| Social status | Rural, urban |

Qualitative classification helps study social and economic categories.

---

## 9. Quantitative Classification

Quantitative classification is based on numerical variables.

Examples:

| Variable | Possible classes |
|---|---|
| Marks | 0-20, 20-40, 40-60 |
| Income | 0-10,000; 10,000-20,000; 20,000-30,000 |
| Age | 0-10, 10-20, 20-30 |
| Expenditure | 1,000-2,000; 2,000-3,000; 3,000-4,000 |

This type is especially important because many statistical tools work with numerical data.

---

## 10. Variables and Attributes

### 10.1 Variable

A variable is a characteristic that can take different numerical values.

Examples:

- Age
- Income
- Marks
- Height
- Weight
- Expenditure

### 10.2 Attribute

An attribute is a quality or characteristic that is not measured directly as a number.

Examples:

- Gender
- Literacy
- Religion
- Occupation category
- Employment status

### Difference

| Basis | Variable | Attribute |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Numerical | Qualitative |
| Example | Income | Occupation type |
| Measurement | Measured in numbers | Classified into categories |
| Statistical use | Used in quantitative analysis | Counted after grouping |

---

## 11. Discrete and Continuous Variables

### 11.1 Discrete Variable

A discrete variable can take only certain separate values, usually whole numbers.

Examples:

- Number of students
- Number of rooms
- Number of children
- Number of books

You can have 3 students or 4 students, but not 3.5 students.

### 11.2 Continuous Variable

A continuous variable can take any value within a range.

Examples:

- Height
- Weight
- Time
- Income
- Distance

A person's height may be 160 cm, 160.5 cm, or 160.52 cm depending on measurement precision.

### Difference

| Basis | Discrete variable | Continuous variable |
|---|---|---|
| Values | Separate values | Any value in a range |
| Fractions | Usually not possible | Possible |
| Example | Number of households | Household income |

---

## 12. Frequency Distribution

A frequency distribution is a table that shows how many times each value or class occurs.

### Simple Example

Marks obtained by 10 students:

```text
10, 20, 20, 30, 30, 30, 40, 40, 50, 50
```

Frequency distribution:

| Marks | Frequency |
|---|---:|
| 10 | 1 |
| 20 | 2 |
| 30 | 3 |
| 40 | 2 |
| 50 | 2 |

Frequency means the number of times a value occurs.

---

## 13. Grouped Frequency Distribution

When data has many values, it is grouped into class intervals.

Example:

| Marks | Number of students |
|---|---:|
| 0-20 | 5 |
| 20-40 | 12 |
| 40-60 | 25 |
| 60-80 | 18 |
| 80-100 | 10 |

This is a grouped frequency distribution.

It is useful when:

- Data is large.
- Individual values are too many.
- Broad pattern is more important than each value.

---

## 14. Class Intervals and Class Limits

### 14.1 Class Interval

A class interval is a group or range used in a frequency distribution.

Example:

In the class 20-40:

- 20 is the lower limit.
- 40 is the upper limit.
- 20-40 is the class interval.

### 14.2 Class Width

Class width is the difference between the upper limit and lower limit.

Example:

For 20-40:

```text
Class width = 40 - 20 = 20
```

### 14.3 Class Midpoint

Class midpoint is the middle value of a class interval.

Formula:

```text
Class midpoint = (Lower limit + Upper limit) / 2
```

Example:

For 20-40:

```text
Midpoint = (20 + 40) / 2 = 30
```

---

## 15. Inclusive and Exclusive Class Intervals

### 15.1 Inclusive Method

In the inclusive method, both lower and upper limits are included in the class.

Example:

| Class |
|---|
| 0-9 |
| 10-19 |
| 20-29 |

The class 0-9 includes both 0 and 9.

This method is commonly used for discrete variables.

### 15.2 Exclusive Method

In the exclusive method, the lower limit is included but the upper limit is excluded.

Example:

| Class |
|---|
| 0-10 |
| 10-20 |
| 20-30 |

Here:

- 10 belongs to 10-20, not 0-10.
- 20 belongs to 20-30, not 10-20.

This method is suitable for continuous variables.

### Difference

| Basis | Inclusive method | Exclusive method |
|---|---|---|
| Upper limit | Included | Excluded |
| Example | 0-9, 10-19 | 0-10, 10-20 |
| Suitable for | Discrete variables | Continuous variables |

---

## 16. How to Form Classes

When making class intervals, decide:

1. Range of the data
2. Number of classes
3. Size of class interval
4. Type of interval
5. Tally and frequency count

### 16.1 Range

Range is the difference between the highest and lowest value.

Formula:

```text
Range = Highest value - Lowest value
```

Example:

If highest mark is 95 and lowest mark is 15:

```text
Range = 95 - 15 = 80
```

### 16.2 Number of Classes

Usually, the number of classes should not be too small or too large. A practical range is often between 6 and 15 classes, depending on data size and purpose.

Too few classes hide details. Too many classes make the table confusing.

### 16.3 Equal and Unequal Class Intervals

Equal intervals have the same width.

Example:

```text
0-10, 10-20, 20-30
```

Unequal intervals have different widths.

Example:

```text
0-1000, 1000-5000, 5000-10000, 10000-50000
```

Unequal intervals may be used when data is heavily concentrated in one part or has a very wide range, such as income.

---

## 17. Tally Marks

Tally marks are used to count observations quickly.

Method:

- Make one vertical mark for each observation.
- For every fifth observation, cross the previous four marks.
- Count tally marks to get frequency.

Example:

| Class | Tally | Frequency |
|---|---|---:|
| 0-10 | ||| | 3 |
| 10-20 | ||||/ | 5 |
| 20-30 | ||||/ || | 7 |

Tally marks reduce counting errors when preparing frequency tables.

---

## 18. Frequency Array

A frequency array is used for discrete data. It shows values and their frequencies.

Example:

| Family size | Number of households |
|---|---:|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 15 |
| 3 | 25 |
| 4 | 35 |
| 5 | 10 |

This is useful when the variable takes only distinct values.

---

## 19. Univariate and Bivariate Frequency Distribution

### 19.1 Univariate Frequency Distribution

A univariate frequency distribution studies one variable.

Examples:

- Marks of students
- Income of households
- Age of workers

### 19.2 Bivariate Frequency Distribution

A bivariate frequency distribution studies two variables together.

Examples:

- Sales and advertisement expenditure
- Income and consumption
- Age and savings
- Education and income

Bivariate data helps examine relationships between two variables.

### Difference

| Basis | Univariate | Bivariate |
|---|---|---|
| Variables studied | One | Two |
| Example | Marks only | Income and expenditure |
| Use | Shows distribution of one characteristic | Shows relationship between two characteristics |

---

## 20. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating raw data as ready for analysis | Raw data must be organised first. |
| Classifying without purpose | Classification depends on the objective of the study. |
| Confusing attribute with variable | Attribute is qualitative; variable is numerical. |
| Using too many classes | Too many classes make the table complex. |
| Using too few classes | Too few classes hide information. |
| Confusing inclusive and exclusive intervals | Inclusive includes upper limit; exclusive excludes upper limit. |
| Forgetting class midpoint formula | Midpoint is average of lower and upper limits. |
