## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how changes in a group of related variables are measured through index numbers. Prices, production, cost of living, stock market values, imports, exports, and industrial output often change over time. Index numbers summarise these changes into one comparable figure.

The main idea is:

> An index number measures relative change in a variable or group of variables compared with a base period.

If a price index is 150, it means prices are 50 percent higher than the base period, assuming the base is 100.

## 1. Meaning of Index Number

An index number is a statistical device that measures relative change in a variable or group of variables over time, place, or situation.

It usually compares the current period with a base period.

Base period value is commonly taken as 100.

Example:

| Price index | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| 100 | No change from base period |
| 120 | Prices are 20 percent higher than base period |
| 80 | Prices are 20 percent lower than base period |
| 250 | Prices are 150 percent higher than base period |

---

## 2. Why Index Numbers Are Needed

Index numbers are used because many economic changes involve several items at once.

Example:

A household buys rice, wheat, milk, vegetables, fuel, clothing, and transport. If all prices change differently, one price cannot show the overall change in cost of living. A price index summarises the change.

Index numbers help in:

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Measuring inflation | Shows rise in general price level |
| Comparing periods | Compares current year with base year |
| Wage adjustment | Dearness allowance may be linked to price index |
| Policy decisions | Government studies price, output, and production trends |
| Business decisions | Firms study demand, production, and market trends |
| Stock market tracking | Sensex tracks selected share price movement |

---

## 3. Important Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Base period | Period used as the reference point |
| Current period | Period being compared with base period |
| Price relative | Current price as a percentage of base price |
| Weight | Relative importance of an item |
| Basket | Selected group of commodities included in an index |
| Inflation | Rise in general price level |
| Purchasing power | Value of money in terms of goods and services it can buy |

## 4. Types of Index Numbers

| Type | Measures |
|---|---|
| Price index | Change in prices |
| Quantity index | Change in quantities produced, sold, or consumed |
| Value index | Change in total value, price x quantity |

This chapter mainly focuses on price index numbers.

## 5. Simple Aggregative Price Index

This method compares the sum of current prices with the sum of base prices.

Formula:

```text
P01 = (Sigma P1 / Sigma P0) x 100
```

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| P01 | Price index for current period with base period reference |
| P1 | Current period price |
| P0 | Base period price |

### Steps

1. Add base period prices.
2. Add current period prices.
3. Divide current price total by base price total.
4. Multiply by 100.

### Limitation

It treats every commodity as equally important. A rise in the price of salt and a rise in the price of foodgrain may be treated alike, even though their importance differs.

---

## 6. Simple Average of Price Relatives

A price relative shows the percentage change in price of each item.

Formula for price relative:

```text
Price Relative = (P1 / P0) x 100
```

Simple average of price relatives:

```text
P01 = Sigma [(P1 / P0) x 100] / N
```

Where N is the number of items.

This method first calculates percentage change for each item, then averages those changes.

---

## 7. Weighted Index Numbers

Weighted index numbers consider the relative importance of items.

Example:

Food usually has more importance in a consumer's budget than a luxury item. Therefore, food should receive a higher weight in a cost of living index.

Weights may be based on:

- Quantity consumed
- Expenditure share
- Production share
- Importance in the basket

---

## 8. Weighted Aggregative Price Index

### 8.1 Laspeyres Price Index

Laspeyres index uses base period quantities as weights.

Formula:

```text
P01 = (Sigma P1 Q0 / Sigma P0 Q0) x 100
```

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Q0 | Base period quantity |

It answers:

> How much would the base period basket cost in the current period compared with the base period?

### 8.2 Paasche Price Index

Paasche index uses current period quantities as weights.

Formula:

```text
P01 = (Sigma P1 Q1 / Sigma P0 Q1) x 100
```

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Q1 | Current period quantity |

It answers:

> How much does the current period basket cost now compared with what it would have cost in the base period?

### Laspeyres vs Paasche

| Basis | Laspeyres | Paasche |
|---|---|---|
| Weight used | Base period quantity | Current period quantity |
| Basket | Base period basket | Current period basket |
| Formula | Sigma P1Q0 / Sigma P0Q0 x 100 | Sigma P1Q1 / Sigma P0Q1 x 100 |
| Practical feature | Easier if base quantities are fixed | Requires current quantities |

---

## 9. Weighted Average of Price Relatives

Formula:

```text
P01 = Sigma WR / Sigma W
```

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| W | Weight |
| R | Price relative = (P1/P0) x 100 |

This method averages percentage price changes after assigning weights.

## 10. Important Index Numbers

### 10.1 Consumer Price Index

Consumer Price Index, or CPI, measures average change in retail prices of goods and services consumed by a particular group of consumers.

It is also called cost of living index.

Uses:

- Measures inflation faced by consumers.
- Used for wage and dearness allowance adjustments.
- Helps understand cost of living.

Different groups may need different CPIs because consumption baskets differ.

Example:

Industrial workers, agricultural labourers, rural consumers, and urban consumers may spend differently on food, housing, fuel, clothing, and services.

### 10.2 Wholesale Price Index

Wholesale Price Index, or WPI, measures changes in wholesale prices.

It usually focuses on goods traded in bulk, such as:

- Primary articles
- Fuel and power
- Manufactured products

WPI data may be available quickly and is often used to track price trends in production and trade.

### 10.3 Index of Industrial Production

Index of Industrial Production, or IIP, measures changes in industrial output.

It covers sectors such as:

- Mining
- Manufacturing
- Electricity

It helps understand growth or slowdown in industrial production.

### 10.4 Sensex

Sensex is a stock market index of the Bombay Stock Exchange. It tracks selected major companies and reflects broad movement in share prices.

If Sensex rises, it may indicate investor confidence and expectations of better company performance. If it falls, it may indicate weak sentiment or concern.

Sensex is not the same as the entire economy, but it is an important market indicator.

## 11. Inflation and Purchasing Power

Inflation means a rise in the general price level.

Index numbers help measure inflation.

If CPI rises from 100 to 125:

```text
Inflation = 25 percent
```

### Purchasing Power of Money

Purchasing power means the amount of goods and services money can buy.

When prices rise, purchasing power falls.

Formula:

```text
Purchasing power of money = 1 / Price index
```

Often expressed with base 100:

```text
Purchasing power = 100 / Price index
```

If price index = 200:

```text
Purchasing power = 100 / 200 = 0.5
```

Money buys only half as much as in the base period.

## 12. Issues in Constructing Index Numbers

Constructing a good index number requires careful decisions.

### 12.1 Purpose

The purpose must be clear.

Example:

An index for cost of living is different from an index for industrial production.

### 12.2 Selection of Base Period

A good base period should be:

- Normal
- Recent enough
- Free from war, famine, crisis, or abnormal events
- Suitable for comparison

### 12.3 Selection of Items

Items included in the index should represent the purpose.

Example:

CPI should include goods and services actually consumed by the target group.

### 12.4 Selection of Weights

Weights should reflect importance.

Example:

If food takes a large share of household expenditure, it should receive a large weight in CPI.

### 12.5 Choice of Formula

Different formulas can give different results. The method should match the objective and available data.

### 12.6 Data Reliability

Prices, quantities, and weights must be collected from reliable sources.

## 13. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying index number is always a price index | Index can measure price, quantity, or value. |
| Ignoring base period | Every index needs a reference period. |
| Treating all items as equally important | Weighted index is needed when importance differs. |
| Confusing Laspeyres and Paasche | Laspeyres uses base quantities; Paasche uses current quantities. |
| Saying CPI and WPI are the same | CPI measures retail consumer prices; WPI measures wholesale prices. |
| Ignoring purchasing power | Higher prices reduce purchasing power of money. |

## Final Takeaway

Index numbers make economic change measurable. They summarise changes in prices, production, cost of living, and markets into comparable figures. A useful index depends on a proper base period, representative items, suitable weights, reliable data, and the correct formula.
