## Chapter Purpose

This chapter introduces why Statistics is studied with Economics. Economics deals with choices, scarcity, production, consumption, distribution, poverty, unemployment, prices, income, and many other real-world problems. To understand these problems clearly, economists need facts. Many of those facts are numerical. Statistics provides the tools to collect, organise, present, analyse, and interpret such facts.

The main idea is:

> Economics gives the problems and questions. Statistics gives the numerical tools needed to study them carefully.

## 1. Why Economics Begins With Scarcity

Human wants are unlimited, but the resources used to satisfy those wants are limited. This is the starting point of Economics.

### Simple Example

Suppose a student has limited pocket money. The student may want:

- A book
- A movie ticket
- Snacks
- Mobile recharge
- A gift for a friend

If the student cannot buy everything, a choice must be made. Choosing one thing often means giving up another. This is an economic problem.

### Key Idea

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Wants | Things people desire |
| Resources | Means used to satisfy wants |
| Scarcity | Resources are limited compared with wants |
| Choice | Selecting one option from available alternatives |
| Alternative uses | The same resource can be used in different ways |

Economics studies how individuals and society make choices when resources are scarce.

---

## 2. Meaning of Economics

Economics may be understood as the study of how people and society choose to use scarce resources that have alternative uses to produce goods and services and distribute them for consumption.

This meaning includes four important points:

| Part | Explanation |
|---|---|
| People and society choose | Economic decisions are made by individuals, families, firms, and governments |
| Scarce resources | Resources are limited |
| Alternative uses | Resources can be used in different ways |
| Production and distribution | Goods and services are produced and then shared among people |

Economics is not only about money. It is about choices, trade-offs, welfare, and the use of limited resources.

---

## 3. Economic Activities

Economic activities are activities related to earning, spending, producing, exchanging, or distributing goods and services.

Examples:

| Activity | Why it is economic |
|---|---|
| Farmer growing wheat | Produces a good |
| Shopkeeper selling notebooks | Exchanges goods for money |
| Teacher teaching in a school | Provides a service |
| Worker receiving wages | Earns income |
| Family buying vegetables | Consumes goods |

Non-economic activities are performed out of love, duty, affection, or personal satisfaction, not mainly for money or exchange.

Example:

A parent helping a child study at home is usually a non-economic activity. A tutor teaching students for fees is an economic activity.

---

## 4. Consumption, Production, and Distribution

Economics studies three major areas.

### 4.1 Consumption

Consumption means using goods and services to satisfy wants.

Examples:

- Eating food
- Wearing clothes
- Using a mobile phone
- Travelling by bus

Consumer decisions depend on income, prices, preferences, and available alternatives.

### 4.2 Production

Production means creating goods and services that satisfy human wants.

Examples:

- Farmer producing rice
- Factory producing shoes
- Doctor providing medical service
- Software company creating an app

Producers decide what to produce, how to produce, and how much to produce.

### 4.3 Distribution

Distribution means how income generated from production is shared among people.

Income may be distributed as:

| Factor payment | Receiver |
|---|---|
| Wages and salaries | Labour |
| Rent | Land owner |
| Interest | Capital owner or lender |
| Profit | Entrepreneur |

Distribution helps us understand inequality, poverty, and the sharing of national income.

---

## 5. Why Economics Needs Facts

Economic problems cannot be understood only through opinions. They require facts.

For example, if we want to study poverty, we need information such as:

- Number of poor households
- Income levels
- Employment status
- Education levels
- Access to health services
- Regional differences

If we want to study unemployment, we need data on:

- Labour force
- Number of employed people
- Number of unemployed people
- Age groups affected
- Education of unemployed persons
- Rural and urban differences

Without reliable facts, economic discussion remains vague.

---

## 6. Meaning of Data

Data means facts or information collected for analysis.

In Economics, data often refers to numerical facts related to economic activities.

Examples:

| Statement | Type |
|---|---|
| Rice production increased from one year to another | Quantitative economic data |
| A region has high unemployment | Economic observation |
| A household earns Rs 20,000 per month | Numerical data |
| The price of wheat is Rs 30 per kg | Numerical data |

Data helps convert general statements into precise statements.

Vague statement:

> Prices are rising.

Data-based statement:

> The price index increased by 6 percent during the year.

The second statement is more useful because it can be verified, compared, and analysed.

## 7. Qualitative and Quantitative Data

### 7.1 Qualitative Data

Qualitative data describes qualities or attributes that are not directly numerical.

Examples:

- Beauty
- Intelligence
- Honesty
- Skill
- Satisfaction
- Preference

Some qualitative features can be converted into numerical form through suitable methods, such as rating scales or categories.

Example:

Customer satisfaction may be recorded as:

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Very dissatisfied |
| 2 | Dissatisfied |
| 3 | Neutral |
| 4 | Satisfied |
| 5 | Very satisfied |

### 7.2 Quantitative Data

Quantitative data is expressed in numbers.

Examples:

- Income in rupees
- Marks in a subject
- Population
- Production in tonnes
- Price in rupees
- Unemployment rate

### Difference

| Basis | Qualitative data | Quantitative data |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Describes quality | Measures quantity |
| Form | Words, categories, ranks | Numbers |
| Example | Skill level | Monthly income |
| Use in Economics | Helps understand behaviour and conditions | Helps measure and compare economic facts |

---

## 8. Meaning of Statistics

In this chapter, Statistics means the study of numerical facts in a systematic form.

Statistics includes:

1. Collection of data
2. Organisation of data
3. Presentation of data
4. Analysis of data
5. Interpretation of data

### Statistics as a Tool

Statistics is not the final answer by itself. It is a tool that helps economists understand economic problems.

Example:

If an economist wants to study rising prices, Statistics can help by:

- Collecting price data
- Comparing prices over time
- Calculating averages or index numbers
- Showing trends
- Helping identify possible causes

---

## 9. Role of Statistics in Economics

### 9.1 It Presents Economic Facts Precisely

Statistics converts vague statements into exact statements.

| Vague statement | Statistical statement |
|---|---|
| Many people are unemployed | Unemployment rate is 7 percent |
| Production has increased | Production rose from 100 units to 130 units |
| Prices are high | Consumer price index increased by 5 percent |

Precision improves understanding and reduces confusion.

### 9.2 It Helps Compare

Statistics allows comparison across:

- Time periods
- Regions
- Income groups
- Industries
- Countries

Example:

Comparing literacy rates across states helps identify regions that need stronger education policy.

### 9.3 It Helps Understand Relationships

Economic variables are often related.

Examples:

| Relationship | Possible question |
|---|---|
| Income and consumption | Does consumption rise when income rises? |
| Price and demand | Does demand fall when price rises? |
| Education and employment | Are educated persons more likely to get jobs? |
| Rainfall and crop output | Does crop output depend on rainfall? |

Statistics helps study such relationships with data.

### 9.4 It Helps in Policy-Making

Policies should be based on facts.

Examples:

| Problem | Data needed | Possible policy use |
|---|---|---|
| Poverty | Income and consumption data | Poverty relief programmes |
| Unemployment | Labour force and job data | Employment schemes |
| Inflation | Price data | Monetary or fiscal measures |
| Low farm output | Production and rainfall data | Irrigation and input support |

Statistics helps identify the size, seriousness, and pattern of a problem.

### 9.5 It Helps Forecast

Past data can be used to estimate future trends.

Examples:

- Future population
- Future demand
- Expected production
- Price movement
- Consumption pattern

Forecasts are not always exact, but they help planning.

## 10. Statistics and Economic Problems

Statistics is useful in studying major economic problems such as:

| Economic problem | How statistics helps |
|---|---|
| Poverty | Measures income, consumption, and living conditions |
| Unemployment | Measures labour force and joblessness |
| Rising prices | Measures inflation and price changes |
| Population growth | Measures birth rate, death rate, and growth rate |
| Inequality | Compares income and wealth distribution |
| Low production | Measures output and productivity |

Economics asks why these problems exist and what should be done. Statistics helps provide the evidence.

## 11. Statistics in Consumption, Production, and Distribution

### Consumption

Statistics can show:

- What people buy
- How much they spend
- How spending changes with income
- How price changes affect demand

### Production

Statistics can show:

- Quantity produced
- Cost of production
- Productivity
- Changes in output over time

### Distribution

Statistics can show:

- Share of wages, rent, interest, and profit
- Income inequality
- Poverty levels
- Regional differences

Thus, Statistics supports all major branches of economic study.

## 12. Limitations and Misuse of Statistics

Statistics is powerful, but it can be misused.

### 12.1 Averages Can Mislead

An average may hide differences.

Example:

If one person earns Rs 1,00,000 and four people earn Rs 5,000 each, the average income may look moderate, but most people are still poor.

### 12.2 Data May Be Incomplete

If data is collected from too few people or from the wrong group, conclusions may be unreliable.

### 12.3 Wrong Interpretation Can Create Wrong Conclusions

Statistics needs careful interpretation. Numbers do not explain themselves.

Example:

If sales increase after advertising, it does not automatically prove that advertising alone caused the increase. Other factors may have changed too.

### 12.4 Statistics Cannot Replace Judgement

Statistics supports decision-making, but human judgement is still needed to interpret context, ethics, and practical realities.

## 13. Core Definitions

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Economics | Study of how people and society choose to use scarce resources with alternative uses |
| Scarcity | Situation where resources are limited compared with wants |
| Choice | Selection among alternatives |
| Consumption | Use of goods and services to satisfy wants |
| Production | Creation of goods and services |
| Distribution | Sharing of income among factors and people |
| Data | Facts collected for analysis |
| Quantitative data | Data expressed in numbers |

| Policy | Measure adopted to solve or reduce an economic problem |

---

## 14. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying Economics is only about money | Economics is about choices under scarcity. |
| Ignoring alternative uses of resources | Scarce resources usually have many possible uses. |
| Treating all data as quantitative | Some data is qualitative, though it may be classified or ranked. |
| Saying Statistics solves economic problems by itself | Statistics helps analyse problems; policies and decisions solve them. |
| Blindly trusting averages | Averages can hide inequalities and details. |
| Writing facts without interpretation | Economic answers must explain what the facts show. |

## 15. Answer Frames

### Frame 1: Explain scarcity and choice

1. Define scarcity.
2. Explain unlimited wants and limited resources.
3. Mention alternative uses.
4. Give an example such as pocket money, land, time, or government budget.
5. Conclude that scarcity creates the need for choice.

### Frame 2: Explain the role of Statistics in Economics

1. Define Statistics.
2. State that Economics studies real economic problems.
3. Explain that data is needed for precise analysis.
4. Mention comparison, relationship, policy-making, and forecasting.
5. Add examples such as poverty, unemployment, prices, or production.

### Frame 3: Distinguish qualitative and quantitative data

1. Define both terms.
2. Give examples.
3. Explain that Economics uses both, but Statistics mainly works with numerical or classified data.
4. Present the distinction in a table.
