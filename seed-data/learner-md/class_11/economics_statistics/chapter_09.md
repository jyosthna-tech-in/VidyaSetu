# Chapter 9: Use of Statistical Tools
### NCERT Class 11 Statistics for Economics

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how the statistical tools learned in earlier chapters can be used in a project. A project is not only a written report. It is a complete process of identifying a problem, collecting data, organising it, presenting it, analysing it, interpreting results, and drawing conclusions.

The main idea is:

> Statistical tools become meaningful when they are used to study real economic and social problems.

---

## Learning Outcomes

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Understand the steps involved in preparing a project.
2. Identify a suitable economic or social problem for study.
3. Choose a target group.
4. Decide whether primary data, secondary data, or both are needed.
5. Prepare questionnaires or interview schedules.
6. Organise and present collected data.
7. Apply averages, dispersion, correlation, diagrams, and other tools.
8. Interpret results carefully.
9. Write conclusions and suggestions.
10. Prepare bibliography and project report structure.

## 1. Why Projects Are Important

A project helps students move from theory to application.

Earlier chapters taught:

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| Collection of data | Gathering facts |
| Organisation of data | Classifying and arranging data |
| Presentation of data | Tables, diagrams, graphs |
| Central tendency | Finding average or typical value |
| Dispersion | Measuring variation |
| Correlation | Studying relationship |
| Index numbers | Measuring relative change |

This chapter shows how these tools can be combined to study a real problem.

---

## 2. Steps in Making a Project

The project process has these major steps:

1. Identify a problem or area of study.
2. Choose the target group.
3. Collect data.
4. Organise and present data.
5. Analyse and interpret data.
6. Draw conclusions.
7. Give suggestions if possible.
8. Prepare bibliography.
9. Write the final report.

---

## 3. Identifying a Problem or Area of Study

The first step is to decide what you want to study.

A good topic should be:

- Clear
- Specific
- Researchable
- Connected with economic or social life
- Possible within available time and resources

Examples:

| Broad area | Better project topic |
|---|---|
| Transport | Problems faced by students using public transport |
| Consumer awareness | Awareness of consumer rights among households |
| Education | Reasons for dropout among school children in a locality |
| Banking | Saving habits of households in a neighbourhood |
| Farming | Problems faced by farmers in a selected village |
| Product study | Effect of advertisement on demand for a product |

Avoid topics that are too large, such as "poverty in India", unless the study is narrowed to a specific area and sample.

---

## 4. Choosing the Target Group

The target group is the group from which information will be collected.

Example:

| Project topic | Target group |
|---|---|
| Use of public transport by students | Students in selected schools or colleges |
| Consumer awareness | Households in selected area |
| Demand for a new soap | Rural and urban consumers |
| Bank saving habits | Earners or households in selected locality |
| School dropout reasons | Students, parents, teachers, and school records |

Choosing the target group correctly helps frame relevant questions.

---

## 5. Collection of Data

The method of data collection depends on the objective.

### 5.1 Primary Data

Primary data is collected directly for the project.

Methods:

- Questionnaire
- Interview schedule
- Personal interview
- Telephone interview
- Observation

Use primary data when:

- Fresh information is needed.
- Local conditions are being studied.
- Existing data is not available.

### 5.2 Secondary Data

Secondary data is already collected by another source.

Sources:

- Government reports
- Census data
- Economic Survey
- Newspapers
- Books
- Research reports
- Official websites
- Institutional records

Use secondary data when:

- Reliable data already exists.
- Time or resources are limited.
- The project needs background information.

### 5.3 Using Both

Many good projects use both primary and secondary data.

Example:

A project on school dropout may use school records as secondary data and interviews with parents as primary data.

---

## 6. Questionnaire Design

A questionnaire should collect only information needed for the project.

Rules:

1. Keep questions clear.
2. Avoid very long questionnaires.
3. Use simple language.
4. Avoid leading questions.
5. Avoid double negatives.
6. Arrange questions from general to specific.
7. Include both closed and open questions if needed.
8. Respect privacy.

### Example

Poor question:

> Don't you think public transport is very bad?

Better question:

> How would you rate the reliability of public transport in your area?

Options:

- Very good
- Good
- Average
- Poor
- Very poor

---

## 7. Organisation and Presentation of Data

After collecting data, it must be processed.

Steps:

1. Check responses for completeness.
2. Remove or mark unclear responses.
3. Classify responses into categories.
4. Prepare frequency tables.
5. Use suitable diagrams.

### Suitable Presentation Tools

| Data type | Suitable presentation |
|---|---|
| Categories | Bar diagram |
| Parts of total | Pie diagram |
| Frequency distribution | Histogram |
| Time-based data | Line graph |
| Exact comparison | Table |

Presentation should make findings easier to understand, not just decorate the report.

---

## 8. Analysis and Interpretation

Analysis means applying statistical tools to the data.

Interpretation means explaining what the results show.

Possible tools:

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| Mean | Average level |
| Median | Middle value |
| Mode | Most common response |
| Range | Simple spread |
| Standard deviation | Variation |
| Correlation | Relationship between two variables |
| Percentage | Share or proportion |
| Index number | Change over time |

Example:

If 70 percent of respondents say bus delays are frequent, the interpretation may be:

> A majority of respondents face reliability problems in public transport.

Do not stop at calculation. Explain the meaning.

---

## 9. Drawing Conclusions

The conclusion should be based on evidence from the data.

A good conclusion:

- Answers the project question.
- Summarises major findings.
- Avoids exaggeration.
- Mentions limitations.
- Gives suggestions if relevant.

Weak conclusion:

> Public transport is bad.

Better conclusion:

> The survey shows that waiting time and overcrowding are the two most common problems reported by students. Therefore, improvement in bus frequency during school and college hours may reduce inconvenience.

---

## 10. Suggestions

Suggestions should follow from findings.

Example:

| Finding | Suitable suggestion |
|---|---|
| Most students report bus delay | Increase frequency during peak hours |
| Many households lack consumer rights awareness | Conduct local awareness campaigns |
| Farmers report lack of irrigation | Improve water access and extension support |
| Low saving among households | Promote financial literacy and small saving schemes |

Avoid suggestions unrelated to data.

---

## 11. Bibliography

Bibliography lists the sources used in the project.

It may include:

- Books
- Reports
- Newspapers
- Websites
- Government documents
- Research papers
- Institutional records

Bibliography increases credibility and shows that secondary sources were used responsibly.

A clean project report may follow this structure:

1. Title page
2. Acknowledgement
3. Certificate, if required by school
4. Index or table of contents
5. Introduction
6. Objective of the study
7. Scope of the study
8. Method of data collection
9. Target group and sample

11. Data organisation and tables
12. Diagrams and graphs
13. Statistical analysis
14. Interpretation
15. Findings
16. Conclusion
17. Suggestions
18. Limitations
19. Bibliography
20. Appendix, if needed

## 14. Mini Project Blueprint

### Topic

Saving habits of households in a selected locality.

### Objective

To study the relationship between income and saving habits of households.

### Target Group

Households in the selected locality.

### Data Collection

Primary data through questionnaire and secondary background from official reports if needed.

### Possible Questions

- Monthly income range
- Monthly saving range
- Main reason for saving
- Bank account ownership
- Use of digital banking
- Awareness of saving schemes

### Presentation

- Income group table
- Saving group table
- Pie chart for purpose of saving
- Bar diagram for bank account ownership

### Analysis

- Average income group, if numerical data is collected
- Average saving
- Percentage of households saving regularly
- Correlation between income and savings, if suitable

### Conclusion

Conclusion should state whether income and saving appear related, what proportion saves regularly, and what improvements may help.

## 15. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Choosing a very broad topic | Narrow the topic to a manageable area and group. |
| Asking irrelevant questions | Every question should support the objective. |
| Collecting data without target group | Define who will be studied first. |
| Showing tables without interpretation | Explain what each table means. |
| Using diagrams for decoration | Diagrams should clarify data. |
| Making conclusions not supported by data | Conclusions must follow evidence. |
| Forgetting bibliography | Sources must be recorded. |
| Ignoring limitations | Every project has limits such as sample size and area. |
