# Chapter 4: Presentation of Data
### NCERT Class 11 Statistics for Economics

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how organised data can be presented clearly. Presentation of data means showing data in a form that is easy to read, compare, and understand.

The main idea is:

> Data becomes more useful when it is presented clearly through text, tables, diagrams, and graphs.

---

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning and need of data presentation.
2. Distinguish textual, tabular, and diagrammatic presentation.
3. Understand the parts of a good table.
4. Explain qualitative, quantitative, temporal, and spatial tabulation.
5. Understand bar diagrams and their types.
6. Explain pie diagrams.
7. Understand histograms, frequency polygons, frequency curves, and ogives.
8. Understand arithmetic line graphs or time series graphs.
9. Choose a suitable presentation method for a given type of data.

## 1. Meaning of Presentation of Data

Presentation of data means arranging data in a clear and systematic form so that it can be easily understood.

After collection and organisation, data may still be large. Presentation makes it:

- Compact
- Readable
- Comparable
- Attractive
- Useful for analysis
- Useful for decision-making

Example:

A paragraph describing literacy rates may be difficult to compare. A table showing male, female, rural, and urban literacy can be understood quickly.

---

## 2. Forms of Data Presentation

There are three main forms:

| Form | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Textual presentation | Data is explained in words and sentences |
| Tabular presentation | Data is arranged in rows and columns |
| Diagrammatic presentation | Data is shown through diagrams or graphs |

Each form has a purpose. Small data can be written in text. Larger data is better shown in tables or diagrams.

---

## 3. Textual Presentation

Textual presentation describes data within written text.

Example:

> In a town, 5 petrol pumps were open and 17 were closed during a bandh. Out of 11 schools, 2 were closed and 9 were open.

### Advantages

- Simple for small data.
- Allows explanation and emphasis.
- Useful when exact comparison is not the main focus.

### Limitations

- Not suitable for large data.
- Comparisons become difficult.
- Reader must go through the whole passage.
- Patterns may not be visible.

Use textual presentation when the data is small and explanation matters more than detailed comparison.

---

## 4. Tabular Presentation

Tabular presentation means presenting data in rows and columns.

A table is useful because it arranges data systematically and makes comparison easier.

Example:

| Category | Rural | Urban | Total |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Male | 79 | 90 | 82 |
| Female | 59 | 80 | 65 |
| Total | 68 | 84 | 74 |

This format is easier to read than a long paragraph.

---

## 5. Parts of a Table

A good table has several parts.

| Part | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Table number | Identifies the table |
| Title | Describes what the table shows |
| Captions | Column headings |
| Stubs | Row headings |
| Body | Main numerical data |
| Unit | Measurement used, such as rupees, percent, kg |
| Source | Origin of data |
| Footnote | Additional clarification if needed |

### Why Parts Matter

Without a title, the reader may not know what the table is about. Without units, numbers can be misunderstood. Without source, reliability is difficult to judge.

---

## 6. Rules for a Good Table

A table should be:

1. Clear and simple.
2. Properly titled.
3. Arranged logically.
4. Given correct units.
5. Not overcrowded.
6. Accurate in totals.
7. Comparable across rows and columns.
8. Supported by source and footnotes where required.

Good tabulation makes data easier to analyse.

---

## 7. Types of Tabular Classification

Data in tables may be classified in four major ways:

| Type | Basis | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Qualitative | Attributes | Literacy by gender |
| Quantitative | Numerical values | Respondents by age group |
| Temporal | Time | Sales by year |
| Spatial | Place | Population by state |

### 7.1 Qualitative Classification

Qualitative classification is based on attributes.

Examples:

- Male/female
- Rural/urban
- Literate/illiterate
- Employed/unemployed

### 7.2 Quantitative Classification

Quantitative classification is based on numerical characteristics.

Examples:

- Age groups
- Income groups
- Height groups
- Production levels

### 7.3 Temporal Classification

Temporal classification is based on time.

Examples:

- Sales from 2018 to 2023
- Population by census year
- Monthly production

### 7.4 Spatial Classification

Spatial classification is based on place.

Examples:

- State-wise literacy
- District-wise population
- Country-wise exports

---

## 8. Diagrammatic Presentation

Diagrammatic presentation shows data through diagrams or graphs.

Advantages:

- Easy to understand.
- Attractive.
- Helps quick comparison.
- Shows patterns visually.
- Useful for presentations and reports.

Limitations:

- Less precise than tables.
- Can mislead if scale or design is wrong.
- Not suitable for showing too many exact details.

Common diagrams:

- Bar diagram
- Pie diagram
- Histogram
- Frequency polygon
- Frequency curve
- Ogive
- Line graph

---

## 9. Bar Diagrams

A bar diagram uses rectangular bars to represent data.

Bars may be vertical or horizontal. The height or length of each bar represents the value.

### Rules

- Bars should have equal width.
- Gaps between bars should be equal.
- Scale should be clear.
- Bars should start from a common baseline.
- The diagram should have a clear title.

### 9.1 Simple Bar Diagram

A simple bar diagram represents one variable.

Example:

| Product | Sales |
|---|---:|
| A | 50 |
| B | 70 |
| C | 40 |

Use: Compare sales of different products.

### 9.2 Multiple Bar Diagram

A multiple bar diagram compares two or more related series.

Example:

| Year | Exports | Imports |
|---|---:|---:|
| 2021 | 100 | 120 |
| 2022 | 130 | 150 |

Use: Compare exports and imports over years.

### 9.3 Component Bar Diagram

A component bar diagram shows total values divided into parts.

Example:

Family expenditure divided into food, rent, education, medicine, and transport.

Use: Show composition of a total.

---

## 10. Pie Diagram

A pie diagram, or pie chart, represents parts of a whole using sectors of a circle.

The full circle represents 100 percent or 360 degrees.

Formula:

```text
Sector angle = (Value of component / Total value) x 360
```

Example:

If food expenditure is Rs 3,000 out of total expenditure Rs 10,000:

```text
Sector angle = (3000 / 10000) x 360 = 108 degrees
```

Use pie diagrams when the main purpose is to show composition.

---

## 11. Frequency Diagrams

Frequency diagrams represent grouped frequency distributions.

Common types:

1. Histogram
2. Frequency polygon
3. Frequency curve
4. Ogive

---

## 12. Histogram

A histogram is a two-dimensional diagram made of adjacent rectangles.

It is used for grouped frequency distributions, especially continuous data.

### Features

- Class intervals are shown on the x-axis.
- Frequencies are shown on the y-axis.
- Rectangles touch each other because the data is continuous.
- Area of each rectangle represents frequency.

### Histogram vs Bar Diagram

| Basis | Histogram | Bar diagram |
|---|---|---|
| Data type | Continuous frequency distribution | Discrete or categorical data |
| Gaps | No gaps between rectangles | Gaps between bars |
| Width | Based on class interval | Usually equal for visual comparison |
| Area meaning | Area represents frequency | Height represents value |

---

## 13. Frequency Polygon

A frequency polygon is formed by joining the midpoints of the tops of histogram rectangles.

Steps:

1. Draw a histogram.
2. Mark the midpoint of the top of each rectangle.
3. Join the midpoints with straight lines.
4. Extend both ends to the baseline using adjoining zero-frequency classes.

Use:

- Study the shape of a distribution.
- Compare two or more distributions on the same graph.

---

## 14. Frequency Curve

A frequency curve is a smooth curve drawn through points of a frequency distribution.

It is similar to a frequency polygon but smoother.

Use:

- Show the general pattern of data.
- Study distribution shape.

---

## 15. Ogive

An ogive is a cumulative frequency curve.

It shows cumulative frequencies rather than simple frequencies.

Types:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Less than ogive | Uses less than cumulative frequencies |
| More than ogive | Uses more than cumulative frequencies |

Use:

- Locate median graphically.
- Study cumulative pattern.
- Compare cumulative distributions.

---

## 16. Arithmetic Line Graph

An arithmetic line graph, also called a time series graph, shows values of a variable over time.

Rules:

- Time is shown on the x-axis.
- Value of variable is shown on the y-axis.
- Points are plotted and joined by line segments.

Examples:

- Imports and exports over years
- Population growth over time
- Monthly sales
- Annual production

Use:

Line graphs are especially useful for showing trends.

---

## 17. Choosing the Right Presentation Method

| Data situation | Best presentation |
|---|---|
| Very small data with explanation | Textual presentation |
| Exact values and comparison needed | Table |
| One variable across categories | Simple bar diagram |
| Two related series | Multiple bar diagram |
| Parts of a total | Component bar or pie diagram |
| Continuous grouped frequency distribution | Histogram |
| Shape of distribution | Frequency polygon or curve |
| Cumulative frequencies | Ogive |
| Values over time | Line graph |

---

## 18. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Using text for large data | Large data should usually be tabulated or diagrammed. |
| Forgetting units in a table | Units are necessary to interpret values. |
| Confusing caption and stub | Captions are column headings; stubs are row headings. |
| Drawing gaps in a histogram | Histogram rectangles touch because the data is continuous. |
| Using pie chart for unrelated values | Pie chart is for parts of a whole. |
| Treating diagrams as exact replacements for tables | Diagrams show patterns; tables show exact details better. |
| Plotting time on y-axis in a line graph | Time is usually placed on the x-axis. |

## Final Takeaway

Presentation of data makes collected and organised data understandable. Tables provide exact and systematic detail, while diagrams and graphs make comparison and patterns easier to see. A good learner chooses the presentation method according to the type of data and the purpose of analysis.
