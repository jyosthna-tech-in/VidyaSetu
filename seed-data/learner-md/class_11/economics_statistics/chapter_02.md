# Chapter 2: Collection of Data
### NCERT Class 11 Statistics for Economics

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how data is collected for economic study. Economics needs facts, and facts must be collected carefully. If the data is weak, biased, incomplete, or unsuitable, the analysis will also be weak.

The main idea is:

> Good economic analysis begins with good data collection.

This chapter teaches the sources of data, methods of collecting data, survey tools, census and sample surveys, sampling methods, sampling errors, and sources of secondary data.

## 1. Why Data Collection Matters

Data collection means gathering facts for a definite purpose.

In Economics, data is collected to:

- Understand a problem
- Explain causes
- Compare situations
- Test ideas
- Prepare policies
- Make decisions

Example:

If we want to study foodgrain production, we need data for different years. Only then can we say whether production increased, decreased, fluctuated, or remained stable.

Without data, statements remain unsupported opinions.

---

## 2. Variables and Observations

A variable is a characteristic whose value can change.

Examples:

- Income
- Price
- Production
- Population
- Marks
- Family size
- Expenditure

An observation is a particular value of a variable.

Example:

| Year | Foodgrain production |
|---|---|
| 2015-16 | 252 million tonnes |
| 2016-17 | 272 million tonnes |

Here:

- Year is one variable.
- Foodgrain production is another variable.
- Each value, such as 252 or 272 million tonnes, is an observation.

---

## 3. Meaning of Data

Data means facts collected for analysis.

Economic data may be about:

- Prices
- Output
- Income
- Employment
- Population
- Consumption
- Education
- Health
- Trade

Data helps transform a general problem into a measurable problem.

Example:

| General statement | Data-based statement |
|---|---|
| People are unemployed | Unemployment rate is 8 percent |
| Prices are rising | Price index increased by 6 percent |
| Production changed | Output increased from 100 units to 125 units |

---

## 4. Sources of Data

Statistical data can be obtained from two main sources:

1. Primary source
2. Secondary source

### 4.1 Primary Data

Primary data is data collected first-hand by the investigator for a specific purpose.

Examples:

- A student surveys classmates about study time.
- A researcher interviews households about monthly income.
- A business asks customers about product satisfaction.

Primary data is original because it is collected directly from the source.

### 4.2 Secondary Data

Secondary data is data already collected and processed by someone else.

Examples:

- Government reports
- Census reports
- Economic Survey
- Newspapers
- Books
- Research reports
- Official websites
- Reports of international organisations

Secondary data saves time and cost because the investigator does not collect it from the original respondents.

### 4.3 Primary vs Secondary Data

| Basis | Primary data | Secondary data |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Collected first-hand | Already collected by another agency |
| Originality | Original for the current study | Not original for the current user |
| Cost | Usually higher | Usually lower |
| Time | Takes more time | Saves time |
| Control | Investigator controls method | Investigator depends on existing data |
| Suitability | Can be designed for exact need | Must be checked for suitability |
| Example | Household survey by researcher | Census report used by researcher |

---

## 5. Survey Method

A survey is a method of collecting information from individuals.

Surveys are used when the investigator wants to know characteristics, opinions, conditions, or behaviour of a group.

Examples:

| Survey topic | Data collected |
|---|---|
| Popularity of a product | Customer opinion |
| Employment status | Whether people are employed or unemployed |
| Household income | Income range of families |
| School facilities | Availability of teachers, classrooms, books |

Survey data must be collected carefully because poor questions lead to poor answers.

---

## 6. Questionnaire and Interview Schedule

The most common survey instrument is a questionnaire or interview schedule.

### 6.1 Questionnaire

A questionnaire is a list of questions answered by respondents.

It may be:

- Self-administered, where respondents fill it themselves.
- Administered by an investigator, where questions are asked and answers recorded.

### 6.2 Interview Schedule

An interview schedule is a list of questions used by an investigator while interviewing respondents.

The investigator asks questions and records answers.

---

## 7. Features of a Good Questionnaire

A good questionnaire should be clear, short, unbiased, and arranged logically.

| Rule | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Keep it short | Too many questions may tire respondents |
| Use simple language | Questions should be easy to understand |
| Avoid ambiguity | A question should not have more than one meaning |
| Move from general to specific | This makes respondents comfortable |
| Avoid leading questions | Do not push respondents toward one answer |
| Avoid double negatives | They confuse respondents |
| Avoid personal questions at the beginning | Sensitive questions should come later if needed |
| Provide suitable answer choices | Options should match the question |

### Poor and Good Questions

| Poor question | Problem | Better question |
|---|---|---|
| Do you not disagree that prices should not be reduced? | Double negative | Should prices be reduced? |
| You like this product, don't you? | Leading question | Do you like this product? |
| Is your school good? | Too vague | How would you rate classroom facilities in your school? |
| What is your income and savings? | Two questions in one | What is your monthly income? What is your monthly saving? |

## 8. Modes of Data Collection

Three common modes are:

1. Personal interview
2. Mailing questionnaire
3. Telephone interview

### 8.1 Personal Interview

In a personal interview, the investigator meets the respondent and asks questions directly.

Advantages:

- Clarification is possible.
- Response rate is usually higher.
- Suitable for complex questions.
- Investigator can observe the respondent's situation.

Disadvantages:

- Costly.
- Time-consuming.
- Investigator bias may enter.
- Not suitable for very large or scattered populations unless resources are available.

Suitable when:

- Respondents need explanation.
- Data is detailed.
- Accuracy is important.

### 8.2 Mailing Questionnaire

In this method, questionnaires are sent to respondents by post or similar means, and respondents fill and return them.

Advantages:

- Less costly for scattered respondents.
- Respondents can answer at their convenience.
- Investigator bias is reduced.

Disadvantages:

- Low response rate.
- Respondents may misunderstand questions.
- Incomplete questionnaires may be returned.
- Only suitable for literate respondents.

Suitable when:

- Respondents are educated.
- Questions are simple.
- Respondents are geographically scattered.

### 8.3 Telephone Interview

In a telephone interview, the investigator asks questions over the phone.

Advantages:

- Faster than personal interview.
- Cheaper than field visits.
- Clarification is possible.
- Useful when respondents are far away.

Disadvantages:

- Limited to people with phone access.
- Not suitable for long or complex schedules.
- Respondents may not give enough time.
- Visual observation is not possible.

Suitable when:

- Questions are short.
- Quick data is needed.
- Respondents can be contacted by phone.

### Comparison

| Basis | Personal interview | Mailing questionnaire | Telephone interview |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | High | Low to moderate | Moderate |
| Time | More | More waiting time | Less |
| Clarification | High | Low | Moderate |
| Response rate | Usually high | Often low | Moderate |
| Suitable for complex questions | Yes | No | Limited |
| Investigator bias | Possible | Low | Possible |

## 9. Census and Sample Surveys

### 9.1 Census or Complete Enumeration

A census survey studies every unit of the population.

Example:

If every household in a country is studied, it is a census.

Features:

- Covers all units.
- Provides detailed information.
- Usually costly and time-consuming.
- Requires large organisation and resources.

Suitable when:

- Population is small, or
- Very high accuracy is needed, or
- Government-level complete information is required.

### 9.2 Sample Survey

A sample survey studies only a part of the population.

Example:

Instead of surveying all households in a city, 1,000 selected households may be surveyed.

Features:

- Less costly.
- Less time-consuming.
- Easier to manage.
- Can provide reliable results if the sample is representative.

Suitable when:

- Population is large.
- Time and cost are limited.
- Complete enumeration is not possible.

### Census vs Sample Survey

| Basis | Census | Sample survey |
|---|---|---|
| Coverage | Every unit | Selected units |
| Cost | High | Lower |
| Time | More | Less |
| Organisation | Difficult | Easier |
| Accuracy | Can be high if properly conducted | Depends on sample design |
| Suitability | Small or very important complete study | Large population and limited resources |

## 10. Population and Sample

### 10.1 Population or Universe

Population means the totality of items or individuals under study.

Examples:

| Study | Population |
|---|---|
| Income of families in a village | All families in that village |
| Marks of Class 11 students in a school | All Class 11 students in that school |
| Employment status of youth in a city | All youth in that city |

Population does not always mean human population. It means all units relevant to the study.

### 10.2 Sample

A sample is a smaller group selected from the population.

Example:

If there are 5,000 households in a town and 500 are selected for study, those 500 households form the sample.

### 10.3 Representative Sample

A representative sample reflects the main characteristics of the population.

Example:

If a town has rich, middle-income, and poor households, a good sample should include households from all relevant groups in suitable proportion.

A bad sample gives misleading results even if calculations are correct.

---

## 11. Sampling

Sampling means selecting a part of the population for study.

The purpose of sampling is to estimate population characteristics using a smaller group.

Two main types:

1. Random sampling
2. Non-random sampling

---

## 12. Random Sampling

Random sampling gives each unit of the population a known or equal chance of selection.

### Simple Random Sampling

In simple random sampling, every unit has an equal chance of being selected.

Example:

If every student's name is written on identical slips and slips are drawn without bias, it is simple random sampling.

Advantages:

- Reduces personal bias.
- Results can be more reliable.
- Suitable for statistical estimation.

Limitations:

- Requires a complete list of population units.
- May be difficult for very large or scattered populations.

---

## 13. Non-Random Sampling

In non-random sampling, selection is not based on random chance. The investigator may choose units according to convenience, judgement, or purpose.

Examples:

- Interviewing people who are easily available.
- Selecting respondents considered typical by the investigator.
- Choosing a specific group for a special study.

Advantages:

- Easier and cheaper.
- Useful for exploratory studies.

Limitations:

- More chance of bias.
- Results may not represent the population accurately.

---

## 14. Sampling Error and Non-Sampling Error

### 14.1 Sampling Error

Sampling error is the difference between the sample estimate and the actual population value.

It occurs because only a part of the population is studied.

Example:

If the actual average income of all households is Rs 25,000 but the sample average is Rs 24,200, the difference is a sampling error.

Sampling error can be reduced by:

- Increasing sample size
- Using proper random sampling
- Ensuring representativeness

### 14.2 Non-Sampling Error

Non-sampling errors can occur in both census and sample surveys.

Examples:

- Wrong answers by respondents
- Incorrect recording by investigator
- Poor questionnaire
- Non-response
- Data entry mistakes
- Misinterpretation of questions
- Deliberate false information

Non-sampling errors can be more serious than sampling errors because they arise from poor method, carelessness, or bias.

## 15. Sources of Secondary Data

Secondary data may be collected from:

| Source | Examples |
|---|---|
| Government publications | Census reports, Economic Survey, statistical abstracts |
| Semi-government bodies | Boards, commissions, public institutions |
| International organisations | World Bank, IMF, United Nations reports |
| Research institutions | University and institute reports |
| Newspapers and journals | Economic articles and data reports |
| Books | Published studies and reference books |
| Websites | Official data portals and institutional databases |

### Precautions Before Using Secondary Data

Before using secondary data, check:

1. Who collected it?
2. Why was it collected?
3. What method was used?
4. Is it reliable?
5. Is it recent enough?
6. Does it suit the present study?
7. Are definitions and units clear?

Secondary data is convenient, but it must not be used blindly.

---

## 16. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating all collected data as primary | Data is primary only for the agency that collects it first-hand. |
| Assuming secondary data is always reliable | It must be checked for source, method, and suitability. |
| Confusing population with sample | Population is the whole group; sample is selected part of it. |
| Thinking census is always best | Census may be too costly and time-consuming. |
| Thinking sample means careless study | A well-designed sample can be reliable. |
| Ignoring questionnaire design | Poor questions produce poor data. |
| Confusing sampling and non-sampling error | Sampling error arises from studying a sample; non-sampling error arises from mistakes and bias. |
