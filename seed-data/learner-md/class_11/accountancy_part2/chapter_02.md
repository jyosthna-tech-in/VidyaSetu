# Chapter 2: Financial Statements - II
### NCERT Class 11 - Accountancy Part 2

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter explains how to prepare final accounts when adjustments are given.

In the previous chapter, final accounts were prepared from a simple trial balance. In real business, some items are not fully recorded by the end of the year. Some expenses may be unpaid, some expenses may be paid in advance, some incomes may be earned but not received, and some incomes may be received before they are earned.

Because accounting follows the accrual basis, all these items must be adjusted before finding profit and preparing the balance sheet.

| Area | Main Skill |
|---|---|
| Accrual basis | Recording income when earned and expense when incurred |
| Adjusting entries | Recording missing or correcting entries at year end |
| Closing stock | Showing unsold goods in trading account and balance sheet |
| Outstanding expenses | Adding unpaid expenses and creating liability |
| Prepaid expenses | Deducting unexpired expenses and creating asset |
| Accrued income | Adding earned income not yet received and creating asset |
| Income received in advance | Deducting unearned income and creating liability |
| Depreciation | Charging asset use to profit and reducing asset value |
| Further bad debts | Writing off additional irrecoverable debtors |
| Provision for doubtful debts | Estimating possible debtor loss |
| Provision for discount on debtors | Estimating discount likely to be allowed to debtors |
| Manager's commission | Calculating commission before or after charging commission |
| Interest on capital | Charging business for use of proprietor's capital |
| Interest on drawings | Charging proprietor for personal withdrawals |
| Final accounts with adjustments | Showing every adjustment in two places |

# Unit 2A: Need for Adjustments

## The Main Problem

Final accounts should show:

1. true profit or loss,
2. true financial position.

This is possible only when all income and expenses relating to the current year are recorded correctly.

Cash paid and cash received are not enough.

## Accrual Basis

Under accrual basis:

1. income is recorded when earned, not necessarily when received,
2. expense is recorded when incurred, not necessarily when paid.

Example:

Salary for March 2017 is paid on April 7, 2017. The salary belongs to the year ending March 31, 2017, even though payment is made later. It must be charged in the year ending March 31, 2017.

## Why Adjustments Are Necessary

Adjustments are necessary because:

1. some expenses of the current year remain unpaid,
2. some expenses paid this year belong to next year,
3. some income earned this year is not received,
4. some income received this year belongs to next year,
5. some losses are discovered at year end,
6. some asset values must be reduced through depreciation,
7. some debtors may become doubtful,
8. some items are wrongly recorded and need correction.

## Every Adjustment Has Two Effects

Every adjustment must appear in two places.

This is the most important rule of this chapter.

| Adjustment Type | First Effect | Second Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Outstanding expense | Add to expense | Show liability |
| Prepaid expense | Deduct from expense | Show asset |
| Accrued income | Add to income | Show asset |
| Income received in advance | Deduct from income | Show liability |
| Depreciation | Debit profit and loss account | Deduct from asset |
| Further bad debts | Add to bad debts | Deduct from debtors |
| Provision for doubtful debts | Debit profit and loss account | Deduct from debtors |
| Closing stock | Credit trading account | Show asset |

## List of Common Adjustments

1. closing stock,
2. outstanding expenses,
3. prepaid expenses,
4. accrued income,
5. income received in advance,
6. depreciation,
7. bad debts,
8. provision for doubtful debts,
9. provision for discount on debtors,
10. manager's commission,
11. interest on capital,
12. interest on drawings,
13. goods withdrawn by proprietor,
14. capital expenditure wrongly debited to expense,
15. goods sent on sale or return basis.

# Unit 2B: Closing Stock

## Meaning

Closing stock is the cost of unsold goods lying with the business at the end of the accounting period.

It is an asset because it will benefit the next accounting period.

## When Closing Stock Is Given Outside Trial Balance

If closing stock is given as an adjustment outside the trial balance, it must be shown in two places:

1. credit side of trading account,
2. asset side of balance sheet.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Closing Stock A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Trading A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Trading account | Credit side |
| Balance sheet | Asset side |

## Example

Closing stock = Rs. 15,000

Entry:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Closing Stock A/c Dr. | 15,000 |  |
| To Trading A/c |  | 15,000 |

Trading account:

Closing stock Rs. 15,000 appears on credit side.

Balance sheet:

Closing stock Rs. 15,000 appears as current asset.

## When Closing Stock Is Given Inside Trial Balance

If closing stock appears in the trial balance, it has already been recorded in the books.

Therefore:

1. do not credit it again to trading account,
2. show it only on the asset side of balance sheet.

## Adjusted Purchases Method

Sometimes opening stock and closing stock are adjusted through purchases.

Entries:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Closing Stock A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Purchases A/c |  | xxx |
| Purchases A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Opening Stock A/c |  | xxx |

In this case:

1. adjusted purchases are debited to trading account,
2. closing stock is shown only in balance sheet,
3. opening stock is not shown separately in trading account.

# Unit 2C: Outstanding Expenses

## Meaning

Outstanding expense is an expense that belongs to the current accounting year but has not been paid by the end of the year.

Examples:

1. outstanding wages,
2. outstanding salaries,
3. outstanding rent,
4. outstanding interest on loan,
5. outstanding taxes,
6. unpaid stationery bill.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Concerned Expense A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Outstanding Expense A/c |  | xxx |

Example:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Wages A/c Dr. | 500 |  |
| To Outstanding Wages A/c |  | 500 |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Trading account or profit and loss account | Add to concerned expense |
| Balance sheet | Show as current liability |

## Example

Wages in trial balance = Rs. 8,000

Outstanding wages = Rs. 500

Correct wages expense:

`Rs. 8,000 + Rs. 500 = Rs. 8,500`

Effect:

1. wages in trading account = Rs. 8,500,
2. outstanding wages in balance sheet = Rs. 500 liability,
3. profit decreases by Rs. 500.

## Important Point

Outstanding expenses are added to expenses because they are incurred in the current year.

# Unit 2D: Prepaid Expenses

## Meaning

Prepaid expense is an expense paid in the current year, but the benefit belongs to the next year.

It is also called unexpired expense.

Examples:

1. prepaid insurance,
2. prepaid rent,
3. advance salary,
4. prepaid taxes,
5. prepaid wages.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Prepaid Expense A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Concerned Expense A/c |  | xxx |

Example:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Prepaid Salary A/c Dr. | 5,000 |  |
| To Salary A/c |  | 5,000 |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Trading account or profit and loss account | Deduct from concerned expense |
| Balance sheet | Show as current asset |

## Example

Salary in trial balance = Rs. 25,000

Prepaid salary = Rs. 5,000

Correct salary expense:

`Rs. 25,000 - Rs. 5,000 = Rs. 20,000`

Effect:

1. salary in profit and loss account = Rs. 20,000,
2. prepaid salary in balance sheet = Rs. 5,000 asset,
3. profit increases by Rs. 5,000 compared with charging full salary.

## Insurance Example

Insurance paid = Rs. 1,200 on July 1 for 12 months.

Accounting year ends on March 31.

Months used in current year = July to March = 9 months.

Months prepaid = April to June = 3 months.

Current-year insurance:

`Rs. 1,200 x 9 / 12 = Rs. 900`

Prepaid insurance:

`Rs. 1,200 x 3 / 12 = Rs. 300`

# Unit 2F: Income Received in Advance

## Meaning

Income received in advance is income received in the current year but not earned in the current year.

It is also called unearned income.

Examples:

1. rent received in advance,
2. commission received in advance,
3. apprenticeship premium received in advance,
4. subscription received in advance, where applicable.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Concerned Income A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Income Received in Advance A/c |  | xxx |

Example:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Rent Received A/c Dr. | 3,000 |  |
| To Rent Received in Advance A/c |  | 3,000 |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Deduct from concerned income |
| Balance sheet | Show as current liability |

## Example

Rent received = Rs. 10,000

Rent received in advance = Rs. 3,000

Correct rent income:

`Rs. 10,000 - Rs. 3,000 = Rs. 7,000`

Balance sheet liability:

`Rent received in advance = Rs. 3,000`

## Important Point

Income received in advance is a liability because the business has received money for service or benefit not yet provided.

# Unit 2H: Further Bad Debts

## Meaning

Bad debts appearing in the trial balance are already recorded.

Further bad debts are additional bad debts discovered after trial balance.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Bad Debts A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Sundry Debtors A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Add to bad debts |
| Balance sheet | Deduct from debtors |

## Example

Debtors = Rs. 15,500

Bad debts in trial balance = Rs. 4,500

Further bad debts = Rs. 2,500

Profit and loss account:

`Bad debts = Rs. 4,500 + Rs. 2,500 = Rs. 7,000`

Balance sheet:

`Debtors = Rs. 15,500 - Rs. 2,500 = Rs. 13,000`

## Important Point

Provision for doubtful debts is calculated after deducting further bad debts from debtors.

# Unit 2I: Provision for Doubtful Debts

## Meaning

Provision for doubtful debts is an estimate of possible loss from debtors who may not pay.

It is created under prudence. Expected loss should be recognised even before it becomes certain.

## Steps to Calculate

1. Start with debtors.
2. Deduct further bad debts.
3. Calculate required provision on the remaining debtors.
4. Compare required provision with existing provision, if any.
5. Transfer only the increase or decrease to profit and loss account.

## Journal Entry When New Provision Is Created or Increased

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c |  | xxx |

## Journal Entry When Existing Provision Is More Than Required

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Profit and Loss A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Debit increase in provision; credit decrease in provision |
| Balance sheet | Deduct required provision from debtors |

## Example 1: No Existing Provision

Debtors after further bad debts = Rs. 13,000

Provision required = 5%

Provision:

`Rs. 13,000 x 5% = Rs. 650`

Profit and loss account:

Debit provision for doubtful debts Rs. 650.

Balance sheet:

Deduct Rs. 650 from debtors.

## Example 2: Existing Provision

Book debts = Rs. 82,740

Existing reserve/provision for bad debts = Rs. 4,650

Required provision = 10% of book debts

Required provision:

`Rs. 82,740 x 10% = Rs. 8,274`

Increase required:

`Rs. 8,274 - Rs. 4,650 = Rs. 3,624`

If bad debts in trial balance are Rs. 1,250, total profit and loss debit for bad debts and provision would be:

`Bad debts Rs. 1,250 + increase in provision Rs. 3,624 = Rs. 4,874`

Balance sheet:

Deduct full required provision Rs. 8,274 from book debts.

## Example 3: Existing Provision More Than Required

Existing provision = Rs. 5,000

Required provision = Rs. 2,400

Excess provision:

`Rs. 5,000 - Rs. 2,400 = Rs. 2,600`

Profit and loss account is credited by Rs. 2,600.

# Unit 2J: Provision for Discount on Debtors

## Meaning

Businesses sometimes allow cash discount to debtors for prompt payment.

If such discount is expected, provision for discount on debtors may be created.

## Correct Base for Calculation

Provision for discount on debtors is calculated after deducting:

1. further bad debts,
2. provision for doubtful debts.

Reason:

Discount will be allowed only to debtors who are expected to pay.

## Formula

`Provision for discount = (Debtors - Further bad debts - Provision for doubtful debts) x Discount rate`

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Provision for Discount on Debtors A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Debit provision for discount on debtors |
| Balance sheet | Deduct from debtors after provision for doubtful debts |

## Example

Debtors = Rs. 15,500

Further bad debts = Rs. 2,500

Provision for doubtful debts = Rs. 650

Provision for discount on debtors = 2%

Calculation:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Debtors | 15,500 |
| Less: Further bad debts | 2,500 |
|  | 13,000 |
| Less: Provision for doubtful debts | 650 |
| Debtors expected to pay | 12,350 |
| Provision for discount at 2% | 247 |

If the raw question uses 1.84% or a rounded amount, it may show a different rupee figure. The method remains the same: calculate discount only on good debtors expected to pay.

## Balance Sheet Extract

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Debtors | 15,500 |
| Less: Further bad debts | 2,500 |
|  | 13,000 |
| Less: Provision for doubtful debts | 650 |
|  | 12,350 |
| Less: Provision for discount on debtors | 247 |
| Net debtors | 12,103 |

# Unit 2K: Manager's Commission

## Meaning

A manager may be entitled to commission based on profit.

The commission may be calculated:

1. on profit before charging commission,
2. on profit after charging commission.

The question must be read carefully.

## Case 1: Commission on Profit Before Charging Commission

Formula:

`Commission = Profit before commission x Rate / 100`

Example:

Profit before commission = Rs. 20,623

Commission = 10% before charging commission

`Commission = Rs. 20,623 x 10 / 100 = Rs. 2,062.30`

Rounded commission = Rs. 2,062

Net profit after commission:

`Rs. 20,623 - Rs. 2,062 = Rs. 18,561`

## Case 2: Commission on Profit After Charging Commission

Formula:

`Commission = Profit before commission x Rate / (100 + Rate)`

Example:

Profit before commission = Rs. 20,623

Commission = 10% after charging commission

`Commission = Rs. 20,623 x 10 / 110 = Rs. 1,875`

Net profit after commission:

`Rs. 20,623 - Rs. 1,875 = Rs. 18,748`

## Journal Entries

For commission payable:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Manager's Commission A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Outstanding Manager's Commission A/c |  | xxx |

For transfer to profit and loss account:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Manager's Commission A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Debit manager's commission |
| Balance sheet | Show outstanding commission as liability |

## If the Question Is Silent

If the question only says "manager is entitled to commission on net profit" and does not say before or after charging commission, the usual textbook assumption is before charging such commission.

# Unit 2L: Interest on Capital

## Meaning

Interest on capital is allowed to proprietor for capital invested in the business.

From business point of view, it is an expense.

From proprietor point of view, it increases capital.

## Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Interest on Capital A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Capital A/c |  | xxx |

## Final Account Treatment

| Account | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Profit and loss account | Debit interest on capital |
| Balance sheet | Add to capital |

## Example

Capital at beginning = Rs. 12,000

Interest on capital = 5%

Interest:

`Rs. 12,000 x 5% = Rs. 600`

Profit decreases by Rs. 600.

Capital increases by Rs. 600.

## Additional Capital Introduced During the Year

If additional capital is introduced during the year, interest is calculated for the period it remained in business.

Example:

Opening capital = Rs. 50,000

Additional capital introduced on January 1 = Rs. 10,000

Year ends March 31.

Rate = 10% p.a.

Interest on opening capital:

`Rs. 50,000 x 10% = Rs. 5,000`

Interest on additional capital for 3 months:

`Rs. 10,000 x 10% x 3 / 12 = Rs. 250`

Total interest on capital:

`Rs. 5,000 + Rs. 250 = Rs. 5,250`

# Unit 2N: Capital Items Hidden Inside Expenses

## Meaning

Sometimes an expense account includes spending that is actually capital expenditure.

Examples:

1. wages include amount spent on installing machinery,
2. advertising includes cost of signboard,
3. repairs include major improvement,
4. purchases include machinery or furniture bought for use,
5. wages include cost of making furniture.

Such items must be removed from expense and added to asset.

## General Journal Entry

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Asset A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Expense A/c |  | xxx |

## Example: Wages for Erection of Machinery

Wages include Rs. 4,000 for erection of new machinery.

Entry:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Machinery A/c Dr. | 4,000 |  |
| To Wages A/c |  | 4,000 |

Final account treatment:

1. deduct Rs. 4,000 from wages,
2. add Rs. 4,000 to machinery,
3. charge depreciation on the corrected machinery value if required.

## Example: Furniture Bought Recorded as Purchases

Furniture costing Rs. 7,000 was entered in purchases book.

Entry:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Furniture A/c Dr. | 7,000 |  |
| To Purchases A/c |  | 7,000 |

Final account treatment:

1. deduct Rs. 7,000 from purchases,
2. show furniture as asset,
3. depreciate furniture if required.

# Unit 2P: Goods Sent on Sale or Return Basis

## Meaning

Goods may be sent to a customer on approval. If the customer has not approved the goods by year end, the transaction is not a final sale.

If it was wrongly recorded as sale, correction is required.

## Treatment When Recorded as Sale

If goods costing Rs. 1,200 were sent on sale or return basis for Rs. 1,400 and recorded as actual sale:

1. reduce sales by Rs. 1,400,
2. reduce debtors by Rs. 1,400,
3. include goods at cost Rs. 1,200 in closing stock.

## Correction Entry for Sale Value

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Sales A/c Dr. | 1,400 |  |
| To Debtors A/c |  | 1,400 |

## Stock Treatment

Closing stock should include Rs. 1,200 because goods still belong to the business.

# Unit 2U: Worked Example - Manager's Commission After Charging Commission

## Problem

Profit before manager's commission = Rs. 21,204.

Manager is entitled to 5% of net profit after charging commission.

## Solution

Use:

`Commission = Profit before commission x Rate / (100 + Rate)`

Calculation:

`Commission = Rs. 21,204 x 5 / 105 = Rs. 1,010`

Net profit:

`Rs. 21,204 - Rs. 1,010 = Rs. 20,194`

Final account treatment:

1. debit manager's commission Rs. 1,010 to profit and loss account,
2. show manager's commission outstanding Rs. 1,010 as liability,
3. transfer net profit Rs. 20,194 to capital.

An item inside trial balance has already passed through double entry.

An item outside trial balance is additional information and must usually be recorded in two places.

# Unit 2X: Short Answer Guide

## 1. Why Are Adjusting Entries Necessary?

Adjusting entries are necessary because final accounts are prepared on accrual basis. They ensure that all incomes earned and all expenses incurred during the year are included, whether cash has been received or paid or not.

Without adjusting entries, profit or loss and financial position will not be true and fair.

## 2. What Is Closing Stock?

Closing stock is unsold goods at the end of the accounting period.

If given outside trial balance:

1. credit it to trading account,
2. show it as asset in balance sheet.

Entry:

`Closing Stock A/c Dr. To Trading A/c`

If given inside trial balance, show it only in balance sheet.

## 3. Meanings of Four Accrual Adjustments

Outstanding expense:

Expense incurred but not paid.

Prepaid expense:

Expense paid but not yet expired.

Income received in advance:

Income received but not yet earned.

Accrued income:

Income earned but not yet received.

## 4. Why Create Provision for Doubtful Debts?

Provision for doubtful debts is created because some debtors may not pay. It follows prudence and matching. It prevents overstatement of profit and debtors.

## 5. What Is Provision for Discount on Debtors?

Provision for discount on debtors is an estimated amount of discount likely to be allowed to debtors for prompt payment.

It is calculated on debtors expected to pay, after deducting further bad debts and provision for doubtful debts.

## 6. Adjustment Entries

Depreciation:

`Depreciation A/c Dr. To Asset A/c`

Discount on debtors:

`Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Provision for Discount on Debtors A/c`

Interest on capital:

`Interest on Capital A/c Dr. To Capital A/c`

Manager's commission:

`Manager's Commission A/c Dr. To Outstanding Manager's Commission A/c`

and

`Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Manager's Commission A/c`

# Unit 2Y: Long Answer Guide

## 1. What Are Adjusting Entries?

Adjusting entries are journal entries passed at the end of an accounting period to record items required under accrual accounting.

They are necessary because all incomes and expenses must be recognised in the period to which they relate. Examples include outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued income, income received in advance, depreciation, further bad debts, provisions, manager's commission, and interest on capital.

Each adjustment has two effects in final accounts. For example, outstanding salary is added to salary in profit and loss account and shown as liability in balance sheet.

## 2. Provision for Doubtful Debts

Provision for doubtful debts is an estimated amount set aside for debtors who may not pay.

Steps:

1. write off further bad debts,
2. calculate required provision on remaining debtors,
3. compare with existing provision,
4. debit profit and loss account with increase or credit it with excess,
5. deduct full required provision from debtors in balance sheet.

Entries:

| Situation | Entry |
|---|---|
| Further bad debts | Bad Debts A/c Dr. To Debtors A/c |
| Create or increase provision | Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c |
| Reduce excess provision | Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c Dr. To Profit and Loss A/c |

## 3. Treatment of Prepaid Expenses, Depreciation, and Closing Stock

Prepaid expense:

If outside trial balance, deduct from expense and show as asset. If inside trial balance, show as asset only.

Depreciation:

If outside trial balance, debit profit and loss account and deduct from asset. If depreciation is already in trial balance, debit it to profit and loss account only; the asset may already be adjusted depending on how the books were kept.

Closing stock:

If outside trial balance, credit trading account and show as asset. If inside trial balance, show as asset only.
