## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter begins the preparation of final accounts for a sole proprietorship.

After journal, ledger, and trial balance, the accountant prepares financial statements. These statements show two things:

1. financial performance during a period,
2. financial position on a particular date.

The chapter also explains the difference between capital and revenue items, because final accounts become wrong if this difference is ignored.

# Unit 1A: Accounting Process up to Financial Statements

## The Sequence

Financial statements are not prepared directly from memory. They are the result of a sequence.

| Stage | Work Done |
|---|---|
| 1. Identify transactions | Select events measurable in money |
| 2. Record in books | Enter transactions in journal or subsidiary books |
| 3. Post to ledger | Transfer entries to individual accounts |
| 4. Balance accounts | Find debit or credit balance of each account |
| 5. Prepare trial balance | List balances and check debit-credit equality |
| 6. Prepare final accounts | Prepare trading account, profit and loss account, and balance sheet |

## Trial Balance as the Starting Point

Trial balance contains:

1. assets,
2. liabilities,
3. capital,
4. expenses,
5. losses,
6. incomes,
7. gains.

Final accounts sort these balances into performance items and position items.

| Trial Balance Item | Goes To |
|---|---|
| Expenses and losses | Trading account or profit and loss account |
| Incomes and gains | Trading account or profit and loss account |
| Assets | Balance sheet |
| Liabilities | Balance sheet |
| Capital | Balance sheet |
| Drawings | Deducted from capital in balance sheet |

## Final Accounts of a Sole Proprietor

The usual final accounts are:

1. trading account,
2. profit and loss account,
3. balance sheet.

Trading account and profit and loss account show performance for a period.

Balance sheet shows position on a particular date.

# Unit 1B: Stakeholders and Their Information Needs

## Meaning of Stakeholder

A stakeholder is any person or group connected with the business and interested in its accounting information.

Stakeholders may be:

1. internal,
2. external.

They may also have:

1. monetary interest,
2. non-monetary interest,
3. direct interest,
4. indirect interest.

## Internal Users

Internal users are inside the business.

| User | Main Interest | Information Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Owner | Profit, capital growth, safety of investment | Profit earned, assets, liabilities, capital position |
| Manager | Planning, control, career performance | Profitability, liquidity, expenses, efficiency, financial position |
| Employees | Job security, salary, bonus, growth | Profitability, stability, future prospects |

## External Users

External users are outside the business.

| User | Main Interest | Information Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Government | Regulation and taxation | Profit, tax liability, compliance information |
| Bank | Loan safety and interest recovery | Profitability, liquidity, asset structure, debt-paying ability |
| Prospective owner | Future investment | Past profit, financial position, risk, expected return |
| Creditors | Payment of dues | Short-term liquidity and payment record |
| Customers | Continuity of supply | Stability and long-term survival |
| Researchers | Study and analysis | Financial trends and business patterns |

## Why One Set of Statements Is Prepared

Every stakeholder wants different details. Preparing a separate report for every user is not practical.

Therefore, the business prepares general-purpose financial statements. These statements give a common base of information to most users.

# Unit 1C: Capital and Revenue Classification

## Why This Classification Matters

Final accounts depend on correct classification.

Capital items go mainly to the balance sheet.

Revenue items go mainly to trading account or profit and loss account.

If a revenue expense is treated as capital, profit is overstated.

If a capital expenditure is treated as revenue, profit is understated and assets are understated.

## Meaning of Expenditure

Expenditure means payment or incurrence of an outlay for a business purpose, other than settling an already existing liability.

An expenditure may give benefit:

1. for one accounting period, or
2. for more than one accounting period.

This difference creates revenue expenditure and capital expenditure.

# Unit 1D: Capital Expenditure

## Meaning

Capital expenditure is expenditure whose benefit extends beyond one accounting period.

It usually:

1. acquires a fixed asset,
2. improves an existing fixed asset,
3. increases earning capacity,
4. extends useful life,
5. gives long-term benefit.

## Examples

1. purchase of machinery,
2. purchase of furniture,
3. purchase of building,
4. installation cost of machinery,
5. registration fee for purchase of building,
6. major improvement to a building,
7. platform constructed for installing a machine,
8. expenditure on making an old building usable at the time of purchase.

## Accounting Treatment

Capital expenditure is not fully charged to profit and loss account in the year of payment.

It is shown as an asset in the balance sheet.

If the asset loses value over time, depreciation is charged each year.

Example:

Furniture costing Rs. 50,000 is expected to be useful for 5 years.

If straight line depreciation is used with no residual value:

`Yearly depreciation = Rs. 50,000 / 5 = Rs. 10,000`

Only Rs. 10,000 is treated as expense each year, not the whole Rs. 50,000 in the first year.

# Unit 1E: Revenue Expenditure

## Meaning

Revenue expenditure is expenditure whose benefit is normally exhausted within the current accounting period.

It is usually incurred for:

1. day-to-day business operations,
2. maintaining earning capacity,
3. administration,
4. selling,
5. distribution,
6. routine repairs.

## Examples

1. salaries,
2. wages,
3. rent,
4. office expenses,
5. carriage outward,
6. advertising for current period,
7. routine repairs,
8. postage,
9. stationary,
10. insurance for current period.

## Accounting Treatment

Revenue expenditure is charged to trading account or profit and loss account.

Example:

Salary for the year is Rs. 25,000. It benefits the current year because employees worked during this year. It is debited to profit and loss account.

# Unit 1F: Deferred Revenue Expenditure

## Meaning

Deferred revenue expenditure is revenue in nature, but its benefit extends over more than one accounting period.

It is not a normal fixed asset, but it is also not fair to charge the full amount to one year.

## Examples

1. heavy advertising campaign whose benefit lasts several years,
2. preliminary expenses, where applicable,
3. large special repair campaign with future benefit,
4. major sales promotion expected to benefit future periods.

## Accounting Treatment

It is written off over the expected period of benefit.

Example:

Advertising expenditure of Rs. 1,00,000 is expected to benefit 4 years.

Amount written off each year:

`Rs. 1,00,000 / 4 = Rs. 25,000`

## Important Caution

Not every large revenue expense becomes deferred revenue expenditure.

The key test is future benefit.

# Unit 1G: Capital and Revenue Expenditure Compared

| Basis | Capital Expenditure | Revenue Expenditure |
|---|---|---|
| Benefit period | More than one accounting period | Usually one accounting period |
| Purpose | Acquire, improve, or extend asset | Run day-to-day business |
| Earning capacity | Increases earning capacity | Maintains earning capacity |
| Recurrence | Usually non-recurring | Usually recurring |
| Final account treatment | Balance sheet, subject to depreciation | Trading account or profit and loss account |
| Examples | Machinery, building improvement | Salary, rent, routine repair |

## Classification Drill

| Item | Classification | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Repairs and whitewashing at time of purchase of old building to make it usable | Capital expenditure | Necessary to bring building into usable condition |
| Extra exit in cinema hall due to government order | Capital expenditure | Improves building and gives long-term benefit |
| Registration fee paid on purchase of building | Capital expenditure | Directly related to acquiring building |
| Maintenance of tea garden that will produce tea after four years | Capital expenditure or deferred revenue treatment | Benefit relates to future earning period |
| Depreciation on plant | Revenue expenditure | Current-period expired cost of fixed asset |
| Platform erected for fixing machine | Capital expenditure | Necessary for installation and use of machine |
| Advertising benefit lasting four years | Deferred revenue expenditure | Revenue nature, but benefit extends beyond current year |

# Unit 1H: Capital and Revenue Receipts

## Capital Receipts

Capital receipts either:

1. create an obligation to return money, or
2. arise from sale of a fixed asset, or
3. increase capital structure.

Examples:

1. additional capital brought by owner,
2. bank loan,
3. sale of old machinery,
4. sale of furniture,
5. long-term borrowing.

## Revenue Receipts

Revenue receipts arise from normal business operations or regular income.

Examples:

1. sales,
2. commission received,
3. rent received,
4. interest received,
5. discount received,
6. dividend received.

## Comparison

| Basis | Capital Receipt | Revenue Receipt |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Non-operating capital inflow or liability-creating inflow | Income from operations or regular income |
| Obligation | May create obligation to repay | Usually no repayment obligation |
| Final account effect | Balance sheet, or capital profit treatment | Trading account or profit and loss account |
| Example | Loan from bank | Sales |

# Unit 1I: Effect of Wrong Classification

## Revenue Expense Treated as Capital

Example:

Revenue earned = Rs. 10,00,000

Expenses shown = Rs. 8,00,000

Profit shown = Rs. 2,00,000

Later, it is found that repairs of machinery Rs. 20,000 were wrongly added to machinery instead of being charged to repairs.

Correct expenses:

`Rs. 8,00,000 + Rs. 20,000 = Rs. 8,20,000`

Correct profit:

`Rs. 10,00,000 - Rs. 8,20,000 = Rs. 1,80,000`

Effect of error:

1. profit overstated by Rs. 20,000,
2. machinery overstated by Rs. 20,000,
3. balance sheet does not show correct asset value.

## Capital Expenditure Treated as Revenue

Example:

Furniture purchased is wrongly debited to purchases.

Effect:

1. expenses are overstated,
2. profit is understated,
3. assets are understated,
4. balance sheet total becomes wrong unless corrected.

## Tax Relevance

Capital profits and revenue profits may be treated differently under tax laws. Therefore, correct classification matters for both accounting and taxation.

# Unit 1J: Meaning of Financial Statements

## Definition

Financial statements are formal statements prepared to show the financial performance and financial position of a business.

For a sole proprietorship, the main statements are:

1. trading and profit and loss account,
2. balance sheet.

## Objectives

Financial statements are prepared:

1. to show true and fair financial performance,
2. to show true and fair financial position,
3. to calculate profit or loss,
4. to show assets and liabilities,
5. to help owners and managers make decisions,
6. to help lenders and creditors judge safety,
7. to support tax and legal compliance.

## Performance vs Position

| Statement | Shows | Period or Date |
|---|---|---|
| Trading account | Gross profit or gross loss | For a period |
| Profit and loss account | Net profit or net loss | For a period |
| Balance sheet | Assets, liabilities, capital | On a date |

# Unit 1L: Trading Account

## Meaning

Trading account is prepared to find gross profit or gross loss from the main trading or manufacturing operations of the business.

It focuses on:

1. opening stock,
2. purchases,
3. purchases returns,
4. direct expenses,
5. sales,
6. sales returns,
7. closing stock.

## Debit Side of Trading Account

Items usually debited:

1. opening stock,
2. purchases,
3. less purchases returns,
4. wages,
5. carriage inward,
6. freight inward,
7. fuel, power, gas, water used in production,
8. factory lighting,
9. factory rent,
10. dock and clearing charges,
11. customs duty,
12. royalty on production,
13. packaging material that forms part of goods.

## Credit Side of Trading Account

Items usually credited:

1. sales,
2. less sales returns,
3. closing stock,
4. gross loss, if debit side is more.

## Direct Expenses

Direct expenses are expenses directly related to:

1. manufacturing goods,
2. purchasing goods,
3. bringing goods to the place of business,
4. making goods ready for sale.

Examples:

1. wages of factory workers,
2. carriage inward,
3. freight inward,
4. customs duty on purchases,
5. factory power,
6. coal, gas, and water used in production.

## Trading Account Format

| Dr. Expenses/Losses | Rs. | Cr. Revenues/Gains | Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Opening stock | xxx | Sales | xxx |
| Purchases | xxx | Less: Sales return | (xxx) |
| Less: Purchases return | (xxx) | Net sales | xxx |
| Net purchases | xxx | Closing stock | xxx |
| Direct wages | xxx | Gross loss c/d | xxx |
| Carriage inward | xxx |  |  |
| Other direct expenses | xxx |  |  |
| Gross profit c/d | xxx |  |  |
| Total | xxx | Total | xxx |

## Gross Profit and Gross Loss

Gross profit means sales revenue exceeds cost of goods sold.

Gross loss means cost of goods sold exceeds sales revenue.

Formula:

`Gross profit = Net sales - Cost of goods sold`

or

`Gross profit = Sales - (Purchases + Direct expenses)` when there is no opening stock and no closing stock.

# Unit 1M: Profit and Loss Account

## Meaning

Profit and loss account is prepared after trading account.

It starts with gross profit or gross loss and then records indirect expenses, losses, incomes, and gains.

The final result is net profit or net loss.

3. rates and taxes,
4. interest paid,
5. commission paid,

8. depreciation,
9. carriage outward,
10. advertisement,
11. office expenses,
12. audit fees,
13. insurance,
14. discount allowed,
15. miscellaneous expenses,
16. loss by fire,
17. loss on sale of asset.

## Credit Side of Profit and Loss Account

Items usually credited:

1. gross profit brought down,
2. commission received,
3. interest received,
4. rent received,
5. discount received,
6. dividend received,

# Unit 1P: Cost of Goods Sold and Closing Stock

## Meaning of Cost of Goods Sold

Cost of goods sold means the cost of goods actually sold during the accounting period.

It is not always equal to purchases.

Why?

Because a business may have:

1. opening stock from last year,
2. purchases during current year,
3. direct expenses,
4. closing stock unsold at year end.

## Formula Without Opening or Closing Stock

`Cost of goods sold = Purchases + Direct expenses`

## Formula With Closing Stock

`Cost of goods sold = Purchases + Direct expenses - Closing stock`

## Formula With Opening and Closing Stock

`Cost of goods sold = Opening stock + Net purchases + Direct expenses - Closing stock`

## Net Purchases

`Net purchases = Purchases - Purchases return`

## Net Sales

`Net sales = Sales - Sales return`

## Closing Stock Entry

Closing stock is usually not in trial balance. It is brought into books by:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Closing Stock A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Trading A/c |  | xxx |

Closing stock appears:

1. on credit side of trading account,
2. on asset side of balance sheet.

## Example: Ankit With Closing Stock

Suppose Ankit has closing stock Rs. 15,000.

Cost of goods sold:

`Purchases Rs. 75,000 + Wages Rs. 8,000 - Closing stock Rs. 15,000 = Rs. 68,000`

Gross profit:

`Sales Rs. 1,25,000 - Cost of goods sold Rs. 68,000 = Rs. 57,000`

|---|---:|---|---:|
| Purchases | 75,000 | Sales | 1,25,000 |
| Wages | 8,000 | Closing stock | 15,000 |
| Gross profit c/d | 57,000 |  |  |
| Total | 1,40,000 | Total | 1,40,000 |

## Profit and Loss Account With Closing Stock

| Dr. Expenses/Losses | Rs. | Cr. Revenues/Gains | Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|

## Important Learning Point

Closing stock increases gross profit because goods not sold should not be treated as cost of goods sold.

# Unit 1R: Operating Profit

## Meaning

Operating profit is profit earned from normal business operations.

It excludes:

1. non-operating incomes,
2. non-operating expenses,
3. abnormal gains,
4. abnormal losses,
5. financial items such as interest, when profit before interest and tax is required.

Operating profit is often understood as EBIT:

Earnings before interest and tax.

## Formula

`Operating profit = Net profit + Non-operating expenses - Non-operating incomes`

Why add non-operating expenses?

Because they were deducted while finding net profit, but they do not belong to normal operations.

Why deduct non-operating incomes?

Because they were added while finding net profit, but they do not belong to normal operations.

## Examples of Operating Expenses

1. salaries,
2. rent,
3. office expenses,
4. advertisement,
5. depreciation on operating assets,
6. wages,
7. factory expenses,
8. selling expenses.

## Examples of Non-Operating Items

| Non-Operating Income | Non-Operating Expense |
|---|---|
| Dividend on investment | Loss on sale of old furniture |
| Profit on sale of investment | Interest on loan, when calculating EBIT |
| Rent from non-business property | Loss by fire |
| Interest on investment | Abnormal loss |

## Example

Net profit = Rs. 19,000

Non-operating expense = interest Rs. 500

Non-operating income = nil

Operating profit:

`Rs. 19,000 + Rs. 500 - nil = Rs. 19,500`

## Worked Example

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Sales | 75,250 |
| Sales return | 1,250 |
| Purchases | 32,250 |
| Purchases return | 250 |
| Opening stock | 7,600 |
| Closing stock | 8,000 |
| Wages | 2,600 |
| Operating indirect expenses | 8,600 |
| Loss on sale of old furniture | 300 |
| Profit on sale of investment | 500 |
| Dividend on investment | 2,500 |

Net sales:

`Rs. 75,250 - Rs. 1,250 = Rs. 74,000`

Net purchases:

`Rs. 32,250 - Rs. 250 = Rs. 32,000`

Cost of goods sold:

`Rs. 7,600 + Rs. 32,000 + Rs. 2,600 - Rs. 8,000 = Rs. 34,200`

Gross profit:

`Rs. 74,000 - Rs. 34,200 = Rs. 39,800`

Operating profit:

`Gross profit Rs. 39,800 - operating indirect expenses Rs. 8,600 = Rs. 31,200`

Net profit:

`Operating profit Rs. 31,200 - loss Rs. 300 + profit on investment Rs. 500 + dividend Rs. 2,500 = Rs. 33,900`

# Unit 1S: Balance Sheet

## Meaning

Balance sheet is a statement showing the financial position of a business on a particular date.

It shows:

1. assets,
2. liabilities,
3. capital.

## Why It Is Called a Balance Sheet

After nominal accounts are closed to trading account and profit and loss account, real and personal account balances remain.

These remaining balances are shown in a statement called balance sheet.

The balance sheet becomes the basis for opening the books of the next year.

## Characteristics

1. It is a statement, not an account.
2. It is prepared on a particular date.
3. It is prepared after trading account and profit and loss account.
4. It shows financial position.
5. Assets are shown on one side.
6. Liabilities and capital are shown on the other side.
7. Both sides must agree because of the accounting equation.

## Accounting Equation

`Assets = Liabilities + Capital`

or

`Capital = Assets - Liabilities`

## Balance Sheet Format

|---|---:|---|---:|
| Capital | xxx | Cash | xxx |
| Add: Net profit | xxx | Bank | xxx |
| Less: Drawings | (xxx) | Debtors | xxx |
| Long-term loan | xxx | Closing stock | xxx |
| Creditors | xxx | Furniture | xxx |
| Bills payable | xxx | Building | xxx |
| Bank overdraft | xxx | Goodwill | xxx |
| Total | xxx | Total | xxx |

# Unit 1T: Balance Sheet Items

## Current Assets

Current assets are assets expected to be converted into cash or used within one year.

Examples:

1. cash in hand,
2. cash at bank,
3. bills receivable,
4. sundry debtors,
5. closing stock,
6. short-term investments,
7. prepaid expenses.

## Current Liabilities

Current liabilities are liabilities expected to be paid within one year.

Examples:

1. creditors,
2. bills payable,
3. bank overdraft,
4. outstanding expenses,
5. short-term loans,
6. tax payable,
7. sales tax collected.

## Fixed Assets

Fixed assets are long-term assets used in business and not held for resale.

Examples:

1. land,
2. building,
3. plant and machinery,
4. furniture,
5. motor car,
6. equipment.

## Intangible Assets

Intangible assets cannot be physically touched but may have business value.

Examples:

1. goodwill,
2. patents,
3. trademarks,
4. copyright,
5. franchise rights.

## Investments

Investments are funds placed in securities, shares, bonds, or other investment assets.

They may be short-term or long-term depending on purpose and expected holding period.

## Long-Term Liabilities

Long-term liabilities are payable after more than one year.

Examples:

1. long-term bank loan,
2. mortgage loan,
3. debentures, where applicable.

## Capital

Capital is the owner's claim in the business.

It is affected by:

1. additional capital,
2. profit,
3. interest on capital,
4. drawings,
5. loss,
6. interest on drawings.

Drawings are amounts withdrawn by the owner for personal use.

They reduce capital.

Balance sheet treatment:

# Unit 1U: Marshalling of Assets and Liabilities

## Meaning

Marshalling means arranging assets and liabilities in a particular order in the balance sheet.

The two common orders are:

1. order of permanence,
2. order of liquidity.

## Order of Permanence

In order of permanence, the most permanent items are shown first.

Asset side:

1. fixed assets,
2. long-term investments,
3. current assets,
4. cash.

Liabilities side:

1. capital,
2. long-term liabilities,
3. current liabilities.

## Example: Order of Permanence

| Liabilities | Rs. | Assets | Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Capital | 16,500 | Furniture | 15,000 |
| Long-term loan | 5,000 | Debtors | 15,500 |
| Creditors | 15,000 | Bank | 5,000 |
|  |  | Cash | 1,000 |
| Total | 36,500 | Total | 36,500 |

## Order of Liquidity

In order of liquidity, items closest to cash or payment are shown first.

Asset side:

1. cash,
2. bank,
3. debtors,
4. stock,
5. fixed assets.

Liabilities side:

1. current liabilities,
2. long-term liabilities,
3. capital.

## Example: Order of Liquidity

| Liabilities | Rs. | Assets | Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Creditors | 15,000 | Cash | 1,000 |
| Long-term loan | 5,000 | Bank | 5,000 |
| Capital | 16,500 | Debtors | 15,500 |
|  |  | Furniture | 15,000 |
| Total | 36,500 | Total | 36,500 |

## Which Order Should Be Used?

For sole proprietorships, there is no single compulsory format in basic textbook problems unless the question specifies.

Use the order asked in the question.

If no order is given, present a clean and consistent balance sheet.

# Unit 1Z: Common Mistakes and Corrections

## Mistake 1: Treating Closing Stock as an Expense

Wrong:

Closing stock is debited to trading account.

Correct:

Closing stock is credited to trading account and shown as asset in balance sheet.

## Mistake 2: Putting Carriage Inward in Profit and Loss Account

Wrong:

Carriage inward is treated as indirect expense.

Correct:

Carriage inward relates to purchases and goes to trading account.

## Mistake 3: Putting Carriage Outward in Trading Account

Wrong:

Carriage outward is treated as direct expense.

Correct:

Carriage outward relates to selling and goes to profit and loss account.

## Mistake 4: Ignoring Purchases Return

Wrong:

Gross purchases are used as purchases.

Correct:

Net purchases = purchases - purchases return.

## Mistake 5: Ignoring Sales Return

Wrong:

Gross sales are used as sales.

Correct:

Net sales = sales - sales return.

## Mistake 6: Treating Drawings as Expense

Wrong:

Drawings are debited to profit and loss account.

Correct:

Drawings are deducted from capital in the balance sheet.

## Mistake 7: Treating Capital Receipts as Income

Wrong:

Loan received is credited to profit and loss account.

Correct:

Loan is liability and appears in balance sheet.

## Mistake 8: Treating Capital Expenditure as Purchase

Wrong:

Machinery bought for business use is debited to purchases.

Correct:

It is fixed asset and appears in balance sheet, subject to depreciation.

# Unit 1AB: Short Answer Guide

## 1. Objectives of Preparing Financial Statements

Financial statements are prepared to:

1. show true and fair financial performance,
2. show true and fair financial position,
3. calculate profit or loss,
4. show assets, liabilities, and capital,
5. help owners, managers, creditors, banks, government, and other users,
6. provide a base for planning and control.

## 2. Purpose of Trading and Profit and Loss Account

Trading account finds gross profit or gross loss from buying, manufacturing, and selling goods.

Profit and loss account finds net profit or net loss after indirect expenses and other incomes.

Together, they show the result of business operations for the accounting period.

## 3. Concept of Cost of Goods Sold

Cost of goods sold is the cost of goods actually sold during the year.

Formula:

`Cost of goods sold = Opening stock + Net purchases + Direct expenses - Closing stock`

It is deducted from net sales to find gross profit.

## 4. Meaning and Characteristics of Balance Sheet

Balance sheet is a statement of assets, liabilities, and capital on a particular date.

Characteristics:

1. it is a statement, not an account,
2. it is prepared on a specific date,
3. it is prepared after profit and loss account,
4. it shows financial position,
5. both sides are equal,
6. it follows the accounting equation.

## 5. Capital and Revenue Expenditure

Capital expenditure gives benefit for more than one accounting period and is shown in balance sheet.

Revenue expenditure benefits the current period and is charged to trading account or profit and loss account.

## 6. Operating Profit

Operating profit is profit earned from normal business operations.

It excludes non-operating and abnormal items.

Formula:

`Operating profit = Net profit + Non-operating expenses - Non-operating incomes`

# Unit 1AC: Long Answer Guide

## 1. What Are Financial Statements?

Financial statements are formal statements prepared at the end of an accounting period to show business performance and position.

They include trading and profit and loss account and balance sheet for a sole proprietorship.

Trading and profit and loss account show profit or loss. Balance sheet shows assets, liabilities, and capital.

They provide information to owners, managers, banks, creditors, government, investors, employees, and other users.

## 2. What Are Closing Entries?

Closing entries transfer nominal account balances to trading account and profit and loss account.

Examples:

1. Trading A/c Dr. To Purchases A/c
2. Trading A/c Dr. To Wages A/c
3. Sales A/c Dr. To Trading A/c
4. Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Salaries A/c
5. Commission Received A/c Dr. To Profit and Loss A/c

After closing entries, nominal accounts are closed and do not appear in the balance sheet.

## 3. Need for Preparing Balance Sheet

Balance sheet is needed to know:

1. assets owned by the business,
2. liabilities owed by the business,
3. owner's capital,
4. liquidity position,
5. long-term financial strength,
6. whether assets are financed by owner or outsiders,
7. balances to be carried forward to the next year.

## 4. Grouping and Marshalling

Grouping means putting similar items under common headings, such as current assets, fixed assets, current liabilities, and long-term liabilities.

Marshalling means arranging assets and liabilities in a particular order.

The two orders are:

1. order of permanence,
2. order of liquidity.

In order of permanence, long-term items appear first.

In order of liquidity, items closest to cash or payment appear first.
