# Chapter 3: Accounts from Incomplete Records
### NCERT Class 11 - Accountancy Part 2

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how to find profit or loss and prepare financial statements when a business has not maintained complete double-entry records.

Small businesses may record only cash transactions and personal accounts of debtors and creditors. Even then, they need to know:

1. profit or loss for the year,
2. capital at the beginning and end,
3. total sales and purchases,
4. debtors, creditors, bills receivable, and bills payable,
5. financial position at year-end.

The chapter gives two main approaches:

```text
statement of affairs method
conversion method
```

# Part 1: Meaning Of Incomplete Records

## Basic Meaning

Incomplete records are accounting records not maintained strictly according to the double-entry system.

They are sometimes called single-entry records, but this term is misleading because there is no complete scientific single-entry system.

In practice, incomplete records mean:

1. some transactions are recorded with both debit and credit,
2. some transactions have only one side recorded,
3. some transactions may not be recorded at all.

---

## What Is Usually Maintained

Small businesses often maintain:

| Record | Usually Maintained? |
| --- | --- |
| cash book | yes |
| debtors' personal accounts | often |
| creditors' personal accounts | often |
| assets records | partial |
| liabilities records | partial |
| expenses records | partial |
| revenue records | partial |

---

# Part 2: Features Of Incomplete Records

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| unsystematic | no uniform double-entry procedure |
| partial records | some accounts are maintained, others are ignored |
| personal transactions mixed | owner's personal transactions may enter cash book |
| lack of uniformity | different businesses record according to convenience |
| dependence on vouchers | invoices, receipts, and physical records are needed |
| estimated results | profit/loss and position may not be fully accurate |

---

## Causes Of Incomplete Records

Businesses may keep incomplete records because:

1. owner lacks accounting knowledge,
2. it is cheaper,
3. fewer books are maintained,
4. it takes less time,
5. small businesses may record only important transactions.

---

## Limitations

| Limitation | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| trial balance cannot be prepared | arithmetical accuracy cannot be checked |
| profit is not fully reliable | complete expenses and income may be missing |
| financial position may be incomplete | assets or liabilities may be omitted |
| difficult to analyse business | profitability, liquidity, and solvency are unclear |
| difficult for tax and insurance | income or loss claims may be hard to prove |

# Part 3: Methods Of Finding Profit Or Loss

## Two Methods

| Method | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Statement of Affairs or Net Worth Method | compares capital at beginning and end |
| Conversion Method | reconstructs missing figures and prepares final accounts |

---

# Part 4: Statement Of Affairs Method

## Meaning

A statement of affairs is a statement of assets and liabilities prepared from incomplete records.

It resembles a balance sheet, but it is not as reliable because many figures are based on estimates, documents, or physical verification rather than full ledger balances.

---

## Format Of Statement Of Affairs

| Liabilities | Amount | Assets | Amount |
| --- | ---: | --- | ---: |
| Bills Payable | ... | Land and Building | ... |
| Creditors | ... | Machinery | ... |
| Outstanding Expenses | ... | Furniture | ... |
| Capital | balancing figure | Stock | ... |
|  |  | Debtors | ... |
|  |  | Cash/Bank | ... |
|  |  | Prepaid Expenses | ... |

Capital is found as:

```text
Capital = Assets - Liabilities
```

If liabilities exceed assets, capital appears on the assets side as a debit balance.

---

## Profit Or Loss Formula

After finding opening capital and closing capital:

```text
Profit or Loss = Closing Capital - Opening Capital + Drawings - Additional Capital Introduced
```

If the result is positive, it is profit.

If the result is negative, it is loss.

---

## Why Drawings Are Added

Drawings reduce closing capital. To find true profit, we add them back.

Example:

```text
If owner withdrew Rs. 10,000, closing capital is Rs. 10,000 lower.
Add drawings back to measure profit generated by the business.
```

---

## Why Additional Capital Is Deducted

Additional capital increases closing capital, but it is not profit.

So it is deducted.

---

## Worked Pattern

Given:

```text
Opening capital = Rs. 1,20,000
Closing capital = Rs. 2,00,000
Additional capital = Rs. 50,000
Drawings = Rs. 30,000
```

Compute:

```text
Closing capital                       2,00,000
Add drawings                            30,000
Adjusted closing capital              2,30,000
Less additional capital                 50,000
Capital from business operations      1,80,000
Less opening capital                  1,20,000
Profit                                  60,000
```

---

# Part 5: Statement Of Affairs Versus Balance Sheet

| Basis | Statement Of Affairs | Balance Sheet |
| --- | --- | --- |
| reliability | less reliable | more reliable |
| records used | incomplete records, estimates, physical counts | double-entry ledger balances |
| objective | estimate capital on a date | show true financial position |
| omission | omissions may remain hidden | omissions likely disturb agreement |
| preparation | used in incomplete records | used after proper accounting records |

# Part 6: Conversion Method

## Meaning

Conversion method reconstructs missing accounting figures using double-entry logic. After missing figures are found, proper final accounts can be prepared.

This method is more detailed than the statement of affairs method.

---

## Common Missing Figures

| Missing Figure | Usually Found Through |
| --- | --- |
| opening capital | opening statement of affairs |
| credit purchases | total creditors account |
| credit sales | total debtors account |
| bills receivable received | total bills receivable account |
| bills payable accepted | total bills payable account |
| cash received from debtors | cash summary or total debtors |
| cash paid to creditors | cash summary or total creditors |
| closing cash/bank | cash summary |

---

# Part 7: Total Creditors Account

## Purpose

Total Creditors Account is prepared to find credit purchases or any other missing creditor-related figure.

---

## Logic

Creditors increase when goods are purchased on credit.

Creditors decrease when:

1. cash is paid,
2. cheque is paid,
3. bills payable are accepted,
4. discount is received,
5. purchases are returned.

---

## Format

| Debit Side | Credit Side |
| --- | --- |
| Cash/Bank paid | Opening balance |
| Bills payable accepted | Bills payable dishonoured |
| Discount received | Credit purchases |
| Purchases return |  |
| Closing balance |  |

Credit purchases are often the balancing figure.

---

## Example Pattern

Given:

```text
Opening creditors = 40,000
Closing creditors = 50,000
Cash paid = 85,000
Discount received = 2,000
```

Credit purchases:

```text
Debit side total needed = Credit side total
Cash paid + Discount received + Closing creditors
= 85,000 + 2,000 + 50,000
= 1,37,000

Credit purchases = 1,37,000 - Opening creditors
= 1,37,000 - 40,000
= 97,000
```

---

# Part 8: Total Debtors Account

## Purpose

Total Debtors Account is prepared to find credit sales or any other missing debtor-related figure.

---

## Logic

Debtors increase when goods are sold on credit.

Debtors decrease when:

1. cash is received,
2. cheque is received,
3. bills receivable are received,
4. discount is allowed,
5. bad debts are written off,
6. sales returns occur.

---

## Format

| Debit Side | Credit Side |
| --- | --- |
| Opening balance | Cash/Bank received |
| Bills receivable dishonoured | Discount allowed |
| Credit sales | Bad debts |
|  | Sales return |
|  | Bills receivable received |
|  | Closing balance |

Credit sales are often the balancing figure.

---

## Net Credit Sales

If sales returns are given:

```text
Net credit sales = Gross credit sales - Sales return
```

Total net sales:

```text
Total sales = Cash sales + Credit sales - Sales return
```

depending on whether credit sales is gross or net in the question.

---

# Part 9: Bills Receivable And Bills Payable Accounts

## Total Bills Receivable Account

Prepared when bills received during the year or another bills receivable figure is missing.

| Debit Side | Credit Side |
| --- | --- |
| Opening bills receivable | Bank/cash: bills honoured |
| Sundry debtors: bills received | Sundry debtors: bills dishonoured |
|  | Closing bills receivable |

---

## Total Bills Payable Account

Prepared when bills accepted during the year or another bills payable figure is missing.

| Debit Side | Credit Side |
| --- | --- |
| Bank/cash: bills matured | Opening bills payable |
| Sundry creditors: bills dishonoured | Sundry creditors: bills accepted |
| Closing bills payable |  |

---

## Example Pattern

If opening bills receivable, closing bills receivable, bills honoured, and bills dishonoured are known, bills received can be found as the balancing figure.

This same logic applies to bills payable accepted.

---

# Part 10: Summary Of Cash

## Purpose

Cash summary is prepared when cash/bank receipts or payments are missing.

It may help find:

1. cash received from debtors,
2. cash paid to creditors,
3. cash sales,
4. cash purchases,
5. opening or closing cash/bank balance.

---

## Basic Structure

| Receipts | Payments |
| --- | --- |
| opening cash/bank | cash purchases |
| cash sales | cash paid to creditors |
| receipts from debtors | expenses |
| capital introduced | drawings |
| loans received | closing cash/bank |

The missing item is the balancing figure.

---

# Part 11: Preparing Final Accounts From Incomplete Records

## Step-By-Step Method

1. Prepare opening statement of affairs to find opening capital.
2. Prepare total debtors account to find credit sales or missing debtor figure.
3. Prepare total creditors account to find credit purchases or missing creditor figure.
4. Prepare bills receivable/payable accounts if bills figures are missing.
5. Prepare cash summary if cash receipts/payments are missing.
6. Combine cash and credit figures.
7. Prepare Trading Account.
8. Prepare Profit and Loss Account.
9. Prepare Balance Sheet.

---

## Trading Account Inputs

| Item | Source |
| --- | --- |
| opening stock | opening list |
| purchases | cash purchases + credit purchases |
| sales | cash sales + credit sales |
| closing stock | closing list |
| direct expenses | cash summary or extra information |

---

## Profit And Loss Account Inputs

| Item | Source |
| --- | --- |
| gross profit | trading account |
| salaries | cash summary or extra information |
| sundry expenses | cash summary |
| discount allowed | total debtors or extra information |
| discount received | total creditors or extra information |
| bad debts | total debtors or extra information |
| depreciation | adjustment |

---

## Balance Sheet Inputs

| Item | Source |
| --- | --- |
| closing assets | closing list |
| closing liabilities | closing list |
| closing capital | opening capital + profit + additional capital - drawings |
| closing cash/bank | cash summary |
| debtors/creditors | closing balances |
| stock | closing valuation |

---

# Part 12: Important Formula Board

## Profit Or Loss

```text
Profit/Loss = Closing Capital - Opening Capital + Drawings - Additional Capital
```

Alternative layout:

```text
Closing Capital
+ Drawings
- Additional Capital
- Opening Capital
= Profit or Loss
```

---

## Credit Purchases

Use Total Creditors Account.

Shortcut idea:

```text
Credit purchases = Payments to creditors + Closing creditors + Discount received + Purchase returns adjustments - Opening creditors - Bills payable dishonoured adjustments
```

Use account format instead of memorising if the question is complex.

---

## Credit Sales

Use Total Debtors Account.

Shortcut idea:

```text
Credit sales = Cash received from debtors + Closing debtors + Discount allowed + Bad debts + Sales returns + Bills receivable received - Opening debtors - Bills receivable dishonoured
```

Use account format for accuracy.

---

## Total Purchases

```text
Total purchases = Cash purchases + Credit purchases
```

## Total Sales

```text
Total sales = Cash sales + Credit sales
```

If sales returns are included in gross sales:

```text
Net sales = Cash sales + Credit sales - Sales returns
```

# Part 13: Common Mistakes

## Mistake 1: Calling Incomplete Records A Proper Single-Entry System

Incomplete records are not a systematic alternative to double entry. They are partial and unsystematic records.

---

## Mistake 2: Treating Statement Of Affairs As Balance Sheet

A statement of affairs resembles a balance sheet but is based on incomplete or estimated information. It is less reliable.

---

## Mistake 3: Forgetting Drawings In Profit Calculation

Drawings reduce capital but are not a business loss. Add them back while computing profit.

---

## Mistake 4: Forgetting Additional Capital

Fresh capital increases closing capital but is not profit. Deduct it.

---

## Mistake 5: Mixing Up Debtors And Creditors Accounts

Use this memory rule:

```text
Debtors account helps find credit sales.
Creditors account helps find credit purchases.
```

---

## Mistake 6: Ignoring Returns, Discounts, Bad Debts, And Bills

These items change debtor and creditor balances. Missing-figure accounts must include them.

# Part 14: Answer-Writing Frames

## Define Incomplete Records

```text
Incomplete records are accounting records not maintained strictly according to the double-entry system.
Some transactions may be fully recorded, some partly recorded, and some not recorded at all.
They are usually maintained by small businesses and are less reliable than double-entry records.
```

---

## Explain Statement Of Affairs Method

```text
Under the statement of affairs method, statements of assets and liabilities are prepared at the beginning and end of the year to find opening and closing capital.
Profit or loss is then calculated by adjusting closing capital for drawings and additional capital.
```

---

## Difference Between Statement Of Affairs And Balance Sheet

```text
A statement of affairs is prepared from incomplete records and often estimated values, mainly to find capital.
A balance sheet is prepared from complete double-entry records and shows the financial position more reliably.
```

---

## Explain Conversion Method

```text
Under the conversion method, missing figures are found by preparing accounts such as total debtors, total creditors, bills receivable, bills payable, and cash summary.
After these figures are found, proper Trading Account, Profit and Loss Account, and Balance Sheet are prepared.
```
