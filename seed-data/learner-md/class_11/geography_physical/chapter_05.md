# Chapter 5: Minerals and Rocks

### NCERT Class 11 - Geography Physical

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains the building materials of the earth's crust: minerals and rocks.

Minerals combine to form rocks. Rocks form landforms, influence soil, affect drainage, control erosion, and provide resources. To understand landforms and geomorphic processes, a learner needs a basic understanding of minerals, rock types, and the rock cycle.

The central idea is:

> Minerals are naturally occurring substances with definite properties, rocks are aggregates of minerals, and the three major rock families are linked through the continuous rock cycle.

# 1. Elements In The Earth's Crust

## 1.1 Basic Idea

The earth is composed of many elements.

In the outer layer of the earth, these elements occur mainly in solid form.

In the interior, materials may occur in hot and molten form.

## 1.2 Major Elements

About 98 percent of the earth's crust is made of eight elements.

| Element | Percentage By Weight |
| --- | ---: |
| Oxygen | 46.60 |
| Silicon | 27.72 |
| Aluminium | 8.13 |
| Iron | 5.00 |
| Calcium | 3.63 |

| Potassium | 2.59 |
| Magnesium | 2.09 |
| Others | 1.41 |

## 1.3 Why Oxygen And Silicon Matter

Oxygen and silicon together form a very large share of the crust.

This is why silicate minerals are extremely common.

Many rock-forming minerals contain silicon and oxygen.

## 1.4 Other Elements

The remaining crustal material includes elements such as:

- titanium,
- hydrogen,
- phosphorus,
- manganese,
- sulphur,
- carbon,
- nickel,
- other elements.

# 2. Meaning Of Minerals

## 2.1 Definition

A mineral is a naturally occurring organic or inorganic substance with:

- orderly atomic structure,
- definite chemical composition,
- definite physical properties.

## 2.2 Minerals Usually Combine Elements

Elements are rarely found alone in the crust.

They usually combine with other elements to form minerals.

## 2.3 Single-Element Minerals

Some minerals are made of a single element.

Examples:

- sulphur,
- copper,
- silver,
- gold,
- graphite.

## 2.4 Mineral Diversity

Although the number of major elements is limited, they combine in many ways.

This produces many mineral varieties.

At least 2,000 minerals have been named and identified in the earth's crust.

Most common rocks are made mainly of a smaller group of major rock-forming minerals.

## 2.5 Source Of Minerals

The basic source of minerals is hot magma inside the earth.

When magma cools:

- crystals of minerals appear,
- minerals form in sequence,
- solid rocks develop.

## 2.6 Organic Mineral Resources

Coal, petroleum, and natural gas are organic substances.

They occur as:

- coal: solid,
- petroleum: liquid,
- natural gas: gas.

# 4. Crystal Form

## 4.1 Meaning

Crystal form is the external shape of a mineral.

It is determined by the internal arrangement of molecules or atoms.

## 4.2 Examples

Crystal forms may include:

- cubes,
- octahedrons,
- hexagonal prisms.

## 4.3 Why It Matters

The same chemical substance may show a typical crystal form if it grows under proper conditions.

Crystal form helps identify minerals.

---

# 5. Cleavage And Fracture

## 5.1 Cleavage

Cleavage is the tendency of a mineral to break along definite flat planes.

It results from internal molecular arrangement.

Minerals may cleave:

- in one direction,
- in more than one direction,
- at different angles.

## 5.2 Fracture

Fracture occurs when a mineral breaks irregularly.

This happens when internal molecular arrangement is complex and no clear planes of weakness exist.

## 5.3 Difference

| Cleavage | Fracture |
| --- | --- |
| Breaks along flat planes | Breaks irregularly |
| Controlled by molecular arrangement | No regular cleavage plane |
| Produces smooth surfaces | Produces rough or uneven surfaces |

---

# 6. Lustre, Colour, And Streak

## 6.1 Lustre

Lustre is the appearance of a mineral surface in reflected light.

Types include:

- metallic,
- silky,
- glossy.

## 6.2 Colour

Some minerals have characteristic colour because of molecular structure.

Examples:

- malachite,
- azurite,
- chalcopyrite.

Some minerals are coloured by impurities.

Example:

- quartz may appear white, green, red, or yellow because of impurities.

## 6.3 Streak

Streak is the colour of the powdered mineral.

It may be the same as the mineral colour or different.

Examples:

- malachite is green and gives a green streak;
- fluorite may be purple or green but gives a white streak.

## 6.4 Why Streak Is Useful

Streak can be more reliable than surface colour because colour may change due to impurities.

# 7. Transparency And Structure

## 7.1 Transparency

Transparency describes how light passes through a mineral.

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Transparent | Light passes through and objects can be seen clearly |
| Translucent | Light passes through but objects appear diffused |
| Opaque | Light does not pass through |

## 7.2 Structure

Structure means arrangement of individual crystals.

It may be:

- fine-grained,
- medium-grained,
- coarse-grained,
- fibrous,
- separable,
- divergent,
- radiating.

# 9. Specific Gravity

## 9.1 Meaning

Specific gravity is the ratio between:

- the weight of a given object,
- the weight of an equal volume of water.

## 9.2 Simple Method

The object is weighed in air and then in water.

Specific gravity can be found by dividing weight in air by the difference between the two weights.

## 9.3 Why It Matters

Specific gravity helps identify minerals because some minerals are much heavier than others even when similar in size.

# 19. Meaning Of Rocks

## 19.1 Definition

A rock is an aggregate of one or more minerals.

## 19.2 Rock Variety

Rocks may be:

- hard,
- soft,
- dark,
- light,
- coarse,
- fine.

Examples:

- granite is hard,
- soapstone is soft,
- gabbro is black,
- quartzite may be milky white.

## 19.3 Composition

Rocks do not have a fixed mineral composition in the same way minerals do.

Feldspar and quartz are among the most common minerals in rocks.

## 19.4 Petrology

Petrology is the science of rocks.

A petrologist studies:

- mineral composition,
- texture,
- structure,
- origin,
- occurrence,
- alteration,
- relationships with other rocks.

## 19.5 Why Geographers Study Rocks

Rocks are closely related to:

- landforms,
- soils,
- drainage,
- erosion,
- minerals,
- resources,
- construction,
- landscapes.

# 20. Three Major Rock Families

Rocks are grouped by mode of formation into three families:

1. igneous rocks,
2. sedimentary rocks,
3. metamorphic rocks.

## 20.1 Quick Comparison

| Rock Type | Formation | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Igneous | Cooling and solidification of magma or lava | Granite, basalt, gabbro |
| Sedimentary | Deposition, compaction, and lithification of sediments | Sandstone, shale, limestone |
| Metamorphic | Existing rocks changed by pressure, temperature, and chemical action | Slate, schist, marble |

# 21. Igneous Rocks

## 21.1 Meaning

Igneous rocks form from magma and lava.

They are called primary rocks because they originate from molten material from the earth's interior.

## 21.2 Word Origin

Igneous comes from the Latin word ignis, meaning fire.

## 21.3 Formation

Igneous rocks form when magma cools and solidifies.

Cooling can occur:

- inside the earth's crust,
- on the surface.

## 21.4 Texture

Igneous rocks are classified mainly by texture.

Texture depends on:

- size of grains,
- arrangement of grains,
- cooling rate,
- physical conditions during cooling.

## 21.5 Slow Cooling

If molten material cools slowly at great depth, mineral grains grow large.

This produces coarse-grained rocks.

## 21.6 Sudden Cooling

If molten material cools suddenly at the surface, grains are small and smooth.

This produces fine-grained rocks.

## 21.7 Intermediate Cooling

Intermediate cooling conditions produce intermediate grain sizes.

## 21.8 Examples

Examples of igneous rocks include:

- granite,
- gabbro,
- pegmatite,
- basalt,
- volcanic breccia,
- tuff.

---

# 22. Sedimentary Rocks

## 22.1 Word Origin

Sedimentary comes from the Latin word sedimentum, meaning settling.

## 22.2 Formation

Rocks at the earth's surface are broken into fragments by denudational agents.

These fragments are:

- transported,
- deposited,
- compacted,
- hardened.

The conversion of deposits into rocks is called lithification.

## 22.3 Layers

Many sedimentary rocks retain layers of deposits after lithification.

This is why sandstone and shale often show bedding.

## 22.4 Major Groups

Sedimentary rocks are classified by mode of formation into:

1. mechanically formed,
2. organically formed,
3. chemically formed.

## 22.5 Mechanically Formed Sedimentary Rocks

These form from fragments of pre-existing rocks.

Examples:

- sandstone,
- conglomerate,
- limestone,
- shale,
- loess.

## 22.6 Organically Formed Sedimentary Rocks

These form from remains of organisms or organic activity.

Examples:

- geyserite,
- chalk,
- limestone,
- coal.

## 22.7 Chemically Formed Sedimentary Rocks

These form from chemical precipitation or evaporation.

Examples:

- chert,
- limestone,
- halite,
- potash.

# 23. Metamorphic Rocks

## 23.1 Meaning

Metamorphic means change of form.

Metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks change under pressure, temperature, or chemical conditions.

## 23.2 PVT Changes

Metamorphism occurs under changes in:

- pressure,
- volume,
- temperature.

These are often referred to as PVT changes.

## 23.3 Causes

Metamorphism occurs when:

- rocks are forced down by tectonic processes,
- hot magma comes in contact with crustal rocks,
- rocks are subjected to high pressure from overlying material,
- temperature and pressure recrystallise minerals.

## 23.4 Recrystallisation

Metamorphism involves recrystallisation and reorganisation of materials within original rocks.

---

# 24. Types Of Metamorphism

## 24.1 Dynamic Metamorphism

Dynamic metamorphism occurs due to mechanical disruption.

Rocks are broken, crushed, and reorganised without major chemical change.

It is linked with pressure and movement.

## 24.2 Thermal Metamorphism

Thermal metamorphism occurs when rocks chemically alter and recrystallise due to heat.

It has two forms:

- contact metamorphism,
- regional metamorphism.

## 24.3 Contact Metamorphism

Contact metamorphism occurs when rocks come into contact with hot intruding magma or lava.

High temperature causes recrystallisation.

New materials from magma or lava may be added.

## 24.4 Regional Metamorphism

Regional metamorphism occurs over large areas.

It is caused by:

- tectonic shearing,
- high temperature,
- high pressure,
- deformation.

---

# 25. Foliation, Lineation, And Banding

## 25.1 Foliation Or Lineation

During metamorphism, minerals or grains may become arranged in layers or lines.

This arrangement is called foliation or lineation.

## 25.2 Banding

Sometimes minerals of different groups form alternating light and dark layers.

This structure is called banding.

Rocks showing banding are called banded rocks.

## 25.3 Major Groups Of Metamorphic Rocks

Metamorphic rocks are classified into:

1. foliated rocks,
2. non-foliated rocks.

## 25.4 Examples

Examples include:

- gneiss,
- slate,
- schist,
- marble,
- quartzite.

The textbook also mentions gneissoid granite and syenite among metamorphic examples.

---

# 26. Rock Cycle

## 26.1 Meaning

The rock cycle is the continuous process through which old rocks transform into new rocks.

Rocks do not remain in their original form forever.

## 26.2 Igneous As Primary Rocks

Igneous rocks are primary rocks.

Other rocks can form from igneous rocks.

## 26.3 Pathways

Igneous rocks can:

- weather and become sediments,
- become sedimentary rocks,
- change into metamorphic rocks under heat and pressure.

Sedimentary rocks can:

- break into fragments,
- form new sedimentary rocks,
- become metamorphic rocks.

Metamorphic rocks can:

- break into sediments,
- melt into magma,
- become new igneous rocks after cooling.

## 26.4 Subduction

Crustal rocks may be carried down into the mantle through subduction.

Subduction occurs when part or all of a crustal plate goes under another plate in a convergence zone.

The rocks melt due to high temperature and become magma.

Magma is the original source for igneous rocks.

## 26.5 Rock Cycle In Words

```text
Magma
-> cooling and solidification
-> igneous rock
-> weathering, erosion, deposition
-> sediments
-> compaction and lithification
-> sedimentary rock
-> heat and pressure
-> metamorphic rock
-> melting
-> magma again
```

# 30. Common Misunderstandings

## 30.1 Minerals And Rocks Are Same

Incorrect.

Minerals have definite composition and structure.

Rocks are aggregates of one or more minerals.

## 30.2 All Minerals Contain Many Elements

Incorrect.

Most minerals contain multiple elements, but some are single-element minerals such as gold, silver, sulphur, and graphite.

## 30.3 Colour Alone Identifies A Mineral

Incorrect.

Colour can change due to impurities.

Streak, hardness, cleavage, and other properties are also needed.

## 30.4 All Igneous Rocks Have Large Grains

Incorrect.

Grain size depends on cooling rate.

Slow cooling gives large grains; sudden cooling gives small grains.

## 30.5 Sedimentary Rocks Are Always Organic

Incorrect.

Sedimentary rocks may be mechanically, organically, or chemically formed.

## 30.6 Metamorphic Rocks Always Melt

Incorrect.

Metamorphism changes rocks under pressure and temperature without complete melting.

If rocks melt completely, they become magma.

## 30.7 Rock Cycle Moves In Only One Direction

Incorrect.

The rock cycle has many pathways.

Any rock type can transform under the right conditions.

## 31.2 Short Answer: Rocks And Major Classes

A rock is an aggregate of one or more minerals.

The three major classes of rocks are:

- igneous rocks,
- sedimentary rocks,
- metamorphic rocks.

## 31.3 Short Answer: Igneous Rock

Igneous rocks form when magma or lava cools and solidifies.

They are called primary rocks because they form from molten material from the earth's interior.

Their texture depends on cooling rate: slow cooling gives large grains, sudden cooling gives small grains.

Examples include granite, gabbro, basalt, pegmatite, volcanic breccia, and tuff.

## 31.4 Short Answer: Sedimentary Rock

Sedimentary rocks form when existing rocks are broken into fragments, transported, deposited, compacted, and lithified.

They often show layers.

They may be mechanically, organically, or chemically formed.

Examples include sandstone, shale, limestone, conglomerate, coal, chert, and halite.

## 31.5 Short Answer: Rock Cycle Relationship

The rock cycle explains how the three major rock types transform into one another.

Igneous rocks can weather into sediments, sediments can become sedimentary rocks, and sedimentary or igneous rocks can become metamorphic rocks under heat and pressure.

Rocks may also melt into magma, which cools to form igneous rocks again.

## 32.3 Metamorphic Rocks

Use this structure:

1. Define metamorphic:
   Rocks changed in form from pre-existing rocks.

2. Cause:
   Pressure, volume, and temperature changes.

3. Dynamic metamorphism:
   Breaking and crushing without major chemical change.

4. Contact metamorphism:
   Heat from magma or lava recrystallises surrounding rocks.

5. Regional metamorphism:
   Tectonic pressure and heat act over large areas.

6. Structures:
   Foliation, lineation, and banding.

7. Examples:
   Slate, schist, gneiss, marble, quartzite.

# 34. Quick Revision Sheet

## 34.1 One-Line Definitions

- Mineral: naturally occurring substance with definite composition and properties.
- Rock: aggregate of one or more minerals.
- Petrology: science of rocks.
- Igneous rock: rock formed from cooling magma or lava.
- Sedimentary rock: rock formed from deposited and lithified sediments.
- Metamorphic rock: rock formed by change in existing rock under pressure and temperature.
- Lithification: compaction and hardening of sediments into rock.
- Foliation: layered or lined arrangement in metamorphic rock.
- Rock cycle: continuous transformation of rocks.

## 34.2 Must-Remember Minerals

- Feldspar: half of crust, ceramics and glass.
- Quartz: silica, sand and granite, radio and radar.
- Pyroxene: green or black, common in meteorites.
- Amphibole: green or black, asbestos industry, hornblende.
- Mica: electrical instruments.
- Olivine: greenish, basaltic rocks, jewellery.

## 34.3 Must-Remember Rock Examples

- Igneous: granite, gabbro, pegmatite, basalt, volcanic breccia, tuff.
- Sedimentary: sandstone, conglomerate, limestone, shale, loess, chalk, coal, chert, halite.
- Metamorphic: slate, schist, marble, quartzite, gneiss.

## 34.4 Must-Remember Process Links

- Magma cools: igneous rock.
- Sediments lithify: sedimentary rock.
- Existing rock recrystallises: metamorphic rock.
- Rock melts: magma.
- Magma cools again: igneous rock.

# 35. Final Chapter Summary

The earth's crust is made mainly of a few elements, especially oxygen and silicon. These elements combine to form minerals. A mineral is a naturally occurring substance with definite chemical composition, orderly atomic structure, and physical properties.

Minerals are identified through properties such as crystal form, cleavage, fracture, lustre, colour, streak, transparency, structure, hardness, and specific gravity. Major rock-forming minerals include feldspar, quartz, pyroxene, amphibole, mica, and olivine.

Rocks are aggregates of minerals. Petrology is the science of rocks. On the basis of origin, rocks are grouped into igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks.

Igneous rocks form from cooling magma or lava. Sedimentary rocks form from deposited fragments that become lithified. Metamorphic rocks form when existing rocks undergo changes due to pressure, temperature, and chemical action.

The rock cycle links all rock types. Igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks can transform into one another through weathering, erosion, deposition, lithification, metamorphism, melting, and cooling.
