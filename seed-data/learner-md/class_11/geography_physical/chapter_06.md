# Chapter 6: Geomorphic Processes

### NCERT Class 11 - Geography Physical

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains the processes that shape the surface of the earth.

The earth's surface is uneven because internal forces build landforms while external forces wear them down, transport material, deposit it, and help form soils. These processes work slowly, continuously, and differently in different climates, rocks, slopes, and vegetation conditions.

The central idea is:

> The earth's surface is a playfield of opposing geomorphic processes: endogenic forces build and deform relief, while exogenic processes weather, erode, transport, deposit, and transform surface materials.

# 1. Why Is The Earth's Surface Uneven?

## 1.1 The Crust Is Dynamic

The earth's crust has moved in the past and continues to move today.

It moves:

- vertically,
- horizontally,
- slowly in most cases,
- sometimes suddenly during earthquakes.

The surface is uneven because different internal forces build and deform the crust in different ways.

## 1.2 Two Opposing Groups Of Forces

The earth's surface is shaped by:

1. endogenic forces,
2. exogenic forces.

## 1.3 Endogenic Forces

Endogenic forces originate within the earth.

They tend to:

- uplift land,
- build mountains,
- create plateaus,
- cause folding,
- cause faulting,
- produce volcanoes,
- trigger earthquakes.

They are mainly land-building forces.

## 1.4 Exogenic Forces

Exogenic forces act at or near the earth's surface.

They are driven mainly by:

- solar energy,
- atmosphere,
- water,
- ice,
- wind,
- gravity.

They tend to:

- weather rocks,
- erode elevations,
- transport materials,
- deposit sediments,
- fill depressions.

They are mainly land-wearing forces.

## 1.5 Why Relief Does Not Become Completely Flat

Exogenic processes constantly try to wear down relief.

Endogenic forces constantly build or uplift parts of the surface.

Because these opposing actions continue, relief variations remain.

# 2. Degradation, Aggradation, And Gradation

## 2.1 Degradation

Degradation means wearing down of relief or elevated areas.

It occurs through:

- weathering,
- mass wasting,
- erosion,
- transport.

## 2.2 Aggradation

Aggradation means filling up of depressions and basins.

It occurs through deposition.

## 2.3 Gradation

Gradation is the process of reducing relief differences through erosion and deposition.

It includes:

- lowering high areas,
- filling low areas.

## 2.4 Simple Memory Line

Degradation lowers.

Aggradation fills.

Gradation tries to level.

# 3. Human Relevance Of Geomorphic Processes

## 3.1 Surface As Life Support

Humans depend on the earth's surface for:

- food,
- water,
- soil,
- settlement,
- transport,
- minerals,
- forests,
- agriculture.

## 3.2 Misuse Of Surface

Human misuse can reduce land potential quickly.

Examples:

- overuse of resources,
- deforestation,
- slope cutting,
- mining,
- unplanned construction,
- overgrazing,
- poor farming,
- riverbank disturbance.

## 3.3 Why Study Processes?

If we understand the processes shaping the surface, we can:

- reduce landslide risk,
- conserve soil,
- plan land use,
- manage slopes,
- protect future productivity,
- reduce environmental damage.

# 4. Meaning Of Geomorphic Processes

## 4.1 Definition

Geomorphic processes are endogenic and exogenic forces that cause physical stresses and chemical actions on earth materials and bring changes in the earth's surface configuration.

## 4.2 Major Types

| Type | Processes |
| --- | --- |
| Endogenic | Diastrophism, volcanism |
| Exogenic | Weathering, mass wasting, erosion, deposition |

## 4.3 Process vs Agent

A process is the force or action applied to earth materials.

An agent is the mobile medium that performs erosion, transport, or deposition.

## 4.4 Examples Of Geomorphic Agents

Geomorphic agents include:

- running water,
- groundwater,
- glaciers,
- wind,
- waves,
- currents.

These agents acquire, transport, and deposit earth materials.

## 4.5 Why Gradients Matter

Agents become effective when they move from higher to lower levels.

Movement requires:

- gravity,
- slope,
- pressure gradient,
- height difference,
- energy difference.

Without gradients, there is no movement.

Without movement, there is no erosion, transport, or deposition.

---

# 5. Role Of Gravity

## 5.1 Gravity As Directional Force

Gravity activates downslope movement of matter.

It also creates stress in earth materials.

## 5.2 Gravity And Mobility

Gravity helps move:

- soil,
- rock debris,
- water,
- ice,
- weathered material.

## 5.3 Gravity And Other Agents

Indirect gravitational stresses also influence:

- waves,
- tides,
- currents,
- winds.

## 5.4 Core Principle

All movement happens along gradients:

- high to low elevation,
- high to low pressure,
- high to low energy.

Gravity switches on the movement of surface material.

---

# 6. Endogenic Processes

## 6.1 Energy Source

Endogenic geomorphic processes are driven by energy from inside the earth.

This energy comes mainly from:

- radioactivity,
- rotational friction,
- tidal friction,
- primordial heat from earth's origin.

## 6.2 Geothermal Gradients

Internal heat creates geothermal gradients and heat flow.

These induce:

- diastrophism,
- volcanism.

## 6.3 Non-Uniform Action

Endogenic forces do not act equally everywhere.

Their action varies due to differences in:

- heat flow,
- geothermal gradients,
- crustal thickness,
- crustal strength,
- tectonic conditions.

This non-uniform action helps create an uneven original crustal surface.

---

# 7. Diastrophism

## 7.1 Meaning

Diastrophism includes all processes that move, elevate, warp, deform, or build up parts of the earth's crust.

## 7.2 Major Components

Diastrophism includes:

1. orogenic processes,
2. epeirogenic processes,
3. earthquakes,
4. plate tectonic movements.

## 7.3 Orogeny

Orogeny is mountain building.

It involves severe folding and deformation in long, narrow belts of the earth's crust.

Example idea:

- young fold mountain belts.

## 7.4 Epeirogeny

Epeirogeny is continental building.

It involves uplift or warping of large parts of the crust.

It usually produces broad and simple deformation rather than intense folding.

## 7.5 Earthquakes

Earthquakes involve local, relatively minor movements compared with large-scale mountain or continent building.

But they can cause sudden surface changes.

## 7.6 Plate Tectonics

Plate tectonics involves horizontal movement of crustal plates.

It can create:

- folding,
- faulting,
- mountain building,
- earthquakes,
- volcanic belts.

## 7.7 Orogeny vs Epeirogeny

| Orogeny | Epeirogeny |
| --- | --- |
| Mountain building | Continental building |
| Severe deformation | Broad uplift or warping |
| Long narrow belts | Large crustal areas |
| Strong folding | Simpler deformation |

## 7.8 Link With Metamorphism

Diastrophic processes cause changes in:

- pressure,
- volume,
- temperature.

These PVT changes can induce metamorphism of rocks.

---

# 8. Volcanism

## 8.1 Meaning

Volcanism includes movement of molten rock, or magma, onto or toward the earth's surface.

## 8.2 Forms Created

Volcanism forms:

- intrusive volcanic forms,
- extrusive volcanic forms,
- lava flows,
- volcanic cones,
- ash deposits,
- igneous rocks.

## 8.3 Volcano vs Volcanism

Volcanism is the process.

A volcano is a landform or opening through which volcanic material reaches the surface.

---

# 9. Exogenic Processes

## 9.1 Energy Source

Exogenic processes derive their energy mainly from:

- atmosphere,
- sun's heat,
- gradients created by tectonic factors.

## 9.2 Why Tectonics Matters For Exogenic Processes

Tectonic forces create slopes and relief.

Exogenic processes act on those slopes.

Without uplift and gradients, running water, ice, and mass movement would have little power to move material.

## 9.3 Stress In Earth Materials

Force applied per unit area is called stress.

Stress may be caused by:

- pushing,
- pulling,
- loading,
- unloading,
- expansion,
- contraction.

## 9.4 Shear Stress

Shear stress acts along faces of earth materials.

It causes:

- separation,
- angular displacement,
- slippage,
- rock breakage.

## 9.5 Molecular Stress

Earth materials also experience molecular stresses caused by:

- temperature changes,
- crystallisation,
- melting,
- chemical reactions,
- hydration.

## 9.6 Chemical Weakening

Chemical processes loosen bonds between grains.

They also dissolve:

- soluble minerals,
- cementing materials.

This prepares rocks for weathering, mass movement, and erosion.

---

# 10. Denudation

## 10.1 Meaning

Denudation means stripping off or uncovering the surface.

## 10.2 Processes Included

Denudation includes:

- weathering,
- mass wasting or mass movement,
- erosion,
- transportation.

## 10.3 Driving Forces

Different denudational processes have different driving forces.

Examples:

- weathering is driven by climate, temperature, water, chemical action, and organisms;
- mass movement is driven directly by gravity;
- erosion and transport are driven by kinetic energy of agents.

---

# 11. Controls Of Exogenic Processes

## 11.1 Climate

Temperature and precipitation are the two most important climatic controls.

They affect:

- weathering type,
- depth of weathering mantle,
- vegetation cover,
- freeze-thaw activity,
- chemical reactions,
- runoff,
- soil formation.

## 11.2 Local Climate Variation

Even within one climatic region, processes vary because of:

- altitude,
- slope aspect,
- north-facing vs south-facing slopes,
- insolation,
- wind velocity,
- wind direction,
- precipitation intensity,
- relation between precipitation and evaporation,
- daily temperature range,
- freezing and thawing frequency,
- depth of frost penetration.

## 11.3 Vegetation

Vegetation depends largely on precipitation and temperature.

Vegetation influences:

- root action,
- soil binding,
- organic acids,
- humus,
- water infiltration,
- erosion resistance.

## 11.4 Rock Type And Structure

If climate is similar, intensity of exogenic processes depends strongly on rock type and structure.

Rock structure includes:

- folds,
- faults,
- bedding planes,
- joints,
- orientation of beds,
- inclination of beds,
- hardness,
- softness,
- mineral chemistry,
- permeability,
- impermeability.

## 11.5 Differential Resistance

Different rocks resist processes differently.

A rock may resist one process but be weak against another.

Under different climates, the same rock may weather at different rates.

This produces differences in topography.

# 12. Weathering

## 12.1 Definition

Weathering is the mechanical disintegration and chemical decomposition of rocks through elements of weather and climate.

## 12.2 In-Situ Process

Weathering happens in place.

Very little or no movement of material occurs.

Therefore, it is an in-situ or on-site process.

## 12.3 Weathering vs Transportation

Weathering breaks or decomposes rock in place.

Transportation moves material from one place to another.

Small motion caused by cracking or expansion is not the same as transport by a geomorphic agent.

## 12.4 Controls

Weathering is controlled by:

- geology,
- climate,
- topography,
- vegetation,
- rock structure,
- water availability.

Climate is especially important.

## 12.5 Major Types

Weathering processes are grouped into:

1. chemical weathering,
2. physical or mechanical weathering,
3. biological weathering.

These rarely act completely alone. Usually one type dominates while others also assist.

---

# 13. Chemical Weathering

## 13.1 Meaning

Chemical weathering decomposes, dissolves, or reduces rocks through chemical reactions.

The main chemical weathering processes are:

- solution,
- carbonation,
- hydration,
- oxidation,
- reduction.

## 13.2 Conditions Needed

Chemical reactions are faster when these are present:

- water,
- oxygen,
- carbon dioxide,
- heat,
- acids.

Carbon dioxide underground increases due to decomposition of plants and animals.

This strengthens chemical weathering.

---

# 14. Solution

## 14.1 Meaning

Solution occurs when minerals dissolve in water or weak acids and are removed in dissolved form.

## 14.2 Minerals Affected

Soluble minerals include:

- nitrates,
- sulphates,
- potassium compounds,
- common salt,
- calcium carbonate,
- calcium magnesium bicarbonate.

## 14.3 Rainy vs Dry Climates

In rainy climates, soluble minerals are easily leached away.

In dry climates, they may accumulate because water evaporates.

## 14.4 Limestone Example

Limestone contains calcium carbonate.

When water contains carbonic acid, calcium carbonate dissolves and is carried away.

This helps form caves in limestone areas.

---

# 15. Carbonation

## 15.1 Meaning

Carbonation is the reaction of carbonate and bicarbonate with minerals.

It is important in breaking down:

- feldspars,
- carbonate minerals.

## 15.2 Carbonic Acid

Carbon dioxide from atmosphere and soil air dissolves in water.

This forms carbonic acid.

Carbonic acid is a weak acid, but it is very effective over long periods.

## 15.3 Cave Formation

Calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate dissolve in carbonic acid.

They are removed in solution.

This can result in cave formation in limestone regions.

---

# 16. Hydration

## 16.1 Meaning

Hydration is the chemical addition of water to minerals.

Minerals take up water and expand.

This increases the volume of the material or rock.

## 16.2 Example

Calcium sulphate takes in water and turns into gypsum.

Gypsum is more unstable than calcium sulphate.

## 16.3 Repetition And Fatigue

Hydration is reversible.

Repeated hydration and dehydration cause fatigue in rocks.

Over time, rocks may disintegrate.

## 16.4 Clay And Salts

Clay minerals swell and contract during wetting and drying.

Salts in pore spaces undergo repeated hydration.

Both processes help crack rocks.

## 16.5 Link With Physical Weathering

Volume changes from hydration also help physical weathering through:

- exfoliation,
- granular disintegration.

---

# 17. Oxidation And Reduction

## 17.1 Oxidation

Oxidation means a mineral combines with oxygen to form oxides or hydroxides.

It occurs where minerals have access to:

- atmosphere,
- oxygenated water.

Minerals commonly involved include:

- iron,
- manganese,
- sulphur.

## 17.2 Colour Change

Iron-rich minerals may change colour when oxidised.

Red iron may become brown or yellow.

Iron rusting is a simple example of oxidation.

## 17.3 Reduction

Reduction occurs when oxidised minerals are placed in oxygen-poor conditions.

Such conditions occur:

- below the water table,
- in stagnant water,
- in waterlogged ground.

## 17.4 Reduction Colour

Iron under reduction may turn greenish or bluish grey.

## 17.5 Interrelation

Chemical weathering processes are interrelated.

Hydration, carbonation, and oxidation often work together and speed up weathering.

---

# 18. Physical Or Mechanical Weathering

## 18.1 Meaning

Physical weathering breaks rocks mechanically without major chemical change.

## 18.2 Applied Forces

Physical weathering depends on forces such as:

- gravitational forces,
- overburden pressure,
- load,
- shearing stress,
- expansion due to temperature change,
- crystal growth,
- animal activity,
- water pressure from wetting and drying.

## 18.3 Main Causes

Most physical weathering processes are caused by:

- thermal expansion,
- pressure release,
- repeated contraction and expansion,
- water freezing,
- salt crystallisation.

## 18.4 Slow But Powerful

These processes may be small and slow.

But repeated stress creates fatigue and can severely damage rocks.

---

# 19. Unloading And Expansion

## 19.1 Meaning

Unloading occurs when overlying rock load is removed by erosion.

This releases vertical pressure.

Upper rock layers expand.

## 19.2 Fracture Development

Pressure release produces fractures roughly parallel to the ground surface.

In curved ground surfaces, arched fractures may form.

## 19.3 Exfoliation Domes

Expansion due to unloading may produce large smooth rounded domes called exfoliation domes.

Exfoliation sheets may extend hundreds or thousands of metres horizontally.

---

# 20. Temperature Changes And Expansion

## 20.1 Mineral Expansion

Different minerals expand and contract at different rates.

When temperature rises, minerals expand.

When temperature falls, minerals contract.

## 20.2 Repetition

Daily temperature changes cause repeated expansion and contraction.

This is especially effective in:

- dry climates,
- high elevations,
- areas with large diurnal temperature ranges.

## 20.3 Rock Fatigue

Repeated internal movement weakens rocks.

Surface layers expand more than deeper rock.

This creates stress and fractures parallel to the surface.

## 20.4 Tors

In granites, exfoliation can produce smooth rounded boulders called tors.

## 20.5 Exfoliation Dome vs Exfoliated Tor

| Exfoliation Dome | Exfoliated Tor |
| --- | --- |
| Large smooth rounded dome | Smaller rounded boulder or rock mass |
| Often linked with unloading and pressure release | Often linked with thermal expansion and exfoliation |
| Massive surface form | Isolated blocky rounded form |

---

# 21. Freezing, Thawing, And Frost Wedging

## 21.1 Meaning

Frost weathering occurs when water freezes inside pores and cracks.

Repeated freezing and thawing widen cracks.

## 21.2 Best Conditions

It is most effective:

- at high elevations,
- in mid-latitudes,
- where freezing and melting repeat often,
- in glacial areas.

## 21.3 Process

Water enters cracks.

It freezes and expands.

Expansion creates pressure.

Cracks become wider.

Repeated cycles break the rock apart.

## 21.4 Rate Of Freezing

Rapid freezing causes sudden expansion and high pressure.

This increases fracturing.

---

# 22. Salt Weathering

## 22.1 Meaning

Salt weathering breaks rocks through:

- salt expansion,
- hydration,
- crystallisation.

## 22.2 Conditions

Salt weathering is common where:

- salts are present,
- moisture evaporates,
- wetting and drying alternate,
- temperature range is high.

## 22.3 Desert Temperatures

Surface temperatures in deserts may range between 30 and 50 degrees C.

Such high ranges favour salt expansion.

## 22.4 Salt Crystallisation

Salt crystallisation is the most effective salt-weathering process.

As crystals grow in pores, they push neighbouring grains aside.

## 22.5 Minerals And Rocks Affected

Salts such as sodium chloride and gypsum may heave up overlying layers.

Polygonal cracks can develop on heaved surfaces.

Chalk breaks down most readily, followed by limestone, sandstone, shale, gneiss, and granite.

---

# 23. Biological Weathering

## 23.1 Meaning

Biological weathering includes physical and chemical changes caused by organisms.

## 23.2 Organisms Involved

Important organisms include:

- earthworms,
- termites,
- rodents,
- plant roots,
- bacteria,
- lichens,
- humans.

## 23.3 Physical Effects

Burrowing and wedging expose new rock surfaces.

Plant roots exert pressure and break rocks.

Animals mix soil and weathered material.

## 23.4 Chemical Effects

Decaying plant and animal matter produces:

- humic acids,
- carbonic acid,
- other organic acids.

These acids increase decay and solubility of minerals.

## 23.5 Human Role

Humans contribute by:

- disturbing vegetation,
- ploughing,
- cultivating soil,
- mixing air, water, and minerals.

Human activity can accelerate weathering and erosion if unmanaged.

---

# 24. Special Effect: Exfoliation

## 24.1 Meaning

Exfoliation is flaking off of curved sheets or shells from rock surfaces.

It produces smooth rounded surfaces.

## 24.2 Result, Not A Separate Process

Exfoliation is a result of weathering processes.

It may result from:

- unloading,
- pressure release,
- thermal expansion,
- salt weathering.

## 24.3 Forms Produced

Exfoliation may produce:

- exfoliation domes,
- tors,
- rounded rock surfaces.

---

# 25. Significance Of Weathering

## 25.1 Rock Breakdown

Weathering breaks rocks into smaller fragments.

It prepares material for:

- regolith,
- soil formation,
- erosion,
- mass movement.

## 25.2 Biodiversity

Weathering helps create weathering mantles and soils.

Vegetation depends on soil and weathered material.

Vegetation supports biomes and biodiversity.

Thus, weathering indirectly supports biodiversity.

## 25.3 Erosion And Relief Reduction

Erosion becomes much more effective when rocks are weathered.

Weathering aids:

- mass wasting,
- erosion,
- relief reduction,
- landform change.

## 25.4 Mineral Enrichment

Weathering can enrich valuable ores.

When some materials are removed by leaching, remaining valuable materials may become concentrated.

Examples include enrichment of ores of:

- iron,
- manganese,
- aluminium,
- copper.

## 25.5 Economic Importance

Without weathering, valuable mineral concentration may be too low for economic exploitation.

Weathering can make some deposits economically useful.

## 25.6 Soil Formation

Weathering is essential for soil formation because it creates the weathered material from which soil develops.

# 26. Mass Movements

## 26.1 Meaning

Mass movements transfer rock debris downslope under the direct influence of gravity.

## 26.2 Difference From Erosion

In erosion, agents such as running water, wind, glaciers, waves, or groundwater carry material.

In mass movement, gravity moves the material directly.

Air, water, or ice may be present within the debris, but they do not act as the main transporting agent.

## 26.3 Weathering And Mass Movement

Weathering is not a strict precondition for mass movement.

However, weathered slopes are more prone to mass movement than fresh, unweathered rock.

## 26.4 Types Of Movement

Mass movements may be:

- slow,
- rapid,
- shallow,
- deep.

They include:

- creep,
- flow,
- slide,
- fall.

---

# 27. Conditions Favouring Mass Movements

Mass movements are favoured by:

- weak unconsolidated materials,
- thinly bedded rocks,
- faults,
- steeply dipping beds,
- vertical cliffs,
- steep slopes,
- abundant precipitation,
- torrential rain,
- scarcity of vegetation.

## 27.1 Activating Causes

Mass movements may be activated by:

1. removal of support from below,
2. increase in slope gradient or height,
3. overloading by natural or artificial filling,
4. heavy rainfall causing saturation and lubrication,
5. removal of load from original slope surface,
6. earthquakes,
7. explosions,
8. machinery vibrations,
9. excessive seepage,
10. rapid drawdown of water from lakes, reservoirs, or rivers,
11. indiscriminate removal of natural vegetation.

---

# 28. Slow Mass Movements

## 28.1 Creep

Creep is extremely slow downslope movement of soil or rock debris.

It occurs on moderately steep, soil-covered slopes.

It is usually imperceptible except through long observation.

## 28.2 Evidence Of Creep

Creep may be noticed through:

- leaning fence posts,
- tilted telephone poles,
- bent tree trunks,
- disturbed walls,
- slow downslope displacement.

## 28.3 Types Of Creep

Depending on material, creep may include:

- soil creep,
- talus creep,
- rock creep,
- rock-glacier creep.

## 28.4 Solifluction

Solifluction is slow downslope flow of water-saturated soil mass or fine-grained rock debris.

It is common in moist temperate areas where:

- surface melting of frozen ground occurs,
- long rain saturates upper layers,
- lower layers remain impervious.

## 28.5 Why Solifluction Is Slow

Even though it is a flow, it is usually slow because movement is gradual and occurs in saturated upper material.

It is not grouped with rapid flow movements in normal classification.

---

# 29. Rapid Mass Movements

Rapid movements are mostly found in humid climates.

They may occur on gentle to steep slopes depending on material and water content.

---

# 30. Earthflow

## 30.1 Meaning

Earthflow is downslope movement of water-saturated clayey or silty material.

It occurs on low-angle terraces or hillsides.

## 30.2 Features

Earthflows may create:

- step-like terraces,
- arcuate scarps at heads,
- bulging accumulation at the toe.

## 30.3 Bedrock Sliding

On steeper slopes, soft sedimentary rocks like shale or deeply weathered igneous rocks may also slide downslope.

---

# 31. Mudflow

## 31.1 Meaning

Mudflow is movement of thick water-saturated weathered material as a stream of mud along definite channels.

## 31.2 Conditions

Mudflows occur when:

- vegetation cover is absent,
- heavy rainfall occurs,
- thick weathered material becomes saturated.

## 31.3 Destructive Nature

When mudflows emerge from channels onto piedmont zones or plains, they can engulf:

- roads,
- bridges,
- houses.

## 31.4 Volcanic Slopes

Mudflows often occur on erupting or recently erupted volcanoes.

Volcanic ash, dust, and fragments mix with rainwater and flow downslope.

---

# 32. Debris Avalanche

## 32.1 Meaning

Debris avalanche is a rapid movement of debris down steep slopes.

## 32.2 Conditions

It is common in humid regions, with or without vegetation cover.

It usually occurs in narrow tracks on steep slopes.

## 32.3 Speed

Debris avalanches can be faster than mudflows.

They are similar in behaviour to snow avalanches, but the material is rock and debris rather than snow.

## 32.4 Exercise Point

Debris avalanche can be included in rapid flow mass movements.

---

# 33. Landslides

## 33.1 Meaning

Landslides are relatively rapid and perceptible movements of material downslope.

The material is relatively dry compared with flows.

## 33.2 Controls

The size and shape of the detached mass depend on:

- discontinuities in rock,
- degree of weathering,
- steepness of slope.

---

# 34. Types Of Landslides

## 34.1 Slump

Slump is slipping of one or several units of rock debris with backward rotation.

It occurs along a curved surface.

## 34.2 Debris Slide

Debris slide is rapid rolling or sliding of earth debris without backward rotation.

## 34.3 Debris Fall

Debris fall is nearly free fall of earth debris from a vertical or overhanging face.

## 34.4 Rockslide

Rockslide is sliding of individual rock masses down:

- bedding planes,
- joints,
- fault surfaces.

On steep slopes, rockslides can be fast and destructive.

## 34.5 Rock Fall

Rock fall is free falling of rock blocks from steep slopes.

Rock fall occurs from superficial layers of a rock face.

This distinguishes it from rockslide, which affects material to greater depth.

---

# 35. Mass Movements In Indian Regions

## 35.1 Himalayas

Debris avalanches and landslides are frequent in the Himalayas because:

- the Himalayas are tectonically active,
- rocks are often sedimentary,
- many deposits are unconsolidated or semi-consolidated,
- slopes are very steep.

## 35.2 Nilgiris And Western Ghats

The Nilgiris and Western Ghats are more tectonically stable and have hard rocks.

Yet landslides and debris avalanches occur because:

- many slopes are steep,
- cliffs and escarpments may be nearly vertical,
- mechanical weathering is pronounced,
- heavy rainfall occurs over short periods.

## 35.3 Main Difference

Himalayan landslides are more frequent due to tectonic activity and weak materials.

Western Ghats and Nilgiris have fewer but still serious slope failures due to steep slopes, rock falls, and intense rainfall.

---

# 36. Erosion

## 36.1 Meaning

Erosion involves acquisition and transportation of rock debris.

It is performed by geomorphic agents such as:

- running water,
- groundwater,
- glaciers,
- wind,
- waves.

## 36.2 Weathering And Erosion

Weathering helps erosion by breaking rocks into smaller fragments.

But weathering is not always an absolute precondition for erosion.

Agents can also erode unweathered rock through abrasion and direct force.

## 36.3 Abrasion

Rock debris carried by agents scrapes and wears down surfaces.

This is called abrasion.

Abrasion greatly aids erosion.

## 36.4 Degradational Processes

The following are degradational processes:

- weathering,
- mass wasting,
- erosion.

They wear down relief.

---

# 37. Kinetic Energy And Erosion

## 37.1 Definition

Erosion can be understood as application of kinetic energy of a moving agent to the land surface.

## 37.2 Formula

```text
KE = 1/2 mv^2
```

Where:

- KE = kinetic energy,
- m = mass,
- v = velocity.

## 37.3 Meaning

Erosive work depends on:

- mass of the moving material,
- velocity of movement.

Velocity is especially powerful because it is squared in the formula.

## 37.4 Comparing Agents

| Agent | State | Climate Control | Erosive Note |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Wind | Gas | Climatically controlled | Less effective due to low mass |
| Running water | Liquid | Climatically controlled | Highly effective where discharge and slope are high |
| Glacier | Solid | Climatically controlled | Very effective despite low speed due to huge mass |
| Waves | Water movement | Location controlled | Work occurs along coast |
| Groundwater | Subsurface water | Lithology controlled | Effective in soluble and permeable rocks |

## 37.5 Glaciers vs Wind

Glaciers move slowly, but their mass is huge.

Wind moves faster, but has low mass.

Therefore, glaciers can be more powerful erosional agents than wind in suitable conditions.

---

# 38. Deposition

## 38.1 Meaning

Deposition is the settling of transported material.

It happens when erosional agents lose velocity and energy.

## 38.2 Not A Separate Agent Work

Deposition is a consequence of erosion and transport.

It occurs when the transporting agent can no longer carry its load.

## 38.3 Sequence Of Deposition

Coarser materials are deposited first.

Finer materials are deposited later.

## 38.4 Aggradation

Deposition fills depressions.

This makes agents depositional or aggradational as well as erosional.

## 38.5 Agents Of Deposition

The same agents that erode also deposit:

- running water,
- glaciers,
- wind,
- waves,
- groundwater.

---

# 39. Mass Movement vs Erosion

## 39.1 Similarity

Both involve movement of material from one place to another.

## 39.2 Difference

| Mass Movement | Erosion |
| --- | --- |
| Directly driven by gravity | Driven by mobile geomorphic agents |
| Material moves downslope as a mass | Material is acquired and transported by water, wind, ice, waves, or groundwater |
| No agent carries the debris as the main transporter | Agent carries the debris |
| Includes creep, slump, slide, fall | Includes river, wind, glacial, wave, groundwater erosion |

## 39.3 Best Term

Mass movement is more precise than mass wasting when focusing on the actual downslope motion of material.

---

# 40. Soil And Soil Contents

## 40.1 Meaning Of Soil

A pedologist defines soil as a collection of natural bodies on the earth's surface containing living and/or dead matter and supporting or capable of supporting plants.

## 40.2 Soil Is Dynamic

Soil is not dead material.

It is a dynamic medium where chemical, physical, and biological activities continue.

## 40.3 Soil As Decay And Growth

Soil is a result of decay.

It is also a medium for growth.

## 40.4 Seasonal Change

Soil properties change with seasons.

Soil may become:

- cold or warm,
- dry or moist,
- biologically active or inactive.

Organic matter increases when:

- leaves fall,
- grasses die,
- organisms decompose.

## 40.5 Soil Adjusts To Conditions

Soil adjusts to:

- climate,
- landform,
- vegetation.

If these controls change, soil also changes internally.

---

# 41. Process Of Soil Formation

## 41.1 Pedogenesis

Soil formation is called pedogenesis.

It depends first on weathering.

Weathering mantle provides the basic input for soil formation.

## 41.2 Colonisation Of Weathered Material

Weathered material or transported deposits are first colonised by:

- bacteria,
- mosses,
- lichens,
- minor organisms.

## 41.3 Humus Accumulation

Dead remains of organisms and plants help accumulate humus.

## 41.4 Plant Succession

Gradually:

- minor grasses grow,
- ferns may grow,
- bushes develop,
- trees grow from seeds brought by birds and wind.

## 41.5 Soil Structure Development

Plant roots penetrate downward.

Burrowing animals bring up particles.

The material becomes porous and sponge-like.

It gains capacity to:

- retain water,
- allow air passage,
- support life.

Finally, mature soil forms as a complex mixture of mineral and organic products.

## 41.6 Is Weathering Alone Enough?

No.

Weathering provides material, but soil formation also needs:

- organisms,
- organic matter,
- climate,
- topography,
- time,
- biological activity.

---

# 42. Soil-Forming Factors

Five basic factors control soil formation:

1. parent material,
2. topography,
3. climate,
4. biological activity,
5. time.

These factors act together and influence one another.

---

# 43. Parent Material

## 43.1 Passive Control

Parent material is a passive control factor.

It provides the starting material for soil.

## 43.2 Types

Parent material may be:

- in-situ weathered rock debris,
- transported deposits.

Residual soils develop from in-situ material.

Transported soils develop from transported deposits.

## 43.3 Properties That Matter

Soil formation depends on parent material's:

- texture,
- structure,
- mineral composition,
- chemical composition,
- weathering rate,
- depth of weathering mantle.

## 43.4 Parent Rock Link

Young soils show stronger links with parent rock.

Mature soils may become less directly related because climate and biological processes modify them.

In limestone areas, soils may show strong relation with parent rock because weathering processes are specific.

---

# 44. Topography

## 44.1 Passive Control

Topography is another passive control factor.

It controls:

- exposure to sunlight,
- surface drainage,
- subsurface drainage,
- erosion,
- percolation.

## 44.2 Slope Effect

Soils are thin on steep slopes because erosion removes material.

Soils are thicker on flat uplands where material stays longer.

## 44.3 Gentle Slopes

Gentle slopes favour soil formation because:

- erosion is slow,
- water percolation is good.

## 44.4 Flat Areas

Flat areas may develop:

- thick clay layers,
- good organic matter accumulation,
- darker soil colour.

## 44.5 Aspect

In middle latitudes:

- south-facing slopes receive more sunlight,
- north-facing slopes are cooler and moister.

This creates different vegetation and soils.

---

# 45. Climate

## 45.1 Active Control

Climate is an important active factor in soil formation.

## 45.2 Moisture

Moisture includes:

- precipitation intensity,
- precipitation frequency,
- precipitation duration,
- evaporation,
- humidity.

Moisture enables chemical and biological activity.

## 45.3 Eluviation And Illuviation

Excess water moves soil components downward.

Removal from upper layers is eluviation.

Deposition in lower layers is illuviation.

## 45.4 Wet Equatorial Climates

In wet equatorial climates, high rainfall removes:

- calcium,
- sodium,
- magnesium,
- potassium,
- much silica.

Removal of silica is called desilication.

## 45.5 Dry Climates

In dry climates, evaporation exceeds precipitation.

Groundwater rises by capillary action.

Water evaporates at the surface and leaves salts behind.

These salts may form hardpans.

## 45.6 Intermediate Tropical Climates

In tropical climates with intermediate precipitation, calcium carbonate nodules may form.

These are called kankar.

## 45.7 Temperature

Temperature affects chemical and biological activity.

Higher temperatures generally increase chemical activity.

Cooler temperatures reduce it.

Freezing conditions stop many chemical processes.

Tropical soils often show deeper profiles.

Frozen tundra soils contain more mechanically broken material.

---

# 46. Biological Activity

## 46.1 Role Of Vegetation And Organisms

Vegetation and organisms add:

- organic matter,
- moisture retention,
- nitrogen,
- humus.

## 46.2 Humification

Dead plants provide humus.

Organic acids formed during humification help decompose minerals in parent material.

## 46.3 Climate And Bacteria

Bacterial activity differs between climates.

In cold climates:

- bacterial growth is slow,
- humus accumulates,
- peat layers may develop.

In humid tropical and equatorial climates:

- bacterial activity is intense,
- dead vegetation decomposes rapidly,
- humus content may be low.

## 46.4 Nitrogen Fixation

Some bacteria convert gaseous nitrogen into plant-usable chemical forms.

This is called nitrogen fixation.

Rhizobium bacteria live in root nodules of leguminous plants and fix nitrogen.

## 46.5 Larger Animals

Animals such as:

- ants,
- termites,
- earthworms,
- rodents,

rework soil up and down.

Earthworms change soil texture and chemistry as soil passes through their bodies.

---

# 47. Time

## 47.1 Control Factor

Time controls soil maturity and profile development.

## 47.2 Mature Soil

A soil becomes mature when soil-forming processes operate long enough to develop a clear profile.

## 47.3 Young Soil

Soils developing from recently deposited alluvium or glacial till are young.

They may have:

- no horizons,
- poorly developed horizons.

## 47.4 No Fixed Time

No exact universal time can be fixed for soil maturity.

Soil formation rate depends on all factors acting together.

---

# 48. Soil Formation Process vs Soil-Forming Factors

## 48.1 Soil Formation Process

The process of soil formation means the actual development of soil through:

- weathering,
- colonisation by organisms,
- humus accumulation,
- mineral transformation,
- eluviation,
- illuviation,
- profile development.

## 48.2 Soil-Forming Factors

Soil-forming factors are controls that influence the process.

They include:

- parent material,
- topography,
- climate,
- biological activity,
- time.

## 48.3 Difference

| Soil Formation Process | Soil-Forming Factors |
| --- | --- |
| Actual steps by which soil develops | Conditions controlling those steps |
| Weathering, humus, profile development | Parent material, climate, topography, biology, time |
| Dynamic activity | Controls or influences |

# 49. High-Value Comparisons

## 49.1 Endogenic vs Exogenic

| Endogenic Processes | Exogenic Processes |
| --- | --- |
| Originate inside earth | Originate at or near surface |
| Land-building | Land-wearing |
| Diastrophism, volcanism | Weathering, mass movement, erosion, deposition |
| Driven by internal heat | Driven by sun, atmosphere, gravity, gradients |

## 49.2 Process vs Agent

| Process | Agent |
| --- | --- |
| Force or action affecting earth material | Mobile medium doing work |
| Weathering, erosion, deposition | Running water, wind, glacier, waves |
| Can be chemical or physical action | Usually transports material |

## 49.3 Weathering vs Erosion

| Weathering | Erosion |
| --- | --- |
| Breaks or decomposes rocks in place | Acquires and transports material |
| In-situ process | Movement by agent |
| No major transport | Transport is essential |
| Prepares material for erosion | Removes material |

## 49.4 Physical vs Chemical Weathering

| Physical Weathering | Chemical Weathering |
| --- | --- |
| Mechanical breakage | Chemical decomposition |
| No major chemical change | Mineral composition changes |
| Frost wedging, salt weathering, unloading | Solution, carbonation, hydration, oxidation |
| Common in dry/cold/high-relief areas | Strong in warm, moist climates |

## 49.5 Slow vs Rapid Mass Movement

| Slow Movement | Rapid Movement |
| --- | --- |
| Imperceptible or gradual | Perceptible and quick |
| Creep, solifluction | Earthflow, mudflow, debris avalanche, landslide |
| Needs long observation | Often sudden hazard |
| May bend poles and fences | May destroy roads and houses |

## 49.6 Erosion vs Deposition

| Erosion | Deposition |
| --- | --- |
| Acquisition and transport | Settling of transported material |
| Needs moving agent and energy | Happens when energy declines |
| Degrades relief | Fills depressions |
| Coarser/finer material moved | Coarser deposited first |

# 52. Common Misunderstandings

## 52.1 Weathering And Erosion Are Same

Incorrect.

Weathering breaks rocks in place.

Erosion removes and transports material.

## 52.2 Exogenic Processes Only Destroy

Incomplete.

They wear down elevations, but deposition also fills basins and creates new surfaces.

## 52.3 Deposition Is A Separate Agent

Incorrect.

Deposition happens when erosional agents lose energy.

## 52.4 Mass Movement Is Erosion

Incorrect.

Mass movement is gravity-driven downslope movement. Erosion requires a geomorphic agent transporting material.

## 52.5 Chemical Weathering Can Dominate Without Water

Incorrect.

Water is essential for most chemical weathering. Water-scarce hot deserts favour physical and salt weathering more than chemical weathering.

## 52.6 Physical And Chemical Weathering Always Work Separately

Incorrect.

They usually work together. For example, hydration can chemically change minerals and physically expand them.

## 52.7 Weathering Alone Forms Soil

Incorrect.

Weathering supplies material, but soil formation also needs organisms, climate, organic matter, topography, and time.

# 54. Long Answer Guide

## 54.1 Earth As Playfield Of Opposing Processes

Use this structure:

1. Start with the idea:
   The earth's surface is shaped by endogenic and exogenic forces.

2. Endogenic:
   Internal forces such as diastrophism and volcanism uplift, deform, and build relief.

3. Exogenic:
   External processes such as weathering, mass wasting, erosion, and deposition wear down relief and fill basins.

4. Interaction:
   Endogenic forces create slopes and elevations; exogenic processes act on them.

5. Result:
   Relief remains uneven because building and wearing continue together.

6. Human relevance:
   Understanding these processes helps prevent land degradation and misuse.

## 54.2 Exogenic Processes And Sun's Heat

Use this structure:

1. Exogenic processes act at the surface.
2. Their ultimate energy comes from the sun's heat.
3. Solar energy drives atmosphere, climate, water cycle, temperature changes, winds, precipitation, and vegetation.
4. Tectonic forces create gradients and slopes.
5. Gravity moves materials downslope.
6. Temperature and precipitation control weathering and soil formation.
7. Wind, running water, and glaciers depend strongly on climatic conditions.

## 54.3 Physical And Chemical Weathering Are Interrelated

Use this structure:

1. Physical weathering breaks rocks mechanically.
2. Chemical weathering decomposes minerals chemically.
3. They often assist each other.
4. Physical cracks increase surface area for chemical attack.
5. Chemical weakening makes rocks easier to break mechanically.
6. Hydration causes chemical addition of water but also expansion and physical cracking.
7. Carbonation dissolves limestone, while cracks allow water to penetrate deeper.
8. Therefore, they are not independent in nature.

## 54.4 Soil Formation And Control Factors

Use this structure:

1. Soil formation is the process of developing soil from weathered material.
2. It includes weathering, colonisation by organisms, humus accumulation, mixing, eluviation, illuviation, and profile development.
3. Soil-forming factors are controls: parent material, topography, climate, biological activity, and time.
4. Climate controls moisture, temperature, leaching, biological activity, and chemical reactions.
5. Biological activity adds humus, nitrogen, acids, and soil mixing.
6. Parent material and topography are passive controls; climate and biology are active controls.
7. Time decides maturity and profile development.

# 57. Final Chapter Summary

Geomorphic processes shape the surface of the earth. Endogenic forces such as diastrophism and volcanism build and deform the crust. Exogenic processes such as weathering, mass wasting, erosion, and deposition wear down relief, transport materials, fill depressions, and form soils.

The earth's surface remains uneven because land-building and land-wearing processes operate together. Gravity and gradients are essential because they create movement from higher to lower levels.

Weathering is the in-place breakdown and decomposition of rocks. It may be chemical, physical, or biological. Chemical weathering includes solution, carbonation, hydration, oxidation, and reduction. Physical weathering includes unloading, thermal expansion, frost wedging, and salt weathering. Biological weathering is caused by plants, animals, microorganisms, and human activity.

Mass movements move rock debris downslope directly under gravity. Slow movements include creep and solifluction. Rapid movements include earthflow, mudflow, debris avalanche, and landslides. These are common on steep, weak, wet, or disturbed slopes.

Erosion involves acquisition and transport of material by agents such as running water, groundwater, glaciers, wind, and waves. Deposition occurs when those agents lose energy and drop their load.

Soil formation begins with weathering but also requires organisms, humus, climate, topography, parent material, and time. Climate and biological activity are active controls, while parent material and topography are passive controls.

To master this chapter, connect every landform-changing process with its force, agent, material, climate, slope condition, and result.
