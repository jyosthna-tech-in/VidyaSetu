## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how the earth receives energy from the sun, how that energy is distributed, how the earth-atmosphere system maintains heat balance, and why temperature varies from place to place.

> The sun supplies energy, the earth receives and redistributes it unevenly, and this uneven heating creates temperature differences, pressure differences, winds, and weather variation.

# 1. Atmosphere And Solar Energy

## 1.1 We Live Under A Pile Of Air

We live at the bottom of a huge mass of air.

We inhale and exhale air constantly.

We usually feel air only when it moves as wind.

## 1.2 Atmosphere And Life

The atmosphere is an envelope of gases around the earth.

It supports life on the earth's surface.

It also participates in:

- heating,
- cooling,
- winds,
- clouds,
- rainfall,
- temperature distribution,
- climate.

## 1.3 Source Of Earth's Energy

The earth receives almost all its energy from the sun.

The sun is therefore the primary source of energy for:

- weather,
- climate,
- winds,
- ocean currents,
- evaporation,
- rainfall,
- temperature differences.

## 1.4 Earth Also Radiates Energy

The earth receives energy from the sun.

It also radiates energy back to space.

Because incoming and outgoing energy are balanced over time, the earth does not keep warming endlessly or cooling endlessly.

## 1.5 Unequal Heating

Different parts of the earth receive different amounts of heat.

This unequal heating creates:

- temperature differences,
- pressure differences,
- winds,
- heat transfer from one region to another,
- weather and climate variation.

---

# 2. Solar Radiation And Insolation

## 2.1 Solar Radiation

Solar radiation is energy from the sun.

The earth's surface receives most of this energy in short wavelengths.

## 2.2 Insolation

Insolation means incoming solar radiation.

It is the solar energy received by the earth.

## 2.3 Exam Definition

Use this definition:

> Insolation is the incoming solar radiation received by the earth.

## 2.4 Why Earth Receives Only A Small Portion

The earth is like a sphere.

The sun is very large and far away.

Sun's rays fall on the top of the atmosphere, but the earth intercepts only a very small part of total solar energy.

## 2.5 Average Solar Energy At Top Of Atmosphere

On average, the earth receives:

```text
1.94 calories per square centimetre per minute
```

at the top of its atmosphere.

## 2.6 Shortwave And Longwave

The earth receives solar energy as shortwave radiation.

After being heated, the earth emits energy as longwave terrestrial radiation.

This distinction is important because the atmosphere behaves differently toward shortwave and longwave radiation.

---

# 3. Aphelion And Perihelion

## 3.1 Earth-Sun Distance Changes

The earth revolves around the sun in an elliptical orbit.

Therefore, its distance from the sun changes slightly during the year.

## 3.2 Aphelion

Aphelion is the position when the earth is farthest from the sun.

Important details:

- date: around July 4;
- distance: about 152 million km.

## 3.3 Perihelion

Perihelion is the position when the earth is nearest to the sun.

Important details:

- date: around January 3;
- distance: about 147 million km.

## 3.4 Insolation Difference

The annual insolation received by the earth around January 3 is slightly more than around July 4.

Reason:

- the earth is nearer to the sun at perihelion.

## 3.5 Why This Does Not Control Daily Weather Strongly

The effect of earth-sun distance variation is masked by other factors such as:

- distribution of land and sea;
- atmospheric circulation;
- seasonal tilt effects;
- clouds;
- winds;
- local conditions.

Therefore, this variation does not have a strong direct effect on daily weather at the earth's surface.

## 3.6 Aphelion vs Perihelion

| Basis | Aphelion | Perihelion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meaning | Earth farthest from sun | Earth nearest to sun |
| Date | Around July 4 | Around January 3 |
| Distance | About 152 million km | About 147 million km |
| Insolation | Slightly less | Slightly more |

---

# 4. Variability Of Insolation

## 4.1 Insolation Is Not Uniform

The amount and intensity of insolation vary:

- during a day,
- during a season,
- during a year,
- from place to place.

## 4.2 Factors Controlling Insolation

The main factors are:

1. rotation of the earth on its axis;
2. angle of inclination of the sun's rays;
3. length of the day;
4. transparency of the atmosphere;
5. configuration of land in terms of aspect.

## 4.3 Relative Importance

The chapter notes that transparency of atmosphere and land aspect have less influence than the larger astronomical factors.

The tilt of the earth's axis and latitude strongly influence insolation.

## 4.4 Earth's Axis

The earth's axis makes an angle of 66.5 degrees with the plane of its orbit around the sun.

This tilt controls:

- seasons,
- changing day length,
- sun angle,
- latitudinal differences in insolation.

---

# 5. Rotation Of The Earth

## 5.1 Daily Cycle

The earth rotates on its axis.

This creates day and night.

## 5.2 Effect On Insolation

Insolation varies during the day because:

- sun angle changes from morning to noon to evening;
- noon rays are more direct;
- morning and evening rays are more slant;
- night receives no direct insolation.

## 5.3 Daily Temperature Lag

The highest daily temperature usually does not occur exactly at noon.

Reason:

- the ground continues receiving more energy than it loses for some time after noon;
- temperature peaks later in the afternoon in many places.

---

# 6. Angle Of Inclination Of Sun's Rays

## 6.1 Basic Rule

The angle of the sun's rays strongly controls insolation.

High angle rays heat more.

Low angle or slant rays heat less.

## 6.2 Latitude Effect

The higher the latitude, the smaller the angle made by the rays with the earth's surface.

Thus, rays become more slant toward the poles.

## 6.3 Vertical Rays

Vertical rays cover a smaller surface area.

Their energy is concentrated.

So energy received per unit area is high.

## 6.4 Slant Rays

Slant rays cover a larger surface area.

Their energy is spread out.

So energy received per unit area is low.

## 6.5 Atmospheric Path Length

Slant rays pass through a greater depth of the atmosphere.

This causes more:

- absorption,
- scattering,
- diffusion.

Therefore, less energy reaches the surface.

## 6.6 Vertical vs Slant Rays

| Basis | Vertical Rays | Slant Rays |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Surface area covered | Small | Large |
| Energy concentration | High | Low |
| Atmospheric path | Shorter | Longer |
| Loss in atmosphere | Less | More |
| Heating effect | Stronger | Weaker |

---

# 7. Length Of Day

## 7.1 Why Day Length Matters

Longer days allow more time for receiving insolation.

Shorter days allow less time.

## 7.2 Solstice Day-Length Table

| Latitude | December 22 | June 21 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 degrees | 12 h 00 m | 12 h |
| 20 degrees N | 10 h 48 m | 13 h 12 m |
| 40 degrees N | 9 h 08 m | 14 h 52 m |
| 60 degrees N | 5 h 33 m | 18 h 27 m |
| 90 degrees N | 0 h | 6 months |

## 7.3 Meaning Of Table

At the equator, day length is almost constant.

Toward the poles, seasonal variation in day length increases.

At the North Pole:

- there is no daylight on December 22;
- there is continuous daylight around June solstice.

## 7.4 Day Length And Temperature

Longer daylight increases potential heating.

Shorter daylight reduces potential heating.

This is one reason high latitudes have strong seasonal temperature variation.

---

# 8. Transparency Of Atmosphere

## 8.1 Meaning

Atmospheric transparency means how much solar radiation can pass through the atmosphere.

## 8.2 What Reduces Transparency

Transparency is reduced by:

- clouds,
- dust,
- water vapour,
- smoke,
- pollution,
- aerosols,
- fog.

## 8.3 Effect On Insolation

If atmosphere is clear, more insolation reaches the surface.

If atmosphere is cloudy or dusty, less insolation reaches the surface.

## 8.4 Example

Subtropical deserts receive high insolation partly because cloudiness is low.

---

# 9. Aspect Of Land

## 9.1 Meaning

Aspect means the direction a slope faces.

## 9.2 Effect On Insolation

Slopes facing the sun receive more insolation.

Slopes facing away from the sun receive less.

## 9.3 Local Importance

Aspect matters especially in:

- hills,
- mountains,
- valleys,
- agricultural slopes,
- settlements in cold regions.

---

# 10. Passage Of Solar Radiation Through Atmosphere

## 10.1 Atmosphere And Shortwave Radiation

The atmosphere is largely transparent to shortwave solar radiation.

This allows incoming solar radiation to pass through before reaching the earth's surface.

## 10.2 Absorption In Troposphere

Within the troposphere:

- water vapour,
- ozone,
- other gases

absorb much of the near-infrared radiation.

## 10.3 Scattering

Very small suspended particles in the troposphere scatter visible radiation.

Scattered radiation goes:

- back to space;
- toward the earth's surface;
- in different directions.

## 10.4 Colour Of Sky

Scattering gives colour to the sky.

Blue colour of the sky is due to scattering of shorter wavelengths.

Red colour of rising and setting sun is due to scattering when sunlight travels through a longer atmospheric path.

## 10.5 Sunrise And Sunset

At sunrise and sunset, solar rays travel through a thicker layer of atmosphere.

Much of the shorter wavelength light is scattered away.

Longer red and orange wavelengths dominate.

This is why the sun appears red or orange.

# 11. Spatial Distribution Of Insolation

## 11.1 Range

Insolation received at the surface varies from about:

```text
320 Watt per square metre in the tropics
```

to about:

```text
70 Watt per square metre near the poles
```

## 11.2 Maximum Insolation

Maximum insolation is received over subtropical deserts.

Reason:

- least cloudiness;
- clear skies;
- strong sun;
- dry air.

## 11.3 Equator vs Tropics

The equator receives comparatively less insolation than the tropics in many cases.

Reason:

- greater cloudiness;
- higher humidity;
- frequent convection and rainfall.

## 11.4 Continents vs Oceans

At the same latitude, insolation is generally more over continents than over oceans.

Reasons may include:

- cloud differences;
- surface heating differences;
- atmospheric moisture differences.

## 11.5 Seasonal Difference

In winter, middle and higher latitudes receive less radiation than in summer.

Reasons:

- lower sun angle;
- shorter days;
- longer atmospheric path of rays.

# 12. Heating And Cooling Of Atmosphere

## 12.1 Main Processes

The atmosphere is heated and cooled through:

1. conduction;
2. convection;
3. advection;
4. terrestrial radiation.

## 12.2 Important Principle

The atmosphere is mainly heated from below.

The sun heats the earth's surface.

The heated earth then heats the air above it.

---

# 13. Conduction

## 13.1 Meaning

Conduction is heat transfer between bodies in direct contact.

## 13.2 Process

When two bodies of unequal temperature touch each other, heat flows from the warmer body to the cooler body.

The transfer continues until:

- both reach the same temperature;
- or contact is broken.

## 13.3 Atmosphere Example

The earth's surface is heated by insolation.

Air in contact with the land surface is then heated by conduction.

## 13.4 Importance

Conduction is important in heating the lower layers of the atmosphere.

It is strongest very close to the surface.

---

# 14. Convection

## 14.1 Meaning

Convection is vertical transfer of heat by rising air currents.

## 14.2 Process

Formation sequence:

1. earth's surface is heated;
2. air touching the surface gets heated;
3. heated air expands and becomes lighter;
4. lighter air rises vertically;
5. heat is carried upward;
6. cooler air may sink to replace it.

## 14.3 Where It Occurs

Convective transfer of energy is confined mainly to the troposphere.

## 14.4 Importance

Convection is important for:

- vertical heat transfer;
- cloud formation;
- thunderstorms;
- instability in lower atmosphere;
- tropical weather.

---

# 15. Advection

## 15.1 Meaning

Advection is horizontal transfer of heat by movement of air.

## 15.2 Importance

Horizontal movement of air is often more important than vertical movement for daily weather changes.

## 15.3 Middle Latitudes

In middle latitudes, much day-to-day weather variation is caused by advection.

Warm or cold air masses move horizontally and change local temperature.

## 15.4 Indian Example

In northern India, the hot summer wind called loo is an outcome of advection.

Hot air moves horizontally and raises local temperature.

## 15.5 Convection vs Advection

| Basis | Convection | Advection |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direction | Vertical | Horizontal |
| Medium | Rising/sinking air currents | Moving air masses or winds |
| Example | Heated air rising | Loo in north India |
| Main effect | Transfers heat upward | Transfers heat from one region to another |

---

# 16. Terrestrial Radiation

## 16.1 Meaning

Terrestrial radiation is longwave radiation emitted by the earth.

## 16.2 Process

Process chain:

1. earth receives shortwave insolation;
2. earth's surface heats up;
3. heated earth becomes a radiating body;
4. earth emits longwave radiation;
5. atmosphere absorbs much of this radiation;
6. atmosphere gets heated from below.

## 16.3 Greenhouse Gases

Longwave terrestrial radiation is absorbed especially by:

- carbon dioxide;
- other greenhouse gases;
- water vapour.

## 16.4 Indirect Heating

The atmosphere is indirectly heated by earth's radiation.

The sun first heats the surface.

The surface then heats the atmosphere.

## 16.5 Final Balance

The atmosphere also radiates and transmits heat to space.

This helps maintain temperature balance.

---

# 17. Heat Budget Of The Earth

## 17.1 Meaning

Heat budget means the balance between incoming solar radiation and outgoing terrestrial radiation.

The earth maintains a balance over time.

## 17.2 Why Heat Budget Is Needed

If incoming energy were greater than outgoing energy forever, the earth would keep warming.

If outgoing energy were greater than incoming energy forever, the earth would keep cooling.

Since neither happens continuously, there must be a heat balance.

## 17.3 100-Unit Model

To understand the heat budget, imagine that 100 units of solar radiation reach the top of the atmosphere.

## 17.4 Reflected Energy

About 35 units are reflected back to space before heating the earth's surface.

This reflected radiation is called the earth's albedo.

Breakup:

- 27 units reflected by clouds;
- 2 units reflected by snow and ice-covered areas;
- 6 units scattered or reflected by the atmosphere.

## 17.5 Absorbed Energy

The remaining 65 units are absorbed.

Breakup:

- 14 units absorbed within the atmosphere;
- 51 units absorbed by the earth's surface.

## 17.6 Earth's Outgoing Radiation

The earth radiates back 51 units as terrestrial radiation.

Of these:

- 17 units go directly to space;
- 34 units are absorbed by the atmosphere.

## 17.7 The 34 Units Absorbed By Atmosphere

The 34 units absorbed by the atmosphere include:

- 6 units absorbed directly by the atmosphere;
- 9 units through convection and turbulence;
- 19 units through latent heat of condensation.

## 17.8 Atmosphere's Outgoing Radiation

The atmosphere has 48 units to send back to space.

This is:

```text
14 units absorbed from insolation + 34 units absorbed from terrestrial radiation = 48 units
```

## 17.9 Final Balance

Total outgoing radiation:

```text
17 units directly from earth + 48 units from atmosphere = 65 units
```

Total absorbed radiation:

```text
65 units
```

Therefore:

```text
65 units absorbed = 65 units returned to space
```

This is the heat balance of the earth.

## 17.10 Heat Budget Table

| Radiation Path | Units |
| --- | --- |
| Incoming at top of atmosphere | 100 |
| Reflected back to space | 35 |
| Absorbed by atmosphere directly | 14 |
| Absorbed by earth surface | 51 |
| Earth radiates back | 51 |
| Directly lost to space from earth | 17 |
| Absorbed by atmosphere from earth radiation and processes | 34 |
| Atmosphere returns to space | 48 |
| Total returned to space after absorption | 65 |

## 17.11 Albedo

Albedo is the reflected amount of radiation.

High albedo surfaces reflect more.

Examples:

- snow,
- ice,
- bright clouds.

Low albedo surfaces absorb more.

Examples:

- dark soil,
- forests,
- water under some conditions.

# 18. Variation In Net Heat Budget

## 18.1 Net Radiation Balance

Net radiation balance means the difference between energy received and energy lost.

## 18.2 Surplus And Deficit

Some parts of the earth have surplus radiation balance.

Other parts have deficit radiation balance.

## 18.3 Surplus Region

There is a surplus of net radiation balance roughly between 40 degrees north and 40 degrees south.

## 18.4 Deficit Region

Regions near the poles have a radiation deficit.

## 18.5 Heat Redistribution

Surplus heat from the tropics is redistributed poleward.

This happens through:

- winds,
- ocean currents,
- atmospheric circulation.

## 18.6 Why Redistribution Matters

Because heat is redistributed:

- tropics do not become progressively hotter;
- high latitudes do not become permanently frozen due only to deficit;
- global temperature balance is maintained.

# 19. Heat And Temperature

## 19.1 Heat

Heat represents molecular movement of particles in a substance.

More molecular movement means more heat energy.

## 19.2 Temperature

Temperature is the measurement in degrees of how hot or cold something is.

It may refer to:

- a thing,
- air,
- land,
- water,
- a place.

## 19.3 Heat vs Temperature

| Basis | Heat | Temperature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meaning | Energy related to molecular movement | Degree of hotness or coldness |
| Unit idea | Energy | Degrees Celsius or other temperature scale |
| Example | Amount of energy in air or water | 30 degrees Celsius air temperature |

## 19.4 Specific Heat

Specific heat is the energy needed to raise the temperature of one gram of a substance by one degree Celsius.

## 19.5 Planck's Law

Planck's law is summarised in this chapter as:

> The hotter a body, the more energy it radiates and the shorter the wavelength of that radiation.

This helps explain why the sun emits shortwave radiation and the cooler earth emits longwave radiation.

# 20. Factors Controlling Temperature Distribution

## 20.1 Main Factors

The temperature of air at a place is influenced by:

1. latitude;
2. altitude;
3. distance from the sea;
4. air-mass circulation;
5. warm and cold ocean currents;
6. local aspects.

---

# 21. Latitude

## 21.1 Basic Idea

Temperature depends strongly on insolation.

Since insolation varies by latitude, temperature also varies by latitude.

## 21.2 Low Latitudes

Low latitudes receive more direct sun rays.

They generally have higher temperatures.

## 21.3 High Latitudes

High latitudes receive more slant rays.

They generally have lower temperatures.

## 21.4 Seasonal Effect

Latitude also affects length of day and seasonal temperature differences.

High latitudes have greater seasonal day-length variation.

---

# 22. Altitude

## 22.1 Basic Idea

The atmosphere is heated from below by terrestrial radiation.

Therefore, places near sea level usually record higher temperatures than places at higher elevations.

## 22.2 Normal Lapse Rate

Temperature generally decreases with height.

This rate of decrease is called the normal lapse rate.

## 22.3 Value

Normal lapse rate:

```text
6.5 degrees Celsius per 1000 metres
```

## 22.4 Example

If two places are at the same latitude but one is on a mountain and the other is near sea level, the mountain place is usually cooler.

---

# 23. Distance From The Sea

## 23.1 Land And Sea Heat Differently

Land heats quickly and cools quickly.

Sea heats slowly and cools slowly.

## 23.2 Coastal Areas

Places near the sea have moderate temperature.

Reason:

- sea breezes and land breezes moderate temperature;
- sea water changes temperature slowly.

## 23.3 Continental Interiors

Places far from the sea have extreme temperature.

They may have:

- hot summers;
- cold winters;
- high annual range of temperature.

## 23.4 Continentality

Continentality means strong temperature contrast caused by distance from the sea.

The Siberian plains are a major example.

---

# 24. Air Masses And Ocean Currents

## 24.1 Air Masses

Air masses affect temperature by bringing air from different source regions.

Warm air masses raise temperature.

Cold air masses lower temperature.

## 24.2 Ocean Currents

Ocean currents affect coastal temperatures.

Warm ocean currents raise temperatures along nearby coasts.

Cold ocean currents lower temperatures along nearby coasts.

## 24.3 Example Logic

A coast influenced by a warm current is warmer than another coast at similar latitude influenced by a cold current.

## 24.4 Local Aspects

Local aspects include:

- slope direction,
- urban surfaces,
- vegetation,
- valley position,
- exposure to sun,
- local winds.

They create local temperature differences.

---

# 25. Distribution Of Temperature

## 25.1 How Distribution Is Studied

Global temperature distribution is commonly studied through maps for:

- January;
- July.

These months represent major seasonal contrasts.

## 25.2 Isotherms

Isotherms are lines joining places of equal temperature.

They are used to show temperature distribution on maps.

## 25.3 General Pattern

Temperature generally decreases from equator toward poles.

Therefore, isotherms are often roughly parallel to latitudes.

## 25.4 Deviations

Isotherms do not always follow latitudes perfectly.

They bend due to:

- land and sea contrast;
- ocean currents;
- altitude;
- air masses;
- seasonal heating and cooling.

## 25.5 Northern vs Southern Hemisphere

The northern hemisphere has more land.

The southern hemisphere has more ocean.

Therefore:

- northern hemisphere shows stronger seasonal and land-sea contrasts;
- southern hemisphere shows smoother, more parallel isotherms.

---

# 26. January Temperature Distribution

## 26.1 General Situation

In January, it is winter in the northern hemisphere and summer in the southern hemisphere.

## 26.2 Northern Hemisphere

In the northern hemisphere, isotherms deviate strongly because of large landmasses.

Land cools quickly in winter.

Oceans cool slowly.

## 26.3 Bending Of Isotherms

In January:

- isotherms bend northward over oceans;
- isotherms bend southward over continents.

## 26.4 North Atlantic Example

Warm ocean currents such as the Gulf Stream and North Atlantic Drift make the North Atlantic warmer.

Therefore, isotherms bend toward the north over the North Atlantic.

## 26.5 Europe And Siberia

Over land, temperature decreases sharply.

Isotherms bend southward over Europe and especially over the Siberian plain.

## 26.6 January Temperature Examples

The chapter gives these examples:

- mean January temperature along 60 degrees E longitude is about -20 degrees Celsius at both 80 degrees N and 50 degrees N;
- equatorial oceans have mean January temperature above 27 degrees Celsius;
- tropics have temperatures above 24 degrees Celsius;
- middle latitudes may have about 2 degrees Celsius to 0 degrees Celsius;
- Eurasian continental interior may record about -18 degrees Celsius to -48 degrees Celsius.

## 26.7 Southern Hemisphere In January

The oceanic influence is strong in the southern hemisphere.

Isotherms are more or less parallel to latitudes.

Temperature variation is more gradual.

Examples:

- 20 degrees Celsius isotherm runs roughly near 35 degrees S;
- 10 degrees Celsius isotherm near 45 degrees S;
- 0 degrees Celsius isotherm near 60 degrees S.

---

# 27. July Temperature Distribution

## 27.1 General Situation

In July, it is summer in the northern hemisphere and winter in the southern hemisphere.

## 27.2 General Pattern

Isotherms generally run parallel to latitudes.

But land-sea contrast still affects them.

## 27.3 Equatorial Oceans

Equatorial oceans record temperatures above 27 degrees Celsius.

## 27.4 Subtropical Continental Asia

Over land, especially subtropical continental Asia, temperatures above 30 degrees Celsius occur along about 30 degrees N latitude.

Reason:

- strong summer heating;
- continentality;
- clear skies in many regions;
- high insolation.

## 27.5 Southern Hemisphere

In the southern hemisphere, ocean influence keeps isotherms more regular.

Temperatures are generally lower in higher southern latitudes during July because it is winter there.

---

# 28. Annual Range Of Temperature

## 28.1 Meaning

Annual range of temperature is the difference between the mean temperature of the warmest month and the mean temperature of the coldest month.

## 28.2 Formula

```text
Annual range = Mean temperature of warmest month - Mean temperature of coldest month
```

## 28.3 Highest Range

The highest annual range of temperature is more than 60 degrees Celsius over the north-eastern part of the Eurasian continent.

Reason:

- strong continentality;
- very cold winters;
- warm summers;
- distance from sea;
- land heats and cools rapidly.

## 28.4 Least Range

The least annual range is about 3 degrees Celsius between 20 degrees S and 15 degrees N.

Reason:

- low-latitude location;
- oceanic influence in many areas;
- less seasonal variation in sun angle and day length.

# 29. Inversion Of Temperature

## 29.1 Normal Lapse Rate

Normally, temperature decreases with height.

This is called normal lapse rate.

## 29.2 Meaning Of Inversion

At times, the normal pattern reverses.

Temperature increases with height for a layer near the surface.

This is called inversion of temperature.

## 29.3 Duration

Temperature inversion is usually of short duration.

But it is common in many places.

## 29.4 Ideal Conditions

Ideal conditions for inversion:

- long winter night;
- clear sky;
- still air;
- strong loss of heat from ground by radiation.

## 29.5 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. during the day, ground receives heat;
2. at night, ground radiates heat away;
3. under clear skies, heat loss is strong;
4. still air prevents mixing;
5. ground becomes colder than air above;
6. air near ground becomes colder;
7. warmer air remains above;
8. inversion forms.

## 29.6 Polar Areas

Over polar areas, temperature inversion is normal throughout the year.

Reason:

- intense surface cooling;
- low sun angle;
- snow and ice surfaces;
- long periods of darkness.

---

# 30. Effects Of Inversion

## 30.1 Stability

Surface inversion promotes stability in the lower atmosphere.

Cold dense air remains near the surface.

Warm air above prevents vertical mixing.

## 30.2 Smoke And Dust

Smoke and dust particles collect beneath the inversion layer.

They spread horizontally in the lower atmosphere.

## 30.3 Fog

Dense morning fogs are common during winter inversions.

## 30.4 Duration

Inversion commonly lasts for a few hours.

It usually breaks after sunrise when the sun warms the ground.

## 30.5 Pollution Problem

Inversion can trap pollutants near the surface.

This can worsen air quality in cities and valleys.

# 31. Air Drainage In Hills And Mountains

## 31.1 Meaning

Air drainage is downslope movement of cold dense air under gravity.

## 31.2 Formation

At night, hill and mountain slopes cool.

Air in contact with the slopes becomes cold and dense.

This cold air flows downslope like water.

It collects in pockets and valley bottoms.

## 31.3 Temperature Arrangement

During air drainage:

- cold air lies in valley bottoms;
- warmer air may remain above;
- inversion develops in valleys.

## 31.4 Effect On Plants

Air drainage can protect plants on slopes from frost damage because cold air drains away downslope.

However, valley bottoms may become frost-prone because cold air accumulates there.

# 32. India: Why Maximum Day Temperature In May?

## 32.1 The Question

In India, why is day temperature maximum in May and not after the summer solstice?

## 32.2 Core Reason

May is a pre-monsoon month with strong heating, clear skies, dry land, and limited cloud cover over large parts of India.

## 32.3 After Summer Solstice

After the June solstice, the monsoon begins to influence India.

Clouds, rainfall, and moist winds reduce daytime heating.

## 32.4 Answer Structure

Use these points:

- high sun angle before monsoon;
- intense land heating;
- dry surface heats quickly;
- clear skies allow strong insolation;
- monsoon clouds and rain after June reduce temperature;
- maritime air moderates heat.

# 33. Siberian Plains: High Annual Range

## 33.1 Main Reason

The Siberian plains have a high annual range of temperature because of continentality.

## 33.2 Why Continentality Matters

Siberia is far from the moderating influence of oceans.

Land heats quickly in summer and cools sharply in winter.

## 33.3 Winter Condition

Winter temperatures fall very low because:

- long nights;
- low sun angle;
- snow cover;
- distance from sea;
- strong radiational cooling.

## 33.4 Summer Condition

Summer temperatures rise more because land heats quickly under longer daylight.

## 33.5 Result

The difference between warmest and coldest months becomes very large.

This creates high annual range.

# 34. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 34.1 Confusing Solar Radiation And Terrestrial Radiation

Solar radiation:

- comes from the sun;
- shortwave.

Terrestrial radiation:

- comes from the earth;
- longwave.

## 34.2 Thinking Earth Is Hottest At Perihelion Everywhere

Earth is nearest to the sun around January 3.

But seasons are controlled mainly by tilt and sun angle, not distance alone.

## 34.3 Confusing Convection And Advection

Convection is vertical heat transfer.

Advection is horizontal heat transfer.

## 34.4 Forgetting The 100-Unit Heat Budget

Remember:

- 35 reflected;
- 65 absorbed;
- 51 absorbed by surface;
- 14 absorbed by atmosphere;
- 17 direct to space;
- 48 from atmosphere to space.

## 34.5 Confusing Albedo And Absorption

Albedo is reflected radiation.

Absorption is energy taken in.

## 34.6 Thinking Equator Always Gets Maximum Insolation

Subtropical deserts may receive more insolation because they have less cloudiness.

## 34.7 Confusing Normal Lapse Rate And Inversion

Normal lapse rate:

- temperature decreases with height.

Inversion:

- temperature increases with height near the surface.

# 37. Long Answer Guide

## 37.1 Latitude, Tilt, And Radiation Received

Use this structure:

1. Start with the earth's shape and tilt.

The earth is spherical and its axis is tilted at 66.5 degrees to the plane of its orbit.

This causes variation in sun angle and day length.

2. Explain latitude.

Low latitudes receive more direct rays, while high latitudes receive slant rays.

Direct rays concentrate energy over a smaller area.

Slant rays spread energy over a larger area.

3. Explain atmospheric path.

Slant rays pass through a greater depth of atmosphere.

This causes more absorption, scattering, and diffusion.

4. Explain day length.

Tilt causes day length to vary with season and latitude.

Longer days receive more insolation.

Shorter days receive less.

5. Explain solstice variation.

At June solstice, the northern hemisphere has longer days and the sun is overhead at 23.5 degrees N.

At December solstice, the southern hemisphere receives more direct rays.

6. Conclude.

Thus latitude and axial tilt control both intensity and duration of insolation.

## 37.2 Heat Balance Of Earth-Atmosphere System

Use this structure:

1. Start with 100 units.

Assume 100 units of solar radiation arrive at the top of the atmosphere.

2. Explain reflection.

35 units are reflected back to space by clouds, snow/ice, and atmosphere.

This is albedo.

3. Explain absorption.

65 units are absorbed: 14 by atmosphere and 51 by earth's surface.

4. Explain terrestrial radiation.

Earth radiates 51 units as longwave radiation.

17 units go directly to space.

34 units are absorbed by the atmosphere.

5. Explain atmosphere's radiation.

The atmosphere radiates 48 units to space, which includes 14 absorbed from insolation and 34 from terrestrial radiation and related processes.

6. Show balance.

17 units directly from earth plus 48 units from atmosphere equals 65 units returned to space.

7. Conclude.

Thus 65 units absorbed are balanced by 65 units lost, so the earth neither keeps warming nor keeps cooling.

## 37.3 January Temperature Distribution In Northern And Southern Hemispheres

Use this structure:

1. State the seasonal contrast.

In January, the northern hemisphere has winter and the southern hemisphere has summer.

2. Northern hemisphere.

The northern hemisphere has more land.

Land cools quickly in winter, so continental interiors become very cold.

Isotherms deviate strongly.

They bend northward over oceans and southward over continents.

The North Atlantic remains warmer due to Gulf Stream and North Atlantic Drift.

Siberia becomes extremely cold.

3. Southern hemisphere.

The southern hemisphere has more ocean.

Oceans moderate temperature.

Isotherms are more parallel to latitudes.

Temperature changes gradually.

4. Conclude.

January temperature distribution is more irregular in the northern hemisphere and more regular in the southern hemisphere because of land-sea contrast.

# 41. Final Chapter Summary

The earth receives almost all its energy from the sun. Incoming solar radiation is called insolation. The earth receives this energy mainly as shortwave radiation and emits energy back as longwave terrestrial radiation. Because the earth is spherical and tilted, insolation varies with latitude, angle of rays, length of day, season, atmospheric transparency, and land aspect.

The earth is farthest from the sun on July 4, called aphelion, and nearest on January 3, called perihelion. Although perihelion brings slightly more solar energy, this effect is masked by land-sea distribution and atmospheric circulation, so it does not control daily weather strongly.

The atmosphere is heated through conduction, convection, advection, and terrestrial radiation. The atmosphere is mainly heated from below because the earth absorbs insolation and radiates longwave energy. The heat budget explains how incoming and outgoing energy remain balanced. In the 100-unit model, 35 units are reflected, 65 are absorbed, and 65 are eventually returned to space.

Temperature distribution is controlled by latitude, altitude, distance from sea, air masses, ocean currents, and local aspects. Isotherms show lines of equal temperature. The northern hemisphere has stronger seasonal contrasts because it has more land, while the southern hemisphere has smoother temperature distribution because it has more ocean.

Temperature inversion occurs when the normal lapse rate is reversed and temperature increases with height near the surface. It commonly forms on clear, calm winter nights and in valleys due to cold air drainage. It traps smoke, dust, fog, and pollutants near the surface.

To master this chapter, connect sun angle, day length, surface type, atmosphere, heat transfer, and temperature distribution into one chain.
