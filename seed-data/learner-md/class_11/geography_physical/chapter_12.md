# 1. Why Study World Climate?

## 1.1 Climate Data Is Complex

The world has many climates.

They differ by:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- seasonality,
- humidity,
- wind systems,
- vegetation,
- latitude,
- altitude,
- distance from sea.

Classification helps organise this complexity.

## 1.2 Purpose Of Classification

Climate classification helps us:

- describe climate regions;
- compare places;
- understand vegetation distribution;
- analyse agriculture potential;
- study human settlement patterns;
- discuss climate change;
- simplify large climate data sets.

## 1.3 Three Approaches

Three broad approaches to classifying climate are:

1. empirical;
2. genetic;
3. applied.

---

# 2. Climate Classification Approaches

## 2.1 Empirical Classification

Empirical classification is based on observed data.

Main data used:

- temperature;
- precipitation.

Koeppen's scheme is an empirical classification.

## 2.2 Genetic Classification

Genetic classification organises climates according to causes.

It asks:

- Why does this climate occur?
- What circulation system controls it?
- What air masses influence it?
- What pressure belts affect it?

## 2.3 Applied Classification

Applied classification is made for a specific purpose.

Examples:

- agricultural climate classification;
- building design climate classification;
- water-resource climate classification;
- disease-risk climate classification.

## 2.4 Empirical vs Genetic

| Basis | Empirical Classification | Genetic Classification |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main basis | Observed data | Causes of climate |
| Main variables | Temperature and precipitation | Radiation, winds, pressure, air masses |
| Example | Koeppen | Circulation-based systems |
| Question asked | What is the climate like? | Why does this climate occur? |

---

# 3. Koeppen's Classification

## 3.1 Why Koeppen's Scheme Is Important

Koeppen's climate classification is the most widely used climatic classification system.

It is popular because it connects climate with vegetation.

## 3.2 Basis

Koeppen identified a close relationship between:

- climate;
- natural vegetation.

He selected values of temperature and precipitation and related them to vegetation distribution.

## 3.3 Type Of Classification

Koeppen's system is empirical.

It is based on:

- mean annual temperature;
- mean monthly temperature;
- mean annual precipitation;
- mean monthly precipitation.

## 3.4 Letter Codes

Koeppen used capital and small letters to designate climatic groups and types.

## 3.5 History

The scheme was developed in 1918 and modified over time.

It remains popular and widely used.

---

# 4. Koeppen Climatic Groups

## 4.1 Major Groups

Koeppen recognised five major climatic groups.

Four are mainly based on temperature.

One is based on precipitation.

## 4.2 Main Group Table

| Group | Name | Main Characteristic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | Tropical | Average temperature of coldest month is 18 degrees Celsius or higher |
| B | Dry climates | Potential evaporation exceeds precipitation |
| C | Warm temperate mid-latitude climates | Coldest month above -3 degrees Celsius but below 18 degrees Celsius |
| D | Cold snow forest climates | Coldest month is -3 degrees Celsius or below |
| E | Cold climates | Average temperature for all months below 10 degrees Celsius |
| H | Highland | Cold due to elevation |

## 4.3 Humid And Dry Groups

Capital letters A, C, D, and E represent humid climates.

Capital letter B represents dry climates.

H represents highland climates controlled by elevation.

## 4.4 Small Letters For Dry Seasons

Small letters indicate seasonality of precipitation:

| Letter | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| f | No dry season |
| m | Monsoon climate |
| w | Winter dry season |
| s | Summer dry season |

## 4.5 Temperature Severity Letters

Small letters a, b, c, and d refer to degree of temperature severity.

They help further distinguish climate types.

## 4.6 Dry Climate Subdivision

B dry climates are subdivided using:

| Letter | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| S | Steppe or semi-arid |
| W | Desert |

# 5. Group A: Tropical Humid Climates

## 5.1 Location

Tropical humid climates exist between:

- Tropic of Cancer;
- Tropic of Capricorn.

## 5.2 Why They Are Hot And Humid

They are hot and humid because:

- the sun is overhead for much of the year;
- insolation is high;
- the ITCZ influences these regions;
- air rises and rainfall is common;
- moisture availability is high.

## 5.3 Temperature

Annual range of temperature is very low.

Temperature is high throughout the year.

## 5.4 Rainfall

Annual rainfall is high.

Rainfall seasonality differs by type.

## 5.5 Three Types

Group A is divided into:

1. Af: tropical wet climate;
2. Am: tropical monsoon climate;
3. Aw: tropical wet and dry climate.

---

# 6. Tropical Wet Climate: Af

## 6.1 Location

Tropical wet climate is found near the equator.

Major areas:

- Amazon Basin in South America;
- western equatorial Africa;
- islands of East Indies.

## 6.2 Rainfall

Significant rainfall occurs in every month.

Rainfall often occurs as afternoon thundershowers.

## 6.3 Temperature

Temperature is uniformly high.

Annual range of temperature is negligible.

Daily temperature examples:

- maximum around 30 degrees Celsius;
- minimum around 20 degrees Celsius.

## 6.4 Vegetation

Tropical evergreen forests occur here.

They have:

- dense canopy cover;
- large biodiversity.

## 6.5 Memory Line

Af means:

```text
Always wet, always warm, evergreen forest.
```

---

# 7. Tropical Monsoon Climate: Am

## 7.1 Location

Tropical monsoon climate occurs in:

- Indian subcontinent;
- north-eastern part of South America;
- northern Australia.

## 7.2 Rainfall

Heavy rainfall occurs mostly in summer.

Winter is dry.

## 7.3 Cause

Monsoon wind reversal brings seasonal rainfall.

## 7.4 India Link

The chapter refers learners to the India: Physical Environment book for a detailed account of this climate type.

## 7.5 Memory Line

Am means:

```text
Monsoon rain in summer, short dry season in winter.
```

# 8. Tropical Wet And Dry Climate: Aw

## 8.1 Location

Aw climate occurs north and south of Af climate regions.

It borders:

- dry climates on the western part of continents;
- Cf or Cw climates on the eastern part.

## 8.2 Major Areas

Extensive Aw climate occurs:

- north and south of Amazon forest in Brazil;
- adjoining parts of Bolivia and Paraguay;
- Sudan;
- south of Central Africa.

## 8.3 Rainfall

Annual rainfall is less than Af and Am.

Rainfall is also more variable.

Wet season is shorter.

Dry season is longer.

Drought is more severe.

## 8.4 Temperature

Temperature remains high throughout the year.

Diurnal range of temperature is greatest in the dry season.

## 8.5 Vegetation

Vegetation includes:

- deciduous forest;
- tree-shredded grasslands.

## 8.6 Memory Line

Aw means:

```text
Hot climate with wet summer and dry winter.
```

# 9. Group B: Dry Climates

## 9.1 Main Characteristic

Dry climates have very low rainfall.

Rainfall is not adequate for plant growth.

## 9.2 Technical Condition

In B climates:

```text
Potential evaporation exceeds precipitation.
```

## 9.3 Latitudinal Extent

Dry climates cover large areas from:

```text
15 degrees to 60 degrees north and south of the equator
```

## 9.4 Low-Latitude Dry Areas

From 15 degrees to 30 degrees, dry climates occur in subtropical high-pressure areas.

Reason:

- sinking air;
- subsidence;
- temperature inversion;
- little rainfall.

## 9.5 Western Margins And Cold Currents

On western margins of continents, dry climates extend more equatorward where cold currents occur.

Example:

- west coast of South America.

## 9.6 Middle-Latitude Dry Areas

Between 35 degrees and 60 degrees, dry climates occur in continental interiors.

Reasons:

- maritime humid winds do not reach;
- areas may be surrounded by mountains;
- distance from oceans reduces moisture.

## 9.7 Main Types

Dry climates are divided into:

- steppe or semi-arid climates: BS;
- desert climates: BW.

## 9.8 Subtypes

| Code | Meaning | Latitude |
| --- | --- | --- |
| BSh | Subtropical steppe | 15-35 degrees |
| BWh | Subtropical desert | 15-35 degrees |
| BSk | Mid-latitude steppe | 35-60 degrees |
| BWk | Mid-latitude desert | 35-60 degrees |

# 10. Subtropical Steppe And Desert: BSh And BWh

## 10.1 Common Features

Subtropical steppe and subtropical desert have similar precipitation and temperature characteristics.

Both have:

- low rainfall;
- high variability of rainfall;
- high summer temperature;
- high annual temperature range;
- high diurnal temperature range.

## 10.2 Steppe

Subtropical steppe lies in the transition zone between humid and dry climates.

It receives slightly more rainfall than desert.

Rainfall is enough for sparse grasslands.

## 10.3 Desert

Subtropical desert receives very low rainfall.

Rain often occurs as short, intense thundershowers.

Such rain is often ineffective in building soil moisture.

## 10.4 Rainfall Variability

Rainfall variability affects steppe life strongly.

Reason:

- steppe areas depend on limited rainfall for grasslands and livestock;
- failure of rain can cause famine.

## 10.5 Coastal Deserts

Fog is common in coastal deserts bordering cold currents.

## 10.6 High Temperature Example

The highest shade temperature of 58 degrees Celsius was recorded at Al Aziziyah, Libya, on 13 September 1922.

## 10.7 Steppe vs Desert

| Basis | Steppe | Desert |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rainfall | Slightly more | Very low |
| Vegetation | Sparse grassland | Very sparse xerophytic vegetation |
| Moisture | Semi-arid | Arid |
| Risk | Drought and famine | Soil moisture rarely builds |

# 13. Mediterranean Climate: Cs

## 13.1 Location

Mediterranean climate occurs:

- around the Mediterranean Sea;
- west coasts of continents in subtropical latitudes between 30 degrees and 40 degrees.

Examples:

- Central California;
- Central Chile;
- south-eastern Australia;
- south-western Australia;
- Mediterranean basin.

## 13.2 Seasonal Controls

These areas come under:

- subtropical high pressure in summer;
- westerly winds in winter.

## 13.3 Climate Character

Mediterranean climate has:

- hot dry summer;
- mild rainy winter.

## 13.4 Temperature

Monthly average temperature:

- summer around 25 degrees Celsius;
- winter below 10 degrees Celsius.

## 13.5 Rainfall

# 14. Humid Subtropical Climate: Cfa

## 14.1 Location

Cfa lies on eastern parts of continents in subtropical latitudes.

Areas include:

- eastern United States;
- southern and eastern China;
- southern Japan;
- northeastern Argentina;
- coastal South Africa;
- eastern coast of Australia.

## 14.2 Rainfall

Air masses are generally unstable and cause rainfall throughout the year.

Annual precipitation:

```text
75 cm to 150 cm
```

## 14.3 Seasonal Weather

Common features:

- thunderstorms in summer;
- frontal precipitation in winter.

## 14.4 Temperature

Mean monthly temperature:

- summer around 27 degrees Celsius;
- winter between 5 degrees and 12 degrees Celsius.

Daily range of temperature is small.

# 15. Marine West Coast Climate: Cfb

## 15.1 Location

Marine west coast climate is located poleward from the Mediterranean climate on the west coasts of continents.

Main areas:

- northwestern Europe;
- west coast of North America north of California;
- southern Chile;
- southeastern Australia;
- New Zealand.

## 15.2 Marine Influence

Because of ocean influence, temperature is moderate.

Mean temperature:

- summer months: 15 degrees to 20 degrees Celsius;
- winter months: 4 degrees to 10 degrees Celsius.

Annual and daily temperature ranges are small.

Precipitation occurs throughout the year.

Annual precipitation varies greatly:

```text
50 cm to 250 cm
```

# 25. Climate Change

## 25.1 Climate Is Not Fixed

The climate we experience now may have prevailed broadly during the last 10,000 years, but with minor and occasionally wide fluctuations.

The earth has witnessed many climate variations since its beginning.

## 25.2 Natural And Continuous Process

Climate change is natural and continuous.

But modern human activity has become an important additional cause.

# 26. Evidence Of Past Climate Change

## 26.1 Geological Records

Geological records show alteration of:

- glacial periods;
- interglacial periods.

## 26.2 Geomorphological Evidence

High-altitude and high-latitude landforms show traces of:

- glacier advances;
- glacier retreats.

## 26.3 Lake Sediments

Sediment deposits in glacial lakes reveal warm and cold periods.

## 26.4 Tree Rings

Tree rings provide clues about:

- wet periods;
- dry periods.

## 26.5 Historical Records

Historical records describe climate variability through:

- crop yields;
- crop failures;
- floods;
- droughts;
- migration of people.

## 26.6 India Example

Archaeological findings show that Rajasthan desert experienced wet and cool climate around 8000 B.C.

The period from 3000 to 1700 B.C. had higher rainfall.

From about 2000 to 1700 B.C., this region was a centre of Harappan civilisation.

Dry conditions became stronger later.

## 26.7 Geological Time Examples

The earth was warm about 500 to 300 million years ago through the Cambrian, Ordovician, and Silurian periods.

During the Pleistocene epoch, glacial and interglacial periods occurred.

The last major peak glacial period was about 18,000 years ago.

The present interglacial period started about 10,000 years ago.

## 27.1 Climate Variability

Climate variability occurs all the time.

The 1990s recorded:

- warmest temperature of the century;

# 28. Causes Of Climate Change

## 28.1 Two Major Groups

Causes of climate change can be grouped into:

1. astronomical causes;
2. terrestrial causes.

Human activities are now an important anthropogenic cause.

---

# 29. Astronomical Causes

## 29.1 Solar Output And Sunspots

Changes in solar output are associated with sunspot activity.

Sunspots are dark and cooler patches on the sun.

They increase and decrease cyclically.

## 29.2 Sunspot Weather Hypothesis

Some meteorologists suggest:

- increase in sunspots may be linked with cooler, wetter, stormier weather;
- decrease in sunspots may be linked with warmer, drier conditions.

However, these findings are not statistically significant.

## 29.3 Milankovitch Oscillations

Milankovitch oscillations refer to cycles in:

- earth's orbital characteristics around the sun;
- wobbling of the earth;
- changes in earth's axial tilt.

## 29.4 Climate Link

These cycles alter the amount of insolation received from the sun.

This may influence climate.

---

# 30. Terrestrial Causes

## 30.1 Volcanism

Volcanism is considered a cause of climate change.

## 30.2 How Volcanoes Affect Climate

Volcanic eruptions throw aerosols into the atmosphere.

These aerosols remain for a considerable period.

They reduce solar radiation reaching the earth's surface.

## 30.3 Cooling Effect

After the Pinatubo and El Chichon volcanic eruptions, average earth temperature fell to some extent for some years.

## 30.4 Human Influence

The most important anthropogenic effect is the increasing concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

This is likely to cause global warming.

---

# 31. Greenhouse Effect

## 31.1 Basic Meaning

The atmosphere behaves like a greenhouse because greenhouse gases absorb longwave radiation emitted by the earth.

## 31.2 Radiation Process

Process chain:

1. incoming solar radiation reaches the earth;
2. earth's surface absorbs it;
3. earth emits longwave radiation upward;
4. greenhouse gases absorb much of this longwave radiation;
5. atmosphere warms.

## 31.3 Greenhouse Analogy

A greenhouse in cold areas is made of glass.

Glass allows shortwave solar radiation to enter.

It blocks much outgoing longwave radiation.

The inside becomes warmer than outside.

## 31.4 Vehicle Example

A closed car or bus becomes hotter in summer than outside air.

In winter, a closed vehicle remains warmer than outside.

This is another example of greenhouse-like heat trapping.

# 32. Greenhouse Gases

## 32.1 Main Greenhouse Gases

Primary greenhouse gases of concern today:

- carbon dioxide: CO2;
- chlorofluorocarbons: CFCs;
- methane: CH4;
- nitrous oxide: N2O;
- ozone: O3.

Other gases such as nitric oxide and carbon monoxide can react with greenhouse gases and affect their concentration.

## 32.2 Effectiveness Of Greenhouse Gases

Effectiveness depends on:

- magnitude of concentration increase;
- lifetime in atmosphere;
- wavelength of radiation absorbed.

## 32.3 CFCs

CFCs are highly effective greenhouse gases.

They are products of human activity.

## 32.4 Ozone

Ozone absorbs ultraviolet radiation in the stratosphere.

But when ozone is present in the lower troposphere, it is very effective in absorbing terrestrial radiation.

## 32.5 Lifetime

The longer a greenhouse gas molecule stays in the atmosphere, the longer the atmospheric system takes to recover from changes.

# 33. Carbon Dioxide

## 33.1 Largest Concentration

Carbon dioxide has the largest concentration among greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

## 33.2 Sources

CO2 emissions come mainly from fossil fuel combustion:

- oil;
- gas;
- coal.

## 33.3 Sinks

Forests and oceans are sinks for carbon dioxide.

Forests use CO2 in their growth.

## 33.4 Deforestation

Deforestation increases CO2 concentration because:

- fewer trees absorb CO2;
- land-use change may release stored carbon.

## 33.5 Adjustment Time

Atmospheric CO2 takes about 20 to 50 years to adjust to changes in sources and sinks.

## 33.6 Rate Of Rise

CO2 is rising at about 0.5 per cent annually.

## 33.7 Climate Model Index

Doubling of CO2 concentration over pre-industrial level is used as an index for estimating climate changes in climate models.

# 34. Ozone Hole And CFCs

## 34.1 Stratospheric Ozone

In the stratosphere, ultraviolet rays convert oxygen into ozone.

Ozone absorbs ultraviolet rays and prevents them from reaching the earth's surface in full intensity.

## 34.2 CFC Damage

CFCs drift into the stratosphere.

They destroy ozone.

## 34.3 Ozone Hole

Large depletion of ozone occurs over Antarctica.

The depletion of ozone concentration in the stratosphere is called the ozone hole.

## 34.4 Effect

Ozone depletion allows more ultraviolet rays to pass through the atmosphere.

This is a serious environmental concern.

# 36. Global Warming

## 36.1 Meaning

Global warming refers to the warming of the earth due to increasing concentration of greenhouse gases.

## 36.2 Why It Is Difficult To Reverse

Once global warming sets in, it is difficult to reverse because greenhouse gases remain in the atmosphere for long periods and affect the earth-atmosphere system slowly.

## 36.3 Uneven Effects

The effects of global warming may not be uniform everywhere.

Some regions may warm more.

Some may experience rainfall changes, droughts, floods, or sea-level impacts.

## 36.4 Possible Impacts

Possible impacts include:

- melting of glaciers;
- melting of ice caps;
- thermal expansion of sea water;
- rise in sea level;
- inundation of coastal areas;
- island flooding;
- social problems;
- pressure on life-support systems.

## 36.5 Need For Lifestyle Change

The chapter emphasises that the world community must respond to climate change and adopt a lifestyle that leaves a liveable world for future generations.

# 38. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 38.1 Calling Koeppen Genetic

Koeppen's classification is empirical, not genetic.

It uses observed temperature and precipitation data.

## 38.2 Confusing A And B Climates

A climates are tropical humid.

B climates are dry.

## 38.3 Forgetting B Is Based On Precipitation

B climates are identified because potential evaporation exceeds precipitation.

## 38.4 Confusing Cs And Cfa

Cs is Mediterranean: dry hot summer and rainy winter.

Cfa is humid subtropical with no dry season.

## 38.5 Confusing Tundra And Ice Cap

Tundra has low-growing vegetation and no true summer.

Ice cap has perennial ice and below-freezing summer temperature.

## 38.6 Treating Climate Change As Only Modern

Climate has changed naturally throughout earth history.

Modern global warming is strongly linked with rising greenhouse gases from human activity.

## 38.7 Confusing Ozone Layer And Greenhouse Ozone

Stratospheric ozone protects from ultraviolet radiation.

Tropospheric ozone is a greenhouse gas.

## 38.8 Forgetting CO2 Sinks

Forests and oceans absorb carbon dioxide.

Deforestation reduces this sink capacity.

# 45. Final Chapter Summary

World climates are classified to organise climate information for easier understanding and comparison. The three approaches are empirical, genetic, and applied. Koeppen's classification is empirical because it uses observed temperature and precipitation data and relates them to natural vegetation.

Koeppen's major groups are A tropical, B dry, C warm temperate, D cold snow forest, E cold, and H highland. A climates are hot and humid and include Af, Am, and Aw. B climates are dry because potential evaporation exceeds precipitation and include steppe and desert types. C climates are warm temperate and include Mediterranean, humid subtropical, and marine west coast types. D climates are cold continental climates. E climates include tundra and ice cap climates. Highland climates vary strongly with elevation.

Climate change is natural and continuous, shown by geological records, glacial landforms, lake sediments, tree rings, and historical records. Causes include astronomical changes such as sunspots and Milankovitch oscillations, terrestrial causes such as volcanism, and human causes such as rising greenhouse gas concentration.

Greenhouse gases absorb outgoing longwave radiation and warm the atmosphere. Major greenhouse gases include carbon dioxide, CFCs, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Carbon dioxide is mainly emitted by fossil fuel burning, while forests and oceans act as sinks. CFCs damage stratospheric ozone and contribute to ozone depletion.

Global warming is the long-term warming linked to increasing greenhouse gases. It can cause glacier melting, sea-level rise, coastal inundation, and stress on life-support systems. International efforts such as the Kyoto Protocol were created to reduce emissions and respond to climate change.
