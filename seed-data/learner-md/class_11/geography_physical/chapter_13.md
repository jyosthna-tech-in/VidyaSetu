# 1. Water And Life

## 1.1 Water Is Essential

Water is essential for all life forms on earth.

Next to air, water is one of the most important elements required for life.

## 1.2 Earth As A Water Planet

Earth has abundant water on its surface.

This is why it is called the Blue Planet.

## 1.3 Water In The Solar System

The chapter notes that water is rare in the solar system.

Earth is fortunate because it has abundant surface water.

## 1.4 Uneven Distribution

Water is not distributed evenly.

Some places have plenty of water.

Other places have very little water.

## 1.5 Core Human Concern

Renewable water on earth is constant, but human demand is increasing.

This leads to water crisis in different places and seasons.

River pollution further worsens the crisis.

---

# 2. Hydrological Cycle

## 2.1 Meaning

The hydrological cycle describes the movement of water on, in, and above the earth.

## 2.2 Definition

The hydrological cycle is the circulation of water within the earth's hydrosphere in different forms:

- liquid;
- solid;
- gaseous.

## 2.3 Continuous Exchange

It is the continuous exchange of water between:

- oceans;
- atmosphere;
- land surface;
- subsurface;
- organisms.

## 2.4 Time Scale

The water cycle has been working for billions of years.

All life on earth depends on it.

## 2.5 Major Processes

Important processes include:

- evaporation;
- evapotranspiration;
- sublimation;
- condensation;
- precipitation;
- snowmelt runoff;
- surface runoff;
- stream flow;
- infiltration;
- groundwater storage;
- groundwater discharge;
- springs.

## 2.6 Basic Cycle Chain

```text
Ocean water -> evaporation -> water vapour -> condensation -> clouds -> precipitation -> runoff/infiltration -> rivers/groundwater -> ocean
```

## 2.7 Why It Is Cyclic

Water is a cyclic resource because it can be used and reused.

Water changes location and form, but it continues circulating.

# 3. Distribution Of Water On Earth

## 3.1 Major Fact

About 71 per cent of planetary water is found in the oceans.

## 3.2 Water Reservoir Table

| Reservoir | Volume | Percentage Of Total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Oceans | 1370 million cubic km | 97.25 |
| Ice caps and glaciers | 29 million cubic km | 2.05 |
| Groundwater | 9.5 million cubic km | 0.68 |
| Lakes | 0.125 million cubic km | 0.01 |
| Soil moisture | 0.065 million cubic km | 0.005 |
| Atmosphere | 0.013 million cubic km | 0.001 |
| Streams and rivers | 0.0017 million cubic km | 0.0001 |
| Biosphere | 0.0006 million cubic km | 0.00004 |

## 3.3 Meaning Of The Table

Most water is saline ocean water.

Freshwater is a very small part of total water.

Most freshwater is stored in ice caps, glaciers, and groundwater.

Rivers and streams contain a very tiny fraction of total water.

## 3.4 Rainfall On Land

Nearly 59 per cent of water that falls on land returns to the atmosphere through evaporation from oceans and other places.

The remainder:

- runs off over the surface;
- infiltrates into the ground;
- becomes part of glaciers in some areas.

---

# 4. Interrelation Of Hydrological Cycle Elements

## 4.1 Processes Are Connected

No element of the hydrological cycle works alone.

Each process leads into another.

## 4.2 Evaporation And Condensation

Evaporation adds water vapour to the atmosphere.

Condensation changes water vapour into droplets or ice crystals.

## 4.3 Condensation And Precipitation

Condensation forms clouds.

When droplets or crystals grow heavy enough, precipitation occurs.

## 4.4 Precipitation And Runoff

Precipitation falling on land may:

- flow as surface runoff;
- enter streams;
- become stream flow;
- reach rivers;
- return to oceans.

## 4.5 Precipitation And Infiltration

Some precipitation infiltrates into the ground.

It becomes soil moisture or groundwater.

## 4.6 Groundwater And Springs

Groundwater may be stored underground.

It may later discharge through springs or feed streams.

## 4.7 Snow And Ice

In cold areas, water may be stored as snow and ice.

Snowmelt later contributes runoff to streams.

## 4.8 Organisms

Plants return water to the atmosphere through transpiration.

Living organisms also store small amounts of water.

## 4.9 Long Answer Chain

```text
Evaporation + transpiration -> atmospheric water vapour -> condensation -> precipitation -> runoff + infiltration + storage -> discharge -> ocean -> evaporation again
```

# 5. Relief Of The Ocean Floor

## 5.1 Ocean Basins

Oceans are confined to great depressions in the earth's outer layer.

## 5.2 Five Oceans

The oceanic part of earth is divided into five oceans:

1. Pacific Ocean;
2. Atlantic Ocean;
3. Indian Ocean;
4. Southern Ocean;
5. Arctic Ocean.

Seas, bays, gulfs, and other inlets are parts of these large oceans.

## 5.3 Ocean Floor Is Not Flat

The ocean floor has complex and varied features like land surfaces.

It includes:

- mountain ranges;
- trenches;
- plains;
- ridges;
- canyons;
- volcanic features.

## 5.4 Depth Range

A major portion of the ocean floor lies between:

```text
3 km and 6 km below sea level
```

## 5.5 Formation Processes

Ocean-floor features are formed by:

- tectonic processes;
- volcanic processes;
- depositional processes.

# 7. Continental Shelf

## 7.1 Meaning

Continental shelf is the extended margin of a continent occupied by relatively shallow seas and gulfs.

## 7.2 Depth And Gradient

It is the shallowest part of the ocean.

Average gradient:

```text
1 degree or less
```

## 7.3 Shelf Break

The shelf usually ends at a steep slope called the shelf break.

## 7.4 Width

Average width of continental shelves:

```text
about 80 km
```

But width varies greatly.

## 7.5 Narrow Or Absent Shelves

Shelves are almost absent or very narrow along:

- coasts of Chile;
- west coast of Sumatra.

## 7.6 Largest Shelf

The Siberian shelf in the Arctic Ocean is the largest in the world.

It stretches up to:

```text
1500 km in width
```

## 7.7 Depth Variation

Shelf depth may vary from:

- as shallow as 30 m;
- to as deep as 600 m.

## 7.8 Sediments

Continental shelves are covered by sediments brought by:

- rivers;
- glaciers;
- wind;
- waves;
- currents.

## 7.9 Economic Importance

Massive sedimentary deposits over long periods become sources of fossil fuels.

Therefore continental shelves are important for petroleum and natural gas.

# 8. Continental Slope

## 8.1 Meaning

Continental slope connects the continental shelf with the ocean basin.

## 8.2 Beginning

It begins where the bottom of the continental shelf drops sharply into a steep slope.

## 8.3 Gradient

Gradient of continental slope varies between:

```text
2 degrees and 5 degrees
```

## 8.4 Depth

Depth varies between:

```text
200 m and 3000 m
```

## 8.5 Continental Boundary

The slope boundary indicates the end of the continents.

## 8.6 Features

Canyons and trenches may be observed in this region.

---

# 9. Deep Sea Plain

## 9.1 Meaning

Deep sea plains are gently sloping areas of ocean basins.

## 9.2 Character

They are the flattest and smoothest regions of the world.

## 9.3 Depth

Depth varies between:

```text
3000 m and 6000 m
```

## 9.4 Sediments

They are covered with fine-grained sediments such as:

- clay;
- silt.

# 10. Oceanic Deeps Or Trenches

## 10.1 Meaning

Oceanic deeps or trenches are the deepest parts of the oceans.

## 10.2 Shape

Trenches are:

- steep-sided;
- narrow;
- deep basins.

## 10.3 Depth Relation

They are about:

```text
3 km to 5 km deeper than surrounding ocean floor
```

## 10.4 Location

They occur:

- at bases of continental slopes;
- along island arcs.

## 10.5 Tectonic Importance

Trenches are associated with:

- active volcanoes;
- strong earthquakes;
- plate movements.

Therefore, they are significant in studying plate tectonics.

## 10.6 Number Explored

As many as 57 deeps had been explored according to the chapter.

Distribution:

- 32 in Pacific Ocean;
- 19 in Atlantic Ocean;
- 6 in Indian Ocean.

# 12. Mid-Oceanic Ridges

## 12.1 Meaning

A mid-oceanic ridge is an underwater mountain system.

## 12.2 Structure

It is composed of two chains of mountains separated by a large depression.

## 12.3 Height

Mountain peaks may be as high as:

```text
2500 m
```

Some peaks rise above the ocean surface.

## 12.4 Example

Iceland is part of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge.

# 13. Seamounts, Guyots, Submarine Canyons, And Atolls

## 13.1 Seamount

A seamount is an underwater mountain with a pointed summit that does not reach the ocean surface.

Seamounts are volcanic in origin.

They may be:

```text
3000 m to 4500 m tall
```

Example:

- Emperor seamount, extension of Hawaiian Islands in Pacific Ocean.

## 13.2 Submarine Canyon

Submarine canyons are deep valleys under the sea.

Some are comparable to the Grand Canyon of the Colorado River.

They may cut across continental shelves and slopes.

They often extend from mouths of large rivers.

Example:

- Hudson Canyon.

## 13.3 Guyot

A guyot is a flat-topped seamount.

Guyots show evidence of gradual subsidence and flattening.

More than 10,000 seamounts and guyots are estimated to exist in the Pacific Ocean alone.

## 13.4 Atoll

An atoll is a low island in tropical oceans made of coral reefs around a central depression.

The central depression may be:

- part of the sea as a lagoon;
- a body of fresh water;
- brackish water;
- highly saline water.

# 14. Ocean Temperature

## 14.1 Basic Idea

Ocean waters are heated by solar energy, just like land.

But ocean water heats and cools more slowly than land.

## 14.2 What This Section Studies

Ocean temperature is studied in two ways:

- spatial or horizontal variation;
- vertical variation with depth.

---

# 15. Factors Affecting Ocean Temperature

## 15.1 Main Factors

Factors affecting ocean temperature distribution:

1. latitude;
2. unequal distribution of land and water;
3. prevailing winds;
4. ocean currents;
5. enclosed or open sea conditions.

## 15.2 Latitude

Surface water temperature decreases from equator toward poles.

Reason:

- insolation decreases poleward.

## 15.3 Land And Water Distribution

Oceans in the northern hemisphere receive more heat because they are in contact with a larger extent of land.

This creates temperature differences between northern and southern hemisphere oceans.

## 15.4 Prevailing Winds

Winds blowing from land toward oceans drive warm surface water away from the coast.

Cold water rises from below.

This is called upwelling.

It lowers coastal water temperature.

Onshore winds pile warm water near coasts.

This raises coastal temperature.

## 15.5 Ocean Currents

Warm currents raise temperature in cold areas.

Cold currents lower temperature in warm areas.

Examples:

- Gulf Stream warms eastern coast of North America and western coast of Europe;
- Labrador Current lowers temperature near north-east coast of North America.

## 15.6 Enclosed Seas

Enclosed seas in low latitudes record relatively higher temperature than open seas.

Enclosed seas in high latitudes have lower temperature than open seas.

---

# 16. Horizontal Distribution Of Ocean Temperature

## 16.1 Average Surface Temperature

Average temperature of surface ocean water is about:

```text
27 degrees Celsius
```

## 16.2 Latitudinal Decrease

Temperature gradually decreases from equator toward poles.

Average rate:

```text
0.5 degrees Celsius per latitude
```

## 16.3 Temperature By Latitude

| Latitude | Average Temperature |
| --- | --- |
| Near equator/low latitudes | Around 27 degrees Celsius |
| 20 degrees | Around 22 degrees Celsius |
| 40 degrees | Around 14 degrees Celsius |
| Near poles | Around 0 degrees Celsius |

## 16.4 Northern And Southern Hemisphere

Oceans in the northern hemisphere record relatively higher temperature than those in the southern hemisphere.

Average annual temperatures:

- northern hemisphere: about 19 degrees Celsius;
- southern hemisphere: about 16 degrees Celsius.

## 16.5 Highest Temperature Zone

Highest ocean temperature is not recorded exactly at the equator but slightly north of it.

Reason:

- unequal land-water distribution;
- ocean-current and atmospheric effects.

---

# 17. Vertical Distribution Of Ocean Temperature

## 17.1 Surface Is Warmest

Maximum ocean temperature is always at the surface.

Reason:

- surface directly receives solar heat.

## 17.2 Heat Transfer Downward

Heat is transmitted to lower ocean layers mainly through convection.

## 17.3 Temperature Decreases With Depth

Temperature decreases with depth, but the rate is not uniform.

Temperature falls rapidly up to about:

```text
200 m depth
```

After that, the rate of decrease slows.

## 17.4 Thermocline

Thermocline is the boundary region where temperature decreases rapidly with depth.

It usually begins around:

```text
100 m to 400 m below sea surface
```

and extends several hundred metres downward.

## 17.5 Deep Ocean

About 90 per cent of total ocean-water volume lies below the thermocline.

In deep ocean, temperatures approach:

```text
0 degrees Celsius
```

---

# 18. Three-Layer Temperature Structure

## 18.1 Where It Occurs

Middle and low latitude oceans generally have a three-layer temperature structure.

## 18.2 First Layer

The first layer is the warm top layer.

Thickness:

```text
about 500 m
```

Temperature:

```text
20 degrees to 25 degrees Celsius
```

In tropical regions, this layer exists throughout the year.

In mid-latitudes, it develops mainly during summer.

## 18.3 Second Layer

The second layer is the thermocline.

It shows rapid decrease in temperature with depth.

Thickness:

```text
500 m to 1000 m
```

## 18.4 Third Layer

The third layer is very cold.

It extends down to the deep ocean floor.

## 18.5 Polar Oceans

In Arctic and Antarctic circles, surface water temperatures are close to 0 degrees Celsius.

Temperature change with depth is slight.

Only one layer of cold water exists from surface to deep ocean floor.

# 19. Salinity Of Ocean Waters

## 19.1 Meaning

Salinity is the total content of dissolved salts in seawater.

## 19.2 Measurement

Salinity is calculated as the amount of salt in grams dissolved in:

```text
1000 grams or 1 kg of seawater
```

## 19.3 Unit

Salinity is usually expressed as:

- parts per thousand;
- ppt.

## 19.4 Brackish Water Limit

Salinity of:

```text
24.7 ppt
```

has been considered the upper limit to demarcate brackish water.

## 19.5 Importance

Salinity is important because it affects:

- density of seawater;
- ocean circulation;
- freezing point;
- marine life;
- stratification of ocean water.

# 21. Factors Affecting Ocean Salinity

## 21.1 Evaporation And Precipitation

Surface salinity depends mainly on evaporation and precipitation.

High evaporation increases salinity.

High precipitation decreases salinity.

## 21.2 River Inflow

Freshwater flow from rivers reduces salinity in coastal regions.

## 21.3 Freezing And Thawing

In polar regions, freezing and thawing affect salinity.

Freezing may increase salinity of remaining water.

Melting ice may reduce salinity.

## 21.4 Wind

Wind transfers water from one area to another.

This can influence salinity distribution.

## 21.5 Ocean Currents

Ocean currents contribute to salinity variations by moving water masses.

## 21.6 Temperature And Density

Salinity, temperature, and density are interrelated.

Changes in temperature or density can influence salinity behaviour and ocean-water stratification.

# 22. Horizontal Distribution Of Salinity

## 22.1 Open Ocean Range

Normal open-ocean salinity ranges between:

```text
33 ppt and 37 ppt
```

## 22.2 High Salinity Water Bodies

Examples:

| Water Body | Salinity |
| --- | --- |
| Lake Van, Turkey | 330 ppt |
| Dead Sea | 238 ppt |
| Great Salt Lake | 220 ppt |

## 22.3 Red Sea

In the landlocked Red Sea, salinity may be as high as:

```text
41 ppt
```

## 22.4 Estuaries And Arctic

In estuaries and the Arctic, salinity may fluctuate seasonally from:

```text
0 ppt to 35 ppt
```

## 22.5 Hot And Dry Regions

In hot and dry regions with high evaporation, salinity may sometimes reach:

```text
70 ppt
```

## 22.6 Pacific Ocean

Pacific salinity variation is influenced by its shape and large areal extent.

In western parts of the northern hemisphere, salinity decreases from about 35 ppt to 31 ppt because of inflow of melted Arctic water.

After 15 to 20 degrees south, it decreases to about 33 ppt.

## 22.7 Atlantic Ocean

Average salinity of Atlantic Ocean is around:

```text
36 ppt
```

Highest salinity is recorded between 15 and 20 degrees latitudes.

Maximum salinity of about 37 ppt is observed between:

- 20 degrees N and 30 degrees N;
- 20 degrees W and 60 degrees W.

It gradually decreases northward.

## 22.8 North Sea And Baltic Sea

North Sea records higher salinity despite higher latitude because saline water is brought by the North Atlantic Drift.

Baltic Sea has low salinity because large amounts of river water enter it.

## 22.9 Mediterranean And Black Sea

Mediterranean Sea records higher salinity due to high evaporation.

Black Sea has very low salinity due to enormous freshwater influx from rivers.

## 22.10 Indian Ocean

Bay of Bengal has low salinity because of river-water influx.

Arabian Sea has higher salinity because of:

- high evaporation;
- low freshwater influx.

# 23. Vertical Distribution Of Salinity

## 23.1 Salinity Changes With Depth

Salinity changes with depth, but the pattern depends on location.

## 23.2 Surface Salinity

Surface salinity increases when water is lost by:

- evaporation;
- freezing.

Surface salinity decreases when freshwater is added by:

- rivers;
- rainfall;
- melting ice.

## 23.3 Deep Salinity

Salinity at depth is more fixed.

Reason:

- water is not easily lost at depth;
- salt is not easily added at depth.

## 23.4 Surface And Deep Difference

There is a marked difference between salinity of surface zones and deep zones.

## 23.5 Halocline

Salinity generally increases with depth.

The distinct zone where salinity increases sharply is called the halocline.

## 23.6 Density Relation

Increasing salinity increases seawater density if other factors remain constant.

High-salinity water generally sinks below low-salinity water.

This creates stratification by salinity.

# 26. Long Answer Guide

## 26.1 Interrelation Of Hydrological Cycle Elements

Use this structure:

1. Define hydrological cycle.

It is the continuous circulation of water in liquid, solid, and gaseous forms through oceans, atmosphere, land, subsurface, and organisms.

2. Explain evaporation and transpiration.

Water from oceans and land evaporates, while plants add water vapour through transpiration.

3. Explain condensation and precipitation.

Water vapour cools and condenses into clouds, then returns as precipitation.

4. Explain runoff and infiltration.

Rainwater flows as surface runoff into streams or infiltrates into the ground.

5. Explain storage.

Water is stored in oceans, glaciers, groundwater, lakes, soil moisture, atmosphere, rivers, and living organisms.

6. Explain return.

Groundwater discharge and stream flow return water to rivers and oceans.

7. Conclude.

Thus every element is linked, and water continuously changes form and location.

## 26.2 Factors Influencing Ocean Temperature

Use this structure:

1. Latitude.

Surface water temperature decreases from equator to poles because insolation decreases poleward.

2. Land-water distribution.

Northern hemisphere oceans are relatively warmer because of greater contact with land.

3. Winds.

Offshore winds push warm surface water away and cause upwelling of cold water.

Onshore winds pile warm water near coasts.

4. Ocean currents.

Warm currents raise temperature in cold areas, while cold currents reduce temperature in warm areas.

5. Enclosed seas.

Low-latitude enclosed seas are warmer than open seas, while high-latitude enclosed seas are colder.

6. Vertical structure.

Temperature decreases with depth. The thermocline marks rapid decrease, and deep waters approach 0 degrees Celsius.

# 30. Final Chapter Summary

Water is essential for life, and earth is called the Blue Planet because of its abundant surface water. The hydrological cycle continuously circulates water through oceans, atmosphere, land, subsurface, ice, and organisms. Most of the earth's water is in oceans, while usable freshwater is limited.

The ocean floor has complex relief. Major divisions include continental shelf, continental slope, deep sea plain, and oceanic deeps. Continental shelves are shallow submerged margins and are important for fossil fuels. Continental slopes mark the end of continents. Deep sea plains are flat and smooth, while trenches are the deepest parts and are linked with plate movements.

Minor ocean relief features include mid-oceanic ridges, seamounts, submarine canyons, guyots, and atolls. These features show that ocean basins are shaped by tectonic, volcanic, and depositional processes.

Ocean temperature varies horizontally and vertically. Surface temperature decreases from equator to poles and is affected by land-water distribution, winds, currents, and enclosed seas. With depth, temperature falls rapidly in the thermocline and then changes slowly in deep water.

Salinity is the dissolved salt content of seawater, expressed in parts per thousand. It is controlled by evaporation, precipitation, river inflow, freezing, thawing, winds, and currents. Salinity generally increases with depth through the halocline, and high-salinity water is denser and tends to sink.
