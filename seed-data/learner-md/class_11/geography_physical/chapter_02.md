## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how the universe, stars, solar system, earth, moon, atmosphere, oceans, and life evolved.

It begins with early ideas about the origin of the earth, moves to modern theories of the universe, and then explains the formation of planets and the transformation of the earth from a hot, barren body into a planet with atmosphere, water, and life.

The central idea is:

> Earth did not begin as the life-supporting planet we know today. It evolved through cosmic, geological, atmospheric, hydrological, and biological processes over billions of years.

Humans have always wondered about stars, planets, and the universe.

Questions such as these led to scientific investigation:

- How did stars form?
- How did planets form?
- How old is the earth?
- How did oceans and atmosphere develop?
- How did life begin?

## 1.2 Scope Of This Chapter

This chapter explains:

# 2. Early Theories Of Earth's Origin

Many philosophers and scientists proposed ideas about the origin of the earth.

Early theories focused mainly on the origin of the earth and planets.

Later theories moved toward the origin of the entire universe.

---

# 3. Nebular Hypothesis

## 3.1 Main Scholars

The nebular hypothesis was first associated with Immanuel Kant.

It was later revised by Laplace in 1796.

## 3.2 Main Idea

The nebular hypothesis suggested that planets formed from a cloud of material associated with a young, slowly rotating sun.

This cloud-like material is called a nebula.

## 3.3 Importance

The nebular hypothesis was important because it connected planet formation with a rotating cloud of gas and dust.

It became the base for later modified theories of planetary formation.

## 3.4 Limitation

Early versions could not fully explain all observed features of the solar system.

Later scientists revised the idea with better physical reasoning.

---

# 4. Chamberlain-Moulton And Binary Theories

## 4.1 Chamberlain And Moulton

In 1900, Chamberlain and Moulton proposed that a wandering star came close to the sun.

Its gravitational pull separated a cigar-shaped extension of material from the solar surface.

As the star moved away, the separated material continued to revolve around the sun.

This material later condensed into planets.

## 4.2 Jeans And Jeffreys

Sir James Jeans and Sir Harold Jeffreys supported similar arguments.

These explanations involved the effect of another star or companion-like object.

## 4.3 Binary Theories

Theories that considered the sun to have a companion or another star-like influence are called binary theories.

## 4.4 Basic Difference From Nebular Hypothesis

| Nebular Hypothesis | Chamberlain-Moulton Type Theory |
| --- | --- |
| Planets formed from cloud around young sun | Planets formed from solar material pulled out by another star |
| Emphasises rotating nebula | Emphasises close encounter and tidal extraction |
| Associated with Kant and Laplace | Associated with Chamberlain and Moulton |

---

# 5. Revised Nebular Hypothesis

## 5.1 Main Scholars

In 1950, Otto Schmidt and Carl Weizsacker revised the nebular hypothesis.

Their details differed, but the general direction was similar.

## 5.2 Main Idea

They suggested that the sun was surrounded by a solar nebula.

This nebula contained mainly:

- hydrogen,
- helium,
- dust particles.

## 5.3 Process

The particles in the nebula experienced:

- friction,
- collision,
- condensation,
- accretion.

This led to a disk-shaped cloud.

Planets formed through accretion.

## 5.4 Importance

This theory moved closer to the modern understanding that planets grew from material in a rotating disk around the young sun.

---

# 6. Modern Theory: Big Bang

## 6.1 Origin Of The Universe

Modern scientists shifted from asking only about the earth to asking about the origin of the universe.

The most accepted theory in the textbook is the Big Bang Theory.

It is also called the expanding universe hypothesis.

## 6.2 Edwin Hubble

Edwin Hubble provided evidence in 1920 that the universe is expanding.

He found that galaxies are moving farther apart.

## 6.3 Meaning Of Expansion

Expansion of the universe means the space between galaxies is increasing.

It does not mean galaxies themselves are expanding in the same way.

## 6.4 Balloon Analogy

The textbook uses a balloon analogy:

- mark points on a balloon as galaxies;
- inflate the balloon;
- points move farther apart.

This helps imagine expansion.

But the analogy is only partly correct because the marked points themselves also stretch on a balloon, whereas real galaxies are not expanding in the same way.

---

# 7. Stages Of The Big Bang Theory

## 7.1 Stage One: Extremely Dense Beginning

At the beginning, all matter of the universe existed in one extremely small state.

The textbook describes it as a tiny ball or singular atom.

It had:

- unimaginably small volume,
- extremely high temperature,
- extremely high density.

## 7.2 Stage Two: Violent Expansion

At the Big Bang, this tiny state expanded violently.

The event took place about 13.7 billion years before present.

Expansion continues even today.

During expansion, some energy converted into matter.

The earliest expansion was extremely rapid.

Later, the rate of expansion slowed.

Within the first three minutes, the first atoms began to form.

## 7.3 Stage Three: Formation Of Atomic Matter

Within about 300,000 years from the Big Bang, temperature dropped to about 4,500 K.

This allowed atomic matter to form.

The universe became transparent.

## 7.4 Big Bang Summary

```text
Tiny dense state
-> violent expansion
-> energy becomes matter
-> first atoms
-> universe becomes transparent
-> galaxies and stars form later
```

---

# 8. Steady State Theory

## 8.1 Main Idea

The steady state theory, associated with Hoyle, proposed that the universe remains roughly the same at any point of time.

## 8.2 Why It Lost Support

As more evidence accumulated for an expanding universe, the scientific community favoured the Big Bang Theory.

## 8.3 Comparison

| Big Bang Theory | Steady State Theory |
| --- | --- |
| Universe began from hot dense state | Universe roughly unchanged over time |
| Universe is expanding | Universe appears steady overall |
| Supported by expanding universe evidence | Less favoured after more evidence |

---

# 9. Formation Of Galaxies And Stars

## 9.1 Uneven Early Universe

Matter and energy were not evenly distributed in the early universe.

Small density differences existed.

## 9.2 Role Of Gravity

Density differences created differences in gravitational force.

Matter was drawn together.

These concentrations became the base for galaxy formation.

## 9.3 Galaxies

A galaxy contains a large number of stars.

Galaxies are vast.

Their diameters may range from about 80,000 to 150,000 light years.

## 9.4 Nebula And Star Formation

A galaxy begins with accumulation of hydrogen gas in a huge cloud called a nebula.

The nebula develops localised clumps of gas.

These clumps become denser.

Eventually, they form stars.

## 9.5 Star Formation Sequence

```text
Uneven matter distribution
-> gravitational attraction
-> gas cloud or nebula
-> dense clumps
-> star formation
```

---

# 10. Light Year

## 10.1 Meaning

A light year is a measure of distance, not time.

It is the distance light travels in one year.

## 10.2 Speed Of Light

Light travels at about 300,000 km per second.

Therefore, one light year equals about:

```text
9.461 x 10^12 km
```

## 10.3 Sun-Earth Distance In Light Time

The mean distance between the sun and the earth is about 149,598,000 km.

In light travel time, this is about 8.311 minutes.

That means sunlight takes a little over 8 minutes to reach earth.

## 10.4 Common Mistake

Do not write that light year is a unit of time.

It is a unit of distance.

---

# 11. Formation Of Planets

The chapter gives three stages in the development of planets.

## 11.1 Stage One: Gas Core And Disk

Stars form as localised lumps of gas within a nebula.

Gravity forms a core in the gas cloud.

A huge rotating disk of gas and dust develops around the core.

## 11.2 Stage Two: Planetesimals

The gas cloud begins to condense.

Matter around the core develops into small rounded objects.

Through cohesion, these objects become planetesimals.

Planetesimals are small bodies that act as building blocks of planets.

## 11.3 Stage Three: Planets

Planetesimals collide and stick together.

Gravity pulls more material into larger bodies.

Many smaller bodies accrete into fewer large bodies.

These large bodies become planets.

## 11.4 Planet Formation Sequence

```text
Nebula
-> gas core and rotating disk
-> condensation of matter
-> small rounded bodies
-> planetesimals
-> accretion
-> planets
```

# 12. Our Solar System

## 12.1 Formation Time

The nebula from which the solar system formed began collapsing and forming a core about 5-5.6 billion years ago.

The planets formed about 4.6 billion years ago.

## 12.2 Components

The solar system includes:

- the sun,
- 8 planets,
- moons,
- asteroids,
- comets,
- dust grains,
- gases.

The textbook mentions 63 moons, based on the source period.

## 12.3 The Eight Planets

The eight planets are:

1. Mercury,
2. Venus,
3. Earth,
4. Mars,
5. Jupiter,
6. Saturn,
7. Uranus,
8. Neptune.

## 12.4 Pluto

Until August 2006, Pluto was considered a planet.

The International Astronomical Union later classified Pluto and similar objects as dwarf planets.

# 13. Inner And Outer Planets

## 13.1 Inner Planets

The inner planets are:

- Mercury,
- Venus,
- Earth,
- Mars.

They lie between the sun and the asteroid belt.

## 13.2 Outer Planets

The outer planets are:

- Jupiter,
- Saturn,
- Uranus,
- Neptune.

They lie beyond the asteroid belt.

## 13.3 Terrestrial Planets

The first four planets are called terrestrial planets.

Terrestrial means earth-like.

They are:

- rocky,
- metallic,
- relatively dense,
- smaller than Jovian planets.

## 13.4 Jovian Planets

The outer four planets are called Jovian or gas giant planets.

Jovian means Jupiter-like.

They are:

- much larger,
- less dense,
- rich in gases,
- surrounded by thick atmospheres,
- mostly made of hydrogen and helium.

---

# 14. Why Inner Planets Are Rocky

The terrestrial planets are rocky for three main reasons.

## 14.1 Nearness To The Sun

They formed close to the sun.

Near the young sun, it was too warm for many gases to condense into solid particles.

So rocky and metallic materials dominated.

## 14.2 Strong Solar Wind

Solar wind was stronger near the sun.

It blew away much gas and dust from the inner planets.

The outer planets were farther away, so solar wind could not remove their gases as effectively.

## 14.3 Low Gravity

Terrestrial planets are smaller.

Their lower gravity could not hold escaping gases easily.

Therefore, they remained rocky and less gas-rich.

## 14.4 Summary

```text
Close to sun + strong solar wind + low gravity
-> gases removed or not retained
-> rocky terrestrial planets
```

# 15. The Moon

## 15.1 Earth's Natural Satellite

The moon is the only natural satellite of the earth.

## 15.2 Earlier View By George Darwin

In 1838, Sir George Darwin suggested that earth and moon once formed a single rapidly rotating body.

He proposed that the mass became dumbbell-shaped and broke.

It was also suggested that moon material came from the area now occupied by the Pacific Ocean.

## 15.3 Modern View: Giant Impact

Present scientists generally support the giant impact theory.

This is also described as the big splat.

## 15.4 Giant Impact Process

Shortly after earth formed, a large body collided with it.

The colliding body may have been one to three times the size of Mars.

The collision blasted part of earth's material into space.

This material continued orbiting earth.

Eventually it formed the moon.

## 15.5 Time

The moon formed about 4.44 billion years ago.

# 16. Early Earth

## 16.1 Initial Condition

Early earth was:

- barren,
- rocky,
- hot,
- volatile,
- without modern oceans,
- without modern atmosphere,
- surrounded by a thin atmosphere of hydrogen and helium.

## 16.2 Present Earth Is Very Different

Today earth has:

- solid crust,
- oceans,
- oxygen-rich atmosphere,
- moderate temperature,
- life-supporting conditions.

This means earth must have changed through several processes.

## 16.3 Main Evolutionary Stages

Earth evolved through:

- evolution of lithosphere,
- evolution of atmosphere,
- evolution of hydrosphere,
- origin and evolution of life.

# 17. Evolution Of Lithosphere

## 17.1 Volatile Primordial Earth

During its primordial stage, earth was mostly volatile and hot.

As density increased, internal temperature increased.

## 17.2 Differentiation

Differentiation is the separation of materials according to density.

Heavy materials such as iron sank toward the centre.

Lighter materials moved toward the surface.

## 17.3 Cooling And Crust Formation

With time, earth cooled.

It solidified and condensed into a smaller size.

The outer surface developed as crust.

## 17.4 Giant Impact Heating

During moon formation, the giant impact heated earth further.

This helped continue internal separation and restructuring.

## 17.5 Layered Earth

Differentiation produced layers:

- crust,
- mantle,
- outer core,
- inner core.

Density increases from crust toward core.

## 17.6 Simple Sequence

```text
Hot volatile earth
-> internal heating
-> density separation
-> heavy material sinks
-> light material rises
-> cooling and solidification
-> crust, mantle, core layers
```

# 18. Evolution Of Atmosphere

The present atmosphere is mainly composed of nitrogen and oxygen.

It evolved in three stages.

## 18.1 Stage One: Loss Of Primordial Atmosphere

Earth's earliest atmosphere contained mainly hydrogen and helium.

Solar winds stripped away this light primordial atmosphere.

This also happened to other terrestrial planets.

## 18.2 Stage Two: Degassing

During cooling, gases and water vapour were released from earth's interior.

This process is called degassing.

Volcanic eruptions contributed gases and water vapour to the atmosphere.

## 18.3 Early Degassed Atmosphere

The early atmosphere contained:

- water vapour,
- nitrogen,
- carbon dioxide,
- methane,
- ammonia,
- very little free oxygen.

## 18.4 Stage Three: Modification By Life

Living organisms later modified the atmosphere through photosynthesis.

Photosynthesis added oxygen to oceans and then atmosphere.

---

# 19. Evolution Of Hydrosphere

## 19.1 Condensation Of Water Vapour

As earth cooled, water vapour released from the interior began to condense.

This caused rainfall.

## 19.2 Carbon Dioxide And Cooling

Carbon dioxide dissolved in rainwater.

This reduced atmospheric carbon dioxide and helped lower temperature further.

More cooling caused more condensation and more rain.

## 19.3 Formation Of Oceans

Rainwater collected in depressions on earth's surface.

These depressions gradually became oceans.

## 19.4 Age Of Oceans

The oceans formed within 500 million years from earth's formation.

Since earth formed about 4.6 billion years ago, oceans are about 4.0 billion years old.

---

# 20. Photosynthesis And Oxygen

## 20.1 Life In Oceans

Life remained confined to oceans for a long time.

## 20.2 Evolution Of Photosynthesis

Around 2.5-3.0 billion years before present, photosynthesis evolved.

Photosynthetic organisms began releasing oxygen.

## 20.3 Oxygen In Oceans

At first, oxygen accumulated in oceans.

Oceans gradually became saturated with oxygen.

## 20.4 Oxygen In Atmosphere

Around 2.0 billion years ago, oxygen began to flood the atmosphere.

This changed earth's environment and made complex life possible later.

---

# 21. Origin Of Life

## 21.1 Early Earth Was Not Suitable For Life

Initially, earth and its atmosphere were not favourable for life.

The surface was hot, barren, rocky, and unstable.

## 21.2 Chemical Origin

Modern scientists explain the origin of life as a chemical process.

Complex organic molecules formed and assembled.

These assemblies eventually became capable of duplicating themselves.

This converted non-living matter into living substance.

## 21.3 Fossil Record

Evidence of ancient life is preserved in rocks as fossils.

Microscopic structures related to present blue-green algae have been found in very old geological formations.

## 21.4 Time Of Life Origin

Life is believed to have begun about 3.8 billion years ago.

## 21.5 Basic Sequence

```text
Simple chemicals
-> complex organic molecules
-> self-duplicating assemblies
-> living substance
-> early unicellular life
-> later complex life
```

# 26. Common Misunderstandings

## 26.1 Light Year Is A Unit Of Time

Incorrect.

Light year is a unit of distance.

It measures how far light travels in one year.

## 26.2 Big Bang Means Explosion In Existing Empty Space

Incomplete.

The Big Bang refers to expansion of the universe itself from an extremely hot, dense state.

## 26.3 Galaxies Expand Like Balloon Dots

Incorrect.

The balloon analogy helps show increasing distance, but real galaxies themselves are not expanding like dots painted on a balloon.

## 26.4 Terrestrial Planets Are Rocky By Chance

Incorrect.

They are rocky because they formed near the sun, where heat and solar wind removed or prevented retention of gases.

## 26.5 Present Atmosphere Existed From The Beginning

Incorrect.

The present atmosphere evolved through loss of primordial gases, degassing, and modification by life.

## 26.6 Oceans Formed Immediately In Their Present Form

Incorrect.

Oceans formed after earth cooled, water vapour condensed, rain fell, and water collected in depressions.

## 26.7 Life Started On Land

Incorrect.

Life remained confined to oceans for a long time.

## 27.2 Short Answer: Why Are Terrestrial Planets Rocky?

Terrestrial planets are rocky because they formed close to the sun, where it was too warm for gases to condense.

Strong solar wind near the sun removed much gas and dust.

Also, terrestrial planets were smaller and their gravity was not strong enough to hold escaping gases.

## 27.3 Short Answer: Kant-Laplace vs Chamberlain-Moulton

Kant and Laplace proposed the nebular hypothesis, in which planets formed from a rotating cloud of material associated with a young sun.

Chamberlain and Moulton proposed that a wandering star came close to the sun and pulled out material from the solar surface, which later condensed into planets.

## 27.4 Short Answer: Differentiation

Differentiation is the process by which earth materials separated according to density.

Heavier materials like iron sank toward the centre, while lighter materials moved toward the surface.

This process produced layers such as crust, mantle, outer core, and inner core.

## 27.5 Short Answer: Initial Earth Surface

Initially, earth was barren, rocky, hot, and volatile.

It had a thin atmosphere of hydrogen and helium and did not have modern oceans or life-supporting conditions.

## 27.6 Short Answer: Initial Atmospheric Gases

The early atmosphere formed after degassing mainly contained:

- water vapour,
- nitrogen,
- carbon dioxide,
- methane,
- ammonia,
- very little free oxygen.

The earliest primordial atmosphere of hydrogen and helium was lost due to solar winds.

## 28.1 Big Bang Theory

Use this structure:

1. State the theory:
   The Big Bang Theory explains the origin of the universe and is also called the expanding universe hypothesis.

2. Mention Hubble:
   Edwin Hubble provided evidence in 1920 that galaxies are moving apart.

3. Initial condition:
   All matter existed in an extremely small, hot, dense state.

4. Explosion and expansion:
   About 13.7 billion years ago, violent expansion began and continues today.

5. Early matter:
   Some energy converted into matter; first atoms began forming within the first few minutes.

6. Transparency:
   Around 300,000 years later, temperature dropped enough for atomic matter to form and the universe became transparent.

7. Galaxies:
   Uneven density and gravity led to galaxy formation.

8. Conclude:
   The Big Bang Theory is favoured over steady state because of evidence for expansion.

## 28.2 Evolution Of The Earth

Use this structure:

1. Initial earth:
   Earth began as a hot, rocky, barren, volatile body with a thin hydrogen-helium atmosphere.

2. Lithosphere:
   Through differentiation, heavy materials sank and lighter materials rose, forming crust, mantle, outer core, and inner core.

3. Atmosphere:
   The primordial atmosphere was lost by solar winds. Later, degassing released water vapour and gases from earth's interior.

4. Hydrosphere:
   As earth cooled, water vapour condensed, rainfall occurred, and water collected in depressions to form oceans.

5. Life:
   Life began in oceans around 3.8 billion years ago. Photosynthesis later produced oxygen, changing oceans and atmosphere.

6. Conclude:
   Earth became habitable through linked geological, atmospheric, hydrological, and biological processes.

## 28.3 Formation Of Planets

Use this structure:

1. Solar nebula surrounded the young sun.
2. Gravity formed a core and rotating disk.
3. Gas and dust condensed into small rounded bodies.
4. These bodies became planetesimals.
5. Planetesimals collided and stuck together through accretion.
6. Fewer large bodies formed as planets.
7. Inner planets became rocky because they formed close to the sun.

## 28.4 Origin Of Moon

Use this structure:

1. Moon is earth's only natural satellite.
2. Earlier theories suggested earth and moon separated from one rotating body, but these are not widely accepted now.
3. Modern explanation is giant impact theory.
4. A large Mars-sized or larger body collided with early earth.
5. The impact blasted material into space.
6. This material orbited earth and accreted into the moon.
7. Moon formed about 4.44 billion years ago.

# 31. Final Chapter Summary

The chapter traces the story of the universe and earth from cosmic origin to life. Early theories such as the nebular hypothesis and binary theories tried to explain planet formation. Modern explanations begin with the Big Bang Theory, which states that the universe began expanding about 13.7 billion years ago.

Matter gathered under gravity to form galaxies. Hydrogen clouds developed into nebulae, and dense clumps inside nebulae formed stars. Around the young sun, gas and dust in a rotating disk condensed into planetesimals, which accreted into planets.

The solar system formed about 5-5.6 billion years ago, and earth formed about 4.6 billion years ago. The inner planets became rocky because they formed close to the sun, where heat and solar wind removed gases. The outer planets remained gas giants.

The moon likely formed through a giant impact shortly after earth formed. Early earth was hot, rocky, barren, and volatile. Differentiation produced layers such as crust, mantle, outer core, and inner core. Degassing created the early atmosphere, condensation created oceans, and photosynthesis later added oxygen.

Life began in oceans around 3.8 billion years ago. The evolution of earth is therefore a linked story of universe expansion, star formation, planet formation, internal differentiation, atmosphere formation, ocean formation, and biological evolution.
