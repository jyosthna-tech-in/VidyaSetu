# 1. From Unequal Heating To Wind

## 1.1 Link With Previous Chapter

The previous chapter explained unequal distribution of temperature over the earth.

This chapter explains what unequal heating does to air pressure and winds.

## 1.2 Heating And Pressure

Air expands when heated.

Air gets compressed when cooled.

This creates pressure differences.

## 1.3 Pressure Differences Create Wind

Air moves from high pressure to low pressure.

Horizontal movement of air is called wind.

## 1.4 Wind Redistributes Heat And Moisture

Wind transfers:

- heat,
- moisture,
- clouds,
- weather systems,
- pollutants,
- dust,
- energy.

This redistribution helps maintain global temperature balance.

## 1.5 Rising Air And Weather

When moist air rises, it cools.

Cooling may cause condensation.

Condensation forms clouds.

Clouds may bring precipitation.

## 1.6 Chapter Chain

```text
Unequal heating -> pressure differences -> winds -> circulation -> air mass interaction -> weather systems
```

# 2. Atmospheric Pressure

## 2.1 Meaning

Atmospheric pressure is the weight of a column of air over a unit area from mean sea level to the top of the atmosphere.

## 2.2 Simple Exam Definition

Use this:

> Atmospheric pressure is the force exerted by the weight of air above a unit area.

## 2.3 Unit

Atmospheric pressure is expressed in millibars.

Short form:

```text
mb
```

## 2.4 Average Sea-Level Pressure

At sea level, average atmospheric pressure is:

```text
1013.25 mb
```

The chapter also rounds it as about 1013.2 mb.

## 2.5 Instruments

Air pressure is measured using:

- mercury barometer;
- aneroid barometer.

## 2.6 Why We Feel Breathless At Height

As height increases, air becomes rarer.

There is less air above us.

Pressure and oxygen availability decrease.

This is why people may feel breathless at high altitude.

---

# 3. Vertical Variation Of Pressure

## 3.1 Pressure Decreases With Height

Atmospheric pressure decreases with height.

Reason:

- gravity keeps air denser near the surface;
- higher levels have less air above them.

## 3.2 Rate In Lower Atmosphere

In the lower atmosphere, pressure decreases rapidly with height.

Approximate rate:

```text
1 mb for every 10 m increase in elevation
```

This rate is not constant everywhere or at every height.

## 3.3 Standard Pressure And Temperature Table

| Level | Pressure | Temperature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sea level | 1013.25 mb | 15.2 degrees Celsius |
| 1 km | 898.76 mb | 8.7 degrees Celsius |
| 5 km | 540.48 mb | -17.3 degrees Celsius |
| 10 km | 265.00 mb | -49.7 degrees Celsius |

## 3.4 Vertical Pressure Gradient

The vertical pressure gradient force is much larger than the horizontal pressure gradient force.

But it is nearly balanced by gravity.

Therefore, we do not normally experience extremely strong upward winds.

## 3.5 Why Vertical Pressure Does Not Throw Air Upward

Two forces oppose each other:

- upward pressure gradient force;
- downward gravity.

They almost balance.

This keeps the atmosphere vertically stable most of the time.

---

# 4. Horizontal Distribution Of Pressure

## 4.1 Why Small Differences Matter

Small horizontal differences in pressure are highly significant.

They control:

- wind direction;
- wind speed;
- weather systems;
- storm formation.

## 4.2 Sea-Level Pressure On Maps

Pressure measured at stations is reduced to sea level for weather maps.

Reason:

- pressure changes with altitude;
- stations are located at different heights;
- sea-level reduction allows fair comparison;
- it helps identify true horizontal pressure differences.

## 4.3 Isobars

Isobars are lines connecting places with equal atmospheric pressure.

They are drawn on weather maps.

## 4.4 Low-Pressure System

A low-pressure system is enclosed by one or more isobars with the lowest pressure at the centre.

## 4.5 High-Pressure System

A high-pressure system is enclosed by one or more isobars with the highest pressure at the centre.

## 4.6 Pressure Systems And Wind

Air generally moves:

```text
from high pressure toward low pressure
```

But the actual wind direction is modified by:

- Coriolis force;
- friction;
- pressure gradient;
- earth's rotation.

---

# 5. World Pressure Belts

## 5.1 Main Pressure Belts

Major pressure belts are:

1. equatorial low;
2. subtropical highs;
3. subpolar lows;
4. polar highs.

## 5.2 Equatorial Low

Near the equator, sea-level pressure is low.

This area is called the equatorial low.

It is associated with:

- high insolation;
- rising air;
- convergence of winds;
- ITCZ.

## 5.3 Subtropical Highs

Around 30 degrees N and 30 degrees S, high-pressure belts occur.

They are called subtropical highs.

They are associated with:

- sinking air;
- dry conditions;
- many subtropical deserts.

## 5.4 Subpolar Lows

Around 60 degrees N and 60 degrees S, low-pressure belts occur.

They are called subpolar lows.

They are associated with:

- convergence of warm and cold air;
- frontal activity;
- extra tropical cyclones.

## 5.5 Polar Highs

Near the poles, high pressure occurs.

It is called polar high.

Reason:

- very cold dense air;
- sinking air.

## 5.6 Pressure Belts Shift

Pressure belts are not fixed permanently.

They oscillate with the apparent movement of the sun.

In the northern hemisphere:

- they move southward in winter;
- they move northward in summer.

# 6. Forces Affecting Wind

## 6.1 Main Forces

Horizontal winds near the earth's surface are affected by:

1. pressure gradient force;
2. frictional force;
3. Coriolis force.

Gravity acts downward.

## 6.2 Wind Direction Is A Combined Result

Wind does not simply move in a straight line from high pressure to low pressure.

Its direction and speed are shaped by multiple forces acting together.

---

# 7. Pressure Gradient Force

## 7.1 Meaning

Pressure gradient is the rate of change of pressure with distance.

Pressure gradient force is the force produced by this pressure difference.

## 7.2 Isobars And Gradient

Close isobars mean strong pressure gradient.

Widely spaced isobars mean weak pressure gradient.

## 7.3 Effect On Wind Speed

Strong pressure gradient force produces faster wind.

Weak pressure gradient force produces slower wind.

## 7.4 Direction

Pressure gradient force acts from high pressure toward low pressure.

It is perpendicular to isobars.

## 7.5 Simple Rule

```text
Closer isobars -> stronger pressure gradient -> faster wind
```

---

# 8. Frictional Force

## 8.1 Meaning

Frictional force resists air movement near the surface.

It slows wind.

## 8.2 Where It Is Strongest

Friction is greatest at the earth's surface.

It is strong over:

- rough land;
- forests;
- mountains;
- urban areas.

## 8.3 Vertical Limit

Its influence generally extends up to about 1 to 3 km above the surface.

## 8.4 Friction Over Sea

Friction is minimal over the sea surface compared with rough land.

## 8.5 Effect On Wind

Friction:

- reduces wind speed;
- changes wind direction near the surface;
- weakens the Coriolis effect because slower winds are deflected less.

---

# 9. Coriolis Force

## 9.1 Meaning

Coriolis force is the apparent force caused by the rotation of the earth.

It affects the direction of moving air.

## 9.2 Direction Of Deflection

Coriolis force deflects wind:

- to the right in the northern hemisphere;
- to the left in the southern hemisphere.

## 9.3 Named After

It is named after the French physicist who described it in 1844.

## 9.4 Relation With Wind Speed

The deflection is greater when wind velocity is high.

Faster wind experiences stronger deflection.

## 9.5 Relation With Latitude

Coriolis force is directly proportional to latitude.

It is:

- maximum at the poles;
- zero at the equator.

## 9.6 Direction Relative To Pressure Gradient

Coriolis force acts perpendicular to the pressure gradient force.

## 9.7 Why Tropical Cyclones Do Not Form Near Equator

At the equator, Coriolis force is zero.

Winds blow nearly perpendicular to isobars and the low pressure tends to get filled.

Cyclonic rotation does not develop properly.

Therefore, tropical cyclones do not form near the equator.

---

# 10. Pressure And Wind

## 10.1 Upper-Air Winds

Winds in the upper atmosphere, about 2 to 3 km above the surface, are mostly free from surface friction.

They are controlled mainly by:

- pressure gradient force;
- Coriolis force.

## 10.2 Geostrophic Wind

When isobars are straight and friction is absent, pressure gradient force is balanced by Coriolis force.

The wind then blows parallel to the isobars.

This wind is called geostrophic wind.

## 10.3 Conditions For Geostrophic Wind

Conditions:

- upper atmosphere;
- little or no friction;
- straight isobars;
- balance between pressure gradient force and Coriolis force.

## 10.4 Why Geostrophic Wind Matters

It helps explain upper-air flow.

It shows why wind may blow parallel to isobars instead of directly across them.

---

# 11. Cyclonic And Anticyclonic Circulation

## 11.1 Cyclonic Circulation

Wind circulation around a low-pressure system is called cyclonic circulation.

## 11.2 Anticyclonic Circulation

Wind circulation around a high-pressure system is called anticyclonic circulation.

## 11.3 Direction Table

| Pressure System | Centre | Northern Hemisphere | Southern Hemisphere |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cyclone | Low pressure | Anticlockwise | Clockwise |
| Anticyclone | High pressure | Clockwise | Anticlockwise |

## 11.4 Low Pressure At Surface

Over a low-pressure area:

- air converges at the surface;
- air rises;
- clouds and precipitation may form if moisture is present.

## 11.5 High Pressure At Surface

Over a high-pressure area:

- air subsides from above;
- air diverges at the surface;
- weather is often clearer and drier.

## 11.6 Other Causes Of Rising Air

Air can rise due to:

- convergence;
- eddies;
- convection currents;
- orographic uplift;
- uplift along fronts.

Rising air is essential for cloud formation and precipitation.

---

# 12. General Circulation Of The Atmosphere

## 12.1 Meaning

General circulation is the large-scale pattern of planetary winds over the globe.

## 12.2 Main Controls

The pattern of planetary winds depends on:

1. latitudinal variation of atmospheric heating;
2. emergence of pressure belts;
3. migration of pressure belts with apparent movement of the sun;
4. distribution of continents and oceans;
5. rotation of the earth.

## 12.3 Why It Matters

General circulation:

- transfers heat from low latitudes to high latitudes;
- shapes climate zones;
- controls planetary winds;
- influences ocean currents;
- affects monsoons and storms.

## 12.4 Atmosphere-Ocean Link

Large-scale winds set ocean water in motion.

Ocean currents then influence climate by redistributing heat and moisture.

# 13. ITCZ And Hadley Cell

## 13.1 ITCZ

ITCZ means Inter Tropical Convergence Zone.

It is a low-pressure zone near the equator where winds from the tropics converge.

## 13.2 Why Air Rises At ITCZ

Air rises at ITCZ because:

- insolation is high;
- surface heating is strong;
- convection is active;
- pressure is low;
- trade winds converge.

## 13.3 Upper Movement

Rising air reaches the top of the troposphere, up to about 14 km.

Then it moves poleward.

## 13.4 Subtropical Accumulation

Air moving poleward accumulates around 30 degrees N and S.

Part of it sinks.

This forms the subtropical high-pressure belts.

## 13.5 Why Air Sinks Around 30 Degrees

Two reasons:

- air accumulates aloft;
- air cools as it moves toward 30 degrees N and S.

## 13.6 Surface Flow Back To Equator

Near the surface, air flows from subtropical highs toward the equatorial low.

Because of Coriolis force, these winds become easterlies.

## 13.7 Hadley Cell

The tropical circulation from surface to upper troposphere and back is called the Hadley cell.

## 13.8 Hadley Cell Process Chain

```text
Strong equatorial heating -> ITCZ low pressure -> rising air -> poleward flow aloft -> sinking near 30 degrees -> subtropical highs -> surface easterlies return to ITCZ
```

# 14. Ferrel Cell And Polar Cell

## 14.1 Ferrel Cell

The Ferrel cell occurs in the middle latitudes.

It involves:

- sinking cold air from polar side;
- rising warm air from subtropical side;
- surface westerlies.

## 14.2 Westerlies

At the surface in middle latitudes, winds are called westerlies.

They generally blow from west to east.

## 14.3 Polar Cell

The polar cell occurs at high latitudes.

Cold dense air subsides near the poles.

It flows toward middle latitudes as polar easterlies.

## 14.4 Polar Easterlies

Polar easterlies are cold winds blowing from polar highs toward subpolar lows.

## 14.5 Three-Cell Model

The three cells are:

1. Hadley cell;
2. Ferrel cell;
3. Polar cell.

Together, they form the basic pattern of general atmospheric circulation.

## 14.6 Text Diagram

```text
Equator: ITCZ low pressure, rising air
0 to 30 degrees: Hadley cell, trade/easterly winds at surface
30 degrees: Subtropical high pressure, sinking air
30 to 60 degrees: Ferrel cell, westerlies at surface
60 degrees: Subpolar low pressure, frontal activity
60 to 90 degrees: Polar cell, polar easterlies
Poles: Polar high pressure, sinking cold air
```

# 15. General Circulation And Oceans

## 15.1 Atmosphere Drives Oceans

Large-scale winds initiate large and slow-moving ocean currents.

## 15.2 Oceans Feed The Atmosphere

Oceans provide:

- water vapour;
- heat energy;
- moisture for storms;
- temperature moderation.

## 15.3 Slow Interaction

Atmosphere-ocean interactions are slow and large-scale.

They affect regional and global climate.

# 16. El Nino, Southern Oscillation, And ENSO

## 16.1 Importance Of Pacific Ocean

Warming and cooling of the Pacific Ocean is very important for general atmospheric circulation.

## 16.2 El Nino

El Nino is the appearance of warm water off the coast of Peru.

This warm water replaces the cool Peruvian current.

## 16.3 Southern Oscillation

Southern Oscillation refers to pressure changes over the Central Pacific and Australia.

## 16.4 ENSO

ENSO means combined El Nino and Southern Oscillation.

## 16.5 Effects Of Strong ENSO

When ENSO is strong, large-scale weather variations occur.

Examples:

- arid west coast of South America may receive heavy rainfall;
- drought may occur in Australia;
- drought may sometimes occur in India;
- floods may occur in China.

## 16.6 Forecasting

ENSO is closely monitored.

It is used for long-range forecasting in many parts of the world.

# 17. Seasonal And Local Winds

## 17.1 Seasonal Winds

Wind circulation changes with seasons because regions of maximum heating, pressure, and wind belts shift.

## 17.2 Monsoons

The most pronounced seasonal wind effect is seen in monsoons.

This is especially important over Southeast Asia and South Asia.

## 17.3 Local Winds

Local or regional winds form because of differences in heating and cooling of surfaces.

They may occur:

- daily;
- seasonally;
- regionally.

Important local winds:

- land breeze;
- sea breeze;
- valley breeze;
- mountain wind;
- katabatic wind;
- warm leeward winds.

# 18. Land And Sea Breezes

## 18.1 Basic Cause

Land and sea heat and cool differently.

Land heats faster and cools faster.

Sea heats slowly and cools slowly.

## 18.2 Sea Breeze

Sea breeze occurs during the day.

Formation sequence:

1. land heats faster than sea;
2. air over land becomes warm and rises;
3. low pressure forms over land;
4. sea remains relatively cool;
5. high pressure forms over sea;
6. wind blows from sea to land.

## 18.3 Land Breeze

Land breeze occurs at night.

Formation sequence:

1. land loses heat faster than sea;
2. land becomes cooler;
3. high pressure forms over land;
4. sea remains relatively warmer;
5. low pressure forms over sea;
6. wind blows from land to sea.

## 18.4 Sea Breeze vs Land Breeze

| Basis | Sea Breeze | Land Breeze |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Time | Day | Night |
| Direction | Sea to land | Land to sea |
| Land condition | Warmer, low pressure | Cooler, high pressure |
| Sea condition | Cooler, high pressure | Warmer, low pressure |

---

# 19. Mountain And Valley Winds

## 19.1 Valley Breeze

Valley breeze occurs during the day in mountain regions.

Formation sequence:

1. slopes heat up during the day;
2. air on slopes becomes warm;
3. warm air moves upslope;
4. air from valley moves upward to fill the gap.

This upslope wind is called valley breeze.

## 19.2 Mountain Wind

Mountain wind occurs at night.

Formation sequence:

1. slopes cool at night;
2. air in contact with slopes becomes cold and dense;
3. dense air descends into valleys;
4. downslope wind forms.

This is called mountain wind.

## 19.3 Katabatic Wind

Katabatic wind is cold dense air draining from high plateaus or ice fields into valleys.

## 19.4 Warm Leeward Wind

Another type of warm wind occurs on the leeward side of mountain ranges.

Formation sequence:

1. moist air crosses mountain range;
2. moisture condenses and precipitates on windward side;
3. air descends on leeward side;
4. dry descending air warms by adiabatic process;
5. snow may melt quickly.

## 19.5 Valley Breeze vs Mountain Wind

| Basis | Valley Breeze | Mountain Wind |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Time | Day | Night |
| Direction | Upslope/up valley | Downslope/down valley |
| Cause | Slope heating | Slope cooling |
| Air character | Warm rising air | Cold dense descending air |

# 20. Air Masses

## 20.1 Meaning

An air mass is a large body of air with little horizontal variation in temperature and moisture.

## 20.2 Formation

An air mass forms when air remains over a homogeneous surface for a sufficiently long time.

It acquires the characteristics of that surface.

## 20.3 Source Region

The homogeneous surface over which an air mass forms is called its source region.

Source regions may be:

- vast ocean surfaces;
- vast plains;
- deserts;
- snow-covered continents;
- ice-covered continents.

## 20.4 Major Source Regions

The chapter lists five major source regions:

1. warm tropical and subtropical oceans;
2. subtropical hot deserts;
3. relatively cold high-latitude oceans;
4. very cold snow-covered continents in high latitudes;
5. permanently ice-covered continents in the Arctic and Antarctica.

## 20.5 Air Mass Types

| Air Mass | Symbol | Source Character |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Maritime tropical | mT | Warm tropical ocean |
| Continental tropical | cT | Hot desert or tropical land |
| Maritime polar | mP | Cold high-latitude ocean |
| Continental polar | cP | Cold snow-covered continent |
| Continental arctic | cA | Very cold ice-covered land |

## 20.6 Temperature Character

Tropical air masses are warm.

Polar and arctic air masses are cold.

## 20.7 Moisture Character

Maritime air masses are moist.

Continental air masses are dry.

---

# 21. Fronts

## 21.1 Meaning

A front is the boundary zone between two different air masses.

## 21.2 Frontogenesis

The process of front formation is called frontogenesis.

## 21.3 Where Fronts Occur

Fronts commonly occur in middle latitudes.

## 21.4 Why Fronts Produce Weather

Air masses have different:

- temperatures;
- moisture levels;
- densities;
- pressures.

When they meet, air rises and weather changes abruptly.

## 21.5 Characteristics Of Fronts

Fronts are characterised by:

- steep temperature gradient;
- steep pressure gradient;
- cloud formation;
- precipitation;
- abrupt weather changes.

## 21.6 Types Of Fronts

There are four main types:

1. cold front;
2. warm front;
3. stationary front;
4. occluded front.

## 21.7 Cold Front

A cold front forms when cold air moves toward a warm air mass.

Cold air pushes warm air upward.

## 21.8 Warm Front

A warm front forms when warm air moves toward a cold air mass.

Warm air rises gradually over cold air.

## 21.9 Stationary Front

A stationary front forms when the boundary between air masses remains nearly stationary.

## 21.10 Occluded Front

An occluded front forms when warm air is fully lifted above the surface.

It often develops when a cold front overtakes a warm front in an extra tropical cyclone.

# 22. Extra Tropical Cyclones

## 22.1 Meaning

Extra tropical cyclones are cyclones that develop in middle and high latitudes beyond the tropics.

They are also called middle-latitude cyclones.

## 22.2 Main Formation Zone

They form along the polar front.

## 22.3 Initial Condition

Initially, the front may be stationary.

In the northern hemisphere:

- warm air lies or moves from the south;
- cold air lies or moves from the north.

## 22.4 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. pressure drops along the front;
2. warm air moves northward;
3. cold air moves southward;
4. cyclonic circulation begins;
5. in the northern hemisphere this circulation is anticlockwise;
6. warm front and cold front develop;
7. warm sector forms between cold air zones;
8. cyclone becomes well developed.

## 22.5 Warm Front Weather

At the warm front:

- warm air glides over cold air;
- a sequence of clouds forms ahead of the front;
- precipitation occurs.

## 22.6 Cold Front Weather

At the cold front:

- cold air pushes warm air up;
- warm air rises sharply;
- cumulus clouds develop;
- precipitation may occur.

## 22.7 Occlusion And Dissipation

The cold front moves faster than the warm front.

It eventually overtakes the warm front.

Warm air is lifted fully above the surface.

The front becomes occluded.

The cyclone weakens and dissipates.

## 22.8 Extra Tropical Cyclone Process Chain

```text
Polar front -> pressure drop -> wave develops -> warm and cold fronts form -> warm sector appears -> cold front overtakes warm front -> occlusion -> cyclone dissipates
```

# 23. Tropical Cyclones

## 23.1 Meaning

Tropical cyclones are violent storms that originate over tropical oceans and move toward coastal areas.

They bring:

- violent winds,
- very heavy rainfall,
- storm surges,
- large-scale destruction.

## 23.2 Names In Different Regions

| Region | Name |
| --- | --- |
| Indian Ocean | Cyclones |
| Atlantic Ocean | Hurricanes |
| Western Pacific and South China Sea | Typhoons |
| Western Australia | Willy-willies |

## 23.3 Conditions For Formation

Favourable conditions:

1. large sea surface with temperature higher than 27 degrees Celsius;
2. presence of Coriolis force;
3. small variations in vertical wind speed;
4. pre-existing weak low-pressure area or low-level cyclonic circulation;
5. upper-level divergence above the sea-level system.

## 23.4 Why They Originate Over Seas

Tropical cyclones need:

- warm ocean water;
- continuous moisture supply;
- evaporation;
- latent heat from condensation;
- large sea surface;
- unstable rising air.

Land cannot supply moisture and heat in the same continuous way.

## 23.5 Energy Source

Energy comes from condensation in towering cumulonimbus clouds around the storm centre.

As moist air rises and condenses, latent heat is released.

This strengthens the storm.

## 23.6 Why Cyclones Weaken Over Land

When a tropical cyclone reaches land:

- moisture supply is cut off;
- surface friction increases;
- energy source weakens;
- storm dissipates.

## 23.7 Landfall

Landfall is the place where a tropical cyclone crosses the coast.

## 23.8 Recurvature

Cyclones crossing about 20 degrees N latitude generally recurve.

They may become more destructive.

# 24. Structure Of A Mature Tropical Cyclone

## 24.1 Main Parts

A mature tropical cyclone has:

- eye;
- eye wall;
- spiral rain bands;
- outer cloud region.

## 24.2 Eye

The eye is the calm central region of the cyclone.

It has:

- subsiding air;
- relatively calm conditions;
- lowest pressure near the centre.

## 24.3 Eye Wall

The eye wall surrounds the eye.

It is the most violent part of the cyclone.

It has:

- strong spiralling ascent of air;
- maximum wind velocity;
- towering cumulonimbus clouds;
- torrential rain.

## 24.4 Maximum Winds And Rain

High velocity winds and torrential rain occur in the eye wall.

Reason:

- strongest pressure gradient;
- strongest uplift;
- intense condensation;
- powerful spiral circulation.

Wind speeds may reach as high as 250 km per hour.

## 24.5 Size

The diameter of the central circulating system may vary between about 150 and 250 km.

Storm systems over the Bay of Bengal, Arabian Sea, and Indian Ocean may have diameters between about 600 and 1200 km.

## 24.6 Movement

The system moves slowly, about 300 to 500 km per day.

## 24.7 Storm Surge

Tropical cyclones create storm surges.

Storm surges inundate coastal lowlands.

## 24.8 Cyclone Weakening

The storm peters out over land because moisture supply is cut off and friction increases.

# 26. Thunderstorms And Tornadoes

## 26.1 Thunderstorms

Thunderstorms are severe local storms.

They are:

- short duration;
- small area;
- violent.

## 26.2 Cause

Thunderstorms are caused by intense convection on moist hot days.

## 26.3 Cloud Type

A thunderstorm is a well-grown cumulonimbus cloud producing thunder and lightning.

## 26.4 Hailstorm

If clouds extend to heights where sub-zero temperature prevails, hail may form.

When hail falls, it is called a hailstorm.

## 26.5 Dust Storm

If moisture is insufficient, a thunderstorm can generate dust storms.

## 26.6 Updraft And Downdraft

Thunderstorm development:

1. warm moist air rises rapidly;
2. strong updraft forms;
3. cloud grows vertically;
4. precipitation forms;
5. later downdraft brings cool air and rain downward.

## 26.7 Tornado

A tornado is a violent spiralling wind descending from a severe thunderstorm.

It may look like a funnel or trunk.

It has:

- very low pressure at centre;
- intense rotating winds;
- massive destructive power.

## 26.8 Where Tornadoes Occur

Tornadoes generally occur in middle latitudes.

## 26.9 Waterspout

A tornado over the sea is called a waterspout.

## 26.10 Energy Conversion

Violent storms convert potential and heat energy into kinetic energy.

After adjustment, the atmosphere returns toward stability.


# 29. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 29.1 Saying Wind Moves Only Straight From High To Low

Pressure gradient force moves air from high to low pressure.

But actual wind is modified by Coriolis force and friction.

## 29.2 Forgetting Hemisphere Direction

Low pressure:

- northern hemisphere: anticlockwise;
- southern hemisphere: clockwise.

High pressure:

- northern hemisphere: clockwise;
- southern hemisphere: anticlockwise.

## 29.3 Confusing Cyclone And Anticyclone

Cyclone is low pressure.

Anticyclone is high pressure.

## 29.4 Confusing El Nino And ENSO

El Nino is warm water off Peru.

Southern Oscillation is pressure change over Pacific and Australia.

ENSO is the combined phenomenon.

## 29.5 Thinking Tropical Cyclones Form On Land

Tropical cyclones form over warm tropical oceans.

They weaken over land because moisture supply is cut off.

## 29.6 Forgetting Eye Wall

The calm part is the eye.

The most violent part is the eye wall.

## 29.7 Confusing Tornado And Tropical Cyclone

Tornado:

- small area,
- short duration,
- descends from thunderstorm.

Tropical cyclone:

- large oceanic storm,
- lasts longer,
- has eye and spiral rain bands.

## 30.2 Short Answer: Unit Of Pressure And Sea-Level Reduction

Atmospheric pressure is measured in millibars.

Pressure measured at a station is reduced to sea level for weather maps because stations are located at different elevations.

Since pressure decreases with height, direct comparison of station pressure would be misleading.

Sea-level reduction removes the altitude effect and helps show true horizontal pressure differences.

## 30.3 Short Answer: Why Tropical Winds Are North-Easterlies In Northern Hemisphere

In the northern hemisphere, air moves from subtropical high pressure toward equatorial low pressure.

The pressure gradient force directs it generally from north to south.

But the Coriolis force deflects moving air to the right.

Therefore, the winds become north-easterlies in the tropics.

## 30.4 Short Answer: Geostrophic Winds

Geostrophic winds are upper-air winds that blow parallel to straight isobars.

They form when friction is absent and pressure gradient force is balanced by Coriolis force.

They occur mainly 2 to 3 km above the surface where surface friction is weak.

## 30.5 Short Answer: Land And Sea Breezes

During the day, land heats faster than sea.

Low pressure develops over land and relatively high pressure over sea.

Wind blows from sea to land as sea breeze.

At night, land cools faster than sea.

High pressure develops over land and relatively low pressure over sea.

Wind blows from land to sea as land breeze.

# 31. Long Answer Guide

## 31.1 Factors Affecting Speed And Direction Of Wind

Use this structure:

1. Start with pressure difference.

Wind begins because of pressure differences.

Air moves from high pressure to low pressure.

2. Pressure gradient force.

Pressure gradient force is stronger where isobars are close.

It controls wind speed.

3. Friction.

Friction slows wind near the surface.

It is strongest over land and weakest over sea.

It affects the lower 1 to 3 km of atmosphere.

4. Coriolis force.

Coriolis force deflects wind right in the northern hemisphere and left in the southern hemisphere.

It is zero at the equator and maximum at poles.

It increases with wind speed.

5. Gravity.

Gravity acts downward and balances vertical pressure gradient.

6. Conclusion.

Wind speed and direction are the result of pressure gradient force, friction, Coriolis force, and gravity.

## 31.2 General Circulation And Subtropical Highs

Use this structure:

1. State the controls.

General circulation depends on latitudinal heating, pressure belts, belt migration, continents/oceans, and earth's rotation.

2. ITCZ.

At the equator, intense heating creates low pressure.

Trade winds converge at the ITCZ and air rises.

3. Upper flow.

Rising air reaches near the top of troposphere and moves poleward.

4. Subtropical highs.

Around 30 degrees N and S, air accumulates and cools.

Part of it sinks.

This sinking air forms subtropical high pressure.

5. Cells.

The tropical circulation is Hadley cell.

Middle latitudes have Ferrel cell.

Polar latitudes have Polar cell.

6. Heat transfer.

These cells transfer heat from lower latitudes to higher latitudes.

## 31.3 Tropical Cyclone Origin And Eye Wall Weather

Use this structure:

1. Origin over seas.

Tropical cyclones originate over warm tropical oceans because they need sea surface temperature above 27 degrees Celsius and continuous moisture.

2. Conditions.

They need Coriolis force, low vertical wind speed variation, a weak low-pressure area, and upper-level divergence.

3. Energy.

Energy comes from condensation in towering cumulonimbus clouds.

Latent heat strengthens the storm.

4. Land weakening.

On land, moisture supply is cut off and friction increases, so the cyclone weakens.

5. Eye and eye wall.

The eye is calm with subsiding air.

The eye wall surrounds the eye and has strongest spiralling uplift.

6. Torrential rain and winds.

Torrential rain and high velocity winds occur in the eye wall because pressure gradient, uplift, condensation, and wind speed are strongest there.

# 35. Final Chapter Summary

Unequal heating creates pressure differences. Air moves from high pressure toward low pressure, and this horizontal movement is called wind. Atmospheric pressure is measured in millibars, and average sea-level pressure is about 1013.25 mb. Pressure decreases rapidly with height, but horizontal pressure differences are most important for wind movement.

Wind speed and direction are controlled by pressure gradient force, frictional force, and Coriolis force. Pressure gradient force starts air movement, friction slows wind near the surface, and Coriolis force deflects wind right in the northern hemisphere and left in the southern hemisphere. In the upper atmosphere, friction is weak and geostrophic winds may blow parallel to isobars.

The earth has major pressure belts: equatorial low, subtropical highs, subpolar lows, and polar highs. These belts shift with the apparent movement of the sun. General circulation is organised through Hadley, Ferrel, and Polar cells. This circulation redistributes heat and also drives ocean currents. Pacific changes such as El Nino and Southern Oscillation combine as ENSO and influence global weather.

Local winds include sea breeze, land breeze, valley breeze, mountain wind, and katabatic wind. Air masses form over homogeneous source regions and are classified by temperature and moisture. Fronts form where different air masses meet and create abrupt weather changes.

Extra tropical cyclones form along fronts in middle and high latitudes. Tropical cyclones form over warm tropical oceans and draw energy from condensation in cumulonimbus clouds. Their strongest winds and heaviest rain occur in the eye wall. Thunderstorms and tornadoes are violent local storms caused by intense convection and atmospheric instability.

To master this chapter, follow the chain from heating to pressure, pressure to wind, wind to circulation, and circulation to weather systems.
