## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains what the atmosphere is made of and how it is arranged vertically above the earth.

The atmosphere is not just "air" in a loose sense.

It is a layered mixture of gases, water vapour, and dust particles that supports life, controls weather, protects the earth from harmful radiation, and helps regulate temperature.

The central idea is:

> The atmosphere is life-supporting because of its composition, and it is weather-producing because of its layered structure and variable elements.

# 1. Why The Atmosphere Matters

## 1.1 Air Is Essential For Life

Humans can survive for some time without food.

Humans can survive for a shorter time without water.

But humans cannot survive even a few minutes without breathing air.

This shows why the atmosphere is essential.

## 1.2 Organisms Depend On Air

The atmosphere supports:

- humans,
- animals,
- plants,
- microorganisms,
- weather systems,
- water cycle,
- temperature balance.

## 1.3 Life-Giving Gases

The atmosphere contains gases needed by living beings.

Oxygen is used by humans and animals for respiration.

Carbon dioxide is used by plants for photosynthesis.

## 1.4 Atmosphere As Earth's Envelope

The atmosphere surrounds the earth on all sides.

It forms a protective envelope around the planet.

## 1.5 Air Is Felt As Wind

Air is:

- colourless,
- odourless,
- invisible.

We usually feel it when it moves as wind.

# 2. Meaning Of Atmosphere

## 2.1 Definition

The atmosphere is a mixture of different gases that surrounds the earth on all sides.

It also contains water vapour and dust particles.

## 2.2 Simple Definition For Exams

Use this answer:

> Atmosphere is the envelope of air surrounding the earth. It is a mixture of gases, water vapour, and dust particles and supports life and weather processes.

## 2.3 Atmosphere As Part Of Earth's Mass

Air is an integral part of the earth's mass.

Most atmospheric mass is close to the surface.

## 2.4 Important Fact

About 99 per cent of the total mass of the atmosphere is confined within 32 km from the earth's surface.

This means:

- air is densest near the surface;
- the atmosphere becomes thinner with height;
- most weather activity occurs close to the earth.

# 3. Components Of The Atmosphere

## 3.1 Three Major Components

The atmosphere is composed of:

1. gases;
2. water vapour;
3. dust particles.

## 3.2 Component Overview

| Component | Nature | Main Importance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gases | Permanent and variable gases | Life, heat balance, radiation filtering |
| Water vapour | Variable gas | Clouds, rainfall, heat balance, stability |
| Dust particles | Small solid particles | Condensation nuclei and cloud formation |

## 3.3 Lower And Higher Atmosphere

Gas proportions are most commonly discussed for the lower atmosphere.

In higher layers, proportions change.

Important height facts:

- oxygen becomes almost negligible around 120 km;
- carbon dioxide and water vapour are mainly found up to about 90 km from the earth's surface.

Its percentage by volume is about 78.08 per cent.

Argon, carbon dioxide, neon, helium, krypton, xenon, and hydrogen occur in much smaller quantities.

Even small quantities can be important.

Carbon dioxide and ozone are good examples.

# 5. Nitrogen And Oxygen

## 5.1 Nitrogen

Nitrogen is the major gas of the atmosphere.

It forms about 78.08 per cent of air by volume.

## 5.2 Importance Of Nitrogen

Nitrogen is important because:

- it dilutes oxygen;
- it helps maintain atmospheric balance;
- it is part of the nitrogen cycle;
- living organisms need nitrogen compounds for proteins and growth.

## 5.3 Oxygen

Oxygen forms about 20.95 per cent of air by volume.

## 5.4 Importance Of Oxygen

Oxygen is important because:

- humans breathe it;
- animals breathe it;
- it supports respiration;
- it supports combustion;
- it is necessary for many chemical processes.

## 5.5 Oxygen With Height

Oxygen becomes almost negligible at about 120 km height.

This shows that the composition of the atmosphere changes in upper layers.

---

# 6. Carbon Dioxide

## 6.1 Percentage

Carbon dioxide forms only about 0.036 per cent of air by volume.

Even though its proportion is small, it is very important.

## 6.2 Meteorological Importance

Carbon dioxide is meteorologically important because:

- it allows incoming solar radiation to pass through;
- it absorbs part of outgoing terrestrial radiation;
- it reflects or re-radiates some heat back toward the earth's surface.

## 6.3 Incoming And Outgoing Radiation

Incoming solar radiation comes from the sun.

Outgoing terrestrial radiation is heat emitted by the earth.

Carbon dioxide is transparent to much incoming solar radiation but relatively opaque to outgoing terrestrial radiation.

## 6.4 Greenhouse Effect

Carbon dioxide is largely responsible for the greenhouse effect.

In simple terms:

1. sunlight reaches the earth;
2. earth absorbs energy and warms;
3. earth radiates heat outward;
4. carbon dioxide absorbs part of this heat;
5. some heat is sent back toward the surface;
6. the lower atmosphere becomes warmer.

## 6.5 Rising Carbon Dioxide

The volume of carbon dioxide has been rising in recent decades.

The main reason given in the chapter is burning of fossil fuels.

Examples of fossil fuels:

- coal,
- petroleum,
- natural gas.

## 6.6 Effect Of Rising Carbon Dioxide

Rising carbon dioxide increases the temperature of air by strengthening the greenhouse effect.

## 6.7 Carbon Dioxide And Plants

Plants use carbon dioxide for photosynthesis.

This is why carbon dioxide is life-supporting as well as climate-influencing.

---

# 7. Ozone

## 7.1 Meaning

Ozone is an important gas in the atmosphere.

It is especially important because it filters harmful solar radiation.

## 7.2 Height Range

Ozone is mainly found between 10 km and 50 km above the earth's surface.

## 7.3 Function

Ozone absorbs ultraviolet rays from the sun.

This prevents harmful ultraviolet radiation from reaching the earth's surface in full strength.

## 7.4 Why Ozone Matters

Without ozone, life would be exposed to intense ultraviolet radiation.

This could harm:

- humans,
- animals,
- plants,
- microorganisms,
- ecosystems.

## 7.5 Ozone And Stratosphere

The ozone layer is mainly located in the stratosphere.

This makes the stratosphere important for life on earth.

---

# 8. Water Vapour

## 8.1 Meaning

Water vapour is water in gaseous form present in the atmosphere.

It is a variable gas.

## 8.2 Variable Gas

Water vapour is called variable because its amount changes with:

- place,
- altitude,
- temperature,
- humidity,
- season,
- distance from water bodies,
- latitude.

## 8.3 Water Vapour With Altitude

Water vapour decreases with altitude.

Most water vapour is found in the lower atmosphere.

## 8.4 Water Vapour With Latitude

Water vapour decreases from the equator toward the poles.

Warm equatorial regions hold more moisture.

Cold polar regions hold less moisture.

## 8.5 Warm And Wet Tropics

In warm and wet tropical areas, water vapour may account for about 4 per cent of air by volume.

## 8.6 Dry And Cold Regions

In deserts and polar regions, water vapour may be less than 1 per cent of air by volume.

## 8.7 Water Vapour And Heat

Water vapour absorbs parts of incoming solar radiation.

It also preserves the earth's radiated heat.

## 8.8 Blanket Effect

Water vapour acts like a blanket.

It prevents the earth from becoming:

- too hot;
- too cold.

## 8.9 Water Vapour And Weather

Water vapour is important for:

- clouds,
- fog,
- rainfall,
- humidity,
- stability of air,
- instability of air,
- storms,
- weather change.

## 8.10 Stability And Instability

Water vapour contributes to air stability and instability.

Moist air may rise, cool, condense, and form clouds.

This affects rainfall and weather systems.

# 9. Dust Particles

## 9.1 Meaning

Dust particles are small solid particles suspended in the atmosphere.

## 9.2 Sources

Atmospheric dust particles may come from:

- sea salts,
- fine soil,
- smoke,
- soot,
- ash,
- pollen,
- dust,
- disintegrated particles of meteors.

## 9.3 Vertical Distribution

Dust particles are generally concentrated in the lower layers of the atmosphere.

However, convectional air currents can carry them to great heights.

## 9.4 Regional Distribution

Higher concentration of dust particles is found in subtropical and temperate regions.

Reason:

- dry winds are more common in these regions.

Dust concentration is generally lower in:

- equatorial regions,
- polar regions.

## 9.5 Hygroscopic Nuclei

Dust and salt particles act as hygroscopic nuclei.

This means water vapour condenses around them.

## 9.6 Dust And Cloud Formation

Cloud droplets need surfaces for condensation.

Dust and salt particles provide those surfaces.

Thus they help produce clouds.

## 9.7 Why Dust Particles Matter

Dust particles are important because they:

- help condensation;
- help cloud formation;
- influence visibility;
- affect air quality;
- transport material through the atmosphere;
- participate in weather processes.

# 11. Structure Of The Atmosphere

## 11.1 Layered Atmosphere

The atmosphere has different layers.

These layers differ in:

- density,
- temperature,
- composition,
- function,
- altitude.

## 11.2 Density With Height

Atmospheric density is highest near the earth's surface.

It decreases with increasing altitude.

This is because most air mass is pulled close to earth by gravity.

## 11.3 Temperature-Based Layers

The atmosphere is divided into layers mainly according to temperature conditions.

The main layers are:

1. troposphere;
2. stratosphere;
3. mesosphere;
4. thermosphere;
5. exosphere.

The chapter also discusses the ionosphere, the zone of charged particles important for radio communication.

---

# 12. Troposphere

## 12.1 Meaning

The troposphere is the lowermost layer of the atmosphere.

It is the layer closest to the earth's surface.

## 12.2 Average Height

The average height of the troposphere is about 13 km.

## 12.3 Height Near Poles And Equator

The troposphere extends:

- about 8 km near the poles;
- about 18 km near the equator.

## 12.4 Why It Is Thickest At The Equator

The troposphere is thickest at the equator because strong heating produces strong convectional currents.

These currents carry heat to greater heights.

## 12.5 Contents

The troposphere contains:

- dust particles,
- water vapour,
- most weather-producing gases,
- most atmospheric mass.

## 12.6 Weather Layer

All important changes in weather and climate occur in the troposphere.

Examples:

- clouds,
- rainfall,
- winds,
- storms,
- humidity changes,
- temperature changes.

## 12.7 Temperature Decrease

Temperature decreases with height in the troposphere.

The rate is about:

```text
1 degree Celsius for every 165 metres of height
```

This is the normal lapse rate used in this chapter.

## 12.8 Importance For Biological Activity

The troposphere is the most important layer for biological activity because:

- it contains oxygen for respiration;
- it contains water vapour;
- it contains weather systems;
- it supports the water cycle;
- it lies closest to living organisms;
- it controls daily weather conditions.

---

# 13. Tropopause

## 13.1 Meaning

The tropopause is the boundary between the troposphere and stratosphere.

## 13.2 Temperature At Tropopause

Approximate air temperature at the tropopause is:

- about -80 degrees Celsius over the equator;
- about -45 degrees Celsius over the poles.

## 13.3 Why It Is Called Tropopause

Temperature is nearly constant in this zone.

That is why it is called the tropopause.

## 13.4 Simple Memory Line

Troposphere changes.

Tropopause pauses.

---

# 14. Stratosphere

## 14.1 Location

The stratosphere lies above the tropopause.

It extends up to about 50 km.

## 14.2 Ozone Layer

The most important feature of the stratosphere is the ozone layer.

## 14.3 Importance Of Ozone Layer

The ozone layer absorbs ultraviolet radiation.

It shields life on earth from intense harmful energy.

## 14.4 Why Stratosphere Matters

The stratosphere is important because:

- it contains ozone;
- it reduces harmful ultraviolet radiation;
- it helps protect living organisms;
- it forms a stable upper layer above the weather-rich troposphere.

## 14.5 Stratosphere In Answer Writing

When writing about stratosphere, include:

- above tropopause;
- up to 50 km;
- contains ozone layer;
- absorbs ultraviolet radiation;
- protects life.

---

# 15. Mesosphere

## 15.1 Location

The mesosphere lies above the stratosphere.

It extends up to about 80 km.

## 15.2 Temperature Pattern

In the mesosphere, temperature decreases with increasing altitude.

At about 80 km height, temperature may reach around -100 degrees Celsius.

## 15.3 Mesopause

The upper limit of the mesosphere is called the mesopause.

## 15.4 Key Point

Remember:

> Mesosphere ends at mesopause.

---

# 16. Ionosphere

## 16.1 Location

The ionosphere is located in the upper atmosphere, beginning around the height of the mesosphere and extending into the thermosphere region.

For this chapter, remember the range:

```text
about 80 km to 400 km
```

## 16.2 Meaning

The ionosphere contains electrically charged particles called ions.

That is why it is called the ionosphere.

## 16.3 Radio Waves

Radio waves transmitted from the earth are reflected back by the ionosphere.

This makes the ionosphere important for long-distance radio communication.

## 16.4 Temperature Pattern

Temperature starts increasing with height in this upper atmospheric region.

## 16.5 Exam Point

If asked which layer reflects radio waves, answer:

> Ionosphere.

---

# 17. Exosphere

## 17.1 Meaning

The exosphere is the uppermost layer of the atmosphere.

It lies above the thermosphere.

## 17.2 Nature

The exosphere is:

- extremely thin;
- highly rarefied;
- poorly known compared with lower layers;
- gradually merging with outer space.

## 17.3 Contents

Whatever contents exist in the exosphere are very rarefied.

Particles are far apart.

## 17.4 Simple Memory Line

Exosphere is the exit layer of the atmosphere.

It gradually merges into outer space.

# 21. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 21.1 Thinking Atmosphere Is Only Oxygen

Oxygen is important, but nitrogen is the largest component.

Nitrogen forms about 78.08 per cent of air.

## 21.2 Ignoring Small Gases

Carbon dioxide is present in small quantity, but it has major climatic importance.

Ozone is also a small component, but it protects life from ultraviolet radiation.

## 21.3 Confusing Water Vapour And Clouds

Water vapour is invisible gas.

Clouds form when water vapour condenses around particles.

## 21.4 Forgetting Dust Particles

Dust particles are not useless impurities only.

They help condensation by acting as hygroscopic nuclei.

## 21.5 Confusing Troposphere And Stratosphere

Troposphere:

- weather layer,
- lowermost layer,
- water vapour and dust,
- biological activity.

Stratosphere:

- above tropopause,
- ozone layer,
- ultraviolet protection.

## 21.6 Confusing Tropopause And Mesopause

Tropopause separates troposphere and stratosphere.

Mesopause is the upper limit of mesosphere.

## 21.7 Forgetting Height Facts

Important height facts:

- 99 per cent atmospheric mass within 32 km;
- ozone between 10 and 50 km;
- troposphere average height 13 km;
- troposphere about 8 km at poles and 18 km at equator;
- stratosphere up to 50 km;
- mesosphere up to 80 km;
- oxygen negligible around 120 km;
- carbon dioxide and water vapour mainly up to 90 km.

# 24. Long Answer Guide

## 24.1 Composition Of The Atmosphere

Use this structure:

1. Define atmosphere.

The atmosphere is the envelope of air around the earth.

2. State the three components.

It is composed of gases, water vapour, and dust particles.

3. Explain permanent gases.

Nitrogen forms about 78.08 per cent and oxygen about 20.95 per cent.

Argon forms about 0.93 per cent, while carbon dioxide and trace gases occur in smaller quantities.

4. Explain carbon dioxide.

Carbon dioxide is important because it absorbs outgoing terrestrial radiation and contributes to the greenhouse effect.

Its quantity has risen due to burning of fossil fuels.

5. Explain ozone.

Ozone occurs mainly between 10 and 50 km and absorbs ultraviolet radiation.

6. Explain water vapour.

Water vapour is variable, decreases with height, is greater in warm wet tropics, and helps regulate temperature and cloud formation.

7. Explain dust particles.

Dust particles include sea salts, fine soil, smoke, soot, ash, pollen, and meteor dust.

They act as hygroscopic nuclei for condensation.

8. Conclude.

Thus even minor components of the atmosphere are essential for weather, climate, and life.

## 24.2 Structure Of The Atmosphere

Use this structure:

1. Start with density.

Atmospheric density is highest near the surface and decreases with altitude.

2. Name the layers.

The main layers are troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere/ionosphere region, and exosphere.

3. Troposphere.

It is the lowermost layer with average height 13 km, 8 km near poles and 18 km near equator.

Weather and climate changes occur here.

Temperature decreases at about 1 degree Celsius per 165 m.

4. Tropopause.

The tropopause separates troposphere from stratosphere and has nearly constant temperature.

5. Stratosphere.

It lies above the tropopause and extends up to 50 km.

It contains ozone which absorbs ultraviolet radiation.

6. Mesosphere.

It extends up to 80 km and temperature decreases to about -100 degrees Celsius.

Its upper limit is mesopause.

7. Ionosphere.

It contains charged particles and reflects radio waves.

8. Exosphere.

It is the uppermost rarefied layer and merges with outer space.

9. Conclude.

Geographers mainly focus on the lower layers, especially troposphere and stratosphere, because they directly affect life and weather.

# 27. Final Chapter Summary

The atmosphere is the envelope of air surrounding the earth. It is a mixture of gases, water vapour, and dust particles. It supports life through oxygen for humans and animals and carbon dioxide for plants. Air is colourless and odourless and is felt when it moves as wind.

The lower atmosphere is made mostly of nitrogen and oxygen. Nitrogen forms about 78.08 per cent of air, while oxygen forms about 20.95 per cent. Carbon dioxide is present in a small amount but is very important because it absorbs outgoing terrestrial radiation and contributes to the greenhouse effect. Its amount has increased because of fossil fuel burning. Ozone, found mainly between 10 and 50 km, absorbs ultraviolet rays and protects life.

Water vapour is a variable gas. It decreases with altitude and from equator toward poles. It is higher in warm wet tropics and lower in deserts and polar regions. It absorbs radiation, preserves earth's heat, and helps cloud and rainfall formation. Dust particles come from sea salts, soil, smoke, soot, ash, pollen, and meteor particles. They act as hygroscopic nuclei around which water vapour condenses.

The atmosphere is layered by density and temperature. The troposphere is the lowermost and most important layer because weather and biological activity occur here. The stratosphere contains ozone. The mesosphere extends up to about 80 km and ends at the mesopause. The ionosphere contains charged particles and reflects radio waves. The exosphere is the uppermost rarefied layer and gradually merges with outer space.

To master this chapter, remember composition first, then layer order, then the function of each component and layer.
