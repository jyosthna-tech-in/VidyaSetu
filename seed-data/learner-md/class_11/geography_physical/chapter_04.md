## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains why continents and oceans are not fixed.

The present arrangement of continents and oceans is only one stage in a long geological history. Continents were once joined, later separated, and are still moving today as parts of lithospheric plates.

The central idea is:

> Continents and oceans change position because the earth's lithosphere is divided into moving plates, and the strongest modern explanation for this movement is plate tectonics.

# 1. Continents And Oceans Are Not Fixed

## 1.1 Present Distribution

Continents cover about 29 percent of the earth's surface.

The remaining about 71 percent is covered by ocean water.

## 1.2 Key Idea

The present positions of continents and oceans have not always been the same.

They will also not remain the same in the future.

## 1.3 Main Questions

This chapter answers:

- What were the past positions of continents?
- Why did continents and oceans change position?
- How do scientists know earlier positions?
- What is the modern explanation for plate movement?

---

# 2. Continental Drift

## 2.1 Early Observations

The coastlines on either side of the Atlantic Ocean look like they could fit together.

This made many thinkers imagine that the Americas, Europe, and Africa may once have been joined.

## 2.2 Abraham Ortelius

Abraham Ortelius, a Dutch map-maker, suggested this possibility as early as 1596.

## 2.3 Antonio Pellegrini

Antonio Pellegrini drew a map showing the continents joined together.

## 2.4 Alfred Wegener

Alfred Wegener, a German meteorologist, gave a comprehensive theory in 1912.

His idea is called the continental drift theory.

## 2.5 Wegener's Main Claim

Wegener argued that all continents were once joined into one supercontinent.

This supercontinent was called Pangaea.

It was surrounded by one huge ocean called Panthalassa.

## 2.6 Meaning Of Names

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Pangaea | All earth |
| Panthalassa | All water |

## 2.7 Breakup Of Pangaea

Around 200 million years ago, Pangaea began to split.

First it broke into:

- Laurasia in the north,
- Gondwanaland in the south.

Later, these broke into smaller continents that exist today.

---

# 3. Evidence For Continental Drift

Wegener and later researchers used several kinds of evidence.

The major evidence includes:

1. matching of continents,
2. rocks of same age across oceans,
3. tillite deposits,
4. placer deposits,
5. distribution of fossils.

---

# 4. Matching Of Continents: Jig-Saw Fit

## 4.1 Africa And South America

The Atlantic-facing coasts of Africa and South America show a remarkable match.

This looks like pieces of a jig-saw puzzle.

## 4.2 Bullard's Computer Fit

In 1964, Bullard used a computer programme to find the best fit of Atlantic margins.

The fit was very strong when tested at the 1,000-fathom line, not merely the present shoreline.

## 4.3 Why 1,000-Fathom Line Matters

Present shorelines change due to erosion, deposition, and sea-level changes.

The 1,000-fathom line gives a better geological margin for matching continents.

## 4.4 Exam Point

If asked for the first evidence of continental drift, mention the jig-saw fit of Africa and South America.

---

# 5. Rocks Of Same Age Across Oceans

## 5.1 Radiometric Dating

Radiometric dating helped scientists compare the age of rocks on different continents.

## 5.2 Brazil And West Africa

Ancient rock belts about 2,000 million years old on the Brazil coast match with rocks of western Africa.

## 5.3 Jurassic Marine Deposits

The earliest marine deposits along the coastlines of South America and Africa are of Jurassic age.

This suggests that the Atlantic Ocean did not exist before that time in its present form.

## 5.4 Importance

Matching rock age and structure across oceans supports the idea that continents were once connected.

---

# 6. Tillite Evidence

## 6.1 Meaning Of Tillite

Tillite is a sedimentary rock formed from glacial deposits.

## 6.2 Gondwana Sediments

The Gondwana sedimentary system of India has counterparts in several southern hemisphere landmasses.

Similar successions are found in:

- Africa,
- Falkland Islands,
- Madagascar,
- Antarctica,
- Australia.

## 6.3 What Tillite Shows

Thick tillite at the base of the sequence indicates extensive and prolonged glaciation.

## 6.4 Importance

Similar glacial deposits across separated continents show:

- similar geological history,
- similar ancient climate,
- former connection or closeness of landmasses.

This is strong evidence of continental drift and palaeoclimate.

---

# 7. Placer Deposits

## 7.1 Ghana Gold Deposits

Rich placer deposits of gold occur on the Ghana coast.

But there is no clear source rock in that region.

## 7.2 Brazil Source

Gold-bearing veins occur in Brazil.

This suggests that the Ghana deposits may have been derived from the Brazil plateau when the two regions were joined.

## 7.3 Importance

Placer deposits support continental drift because mineral sources and deposits make more sense when continents are reconstructed together.

---

# 8. Fossil Evidence

## 8.1 Problem Of Distribution

Identical land or freshwater species found across ocean barriers create a problem.

Such organisms could not easily cross wide oceans.

## 8.2 Lemurs And Lemuria

Lemurs occur in India, Madagascar, and Africa.

This led some scientists to imagine a land connection called Lemuria.

## 8.3 Mesosaurus

Mesosaurus was a small reptile adapted to shallow brackish water.

Its skeletons are found in:

- southern Cape province of South Africa,
- Iraver formations of Brazil.

These places are now about 4,800 km apart with an ocean between them.

## 8.4 Importance

Fossil similarity suggests that these landmasses were once connected or much closer.

---

# 9. Wegener's Forces For Drift

Wegener suggested two forces:

1. pole-fleeing force,
2. tidal force.

## 9.1 Pole-Fleeing Force

Pole-fleeing force relates to earth's rotation.

Earth is not a perfect sphere.

It bulges at the equator due to rotation.

Wegener thought this force could help move continents away from poles.

## 9.2 Tidal Force

Tidal force relates to attraction of the moon and the sun.

These attractions cause tides in ocean water.

Wegener thought they might help move continents over millions of years.

## 9.3 Criticism

Most scholars considered these forces too weak to move continents.

This was a major weakness of the continental drift theory.

---

# 10. Post-Drift Discoveries

After Wegener, especially after World War II, scientists gained new information from ocean floor studies.

These discoveries revived interest in the distribution of continents and oceans.

## 10.1 Key Change

Wegener's evidence came mostly from continents.

Post-drift discoveries came largely from ocean floors.

## 10.2 Importance

Ocean floor mapping showed that ocean basins are active, structured, and geologically young.

This helped develop sea floor spreading and plate tectonics.

---

# 11. Convection Current Theory

## 11.1 Arthur Holmes

Arthur Holmes discussed convection currents in the mantle in the 1930s.

## 11.2 Main Idea

Radioactive elements generate heat inside the earth.

This creates thermal differences in the mantle.

Heated material rises, spreads, cools, and sinks.

This creates convection currents.

## 11.3 Importance

Holmes' idea offered a possible driving force for continental movement.

It later influenced Harry Hess' sea floor spreading idea.

## 11.4 Simple Sequence

```text
Radioactive heat
-> mantle temperature differences
-> hot material rises
-> cool material sinks
-> convection current
-> plate movement
```

---

# 12. Mapping Of The Ocean Floor

## 12.1 Ocean Floor Is Not Plain

Detailed research showed that the ocean floor is not a flat plain.

It has:

- submerged mountain ranges,
- deep trenches,
- abyssal plains,
- ridges,
- volcanic zones.

## 12.2 Post-War Expeditions

Ocean floor mapping after World War II revealed:

- mid-oceanic ridges,
- deep trenches near continental margins,
- active volcanic zones at ridges,
- young oceanic rocks.

## 12.3 Rock Age Pattern

Rocks on both sides of mid-ocean ridges are similar in:

- age,
- composition,
- magnetic properties.

Rocks become older as distance from ridge crest increases.

This pattern became crucial for sea floor spreading.

---

# 13. Ocean Floor Configuration

The ocean floor can be divided into three major divisions:

1. continental margins,
2. deep-sea basins,
3. mid-ocean ridges.

---

# 14. Continental Margins

## 14.1 Meaning

Continental margins form the transition between continental shores and deep-sea basins.

## 14.2 Components

They include:

- continental shelf,
- continental slope,
- continental rise,
- deep-oceanic trenches.

## 14.3 Deep-Oceanic Trenches

Deep-oceanic trenches are especially important for this chapter.

They are linked with subduction, earthquakes, and consumption of oceanic crust.

---

# 15. Abyssal Plains

## 15.1 Meaning

Abyssal plains are extensive deep-sea plains.

They lie between continental margins and mid-oceanic ridges.

## 15.2 Sediments

They receive sediments that move beyond continental margins.

## 15.3 Importance

Abyssal plains show that ocean basins have depositional areas, not only ridges and trenches.

---

# 16. Mid-Oceanic Ridges

## 16.1 Meaning

Mid-oceanic ridges are interconnected submerged mountain systems in the ocean.

## 16.2 Scale

They form the longest mountain chain on the surface of the earth, though submerged.

## 16.3 Structure

They are characterised by:

- central rift system at the crest,
- fractured plateau,
- flank zones.

## 16.4 Volcanic Activity

The rift system at the crest is a zone of intense volcanic activity.

New oceanic crust forms here.

---

# 17. Distribution Of Earthquakes And Volcanoes

## 17.1 Mid-Ocean Ridge Belt

Earthquake and volcanic activity occurs along mid-oceanic ridges.

In the Atlantic, a line of activity runs through the central ocean, roughly parallel to coastlines.

It extends into the Indian Ocean.

## 17.2 Alpine-Himalayan And Pacific Belts

Another belt of earthquake and volcanic activity follows:

- Alpine-Himalayan system,
- rim of the Pacific Ocean.

## 17.3 Shallow And Deep Earthquakes

Earthquakes at mid-oceanic ridges are generally shallow.

Earthquakes along the Alpine-Himalayan belt and Pacific rim may be deep-seated.

## 17.4 Ring Of Fire

The Pacific rim is called the Ring of Fire because it has many active volcanoes.

## 17.5 Importance

Earthquakes and volcanoes are not randomly distributed.

Their distribution matches plate boundaries.

---

# 18. Sea Floor Spreading

## 18.1 Background

Ocean floor mapping and palaeomagnetic studies revealed facts not available to Wegener.

These facts led Harry Hess to propose sea floor spreading in 1961.

## 18.2 Main Idea

Sea floor spreading means new oceanic crust forms at mid-ocean ridges and moves away from the ridge.

At trenches, old oceanic crust sinks and is consumed.

## 18.3 Hess' Explanation

Constant eruptions at ridge crests rupture the oceanic crust.

New lava wedges into the gap.

This pushes oceanic crust on both sides away from the ridge.

The ocean floor spreads.

## 18.4 Consumption At Trenches

If new crust forms continuously, old crust must be removed somewhere.

Hess argued that oceanic crust sinks at oceanic trenches and gets consumed.

This explains why ocean floors are young.

---

# 19. Evidence For Sea Floor Spreading

## 19.1 Volcanism At Ridges

Volcanic eruptions are common along mid-oceanic ridges.

They bring large amounts of lava to the surface.

## 19.2 Symmetry Of Rocks

Rocks at equal distances on both sides of ridge crests show remarkable similarity in:

- age,
- chemical composition,
- magnetic properties.

## 19.3 Age Pattern

Rocks closest to ridges are youngest.

Rock age increases away from the ridge crest.

## 19.4 Magnetic Polarity

Rocks near ridges show normal polarity.

Oceanic rocks record alternating bands of normal and reverse magnetic polarity.

These bands are parallel to mid-oceanic ridges.

## 19.5 Oceanic Crust Is Young

Oceanic crust is nowhere older than about 200 million years.

Some continental rocks are as old as about 3,200 million years.

## 19.6 Thin Ocean Sediments

Sediments on the ocean floor are unexpectedly thin.

No sediment column was found older than about 200 million years.

## 19.7 Earthquake Depth Pattern

Trenches have deep-seated earthquakes.

Mid-ocean ridges have shallow-focus earthquakes.

## 19.8 Conclusion

These facts support creation of crust at ridges and consumption at trenches.

# 20. Plate Tectonics

## 20.1 Development

After sea floor spreading, interest in continental and ocean distribution revived.

In 1967, McKenzie and Parker, and also Morgan, independently developed the concept of plate tectonics.

## 20.2 Meaning Of Plate

A tectonic plate, or lithospheric plate, is a massive irregular slab of solid rock.

It may include:

- continental lithosphere,
- oceanic lithosphere,
- both.

## 20.3 Plate Movement

Plates move horizontally over the asthenosphere as rigid units.

## 20.4 Lithosphere Thickness

The lithosphere includes:

- crust,
- top mantle.

Its thickness varies:

- about 5-100 km in oceanic parts,
- about 200 km in continental areas.

## 20.5 Continental And Oceanic Plates

A plate is called continental or oceanic depending on which type occupies a larger part.

Examples:

- Pacific plate is largely oceanic;
- Eurasian plate is largely continental.

## 20.6 Major Idea

It is not simply continents that move.

Continents are parts of plates.

Plates move.

---

# 21. Major Plates

The earth's lithosphere is divided into seven major plates and several minor plates.

The major plates are:

1. Antarctica and surrounding oceanic plate,
2. North American plate,
3. South American plate,
4. Pacific plate,
5. India-Australia-New Zealand plate,
6. Africa with eastern Atlantic floor plate,
7. Eurasia and adjacent oceanic plate.

## 21.1 Plate Boundary Features

Major plates are often surrounded by:

- young fold mountain ridges,
- trenches,
- faults.

These features mark plate interactions.

# 23. Plate Boundaries

There are three main types of plate boundaries:

1. divergent boundaries,
2. convergent boundaries,
3. transform boundaries.

---

# 24. Divergent Boundaries

## 24.1 Meaning

Divergent boundaries occur where plates move away from each other.

## 24.2 Crust Formation

New crust is generated at divergent boundaries.

These are also called spreading sites.

## 24.3 Example

The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is the best-known example.

At this ridge, American plates separate from the Eurasian and African plates.

## 24.4 Associated Features

Divergent boundaries are associated with:

- mid-ocean ridges,
- shallow earthquakes,
- volcanic activity,
- new oceanic crust.

---

# 25. Convergent Boundaries

## 25.1 Meaning

Convergent boundaries occur where plates move toward each other.

## 25.2 Crust Destruction

Crust is destroyed when one plate dives under another.

This sinking zone is called a subduction zone.

## 25.3 Types Of Convergence

Convergence may occur:

1. between oceanic and continental plate,
2. between two oceanic plates,
3. between two continental plates.

## 25.4 Associated Features

Convergent boundaries may form:

- trenches,
- volcanic arcs,
- fold mountains,
- deep earthquakes,
- subduction zones.

## 25.5 Indian Example

The Himalayas formed from continent-continent convergence between the Indian plate and Eurasian plate.

---

# 26. Transform Boundaries

## 26.1 Meaning

Transform boundaries occur where plates slide horizontally past each other.

## 26.2 Crust Balance

At transform boundaries:

- crust is not created,
- crust is not destroyed.

## 26.3 Transform Faults

Transform faults are planes of separation, usually perpendicular to mid-oceanic ridges.

## 26.4 Cause Of Movement

Different parts of a plate may move away from ridge axes at different rates or times.

Earth's rotation also affects separated blocks.

This creates transform faults.

---

# 27. Rates Of Plate Movement

## 27.1 How Rates Are Measured

Strips of normal and reverse magnetic polarity parallel to mid-oceanic ridges help scientists determine plate movement rates.

The age and distance of magnetic stripes show how fast the plate has moved.

## 27.2 Variation In Rates

Rates vary significantly.

Examples:

- Arctic Ridge: less than 2.5 cm per year;
- East Pacific Rise near Easter Island: more than 15 cm per year.

## 27.3 Importance

Plate movement is slow by human standards.

But over millions of years, even a few centimetres per year can move continents across oceans.

---

# 28. Force For Plate Movement

## 28.1 Dynamic Earth

The earth is not static.

Both surface and interior are dynamic.

## 28.2 Convection Cells

Hot softened mantle material below rigid plates moves in a circular manner.

The cycle is:

1. heated material rises,
2. it spreads near the surface,
3. it cools,
4. it sinks back,
5. the cycle repeats.

This is called a convection cell or convective flow.

## 28.3 Sources Of Heat

Heat inside the earth comes mainly from:

- radioactive decay,
- residual heat from earth's formation.

## 28.4 Driving Force

The slow movement of hot, softened mantle below rigid plates is the driving force of plate movement.

---

# 29. Movement Of The Indian Plate

## 29.1 Indian Plate Components

The Indian plate includes:

- Peninsular India,
- Australian continental portions.

## 29.2 Northern Boundary

The northern boundary lies along the Himalayas.

It is a continent-continent convergence zone.

The Indian plate converges with the Eurasian plate.

## 29.3 Eastern Boundary

In the east, the boundary extends through:

- Rakhine Yoma mountains of Myanmar,
- island arc along the Java Trench.

## 29.4 Eastern Margin Near Australia

The eastern margin is a spreading site east of Australia in the southwest Pacific.

## 29.5 Western Boundary

The western margin follows:

- Kirthar Mountains of Pakistan,
- Makran coast,
- Red Sea rift spreading site,
- Chagos Archipelago region.

## 29.6 Southern Boundary

The boundary between Indian and Antarctic plates is marked by an oceanic ridge.

It runs roughly west-east and merges with a spreading site south of New Zealand.

---

# 30. Indian Plate Journey

## 30.1 India As Island

India was once a large island off the Australian coast.

It lay in a vast ocean.

The Tethys Sea separated India from Asia until about 225 million years ago.

## 30.2 Northward Movement

India began moving north around 200 million years ago when Pangaea broke.

## 30.3 Position Around 140 Million Years Ago

Around 140 million years before present, the Indian subcontinent was located near 50 degrees S latitude.

## 30.4 Deccan Traps

During the northward movement of the Indian plate, a major lava outpouring occurred.

This formed the Deccan Traps.

The event began around 60 million years ago and continued for a long period.

At that time, the subcontinent was still close to the equator.

## 30.5 Collision With Asia

India collided with Asia about 40-50 million years ago.

This caused rapid uplift of the Himalayas.

The process of Himalayan uplift is still continuing.

# 34. Common Misunderstandings

## 34.1 Continents Move Alone

Incorrect.

In modern plate tectonics, continents are parts of plates.

Plates move, carrying continents with them.

## 34.2 Wegener Had No Evidence

Incorrect.

Wegener had strong continental evidence, but his explanation of force was weak.

## 34.3 Ocean Floors Are As Old As Continents

Incorrect.

Oceanic crust is much younger, nowhere more than about 200 million years old in the textbook account.

## 34.4 Sea Floor Spreading Only Creates Crust

Incomplete.

It creates crust at ridges, but old crust must also be consumed at trenches.

## 34.5 All Plate Boundaries Create Crust

Incorrect.

Only divergent boundaries create new crust.

Convergent boundaries destroy or deform crust.

Transform boundaries neither create nor destroy crust.

## 34.6 Himalayas Formed By Ocean-Continent Convergence

Incorrect.

The Himalayas formed mainly by continent-continent convergence between Indian and Eurasian plates.

Wegener suggested pole-fleeing force and tidal force.

Pole-fleeing force was linked with earth's rotation and equatorial bulge.

Tidal force was linked with the attraction of the moon and sun.

These forces were later considered too weak to move continents.

## 35.3 Short Answer: Mantle Convection

Convection currents in the mantle are initiated by heat from radioactive decay and residual heat inside the earth.

Heated material rises, spreads, cools, and sinks again.

This circular movement is maintained as a convection cell and helps drive plate movement.

## 35.4 Short Answer: Transform vs Convergent/Divergent Boundary

At divergent boundaries, plates move apart and new crust forms.

At convergent boundaries, plates move together and crust may be destroyed or mountains may form.

At transform boundaries, plates slide past each other horizontally, and crust is neither created nor destroyed.

## 35.5 Short Answer: Indian Landmass During Deccan Traps

During formation of the Deccan Traps, the Indian landmass was moving northward and was still close to the equator.

The lava outpouring began around 60 million years ago.

# 39. Final Chapter Summary

Continents and oceans are not permanent in their present positions. Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912, arguing that all continents were once joined as Pangaea and surrounded by Panthalassa. Pangaea later split into Laurasia and Gondwanaland and eventually into present continents.

Evidence for continental drift includes matching coastlines, similar rocks across oceans, Gondwana tillites, placer deposits, and fossil similarities. Wegener's weakness was that his proposed forces, pole-fleeing and tidal force, were too weak.

Post-World War II ocean-floor mapping changed the debate. Scientists discovered mid-ocean ridges, trenches, young oceanic rocks, thin sediments, symmetrical magnetic stripes, and earthquake-volcano belts. Hess used these to propose sea floor spreading: new crust forms at ridges and old crust is consumed at trenches.

Plate tectonics explains the whole system. Lithospheric plates move over the asthenosphere. Their boundaries may be divergent, convergent, or transform. These boundaries explain ridges, trenches, earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountains.

The Indian plate moved north after Pangaea broke. Its movement caused the Deccan Trap lava outpouring and later the collision with Asia, producing the Himalayas. The process of uplift continues even today.
