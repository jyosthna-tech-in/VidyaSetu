# 1. Water In The Atmosphere

## 1.1 Water Vapour In Air

Air contains water vapour.

Water vapour varies from:

```text
0 to 4 per cent by volume of the atmosphere
```

Even though this amount is small, it is very important for weather.

## 1.2 Forms Of Water In Atmosphere

Water is present in the atmosphere in three forms:

1. gaseous form;
2. liquid form;
3. solid form.

## 1.3 Examples

| Form | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Gaseous | Water vapour |
| Liquid | Cloud droplets, fog, rain |
| Solid | Ice crystals, snow, hail, frost |

## 1.4 Sources Of Atmospheric Moisture

Atmospheric moisture comes from:

- water bodies through evaporation;
- plants through transpiration.

## 1.5 Continuous Water Exchange

Water is continuously exchanged between:

- atmosphere,
- oceans,
- continents.

Main processes:

- evaporation,
- transpiration,
- condensation,
- precipitation.

## 1.6 Water Cycle Link

The chapter is part of the larger water cycle.

Process chain:

```text
Evaporation/transpiration -> water vapour -> condensation -> clouds -> precipitation -> return to surface
```

# 2. Humidity

## 2.1 Meaning

Humidity means water vapour present in the air.

## 2.2 Why Humidity Matters

Humidity affects:

- comfort,
- cloud formation,
- rainfall,
- fog,
- dew,
- frost,
- storm development,
- evaporation rate.

## 2.3 Ways To Express Humidity

Humidity is expressed quantitatively in different ways.

The chapter focuses on:

- absolute humidity;
- relative humidity.

---

# 3. Absolute Humidity

## 3.1 Meaning

Absolute humidity is the actual amount of water vapour present in the atmosphere.

## 3.2 Measurement

It is the weight of water vapour per unit volume of air.

Unit:

```text
grams per cubic metre
```

## 3.3 Variation

Absolute humidity differs from place to place.

It depends on:

- temperature,
- nearness to water bodies,
- evaporation,
- vegetation,
- wind,
- season.

## 3.4 Temperature Link

The ability of air to hold water vapour depends entirely on its temperature.

Warm air can hold more water vapour.

Cold air can hold less water vapour.

---

# 4. Relative Humidity

## 4.1 Meaning

Relative humidity is the percentage of moisture present in the atmosphere compared with its full capacity at a given temperature.

## 4.2 Simple Formula Idea

```text
Relative humidity = actual moisture / moisture-holding capacity x 100
```

## 4.3 Temperature Effect

When air temperature changes, the moisture-holding capacity of air changes.

Therefore, relative humidity also changes.

## 4.4 Warm Air And Relative Humidity

If temperature rises and actual moisture stays the same, relative humidity decreases.

Reason:

- warm air can hold more moisture.

## 4.5 Cool Air And Relative Humidity

If temperature falls and actual moisture stays the same, relative humidity increases.

Reason:

- cool air can hold less moisture.

## 4.6 Distribution

Relative humidity is:

- greater over oceans;
- least over continents.

Reason:

- oceans provide abundant moisture;
- continental interiors are often drier.

---

# 5. Saturated Air And Dew Point

## 5.1 Saturated Air

Air containing moisture to its full capacity at a given temperature is called saturated air.

## 5.2 Meaning Of Saturation

Saturated air cannot hold additional moisture at that temperature.

Any further cooling or addition of moisture may cause condensation.

## 5.3 Dew Point

Dew point is the temperature at which saturation occurs in a given sample of air.

## 5.4 Dew Point Importance

Dew point helps explain:

- dew,
- frost,
- fog,
- clouds,
- rainfall.

## 5.5 Simple Chain

```text
Air cools -> reaches dew point -> becomes saturated -> condensation begins
```

---

# 6. Evaporation

## 6.1 Meaning

Evaporation is the process by which water changes from liquid to gaseous state.

## 6.2 Main Cause

Heat is the main cause of evaporation.

## 6.3 Latent Heat Of Vapourisation

Latent heat of vapourisation is the heat energy involved in transforming liquid water into water vapour.

## 6.4 Temperature And Evaporation

Increase in temperature increases:

- water absorption capacity of air;
- moisture retention capacity of air;
- evaporation rate.

## 6.5 Dry Air And Evaporation

If moisture content of air is low, air has more potential to absorb and retain moisture.

Dry air favours evaporation.

## 6.6 Wind And Evaporation

Movement of air replaces saturated air near the water surface with unsaturated air.

Therefore:

```text
Greater air movement -> greater evaporation
```

## 6.7 Factors Favouring Evaporation

Evaporation is high when:

- temperature is high;
- air is dry;
- wind is strong;
- water surface is exposed;
- humidity is low.

---

# 7. Condensation

## 7.1 Meaning

Condensation is the transformation of water vapour into liquid water.

## 7.2 Main Cause

Condensation is caused by loss of heat.

## 7.3 Process

Process chain:

1. moist air cools;
2. its capacity to hold water vapour decreases;
3. air reaches dew point;
4. excess water vapour condenses;
5. liquid droplets or ice crystals form.

## 7.4 Sublimation

If water vapour directly condenses into solid form, the process is called sublimation.

## 7.5 Condensation In Free Air

In free air, condensation occurs around tiny particles called hygroscopic condensation nuclei.

## 7.6 Good Condensation Nuclei

Good nuclei include:

- dust,
- smoke,
- salt particles from ocean.

These particles absorb water.

## 7.7 Condensation On Cold Objects

Condensation may also occur when moist air touches a colder object.

This produces dew or frost on surfaces.

## 7.8 Controls Of Condensation

Condensation depends on:

- amount of cooling;
- relative humidity;
- volume of air;
- temperature;
- pressure;
- moisture content.

## 7.9 Conditions For Condensation

Condensation may occur when:

1. air temperature is reduced to dew point while volume remains constant;
2. both volume and temperature are reduced;
3. moisture is added to the air through evaporation.

## 7.10 Most Favourable Condition

The most favourable condition for condensation is decrease in air temperature.

---

# 8. Forms Of Condensation

## 8.1 Main Forms

After condensation, moisture in the atmosphere may appear as:

- dew,
- frost,
- fog,
- clouds.

## 8.2 Basis Of Classification

Forms of condensation are classified on the basis of:

- temperature;
- location.

## 8.3 Dew Point And Freezing Point

Condensation may occur:

- when dew point is above freezing point;
- when dew point is at or below freezing point.

This difference helps separate dew from frost.

---

# 9. Dew

## 9.1 Meaning

Dew is moisture deposited as water droplets on cool solid surfaces.

Examples of surfaces:

- stones,
- grass blades,
- plant leaves,
- metal surfaces,
- soil surface.

## 9.2 How Dew Forms

Formation sequence:

1. night becomes cold;
2. solid surfaces lose heat;
3. air touching them cools;
4. air reaches dew point;
5. water vapour condenses as droplets;
6. dew forms.

## 9.3 Dew Forms On Surfaces

Dew forms on solid objects, not around nuclei in free air above the surface.

## 9.4 Ideal Conditions

Ideal conditions for dew:

- clear sky;
- calm air;
- high relative humidity;
- cold and long nights.

## 9.5 Dew Point Condition

For dew formation, dew point must be above freezing point.

---

# 10. Frost

## 10.1 Meaning

Frost forms when moisture is deposited as ice crystals on cold surfaces.

## 10.2 Temperature Condition

Frost forms when condensation takes place below freezing point.

The dew point is at or below 0 degrees Celsius.

## 10.3 How Frost Forms

Formation sequence:

1. surface becomes very cold;
2. air touching surface cools to freezing or below;
3. water vapour deposits directly as ice crystals;
4. frost appears.

## 10.4 Frost vs Dew

| Basis | Dew | Frost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Form | Water droplets | Ice crystals |
| Temperature | Dew point above freezing | Dew point at or below freezing |
| Surface | Cool surfaces | Cold/freezing surfaces |
| Conditions | Clear, calm, humid, long cold night | Same as dew plus temperature at/below freezing |

---

# 11. Fog, Mist, And Smog

## 11.1 Fog

Fog forms when an air mass containing much water vapour cools suddenly and condensation occurs on fine dust particles.

Fog is a cloud with its base at or very near the ground.

## 11.2 Visibility

Fog and mist reduce visibility.

Visibility may become poor or nearly zero.

## 11.3 Urban Fog

In urban and industrial centres, smoke provides many condensation nuclei.

This helps fog and mist formation.

## 11.4 Smog

Smog is a mixture of smoke and fog.

## 11.5 Mist

Mist is similar to fog but differs in moisture and visibility characteristics.

The chapter describes mist as having thicker moisture layers around nuclei.

Mists are frequent over mountains where rising warm air meets a cold surface.

## 11.6 Fog Conditions

Fogs are common where warm currents of air meet cold currents.

Fog may be understood as a mini cloud near the ground, formed around dust, smoke, and salt particles.

## 11.7 Fog vs Cloud

| Basis | Fog | Cloud |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Location | At or near ground | At some height above surface |
| Visibility impact | Strongly reduces visibility at ground | May not reduce ground visibility directly |
| Formation | Condensation near ground | Condensation in free air aloft |

---

# 12. Clouds

## 12.1 Meaning

A cloud is a mass of minute water droplets or tiny ice crystals formed by condensation of water vapour in free air.

## 12.2 Height

Clouds form at some height above the earth's surface.

Because they form at different heights and conditions, they take different shapes.

## 12.3 Basis Of Classification

Clouds are grouped according to:

- height,
- expanse,
- density,
- transparency or opaqueness.

## 12.4 Four Main Types

The four main cloud types are:

1. cirrus;
2. cumulus;
3. stratus;
4. nimbus.

---

# 13. Cirrus Clouds

## 13.1 Height

Cirrus clouds form at high altitudes:

```text
8000 to 12000 m
```

## 13.2 Appearance

They are:

- thin,
- detached,
- feathery,
- white.

## 13.3 Memory Line

Cirrus clouds are high, thin, white, and feathery.

---

# 14. Cumulus Clouds

## 14.1 Height

Cumulus clouds generally form at:

```text
4000 to 7000 m
```

## 14.2 Appearance

They look like cotton wool.

They:

- exist in patches;
- are scattered;
- have a flat base.

## 14.3 Weather Link

Cumulus clouds can grow vertically.

When strongly developed, they may become cumulonimbus clouds associated with thunderstorms.

---

# 15. Stratus Clouds

## 15.1 Meaning

Stratus clouds are layered clouds.

## 15.2 Appearance

They cover large portions of the sky.

## 15.3 Formation

They generally form due to:

- loss of heat;
- mixing of air masses with different temperatures.

---

# 16. Nimbus Clouds

## 16.1 Appearance

Nimbus clouds are black or dark grey.

## 16.2 Height

They form at:

- middle levels;
- or very near the surface.

## 16.3 Properties

Nimbus clouds are:

- extremely dense;
- opaque to sun rays;
- shapeless masses of thick vapour.

## 16.4 Weather Link

Nimbus clouds are rain-bearing clouds.

---

High clouds include:

## 17.2 Middle Clouds

Middle clouds include:

## 17.3 Low Clouds

Low clouds include:

## 17.4 Clouds With Vertical Development

Clouds with extensive vertical development include:

# 18. Precipitation

## 18.1 Meaning

Precipitation is the release of moisture from the atmosphere after condensation.

It occurs when condensed particles grow large enough to fall to the earth under gravity.

## 18.2 Formation Process

Process chain:

```text
Condensation -> droplets/ice crystals grow -> air cannot hold them -> gravity pulls them down -> precipitation
```

## 18.3 Forms

Precipitation may occur in liquid or solid form.

Main forms:

- rainfall,
- snowfall,
- sleet,
- hail.

---

# 19. Rainfall And Snowfall

## 19.1 Rainfall

Precipitation in the form of liquid water is called rainfall.

## 19.2 Snowfall

When temperature is below 0 degrees Celsius, precipitation may occur as fine flakes of snow.

This is called snowfall.

## 19.3 Snow Crystals

Snow forms as hexagonal crystals.

These crystals combine to form flakes of snow.

---

# 20. Sleet And Hail

## 20.1 Sleet

Sleet is frozen raindrops or refrozen melted snow-water.

## 20.2 Sleet Formation

Sleet forms when:

1. a warmer layer of air lies above a subfreezing layer near the ground;
2. raindrops leave warmer air;
3. they encounter colder air below;
4. they freeze into small ice pellets;
5. they reach the ground as sleet.

## 20.3 Hailstones

Hailstones are small rounded solid pieces of ice that reach the earth's surface.

## 20.4 Hail Formation

Hailstones form when raindrops pass through colder layers and freeze.

They often have several concentric layers of ice.

## 20.5 Sleet vs Hail

| Basis | Sleet | Hail |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Form | Small frozen raindrops or refrozen snow-water | Rounded ice pieces |
| Size | Usually not bigger than original raindrops | Can grow larger in strong storms |
| Formation | Warm layer above cold near-surface layer | Repeated freezing in cold cloud layers |
| Occurrence | Limited | Sporadic and local |

# 21. Types Of Rainfall

## 21.1 Classification By Origin

Rainfall may be classified into three main types:

1. convectional rainfall;
2. orographic or relief rainfall;
3. cyclonic or frontal rainfall.

---

# 22. Convectional Rainfall

## 22.1 Basic Cause

Convectional rainfall is caused by strong heating of air near the surface.

## 22.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. surface is heated strongly;
2. air near surface becomes warm and light;
3. warm air rises in convection currents;
4. rising air expands;
5. air loses heat;
6. condensation occurs;
7. cumulus clouds form;
8. heavy rainfall occurs, often with thunder and lightning.

## 22.3 Duration

Convectional rain is usually heavy but does not last long.

## 22.4 Occurrence

It is common:

- in summer;
- during the hotter part of the day;
- in equatorial regions;
- in interior parts of continents, especially in the northern hemisphere.

---

# 23. Orographic Or Relief Rainfall

## 23.1 Basic Cause

Orographic rainfall occurs when a saturated air mass is forced to rise over a mountain.

## 23.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. moist air approaches a mountain;
2. mountain forces air to ascend;
3. rising air expands;
4. temperature falls;
5. moisture condenses;
6. rainfall occurs on windward slope.

## 23.3 Windward Slope

The windward slope receives more rainfall.

Reason:

- air is forced upward there;
- cooling and condensation occur there.

## 23.4 Leeward Slope

After giving rainfall, air descends on the other side.

As it descends:

- temperature rises;
- moisture-holding capacity increases;
- rainfall decreases.

## 23.5 Rain-Shadow Area

The dry area on the leeward side of a mountain is called the rain-shadow area.

## 23.6 Memory Chain

```text
Moist air -> mountain barrier -> forced ascent -> cooling -> condensation -> windward rain -> descending dry air -> leeward rain shadow
```

---

# 24. Cyclonic Or Frontal Rainfall

## 24.1 Meaning

Cyclonic rainfall is rainfall associated with cyclones or fronts.

It is also called frontal rainfall when it occurs along fronts.

## 24.2 Link With Previous Chapter

Extra tropical cyclones and fronts were explained in the previous chapter.

When warm and cold air masses meet, warm air rises over cold air.

This cooling causes condensation, clouds, and rainfall.

## 24.3 Where Common

Cyclonic or frontal rainfall is common in middle latitudes where air masses and fronts interact.

---

# 25. World Distribution Of Rainfall

## 25.1 Rainfall Varies

Different places receive different amounts of rainfall.

Rainfall also occurs in different seasons in different regions.

## 25.2 General Latitudinal Trend

In general, rainfall decreases from equator toward poles.

## 25.3 Coastal vs Interior

Coastal areas receive more rainfall than continental interiors.

Reason:

- oceans supply moisture;
- coastal winds bring humid air;
- interiors are farther from moisture source.

## 25.4 Oceans vs Land

Rainfall is generally more over oceans than over landmasses because oceans are great sources of water vapour.

## 25.5 Eastern Coasts Between 35 And 40 Degrees

Between 35 degrees and 40 degrees N and S, rain is heavier on eastern coasts and decreases toward the west.

## 25.6 Western Margins Between 45 And 65 Degrees

Between 45 degrees and 65 degrees N and S, westerlies cause rainfall first on western margins of continents.

Rainfall decreases toward the east.

## 25.7 Mountains Parallel To Coast

Where mountains run parallel to the coast:

- coastal plain and windward side receive more rain;
- rainfall decreases toward leeward side.

## 25.8 Rain Shadow

Leeward slopes receive less rainfall because descending air becomes warmer and drier.

---

# 26. Major Precipitation Regimes

## 26.1 Heavy Rainfall: More Than 200 cm

Regions receiving heavy rainfall over 200 cm per year include:

- equatorial belt;
- windward slopes of mountains along western coasts in cool temperate zone;
- coastal areas of monsoon lands.

## 26.2 Moderate Rainfall: 100 To 200 cm

Some interior continental areas and many coastal areas receive moderate rainfall between 100 and 200 cm per year.

## 26.3 Moderate To Low Rainfall: 50 To 100 cm

Rainfall between 50 and 100 cm occurs in:

- central parts of tropical lands;
- eastern and interior parts of temperate lands.

## 26.4 Low Rainfall: Less Than 50 cm

Very low rainfall occurs in:

- rain-shadow zones;
- interiors of continents;
- high latitudes.

## 26.5 Seasonal Distribution

Seasonal distribution of rainfall is important.

Rainfall effectiveness depends not only on total amount but also on when it falls.

## 26.6 Even Rainfall Regions

Some regions receive rainfall throughout the year.

Examples:

- equatorial belt;
- western parts of cool temperate regions.

# 27. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 27.1 Confusing Absolute And Relative Humidity

Absolute humidity is actual water vapour amount.

Relative humidity is percentage compared with capacity.

## 27.2 Saying Warm Air Always Has High Relative Humidity

Warm air can hold more moisture.

If actual moisture does not increase, relative humidity may fall.

## 27.3 Confusing Dew And Frost

Dew forms as water droplets above freezing point.

Frost forms as ice crystals at or below freezing point.

## 27.4 Confusing Fog And Cloud

Fog is a cloud near ground.

Clouds are formed at some height above the surface.

## 27.5 Confusing Rainfall Types With Precipitation Forms

Forms of precipitation:

- rain,
- snow,
- sleet,
- hail.

Types of rainfall by origin:

- convectional,
- orographic,
- cyclonic.

## 27.6 Forgetting Windward And Leeward

Windward side receives more rain.

Leeward side is drier and may become rain-shadow area.

# 29. Exercise Support

## 29.1 Multiple Choice Answers

1. The most important atmospheric constituent for human beings is oxygen.
2. The process transforming liquid into vapour is evaporation.
3. Air containing moisture to full capacity is saturated air.
4. The highest cloud among the given options is cirrus.

## 29.2 Short Answer: Types Of Precipitation

Common forms of precipitation are:

- rainfall;
- snowfall;
- sleet;
- hail.

If only three are required, write rainfall, snowfall, and hail, and mention sleet as another form.

## 29.3 Short Answer: Relative Humidity

Relative humidity is the percentage of moisture present in air compared with its full capacity at a given temperature.

It changes when air temperature changes because warm air can hold more moisture and cold air can hold less.

## 29.4 Short Answer: Why Water Vapour Decreases With Altitude

Water vapour decreases rapidly with altitude because its main sources are evaporation from water bodies and transpiration from plants near the earth's surface.

Temperature also decreases with height, so air at higher levels has less capacity to hold moisture.

Condensation and precipitation further remove water vapour from rising air.

## 29.5 Short Answer: Cloud Formation And Classification

Clouds form when moist air rises, cools, reaches dew point, and water vapour condenses around dust, smoke, or salt particles.

Clouds are classified by height, expanse, density, and transparency.

Main types are cirrus, cumulus, stratus, and nimbus.

Combined groups include high clouds, middle clouds, low clouds, and clouds with vertical development.

# 30. Long Answer Guide

## 30.1 World Distribution Of Precipitation

Use this structure:

1. Start with uneven distribution.

Rainfall differs by place and season.

2. Latitude.

In general, rainfall decreases from equator toward poles.

3. Oceans and coasts.

Oceans and coastal areas receive more rainfall because oceans supply moisture.

Interior continental areas receive less.

4. Wind belts.

Between 35 and 40 degrees N and S, eastern coasts receive heavier rain.

Between 45 and 65 degrees N and S, westerlies bring rain first to western margins.

5. Relief.

Where mountains run parallel to coast, windward slopes receive heavy rain and leeward slopes form rain-shadow areas.

6. Rainfall regimes.

Equatorial belt, windward western coasts in cool temperate zones, and monsoon coasts receive over 200 cm.

Interior and coastal areas may receive 100 to 200 cm.

Central tropical lands and interior/eastern temperate regions often receive 50 to 100 cm.

Rain-shadow zones, continental interiors, and high latitudes receive less than 50 cm.

7. Seasonal distribution.

Effectiveness of rainfall depends on seasonal distribution as well as total amount.

## 30.2 Forms Of Condensation And Dew/Frost Formation

Use this structure:

1. Define condensation.

Condensation is the change of water vapour into liquid water due to loss of heat.

2. List forms.

Forms include dew, frost, fog, and clouds.

3. Dew.

Dew forms when moist air touches cool surfaces such as grass, leaves, or stones and cools to dew point above freezing.

Clear sky, calm air, high relative humidity, and long cold nights favour dew.

4. Frost.

Frost forms when dew point is at or below freezing.

Water vapour deposits as ice crystals instead of droplets.

# 33. Quick Revision Sheet

## 33.1 Must-Remember Definitions

- Humidity: water vapour in air.
- Absolute humidity: actual water vapour per unit volume.
- Relative humidity: moisture percentage compared with full capacity.
- Saturated air: air holding moisture to full capacity.
- Dew point: temperature at which saturation occurs.
- Evaporation: liquid to gas.
- Condensation: gas to liquid.
- Sublimation: gas to solid.
- Precipitation: release of moisture from atmosphere.

## 33.2 Must-Remember Condensation Forms

- Dew: water droplets on cool surfaces.
- Frost: ice crystals on freezing surfaces.
- Fog: cloud near ground.
- Cloud: droplets or ice crystals in free air at height.

## 33.3 Must-Remember Cloud Types

- Cirrus: high, white, feathery.
- Cumulus: cotton-like, flat base.
- Stratus: layered, broad sky cover.
- Nimbus: dark, dense, rain-bearing.

## 33.4 Must-Remember Rainfall Types

- Convectional: heated air rises.
- Orographic: moist air rises over mountain.
- Cyclonic: air rises along fronts or cyclones.

## 33.5 Must-Remember Rainfall Distribution

- Decreases from equator to poles.
- Coasts receive more than interiors.
- Oceans receive more than land.
- Windward slopes receive more than leeward slopes.
- Rain-shadow areas are dry.
- Heavy rainfall over 200 cm in equatorial, monsoon coastal, and some windward western-coast temperate regions.

# 34. Final Chapter Summary

Water in the atmosphere occurs as vapour, liquid droplets, and ice crystals. Water vapour varies from 0 to 4 per cent by volume but has major importance for weather. Moisture enters the atmosphere through evaporation from water bodies and transpiration from plants, and returns through condensation and precipitation.

Humidity is water vapour in the air. Absolute humidity is the actual amount of water vapour per unit volume of air. Relative humidity is the percentage of moisture compared with full capacity at a given temperature. Saturated air holds moisture to full capacity, and dew point is the temperature at which saturation occurs.

Evaporation changes liquid water into vapour and is favoured by heat, dry air, and wind. Condensation changes vapour into liquid or solid form when moist air cools. Condensation forms dew, frost, fog, and clouds. Dew forms as water droplets above freezing point; frost forms as ice crystals at or below freezing point.

Clouds are formed by condensation in free air and are classified mainly as cirrus, cumulus, stratus, and nimbus. Precipitation occurs when condensed particles grow large enough to fall. It may occur as rainfall, snowfall, sleet, or hail.

Rainfall by origin is convectional, orographic, or cyclonic. World rainfall is uneven: it generally decreases from equator to poles, is greater over oceans and coasts than interiors, and is strongly influenced by wind belts and mountains. Windward slopes receive more rainfall, while leeward slopes may form rain-shadow areas.
