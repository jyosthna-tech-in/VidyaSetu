## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how landforms are made, changed, destroyed, and rebuilt by geomorphic agents.

The previous chapter introduced weathering, mass movement, erosion, deposition, and soil formation.

This chapter takes the next step:

> It shows what those processes produce on the earth's surface.

The central idea is simple:

> Every landform has a process, an agent, a material, and a history.

If you can connect these four things, the chapter becomes easy.

# 1. Landforms And Landscapes

## 1.1 What Is A Landform?

A landform is a small to medium part of the earth's surface.

It has:

- a shape,
- a size,
- a material composition,
- an origin,
- a history of change.

Examples:

- valley,
- floodplain,
- delta,
- cave,
- cirque,
- beach,
- sand dune.

## 1.2 What Is A Landscape?

A landscape is a larger area made of several related landforms.

For example:

- a river landscape may include valleys, floodplains, terraces, meanders, oxbow lakes, levees, and deltas;
- a glacial landscape may include cirques, horns, aretes, U-shaped valleys, moraines, eskers, and drumlins;
- a coastal landscape may include cliffs, beaches, bars, spits, lagoons, and dunes.

## 1.3 Landform vs Landscape

| Basis | Landform | Landscape |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Size | Small to medium | Large tract of surface |
| Composition | One distinct form | Group of related forms |
| Example | Valley | River valley landscape |
| Study focus | Shape and origin of one feature | Pattern and history of many features |

## 1.4 Core Learning Line

Do not study landforms as isolated names.

Study them as process results.

Ask:

1. Which agent made it?
2. Was the process erosional or depositional?
3. What material was acted upon?
4. What slope, climate, or rock condition helped it form?
5. How will it change later?

# 2. Meaning Of Landform Evolution

## 2.1 What Evolution Means Here

Landform evolution means change through time.

It may mean:

- one landform changing into another,
- one surface passing through stages of development,
- one landform changing shape, size, and character after it is formed.

## 2.2 Every Landform Has A Beginning

Every landform begins under a certain set of conditions.

For example:

- a rill begins when sheet flow concentrates into a small channel;
- a gully begins when a rill deepens and widens;
- a valley begins when gullies deepen, lengthen, and connect;
- a delta begins when river load is deposited at a river mouth;
- a cave begins when groundwater dissolves limestone along joints or bedding planes.

## 2.3 Every Landform Has A History

A landform is not fixed forever.

It may:

- grow,
- widen,
- deepen,
- shift,
- collapse,
- be buried,
- be eroded,
- be converted into another landform.

## 2.4 Two Important Aspects Of Landform Evolution

The two important aspects are:

1. transformation of a land surface from one form to another;
2. transformation of individual landforms after they are formed.

## 2.5 Why Landforms Change

Landforms change because of:

- continued erosion,
- continued deposition,
- weathering,
- mass movement,
- climate change,
- uplift or subsidence,
- sea-level change,
- changes in vegetation,
- changes in river discharge,
- changes in sediment supply.

## 2.6 Youth, Mature, And Old Age

The chapter uses the idea that a landmass may pass through stages:

- youth,
- maturity,
- old age.

This does not mean landforms are living things.

It means their development can be studied as a sequence.

# 3. Geomorphology

## 3.1 Meaning

Geomorphology is the study of landforms and the processes that shape them.

It reconstructs the history of the earth's surface by studying:

- forms,
- materials,
- processes,
- agents,
- stages of development.

## 3.2 Why Landforms Reveal Processes

Many geomorphic processes are slow and cannot be observed easily in a short time.

But their results can be observed.

Those results are landforms.

For example:

- a U-shaped valley tells us about glacier erosion;
- a meander tells us about lateral erosion and deposition by a river;
- a sinkhole tells us about solution in limestone;
- a beach tells us about wave deposition;
- a barchan dune tells us about wind direction and sand movement.

## 3.3 Visible And Less Visible Processes

Some processes may be seen directly:

- landslides,
- floods,
- wave attack,
- sand movement by wind,
- river bank erosion.

Some processes are usually slow or hidden:

- chemical solution underground,
- long-term valley deepening,
- glacier abrasion under ice,
- slow retreat of slopes,
- development of river terraces through time.

# 4. Erosional And Depositional Landforms

## 4.1 Erosional Landforms

Erosional landforms are produced by removal of material.

They are also called destructional landforms.

Examples:

- gorge,
- canyon,
- pothole,
- incised meander,
- sinkhole,
- cave,
- cirque,
- sea cliff,
- pedestal rock.

## 4.2 Depositional Landforms

Depositional landforms are produced by accumulation of material.

They are also called constructional landforms.

Examples:

- alluvial fan,
- delta,
- floodplain,
- natural levee,
- point bar,
- stalagmite,
- moraine,
- esker,
- beach,
- spit,
- sand dune.

## 4.3 Same Agent, Two Kinds Of Work

The same geomorphic agent can both erode and deposit.

For example:

- rivers cut valleys but also build floodplains;
- groundwater dissolves caves but also builds stalactites;
- glaciers carve troughs but also deposit moraines;
- waves cut cliffs but also build beaches;
- wind makes deflation hollows but also builds dunes.

## 4.4 The Energy Rule

When an agent has high energy, erosion and transport dominate.

When energy falls, deposition dominates.

For rivers:

- steep gradient = fast flow = more erosion;
- gentle gradient = slow flow = more deposition.

For wind:

- strong wind = transport and abrasion;
- weak wind = deposition of sand.

For waves:

- strong breaking waves = cliff erosion;
- reduced energy zones = beach and bar deposition.

# 5. Controls Of Landform Evolution

## 5.1 Main Controls

Landform evolution is influenced by:

1. rock type and structure;
2. stability of sea level;
3. tectonic stability of landmasses;
4. climate.

## 5.2 Rock Type And Structure

Landforms depend on rock properties such as:

- hardness,
- softness,
- permeability,
- impermeability,
- joints,
- fractures,
- bedding planes,
- folds,
- faults.

Examples:

- limestone forms karst because it is soluble in carbonated water;
- hard rock may form gorges;
- horizontal sedimentary rocks may form canyons with step-like sides;
- jointed rocks allow groundwater to enter and dissolve material.

## 5.3 Sea-Level Stability

Sea level controls coastal and river-base conditions.

If sea level changes:

- rivers may erode deeper,
- rivers may deposit more,
- coastal cliffs may retreat,
- coastal plains may be drowned or exposed,
- terraces may form near coasts.

## 5.4 Tectonic Stability

Tectonic movement affects landforms by:

- uplifting land,
- lowering land,
- creating slopes,
- disturbing drainage,
- causing river incision,
- changing coastal emergence or submergence.

## 5.5 Climate

Climate controls:

- rainfall,
- runoff,
- vegetation,
- temperature,
- freezing and thawing,
- wind action,
- glacier presence,
- chemical weathering.

Examples:

- humid climates favour running water;
- limestone karst develops strongly where water is available;
- high mountains and polar areas favour glaciers;
- deserts favour wind action and sheet floods;
- coasts are shaped by waves and currents.

# 6. Running Water: Basic Idea

## 6.1 Importance In Humid Regions

In humid regions with heavy rainfall, running water is the most important geomorphic agent for degrading the land surface.

It removes material from high areas and transports it to lower areas.

It cuts valleys and builds depositional plains.

## 6.2 Two Components Of Running Water

Running water acts in two main forms:

1. overland flow;
2. stream or river flow.

## 6.3 Overland Flow

Overland flow moves across the general land surface like a sheet.

It causes sheet erosion.

It may later concentrate into:

- rills,
- gullies,
- valleys.

## 6.4 Stream And River Flow

Stream or river flow is linear flow through channels.

It is more concentrated and can produce stronger erosion.

It forms:

- V-shaped valleys,
- gorges,
- canyons,
- potholes,
- plunge pools,
- meanders,
- terraces,
- floodplains,
- deltas.

## 6.5 Gradient And River Work

River work changes with slope.

| River Condition | Main Result |
| --- | --- |
| Steep gradient | Downward cutting and valley deepening |
| Moderate gradient | Lateral erosion and floodplain growth |
| Gentle gradient | Deposition, meanders, levees, deltas |

## 6.6 Downcutting And Lateral Erosion

Downcutting means vertical erosion into the river bed.

It deepens valleys.

Lateral erosion means sideways erosion of river banks.

It widens valleys and floodplains.

## 6.7 From Relief To Plain

Running water gradually:

1. removes surface material;
2. cuts rills;
3. cuts gullies;
4. develops valleys;
5. lowers divides;
6. widens valleys;
7. reduces relief;
8. may form a peneplain.

## 6.8 Peneplain

A peneplain is an almost plain surface formed by long-continued river erosion.

It may have low resistant remnants called monadnocks.

## 6.9 Monadnock

A monadnock is an isolated residual hill standing above a low erosional plain.

It remains because it is more resistant than surrounding material.

# 7. Stages Of River Landscape Development

## 7.1 Why Stages Matter

River landscapes do not look the same throughout their development.

A youthful river landscape has different features from a mature or old river landscape.

## 7.2 Youth Stage

Main features:

- few streams,
- poor drainage integration,
- original slopes still visible,
- shallow V-shaped valleys,
- no floodplains or very narrow floodplains,
- broad and flat divides,
- marshes, swamps, and lakes on divides,
- waterfalls and rapids where hard rocks are exposed,
- meanders may occur on broad upland surfaces.

## 7.3 Youth Stage: Process Meaning

In youth, downward cutting dominates.

The river is trying to cut into the land surface.

Valley deepening is more important than valley widening.

## 7.4 Mature Stage

Main features:

- many streams,
- well-integrated drainage network,
- deep V-shaped valleys,
- broad trunk valleys,
- wider floodplains along main streams,
- meanders confined within valleys,
- sharp stream divides,
- waterfalls and rapids mostly disappear,
- swamps and marshes of youth disappear.

## 7.5 Mature Stage: Process Meaning

In maturity, both downcutting and lateral erosion operate.

The river system is better organised.

Valleys are deeper and wider than in youth.

## 7.6 Old Stage

Main features:

- fewer smaller tributaries,
- gentle gradients,
- large floodplains,
- free meanders,
- natural levees,
- oxbow lakes,
- broad and flat divides,
- lakes, swamps, and marshes,
- landscape at or slightly above sea level.

## 7.7 Old Stage: Process Meaning

In old age, lateral erosion and deposition dominate.

Relief is low.

Rivers flow slowly and meander widely.

## 7.8 Stage Comparison

| Feature | Youth | Mature | Old |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Main process | Downcutting | Downcutting plus lateral erosion | Lateral erosion and deposition |
| Stream number | Few | Many | Smaller tributaries fewer |
| Valley form | Shallow V-shaped | Deep V-shaped | Broad valleys and floodplains |
| Floodplain | Absent or narrow | Wider along trunk streams | Vast |
| Divides | Broad and flat | Sharp | Broad and flat |
| Waterfalls | Present | Disappear | Absent |
| Meanders | May exist on uplands | Confined in valleys | Free over floodplains |
| Relief | High and irregular | Moderately dissected | Low |

# 9. Valleys

## 9.1 Formation Sequence

Valleys form through a sequence:

1. sheet flow removes surface material;
2. flow concentrates into small rills;
3. rills deepen and widen into gullies;
4. gullies deepen, widen, lengthen, and join;
5. a valley network develops.

## 9.2 Valley Shapes

Valleys may be:

- V-shaped valleys,
- gorges,
- canyons.

## 9.3 V-Shaped Valley

A V-shaped valley is produced by river downcutting.

It usually forms in youthful or actively eroding stream landscapes.

## 9.4 Gorge

A gorge is a deep valley with very steep to almost straight sides.

It is narrow at both top and bottom.

Gorges commonly form in hard rocks.

## 9.5 Canyon

A canyon is a deep valley with steep step-like side slopes.

It is wider at the top than at the bottom.

Canyons commonly form in horizontally bedded sedimentary rocks.

## 9.6 Gorge vs Canyon

| Basis | Gorge | Canyon |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sides | Very steep or straight | Steep and step-like |
| Width | Nearly equal at top and bottom | Wider at top than bottom |
| Common rock setting | Hard rocks | Horizontal sedimentary rocks |
| Relation | Basic deep narrow valley | Variant of gorge |

## 9.7 Examples From Chapter

The chapter refers to:

- Kaveri river near Hogenakkal as a gorge example;
- Colorado river entrenched meander as a canyon-like example.

# 10. Potholes And Plunge Pools

## 10.1 Potholes

Potholes are circular depressions on rocky stream beds.

They form in hill streams where water carries pebbles and boulders.

## 10.2 Formation Of Potholes

Process:

1. a small depression appears on a rocky bed;
2. pebbles and boulders collect in it;
3. flowing water rotates these fragments;
4. fragments abrade the depression;
5. the depression becomes deeper and wider;
6. nearby potholes may join and deepen the valley floor.

## 10.3 Plunge Pools

Plunge pools are deep and wide holes at the base of waterfalls.

They form because of:

- forceful falling water,
- impact at the waterfall base,
- rotation of boulders,
- abrasion by rock fragments.

## 10.4 Importance

Potholes and plunge pools help deepen stream valleys.

They show that rivers erode not only by water force but also by abrasion from carried rock fragments.

## 10.5 Waterfalls Are Temporary

Waterfalls recede gradually.

Over time, the valley floor above the waterfall may be brought down to the level below.

# 11. Incised Or Entrenched Meanders

## 11.1 Ordinary Meanders

Meanders are loop-like bends in a river channel.

They usually develop where:

- river gradient is gentle,
- flow is slow,
- lateral erosion is active,
- floodplain or delta plain is wide.

## 11.2 Incised Meanders

Incised or entrenched meanders are deep meander loops cut into hard rock.

They look like ordinary meanders in plan view but are deeply carved into bedrock.

## 11.3 Formation

Formation sequence:

1. river develops meanders on an older gentle surface;
2. land is slowly uplifted or river erosion increases;
3. river begins cutting downward while keeping its meander pattern;
4. meander loops become deep gorges or canyons.

## 11.4 What They Indicate

Incised meanders indicate:

- an older gentle land surface,
- later downcutting,
- possible slow uplift,
- river persistence through changing relief.

## 11.5 Incised Meanders vs Floodplain Meanders

| Basis | Incised Meander | Floodplain Meander |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Surface | Hard rock or bedrock | Alluvial floodplain |
| Depth | Deeply cut | Shallow relative to floodplain |
| Dominant process | Vertical erosion plus lateral pattern | Lateral erosion and deposition |
| Valley form | Gorge or canyon-like | Broad floodplain |
| Meaning | Older meander preserved during downcutting | Current low-gradient river adjustment |

# 12. River Terraces

## 12.1 Meaning

River terraces are old valley floor or floodplain levels left standing above the present river level.

They mark former river bed levels.

## 12.2 Types Of River Terraces

River terraces may be:

- bedrock terraces,
- alluvial terraces.

## 12.3 Bedrock Terrace

A bedrock terrace is an old valley floor surface cut into rock.

It may have little or no alluvial cover.

## 12.4 Alluvial Terrace

An alluvial terrace consists of older stream deposits.

It forms when a river cuts down into its own earlier floodplain.

## 12.5 Paired Terraces

Paired terraces occur at the same elevation on both sides of a river.

They usually indicate a more uniform episode of river downcutting.

## 12.6 Unpaired Terraces

Unpaired terraces occur:

- only on one side of a stream,
- or at different elevations on opposite sides.

They may form due to:

- slow uplift,
- uneven water-column changes,
- lateral shifting of channel.

## 12.7 Causes Of River Terraces

River terraces may result from:

1. receding water after peak flow;
2. change in hydrological regime due to climate change;
3. tectonic uplift of land;
4. sea-level changes near coasts;
5. renewed vertical erosion by a river.

## 12.8 Why Terraces Matter

Terraces are like records of earlier river levels.

They help reconstruct:

- old valley floors,
- former floodplain levels,
- river incision history,
- uplift or climate change.

# 13. Running Water Depositional Landforms

## 13.1 List

Important depositional landforms made by running water include:

- alluvial fans,
- deltas,
- floodplains,
- natural levees,
- point bars,
- oxbow lakes,
- braided channels.

## 13.2 Why Rivers Deposit

Rivers deposit when they lose carrying power.

This happens when:

- gradient becomes gentle,
- velocity decreases,
- discharge decreases,
- river enters standing water,
- load is too heavy,
- channel spreads out.

## 13.3 Deposition Order

Coarser materials are deposited first.

Finer materials are carried farther.

This rule explains:

- coarse alluvial fan deposits near mountain fronts;
- sorted delta deposits at river mouths;
- fine silt and clay over floodplains.

---

# 14. Alluvial Fans

## 14.1 Meaning

An alluvial fan is a broad cone-shaped deposit formed where a stream flows from a high slope onto a low-gradient plain.

## 14.2 Location

Alluvial fans form at:

- mountain fronts,
- foot slopes,
- places where stream gradient suddenly decreases.

## 14.3 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. a stream flows down a steep mountain slope;
2. it carries coarse load;
3. it reaches a gentler foot slope plain;
4. velocity drops;
5. coarse load becomes too heavy to carry;
6. sediment is dumped and spread out;
7. a fan-shaped deposit forms.

## 14.4 Distributaries On Fans

Streams on alluvial fans often shift channels.

They may split into many distributaries.

This happens because the deposited material blocks or alters the channel.

## 14.5 Humid vs Arid Fans

| Setting | Fan Shape |
| --- | --- |
| Humid areas | Low cones with gentle slopes |
| Arid and semi-arid areas | Higher cones with steeper slopes |

## 14.6 Example From Chapter

The chapter refers to an alluvial fan deposited by a hill stream on the way to Amarnath in Jammu and Kashmir.

---

# 15. Deltas

## 15.1 Meaning

A delta is a depositional landform built where a river deposits sediment into a sea, lake, or other standing body of water.

## 15.2 How Delta Is Like An Alluvial Fan

A delta is similar to an alluvial fan because both form when a stream or river drops its load after losing velocity.

## 15.3 How Delta Is Different

A delta forms at a river mouth.

An alluvial fan forms at a mountain foot or foot slope plain.

## 15.4 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. a river carries sediment to its mouth;
2. river enters the sea or a lake;
3. velocity decreases sharply;
4. sediment is deposited;
5. coarse material settles first;
6. finer silt and clay move farther into the water;
7. distributaries increase in length;
8. delta grows outward.

## 15.5 Important Conditions

Deltas develop well when:

- river brings abundant sediment;
- sea waves and currents do not remove all the sediment;
- deposited load can accumulate near the river mouth.

## 15.6 Delta Deposits

Delta deposits are:

- well sorted,
- clearly stratified,
- arranged by particle size.

## 15.7 Example From Chapter

The chapter refers to Krishna river delta in Andhra Pradesh.

## 15.8 Alluvial Fan vs Delta

| Basis | Alluvial Fan | Delta |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Location | Foot of mountain or high slope | River mouth |
| Medium entered | Gentle land surface | Sea or lake |
| Deposit sorting | Often poorly sorted | Well sorted and stratified |
| Channel pattern | Distributaries over fan | Distributaries extending into water |
| Main cause | Sudden decrease in slope | Entry into standing water |

# 16. Floodplains

## 16.1 Meaning

A floodplain is a flat depositional landform built along a river.

It forms when a river deposits sediment over its bed and banks during normal flow and floods.

## 16.2 Why Floodplains Form

Floodplains form when:

- river gradient is gentle,
- river velocity is low,
- lateral erosion is active,
- fine sediments are deposited,
- floodwater spills over banks.

## 16.3 Active Floodplain

The active floodplain is the part of the river bed made of recent river deposits.

It is closely linked with present channel processes.

## 16.4 Inactive Floodplain

The inactive floodplain lies above the bank.

It is flooded less frequently.

It contains:

- flood deposits,
- channel deposits.

## 16.5 Flood Deposits

Flood deposits are laid down when water spills over the banks.

They are usually finer materials such as:

- silt,
- clay,
- fine sand.

## 16.6 Channel Deposits

Channel deposits occur where old channels are abandoned or cut off.

They often contain coarser material.

## 16.7 Delta Plains

Floodplains in delta regions are called delta plains.

---

# 17. Natural Levees

## 17.1 Meaning

Natural levees are low, linear ridges of coarse deposits along river banks.

They are common along large rivers.

## 17.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. river floods and spills over bank;
2. water leaves the channel;
3. velocity suddenly decreases;
4. coarse and heavy materials are deposited near the bank;
5. finer material is carried farther onto the floodplain;
6. repeated floods build low ridges along the bank.

## 17.3 Shape

Natural levees are:

- linear,
- parallel to the river,
- higher near the bank,
- gently sloping away from the river.

## 17.4 Deposit Character

Levee deposits are coarser than flood deposits farther away from the river.

## 17.5 River Shifting

When rivers shift laterally, several natural levees may form on a floodplain.

---

# 18. Point Bars

## 18.1 Meaning

Point bars are also called meander bars.

They are depositional bars formed on the inner side of a meander bend.

## 18.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. river bends into a meander;
2. flow is faster near the outer bank;
3. erosion cuts the outer bank;
4. flow is slower near the inner bank;
5. sediment is deposited on the inner bend;
6. a point bar develops.

## 18.3 Shape And Sediment

Point bars are:

- linear or curved along the meander bend;
- usually uniform in profile;
- made of mixed sediment sizes;
- repeated as ridges if the river keeps migrating.

## 18.4 Natural Levees vs Point Bars

| Basis | Natural Levee | Point Bar |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Location | Along river banks | Inner side of a meander bend |
| Formation | Floodwater spills over bank | Slow flow deposits sediment inside bend |
| Shape | Long ridge parallel to river | Curved bar following meander |
| Material | Coarser than floodplain deposits | Mixed sediments |
| Process link | Overbank flooding | Meander migration |

# 19. Meanders And Oxbow Lakes

## 19.1 Meaning Of Meander

A meander is a loop-like bend in a river channel.

Important:

> A meander is a channel pattern, not a separate landform in the strict sense.

## 19.2 Where Meanders Develop

Meanders develop mainly in:

- floodplains,
- delta plains,
- broad valleys,
- low-gradient channels.

## 19.3 Why Rivers Meander

Meandering is favoured by:

1. very gentle gradient;
2. tendency of water to work laterally;
3. unconsolidated alluvial banks;
4. irregularities along banks;
5. deflection of moving water;
6. deposition on inner bends;
7. erosion on outer bends.

## 19.4 Meander Growth

Formation sequence:

1. river flows over a gentle slope;
2. slight bank irregularity appears;
3. water attacks one side more strongly;
4. bend develops;
5. erosion deepens the outer bend;
6. deposition builds the inner bend;
7. bend becomes a large loop.

## 19.5 Cut Bank And Slip-Off Bank

Outer bend:

- faster flow,
- active erosion,
- undercutting,
- steep bank,
- cut bank.

Inner bend:

- slower flow,
- deposition,
- gentle slope,
- slip-off bank,
- point bar.

## 19.6 Oxbow Lake

An oxbow lake forms when a meander loop is cut off from the main river.

Formation sequence:

1. meander loop becomes deeper and narrower at the neck;
2. erosion cuts through the neck;
3. river takes a shorter course;
4. old loop is abandoned;
5. ends are sealed by deposition;
6. a crescent-shaped oxbow lake remains.

## 19.7 Example From Chapter

The chapter refers to the Burhi Gandak river near Muzaffarpur, Bihar, showing oxbow lakes and cut-offs.

# 20. Braided Channels

## 20.1 Meaning

A braided channel is a river pattern with many thread-like channels separated by bars and islands.

The channels divide and rejoin repeatedly.

## 20.2 Conditions For Braiding

Braiding is favoured when:

- river carries coarse material;
- sediment load is high;
- discharge is low or variable;
- valley widens;
- water column becomes shallow;
- deposition forms central bars;
- bars divert flow toward banks;
- lateral erosion increases.

## 20.3 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. river carries coarse sediment;
2. coarser material is selectively deposited;
3. central bar forms;
4. flow divides around the bar;
5. lateral erosion widens the channel;
6. more bars and islands develop;
7. multiple channels form;
8. channels rejoin and subdivide repeatedly.

## 20.4 Why Deposition And Erosion Both Matter

Braided patterns need both:

- deposition to form bars and islands;
- lateral erosion to widen the channel and create space for multiple threads.

## 20.5 Examples From Chapter

The chapter refers to braided channel segments of:

- Gandak river;
- Son river.

# 22. Groundwater: Basic Idea

## 22.1 What This Section Studies

This section does not study groundwater as a water resource.

It studies groundwater as a geomorphic agent.

The focus is:

- erosion by solution,
- deposition by precipitation of dissolved minerals,
- landforms in limestone and dolomite regions.

## 22.2 Why Groundwater Work Is Special

Groundwater usually does not remove material mechanically like rivers.

Its physical removal power is small.

Its main landform-making power is chemical solution.

## 22.3 Best Rock Conditions

Groundwater landforms develop well where rocks are:

- permeable,
- thinly bedded,
- highly jointed,
- cracked,
- rich in calcium carbonate,
- soluble in carbonated water.

## 22.4 Downward And Horizontal Flow

Water first moves downward through:

- pores,
- joints,
- cracks,
- bedding planes.

After reaching some depth, it may move horizontally along bedding planes and joints.

This downward and horizontal movement dissolves rock and enlarges openings.

## 22.5 Limestone And Dolomite

Limestone and dolomite are rich in calcium carbonate or related carbonate minerals.

Carbonated water dissolves them.

This is why they produce special landforms.

# 23. Karst Topography

## 23.1 Meaning

Karst topography is a limestone or dolomite landscape shaped mainly by groundwater through:

- solution,
- precipitation deposition.

## 23.2 Name Origin

The name comes from the Karst region in the Balkans near the Adriatic Sea.

## 23.3 Main Processes

Karst depends on two processes:

1. solution;
2. deposition or precipitation.

## 23.4 Solution

Solution removes limestone by dissolving calcium carbonate in carbonated water.

It forms:

- swallow holes,
- sinkholes,
- dolines,
- uvalas,
- lapies,
- caves.

## 23.5 Deposition

Deposition occurs when water carrying dissolved calcium carbonate:

- evaporates,
- loses carbon dioxide,
- deposits calcium carbonate on cave surfaces.

It forms:

- stalactites,
- stalagmites,
- pillars.

## 23.6 Surface Drainage In Karst

In limestone areas, surface runoff may be weak because water quickly enters the ground through:

- joints,
- cracks,
- sinkholes,
- swallow holes.

Thus underground drainage is common.

# 25. Swallow Holes And Sinkholes

## 25.1 Swallow Holes

Swallow holes are small to medium shallow depressions on limestone surfaces.

Surface water enters them and disappears underground.

## 25.2 Sinkholes

A sinkhole is a depression that is more or less circular at the top and funnel-shaped toward the bottom.

It may vary greatly in:

- area,
- depth,
- shape,
- origin.

## 25.3 Size Range

Sinkholes may range:

- from a few square metres to a hectare in area;
- from less than half a metre to thirty metres or more in depth.

## 25.4 Solution Sinks

Solution sinks form mainly by chemical solution.

Water dissolves limestone and gradually enlarges depressions.

## 25.5 Collapse Sinks

Collapse sinks form when:

1. a void or cave exists underground;
2. the roof becomes weak;
3. the roof collapses;
4. a large opening appears at the surface.

## 25.6 Doline

Doline is a term often used for collapse sinks or sinkholes in karst areas.

## 25.7 Soil-Covered Sinkholes

Some sinkholes are covered by soil.

They may appear as shallow water pools.

This can be dangerous because the surface may collapse or sink.

---

# 26. Uvalas

## 26.1 Meaning

Uvalas are long, narrow to wide trenches formed when sinkholes and dolines join together.

They are also called valley sinks.

## 26.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. several sinkholes or dolines form close together;
2. their margins slump or collapse;
3. cave roofs may collapse;
4. depressions join;
5. a long trench-like depression forms.

## 26.3 Why Uvalas Matter

Uvalas show advanced solution and collapse in karst terrain.

They are larger and more connected than individual sinkholes.

---

# 27. Lapies And Limestone Pavements

## 27.1 Lapies

Lapies are irregular limestone surfaces with:

- sharp pinnacles,
- grooves,
- ridges,
- points.

## 27.2 Formation Of Lapies

Lapies form when limestone is dissolved unevenly.

Solution is stronger along:

- joints,
- cracks,
- parallel or sub-parallel lines of weakness.

## 27.3 Limestone Pavement

A limestone pavement is a more even, pavement-like limestone surface.

It may develop when lapies are further modified by solution.

## 27.4 Exam Definition Of Lapies

For MCQ answers, remember:

> Lapies are irregular surfaces with sharp pinnacles, grooves, and ridges.

# 28. Caves And Tunnels

## 28.1 Where Caves Form

Caves form prominently where:

- limestone or dolomite beds are thick and massive;
- limestone alternates with shale, sandstone, or quartzite;
- rocks have joints, cracks, and bedding planes;
- groundwater can move horizontally.

## 28.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. water percolates downward;
2. water absorbs carbon dioxide and becomes weak carbonic acid;
3. water reaches limestone bedding planes or joints;
4. limestone dissolves along these lines;
5. gaps widen;
6. long narrow or wide caves form.

## 28.3 Caves At Different Elevations

There may be many caves at different elevations.

This depends on:

- limestone bed levels;
- intervening rock layers;
- former groundwater levels;
- stages of solution.

## 28.4 Cave Streams

Caves often have openings through which underground streams discharge.

Surface water may disappear into sinkholes and reappear downstream through cave openings.

## 28.5 Tunnels

Caves with openings at both ends are called tunnels.

# 29. Karst Depositional Landforms

## 29.1 Why Deposition Occurs In Caves

Water moving through limestone may carry calcium carbonate in solution.

When this water:

- evaporates,
- loses carbon dioxide,
- trickles over rough surfaces,
- drips from cave roofs,

calcium carbonate is deposited.

## 29.2 Main Cave Deposits

The main deposits are:

- stalactites,
- stalagmites,
- pillars.

## 29.3 Stalactites

Stalactites hang from cave roofs.

They look like icicles.

They are usually:

- broad at the base,
- narrow toward the free end,
- formed by dripping mineral-rich water.

## 29.4 Stalagmites

Stalagmites rise from cave floors.

They form where drops fall from the roof or from stalactites.

They may look like:

- columns,
- discs,
- rounded mounds,
- small crater-like forms.

## 29.5 Pillars Or Columns

When a stalactite growing downward and a stalagmite growing upward join, they form a pillar or column.

## 29.6 Stalactite vs Stalagmite

| Basis | Stalactite | Stalagmite |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Position | Hangs from roof | Rises from floor |
| Direction of growth | Downward | Upward |
| Formation | Mineral-rich water drips from roof | Drops fall and deposit on floor |
| Shape | Icicle-like | Column, mound, disc, or cone-like |
| Joined form | Forms pillar with stalagmite | Forms pillar with stalactite |

# 31. Glaciers: Basic Idea

## 31.1 Meaning Of Glacier

A glacier is a moving mass of ice on land.

It may move:

- as a broad sheet,
- as a piedmont spread,
- as a valley glacier.

## 31.2 Types Mentioned In The Chapter

| Glacier Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Continental glacier | Vast ice sheet over land |
| Piedmont glacier | Ice spread over plains at foot of mountains |
| Mountain or valley glacier | Linear ice flow down mountain valleys |

## 31.3 Movement

Glaciers move slowly.

Their movement may be:

- a few centimetres a day,
- a few metres a day,
- sometimes less or more.

They move mainly because of gravity.

## 31.4 Indian Examples

The chapter refers to glaciers in:

- Uttarakhand;
- Himachal Pradesh;
- Jammu and Kashmir;
- the Himalayas.

Specific examples:

- Gangotri glacier feeds the Bhagirathi through meltwater near Gaumukh;
- Alkapuri glacier feeds the Alakananda;
- Bhagirathi and Alakananda join near Devprayag to form the Ganga.

## 31.5 Why Glacier Erosion Is Powerful

Glacier erosion is powerful because:

- ice has great weight;
- ice drags rock fragments;
- rock blocks grind against valley floors and sides;
- glaciers can attack even unweathered rocks;
- movement continues over long periods.

## 31.6 Main Glacial Erosion Processes

Glaciers erode mainly by:

1. plucking;
2. abrasion.

## 31.7 Plucking

Plucking means removal of rock blocks by moving glacier ice.

The glacier loosens and pulls angular blocks from the valley floor and sides.

## 31.8 Abrasion

Abrasion means wearing down by rock fragments dragged under or beside the glacier.

These fragments act like tools.

They scrape, polish, and cut the bedrock.

## 31.9 Long-Term Effect

Over long periods, glaciers can:

- remove debris,
- lower divides,
- deepen valleys,
- widen valleys,
- reduce high mountains,
- leave low hills and outwash plains.

# 33. Cirques And Tarn Lakes

## 33.1 Cirque

A cirque is a deep, long, and wide basin found at the head of a glacial valley.

It has:

- steep concave headwall,
- steep sides,
- basin-like floor,
- glacial origin.

## 33.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. snow accumulates near a mountain headwall;
2. ice develops and begins to move;
3. ice cuts into the mountain top;
4. plucking and abrasion deepen the hollow;
5. a steep-walled basin forms.

## 33.3 Tarn Lake

After the glacier disappears, water may collect in the cirque.

Such a lake is called a tarn or cirque lake.

## 33.4 Stepped Cirques

Sometimes two or more cirques occur one below another in a stepped sequence.

This shows repeated or connected glacial erosion at different levels.

## 33.5 Exam Clue

If a question asks:

"A deep, long and wide trough or basin with very steep concave high walls at its head and sides is known as what?"

Answer:

> Cirque.

# 34. Horns And Aretes

## 34.1 Horn

A horn is a sharp, steep-sided mountain peak formed when several cirques erode headward toward one another.

## 34.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. three or more glaciers form on different sides of a mountain;
2. each glacier cuts a cirque;
3. cirque headwalls erode backward;
4. cirques meet or nearly meet;
5. a sharp peak remains between them.

## 34.3 Examples

The chapter refers to:

- Matterhorn in the Alps;
- Everest in the Himalayas.

Both are explained as horns shaped by headward erosion of radiating cirques.

## 34.4 Arete

An arete is a sharp serrated ridge between glacial valleys or cirque walls.

It has:

- sharp crest,
- narrow divide,
- saw-toothed outline,
- steep sides.

## 34.5 Horn vs Arete

| Basis | Horn | Arete |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Shape | Sharp peak | Sharp ridge |
| Formation | Erosion by three or more cirques | Erosion between two glacial valleys or cirques |
| Appearance | Pointed summit | Serrated crest |
| Example | Matterhorn, Everest | Saw-toothed glacial ridge |

# 35. Glacial Valleys Or Troughs

## 35.1 Meaning

Glacial valleys are trough-like valleys made by glacier erosion.

They are usually U-shaped.

## 35.2 Features

Glacial valleys have:

- broad floors,
- smooth valley bottoms,
- steep sides,
- U-shaped cross-section,
- debris or moraines,
- swampy valley floors in some places,
- lakes gouged out of rock,
- lakes dammed by debris.

## 35.3 River Valley vs Glacial Valley

| Basis | River Valley | Glacial Valley |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Agent | Running water | Glacier |
| Typical shape | V-shaped | U-shaped |
| Main erosion | Downcutting by water | Abrasion and plucking by ice |
| Valley floor | Narrow in youth | Broad and smooth |
| Valley sides | Sloping | Steep |
| Deposits | Alluvium | Till and outwash |

## 35.4 Hanging Valley

A hanging valley is a smaller valley that joins a main glacial valley at a higher elevation.

It appears hanging above the main trough.

## 35.5 Why Hanging Valleys Form

Main glaciers are larger and erode deeper.

Tributary glaciers are smaller and erode less deeply.

After ice melts, the tributary valley floor remains higher than the main valley floor.

## 35.6 Truncated Spurs

Truncated spurs are cut-off projecting valley sides.

Glaciers cut through spurs instead of flowing around them.

The cut faces may appear like triangular facets.

## 35.7 Fjords

Fjords are deep glacial troughs filled with sea water.

They occur along high-latitude coasts where glacial valleys have been submerged.

# 38. Moraines

## 38.1 Meaning

Moraines are ridges or sheets of glacial till.

They are made of unsorted debris dropped by melting glaciers.

## 38.2 Terminal Moraine

A terminal moraine is a ridge of debris deposited at the end or toe of a glacier.

It marks the farthest or paused position of glacier advance.

## 38.3 Lateral Moraine

A lateral moraine forms along the side of a glacial valley.

It lies parallel to the valley.

There can be many lateral moraines on either side of a valley.

## 38.4 Horseshoe Ridge

Lateral moraines may join a terminal moraine.

Together they may form a horseshoe-shaped ridge.

## 38.5 Ground Moraine

A ground moraine is an irregular sheet of till spread over the floor of a glacial valley.

It is common where valley glaciers retreat rapidly.

It varies in:

- thickness,
- surface shape,
- continuity.

## 38.6 Medial Moraine

A medial moraine lies in the centre of a glacial valley.

It is flanked by lateral moraines.

It may be less perfectly formed than lateral moraines.

Sometimes it is hard to distinguish from ground moraine.

## 38.7 Moraine Location Table

| Moraine Type | Location |
| --- | --- |
| Terminal moraine | At glacier end or toe |
| Lateral moraine | Along valley sides |
| Ground moraine | Spread over valley floor |
| Medial moraine | In centre of glacial valley |

# 39. Eskers

## 39.1 Meaning

An esker is a sinuous ridge formed by deposits of meltwater streams flowing beneath glacier ice.

## 39.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. glacier melts in summer;
2. meltwater flows on, beside, or through the ice;
3. water accumulates beneath the glacier;
4. it flows like a stream in a channel under the ice;
5. ice forms the banks of this stream;
6. coarse material settles in the ice-walled channel;
7. glacier melts away;
8. deposited material remains as a winding ridge.

## 39.3 Material

Eskers may contain:

- boulders,
- blocks,
- gravel,
- sand,
- smaller rock debris.

## 39.4 Shape

Eskers are usually:

- long,
- narrow,
- winding,
- ridge-like.

# 40. Outwash Plains

## 40.1 Meaning

Outwash plains are broad plains formed by glacio-fluvial deposits.

They occur:

- at the foot of glacial mountains;
- beyond the limits of continental ice sheets.

## 40.2 Formation

Meltwater streams carry debris from glaciers.

They deposit it beyond the ice margin.

Broad flat deposits may join to form outwash plains.

## 40.3 Material

Outwash plains may contain:

- gravel,
- sand,
- silt,
- clay.

## 40.4 River Alluvial Plain vs Glacial Outwash Plain

| Basis | River Alluvial Plain | Glacial Outwash Plain |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Agent | River | Glacial meltwater |
| Source | River basin sediment | Glacier-carried debris |
| Setting | River valleys and plains | Ice margins and glacial mountain fronts |
| Sorting | Water-sorted | Water-sorted but glacial source |
| Related landforms | Levees, meanders, deltas | Moraines, eskers, drumlins |

# 41. Drumlins

## 41.1 Meaning

Drumlins are smooth oval ridge-like features made mainly of glacial till.

They may also contain gravel and sand.

## 41.2 Shape

Drumlins are:

- smooth,
- oval,
- ridge-like,
- aligned with glacier movement.

## 41.3 Size

They may measure up to:

- about 1 kilometre in length;
- about 30 metres in height.

## 41.4 Stoss End And Tail

The stoss end is the end facing the glacier.

It is:

- blunter,
- steeper.

The tail is the opposite end.

It is:

- gentler,
- tapered.

## 41.5 Formation

Drumlins form when rock debris is dumped beneath heavily loaded ice through fissures in the glacier.

The moving ice shapes the deposit.

The stoss end becomes blunt because of pushing by the glacier.

## 41.6 Importance

Drumlins indicate the direction of glacier movement.

Their long axes are parallel to ice movement.

# 43. Waves And Currents: Basic Idea

## 43.1 Why Coastal Processes Matter

Coastal processes are very dynamic.

They can be destructive.

Some coastal changes happen quickly.

At the same place:

- one season may show erosion;
- another season may show deposition.

## 43.2 Main Agent

Most coastal changes are done by waves.

Currents also move sediment along the coast.

## 43.3 How Waves Work

When waves break:

- water is thrown forcefully onto the shore;
- sediments on the sea bottom are churned;
- rock debris strikes the coast;
- cliffs and beaches are modified.

## 43.4 Strong Events

Storm waves and tsunami waves can create major changes in a short time.

They are far more powerful than normal breaking waves.

## 43.5 Controls Of Coastal Landforms

Coastal landforms depend on:

1. configuration of land;
2. configuration of sea floor;
3. whether coast is emerging seaward;
4. whether coast is submerging landward;
5. supply of sediment;
6. wave energy;
7. longshore currents.

---

# 44. High Rocky Coasts

## 44.1 Meaning

High rocky coasts are steep, irregular coasts where erosion dominates.

They are often treated as submerged or retreating coasts.

## 44.2 Main Features

High rocky coasts show:

- irregular coastline;
- indented shoreline;
- drowned river valleys;
- fjords where glacial valleys are submerged;
- hillsides dropping sharply into water;
- few depositional features at first;
- strong erosional features.

## 44.3 Process

Waves break strongly against the land.

They shape the hillsides into cliffs.

Repeated pounding makes cliffs retreat.

## 44.4 Evolution Sequence

Formation sequence:

1. high rocky coast is attacked by waves;
2. cliffs form;
3. cliff base is cut;
4. cliff retreats;
5. wave-cut platform forms;
6. fallen material is broken and rounded;
7. material is deposited offshore;
8. wave-built terrace may develop;
9. beaches, bars, barriers, spits, and lagoons may develop later.

## 44.5 Indian Example

The west coast of India is described as a high rocky retreating coast.

Erosional forms dominate there.

---

# 45. Low Sedimentary Coasts

## 45.1 Meaning

Low sedimentary coasts are smooth, gently sloping coasts where deposition dominates.

They are often treated as emerged or advancing coasts.

## 45.2 Main Features

Low sedimentary coasts show:

- smooth coastline;
- gentle slope into the sea;
- coastal plains;
- deltas;
- lagoons;
- tidal creeks;
- marshes;
- swamps;
- bars;
- spits.

## 45.3 Process

Waves churn bottom sediments.

Because the coast is gently sloping, sediments move readily and build depositional features.

## 45.4 Lagoon To Coastal Plain

Lagoons may gradually become:

1. shallow;
2. sediment-filled;
3. swampy;
4. part of a coastal plain.

## 45.5 Indian Example

The east coast of India is described as a low sedimentary coast.

Depositional forms dominate there.

## 45.6 High Rocky Coast vs Low Sedimentary Coast

| Basis | High Rocky Coast | Low Sedimentary Coast |
| --- | --- | --- |
| General nature | Steep and rocky | Low and gently sloping |
| Coastline | Irregular and indented | Smooth |
| Dominant process | Erosion | Deposition |
| Rivers | May appear drowned | Extend by building deltas |
| Common forms | Cliffs, caves, stacks, wave-cut terraces | Beaches, bars, spits, lagoons, deltas |
| Indian example | West coast | East coast |

- sea cliffs,
- wave-cut terraces,

# 47. Sea Cliffs And Wave-Cut Terraces

## 47.1 Sea Cliff

A sea cliff is a steep coastal rock face made by wave erosion.

It may range from a few metres to more than 30 metres in height.

## 47.2 Formation Of Sea Cliff

Formation sequence:

1. waves strike the base of a rocky coast;
2. rock debris is smashed against the cliff;
3. hollows and notches form;
4. base is weakened;
5. upper part collapses;
6. cliff retreats inland.

## 47.3 Wave-Cut Terrace

A wave-cut terrace is a flat or gently sloping platform at the foot of a sea cliff.

It forms as waves cut the cliff base and the cliff retreats.

## 47.4 Rock Debris

Rock debris from the cliff may:

- accumulate on the platform;
- be broken into smaller fragments;
- become rounded by wave action;
- be deposited offshore.

## 47.5 Wave-Built Terrace

After long cliff retreat, offshore deposits may accumulate and form a wave-built terrace in front of the wave-cut terrace.

---

# 48. Sea Caves And Sea Stacks

## 48.1 Sea Cave

A sea cave is a hollow formed when waves cut into a cliff.

## 48.2 Formation Of Sea Cave

Formation sequence:

1. waves lash against cliff base;
2. rock debris strikes the cliff with waves;
3. small hollows form;
4. hollows widen and deepen;
5. sea cave forms.

## 48.3 Cave Collapse

Sea cave roofs may collapse.

When this happens, the sea cliff retreats further inland.

## 48.4 Sea Stack

A sea stack is an isolated resistant rock mass standing just offshore.

It was originally part of a cliff or hill.

## 48.5 Formation Of Sea Stack

Formation sequence:

1. waves erode a headland or cliff;
2. caves and arches may form;
3. weaker parts collapse;
4. resistant part remains offshore;
5. isolated rock mass becomes a stack.

## 48.6 Temporary Nature

Sea stacks are temporary.

Wave erosion eventually destroys them.

## 49.1 List

Important coastal depositional landforms include:

- beaches,
- shingle beaches,
- coastal dunes,
- offshore bars,
- barrier bars,
- spits,
- lagoons.

## 49.2 Why Coastal Deposition Occurs

Coastal deposition occurs when waves and currents have enough sediment but reduced energy.

Sediment may come from:

- rivers,
- streams,
- wave erosion of cliffs,
- offshore deposits.

# 50. Beaches And Coastal Dunes

## 50.1 Beach

A beach is a depositional shoreline landform made mostly of loose sediment.

Most beaches are made of sand-sized material.

## 50.2 Temporary Nature Of Beaches

Beaches are temporary features.

A beach may be wide in one season and narrow in another.

Wave energy, storms, and sediment supply can change it quickly.

## 50.3 Shingle Beach

A shingle beach is made of small pebbles and cobbles.

It is coarser than a sandy beach.

## 50.4 Coastal Dunes

Coastal dunes form just behind beaches.

Sand is:

1. lifted from the beach;
2. winnowed by wind;
3. deposited behind the beach;
4. arranged into ridges parallel to the coastline.

## 50.5 Where Coastal Dunes Are Common

Coastal dunes are common along low sedimentary coasts.

# 51. Bars, Barriers, Spits, And Lagoons

## 51.1 Offshore Bar

An offshore bar is a ridge of sand or shingle in the offshore zone.

It lies roughly parallel to the coast.

## 51.2 Barrier Bar

A barrier bar is an offshore bar exposed above water.

It forms when more sand is added to the bar.

## 51.3 Spit

A spit is a barrier bar attached to a headland or one end of a bay.

Spits may also develop attached to hills or headlands.

## 51.4 Lagoon

A lagoon is a shallow water body partly or almost completely separated from the sea by bars, barriers, or spits.

## 51.5 Formation Sequence

Formation sequence:

1. offshore bar forms parallel to coast;
2. more sediment exposes it as a barrier bar;
3. barrier may attach to a headland as a spit;
4. bars and spits extend across a bay mouth;
5. bay opening becomes narrow;
6. lagoon forms;
7. lagoon gradually fills with sediment;
8. swamp or coastal plain may replace it.

## 51.6 Coastal Defence Value

Offshore bars, barrier bars, beaches, beach dunes, and mangroves reduce wave impact.

They act as natural buffers against:

- storms,
- tsunami waves,
- coastal erosion.

## 51.7 Sediment Budget Warning

If sediment supply or mangroves are disturbed, coastal protective landforms may erode.

Then settlements become more exposed to storm and tsunami waves.

# 53. Wind: Basic Idea

## 53.1 Wind In Hot Deserts

Wind is one of the dominant geomorphic agents in hot deserts.

Dry and barren desert floors heat quickly.

The heated surface warms the air above it.

This produces:

- upward movement,
- turbulence,
- eddies,
- whirlwinds,
- updrafts,
- downdrafts.

## 53.2 Wind Movement Over Desert Floors

Wind may move with great speed along desert floors.

Obstacles create turbulence.

Storm winds can be highly destructive.

## 53.3 Main Wind Processes

Wind acts through:

1. deflation;
2. abrasion;
3. impact.

## 53.4 Deflation

Deflation is lifting and removal of dust and fine particles from the surface.

## 53.5 Abrasion

Abrasion occurs when sand and silt carried by wind scrape rock surfaces.

Wind-borne sand acts like a natural sand-blasting tool.

## 53.6 Impact

Impact is the force of wind-blown sand striking a rock surface.

It depends on the momentum of moving particles.

## 53.7 Wind Is Not The Only Desert Agent

Desert erosion is not done by wind alone.

Other important processes are:

- mass wasting,
- running water,
- sheet floods,
- sheet wash,
- mechanical weathering.

## 53.8 Role Of Rain In Deserts

Rain is scarce in deserts, but when it occurs it may be torrential.

Short, intense rain removes weathered debris quickly.

Thus sheet floods and sheet wash help shape desert landscapes.

## 53.9 Desert Stream Channels

Desert stream channels are often:

- broad,
- smooth,
- indefinite,
- active only briefly after rain.

# 55. Pediments And Pediplains

## 55.1 Pediment

A pediment is a gently inclined rocky floor at the foot of mountains in desert regions.

It may have:

- bare rock surface,
- thin debris cover,
- gentle slope away from mountain front.

## 55.2 Formation Of Pediment

Pediments form through erosion of mountain fronts by:

- lateral erosion by streams,
- sheet flooding,
- backwasting.

## 55.3 Backwasting

Backwasting means parallel retreat of slopes.

The steep slope or free face retreats backward while maintaining its general angle.

## 55.4 Formation Sequence

Formation sequence:

1. erosion starts at steep mountain margins;
2. streams and sheet floods cut the mountain front;
3. a rocky foot-slope surface forms;
4. steep face retreats backward;
5. pediment extends at the expense of the mountain.

## 55.5 Pediplain

A pediplain is a low, almost featureless plain formed by the expansion and joining of pediments.

## 55.6 Inselberg

An inselberg is an isolated remnant hill left after surrounding high relief is reduced.

It stands above the pediplain.

## 55.7 Pediment To Pediplain Chain

Pediment formation chain:

```text
Mountain front -> pediment -> backward retreat -> larger pediment -> inselberg remnants -> pediplain
```

# 56. Playas And Alkali Flats

## 56.1 Playa

A playa is a shallow temporary lake or nearly level basin floor in a desert.

It forms where drainage moves toward the centre of a basin.

## 56.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. basin is surrounded by mountains or hills;
2. drainage flows toward basin centre;
3. sediments are deposited from basin margins;
4. nearly level plain forms;
5. during wet periods, shallow water covers the plain;
6. water evaporates quickly;
7. salts may accumulate.

## 56.3 Alkali Flat

An alkali flat is a playa plain covered with salts.

## 56.4 Why Salts Accumulate

Salts accumulate because:

- water remains only briefly;
- evaporation is strong;
- dissolved minerals are left behind.

---

# 57. Deflation Hollows And Wind Caves

## 57.1 Deflation Hollows

Deflation hollows are shallow depressions formed by wind removal of loose material.

## 57.2 Formation Process

Formation sequence:

1. wind blows persistently in one direction;
2. loose weathered mantle or bare soil is removed;
3. surface is lowered;
4. shallow depression forms.

## 57.3 Blowouts

Blowouts are shallow depressions or hollows made by wind action.

They may enlarge over time.

## 57.4 Wind Caves

Wind caves can form when:

- rock faces are abraded by wind-borne sand;
- shallow hollows deepen and widen;
- blowouts become larger cavities.

## 57.5 Important Note

Wind caves are formed by abrasion and deflation, not by groundwater solution.

This separates them from limestone caves.

# 58. Mushroom, Table, And Pedestal Rocks

## 58.1 Basic Idea

Many desert rock outcrops are shaped by wind deflation and abrasion.

Softer or more exposed parts are worn away faster.

Resistant parts remain.

## 58.2 Mushroom Rock

A mushroom rock has:

- narrow stalk,
- broad rounded cap,
- polished surface.

## 58.3 Table Rock

A table rock has a broad flat top, like a table.

## 58.4 Pedestal Rock

A pedestal rock stands like a pillar or support.

## 58.5 Why Bases Are Often Narrow

Wind-blown sand is most active close to the ground.

Therefore lower parts of rocks may be abraded more strongly than upper parts.

This creates narrow bases and broader tops.

# 59. Wind Depositional Landforms

## 59.1 Main Landform

The most important wind depositional landforms are sand dunes.

## 59.2 Wind As A Sorting Agent

Wind sorts material very effectively.

Depending on wind velocity, grains may move by:

- rolling,
- saltation,
- suspension.

## 59.3 Deposition Rule

When wind slows down, grains settle according to size and critical velocity.

Coarser grains settle earlier.

Finer particles may travel farther.

## 59.4 Conditions For Dune Formation

Sand dunes form where there is:

- abundant sand;
- dry surface;
- open land;
- wind;
- an obstacle or surface irregularity;
- suitable wind direction.

## 59.5 Dunes Can Move

Most desert dunes shift.

Some may become stabilised, especially near vegetation or human habitations.

---

# 60. Types Of Sand Dunes

## 60.1 Barchan

A barchan is a crescent-shaped sand dune.

Its wings or horns point downwind.

It forms where:

- wind direction is constant;
- wind is moderate;
- sand supply is limited;
- the surface is fairly uniform.

## 60.2 Parabolic Dune

A parabolic dune forms where sandy surfaces are partly covered with vegetation.

It is like a reversed barchan if wind direction is the same.

Vegetation anchors the arms.

## 60.3 Seif Dune

A seif dune is similar to a barchan but generally has one wing or point.

It forms when wind conditions shift.

The single wing can grow long and high.

## 60.4 Longitudinal Dune

A longitudinal dune is a long ridge parallel to the wind direction.

It forms where:

- sand supply is poor;
- wind direction is constant.

It is usually long but low.

## 60.5 Transverse Dune

A transverse dune is aligned perpendicular to the wind direction.

It forms where:

- wind direction is constant;
- sand source is elongated at right angles to wind direction;
- sand supply is sufficient.

## 60.6 Dune Comparison

| Dune Type | Shape | Wind Relation | Main Condition |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Barchan | Crescent | Horns point downwind | Constant moderate wind, limited sand |
| Parabolic | Reversed crescent | Arms anchored by vegetation | Partial vegetation |
| Seif | Long with one wing | Linked to shifted wind | Changing wind conditions |
| Longitudinal | Long ridge | Parallel to wind | Poor sand supply, constant wind |
| Transverse | Long ridge | Perpendicular to wind | Sand source at right angles to wind |

# 64. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 64.1 Calling Meander A Landform Without Qualification

A meander is mainly a channel pattern.

It is associated with landforms such as:

- point bars,
- cut banks,
- oxbow lakes,
- floodplains.

## 64.2 Confusing Gorge And Canyon

A gorge is very steep and narrow.

A canyon has step-like sides and is wider at the top.

## 64.3 Confusing Sinkhole And Stalagmite

Sinkhole is an erosional surface depression.

Stalagmite is a depositional cave feature rising from the floor.

## 64.4 Confusing Stalactite And Stalagmite

Stalactite hangs from the roof.

Stalagmite rises from the floor.

## 64.5 Thinking Glaciers Only Deposit

Glaciers erode powerfully by:

- plucking,
- abrasion.

They also deposit till and outwash.

## 64.6 Thinking Wind Alone Makes Desert Relief

Wind moves fine material, but sheet floods and sheet wash are also important in deserts.

## 64.7 Confusing Offshore Bar And Barrier Bar

Offshore bar is submerged.

Barrier bar is exposed above water.

## 64.8 Forgetting Climate

Climate controls which agent dominates:

- humid climate: running water;
- limestone with water: karst;
- high mountains/polar regions: glaciers;
- coasts: waves and currents;
- deserts: wind plus sheet wash.

1. Downward cutting is dominated in the youth stage.

4. Lapies are irregular surfaces with sharp pinnacles, grooves, and ridges.

## 65.2 Short Answer: Incised Meanders And Plain Meanders

Incised meanders in rocks indicate that a river once developed meander loops on a gentler surface and later cut downward into bedrock, often due to uplift or renewed erosion.

Meanders in alluvial plains indicate low gradient, lateral erosion, deposition, and floodplain development.

## 65.3 Short Answer: Evolution Of Uvalas

Uvalas evolve when sinkholes and dolines in limestone areas enlarge and join.

Their joining may happen through solution, slumping of margins, or collapse of cave roofs.

The result is a long, narrow to wide trench called a valley sink or uvala.

## 65.4 Short Answer: Underground Flow In Limestone

Underground flow is common in limestone areas because limestone is soluble, jointed, cracked, and permeable.

Surface water enters swallow holes and sinkholes, moves along joints and bedding planes, dissolves rock, and flows as underground streams.

## 65.5 Short Answer: Linear Depositional Forms In Glacial Valleys

Glacial valleys show several linear depositional forms:

- lateral moraines along valley sides;
- terminal moraines at the glacier toe;
- medial moraines in the centre of the glacial valley;
- eskers as sinuous ridges deposited by subglacial streams.

Ground moraines may cover valley floors as irregular sheets of till.

## 65.6 Short Answer: Wind Work In Deserts

Wind performs its work through deflation, abrasion, and impact.

It lifts fine particles, uses sand as an abrasive tool, and strikes rock surfaces with moving grains.

Wind is not the only agent in deserts. Sheet floods, sheet wash, weathering, and mass wasting also shape desert landforms.

In humid regions, heavy rainfall produces overland flow, streams, and rivers.

Running water causes sheet erosion, rill formation, gully development, valley cutting, floodplain building, and delta formation.

Youthful rivers cut vertically, mature rivers widen valleys, and old rivers form broad floodplains.

3. Explain arid regions.

In deserts, rainfall is scarce but often torrential.

Short, intense rains produce sheet floods and sheet wash.

These remove weathered debris and help form pediments, pediplains, and playas.

4. Add wind relation.

Wind moves fine material in deserts, but larger-scale removal of weathered debris is often done by sheet floods.

5. Conclude.

Thus running water is important in humid regions through regular river flow and in arid regions through occasional but powerful sheet floods.

## 66.2 Limestone In Humid And Arid Climates

Use this structure:

1. Start with limestone's property.

Limestone contains calcium carbonate and dissolves in carbonated water.

2. Explain humid climate.

In humid climates, abundant water and carbon dioxide help solution.

Surface water enters joints, cracks, and bedding planes.

Underground drainage becomes common.

Karst landforms develop strongly.

3. Explain arid climate.

In arid climates, water is scarce.

Chemical solution is weaker.

Mechanical weathering and limited runoff may dominate more than solution.

4. Dominant process.

The dominant geomorphic process in limestone areas is solution.

Deposition by precipitation of calcium carbonate is also important inside caves.

5. Results.

Solution forms swallow holes, sinkholes, dolines, uvalas, lapies, limestone pavements, caves, and tunnels.

Deposition forms stalactites, stalagmites, columns, and pillars.

## 66.3 How Glaciers Reduce High Mountains Into Low Hills And Plains

Use this structure:

1. Define glacier.

A glacier is a moving mass of ice on land.

2. Explain force.

Glaciers move due to gravity.

Because ice is heavy, its friction against bedrock is powerful.

3. Explain erosion.

Glaciers erode by plucking and abrasion.

They remove rock blocks and drag debris along valley floors and sides.

4. Explain landforms.

They form cirques, horns, aretes, U-shaped valleys, hanging valleys, truncated spurs, and fjords.

5. Explain deposition.

When glaciers melt, they deposit till and outwash.

They form moraines, eskers, outwash plains, and drumlins.

6. Conclude.

Over long periods, glaciers remove debris, lower divides, reduce slopes, and convert high mountain relief into lower hills and plains.

# 70. Final Chapter Summary

Landforms are small to medium parts of the earth's surface, while landscapes are larger areas made of related landforms. Landform evolution means the transformation of landforms and land surfaces through time. The study of landforms helps us reconstruct the history of the earth's surface because every process leaves a distinct imprint.

Running water is especially powerful in humid regions. It begins with sheet erosion, then forms rills, gullies, valleys, floodplains, and plains. In youthful stages, rivers cut downward. In mature stages, valleys deepen and widen. In old stages, rivers meander across broad floodplains and deposit sediment. Running water forms erosional landforms such as gorges, canyons, potholes, plunge pools, incised meanders, and terraces, and depositional landforms such as alluvial fans, deltas, natural levees, point bars, and braided channels.

Groundwater produces special landforms mainly in limestone and dolomite areas. Carbonated water dissolves calcium carbonate and creates karst topography. Erosional karst landforms include sinkholes, dolines, uvalas, lapies, limestone pavements, caves, and tunnels. Depositional karst landforms include stalactites, stalagmites, and pillars.

Glaciers are moving masses of ice. They erode by plucking and abrasion and can reduce high mountains over long periods. Their erosional forms include cirques, horns, aretes, U-shaped valleys, hanging valleys, truncated spurs, and fjords. Their depositional forms include moraines, eskers, outwash plains, and drumlins.

Waves and currents shape coasts rapidly. High rocky coasts are dominated by erosion and show cliffs, wave-cut terraces, caves, and stacks. Low sedimentary coasts are dominated by deposition and show beaches, dunes, bars, barrier bars, spits, lagoons, deltas, marshes, and coastal plains.

Wind shapes deserts through deflation, abrasion, and impact, but it is not the only desert agent. Sheet floods and sheet wash also remove weathered debris. Desert landforms include pediments, pediplains, inselbergs, playas, deflation hollows, mushroom rocks, table rocks, pedestal rocks, and many types of sand dunes.

To master this chapter, always connect each landform with its agent, process, material, environment, and stage of evolution.
