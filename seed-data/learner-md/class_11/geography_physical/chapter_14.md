## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how ocean water moves through waves, tides, and currents, and how these movements affect coasts, navigation, climate, fishing, and human activity.

The central idea is:

> Ocean water is dynamic. It moves horizontally through waves and currents, vertically through tides, upwelling, and sinking, and these movements are controlled by wind, gravity, the sun, the moon, temperature, salinity, density, and the rotation of the earth.

# 1. Ocean Water Is Dynamic

## 1.1 Meaning

Ocean water is always moving.

It moves because of:

- temperature differences;
- salinity differences;
- density differences;
- winds;
- gravity;
- moon's attraction;
- sun's attraction;
- earth's rotation.

## 1.2 Two Main Motions

Ocean water has:

1. horizontal motion;
2. vertical motion.

## 1.3 Horizontal Motion

Horizontal motion includes:

- waves;
- ocean currents.

## 1.4 Vertical Motion

Vertical motion includes:

- tides;
- upwelling;
- sinking of surface water.

## 1.5 Waves vs Currents

Waves move energy across the surface.

Ocean currents move water from one place to another in definite directions.

## 1.6 Simple Difference

| Feature | Waves | Currents |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What mainly moves | Energy | Water mass |
| Direction | Across surface as wave trains | Definite path and direction |
| Main cause | Wind | Wind, density, gravity, Coriolis force |
| Example | Surf at beach | Gulf Stream |

# 2. Waves

## 2.1 Meaning

Waves are energy moving across the ocean surface.

The water itself does not travel far with the wave.

Water particles move in small circles as a wave passes.

## 2.2 Source Of Wave Energy

Wind provides energy to waves.

Most waves are caused by wind blowing against water.

## 2.3 How Wind Creates Waves

Formation sequence:

1. wind blows over calm water;
2. friction transfers energy from wind to water;
3. small ripples form;
4. stronger wind makes larger waves;
5. white caps appear in breaking waves;
6. waves travel across the ocean;
7. energy is released along shorelines.

## 2.4 Breeze And Ripples

When a breeze of two knots or less blows over calm water, small ripples form.

As wind speed increases, waves grow.

## 2.5 Wave Travel

Waves may travel thousands of kilometres before reaching shore.

They break and dissolve as surf.

## 2.6 Largest Waves

The largest waves are found in open oceans.

They grow as they absorb energy from wind.

## 2.7 Young And Distant Waves

Steep waves are usually young and formed by local wind.

Slow and steady waves may originate far away, even from another hemisphere.

## 2.8 Maximum Wave Height

Maximum wave height depends on:

- strength of wind;
- how long wind blows;
- area over which wind blows in one direction.

## 2.9 Why Waves Break Near Shore

As a wave approaches a beach, it slows down due to friction between moving water and the sea floor.

When water depth becomes less than half the wavelength, the wave breaks.

---

# 3. Motion Of Water Beneath Waves

## 3.1 Circular Motion

The actual motion of water beneath waves is circular.

As a wave approaches, objects are carried:

- up and forward;
- then down and back.

## 3.2 Energy Moves, Water Mostly Circulates

This is why waves can travel far while water particles do not move the same distance.

## 3.3 Surface And Deep Water

Wave motion near the surface seldom affects stagnant deep bottom water.

Deep ocean water is usually not moved much by ordinary surface waves.

---

# 4. Characteristics Of Waves

## 4.1 Wave Crest

The highest point of a wave is called the crest.

## 4.2 Wave Trough

The lowest point of a wave is called the trough.

## 4.3 Wave Height

Wave height is the vertical distance from the bottom of a trough to the top of a crest.

## 4.4 Wave Amplitude

Wave amplitude is one-half of wave height.

## 4.5 Wave Period

Wave period is the time interval between two successive crests or troughs passing a fixed point.

## 4.6 Wavelength

Wavelength is the horizontal distance between two successive crests.

## 4.7 Wave Speed

Wave speed is the rate at which the wave moves through water.

It is measured in knots.

## 4.8 Wave Frequency

Wave frequency is the number of waves passing a fixed point during one second.

## 4.9 Wave Terms Table

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Crest | Highest point |
| Trough | Lowest point |
| Height | Crest-to-trough vertical distance |
| Amplitude | Half of height |
| Period | Time between successive crests/troughs |
| Wavelength | Distance between successive crests |
| Speed | Rate of movement through water |
| Frequency | Number of waves per second |

# 5. Tides

## 5.1 Meaning

Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea level once or twice a day.

They are mainly caused by the attraction of the moon and the sun.

## 5.2 Tides vs Surges

Tides are regular.

Surges are irregular.

Surges are caused by meteorological effects such as:

- winds;
- atmospheric pressure changes.

## 5.3 Why Tides Are Complex

Tides vary by:

- frequency;
- magnitude;
- height;
- location;
- time;
- coastline shape;
- ocean-basin form.

---

# 6. Causes Of Tides

## 6.1 Main Causes

Tides are caused mainly by:

- moon's gravitational pull;
- sun's gravitational pull;
- centrifugal force.

## 6.2 Moon's Role

The moon's gravitational pull is the major cause of tides.

The sun's pull is also important but less dominant for tides.

## 6.3 Centrifugal Force

Centrifugal force acts to counterbalance gravity.

Together, gravitational pull and centrifugal force create two major tidal bulges.

## 6.4 Tidal Bulge Facing Moon

On the side of earth facing the moon, moon's attraction is stronger than centrifugal force.

Water bulges toward the moon.

## 6.5 Opposite Tidal Bulge

On the opposite side of earth, moon's attraction is weaker because that side is farther from the moon.

Centrifugal force dominates.

Water bulges away from the moon.

## 6.6 Tide-Generating Force

The tide-generating force is the difference between:

- gravitational attraction of the moon;
- centrifugal force.

## 6.7 Horizontal Forces Matter

On the earth's surface, horizontal tide-generating forces are more important than vertical forces in creating tidal bulges.

---

# 7. Local Control Of Tide Height

## 7.1 Continental Shelves

Tidal bulges on wide continental shelves have greater height.

## 7.2 Mid-Oceanic Islands

When tidal bulges hit mid-oceanic islands, they become low.

## 7.3 Bays And Estuaries

Shape of bays and estuaries can magnify tide intensity.

Funnel-shaped bays greatly increase tidal magnitude.

## 7.4 Tidal Currents

When tides are channelled between islands or into bays and estuaries, they are called tidal currents.

## 7.5 Bay Of Fundy Example

The highest tides in the world occur in the Bay of Fundy, Nova Scotia, Canada.

Tidal bulge:

```text
15 m to 16 m
```

Because there are two high tides and two low tides daily, tide can rise very quickly.

The chapter estimates about:

```text
240 cm per hour
```

---

# 8. Types Of Tides By Frequency

## 8.1 Basis

Tides may be grouped by frequency of occurrence in a day.

## 8.2 Semi-Diurnal Tide

Semi-diurnal tide is the most common tidal pattern.

It has:

- two high tides;
- two low tides;
- each day.

Successive high or low tides are approximately of the same height.

## 8.3 Diurnal Tide

Diurnal tide has:

- one high tide;
- one low tide;
- each day.

Successive high and low tides are approximately of the same height.

## 8.4 Mixed Tide

Mixed tides have variations in height.

They generally occur:

- along west coast of North America;
- on many Pacific Ocean islands.

## 8.5 Frequency Table

| Tide Type | Daily Pattern | Height Character |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Semi-diurnal | Two high, two low | Heights nearly same |
| Diurnal | One high, one low | Heights nearly same |
| Mixed | Two or more with variation | Heights vary |

# 9. Spring And Neap Tides

## 9.1 Basis

Spring and neap tides are based on positions of:

- sun;
- moon;
- earth.

## 9.2 Spring Tide

Spring tides occur when sun, moon, and earth are in a straight line.

Tide height is higher.

## 9.3 When Spring Tides Occur

Spring tides occur twice a month:

- during full moon;
- during new moon.

## 9.4 Neap Tide

Neap tides occur when sun and moon are at right angles to each other.

Their forces tend to counteract each other.

Tidal range is lower.

## 9.5 Interval

Normally, there is about a seven-day interval between spring tides and neap tides.

## 9.6 Spring vs Neap

| Basis | Spring Tide | Neap Tide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sun-moon-earth position | Straight line | Right angle |
| Tide height/range | Higher | Lower |
| Occurrence | Full moon and new moon | About seven days after spring tide |
| Force relation | Sun and moon reinforce | Sun and moon partly counteract |

---

# 10. Perigee, Apogee, Perihelion, And Aphelion

## 10.1 Perigee

Perigee is when the moon's orbit is closest to earth.

At perigee, unusually high and low tides may occur.

Tidal range is greater than normal.

## 10.2 Apogee

Apogee is when the moon is farthest from earth.

Moon's gravitational force is weaker.

Tidal ranges are less than average.

## 10.3 Perihelion

Perihelion is when earth is closest to the sun.

It occurs around:

```text
3 January
```

Tidal ranges are much greater, with unusually high and unusually low tides.

## 10.4 Aphelion

Aphelion is when earth is farthest from the sun.

It occurs around:

```text
4 July
```

Tidal ranges are much less than average.

---

# 11. Ebb And Flow

## 11.1 Ebb

Ebb is the time between high tide and low tide when water level is falling.

## 11.2 Flow Or Flood

Flow or flood is the time between low tide and high tide when water level is rising.

## 11.3 Simple Memory Line

Ebb means water falls.

Flow means water rises.

---

# 12. Importance Of Tides

## 12.1 Predictability

Tides can be predicted well in advance because earth-moon-sun positions are known accurately.

## 12.2 Navigation

Tides help navigators and fishermen plan activities.

Tidal flows are important in navigation.

## 12.3 Harbours

Tidal heights are important for harbours, especially those near rivers and estuaries.

Some harbour entrances have shallow bars that prevent ships from entering at low water.

High tide may allow entry.

## 12.4 Estuary Cleaning

Tides help:

- desilt sediments;
- remove polluted water from river estuaries.

## 12.5 Tidal Power

Tides are used to generate electricity.

Countries using tidal power include:

- Canada;
- France;
- Russia;
- China.

## 12.6 India Example

A 3 MW tidal power project at Durgaduani in the Sundarbans of West Bengal was under way according to the chapter.

# 13. Ocean Currents

## 13.1 Meaning

Ocean currents are like river flow in oceans.

They are regular movements of a volume of water in a definite path and direction.

## 13.2 Definition

Ocean currents are continuous flows of large amounts of ocean water in definite directions.

## 13.3 Two Types Of Forces

Ocean currents are influenced by:

1. primary forces that initiate water movement;
2. secondary forces that influence how currents flow.

---

# 14. Primary Forces Affecting Currents

## 14.1 Main Primary Forces

Primary forces include:

1. heating by solar energy;
2. wind;
3. gravity;
4. Coriolis force.

## 14.2 Heating By Solar Energy

Solar heating causes water to expand.

Near the equator, ocean water is about 8 cm higher in level than in middle latitudes.

This creates a slight gradient.

Water tends to flow down this slope.

## 14.3 Wind

Wind blowing on the ocean surface pushes water to move.

Friction between wind and water affects water movement.

## 14.4 Gravity

Gravity pulls water down gradients.

It helps create movement where water levels differ.

## 14.5 Coriolis Force

Coriolis force causes moving ocean water to turn:

- right in the northern hemisphere;
- left in the southern hemisphere.

## 14.6 Gyres

Large accumulations of water and flow around them are called gyres.

Gyres produce large circular currents in ocean basins.

---

# 15. Density And Vertical Mobility

## 15.1 Density Matters

Differences in water density affect vertical mobility of ocean currents.

## 15.2 Salinity And Density

High-salinity water is denser than low-salinity water.

## 15.3 Temperature And Density

Cold water is denser than warm water.

## 15.4 Sinking And Rising

Denser water tends to sink.

Lighter water tends to rise.

## 15.5 Cold And Warm Currents

Cold-water currents occur when cold polar water sinks and slowly moves toward the equator.

Warm-water currents move from the equator along the surface toward the poles to replace sinking cold water.

---

# 16. Types Of Ocean Currents By Depth

## 16.1 Surface Currents

Surface currents make up about:

```text
10 per cent of all ocean water
```

They occur in the upper:

```text
400 m
```

of the ocean.

## 16.2 Deep-Water Currents

Deep-water currents make up the remaining:

```text
90 per cent of ocean water
```

They move through ocean basins due to:

- density differences;
- gravity.

## 16.3 Where Deep Waters Sink

Deep waters sink into deep ocean basins at high latitudes where low temperature increases density.

---

# 17. Types Of Ocean Currents By Temperature

## 17.1 Cold Currents

Cold currents bring cold water into warm-water areas.

They are usually found:

- on west coasts of continents in low and middle latitudes in both hemispheres;
- on east coasts in higher latitudes of the northern hemisphere.

## 17.2 Warm Currents

Warm currents bring warm water into cold-water areas.

They are usually found:

- on east coasts of continents in low and middle latitudes in both hemispheres;
- on west coasts of continents in high latitudes of the northern hemisphere.

## 17.3 Warm vs Cold Currents

| Warm Current | Cold Current |
| --- | --- |
| Moves warm water poleward | Moves cold water equatorward |
| Raises coastal temperature | Lowers coastal temperature |
| Often east coasts in low/mid latitudes | Often west coasts in low/mid latitudes |
| Can increase rainfall | Often linked with fog and aridity |

---

# 18. Characteristics Of Ocean Currents

## 18.1 Drift

Currents are referred to by their drift.

Drift means speed of the current.

It is measured in knots.

## 18.2 Strength

The strength of a current refers to its speed.

A fast current is considered strong.

## 18.3 Surface Strength

Currents are usually strongest near the surface.

They may reach speeds over:

```text
5 knots
```

## 18.4 Depth Strength

At depth, currents are generally slow.

They often have speeds less than:

```text
0.5 knots
```

## 18.5 Speed With Depth

Current strength generally decreases with depth.

---

# 19. Major Ocean Currents And Circulation Pattern

## 19.1 Main Controls

Major currents are influenced by:

- prevailing winds;
- Coriolis force;
- atmospheric circulation;
- monsoon winds in monsoonal regions.

## 19.2 Atmospheric Link

Oceanic circulation roughly corresponds to atmospheric circulation.

Middle-latitude air circulation over oceans is mainly anticyclonic.

Ocean circulation reflects this pattern.

## 19.3 High Latitudes

At higher latitudes, wind flow is mostly cyclonic.

Ocean circulation follows this pattern.

## 19.4 Monsoonal Regions

In regions of strong monsoonal flow, monsoon winds influence current movements.

## 19.5 Coriolis Direction

Warm currents from low latitudes tend to move:

- right in northern hemisphere;
- left in southern hemisphere.

## 19.6 Heat Transport

Ocean circulation transports heat from one latitude belt to another.

It works similarly to atmospheric circulation.

Cold polar waters move toward warmer tropical and equatorial waters.

Warm low-latitude waters move poleward.

---

# 20. Effects Of Ocean Currents

## 20.1 Climate Effects

Ocean currents directly and indirectly influence human activities and climates.

## 20.2 West Coasts In Tropical And Subtropical Latitudes

West coasts of continents in tropical and subtropical latitudes are usually bordered by cool waters, except close to the equator.

Effects:

- low average temperatures;
- narrow diurnal and annual ranges;
- fog;
- arid conditions.

## 20.3 West Coasts In Middle And Higher Latitudes

West coasts in middle and higher latitudes are bordered by warm waters.

Effects:

- distinct marine climate;
- cool summers;
- mild winters;
- narrow annual temperature range.

## 20.4 East Coasts In Tropical And Subtropical Latitudes

Warm currents flow parallel to east coasts of continents in tropical and subtropical latitudes.

Effects:

- warm climate;
- rainy climate.

## 20.5 Northwest Europe Example

Warm ocean currents, especially the North Atlantic Drift, warm the coastal areas of northwestern Europe.

This gives the region milder winters than expected for its latitude.

## 20.6 Fishing Grounds

Mixing of warm and cold currents helps replenish oxygen and supports plankton growth.

Plankton is primary food for fish.

The best fishing grounds occur mainly in these mixing zones.

# 21. Common Mistakes To Avoid

## 21.1 Saying Waves Move Water Across The Ocean

Waves move energy.

Water particles mainly move in small circles.

## 21.2 Confusing Tides And Surges

Tides are regular and caused mainly by sun-moon attraction.

Surges are irregular and caused by weather conditions.

## 21.3 Confusing Perigee And Perihelion

Perigee relates to moon-earth distance.

Perihelion relates to earth-sun distance.

## 21.4 Confusing Ebb And Flow

Ebb means falling water level.

Flow or flood means rising water level.

## 21.5 Thinking Currents Are Only Wind-Driven

Winds are important, but currents are also affected by:

- gravity;
- Coriolis force;
- density;
- temperature;
- salinity.

## 21.6 Forgetting Fishing Link

Mixing of warm and cold currents increases oxygen and plankton.

This supports major fishing grounds.

## 23.2 Short Answer: Waves

Waves are movements of energy across the ocean surface.

Water particles move mainly in small circular paths as the wave passes.

Most waves are produced by wind blowing over water.

## 23.3 Short Answer: Source Of Wave Energy

Waves get their energy from wind.

Wind transfers energy to the ocean surface through friction.

The stronger and longer the wind blows over a larger area, the higher the waves can become.

## 23.4 Short Answer: Tides

Tides are periodic rise and fall of sea level once or twice a day.

They are caused mainly by gravitational attraction of the moon and the sun, along with centrifugal force.

Tides are caused by the difference between moon's gravitational attraction and centrifugal force.

Tides help navigators and fishermen plan activities because they can be predicted in advance.

High tides allow ships to enter shallow harbours and estuaries where bars may block entry at low tide.

# 24. Long Answer Guide

## 24.1 How Currents Affect Temperature

Use this structure:

1. State the principle.

Ocean currents transfer heat from one latitude to another.

2. Warm currents.

Warm currents carry warm water into colder regions and raise coastal temperatures.

3. Cold currents.

Cold currents carry cold water into warmer regions and reduce coastal temperatures.

4. Coastal effects.

West coasts in tropical and subtropical latitudes often have cool currents, lower temperatures, fog, and aridity.

East coasts in tropical and subtropical latitudes often have warm currents and rainy climate.

5. Northwest Europe.

The North Atlantic Drift warms northwestern Europe, giving it milder winters and a marine climate despite its high latitude.

6. Conclusion.

Ocean currents are major controls of coastal temperature and climate.

## 24.2 Causes Of Ocean Currents

Use this structure:

1. Define currents.

Ocean currents are continuous flows of ocean water in definite directions.

2. Solar heating.

Solar heating causes expansion of water near the equator, creating a slight gradient.

3. Wind.

Winds push surface water through friction.

4. Gravity.

Gravity pulls water down gradients.

5. Coriolis force.

Coriolis force deflects currents right in the northern hemisphere and left in the southern hemisphere.

6. Density.

Differences in temperature and salinity create density differences.

Cold and saline water sinks; warmer and less dense water rises.

7. Conclusion.

Currents are caused by combined effects of heating, wind, gravity, Coriolis force, and density differences.

# 27. Quick Revision Sheet

## 27.1 Must-Remember Movement Types

- Waves: energy movement across surface.
- Currents: horizontal flow of ocean water.
- Tides: periodic rise and fall of sea level.
- Upwelling: cold subsurface water rises.
- Sinking: dense surface water descends.

## 27.2 Must-Remember Wave Terms

- Crest: highest point.
- Trough: lowest point.
- Height: trough-to-crest distance.
- Amplitude: half height.
- Period: time between crests.
- Wavelength: distance between crests.
- Speed: wave movement rate.
- Frequency: waves per second.

## 27.3 Must-Remember Tide Types

- Semi-diurnal: two high and two low tides daily.
- Diurnal: one high and one low tide daily.
- Mixed: tide heights vary.
- Spring: sun, moon, earth in line.
- Neap: sun and moon at right angles.

## 27.4 Must-Remember Tide Distance Terms

- Perigee: moon closest to earth, larger tidal range.
- Apogee: moon farthest from earth, smaller tidal range.
- Perihelion: earth closest to sun around January 3, larger tidal range.
- Aphelion: earth farthest from sun around July 4, smaller tidal range.

## 27.5 Must-Remember Current Facts

- Surface currents: upper 400 m, about 10 per cent of ocean water.
- Deep currents: about 90 per cent of ocean water.
- Warm currents raise coastal temperature.
- Cold currents lower coastal temperature.
- Current speed is called drift and measured in knots.
- Best fishing grounds often occur where warm and cold currents mix.

# 28. Final Chapter Summary

Ocean water is dynamic. It moves horizontally through waves and currents and vertically through tides, upwelling and sinking. Waves are movements of energy across the ocean surface; water particles move mainly in small circles. Wind gives waves their energy, and waves break near shore when friction with the sea floor slows them.

Tides are regular rises and falls of sea level caused mainly by the gravitational pull of the moon and sun along with centrifugal force. Two tidal bulges form: one toward the moon and one away from it. Tides vary by frequency as semi-diurnal, diurnal and mixed tides, and by sun-moon-earth position as spring and neap tides. Tides are important for navigation, fishing, harbour entry, estuary cleaning and power generation.

Ocean currents are continuous flows of water in definite directions. They are caused by solar heating, wind, gravity, Coriolis force and density differences. Surface currents occupy the upper 400 m, while deep currents move most ocean water through density and gravity effects. Warm currents carry heat poleward and cold currents move cold water toward lower latitudes.

Ocean currents influence climate and human activity. Cold currents along tropical west coasts often create fog and aridity, while warm currents along some coasts create mild and rainy climates. Mixing zones of warm and cold currents support plankton growth and major fishing grounds.
