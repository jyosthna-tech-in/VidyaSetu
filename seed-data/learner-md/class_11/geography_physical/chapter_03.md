## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains what lies inside the earth and how scientists know about it.

No human being has reached the centre of the earth. Yet scientists understand the earth's interior by using direct sources such as rocks and volcanic material, and indirect sources such as seismic waves, gravity, magnetic fields, and meteorites.

The central idea is:

> The earth has a layered interior, and the best evidence for this comes from the behaviour of earthquake waves as they pass through materials of different density and state.

# 1. Why Study The Earth's Interior?

## 1.1 Surface Landscapes Begin Inside The Earth

Many surface features are shaped by internal processes.

Examples:

- mountains,
- plateaus,
- earthquakes,
- volcanoes,
- rift valleys,
- ocean basins,
- tsunamis.

Understanding the interior helps explain why the surface looks the way it does.

## 1.2 Endogenic And Exogenic Processes

Two broad types of processes shape the landscape:

- endogenic processes: processes from inside the earth;
- exogenic processes: processes acting on the surface from outside, such as rivers, wind, glaciers, and waves.

This chapter focuses mainly on endogenic forces.

## 1.3 Why Direct Knowledge Is Difficult

The earth's radius is about 6,370 km.

No one can reach the centre of the earth.

Depth brings:

- very high temperature,
- very high pressure,
- inaccessible conditions.

Therefore, scientists rely heavily on indirect evidence.

---

# 2. Direct Sources Of Information

Direct sources provide actual material from the earth.

## 2.1 Surface Rocks

Surface rocks are the most easily available solid earth material.

Geologists study:

- rock type,
- mineral composition,
- texture,
- structure,
- age.

## 2.2 Mining

Mines provide rocks from below the surface.

Gold mines in South Africa reach about 3-4 km depth.

Going deeper becomes very difficult because temperature increases rapidly.

## 2.3 Deep Drilling

Deep drilling projects give information about the crust.

Examples mentioned in the chapter:

- Deep Ocean Drilling Project,
- Integrated Ocean Drilling Project,
- Kola deep drilling project.

The deepest drill at Kola reached about 12 km.

This is still very shallow compared with the earth's radius.

## 2.4 Volcanic Material

Volcanic eruptions bring molten material to the surface.

This material can be analysed in laboratories.

It gives information about magma and rocks from below the surface.

## 2.5 Limitation Of Volcanic Material

Although lava can be studied directly, it is difficult to know the exact depth from which the magma came.

So volcanic material is useful but incomplete evidence.

---

# 3. Indirect Sources Of Information

Indirect sources do not provide material from the deep interior directly.

They help scientists infer internal structure.

## 3.1 Temperature, Pressure, And Density

Mining shows that temperature and pressure increase with depth.

Density also generally increases toward the interior.

By estimating the rate of change, scientists infer conditions at different depths.

## 3.2 Meteors

Meteors that reach earth are not from the earth's interior.

But their material and structure are similar to materials from which planets formed.

So they help scientists understand the nature of planetary material.

## 3.3 Gravitation

Gravity is not the same everywhere on earth.

It is:

- greater near the poles,
- lower near the equator.

This is partly because the equator is farther from the earth's centre than the poles.

## 3.4 Gravity Anomaly

Expected gravity values and actual measured gravity values may differ.

This difference is called gravity anomaly.

Gravity anomalies give information about uneven distribution of mass in the crust.

## 3.5 Magnetic Field

Magnetic surveys provide information about magnetic materials in the crust.

They help understand distribution of rocks and minerals.

## 3.6 Seismic Activity

Seismic activity is one of the most important sources of information about the earth's interior.

Seismic waves change speed and direction when they pass through different materials.

This behaviour reveals the earth's layered structure.

# 4. Earthquake: Basic Meaning

## 4.1 Definition

An earthquake is shaking of the earth caused by sudden release of energy.

This energy generates waves that travel in all directions.

## 4.2 Why Earth Shakes

Earthquakes occur when rocks along a fault move suddenly.

Rocks on opposite sides of a fault may tend to move in different directions.

Friction locks them together for some time.

When the stress becomes greater than friction, the rocks slip abruptly.

This releases energy.

## 4.3 Fault

A fault is a sharp break in crustal rocks.

Movement along faults is the main cause of tectonic earthquakes.

## 4.4 Focus Or Hypocentre

The point inside the earth where energy is released is called the focus.

It is also called the hypocentre.

## 4.5 Epicentre

The point on the surface directly above the focus is called the epicentre.

It is the first point on the surface to experience the waves.

## 4.6 Simple Diagram In Words

```text
Surface point directly above focus = Epicentre
       |
       |
Point inside earth where energy is released = Focus/Hypocentre
```

# 5. Earthquake Waves

## 5.1 Seismograph

A seismograph records earthquake waves reaching the surface.

The recorded curve shows different wave arrivals.

## 5.2 Main Types

Earthquake waves are of two broad types:

1. body waves,
2. surface waves.

## 5.3 Body Waves

Body waves are generated at the focus.

They travel through the interior body of the earth.

There are two types:

- P-waves,
- S-waves.

## 5.4 Surface Waves

Body waves interact with surface rocks and generate surface waves.

Surface waves move along the earth's surface.

They arrive later but are usually the most destructive.

---

# 6. P-Waves

## 6.1 Meaning

P-waves are primary waves.

They are the fastest earthquake waves and reach the surface first.

## 6.2 Material Travel

P-waves can travel through:

- solids,
- liquids,
- gases.

## 6.3 Movement

P-waves vibrate parallel to the direction of wave movement.

They compress and expand the material.

This creates:

- squeezing,
- stretching,
- density differences.

## 6.4 Comparison With Sound

P-waves are similar to sound waves because both are compressional waves.

---

# 7. S-Waves

## 7.1 Meaning

S-waves are secondary waves.

They arrive after P-waves.

## 7.2 Material Travel

S-waves travel only through solids.

They cannot travel through liquids.

This fact is extremely important for understanding the earth's interior.

## 7.3 Movement

S-waves vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave movement.

They create troughs and crests in the material through which they pass.

## 7.4 Importance

Because S-waves cannot pass through liquids, their absence in certain zones helped scientists infer that the outer core is liquid.

---

# 8. Surface Waves

## 8.1 Arrival

Surface waves are the last to arrive on a seismograph.

## 8.2 Movement

They move along the surface of the earth.

## 8.3 Damage

Surface waves are considered the most damaging earthquake waves.

They displace rocks and cause structural collapse.

## 8.4 Exam Point

If asked which earthquake waves are most destructive, answer:

- surface waves.

---

# 9. Wave Velocity, Reflection, And Refraction

## 9.1 Velocity And Density

The velocity of earthquake waves changes when they pass through materials of different density.

Generally:

- denser material increases wave velocity,
- less dense material lowers wave velocity.

## 9.2 Reflection

Reflection means waves rebound when they meet a boundary.

## 9.3 Refraction

Refraction means waves bend or change direction when they enter a different material.

## 9.4 Why This Matters

Changes in wave speed and direction show that the earth's interior is not uniform.

Different layers have different:

- density,
- composition,
- physical state,
- temperature,
- pressure.

---

# 10. Earthquake Shadow Zones

## 10.1 Meaning

A shadow zone is an area where earthquake waves are not recorded by seismographs.

## 10.2 Why Shadow Zones Form

Shadow zones form because waves are:

- reflected,
- refracted,
- blocked,
- unable to pass through certain materials.

## 10.3 P-Wave Shadow Zone

P-waves are recorded up to about 105 degrees from the epicentre.

They are not recorded in the zone between about 105 degrees and 145 degrees.

Beyond about 145 degrees, P-waves are recorded again.

So the P-wave shadow zone is a band between 105 degrees and 145 degrees from the epicentre.

## 10.4 S-Wave Shadow Zone

S-waves are recorded up to about 105 degrees from the epicentre.

Beyond 105 degrees, S-waves are not recorded.

This is because S-waves cannot pass through the liquid outer core.

## 10.5 Size Difference

The S-wave shadow zone is much larger than the P-wave shadow zone.

It covers a little over 40 percent of the earth's surface.

## 10.6 Importance

Shadow zones helped scientists infer:

- earth has layers,
- outer core is liquid,
- wave paths bend due to density changes.

---

# 11. Types Of Earthquakes

## 11.1 Tectonic Earthquakes

Tectonic earthquakes are the most common.

They are generated by sliding of rocks along a fault plane.

## 11.2 Volcanic Earthquakes

Volcanic earthquakes occur in areas of active volcanoes.

They are linked with movement of magma and volcanic activity.

## 11.3 Collapse Earthquakes

In areas of intense mining, roofs of underground mines may collapse.

This causes minor tremors called collapse earthquakes.

## 11.4 Explosion Earthquakes

Ground shaking may occur due to explosions of chemical or nuclear devices.

These are called explosion earthquakes.

## 11.5 Reservoir-Induced Earthquakes

Earthquakes in areas of large reservoirs are called reservoir-induced earthquakes.

They may be linked with pressure changes and water seepage in rocks.

---

# 12. Measuring Earthquakes

Earthquakes are measured by magnitude and intensity.

## 12.1 Richter Scale

The Richter scale measures magnitude.

Magnitude relates to the energy released during an earthquake.

It is expressed in numbers, commonly from 0 to 10 in school texts.

## 12.2 Mercalli Scale

The Mercalli scale measures intensity.

Intensity is based on visible damage and human experience.

It ranges from 1 to 12.

## 12.3 Difference

| Richter Scale | Mercalli Scale |
| --- | --- |
| Measures magnitude | Measures intensity |
| Based on energy released | Based on visible damage |
| Numerical scale in 0-10 range in textbook | 1-12 scale |
| More scientific/instrumental | More observational |

---

# 13. Effects Of Earthquakes

Earthquakes are natural hazards.

Major immediate effects include:

- ground shaking,
- differential ground settlement,
- landslides,
- mudslides,
- soil liquefaction,
- ground lurching,
- avalanches,
- ground displacement,
- floods from dam and levee failures,
- fires,
- structural collapse,
- falling objects,
- tsunami.

## 13.1 Landform-Related Effects

The first six effects are strongly related to landforms and ground behaviour.

These include:

- ground shaking,
- differential settlement,
- landslides,
- mudslides,
- liquefaction,
- ground lurching.

## 13.2 Human Impact

Earthquakes can cause:

- loss of life,
- injury,
- building collapse,
- fire,
- road damage,
- dam failure,
- bridge damage,
- disruption of services.

## 13.3 Tsunami

A tsunami may occur if:

- the earthquake epicentre is below ocean water,
- the magnitude is sufficiently high,
- seafloor displacement occurs.

Tsunamis are waves generated by tremors.

They are not earthquakes themselves.

## 13.4 Magnitude And Damage

Earthquake activity may last only a few seconds.

But if magnitude is high, especially above 5 on the Richter scale, effects can be destructive.

---

# 14. Frequency Of Earthquakes

High-magnitude earthquakes are rare.

Earthquakes of magnitude 8 or more may occur once in 1-2 years globally.

Small tremors occur very frequently, even almost every minute.

Not all parts of the world experience major shocks equally.

Distribution of earthquakes is linked with tectonic settings, which is studied in later chapters.

---

# 15. Structure Of The Earth

Earth has three major internal layers:

1. crust,
2. mantle,
3. core.

These layers differ in:

- thickness,
- density,
- composition,
- physical state.

---

# 16. The Crust

## 16.1 Meaning

The crust is the outermost solid part of the earth.

It is brittle in nature.

## 16.2 Oceanic And Continental Crust

The thickness of the crust varies under oceans and continents.

| Type | Approximate Thickness | Feature |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Oceanic crust | About 5 km | Thinner, basaltic, denser |
| Continental crust | About 30 km average | Thicker, less dense |
| Mountain belts | Up to about 70 km | Very thick crust, e.g., Himalayas |

## 16.3 Density

Oceanic crust is basaltic and relatively dense, around 3 g/cm3.

Continental crust has average density around 2.7 g/cm3.

## 16.4 Importance

The crust is where humans live, mine, build, farm, and directly observe rocks.

But it is only a thin outer shell compared with earth's total radius.

---

# 17. The Mantle

## 17.1 Extent

The mantle lies below the crust.

It extends from Moho discontinuity to about 2,900 km depth.

## 17.2 Asthenosphere

The upper part of the mantle contains a weak zone called the asthenosphere.

Astheno means weak.

It may extend up to about 400 km depth.

## 17.3 Source Of Magma

The asthenosphere is the main source of magma that reaches the surface during volcanic eruptions.

## 17.4 Density

The mantle has higher density than the crust.

The chapter gives about 3.4 g/cm3 for the asthenosphere region.

## 17.5 Lithosphere

The crust and the uppermost part of the mantle together form the lithosphere.

The lithosphere is rigid.

Its thickness ranges from about 10 km to 200 km.

## 17.6 Lower Mantle

The lower mantle extends below the asthenosphere.

It is in solid state.

---

# 18. The Core

## 18.1 Boundary

The core-mantle boundary is located at about 2,900 km depth.

## 18.2 Outer Core

The outer core is liquid.

This is supported by the behaviour of S-waves, which cannot pass through it.

## 18.3 Inner Core

The inner core is solid.

## 18.4 Density

Density increases greatly toward the centre.

At the mantle-core boundary, density is about 5 g/cm3.

At the centre, around 6,300 km depth, density may be about 13 g/cm3.

## 18.5 Composition

The core is made of heavy material, mainly:

- nickel,
- iron.

It is sometimes called the nife layer.

## 18.6 Layer Summary

| Layer | Depth/Thickness | State/Feature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Crust | 5 km oceanic, 30 km continental average | Solid, brittle |
| Mantle | Below crust to 2,900 km | Solid, with weak asthenosphere above |
| Outer core | Below 2,900 km | Liquid |
| Inner core | Central part | Solid |

---

# 19. Volcanoes

## 19.1 Meaning

A volcano is a place where gases, ash, and molten rock material escape to the surface.

## 19.2 Active Volcano

A volcano is called active if materials are being released or have been released in the recent past.

## 19.3 Magma And Lava

Molten rock below the surface is called magma.

When it reaches the surface, it is called lava.

## 19.4 Volcanic Materials

Materials reaching the surface include:

- lava flows,
- pyroclastic debris,
- volcanic bombs,
- ash,
- dust,
- gases.

Volcanic gases may include:

- nitrogen compounds,
- sulphur compounds,
- small amounts of chlorine,
- hydrogen,
- argon.

## 19.5 Source

Molten material mainly comes from the upper mantle region, especially the asthenosphere.

---

# 20. Types Of Volcanoes

Volcanoes are classified on the basis of:

- nature of eruption,
- form developed at the surface.

Major types include:

1. shield volcanoes,
2. composite volcanoes,
3. calderas,
4. flood basalt provinces,
5. mid-ocean ridge volcanoes.

---

# 21. Shield Volcanoes

## 21.1 Size

Shield volcanoes are among the largest volcanoes, except for flood basalt provinces.

## 21.2 Example

Hawaiian volcanoes are famous examples.

## 21.3 Lava Type

They are mostly made of basalt.

Basaltic lava is very fluid when erupted.

## 21.4 Shape

Because lava flows easily, shield volcanoes are broad and not steep.

## 21.5 Eruption Style

They usually have low explosivity.

They become explosive mainly if water enters the vent.

## 21.6 Cinder Cone Link

Sometimes lava fountains throw material around the vent, building a cone called a cinder cone.

---

# 22. Composite Volcanoes

## 22.1 Lava Type

Composite volcanoes erupt cooler and more viscous lava than basalt.

## 22.2 Eruption Style

They often have explosive eruptions.

## 22.3 Materials

They release:

- lava,
- pyroclastic material,
- ash.

## 22.4 Layered Structure

The materials accumulate around the vent in layers.

This creates the composite structure.

## 22.5 Shape

Composite volcanoes are usually steep-sided volcanic cones.

---

# 23. Calderas

## 23.1 Meaning

Calderas are large collapsed volcanic depressions.

## 23.2 Formation

They form when explosive volcanoes collapse into themselves after eruption.

## 23.3 Explosiveness

Caldera-forming volcanoes are among the most explosive.

Their explosiveness suggests a huge magma chamber near the surface.

## 23.4 Key Point

Calderas are not tall cones.

They are collapsed depressions.

---

# 24. Flood Basalt Provinces

## 24.1 Lava Type

Flood basalt provinces form from highly fluid lava.

This lava spreads over long distances.

## 24.2 Scale

Some parts of the world are covered by thousands of square kilometres of thick basalt flows.

Individual flows may extend for hundreds of kilometres.

Some flows may be more than 50 m thick.

## 24.3 Deccan Traps

The Deccan Traps of India are a major flood basalt province.

They presently cover much of the Maharashtra plateau.

They originally covered a larger area than today.

## 24.4 Exam Answer

If asked which eruptions caused the Deccan Trap formations, answer:

- flood basalt eruptions.

---

# 25. Mid-Ocean Ridge Volcanoes

## 25.1 Location

Mid-ocean ridge volcanoes occur in oceanic areas.

## 25.2 Global Ridge System

There is a system of mid-ocean ridges more than 70,000 km long across ocean basins.

## 25.3 Eruptions

The central portions of these ridges experience frequent eruptions.

## 25.4 Importance

Mid-ocean ridge volcanism is linked with the creation of new oceanic crust.

This is discussed more in plate tectonics.

---

# 26. Volcanic Landforms And Igneous Rocks

## 26.1 Cooling Of Lava

Lava released during eruptions cools and forms igneous rocks.

## 26.2 Volcanic Rocks

If lava cools at the surface, it forms volcanic rocks.

These are also called extrusive igneous rocks.

## 26.3 Plutonic Rocks

If magma cools inside the crust, it forms plutonic rocks.

These are intrusive igneous rocks.

## 26.4 Intrusive Forms

When magma cools within the crust, it may form different shapes.

These are called intrusive forms.

---

# 27. Batholiths

## 27.1 Meaning

A batholith is a large body of magmatic material that cools deep inside the crust.

## 27.2 Shape

It develops as a large dome-like body.

## 27.3 Exposure

Batholiths appear at the surface only after erosion and denudation remove overlying material.

## 27.4 Composition

They are granitic bodies.

## 27.5 Origin

Batholiths are cooled portions of magma chambers.

---

# 28. Lacoliths

## 28.1 Meaning

A lacolith is a large dome-shaped intrusive body with a level base.

## 28.2 Connection

It is connected from below by a pipe-like conduit.

## 28.3 Appearance

It resembles a volcanic dome, but it forms below the surface.

## 28.4 Example

The Karnataka plateau has domal hills of granite rocks.

Many are examples of lacoliths or batholiths after exfoliation.

---

# 29. Lapoliths, Phacoliths, Sills, And Sheets

## 29.1 Lapolith

A lapolith is a saucer-shaped intrusive body.

It is concave upward.

## 29.2 Phacolith

A phacolith is a wavy mass of intrusive rock.

It may occur:

- at the base of synclines,
- at the top of anticlines,
- in folded igneous country.

It has a conduit linking it with a deeper magma chamber.

## 29.3 Sill

A sill is a thick, near-horizontal intrusive igneous body.

## 29.4 Sheet

A sheet is a thinner near-horizontal intrusive body.

## 29.5 Difference Between Sill And Sheet

| Sill | Sheet |
| --- | --- |
| Thicker horizontal intrusive body | Thinner horizontal intrusive body |
| Forms when magma spreads along a weak plane | Same process, but thinner deposit |

---

# 30. Dykes

## 30.1 Meaning

A dyke is a wall-like intrusive body.

## 30.2 Formation

Lava or magma moves through cracks and fissures.

It solidifies almost perpendicular to the ground.

The cooled body forms a wall-like structure.

## 30.3 Example

Dykes are common in western Maharashtra.

They are considered feeders for eruptions that formed the Deccan Traps.

---

# 31. Locating An Epicentre

The chapter includes a simplified activity for locating an earthquake epicentre.

## 31.1 Required Data

You need arrival times of:

- P-waves,
- S-waves,

from at least three seismograph stations.

## 31.2 Method

1. Find arrival time of P-waves and S-waves for each station.
2. Calculate time lag between S-wave and P-wave arrival.
3. Use the basic rule: each second of lag is roughly 8 km distance.
4. Convert lag into distance.
5. Mark the three seismograph stations on a map.
6. Draw circles around each station using calculated distance as radius.
7. The point where circles intersect is the epicentre.

## 31.3 Why Three Stations?

One station gives a distance, not a direction.

Two stations narrow the possible area.

Three stations locate the intersection point more clearly.

## 31.4 Real Practice

Real epicentre location uses computer models and knowledge of crustal structure.

The classroom method is simplified but based on the same principle.

# 32. High-Value Comparisons

## 32.1 Direct vs Indirect Sources

| Direct Sources | Indirect Sources |
| --- | --- |
| Provide actual material | Provide inferred information |
| Rocks, mines, drilling, lava | Seismic waves, gravity, magnetism, meteors |
| Limited depth | Can reveal deep interior |
| Easier to analyse physically | Requires interpretation |

## 32.2 P-Waves vs S-Waves

| P-Waves | S-Waves |
| --- | --- |
| Primary waves | Secondary waves |
| Fastest | Slower than P-waves |
| Arrive first | Arrive after P-waves |
| Travel through solids, liquids, gases | Travel only through solids |
| Compress and expand material | Move perpendicular to direction |
| Help identify layers | Help prove outer core is liquid |

## 32.3 Body Waves vs Surface Waves

| Body Waves | Surface Waves |
| --- | --- |
| Travel through earth's interior | Travel along earth's surface |
| Include P and S waves | Generated by interaction with surface rocks |
| Arrive earlier | Arrive later |
| Less damaging than surface waves generally | Most destructive |

## 32.4 Richter vs Mercalli

| Richter Scale | Mercalli Scale |
| --- | --- |
| Magnitude | Intensity |
| Energy released | Damage observed |
| Instrumental | Observational |
| Same event has one magnitude | Intensity varies by location |

## 32.5 Magma vs Lava

| Magma | Lava |
| --- | --- |
| Molten rock below surface | Molten rock at surface |
| Inside crust or mantle | Erupted onto ground |
| Can form intrusive rocks | Forms volcanic rocks |

## 32.6 Volcanic vs Plutonic Rocks

| Volcanic Rocks | Plutonic Rocks |
| --- | --- |
| Cool at surface | Cool inside crust |
| Extrusive | Intrusive |
| Often fine-grained | Often coarse-grained |
| Lava flows, ash-related rocks | Batholiths, lacoliths, sills, dykes |

# 35. Common Misunderstandings

## 35.1 We Know The Interior By Direct Observation

Incorrect.

Most knowledge is based on indirect evidence, especially seismic waves.

## 35.2 Lava And Magma Are Same In All Conditions

Incomplete.

Molten rock below the surface is magma.

After reaching the surface, it is lava.

## 35.3 S-Waves Pass Through Liquid

Incorrect.

S-waves travel only through solids.

This is why they reveal the liquid outer core.

## 35.4 Surface Waves Are First To Arrive

Incorrect.

P-waves arrive first, S-waves second, surface waves last.

Surface waves are usually most destructive.

## 35.5 The Lithosphere Is Only The Crust

Incorrect.

Lithosphere includes the crust plus the uppermost solid mantle.

## 35.6 Calderas Are Tall Volcanic Cones

Incorrect.

Calderas are collapsed depressions formed after explosive eruptions.

## 35.7 All Volcanoes Are Steep

Incorrect.

Shield volcanoes are broad and gentle because basaltic lava is very fluid.

## 36.2 Short Answer: Body Waves

Body waves are earthquake waves generated at the focus.

They travel through the body of the earth in all directions.

They include P-waves and S-waves.

## 36.3 Short Answer: Direct Sources

Direct sources of information about the earth's interior include:

- surface rocks,
- rocks from mining areas,
- rocks from deep drilling,
- volcanic material such as lava.

## 36.4 Short Answer: Why Shadow Zones Develop

Shadow zones develop because seismic waves change direction, speed, or disappear when they pass through materials of different density and physical state.

P-waves are refracted inside the earth, creating a shadow zone between about 105 degrees and 145 degrees.

S-waves cannot pass through the liquid outer core, so they are absent beyond about 105 degrees from the epicentre.

## 36.5 Short Answer: Indirect Sources Other Than Seismic Activity

Indirect sources include:

- temperature, pressure, and density changes with depth;
- meteors, which have material similar to planetary material;
- gravity anomalies, which show uneven mass distribution;
- magnetic surveys, which reveal magnetic materials in the crust.

# 37. Long Answer Guide

## 37.1 Effects Of Propagation Of Earthquake Waves

Use this structure:

1. Earthquake waves travel through rocks and make them vibrate.
2. P-waves vibrate parallel to direction of propagation.
3. P-waves compress and expand material, causing density differences.
4. S-waves vibrate perpendicular to direction of propagation.
5. S-waves create troughs and crests.
6. Surface waves travel along the surface and cause the greatest damage.
7. Wave velocity changes with density.
8. Waves reflect and refract at boundaries.
9. These changes help scientists identify layers inside the earth.

## 37.2 Intrusive Forms

Use this structure:

1. Define intrusive forms:
   Intrusive forms are bodies formed when magma cools inside the crust.

2. Batholith:
   Large dome-like granitic body formed deep in the crust; exposed after denudation.

3. Lacolith:
   Dome-shaped body with level base and pipe-like conduit.

4. Lapolith:
   Saucer-shaped body concave upward.

5. Phacolith:
   Wavy body found in folded rocks, at syncline bases or anticline tops.

6. Sill and sheet:
   Near-horizontal intrusive bodies; thick ones are sills, thin ones are sheets.

7. Dyke:
   Wall-like body formed when magma solidifies in vertical cracks.

8. Conclude:
   Intrusive forms show how magma moves and cools below the surface.

## 37.3 Structure Of The Earth

Use this structure:

1. Crust:
   Outermost brittle layer. Oceanic crust is thin, about 5 km; continental crust averages about 30 km and may reach 70 km in Himalayas.

2. Mantle:
   Extends from Moho to 2,900 km depth. Upper weak zone is asthenosphere, source of magma.

3. Lithosphere:
   Crust plus uppermost solid mantle, about 10-200 km thick.

4. Core:
   Starts below 2,900 km. Outer core is liquid, inner core is solid. Made mainly of nickel and iron.

5. Evidence:
   Seismic wave behaviour, especially S-wave shadow zone, proves layered structure.

# 40. Final Chapter Summary

The earth's interior cannot be directly observed because the earth is too deep, hot, and pressurised. Scientists use direct sources such as rocks, mines, drilling, and volcanic material, and indirect sources such as seismic waves, gravity, magnetism, and meteors.

Earthquakes occur when energy is suddenly released along faults. The point of release is the focus or hypocentre, and the surface point above it is the epicentre. Earthquake waves include body waves and surface waves. P-waves travel fastest through solids, liquids, and gases. S-waves travel only through solids. Surface waves are the most destructive.

Shadow zones of P-waves and S-waves prove that the earth's interior is layered. The S-wave shadow zone especially shows that the outer core is liquid.

The earth has crust, mantle, and core. The crust is thin and brittle. The mantle extends to about 2,900 km and contains the weak asthenosphere. The core is rich in nickel and iron; the outer core is liquid and the inner core is solid.

Volcanoes release lava, ash, gases, and pyroclastic material. Major types include shield, composite, caldera, flood basalt, and mid-ocean ridge volcanoes. Magma cooling inside the crust forms intrusive landforms such as batholiths, lacoliths, lapoliths, phacoliths, sills, sheets, and dykes.

To master this chapter, focus on evidence and process: how scientists infer the interior, how waves behave, how layers differ, and how magma creates volcanic landforms.
