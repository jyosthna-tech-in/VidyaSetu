# Part 1: Temperature And Heat

## Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the hotness or coldness of a body.

It tells us the direction in which heat will flow:

```text
Heat flows from higher temperature to lower temperature.
```

### Everyday Example

A cup of hot tea cools on a table because heat flows from the tea to the cooler surroundings. A glass of ice-cold water warms because heat flows from the warmer surroundings into the water.

---

## Heat

Heat is energy transferred between systems because of a temperature difference.

### Important Distinction

| Quantity | Meaning | SI Unit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Temperature | degree of hotness or coldness | kelvin, `K` |
| Heat | energy transferred due to temperature difference | joule, `J` |

### Common Mistake

Do not say "a body contains heat" in the strict physics sense. A body has internal energy. Heat is energy in transit due to temperature difference.

---

## Thermal Equilibrium

Two bodies are in thermal equilibrium when there is no net heat flow between them.

This happens when they have the same temperature.

### Learner Explanation

If hot tea and the room are at different temperatures, heat flows. After enough time, if no other effects interfere, the tea and room reach the same temperature. At that point, heat transfer stops in the net sense.

# Part 2: Measuring Temperature

## Why Touch Is Not Reliable

Human temperature sense is limited and relative. The same object may feel hot or cold depending on what your hand touched earlier.

Physics needs a repeatable measurement device, so thermometers are used.

---

## Thermometric Property

A thermometric property is a physical property that changes regularly with temperature.

Examples:

| Thermometric Property | Used In |
| --- | --- |
| volume of liquid | mercury or alcohol thermometer |
| pressure of gas at constant volume | gas thermometer |
| electrical resistance | resistance thermometer |
| thermal radiation | radiation thermometer |

---

## Fixed Points Of A Temperature Scale

A standard temperature scale needs reference points.

For water at standard pressure:

| Fixed Point | Celsius | Fahrenheit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ice point | `0 degree C` | `32 degree F` |
| steam point | `100 degree C` | `212 degree F` |

There are 100 equal intervals on the Celsius scale and 180 equal intervals on the Fahrenheit scale between these two points.

---

## Celsius-Fahrenheit Conversion

The relation is:

```text
(tF - 32) / 180 = tC / 100
```

Simplified:

```text
tF = (9/5)tC + 32
```

and:

```text
tC = (5/9)(tF - 32)
```

### Example

Convert `37 degree C` to Fahrenheit:

```text
tF = (9/5)(37) + 32
tF = 66.6 + 32
tF = 98.6 degree F
```

# Part 3: Ideal Gas Equation And Absolute Temperature

## Boyle's Law

For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature:

```text
PV = constant
```

Pressure and volume are inversely related.

### Meaning

If volume decreases, pressure increases, provided temperature remains constant.

---

## Charles' Law

For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure:

```text
V/T = constant
```

Volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature.

### Important

Temperature must be in kelvin, not Celsius.

---

## Ideal Gas Equation

For a low-density gas:

```text
PV = nRT
```

The raw source uses the symbol `mu` for number of moles; here `n` is used because it is common in learner notes.

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `P` | pressure |
| `V` | volume |
| `n` | number of moles |
| `R` | universal gas constant |
| `T` | absolute temperature in kelvin |

```text
R = 8.31 J mol^-1 K^-1
```

---

## Absolute Zero

Experiments with low-density gases show that pressure would become zero at:

```text
-273.15 degree C
```

This is called absolute zero.

On the Kelvin scale:

```text
0 K = -273.15 degree C
```

---

## Celsius-Kelvin Relation

```text
T = tC + 273.15
```

For most school-level numerical problems:

```text
T approximately equals tC + 273
```

### Example

```text
27 degree C = 27 + 273 = 300 K
```

# Part 4: Thermal Expansion

## Meaning Of Thermal Expansion

Most substances expand when heated and contract when cooled.

Thermal expansion is the increase in size of a body due to rise in temperature.

---

## Types Of Expansion

| Type | Quantity That Changes |
| --- | --- |
| Linear expansion | length |
| Area expansion | area |
| Volume expansion | volume |

---

## Linear Expansion

For a rod of initial length `l`, a temperature rise `Delta T` causes:

```text
Delta l = alpha_l l Delta T
```

or:

```text
Delta l / l = alpha_l Delta T
```

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Delta l` | change in length |
| `l` | original length |
| `alpha_l` | coefficient of linear expansion |
| `Delta T` | change in temperature |

SI unit of `alpha_l`:

```text
K^-1
```

---

## Area Expansion

For small temperature changes:

```text
Delta A / A = alpha_A Delta T
```

For an isotropic solid:

```text
alpha_A = 2 alpha_l
```

### Why

If both length and breadth expand, area gets contributions from both directions. The very tiny product term is neglected.

---

## Volume Expansion

For volume:

```text
Delta V / V = alpha_V Delta T
```

For an isotropic solid:

```text
alpha_V = 3 alpha_l
```

### Derivation Sketch

For a cube of side `l`:

```text
V = l^3
```

If each side increases by `Delta l`:

```text
V' = (l + Delta l)^3
```

Neglecting very small higher-order terms:

```text
Delta V approximately equals 3l^2 Delta l
```

Divide by `V = l^3`:

```text
Delta V/V = 3 Delta l/l
```

Since:

```text
Delta l/l = alpha_l Delta T
```

we get:

```text
alpha_V = 3 alpha_l
```

---

## Why Metal Lids Open After Heating

A metal lid expands when heated. If a tightly closed bottle is dipped with its lid in hot water, the lid expands slightly and loosens.

This is a direct application of thermal expansion.

---

## Blacksmith's Iron Ring Example

The raw source gives a classic example: an iron ring is heated so it expands and fits over the wooden wheel rim. After cooling, it contracts and grips the rim tightly.

Given:

```text
T1 = 27 degree C
initial ring diameter = 5.231 m
required diameter = 5.243 m
alpha_l = 1.20 x 10^-5 K^-1
```

Use:

```text
L2 = L1[1 + alpha_l(T2 - T1)]
```

Substitution gives:

```text
T2 = 218 degree C
```

### Learner Interpretation

The ring does not need to melt or soften. It only needs to expand enough so its diameter becomes equal to the wheel rim diameter.

---

## Thermal Stress

If a body is heated but prevented from expanding, stress develops inside it.

This is called thermal stress.

### Example

Railway tracks have gaps because rails expand in hot weather. If expansion is completely prevented, the stress can become large enough to bend the rails.

### Formula Idea

If expansion is prevented:

```text
thermal strain = alpha_l Delta T
```

Stress:

```text
stress = Y alpha_l Delta T
```

where `Y` is Young's modulus.

# Part 5: Anomalous Expansion Of Water

## The Special Behaviour

Water behaves unusually between `0 degree C` and `4 degree C`.

When water is heated from `0 degree C` to `4 degree C`, it contracts instead of expanding.

Water has maximum density at:

```text
4 degree C
```

---

## Why Lakes Freeze From The Top

As lake water cools toward `4 degree C`, it becomes denser and sinks. Warmer water rises and the mixing continues.

But below `4 degree C`, water becomes less dense and stays near the surface. This surface water freezes first.

### Importance

If lakes froze from the bottom upward, aquatic life would be badly harmed. Because ice forms on top, water below can remain liquid.

# Part 6: Specific Heat Capacity

## Heat Capacity

Heat capacity of a body is the heat required to raise its temperature by one unit.

```text
S = Delta Q / Delta T
```

SI unit:

```text
J K^-1
```

---

## Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is heat required per unit mass per unit temperature rise.

```text
s = Delta Q / (m Delta T)
```

or:

```text
Delta Q = m s Delta T
```

SI unit:

```text
J kg^-1 K^-1
```

### Meaning

If a substance has high specific heat capacity, it needs a lot of heat for a small temperature rise.

Water has high specific heat capacity, so it heats and cools slowly compared with many other substances.

---

## Molar Specific Heat Capacity

If the amount of substance is measured in moles:

```text
C = Delta Q / (n Delta T)
```

SI unit:

```text
J mol^-1 K^-1
```

For gases, two molar heat capacities are important:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Cp` | molar specific heat capacity at constant pressure |
| `Cv` | molar specific heat capacity at constant volume |

### Why Gases Need Conditions

When a gas is heated, it may expand and do work. The heat needed depends on whether pressure or volume is kept constant.

---

# Part 7: Calorimetry

## Principle Of Calorimetry

In an insulated system:

```text
heat lost by hot body = heat gained by cold body
```

This assumes no heat is lost to the surroundings.

---

## Standard Calorimetry Equation

For each substance:

```text
Q = m s Delta T
```

Then compare heat lost and gained.

### Sign-Safe Method

Use magnitudes:

```text
heat lost = m s (higher temperature - final temperature)
heat gained = m s (final temperature - lower temperature)
```

---

## Example: Ice Mixed With Water

The raw source includes a problem where `0.15 kg` ice at `0 degree C` is mixed with `0.30 kg` water at `50 degree C`, and final temperature is `6.7 degree C`.

Heat lost by warm water:

```text
Q_lost = 0.30 x 4186 x (50.0 - 6.7)
Q_lost = 54376.14 J
```

Heat used to melt ice:

```text
Q_melt = 0.15 Lf
```

Heat used to warm melted ice water to `6.7 degree C`:

```text
Q_warm = 0.15 x 4186 x 6.7
Q_warm = 4206.93 J
```

Using calorimetry:

```text
54376.14 = 0.15 Lf + 4206.93
Lf = 3.34 x 10^5 J kg^-1
```

# Part 8: Change Of State

## What Is Change Of State?

Matter can change between solid, liquid, and gas.

| Change | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| melting | solid to liquid |
| freezing | liquid to solid |
| boiling/vaporisation | liquid to gas |
| condensation | gas to liquid |
| sublimation | solid directly to gas |

---

## Temperature Remains Constant During Phase Change

During melting or boiling, heat is supplied but temperature does not change.

### Why

The heat supplied is used to change the internal arrangement of particles, not to increase their average kinetic energy.

---

## Latent Heat

Latent heat is heat required per unit mass during a change of state at constant temperature.

```text
Q = mL
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Q` | heat transferred |
| `m` | mass |
| `L` | latent heat |

SI unit of `L`:

```text
J kg^-1
```

---

## Latent Heat Of Fusion And Vaporisation

| Quantity | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `Lf` | latent heat of fusion, for solid-liquid change |
| `Lv` | latent heat of vaporisation, for liquid-gas change |

For water at standard atmospheric pressure:

```text
Lf = 3.33 x 10^5 J kg^-1
Lv = 22.6 x 10^5 J kg^-1
```

### Why Steam Burns Are Serious

Steam at `100 degree C` contains extra latent heat compared with water at `100 degree C`. When steam condenses on skin, it releases this latent heat, causing more severe burns.

---

## Heating Ice To Steam: Step Method

The raw source gives the full method for converting ice at `-12 degree C` to steam at `100 degree C`.

Break the process into stages:

1. Warm ice from `-12 degree C` to `0 degree C`.
2. Melt ice at `0 degree C`.
3. Warm water from `0 degree C` to `100 degree C`.
4. Convert water at `100 degree C` to steam at `100 degree C`.

For `3 kg` ice:

```text
Q1 = m s_ice Delta T = 75600 J
Q2 = m Lf = 1005000 J
Q3 = m s_water Delta T = 1255800 J
Q4 = m Lv = 6768000 J
```

Total:

```text
Q = Q1 + Q2 + Q3 + Q4
Q = 9.1 x 10^6 J
```

### Exam Habit

Never try to do such a problem in one formula. Draw the heating path and calculate each stage separately.

---

## Boiling Point And Pressure

Boiling point increases when pressure increases and decreases when pressure decreases.

### Pressure Cooker

In a pressure cooker, pressure is higher than atmospheric pressure. Therefore, water boils at a temperature higher than `100 degree C`, and food cooks faster.

### High Altitudes

At high altitude, atmospheric pressure is lower. Water boils at a lower temperature, so cooking becomes slower.

---

## Triple Point

The triple point is the temperature and pressure at which solid, liquid, and vapour phases coexist.

For water:

```text
T = 273.16 K
P = 6.11 x 10^-3 Pa
```

### Phase Diagram

A pressure-temperature phase diagram separates regions where the substance exists as solid, liquid, or vapour. The curves show where two phases coexist.

# Part 9: Heat Transfer

Heat can be transferred by three modes:

1. conduction,
2. convection,
3. radiation.

---

## Conduction

Conduction is heat transfer between adjacent parts of a body due to temperature difference, without bulk motion of matter.

### Example

If one end of a metal rod is heated, the other end becomes hot after some time.

---

## Heat Current In Conduction

For a bar of length `L`, area `A`, and end temperatures `T_hot` and `T_cold`:

```text
H = K A (T_hot - T_cold) / L
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `H` | heat current or rate of heat flow |
| `K` | thermal conductivity |
| `A` | cross-sectional area |
| `L` | length |

SI unit of `K`:

```text
W m^-1 K^-1
```

---

## Conductors And Insulators

| Material Type | Heat Conductivity |
| --- | --- |
| metals such as copper | high |
| wood, air, glass wool, foam | low |

### Everyday Uses

| Situation | Physics |
| --- | --- |
| copper bottom of cooking pot | spreads heat quickly |
| plastic or wooden handles | reduce heat conduction to hand |
| foam insulation in houses | slows heat transfer |
| air pockets in wool | reduce conduction |

---

## Composite Bar In Steady State

The source includes examples where two rods are joined end to end.

### Key Principle

In steady state:

```text
heat current through rod 1 = heat current through rod 2
```

For two rods:

```text
K1 A1 (T1 - Tj)/L1 = K2 A2 (Tj - T2)/L2
```

where `Tj` is the junction temperature.

### Learner Method

1. Write heat-current equation for each part.
2. Set them equal.
3. Solve for junction temperature.
4. Use the same heat current through either section.

---

## Convection

Convection is heat transfer by actual motion of matter. It occurs in fluids: liquids and gases.

### Natural Convection

When a fluid is heated from below:

1. hot fluid expands,
2. density decreases,
3. hot fluid rises,
4. cooler fluid moves down to replace it,
5. a convection current forms.

### Forced Convection

Forced convection happens when fluid motion is driven by a pump, fan, heart, or mechanical system.

Examples:

| Example | Type |
| --- | --- |
| blood circulation | forced convection |
| automobile cooling system | forced convection |
| room heater with fan | forced convection |
| sea breeze | natural convection |

---

## Sea Breeze And Land Breeze

### Daytime: Sea Breeze

Land heats faster than water. Air above land becomes warm and rises. Cooler air from the sea moves toward land.

This produces sea breeze.

### Night: Land Breeze

Land cools faster than water. Air above the sea is now warmer and rises. Cooler air from land moves toward sea.

This produces land breeze.

---

## Radiation

Radiation is heat transfer by electromagnetic waves. It does not need a material medium.

### Example

The Sun heats Earth through empty space. This cannot be conduction or convection because space between the Sun and Earth is mostly vacuum.

---

## Thermal Radiation

All bodies emit radiation due to their temperature.

Hotter bodies emit more intense radiation.

Black surfaces are good absorbers and good emitters of radiation. Light-coloured or shiny surfaces are poorer absorbers.

### Everyday Applications

| Observation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| white clothes in summer | absorb less solar radiation |
| dark clothes in winter | absorb more radiation |
| blackened cooking vessel bottoms | absorb heat better |
| silvered thermos bottle walls | reflect radiation |

---

## Dewar Flask Or Thermos Bottle

A thermos reduces heat transfer by all three modes.

| Design Feature | Heat Transfer Reduced |
| --- | --- |
| vacuum between walls | conduction and convection |
| silvered walls | radiation |
| insulating cork/support | conduction |

### Purpose

It can keep hot things hot and cold things cold because it slows heat exchange with surroundings.

---

# Part 10: Newton's Law Of Cooling

## Statement

When the temperature difference between a body and its surroundings is small, the rate of cooling is proportional to that temperature difference.

```text
rate of cooling proportional to (T - Ts)
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `T` | body temperature |
| `Ts` | surrounding temperature |

---

## Meaning

A very hot object cools faster at first because the temperature difference is large. As its temperature approaches room temperature, cooling becomes slower.

---

## Cooling Curve

The temperature-time graph is not a straight line for the whole process. It slopes steeply at first and gradually becomes flatter.

### Learner Interpretation

Cooling slows down as the object becomes closer to the surrounding temperature.

# Part 13: Common Mistakes

## Mistake 1: Confusing Heat With Temperature

Temperature is not heat. Heat is energy transferred due to temperature difference.

---

## Mistake 2: Using Celsius In Gas Equations

The ideal gas equation requires temperature in kelvin.

Wrong:

```text
PV = nR(27)
```

Correct:

```text
27 degree C = 300 K
PV = nR(300)
```

---

## Mistake 3: Forgetting Latent Heat

When ice melts or water boils, temperature remains constant but heat is still absorbed.

Use:

```text
Q = mL
```

not:

```text
Q = ms Delta T
```

because `Delta T = 0` during the phase change.

---

## Mistake 4: Treating All Heat Transfer As Conduction

If matter moves in bulk, it is convection. If no medium is needed, it is radiation.

---

## Mistake 5: Ignoring Pressure In Boiling

Boiling point depends on pressure. Water does not always boil at `100 degree C`; that is its normal boiling point at standard atmospheric pressure.
