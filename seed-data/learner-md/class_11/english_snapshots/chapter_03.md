# Chapter 3: Ranga's Marriage
### NCERT Class 11 English Snapshots

---

## Chapter Purpose

"Ranga's Marriage" by Masti Venkatesha Iyengar is a humorous story set in the village of Hosahalli. It presents village life, social customs, the influence of English education, and the traditional practice of arranging marriages. The narrator plays an active role in arranging Ranga's marriage to Ratna.

The central idea is:

> The story uses humour and conversational narration to show how tradition, cleverness, social expectation, and personal affection shape marriage in a village community.

---

## 2. Plot Summary

Ranga, a young man from Hosahalli, returns to his village after studying in Bangalore. Because English education is rare in the village, everyone is curious to see whether he has changed. Ranga remains respectful, but his ideas about marriage have become modern. He says he will not marry immediately and wants a mature girl whom he can admire.

The narrator believes Ranga should marry Ratna, a young and talented girl living with Rama Rao's family. He arranges for Ranga to see Ratna and hear her singing. Ranga is attracted to her, though he tries to hide his feelings.

To push the matter forward, the narrator takes Ranga to Shastri, the astrologer. The narrator has already coached Shastri, so Shastri pretends to read Ranga's condition through astrology and hints that the girl troubling his mind is Ratna. Ranga becomes convinced and later marries Ratna.

Years later, the narrator visits Ranga's home and finds that the couple has a child named after him. This ending shows the narrator's pride in his successful matchmaking.

---

## 3. Hosahalli: The Village Setting

The narrator begins by describing Hosahalli with great affection and exaggeration. His description is humorous because he praises the village as if it were extremely important, while also admitting that outsiders may not even know it.

Hosahalli represents:

- Rural Karnataka life
- Strong community bonds
- Oral storytelling
- Local pride
- Traditional social customs
- A world where everyone's affairs become public interest

The village is not just a background. It shapes the story's humour and social expectations.

## 4. Ranga's Character

| Trait | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Educated | He has studied in Bangalore and knows English |
| Respectful | He still shows respect to elders |
| Modern in thought | He initially rejects immediate marriage |
| Romantic but reserved | He likes Ratna but does not openly admit it |
| Impressionable | He is influenced by the narrator and Shastri's plan |
| Socially rooted | Despite education, he remains part of village customs |

Ranga's return creates excitement because English education gives him unusual status in the village.

---

## 5. Ratna's Character

Ratna is presented as a suitable bride for Ranga.

| Trait | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Young and graceful | She attracts Ranga's attention |
| Talented | She can sing and play musical instruments |
| Culturally refined | Her skills make her admired in the traditional setting |
| Quietly central | She does not speak much, but the plot revolves around her |

Ratna is not developed as deeply as Ranga or the narrator, but she is important because she becomes the emotional focus of Ranga's change.

---

## 6. The Narrator's Role

The narrator is the most active force in the story. He is not a neutral storyteller; he shapes events.

He:

1. Introduces Hosahalli with comic pride.
2. Observes Ranga's changed ideas.
3. Decides that Ratna is suitable for Ranga.
4. Creates an opportunity for Ranga to see and hear Ratna.
5. Notices Ranga's attraction.
6. Coaches Shastri before Ranga meets him.
7. Uses astrology as a tool to influence Ranga.
8. Takes pride in the successful marriage.

### Is the Narrator Reliable?

He is entertaining but not fully objective. He exaggerates, digresses, manipulates events, and directly tries to influence the reader. This makes him humorous and memorable.

---

## 7. Shastri and Astrology

Shastri is the village astrologer. In the story, astrology is used comically.

The narrator secretly tells Shastri what to say. Shastri then pretends to discover Ranga's feelings through astrology.

This shows:

- The social influence of astrologers
- People's faith in traditional signs
- The narrator's clever manipulation
- Humour based on dramatic irony

Dramatic irony appears because the reader knows the trick, while Ranga does not.

---

## 8. Influence of English

English education is important in the story.

Ranga's English education makes him special in the village. It also changes his thinking about marriage. He wants to choose carefully and does not want to marry a very young girl.

The story reflects:

| English influence | Traditional response |
|---|---|
| Education in town | Village curiosity |
| Modern marriage ideas | Elder-led arranged marriage |
| Individual preference | Community involvement |
| New social prestige | Local suspicion and admiration |

The narrator is both amused by and critical of the blind admiration for English.

---

## 9. Arranged Marriage in the Story

The story presents arranged marriage as a social process involving elders, suitability, astrology, family reputation, and community approval.

Modern readers may find the narrator's manipulation questionable. But in the story's social world, he sees himself as helping Ranga.

Important point for answers:

> The story should be read as a humorous portrayal of an older social custom, not as a simple model for modern marriage.

---

## 10. Humour in the Story

Humour comes from:

- The narrator's exaggeration about Hosahalli
- His digressive storytelling
- His direct address to the reader
- The village crowd's curiosity about Ranga
- Ranga's attempt to hide his feelings
- The staged astrology scene
- The narrator's pride in his own cleverness

The humour is gentle, not cruel. It reveals social habits and human weakness.
