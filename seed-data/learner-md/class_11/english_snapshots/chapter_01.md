### How To Use This Guide

Read this chapter as a story about a moral conflict, not merely as an event about a stolen horse.

The surface plot is simple: two boys ride a white horse that does not belong to them, keep it hidden for a short time, and later return it. The deeper story is about reputation, desire, innocence, honesty, poverty, community, trust, and the way children negotiate between impulse and conscience.

For strong answers, always connect events to values:

| Event | Value tested |
|---|---|
| Mourad arrives with the horse | Desire versus honesty |
| Aram agrees to ride | Innocence mixed with self-deception |
| John Byro visits | Personal joy versus another person's loss |
| The boys meet John Byro on the road | Trust as a moral mirror |
| The horse is returned | Conscience and family honor restored |

## Learning Goals

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Retell the story clearly without copying the original text.
2. Explain Aram's inner conflict between longing and honesty.
3. Analyse Mourad as imaginative, impulsive, affectionate, and morally aware.
4. Explain how John Byro's trust influences the boys more strongly than accusation would have done.
5. Describe the importance of the Garoghlanian family's reputation for honesty.
6. Identify humour, irony, nostalgia, and character contrast in the story.
7. Write exam answers that use events as evidence.
8. Distinguish childish mischief from deliberate criminal intention, while still recognizing that the act is morally wrong.

## Clean Chapter Summary

The narrator, Aram, recalls a childhood incident from when he was nine. He belonged to the Garoghlanian family, an Armenian tribe known for its extreme poverty and even stronger reputation for honesty. The family had very little money, but its members valued pride, truth, right conduct, and community respect.

One early summer morning, Aram's cousin Mourad appears at his window on a magnificent white horse. Aram is astonished because the family cannot afford such an animal. Since Mourad could not have bought it, the horse must have been taken from someone else. Aram knows this, but he tries to convince himself that borrowing a horse for the joy of riding may not be the same as stealing.

The two boys ride through the countryside. Mourad handles the horse beautifully and claims to have a natural understanding with animals. Aram, who has always longed to ride, tries to ride the horse alone but cannot control it. The horse runs through a vineyard and throws him off. Mourad recovers the horse, and instead of returning it immediately, hides it in a deserted barn.

Later, Aram learns that the horse belongs to John Byro, an Assyrian farmer. John Byro visits Aram's house and speaks sadly about the loss of his horse. Aram understands that their private enjoyment has caused real hardship to another person.

Aram asks Mourad to keep the horse until he learns to ride. Mourad reacts as if Aram has suggested true theft. This response shows that Mourad has drawn a strange moral line: he has taken the horse temporarily, but he does not think of himself as a thief because he never intends to sell it or keep it permanently.

For two weeks, the boys secretly ride the horse every morning. Then they meet John Byro on the road. He examines the horse closely and recognizes it, but he refuses to accuse the boys because he trusts their family name. He says, in effect, that his eyes tell him one thing, but his heart trusts their honesty.

John Byro's trust has a powerful effect. Early the next morning, the boys return the horse to his barn. Later, John Byro reports that the horse is back and is healthier and better-tempered than before. Uncle Khosrove responds in his usual loud, dismissive manner, telling everyone to stop worrying now that the horse has been returned.

The story ends quietly, without punishment, confession, or public shame. The boys restore the truth because trust and conscience work on them from within.

## Story In Logical Stages

### Stage 1: Aram's Childhood World

The story begins as a memory. Aram looks back at childhood as a time when the world seemed full of wonder. This nostalgic opening matters because it prepares us for a story told through the eyes of a child.

Aram is not describing events like a police report. He is remembering how the world felt to him:

| Childhood feeling | Effect on the story |
|---|---|
| Wonder | The horse appears almost magical |
| Longing | Aram's desire to ride feels intense |
| Innocence | He tries to explain away wrongdoing |
| Confusion | He cannot fully separate adventure from theft |
| Admiration | He sees Mourad as wild, free, and gifted |

### Stage 2: The White Horse Appears

Mourad appears outside Aram's window at dawn, sitting on a beautiful white horse. The time of day is important. Dawn is between night and day, secrecy and openness. The boys are doing something hidden, but the world is beginning to become visible.

The horse is not merely an animal in the plot. It represents freedom, beauty, childhood desire, and temptation.

Aram immediately understands the problem:

| Fact | Meaning |
|---|---|
| His family is poor | Mourad could not have bought the horse |
| The horse is valuable | It must belong to someone else |
| The family is famous for honesty | Theft seems impossible |
| Aram loves horses | Desire makes him bend his reasoning |

This is the first major moral conflict of the story.

### Stage 3: Aram's Self-Deception

Aram does not openly accept that the horse has been stolen. Instead, he creates a childish argument:

1. The boys only want to ride the horse.
2. They do not plan to sell it.
3. They love horses.
4. Therefore, perhaps it is not really stealing.

This reasoning is weak, but it is psychologically believable. Aram is not evil. He is a child who wants something so badly that he temporarily reshapes morality around his desire.

This is one of the most important parts of the chapter. The story shows how people often justify wrongdoing before they admit it to themselves.

### Stage 4: The Ride

The ride is full of delight. Mourad is confident, energetic, and in control. He sings loudly as he rides. Aram experiences the movement of the horse as a dream come true.

However, the ride also exposes the difference between the cousins:

| Mourad | Aram |
|---|---|
| Bold | Hesitant |
| Experienced with the horse | Inexperienced |
| Physically confident | Easily frightened |
| Makes decisions quickly | Thinks and questions |
| Claims a special bond with animals | Wants to learn that bond |

When Aram rides alone, the horse throws him off. This moment is comic, but it also teaches a lesson: desire is not the same as mastery. Aram wants the joy of riding, but he does not yet have the discipline or skill needed for it.

### Stage 5: Hiding The Horse

After the ride, the boys hide the horse in a deserted barn. This makes the wrongdoing more serious. A sudden act of mischief becomes a repeated secret.

The hiding place is symbolically useful:

| Place | Symbolic meaning |
|---|---|
| Deserted vineyard | A place outside normal social rules |
| Barn | Hidden guilt |
| Early morning rides | Secret pleasure |
| Roads and fields | Freedom without full responsibility |

The boys are living in two worlds. At home, they belong to an honest family. In the barn, they maintain a secret that contradicts that reputation.

### Stage 6: John Byro's Loss

John Byro is the owner of the horse. He is not a rich villain or an unpleasant stranger. He is lonely, hardworking, and dependent on the horse for his surrey. His loss is practical and emotional.

This detail changes the moral weight of the story. The boys are not merely enjoying an object. Their enjoyment creates difficulty for another person.

John Byro's visit to Aram's house forces Aram to see the other side of the event. The horse is not just a beautiful creature for riding. It is someone's property and livelihood.

### Stage 7: Uncle Khosrove's Comic Role

Uncle Khosrove responds to almost everything with loud dismissal. He minimizes problems and refuses to let anyone complain. His comic exaggeration adds humour to the story.

But Khosrove is not simply foolish. His behaviour is connected to homesickness, loss, and old grief. When he dismisses the loss of a horse, he compares it with the loss of a homeland. His reaction is unreasonable in the immediate situation, but it has an emotional background.

This makes him a comic character with hidden sadness.

### Stage 8: Mourad And The Injured Bird

Mourad is seen caring for a young bird with an injured wing. This scene is small but very important. It shows that Mourad has tenderness beneath his wild behaviour.

The bird scene helps us understand why Mourad cannot be seen as simply bad. He is impulsive, secretive, and morally confused, but he is also gentle and loving toward living creatures.

The bird also parallels the horse:

| Bird | Horse |
|---|---|
| Injured and needing care | Taken and needing return |
| Mourad handles it tenderly | Mourad claims understanding with it |
| Released into the air | Returned to its rightful place |
| Symbol of freedom | Symbol of freedom and temptation |

### Stage 9: Meeting John Byro On The Road

The road meeting is the moral turning point. John Byro sees the horse and recognizes it. He examines it carefully. He has enough evidence to accuse the boys.

But he does not accuse them.

Instead, he trusts the reputation of their family. This trust is stronger than direct blame. If he had shouted, the boys might have become defensive. By refusing to shame them, he forces them to judge themselves.

This is why the story is powerful despite having little external action. The real action happens inside the conscience.

### Stage 10: Returning The Horse

The boys return the horse quietly the next morning. There is no dramatic confession. No police are involved. No adult forces them to do it. They return it because their internal moral world has been restored.

The ending suggests that the boys have learned something about honesty:

1. Good family reputation is not a decoration.
2. Trust must be deserved through action.
3. Temporary wrongdoing is still wrongdoing.
4. A person can correct a mistake before it destroys character.
5. Conscience may work more deeply than punishment.

## Character Study

### Aram

Aram is the narrator and the moral lens of the story. He is nine years old during the event and older when he narrates it.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Innocent | He sees the world as full of wonder | Creates the nostalgic tone |
| Imaginative | He turns the horse into a dreamlike event | Makes the story emotionally rich |
| Honest at heart | He knows the situation is morally troubling | Shows conscience |
| Easily tempted | He joins Mourad because he longs to ride | Makes him human and believable |
| Reflective | He thinks about poverty, honesty, and family name | Gives depth to the narration |
| Less bold than Mourad | He cannot control the horse | Creates contrast |

Aram is important because he shows how a basically good child can participate in wrongdoing through desire and rationalization.

### Mourad

Mourad is one of the most memorable figures in the story. He is considered eccentric by his community. He is daring, unpredictable, and full of life.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Adventurous | He takes the horse and rides at dawn | Drives the plot |
| Confident | He handles the horse skillfully | Makes Aram admire him |
| Affectionate toward animals | He understands the horse and cares for a bird | Softens his character |
| Humorous | He speaks with bold confidence | Adds liveliness |
| Morally unusual | He does wrong but denies being a thief | Creates the central irony |
| Loyal to family honor | He eventually returns the horse | Shows conscience |

Mourad should not be described as a simple thief. He has taken the horse wrongly, but his intention is not greed. His mistake comes from passion, freedom, and childish entitlement. Still, the story does not excuse the act; it shows how he is brought back to moral responsibility.

### John Byro

John Byro is the owner of the horse. He is an Assyrian farmer who has learned Armenian because he is lonely among Armenian neighbours.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Practical | He needs the horse for his surrey | Makes the loss real |
| Patient | He does not explode in anger | Shows maturity |
| Observant | He recognizes the horse | Builds tension |
| Trusting | He refuses to accuse the boys openly | Creates the turning point |
| Wise | He lets conscience work | Resolves the conflict indirectly |

John Byro's trust saves the boys from public shame while also making them feel morally accountable. He is one of the quiet moral centers of the story.

### Uncle Khosrove

Uncle Khosrove is loud, impatient, and comic. He dismisses problems in a dramatic way. Yet he is also homesick, wounded, and emotionally intense.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Short-tempered | He interrupts and roars | Adds humour |
| Dismissive | He minimizes serious problems | Creates comic contrast |
| Homesick | His anger is linked to lost homeland | Adds depth |
| Emotionally large | His presence fills scenes | Makes the family vivid |

Khosrove reflects the older generation's history of displacement. The young boys worry about a horse; Khosrove thinks in terms of homeland and permanent loss.

### Aram's Mother

Aram's mother appears briefly but helps us understand Khosrove. She explains that beneath his roughness, he has a gentle heart. Her comment prevents readers from dismissing him as merely rude.

### Zorab

Zorab, Mourad's father, is described as practical. The contrast between practical Zorab and wild Mourad allows the story to make a humorous point: a child may inherit a temperament from the wider family spirit rather than from the father alone.

## Major Themes

### 1. Honesty And Reputation

The Garoghlanian family is poor, but it is rich in reputation. Its honesty is its social wealth.

The story asks an important question: What does honesty mean if no one is watching?

At first, the boys depend on the family's reputation while secretly betraying it. By the end, they act in a way that deserves that reputation.

Strong answer point:

The chapter shows that inherited honor has value only when individuals renew it through their own choices.

### 2. Poverty And Pride

The family has very little money, but it possesses pride. Poverty does not make them dishonest. In fact, their honesty is more impressive because temptation exists.

The white horse is tempting partly because it belongs to a world beyond their economic reach.

The chapter avoids a shallow view of poverty. It does not suggest that poor people are naturally dishonest. It shows the opposite: moral dignity can exist without wealth.

### 3. Childhood Desire

Aram's longing to ride is intense. He has dreamed of horses since early childhood. This longing makes the horse seem irresistible.

Childhood desire in the story is:

1. Beautiful because it is full of wonder.
2. Dangerous because it can ignore consequences.
3. Temporary because maturity brings moral recognition.

### 4. Conscience

No one punishes the boys. No one publicly exposes them. Yet they return the horse.

This makes conscience the main force of the story.

Conscience appears through:

| Moment | Effect |
|---|---|
| Aram's doubt at the beginning | He knows something is wrong |
| John Byro's complaint | The loss becomes real |
| John Byro's trust | The boys feel morally seen |
| The return of the horse | Conscience becomes action |

### 5. Trust

Trust is more powerful than accusation in the story. John Byro's trust gives the boys a chance to restore themselves.

This does not mean all wrongdoing should be ignored. It means that in this particular story, the boys still have a living conscience, and John Byro's trust awakens it.

### 6. Freedom And Responsibility

The ride gives the boys freedom. The stolen horse gives them speed, beauty, and excitement. But freedom without responsibility becomes selfish.

The boys mature when they understand that joy cannot be built on another person's loss.

### 7. Human Complexity

The story avoids simple labels.

| Character | Simple label | Deeper truth |
|---|---|---|
| Mourad | Thief | A wild child with conscience and tenderness |
| Aram | Innocent boy | Innocent but capable of self-deception |
| Khosrove | Angry man | Comic, wounded, homesick, and gentle inside |
| John Byro | Victim | Patient, observant, and morally wise |

## Title Analysis

The title combines season, beauty, and animal imagery.

| Word or phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Summer | Childhood freedom, warmth, openness, long days |
| Beautiful | The horse's physical grace and emotional appeal |
| White | Purity, innocence, moral contrast, visibility |
| Horse | Freedom, desire, movement, temptation |

The title does not say "The Stolen Horse". That is important. The story is not framed as a crime story. It is framed as a memory of beauty, temptation, and moral growth.

## Symbolism

### The White Horse

The horse symbolizes:

1. Freedom.
2. Childhood longing.
3. Beauty beyond poverty.
4. Temptation.
5. Moral testing.
6. Energy that must be guided.

The horse is also a living being, not an object. Mourad's bond with it reveals his tenderness, while the act of taking it reveals his irresponsibility.

### Dawn

Dawn symbolizes an in-between state. The boys are between innocence and guilt, darkness and light, secrecy and exposure. Their actions begin in concealment but move toward moral clarity.

### The Deserted Barn

The barn is the place where the boys hide the horse. It represents hidden guilt and secret pleasure. It is outside the ordinary structure of family and community.

### The Road

The road is where the boys meet John Byro. Symbolically, the road brings private action into public view. The hidden act can no longer remain hidden.

### The Bird

The injured bird shows Mourad's tenderness. Its release into the air mirrors the final restoration of freedom and right order.

## Important Contrasts

### Aram And Mourad

| Aram | Mourad |
|---|---|
| Narrator | Doer |
| Reflective | Impulsive |
| Nervous | Bold |
| Wants to ride | Already rides well |
| Questions morality | Redefines morality |
| Learns through observation | Learns through confrontation with trust |

### Poverty And Honor

The family lacks money but possesses moral wealth. This contrast is central to the story.

### Desire And Duty

The boys desire the horse, but duty requires them to return it.

### Accusation And Trust

John Byro could accuse the boys, but he trusts them. This trust creates a deeper moral pressure.

### Comedy And Seriousness

The story is funny in places, especially through Khosrove and the boys' behaviour. But underneath the humour lies a serious question about honesty.

## Tone And Narrative Style

### Nostalgic Tone

The story is told as a memory. The narrator looks back on childhood with warmth and wonder. This tone makes the incident feel less like a legal case and more like a moral awakening.

### Humorous Tone

Humour appears in:

1. Khosrove's loud dismissals.
2. Aram's attempt to justify the horse-riding.
3. Mourad's exaggerated confidence.
4. The horse throwing Aram off.

### Gentle Irony

Irony occurs because the family is famous for honesty, yet the boys are hiding a horse. The tension between reputation and action drives the story.

### First-Person Narration

The story is told by Aram. This creates intimacy. We see not only what happens but also how a child thinks through it.

Advantages of first-person narration:

| Advantage | Effect |
|---|---|
| Personal voice | Makes the memory vivid |
| Inner conflict | Shows self-deception clearly |
| Limited understanding | Keeps the child's perspective alive |
| Mature recollection | Adds gentle wisdom |

## Language Notes

### Simple But Suggestive Language

Saroyan's language often appears simple, but it carries emotional meaning. The story does not use heavy philosophical language. Instead, it lets ordinary scenes reveal moral depth.

### Repetition

Ideas such as honesty, poverty, and having a way with animals recur throughout the story. Repetition helps build character and theme.

### Comic Exaggeration

Khosrove's reactions are exaggerated for comic effect. His style makes him unforgettable and gives rhythm to the story.

### Childlike Reasoning

Aram's thoughts show a child's ability to bend logic when desire is strong. This creates humour and psychological realism.

## Moral Questions Raised By The Story

### Was Taking The Horse Wrong?

Yes. The horse belonged to John Byro, and he suffered because it was missing. The boys had no right to use it without permission.

### Were The Boys Hardened Criminals?

No. Their action was wrong, but their intention was not greed. They did not plan to sell the horse or harm the owner. They were driven by childish longing.

### Does Good Intention Erase Wrongdoing?

No. The story shows that even a temporary wrong remains wrong when it harms someone else.

### Why Does John Byro Not Accuse Them?

He trusts their family reputation and perhaps gives them a chance to correct themselves. His restraint becomes a moral test.

### Why Do The Boys Return The Horse?

They return it because John Byro's trust awakens their conscience. They understand that keeping the horse would destroy the very honor their family is known for.

## Model Answers

### 1. What makes the story interesting even though it has little outward action?

The story is interesting because its real drama is internal rather than external. It does not depend on chase scenes, danger, or suspenseful action. Instead, it explores how two children respond when desire conflicts with honesty. Aram and Mourad love the horse and enjoy riding it, but they also belong to a family famous for truthfulness. This creates a moral tension that keeps the reader involved.

The characters also make the story lively. Mourad is bold and unpredictable, Aram is innocent and reflective, John Byro is trusting and dignified, and Uncle Khosrove adds humour through his loud dismissals. The story becomes memorable because it combines childhood wonder, comedy, temptation, and conscience. Its quiet ending is powerful because the boys return the horse not through punishment, but through an awakened sense of honor.

### 2. Did the boys return the horse because they were conscience-stricken or because they were afraid?

The boys returned the horse mainly because they were conscience-stricken. They may have felt some fear after John Byro recognized the horse, but he did not accuse them or threaten them. Instead, he trusted their family name. This trust affected them deeply because the Garoghlanian family was known for honesty.

If John Byro had insulted or exposed them, the boys might have reacted defensively. His quiet faith in their character made them see their act more clearly. They realized that keeping the horse would make them unworthy of the reputation they had inherited. Therefore, the return of the horse is best understood as an act of conscience and restored family honor.

### 3. How does Aram justify riding the horse?

Aram justifies riding the horse by convincing himself that taking a horse for a ride is not the same as stealing it permanently. He believes that since he and Mourad love horses and have no intention of selling the horse, their act may not be real theft. This is childish reasoning, shaped by desire.

However, the reader can see that Aram's argument is weak. The horse belongs to John Byro, and using it without permission causes him trouble. Aram's self-justification shows how desire can temporarily distort moral judgement, especially in childhood.

### 4. Explain the importance of the Garoghlanian family's reputation.

The Garoghlanian family's reputation for honesty is central to the story. The family is poor, but it possesses moral pride. Its members are known in the community as people who would not take advantage of others. This reputation is so strong that Aram initially cannot believe Mourad has stolen the horse.

The reputation also shapes John Byro's response. When he sees the horse with the boys, he recognizes it but does not accuse them because he trusts their family. This trust becomes the turning point of the story. The boys return the horse because they understand that their actions must match the honor attached to their family name.

### 5. Write a character sketch of Mourad.

Mourad is Aram's cousin and one of the liveliest characters in the story. He is adventurous, fearless, and full of energy. He arrives at Aram's window at dawn with a white horse and invites him to ride. His boldness makes him fascinating to Aram.

Mourad also has a deep connection with animals. He handles the horse skillfully and cares tenderly for an injured bird. This shows that he is not cruel, even though he has acted wrongly by taking the horse. His morality is unusual: he does not see himself as a thief because he does not intend to keep or sell the horse. In the end, he returns it, proving that his conscience is still alive. Mourad is therefore flawed, impulsive, affectionate, and morally recoverable.

### 6. What role does John Byro play in the story?

John Byro plays the role of the wronged owner and the moral awakener. His horse has been taken, and this causes him real difficulty because he needs it for his surrey. Yet he does not behave with cruelty or anger toward the boys.

When he meets Aram and Mourad with the horse, he recognizes it but chooses not to accuse them directly. He trusts their family reputation for honesty. This trust affects the boys more deeply than a direct accusation would have done. Through John Byro, the story shows that moral faith can sometimes guide people back to right conduct.

### 7. Why is Uncle Khosrove important in the story?

Uncle Khosrove is important because he adds humour and emotional background. He is loud, impatient, and dismissive, often treating serious matters as if they are unimportant. His behaviour creates comic relief, especially when John Byro speaks about his lost horse.

At the same time, Khosrove is not merely a comic figure. His anger is connected to homesickness and the pain of losing a homeland. Through him, the story hints at the history and emotional life of the Armenian community. He also makes the family world more vivid and memorable.

### 8. What does the horse symbolize?

The white horse symbolizes freedom, beauty, temptation, and moral testing. For Aram, it represents the fulfilment of a childhood dream. For Mourad, it becomes an expression of wild energy and his claimed bond with animals.

At the same time, the horse is a test of honesty. Because it belongs to John Byro, the boys cannot enjoy it innocently. The horse therefore represents the conflict between desire and duty. When the boys return it, they restore moral balance.

## Short Answer Bank

### Why was Aram surprised to see Mourad with a horse?

Aram was surprised because his family was poor and could not afford such a horse. He also knew that the family was famous for honesty, so the idea that Mourad might have taken it from someone else seemed impossible and disturbing.

### Why did Aram join Mourad despite suspecting the truth?

Aram joined Mourad because he had always longed to ride a horse. His desire was so strong that he temporarily convinced himself that riding a borrowed horse was not the same as stealing it.

### What does Mourad mean when he says he has a way with animals?

He means that he believes he can understand and control animals through instinct, affection, and confidence. The story supports this partly through his handling of the horse and his care for the injured bird.

### Why does John Byro's trust matter?

His trust matters because it makes the boys feel morally responsible. He refuses to accuse them directly because he respects their family name. That trust pushes them to return the horse.

### What lesson does Aram learn?

Aram learns that desire cannot justify wrongdoing. He also learns that family honor must be protected through actual conduct, not merely inherited reputation.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

1. Do not call Mourad simply a thief without explaining his complexity.
2. Do not say the boys returned the horse only because they were afraid.
3. Do not ignore John Byro's practical loss.
4. Do not treat Uncle Khosrove as only comic; he also reflects homesickness and grief.
5. Do not copy long portions of the story in answers.
6. Do not confuse Aram, the narrator, with Mourad, the main doer.
7. Do not say poverty caused theft; the family is poor but deeply honorable.
8. Do not miss the difference between childish intention and moral responsibility.

## Important Lines Of Thought For Exams

### The Story As A Moral Memory

The story is not just about what happened. It is about how an adult narrator remembers a childhood error. This gives the story tenderness. The narrator does not condemn the boys harshly, but he does not excuse them either.

### The Power Of Being Trusted

John Byro's trust is central. It is easier to rebel against accusation than against trust. When someone expects you to be honorable, failing that expectation becomes painful.

### Family Honor As A Living Duty

The Garoghlanian name protects the boys from suspicion, but it also demands honesty from them. The story shows that reputation must be earned again in each generation.

### Humour With Serious Purpose

The humour does not weaken the moral issue. Instead, it makes the story more human. Real moral growth often happens inside ordinary, funny, confusing life.

John Byro's strength lies in restraint. He has lost his horse and has reason to be angry, yet he does not publicly accuse Aram and Mourad when he sees them with it. ### Model Long Answer

The story shows that conscience can be stronger than punishment through the way Aram and Mourad return John Byro's horse. The boys take the horse because they love riding and are carried away by childhood desire. Their act is wrong, but they are not hardened criminals. Aram's thoughts show that he is already uneasy because his family is known for honesty.
