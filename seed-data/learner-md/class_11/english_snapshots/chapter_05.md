# Chapter 5: Mother's Day
### NCERT Class 11 English Snapshots

---

## Chapter Purpose

"Mother's Day" is a humorous one-act play about a mother who is taken for granted by her family. Through a comic body-swap device, the play exposes how unpaid domestic work is ignored and how family members often expect the mother to serve them without respect.

The central idea is:

> A family must respect the mother as an individual, not treat her as a servant available for everyone's convenience.

---

## 2. Plot Summary

Mrs Pearson is a kind but weak-willed mother. Her husband George and children Doris and Cyril take her for granted. They expect her to cook, serve, arrange things, and support their plans without caring about her own needs.

Mrs Fitzgerald, her stronger neighbour, advises her to take a firm stand. Since Mrs Pearson cannot do this herself, Mrs Fitzgerald uses a supernatural trick to exchange personalities with her. Mrs Fitzgerald's strong personality enters Mrs Pearson's body.

When Doris, Cyril, and George return, they are shocked. The usually gentle Mrs Pearson now refuses to obey their demands. She criticises their selfishness, refuses to prepare everything for them, and forces them to recognise her value.

Eventually, the personalities are restored. Mrs Pearson gains confidence, and the family becomes more willing to spend time together and treat her better.

---

## 3. Main Conflict

The conflict is between:

| Side | What it represents |
|---|---|
| Mrs Pearson's family | Selfish expectations, entitlement, lack of gratitude |
| Mrs Pearson's need | Respect, rest, equality, recognition |
| Mrs Fitzgerald | Courage, firmness, practical reform |

The play shows that love without respect becomes exploitation.

---

## 4. Mrs Pearson

| Trait | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Loving | She cares deeply for her family |
| Overworked | She performs household work without appreciation |
| Timid | She finds it difficult to confront her family |
| Emotionally neglected | Her family uses her but does not value her |
| Capable of change | By the end, she begins to assert herself |

Mrs Pearson's weakness is not lack of intelligence. It is her habit of self-sacrifice and fear of upsetting the family.

---

## 5. Mrs Fitzgerald

Mrs Fitzgerald is bold, direct, and practical. She understands Mrs Pearson's problem clearly.

| Trait | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Strong-willed | She believes in taking firm action |
| Sharp | She reads people accurately |
| Helpful | She wants Mrs Pearson to gain respect |
| Unconventional | She uses a magical personality exchange |
| Reform-minded | She wants the family structure to change |

Mrs Fitzgerald says what Mrs Pearson is unable to say.

---

## 6. Doris, Cyril, and George

### Doris

Doris is Mrs Pearson's daughter. She is self-centred and expects her mother to help her with clothes, plans, and social life.

### Cyril

Cyril is Mrs Pearson's son. He expects food and attention but gives little respect in return.

### George

George is Mrs Pearson's husband. He takes his wife for granted and values his club life. He is shocked when Mrs Pearson refuses to behave submissively.

Together, the three represent a family that has become comfortable with the mother's silent labour.

---

## 7. Body-Swap Device

The body-swap is not meant to be realistic. It is a dramatic device used for comedy and social reform.

It allows:

- Mrs Fitzgerald to confront the family directly.
- The family to hear hard truths from "Mrs Pearson".
- Mrs Pearson to see that firmness is possible.
- The audience to laugh while understanding a serious issue.

The device turns a domestic problem into effective theatre.

---

## 8. Humour and Satire

The play is funny because the family expects the old submissive Mrs Pearson but meets a bold and sarcastic version of her.

Humour comes from:

- Shocked reactions of Doris, Cyril, and George
- Mrs Pearson's unexpected sharp replies
- Role reversal
- Dramatic irony
- The contrast between Mrs Pearson and Mrs Fitzgerald

Satire comes from exposing selfish family behaviour.

---

## 9. Social Message

The play raises issues that remain relevant:

- Domestic work is often undervalued.
- Mothers are expected to sacrifice without complaint.
- Family members must share responsibility.
- Respect is as important as affection.
- Women should not be reduced to unpaid service providers.

The play's reform is comic, but its message is serious.

---

## 10. Resolution

The play resolves the conflict by making the family realise that Mrs Pearson cannot be treated carelessly. The ending suggests a more balanced family life, where the mother is respected and included.

The resolution is gentle, not violent. The aim is correction, not revenge.

## 13. Answer Frames

### Frame 1: Central Message

1. State that the play is about respect for mothers.
2. Explain how Mrs Pearson is taken for granted.
3. Mention Mrs Fitzgerald's intervention.
4. Show how the family is forced to recognise the problem.
5. Conclude with the need for shared responsibility.

### Frame 2: Mrs Fitzgerald's Role

1. Introduce her as Mrs Pearson's neighbour.
2. Explain her strong personality.
3. Describe the body-swap plan.
4. Show how she teaches the family a lesson.
5. Conclude that she acts as a reforming force.

### Frame 3: Humour in the Play

1. Explain the comic situation.
2. Mention role reversal.
3. Add examples of shocked family reactions.
4. Explain that humour carries a serious message.

## Final Takeaway

"Mother's Day" uses comedy to make a serious point: a mother is not a machine for household service. A healthy family requires respect, shared responsibility, and recognition of the mother's individuality.
