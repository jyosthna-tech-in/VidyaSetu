# Chapter 8: The Tale of Melon City
### NCERT Class 11 English Snapshots

---

## Chapter Purpose

"The Tale of Melon City" by Vikram Seth is a satirical poem about foolish rulers, weak ministers, blind justice, and passive citizens. It uses humour and absurdity to criticise systems where decisions are made without wisdom, responsibility, or moral seriousness.

The central idea is:

> A state without intelligent leadership and responsible citizens can become so absurd that even a melon may be accepted as king.

## 2. Plot Summary

The king orders an arch to be built across a major road. When he rides under it, the arch knocks off his crown. The king becomes angry and demands punishment.

Instead of investigating sensibly, responsibility is passed from one group to another:

1. The chief builder is blamed.
2. The workmen blame the bricks.
3. The masons blame the architect.
4. The architect reminds the king that the king himself changed the plans.
5. The king becomes confused and calls for the wisest man.
6. The old wise man absurdly suggests that the arch itself should be punished.
7. The crowd becomes restless and wants a hanging.
8. The king orders that someone must be hanged.
9. The noose fits only the king.
10. The king is hanged by his own order.

After the king's death, the ministers need a new ruler. Following an old custom, they ask the next passer-by to choose the ruler. The passer-by says "melon", so a melon is crowned king. The people accept this because they feel they are left in peace and liberty.

---

## 3. The King: "Just and Placid"

The poem repeatedly calls the king just and placid, but the phrase is ironic.

| Word | Surface meaning | Actual effect in the poem |
|---|---|---|
| Just | Fair and lawful | He makes foolish and shifting decisions |
| Placid | Calm and peaceful | He is passive, confused, and weak |

The king is not truly wise or just. He simply wants to appear fair while avoiding real responsibility.

---

## 4. Satire in the Poem

Satire uses humour, irony, and exaggeration to criticise human foolishness or social systems.

This poem satirises:

- Foolish rulers
- Mechanical justice
- Ministers who care more about order than wisdom
- Blind obedience to custom
- Citizens who accept any ruler if they are not disturbed
- A state where appearance matters more than reason

The melon king is the strongest symbol of absurd governance.

---

## 5. Absurdity in the Poem

The poem is funny because events become increasingly unreasonable.

Examples of absurdity:

| Event | Why it is absurd |
|---|---|
| The arch is treated like a culprit | A structure cannot be morally guilty |
| Blame keeps shifting | Nobody accepts responsibility |
| The crowd demands any hanging | Justice becomes entertainment |
| The king is hanged by his own decree | Law becomes mindless and self-destructive |
| A melon is crowned king | Leadership becomes meaningless |

Absurdity reveals the failure of thought in the kingdom.

---

## 6. Ministers and Administration

The ministers are practical but not wise. Their main concern is to avoid public disorder. They are relieved when someone is found to hang, even if that person is the king.

After the king dies, they follow custom mechanically and crown a melon because a passer-by says so.

This shows:

- Lack of independent judgement
- Fear of public unrest
- Blind obedience to procedure
- Administrative emptiness

---

## 7. Citizens and "Peace and Liberty"

The people accept the melon king because they are left undisturbed. Their attitude is passive.

This raises an important question:

> Is a state truly good if citizens have peace but no meaningful leadership?

The poem suggests that people may tolerate absurd rulers if their daily comfort is not affected. This is a criticism of political indifference.

---

## 8. Laissez-Faire

The poem refers to the principle of laissez-faire, meaning minimal interference by authority.

In the poem, it is used ironically. The people are satisfied because the melon does nothing and leaves them alone.

This is not presented as ideal governance. It is a comic exaggeration of passive rule.

---

## 9. Verse-Narrative Style

The poem tells a story in verse form.

Features:

- Simple rhyming lines
- Quick movement of events
- Comic rhythm
- Repetition
- Mock-serious tone
- Fairy-tale style opening and ending

The verse form makes the absurd story memorable and playful.

## 11. Answer Frames

### Frame 1: Satire in the Poem

1. Define satire briefly.
2. Mention the foolish king and blame game.
3. Explain the absurd hanging decision.
4. Add the crowning of the melon.
5. Conclude that the poem mocks weak governance and passive citizens.

### Frame 2: "Just and Placid"

1. State that the phrase is ironic.
2. Explain the king's changing orders.
3. Mention his inability to judge properly.
4. Show how his own decree leads to his death.
5. Conclude that he is placid but not wise.

### Frame 3: Melon as King

1. Explain how the melon is chosen.
2. Mention blind custom and foolish administration.
3. Explain people's acceptance.
4. Connect it with political passivity.
5. Conclude that the melon symbolises meaningless authority.

## Final Takeaway

"The Tale of Melon City" is funny because it is absurd, but its criticism is serious. It warns that when rulers lack wisdom, ministers lack courage, and citizens lack concern, government can become empty ritual.
