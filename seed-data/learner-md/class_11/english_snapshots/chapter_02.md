### How To Read This Chapter

This story is quiet but emotionally heavy. It is not built around external action. Its power comes from the narrator's encounter with objects from her lost home and her decision not to reclaim them.

The most important point is this:

The narrator does not reject the objects because they are worthless. She rejects them because they are separated from the life, people, rooms, and emotional world that once gave them meaning.

In exam answers, avoid retelling only the door visit. Connect every event to one of these ideas:

| Event | Deeper meaning |
|---|---|
| The narrator visits Marconi Street | Return to a painful past |
| Mrs Dorling refuses to welcome her | Guilt, selfishness, and moral discomfort |
| The narrator remembers her mother | Love mixed with loss |
| Objects appear in a strange room | Memory becomes painful instead of comforting |
| The narrator leaves | Choosing emotional survival over material recovery |

## Clean Chapter Summary

The narrator visits a house at Number 46, Marconi Street. She is looking for Mrs Dorling, a woman who had taken many of her mother's household possessions during the war, supposedly to keep them safe. The narrator introduces herself as Mrs S's daughter, but Mrs Dorling pretends not to recognize her at first. The narrator then notices that Mrs Dorling is wearing her mother's green cardigan, which confirms that she has come to the right place.

Mrs Dorling behaves coldly. She refuses to let the narrator in properly and claims that the visit is inconvenient. The narrator leaves and remembers how her mother had once explained Mrs Dorling's visits. During the war, Mrs Dorling had repeatedly carried away silver, plates, vases, and other household items from the narrator's home. The mother believed that Mrs Dorling was protecting these things from loss. The narrator, even then, was not fully convinced.

After the war, the narrator had not immediately wanted to recover the stored objects. She was afraid of seeing things connected to a life that had been destroyed. But as daily life slowly became more normal, curiosity returned. She wanted to see, touch, and remember the possessions of her former home.

On her second visit to Mrs Dorling's house, Mrs Dorling is not at home. Her young daughter lets the narrator enter. Inside, the narrator sees her mother's belongings arranged in Mrs Dorling's living room. The objects are familiar, but their new surroundings make them feel strange and oppressive. The tablecloth, silver, plates, and other things no longer belong to the world of the narrator's home. They have been absorbed into another household.

The narrator realizes that objects lose their emotional value when they are separated from the people, rooms, habits, and memories that once gave them meaning. She also recognizes that she has no real place for them in her present life. She leaves before Mrs Dorling returns and decides never to go back. She chooses to forget the address.

## Story In Logical Stages

### Stage 1: Arrival At Marconi Street

The narrator arrives at Number 46, Marconi Street. This address was given to her years earlier by her mother. The address is not just a physical location. It is a link to her mother, her lost home, and a painful past.

The first meeting creates tension:

| Detail | Meaning |
|---|---|
| The door opens only slightly | Mrs Dorling is defensive |
| Mrs Dorling denies recognition | She wants to avoid responsibility |
| The narrator identifies herself | The past returns |
| The green cardigan appears | The truth becomes visible |
| The door closes | The past is shut out |

The cardigan is important because it proves that Mrs Dorling has kept the mother's belongings. She cannot fully hide the truth because she is literally wearing part of the narrator's past.

### Stage 2: Mrs Dorling's Refusal

Mrs Dorling refuses to speak properly with the narrator. She says the time is inconvenient and closes the door carefully. Her behaviour suggests guilt. She does not behave like someone who has merely stored items for safekeeping.

Her refusal also shows how war can expose selfishness. She had taken things from a vulnerable family, and now that the rightful heir has returned, she does not want to return them.

### Stage 3: The Walk Back And Memory

As the narrator walks back, she remembers her mother explaining Mrs Dorling's role during the war. Mrs Dorling had presented herself as helpful. She claimed she was saving valuable household items in case the family had to leave.

The mother trusted her. The narrator was more doubtful.

This memory matters because it shows the difference between:

| The mother's view | The narrator's suspicion |
|---|---|
| Mrs Dorling is helping | Mrs Dorling may be exploiting the situation |
| Keeping things safe is kind | Carrying them away may be opportunistic |
| Agreement is unnecessary because trust exists | Lack of formal agreement is dangerous |

### Stage 4: After The War

After the war, the narrator does not immediately seek the possessions. She is afraid of them. This fear is important.

The objects survived, but the world they belonged to did not. Seeing them would not simply bring comfort. It could reopen grief.

The narrator slowly returns to ordinary life. Bread improves, a bed becomes safe, and daily routine becomes possible again. Only then does curiosity awaken. She wants to see what remains.

### Stage 5: Second Visit

On the second visit, Mrs Dorling's daughter opens the door. She is innocent in a way her mother is not. She does not fully understand the moral history of the objects in the house.

The girl lets the narrator inside. This creates the emotional center of the story: the narrator enters a room filled with familiar things arranged in an unfamiliar way.

### Stage 6: A Room Both Known And Unknown

The narrator sees her mother's possessions, but they no longer feel like home. The objects are physically the same, yet emotionally changed.

Why?

1. They are in the wrong house.
2. They are used without memory or reverence.
3. They are mixed with ugly or unsuitable surroundings.
4. They are separated from the narrator's mother.
5. They remind the narrator of a life that cannot be restored.

This is the central insight of the story: objects do not carry meaning by themselves. Meaning comes from human connection.

### Stage 7: Tea With Mrs Dorling's Daughter

The girl casually offers tea and speaks about the possessions as household things. She does not understand their emotional weight. To her, the items are normal objects in her home.

This creates painful irony. The narrator recognizes the tablecloth, silver, plates, and other items as parts of her lost family life. The girl sees them as ordinary property.

The narrator's voice begins to sound strange to herself because she is speaking in a place where her memories are present but not respected.

### Stage 8: The Decision To Leave

When the girl moves toward the drawer containing cutlery, the narrator suddenly leaves. She does not want to continue. The sound of the silver becomes unbearable because it brings memory too close.

At the corner of the road, she confirms that the address is correct. But correctness no longer matters. She has found the place, but not what she truly wanted.

She decides to forget the address.

### Stage 9: Final Realization

The narrator understands that reclaiming the possessions will not restore the past. The objects belonged to a former life. Without her mother and without the old home, they are only things.

Her decision is not weakness. It is an act of self-preservation. She chooses not to remain trapped in a material reminder of loss.

## Character Study

### The Narrator

The narrator is the daughter of Mrs S. She has survived the war and returns to look for her mother's possessions.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Sensitive | Objects deeply affect her | Shows emotional depth |
| Observant | She notices the cardigan and room details | Builds realism |
| Wounded | She avoids familiar streets and memories | Shows trauma |
| Thoughtful | She reflects on why objects lose meaning | Gives the story philosophical weight |
| Dignified | She does not create a public scene | Shows inner strength |
| Self-protective | She chooses to forget the address | Shows maturity |

The narrator's journey is not from ignorance to possession. It is from curiosity to renunciation. She learns that reclaiming objects cannot heal the loss of people and home.

### Mrs S

Mrs S is the narrator's mother. She appears through memory. She trusted Mrs Dorling and allowed her to take household items for safekeeping.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Trusting | She accepts Mrs Dorling's offer | Makes the loss possible |
| Gentle | She worries about Mrs Dorling carrying heavy items | Shows kindness |
| Attached to home | Her possessions are carefully remembered | Deepens the narrator's grief |
| Vulnerable | War places her household at risk | Shows human helplessness in crisis |

Mrs S represents the lost world of family, home, and pre-war security.

### Mrs Dorling

Mrs Dorling is an old acquaintance of Mrs S. She repeatedly takes household belongings during the war, supposedly to save them. After the war, she avoids returning them.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Opportunistic | She takes many valuables during war | Reveals moral failure |
| Guilty | She avoids recognition | Shows she knows the truth |
| Possessive | She keeps and uses the objects | Shows selfishness |
| Emotionally cold | She refuses the narrator entry | Contrasts with Mrs S's trust |
| Defensive | She keeps the door nearly closed | Reveals fear of exposure |

Mrs Dorling is not shown through long explanation. Her behaviour reveals her character. The door, the cardigan, and her refusal all speak for her.

### Mrs Dorling's Daughter

Mrs Dorling's daughter is about fifteen. She lets the narrator in and speaks casually about the objects.

Key traits:

| Trait | Evidence from the story | Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Innocent | She does not seem to know the full history | Creates contrast with her mother |
| Casual | She treats the objects as ordinary | Intensifies the narrator's pain |
| Unaware | She does not understand the narrator's reaction | Shows how memory can be private |
| Hospitable | She offers tea | Makes the scene more complex |

The daughter is not the villain. She has inherited the objects without understanding their moral history.

## Major Themes

### 1. War And Human Loss

The story shows war through domestic aftermath rather than battlefield violence. We do not see bombs or soldiers. Instead, we see missing people, displaced objects, broken homes, and emotional survival.

War damages:

1. Families.
2. Houses.
3. Trust.
4. Memory.
5. The meaning of ordinary things.
6. The ability to return to the past.

The story's quietness makes the damage feel more personal.

### 2. Memory And Objects

Household objects are central to the story. Tablecloths, silver, plates, vases, cardigans, and candle-holders appear as memory carriers.

But the story complicates this idea. Objects can carry memory only when they remain connected to the right emotional world. Once they are placed in a strange house and used without the original memories, they become painful and almost meaningless.

### 3. The Limits Of Recovery

The narrator can find the address. She can see the possessions. She may even have a moral claim to them. But she cannot recover her mother, her childhood rooms, or the lost security of home.

The story therefore distinguishes between:

| Material recovery | Emotional recovery |
|---|---|
| Getting objects back | Healing grief |
| Locating an address | Returning to a former life |
| Touching old things | Restoring lost relationships |
| Proving ownership | Finding peace |

The narrator realizes that material recovery is not enough.

### 4. Betrayal And Opportunism

Mrs Dorling uses the war as an opportunity. She takes advantage of Mrs S's fear and trust. The story does not describe her inner thoughts, but her later behaviour suggests that she never intended to return the possessions willingly.

Betrayal is especially painful because it comes disguised as help.

### 5. Survival And Moving On

The narrator's decision to forget the address is a survival choice. She does not deny the past. She refuses to be imprisoned by objects that now hurt more than they comfort.

Moving on does not mean forgetting the mother. It means refusing to confuse the mother with the objects.

### 6. The Strangeness Of Familiar Things

The narrator enters a room where familiar objects are arranged wrongly. This creates emotional shock. The known becomes unknown.

This feeling is common after loss: something physically present can feel spiritually absent.

### 7. Human Predicament After War

The chapter presents human predicament through the narrator's situation. She has survived, but survival comes with difficult questions:

1. What should one do with the belongings of the dead or lost?
2. Can old possessions restore old life?
3. How does one face people who profited from suffering?
4. Is remembering always healing?
5. When is forgetting necessary?

## Title Analysis

The title is short and powerful: "The Address".

An address usually helps a person find a place. In this story, the address helps the narrator find Mrs Dorling's house, but it does not help her recover home.

The title works on several levels:

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Literal | Number 46, Marconi Street |
| Emotional | A connection to the narrator's mother |
| Moral | A place where betrayal is hidden |
| Psychological | A memory the narrator chooses to erase |
| Ironic | The correct address leads to emotional refusal, not recovery |

By the end, the address becomes something to forget. This reversal gives the title its depth.

## Symbolism

### The Address

The address symbolizes the narrator's link to the past. It is precise, but emotionally useless. The narrator can locate the house but cannot locate the lost life.

### The Green Cardigan

The cardigan symbolizes proof and violation. It proves that Mrs Dorling has the mother's belongings. It is also painful because something intimate has been absorbed into another woman's life.

### The Tablecloth

The tablecloth symbolizes domestic memory. The narrator remembers its pattern and even a burn mark. Such small details show how deeply home lives in memory.

### The Silver Cutlery

The silver represents family continuity, daily life, and value. When the narrator hears the cutlery, the sound becomes emotionally unbearable because it connects ordinary domestic use with loss.

### The Door

The half-open door symbolizes exclusion. Mrs Dorling allows only a limited encounter. She wants to keep the narrator outside both the house and the truth.

### The Strange Room

The room symbolizes the distortion of memory. The objects are familiar, but their arrangement is alien. This creates the narrator's realization that the past cannot be rebuilt from things alone.

### Possession And Belonging

The story makes a clear distinction between owning something and belonging to something.

Mrs Dorling physically possesses the objects, but they do not morally belong to her. At the same time, when the narrator sees them in a strange room, she feels that they no longer belong to her life either.

This is one of the story's most painful insights.

## Tone And Narrative Style

### Restrained Tone

The narrator does not shout, accuse, or become melodramatic. Her restrained voice makes the pain stronger. The quietness suggests deep trauma.

### First-Person Narration

The story is told from the narrator's perspective. This allows readers to experience memory, hesitation, recognition, and emotional withdrawal directly.

### Movement Between Present And Past

The story moves between the narrator's present visits and her memories of the war. This structure reflects how trauma works: the present constantly awakens the past.

### Subtle Irony

Mrs Dorling had supposedly saved the objects, but she has turned them into evidence of selfishness. The address was meant to lead to recovery, but it leads to renunciation.

### Minimal Dialogue

The dialogue is brief and tense. Much is left unsaid. Silence, gestures, objects, and movements carry meaning.

## Why The Objects Lose Their Value

This is a central exam point.

The objects lose their value because they have been separated from:

1. The narrator's mother.
2. The old home.
3. Shared family routines.
4. The emotional atmosphere of the past.
5. The dignity of rightful belonging.

In Mrs Dorling's house, they are still physically present but emotionally dead. They remind the narrator not of recovery, but of absence.

## Why The Narrator Wants To Forget The Address

The narrator wants to forget the address because remembering it would keep her tied to a painful and useless hope.

The address can give her:

1. A location.
2. Proof that the objects exist.
3. A chance to confront Mrs Dorling.

But it cannot give her:

1. Her mother back.
2. Her old home back.
3. Her former life back.
4. Emotional peace through possessions.

Therefore, forgetting the address becomes a way to protect herself from unnecessary pain.

## Model Answers

### 1. What clue is given by Mrs Dorling's remark that she thought no one had come back?

Mrs Dorling's remark suggests that the story is connected with war, displacement, and the possible death or disappearance of the narrator's family. Her words imply that she had assumed none of the original owners of the possessions would return to claim them. This explains her discomfort when the narrator appears.

The remark also reveals Mrs Dorling's guilt. She has kept the possessions because she believed that there would be no one left to ask for them. The narrator's return disturbs the false security in which Mrs Dorling has been living.

### 2. What hardships did the narrator probably face during the war and after it?

The narrator likely faced separation from home, fear, uncertainty, loss of family, and the collapse of ordinary life during the war. The story suggests that she lost her mother and the secure household of her childhood. After the war, she had to rebuild life slowly and cautiously.

Her present circumstances also show hardship. She lives in a small rented room and has limited space and possessions. More importantly, she suffers emotionally. Familiar streets and old objects are painful because they remind her of a world that has vanished. Her hardship is therefore both material and psychological.

### 3. Why did the narrator want to forget the address?

The narrator wanted to forget the address because it no longer offered comfort or restoration. She had gone there hoping to see her mother's belongings and perhaps reconnect with the past. But when she saw those objects in Mrs Dorling's house, they felt strange, lifeless, and oppressive.

The possessions had lost their meaning because they were separated from her mother, her home, and the life in which they once belonged. Reclaiming them would not bring back the past. It would only deepen her pain. Therefore, she decided to forget the address as a way of freeing herself from a memory that could not heal her.

### 4. How is "The Address" a story of human predicament after war?

"The Address" is a story of human predicament after war because it shows the emotional and moral damage war leaves behind. The narrator survives, but survival does not mean that life returns to normal. She has lost her mother, home, and the familiar world of her childhood.

The story also shows how war can expose selfishness. Mrs Dorling takes advantage of Mrs S's fear and trust by carrying away household possessions. After the war, she avoids returning them. The narrator's problem is not simply how to recover objects, but how to face memories attached to a destroyed life. Her decision to forget the address shows the painful choice survivors may have to make: to let go of material links in order to continue living.

### 5. Describe the narrator's reaction when she enters Mrs Dorling's living room.

When the narrator enters Mrs Dorling's living room, she is shocked and oppressed. She sees familiar objects from her mother's house, but they are arranged in a strange and tasteless setting. The room feels both known and unknown.

This reaction shows that the objects no longer carry the comfort of home. Instead, they emphasize the loss of home. The narrator feels that the things have been cut off from the life that gave them meaning. This realization leads to her decision to leave without claiming them.

### 6. Was Mrs Dorling genuinely helping Mrs S?

Mrs Dorling claimed that she was helping Mrs S by keeping her valuable things safe during the war. However, her later behaviour suggests that her help was not genuine. She refuses to recognize the narrator, does not welcome her, and is seen wearing the mother's cardigan.

If Mrs Dorling had truly acted from kindness, she would have been ready to return the possessions or at least speak honestly. Her evasive behaviour shows that she has treated the belongings as her own. She represents selfishness disguised as assistance.

### 7. What is the significance of the green cardigan?

The green cardigan is significant because it proves that Mrs Dorling has the narrator's mother's belongings. It is also emotionally painful because it was a personal item, not merely a decorative object. Seeing Mrs Dorling wearing it makes the narrator realize that her mother's things have been absorbed into another woman's daily life.

The cardigan therefore symbolizes both proof and violation. It exposes Mrs Dorling's dishonesty and deepens the narrator's sense of loss.

### 8. Why does the narrator not wait for Mrs Dorling during the second visit?

The narrator does not wait because she has already understood the truth she needed to understand. Seeing the objects in Mrs Dorling's room makes her realize that recovering them would not restore her past. The memories attached to them have become painful in their new surroundings.

She also recognizes that confrontation with Mrs Dorling would not give her emotional peace. Leaving becomes a way of protecting herself from further hurt.

1. Do not say the narrator forgets the address because she does not care about her mother.
2. Do not describe Mrs Dorling as generous without explaining her later selfishness.
3. Do not treat the objects as only expensive items; they are memory carriers.
4. Do not ignore the war background.
5. Do not say the narrator is weak because she leaves; her leaving is a mature choice.
6. Do not confuse Mrs S with Mrs Dorling.
7. Do not reduce the story to a property dispute.

## Important Lines Of Thought For Exams

### The Story Is About More Than Property

The narrator has a moral claim to the possessions, but the story is not mainly about legal ownership. It is about whether objects can restore lost life. The answer is no.

### The Past Cannot Be Rebuilt From Things

The mother's belongings survive, but the mother and home do not. The objects therefore become reminders of absence rather than instruments of recovery.

### Silence Reveals Character

Mrs Dorling's silence, refusal, and half-open door reveal her guilt more effectively than a direct confession would.

### Forgetting Can Be A Form Of Healing

The narrator does not erase her mother from memory. She decides to forget only the address because it has become a painful link to betrayal and useless recovery.

## Sample Paragraphs

### Household Objects And Memory

In "The Address", household objects carry emotional memory. The tablecloth, silver, plates, cardigan, and other possessions are not important merely because they are valuable. They are connected with the narrator's mother and the life she lived before the war. However, when the narrator sees these objects in Mrs Dorling's house, they appear strange and oppressive. Their meaning has been damaged because they are separated from their rightful emotional setting. The story therefore shows that objects get their real value from people, places, and memories.

### Mrs Dorling's Moral Failure

Mrs Dorling represents selfishness hidden behind the appearance of help. During the war, she takes Mrs S's possessions, claiming that she wants to save them. Mrs S trusts her, but Mrs Dorling's later behaviour proves that the trust was misplaced. She refuses to recognize the narrator properly and avoids letting her enter the house. The fact that she wears the mother's cardigan shows that she has made the belongings part of her own life. Her coldness reveals guilt and moral failure.

### The Narrator's Decision

The narrator's decision to forget the address is not a sign of indifference. It is a sign of emotional maturity. She first goes to Mrs Dorling's house because she wants to see and touch the objects connected with her mother. But once she sees them in a strange room, she realizes that they cannot restore her lost home. They have become painful reminders rather than comforting memories. By leaving them behind, she chooses to move forward without letting the past wound her endlessly.

### Model Long Answer

"The Address" presents the emotional aftermath of war through the narrator's search for her mother's possessions. The story does not show war directly through fighting or destruction. Instead, it shows what remains after war: missing people, broken homes, displaced objects, and painful memory.

During the war, Mrs Dorling takes many of Mrs S's household belongings, claiming that she will keep them safe. Mrs S trusts her, but after the war Mrs Dorling avoids the narrator and behaves as if she does not want to return anything. This shows how war can create opportunities for selfish people to exploit the vulnerable.

The narrator's deepest suffering appears when she enters Mrs Dorling's living room and sees her mother's possessions in strange surroundings. The familiar objects no longer comfort her. They remind her that the life to which they belonged has disappeared. The narrator realizes that reclaiming those things will not restore her mother, her home, or her former self.

Her final decision to forget the address is therefore meaningful. It shows that survival after war sometimes requires letting go of material reminders. The story presents war as a force that damages not only buildings and bodies but also memory, trust, and the emotional meaning of ordinary things.
