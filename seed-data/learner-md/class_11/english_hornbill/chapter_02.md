# Chapter 2: We're Not Afraid to Die... if We Can All Be Together

## Chapter Snapshot

This chapter is a first-person account of a dangerous sea voyage by Gordon Cook and Alan East. It describes how a family and two crewmen survive a near-fatal disaster in the southern Indian Ocean.

The chapter is about adventure, courage, leadership, family love, teamwork, hope, and the human will to survive.

These notes explain the narrative, characters, themes, vocabulary, and textbook answers. They do not reproduce the full prose text.

## Learning Targets

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

4. Describe the courage of the children, adults, and crew.
5. Explain the role of optimism in survival.
6. Understand key nautical vocabulary.
7. Explain the structure and shifts in narration.
8. Write complete answers for comprehension and exam questions.

## 1. Background of the Voyage

The narrator and his wife Mary had long dreamed of sailing around the world.

They wanted to follow the route of Captain James Cook, the famous explorer.

This was not a sudden adventure. The family had prepared carefully for many years.

Their boat, Wavewalker, was:

- 23 metres long
- 30 tons in weight
- wooden-hulled
- professionally built
- tested in rough weather

The planned journey was long and demanding. It was expected to take three years and cover about 105,000 kilometres.

## 2. The People on Board

| Person | Role | Important Qualities |
|---|---|---|
| Gordon Cook | Captain, narrator, father | Skilled, responsible, determined |
| Mary | Wife and mother | Brave, steady, supportive |
| Jonathan | Son, age 6 | Innocent, courageous, emotionally strong |
| Suzanne or Sue | Daughter, age 7 | Brave, selfless, hopeful |
| Larry Vigil | American crewman | Cheerful, hardworking, optimistic |
| Herb Seigler | Swiss crewman | Reliable, practical, optimistic |

The survival of the group depends on everyone. The narrator leads, but he is not alone. Mary, Larry, Herb, Sue, and Jonathan all contribute emotionally or physically.

## 3. Route and Early Journey

The family sets sail from Plymouth, England.

The first leg of the journey goes down the west coast of Africa to Cape Town.

At Cape Town, they take on two crewmen, Larry and Herb, because the southern Indian Ocean is one of the roughest seas in the world.

For the first part, the journey is pleasant.

Then strong gales begin.

The waves grow extremely large, sometimes as high as the main mast.

## 4. Build-Up to Disaster

By December 25, the boat is about 3,500 kilometres east of Cape Town.

The weather is terrible, but the family still manages to celebrate Christmas.

On New Year's Day, the weather does not improve.

On January 2, the sea becomes extremely dangerous.

The narrator and crew take protective measures, but a gigantic wave strikes the boat in the evening.

## 5. Steps Taken Before the Wave Hit

When rough weather began, the captain took several precautions.

He:

- dropped the storm jib to slow the boat
- lashed a heavy mooring rope across the stern
- double-lashed everything that could move
- went through life-raft drill with everyone
- attached lifelines
- put on oilskins
- put on life jackets
- waited for the worst with the crew prepared

These actions show preparedness and seamanship.

The captain cannot stop the storm, but he can reduce risk.

## 6. The Gigantic Wave

The first sign of disaster is an unnatural silence.

The wind drops, the sky darkens, and a massive wave appears behind the ship.

The wave is far larger than the other waves.

It strikes Wavewalker with tremendous force.

The narrator is thrown overboard, injured, and almost loses consciousness.

His lifeline saves him.

He is pulled back toward the boat and manages to regain the wheel.

The boat is badly damaged and begins taking in water.

## 7. Damage to Wavewalker

The wave causes severe damage.

Damage includes:

- smashed decks
- water flooding the boat
- starboard side bashed open
- broken timbers
- floating debris inside cabins
- blocked hand pumps
- electric pump short-circuiting
- spare hand pumps washed away
- sails and equipment lost
- main rib frames damaged
- auxiliary engine disabled

The boat is still floating, but it is structurally weakened.

## 8. Steps Taken to Check Flooding

After the wave damages the boat, the captain and crew work to stop the flooding.

They:

- keep pumping water out
- inspect the damaged areas
- use hammer, screws, and canvas
- stretch canvas across gaping holes
- secure waterproof hatch covers
- deflect water over the side
- clear pumps when possible
- use another electric pump from under the chartroom floor
- pump continuously through the night

This is the practical struggle for survival.

Without quick repair and pumping, the boat would sink.

## 9. Sue's Courage

Sue is badly injured.

Her head swells, her eyes darken, and she has a cut on her arm.

But she does not complain at first.

When asked why she hid the injuries, she explains that she did not want to worry the adults while they were saving everyone.

This shows:

- self-control
- maturity beyond her age
- concern for others
- courage
- emotional strength

Later, she makes a card for her parents to make them laugh and hope for the best.

## 10. Jonathan's Courage

Jonathan is only six years old, but his courage gives the title its meaning.

When he asks whether they are going to die, he is not simply frightened. He wants emotional truth.

His statement that they are not afraid if they are together shows:

- love for family
- acceptance
- bravery
- trust in parents
- emotional unity

His words strengthen the narrator's determination to keep fighting.

## 11. Mary's Role

Mary remains calm and useful in the crisis.

She:

- alerts the narrator that the boat is flooding
- takes the wheel when needed
- supports the children
- stays with the narrator during crucial hours
- shares the fear but does not collapse

Her courage is practical and quiet.

## 12. Larry and Herb's Role

Larry and Herb work tirelessly.

They pump water, help repair the ship, and remain cheerful and optimistic under terrible stress.

The narrator remembers them with gratitude after reaching land.

They represent teamwork and steady courage.

## 13. January 3: Temporary Control

By the morning of January 3, the water level is partly under control.

The crew can rest in rotation for short periods.

But the boat is still badly damaged.

The narrator discovers that the hull is dangerously weakened.

Australia is too far away.

The only hope is to reach small islands to the east, especially Ile Amsterdam.

## 14. January 4: Hope and Renewed Danger

After 36 hours of pumping, the water level is finally reduced to a few centimetres.

The crew eats its first meal in almost two days.

This gives them a short period of relief.

But the weather worsens again in the evening.

By the next morning, the situation is again desperate.

## 15. January 5: Near Despair

On January 5, the storm becomes dangerous again.

The boat is weak, water continues entering, and the family fears death.

The narrator and Mary feel the end may be near.

This is when Jonathan's courage and Sue's hopeful card become emotionally important.

The children help the adults survive mentally.

## 16. January 6: Navigation and Hope

The wind eases enough for the narrator to use a sextant.

He works with:

- wind speed
- drift
- current
- changes in course
- magnetic variation
- imperfect compass readings

He calculates a course toward Ile Amsterdam.

The island is very small compared with the vast ocean, so the chance of finding it is extremely uncertain.

Yet the calculation works.

By evening, the island appears ahead.

## 17. Ile Amsterdam

Ile Amsterdam is a small volcanic island in the southern Indian Ocean.

To the exhausted voyagers, it becomes the most beautiful place in the world because it means survival.

The inhabitants help them ashore the next morning.

The landfall ends the immediate danger.

## 18. Narrative Structure

The chapter can be divided into three major sections.

| Section | Suggested Subtitle | Main Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Section 1 | Dream Voyage and Disaster | Preparation, route, storm, gigantic wave |
| Section 2 | Battle to Keep Wavewalker Alive | Repairs, pumping, injuries, fear, hope |
| Section 3 | Last Calculations and Rescue | Navigation, children's courage, sighting the island |

The structure moves from adventure to crisis to survival.

## 19. Mental Condition on January 4 and 5

On January 4, the voyagers feel a brief sense of relief because the pumps have reduced the water level and they eat their first meal in almost two days.

But the relief is short-lived because the weather worsens again.

On January 5, their condition becomes desperate. The adults fear that death may be near, but they continue working. The children show extraordinary courage and emotional strength. Jonathan says they are not afraid if the family is together, and Sue gives a hopeful card despite her injuries.

Their mental state moves from exhaustion to fragile hope, then to fear, and finally to renewed determination.

## 20. Adults and Children in Danger

The adults respond with action and responsibility.

They:

- steer
- repair
- pump water
- calculate position
- comfort children
- try to save the boat

The children respond with emotional courage.

They:

- do not panic
- hide pain to avoid worrying others
- express love
- encourage their parents
- accept danger with surprising calm

The adults provide physical survival.

The children provide moral strength.

## 21. Optimism in the Story

Optimism is not blind cheerfulness. It is the decision to keep working even when success seems unlikely.

Optimism appears in:

- the crew's continued pumping
- the narrator's repeated calculations
- Mary's steadiness
- Sue's hopeful card
- Jonathan's trust
- Larry and Herb's cheerfulness

The story suggests that optimism helps people endure extreme stress because it prevents surrender.

## 22. Lessons from the Experience

The chapter teaches several lessons:

- preparation matters
- courage is not absence of fear
- teamwork can save lives
- leadership requires calm action
- family unity gives emotional strength
- children can show great courage
- hope is practical, not merely emotional
- nature is powerful and unpredictable
- survival depends on skill, discipline, and resilience

## 23. Why People Undertake Risky Expeditions

People undertake adventurous expeditions because:

- they want to test their abilities
- they seek discovery
- they love nature and travel
- they want to follow historic routes
- they enjoy challenge
- they want meaningful experience
- they seek personal growth
- they value courage and exploration

Such journeys involve risk, but they can also reveal human strength.

## 25. Character Sketch: The Narrator

The narrator is brave, skilled, responsible, and determined.

He prepares carefully before the voyage. During the crisis, he keeps steering even after serious injury. He repairs the hull, arranges pumping, calculates the course, and refuses to give up.

He is also honest. He admits fear and uncertainty. He does not pretend to be confident when he is not. This honesty makes his courage more believable.

## 26. Character Sketch: Sue

Sue is brave, selfless, and emotionally mature.

Although she is badly injured, she does not complain because she does not want to worry the adults. Later, she makes a cheerful card for her parents.

Her courage is quiet but powerful.

She helps the family emotionally when they are close to despair.

## 27. Character Sketch: Jonathan

Jonathan is young but deeply courageous.

He understands the danger but says he is not afraid if the family is together.

His words give the narrator new strength.

Jonathan shows that courage can come from love and trust, not only physical strength.

## 28. Character Sketch: Mary

Mary is calm, brave, and practical.

She alerts the narrator to the flooding, takes the wheel, cares for the children, and remains steady during the crisis.

Her courage supports the captain's leadership.

## 29. Character Sketch: Larry and Herb

Larry and Herb are hardworking, cheerful, and dependable.

They pump water, help manage the crisis, and remain optimistic under extreme stress.

Their role shows that survival depends on teamwork, not only on one leader.

## 32. Understanding the Text: Answers

### Q1. List the steps taken by the captain to protect the ship when rough weather began.

The captain dropped the storm jib to slow the boat. He lashed a heavy mooring rope across the stern, double-lashed everything, went through life-raft drill, attached lifelines, and made everyone wear oilskins and life jackets. These steps were taken to reduce damage and prepare for emergency.

### Q2. List the steps taken to check flooding in the ship.

The captain used a hammer, screws, and canvas to cover the damaged starboard side. He secured waterproof hatch covers over the holes so most water would be deflected over the side. The crew pumped continuously. When hand pumps blocked and the electric pump failed, he found another electric pump under the chartroom floor and connected it to an out-pipe.

### Q3. Describe the mental condition of the voyagers on January 4 and 5.

On January 4, they felt temporary relief because the water level was under control and they ate after almost two days. However, their hope was fragile because the boat was still badly damaged. On January 5, the weather worsened again and the situation became desperate. The adults feared death, but they continued fighting. The children showed extraordinary courage, giving emotional strength to everyone.

### Q4. Describe the shifts in narration and give subtitles.

The first section may be titled "The Dream Voyage and the Great Wave." It describes the preparation, route, rough weather, and the wave that damages Wavewalker.

The second section may be titled "The Fight to Stay Afloat." It describes repairs, pumping, injuries, fear, and the struggle to keep the boat alive.

The third section may be titled "Finding Ile Amsterdam." It describes the final calculations, the children's encouragement, and the successful sighting of the island.

## 33. Deeper Questions: Answers

### Q1. What difference is seen between adults and children when faced with danger?

The adults respond with practical responsibility. They repair, pump, steer, calculate, and try to save the boat. The children respond with emotional courage. Sue hides her injuries to avoid worrying others, and Jonathan says he is not afraid if the family is together. The adults protect physically; the children strengthen everyone emotionally.

### Q2. How does optimism help people endure extreme stress?

Optimism prevents people from giving up. In the story, Larry and Herb remain cheerful, Mary stays steady, Sue makes a hopeful card, Jonathan trusts his family, and the narrator keeps calculating the route. Their hope does not remove danger, but it gives them the strength to keep working until they reach land.

### Q3. What lessons do we learn from hazardous experiences close to death?

Such experiences teach courage, humility, preparedness, teamwork, and the value of family. They show that life is uncertain and that survival often depends on discipline, skill, hope, and cooperation.

### Q4. Why do people undertake adventurous expeditions despite risks?

People take such risks because they seek challenge, discovery, adventure, personal growth, and meaningful achievement. Some want to test their skills or follow historic journeys. Risk is part of adventure, but it can reveal courage and deepen one's understanding of life.

## 34. Thinking About Language: Answers

### Q1. Words for storm

English words include storm, gale, cyclone, typhoon, hurricane, squall, tempest, and thunderstorm.

In a learner's own language, the answer will vary. A good answer should list local words and, if possible, explain differences in intensity.

### Q2. Terms for vessels

English terms include yacht, boat, canoe, ship, steamer, schooner, raft, ferry, barge, dinghy, and catamaran.

In a learner's own language, the answer will vary depending on region and water culture.

### Q3. Which Indian language does catamaran come from?

The word catamaran comes from Tamil. It is derived from words referring to tied logs or a type of raft.

### Q4. What emotions do boatmen's songs usually express?

Boatmen's songs often express rhythm, labour, longing, loneliness, courage, devotion, hope, homesickness, and closeness to nature.

## 39. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Courage of the Children

The children show remarkable courage during the crisis. Sue is badly injured, with a swollen head, black eyes, and a cut arm, but she does not complain because she does not want to worry the adults while they are saving the boat. Later, she makes a cheerful card for her parents, thanking them and hoping for the best. Jonathan also shows extraordinary courage when he says that he is not afraid of dying if the family is together. The children do not repair the boat physically, but their emotional strength gives the adults new determination. Their courage is one of the main reasons the story is so moving.

## 40. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Role of Teamwork

Teamwork is central to the survival of the voyagers. The narrator leads by steering, repairing the boat, pumping water, and calculating the route. Mary takes the wheel and supports the family. Larry and Herb pump continuously and remain cheerful under terrible stress. Sue hides her injuries and gives hope through her card. Jonathan's brave words strengthen the narrator emotionally. Each person contributes in a different way. The story shows that in extreme danger, survival depends not only on individual courage but also on cooperation.

## 41. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Optimism and Survival

The story shows that optimism helps people survive extreme danger. The voyagers are exhausted, injured, and trapped in a damaged boat in the southern Indian Ocean. Yet they continue working. Larry and Herb remain cheerful, Mary stays steady, the narrator keeps repairing and calculating, Sue makes a hopeful card, and Jonathan expresses trust in family unity. Their optimism is not unrealistic. It is shown through action, discipline, and refusal to surrender. Because they keep hope alive, they continue fighting until they reach Ile Amsterdam.

## 42. Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Saying the narrator alone saved everyone.

Correction: The narrator led the effort, but Mary, Larry, Herb, Sue, and Jonathan all contributed.

Mistake 2: Saying the children were too young to understand danger.

Correction: Jonathan clearly understood death, and Sue understood the need not to worry the adults.

Mistake 3: Treating optimism as simple happiness.

Correction: Optimism here means continuing to act despite fear and danger.

Mistake 4: Ignoring the captain's preparation.

Correction: The family had trained for years, and the captain took many precautions before the disaster.

Mistake 5: Calling Ile Amsterdam beautiful in an ordinary scenic sense.

Correction: It appears beautiful because it means land, safety, and survival.

## 44. One-Page Exam Summary

We're Not Afraid to Die... if We Can All Be Together is a first-person survival account by Gordon Cook and Alan East. The narrator, his wife Mary, their children Jonathan and Sue, and crewmen Larry and Herb sail on Wavewalker to follow Captain James Cook's round-the-world route. After leaving Cape Town, they enter the dangerous southern Indian Ocean.

On January 2, a gigantic wave strikes the boat. The narrator is thrown overboard but survives through his lifeline. Wavewalker is badly damaged and begins taking in water. The captain and crew repair holes with canvas and hatch covers, pump continuously, and try to keep the boat afloat. Sue is badly injured but hides her pain. Jonathan shows great courage by saying that he is not afraid if the family remains together.

The boat is too damaged to reach Australia, so the narrator aims for Ile Amsterdam, a tiny island in the vast ocean. After exhausting calculations and continued hope, the island is sighted. The story shows that courage, preparation, teamwork, optimism, and family love can help people survive even the most dangerous situations.
