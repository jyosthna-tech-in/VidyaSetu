# Chapter 5: The Ailing Planet: The Green Movement's Role
### NCERT Class 11 English Hornbill

---

## Chapter Purpose

This prose piece by Nani Palkhivala is an argument about the environmental crisis and the responsibility of human beings toward the earth. The chapter asks learners to think beyond personal comfort and short-term development. It presents the earth as a living system whose forests, fisheries, grasslands, croplands, and future generations are being damaged by careless human action.

The central idea is:

> Human beings must move from exploitation of nature to responsible and sustainable stewardship of the planet.

---

5. Explain the link between population growth and environmental stress.
6. Understand the responsibility of industry, government, and citizens.

## 2. Background: The Green Movement

The Green Movement refers to the global awareness and activism about environmental protection. The chapter presents it as one of the most significant movements in modern history because it changed how people see the earth.

Earlier, many people viewed nature as a resource to be used by humans. The Green Movement encouraged a different view:

- The earth is a living system.
- Human beings are part of nature, not masters outside it.
- Economic development must not destroy ecological balance.
- Future generations have a claim on the planet's resources.

This shift is described as revolutionary because it changes human perception itself.

## 3. Main Argument of the Chapter

The chapter develops a clear argument:

1. The Green Movement created worldwide environmental awareness.
2. The earth's biological systems are under pressure.
3. Development must be sustainable.
4. Population growth worsens ecological damage.
5. Industry and political leadership must accept responsibility.
6. Humanity must protect the planet for future generations.

## 4. Sustainable Development

Sustainable development means development that meets present needs without destroying the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

### Simple Meaning

Use resources, but do not exhaust or damage them so badly that future people suffer.

### Example

| Unsustainable action | Sustainable alternative |
|---|---|
| Cutting forests without replanting | Planned forestry and replanting |
| Overfishing | Fishing limits and breeding protection |
| Excessive pollution | Cleaner production and waste control |
| Using soil without restoring fertility | Soil conservation and natural fertilisers |

Sustainability asks us to balance present development with future survival.

---

## 5. The Earth as a Living System

The chapter presents the earth as an interconnected living system. Human beings are only one part of this larger system.

This is called a holistic and ecological view.

| Old view | Ecological view |
|---|---|
| Nature is separate from humans | Humans are part of nature |
| Earth is a storehouse of resources | Earth is a living life-support system |
| Development means extraction | Development must include renewal and balance |
| Present needs dominate | Future generations matter too |

The ecological view is important because damage to one part of the system affects the whole.

---

## 6. Four Principal Biological Systems

The chapter highlights four major biological systems that support the global economy and human survival:

1. Fisheries
2. Forests
3. Grasslands
4. Croplands

These systems provide food and raw materials. If they are damaged, the economic system and human life are affected.

### Threat Map

| Biological system | Threat | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Fisheries | Overfishing | Decline in fish supply |
| Forests | Deforestation | Soil erosion, loss of biodiversity, climate impact |
| Grasslands | Overgrazing | Land degradation and desertification |
| Croplands | Overuse and poor practices | Reduced fertility and food insecurity |

---

## 7. Forests and Environmental Decline

Forests are presented as one of the most visibly damaged systems.

The chapter points to:

- Rapid loss of tropical forests
- Official forest land becoming nearly treeless
- Use of dung as fuel, which deprives soil of natural fertiliser
- Pressure from population and poverty

Forests are not only trees. They protect soil, water, climate, wildlife, and human livelihoods.

---

## 8. Population Growth

The chapter treats population growth as a major factor that worsens environmental stress.

When population increases rapidly:

- More food is needed.
- More land is used.
- More forests may be cut.
- More fuel is required.
- More waste and pollution are produced.
- Public services come under pressure.

Population growth is not discussed only as a number. It is connected with poverty, education, health, and environmental pressure.

---

## 9. Role of Industry

Industry has a major role in environmental protection because industries use resources and can create pollution.

The chapter suggests that businesses must see environmental performance as part of their responsibility, not as an optional extra.

Good industrial responsibility includes:

- Cleaner technology
- Waste reduction
- Pollution control
- Resource efficiency
- Compliance with environmental law
- Long-term ecological planning

The chapter's message is that industry cannot separate profit from environmental responsibility.

---

## 10. Responsibility Toward Future Generations

One of the strongest ideas in the chapter is that the present generation does not own the earth absolutely. It holds the earth in trust for future generations.

This means:

- We must not exhaust resources selfishly.
- We must not leave behind a damaged planet.
- We must think beyond short-term gain.
- Environmental responsibility is a moral duty.

The chapter repeatedly connects ecology with ethics.

## 13. Tone and Style

The tone is serious and persuasive. The writer uses:

- Facts and references
- Moral warnings
- Rhetorical questions
- Strong environmental imagery
- Comparisons with historical shifts in thinking
- Formal vocabulary

The prose is not a story; it is an argument. While answering, focus on the writer's claims and evidence.

## 14. Answer Frames

### Frame 1: The Green Movement's Importance

1. Define the Green Movement.
2. Explain worldwide environmental awareness.
3. Mention the shift to an ecological view.
4. Connect it to sustainable development.
5. Conclude with responsibility toward future generations.

1. Define sustainable development.
2. Explain present needs and future needs.
3. Give examples such as forests, fisheries, and croplands.
4. Explain why uncontrolled development is dangerous.
5. Conclude with balanced growth.

### Frame 3: Biological Systems

1. Name the four biological systems.
2. Explain their role in food and raw materials.
3. Mention how they are being depleted.
4. Connect depletion with human survival and economy.

### Frame 4: Population and Environment

1. State that population growth increases pressure.
2. Explain demand for food, fuel, land, and services.
3. Connect poverty and ecological damage.
4. Conclude with need for planning and awareness.

## Final Takeaway

"The Ailing Planet" is a warning and a call to responsibility. It teaches that environmental protection is not separate from economics, politics, industry, or morality. The future of humanity depends on whether we learn to treat the earth as a shared living system rather than a possession to be exhausted.
