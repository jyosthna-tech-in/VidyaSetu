# Chapter 1: The Portrait of a Lady and A Photograph

## Chapter Snapshot

This chapter contains two closely related literary pieces:

1. The Portrait of a Lady by Khushwant Singh.
2. A Photograph by Shirley Toulson.

Both pieces explore memory, love, ageing, death, and the quiet pain of loss. The prose piece presents the narrator's relationship with his grandmother. The poem presents a daughter's memory of her mother through an old photograph.

These notes do not reproduce the full prose or poem. They explain the ideas, structure, language, themes, and answers in a learner-friendly form.

## 1. About The Portrait of a Lady

The Portrait of a Lady is an autobiographical character sketch. Khushwant Singh remembers his grandmother and presents her with affection, humour, respect, and restraint.

The title is important. A "portrait" is usually a visual representation, but here the author paints a portrait through words. He describes her physical appearance, habits, beliefs, love, loneliness, strength, and death.

The story is not driven by dramatic action. It is driven by memory. The narrator moves through different stages of life, and the grandmother's role changes with each stage.

## 2. Central Idea of The Portrait of a Lady

The prose piece presents a deeply affectionate relationship between the narrator and his grandmother. Their bond changes over time because of education, urban life, and growing age, but their emotional connection does not disappear.

The grandmother is shown as:

- old but beautiful in a spiritual sense
- religious and disciplined
- loving but not sentimental
- strong and self-contained
- lonely yet accepting
- connected to animals and birds
- calm even near death

The story ends with the sparrows' silent grief, which gives the grandmother's death a tender and almost sacred quality.

## 3. Narrative Point of View

The story is told in the first person.

The narrator speaks as an adult looking back on childhood and youth.

This point of view matters because:

- the tone is personal
- the grandmother is seen through memory
- childhood misunderstandings are presented with gentle humour
- the narrator's affection becomes clearer as he grows older
- the story feels intimate and truthful

The narrator does not over-explain emotions. He lets small details reveal love and loss.

## 4. Structure of the Prose Piece

The prose moves in a chronological pattern.

| Stage | Place | Relationship |
|---|---|---|
| Childhood | Village | Narrator and grandmother are close companions |
| City school years | City | Their daily bond weakens |
| University years | City | Physical and emotional distance increases |
| Departure abroad | Railway station | Silent affection remains |
| Return home | Station and house | Grandmother is unchanged in devotion |
| Death | Home | Her spiritual calm and the sparrows' grief complete the portrait |

The movement from closeness to distance and then to loss gives the story its emotional shape.

## 5. First Phase: Village Childhood

In the village, the narrator and his grandmother are very close.

She:

- wakes him up
- gets him ready for school
- says prayers while caring for him
- prepares his school things
- feeds him
- walks with him to school
- sits in the temple while he studies
- feeds village dogs on the way back

This phase shows companionship. The narrator depends on her physically, emotionally, and spiritually.

The grandmother is not only a caretaker. She is his first moral and cultural guide.

## 6. Second Phase: City School

The relationship changes when they move to the city.

The narrator begins attending an English school by motor bus. The grandmother no longer goes with him.

The city school teaches:

- English
- science
- Western learning
- things like gravity and the shape of the earth

The grandmother feels unhappy because:

- she cannot help him with lessons
- she does not understand or approve of the new education
- there is no teaching about God and scriptures
- she disapproves of music lessons because of her cultural beliefs

This phase shows the generation gap between traditional religious life and modern Western education.

## 7. Third Phase: University and Separation

When the narrator goes to university, he gets a room of his own.

The common link between him and his grandmother breaks.

She accepts her loneliness without complaint.

Her routine becomes:

- spinning at the wheel
- praying
- telling the beads of her rosary
- feeding sparrows in the afternoon

The phrase "accepted her seclusion with resignation" shows that she is emotionally strong. She does not protest, demand attention, or dramatize her loneliness.

## 8. Departure Abroad

When the narrator leaves for further studies abroad, he expects his grandmother to be emotional.

But she remains calm.

At the railway station:

- she does not speak much
- she continues praying
- she moves the beads of her rosary
- she silently kisses his forehead

This moment shows deep love without outward drama.

Her affection is not expressed through long speeches. It is expressed through prayer, touch, and silence.

## 9. Return from Abroad

After five years, the narrator returns.

The grandmother is still alive and seems unchanged.

She meets him at the station and embraces him. Her lips are still moving in prayer.

On the first day of his return, she feeds the sparrows longer than usual.

This suggests that her joy is real, but she expresses it in her own way.

## 10. The Unusual Evening Before Death

In the evening after the narrator's return, the grandmother does something unusual.

For the first time, she stops praying and gathers women from the neighbourhood. She beats an old drum and sings songs of homecoming.

This is unusual because her life has always been centered on prayer.

The family has to stop her because they fear she may overstrain herself.

The next morning she falls ill and senses that her end is near.

## 11. The Grandmother's Death

The grandmother believes her end is near.

She refuses to waste time in conversation.

Instead, she lies peacefully, prays, and tells the beads of her rosary.

Her death is quiet, controlled, and dignified.

The rosary falling from her fingers is a powerful image. It marks the moment when prayer and life stop together.

## 12. The Sparrows' Grief

After her death, thousands of sparrows gather near her body.

They do not chirp.

They do not eat the bread offered by the narrator's mother.

When the body is taken away, they leave quietly.

This scene suggests that the sparrows share the family's grief.

The silence of the birds is more moving than loud mourning.

## 13. Character Sketch of the Grandmother

The grandmother is the emotional centre of the prose piece.

### Physical Appearance

She is described as:

- old
- short
- slightly bent
- wrinkled
- dressed in spotless white
- carrying a rosary
- moving slowly

The narrator cannot imagine that she was ever young. This shows a child's limited understanding of age.

### Inner Beauty

The narrator says she was not pretty, but she was beautiful.

This contrast is important.

Pretty refers mainly to youthful physical attractiveness.

Beautiful refers to peace, purity, dignity, and spiritual presence.

Her beauty comes from calmness, devotion, and kindness.

### Religious Nature

Her religious nature is shown through:

- morning prayers
- reading scriptures
- rosary beads
- constant prayer
- disapproval of education without spiritual learning
- desire to spend her final moments praying

Religion is not occasional for her. It is the rhythm of her life.

### Love and Care

She cares for the narrator in childhood by waking him, dressing him, feeding him, and taking him to school.

She expresses love through action rather than emotional speech.

### Strength

She accepts change without complaint.

She adjusts to city life.

She accepts loneliness after the narrator grows up.

She does not break down when he leaves for abroad.

She faces death calmly.

### Connection with Animals and Birds

In the village, she feeds dogs.

In the city, she feeds sparrows.

This shows kindness, routine, and companionship.

Her bond with sparrows becomes so deep that they seem to mourn her death.

## 14. Character Sketch of the Narrator

The narrator is affectionate, observant, and honest.

As a child, he is close to his grandmother and depends on her.

As he grows, he becomes absorbed in modern education and gradually moves away from her.

He does not openly rebel against her, but their worlds become different.

As an adult narrator, he looks back with tenderness and recognizes the depth of the relationship.

His narration is restrained. He does not exaggerate his grief, which makes the story more powerful.

## 15. Themes in The Portrait of a Lady

### Ageing

The grandmother appears old from the beginning and remains almost timeless in the narrator's memory.

Ageing is shown not as weakness alone, but as dignity, faith, and endurance.

### Memory

The story is built from remembered images:

- the grandfather's portrait
- the grandmother's white clothes
- the school routine
- the sparrows
- the railway station
- the rosary

These images make memory vivid.

### Generation Gap

The grandmother belongs to a world of religion, village life, scriptures, and oral tradition.

The narrator enters a world of English, science, music lessons, motor buses, university, and foreign education.

Their values differ, but affection remains.

### Silent Love

The grandmother rarely expresses feelings directly.

Her love appears through service, prayer, feeding, waiting, and silent gestures.

### Spirituality

Prayer is central to her life.

Even death is presented through prayer.

The story suggests that her spirituality gives her peace and strength.

### Loss

Loss enters gradually.

First, the narrator loses daily companionship with her.

Then he loses emotional closeness.

Finally, he loses her physically.

The sparrows' silence deepens the sense of loss.

## 18. Language and Style

Khushwant Singh's style is simple but rich in detail.

Key features:

- first-person narration
- gentle humour
- vivid physical description
- contrast between outer appearance and inner beauty
- short remembered scenes
- restrained emotion
- symbolic images
- simple vocabulary with deep emotional effect

The story does not rely on dramatic dialogue. It relies on observation and memory.

## 19. Understanding the Text: Answers

### Q1. Mention the three phases of the author's relationship with his grandmother before he left the country.

The first phase is in the village, where the narrator and his grandmother are constantly together. She wakes him up, prepares him for school, accompanies him to the temple school, and feeds village dogs on the way home.

The second phase begins when they move to the city. The narrator attends an English school by motor bus, and the grandmother no longer accompanies him. Their daily closeness begins to weaken.

The third phase begins when the narrator goes to university and gets a separate room. The common link of friendship is broken, and the grandmother spends most of her time praying, spinning, and feeding sparrows.

### Q2. Give three reasons why the grandmother was disturbed when he started going to the city school.

She was disturbed because she could not help him with English and science lessons.

She disliked the fact that the city school did not teach God and scriptures.

She was upset when he began music lessons because, according to her traditional beliefs, music was associated with people of low social respectability.

### Q3. Give three ways in which the grandmother spent her days after he grew up.

She spent her days spinning at the wheel.

She recited prayers and moved the beads of her rosary.

She fed the sparrows in the afternoon, which became the happiest part of her day.

### Q4. What was odd about the grandmother's behaviour just before she died?

The odd behaviour was that she stopped praying for the first time and sang songs of homecoming with women from the neighbourhood while beating an old drum. This was unusual because prayer had always been central to her life. The next morning she became ill and said her end was near.

### Q5. How did the sparrows express sorrow when the grandmother died?

The sparrows gathered silently around her body. They did not chirp and did not eat the bread crumbs offered to them. When the body was carried away, they flew away quietly. Their silence and refusal to eat suggest mourning.

## 20. Deeper Questions: Answers

### Q1. How do we know that the grandmother was religious?

Her religious nature is shown through her daily prayers, rosary beads, reading of scriptures, and temple visits. She recites prayers while preparing the narrator for school. She is unhappy that his city education does not include God and scriptures. In her final moments, she chooses prayer over conversation.

### Q2. Describe the changing relationship between the author and his grandmother. Did their feelings change?

Their relationship changes in outward form but not in inner affection. In the village they are close companions. In the city, school separates their daily routine. At university, physical distance increases. When he goes abroad, their bond is expressed through silence and prayer. Their feelings do not truly change; only their circumstances and habits change.

### Q3. Was the grandmother strong in character?

Yes. She is strong because she accepts loneliness without complaint, remains disciplined in prayer and spinning, does not show emotional weakness when the narrator leaves for abroad, and faces death calmly. She has firm beliefs and lives by them consistently.

### Q4. Why is the grandmother described as beautiful though not pretty?

Pretty usually suggests youthful physical attractiveness. The grandmother is old and wrinkled, so she is not pretty in that sense. But she is beautiful because of her peace, purity, love, faith, and dignity.

### Q5. What is the role of sparrows in the story?

The sparrows show the grandmother's tenderness and loneliness. Feeding them becomes her happiest daily routine. At her death, their silence creates a powerful image of grief and suggests that her kindness had created a bond beyond human relationships.

## 21. Language Questions: Answers

### Q1. Which language do you think the author and his grandmother used while talking to each other?

They probably used their mother tongue or a local language, not English. Their relationship began in a village setting, and the grandmother was connected to traditional and religious life.

### Q2. Which language do you use to talk to elderly relatives in your family?

This answer depends on the learner. A good answer should name the language used at home and explain that people often use their mother tongue or regional language with elders because it feels natural and emotionally close.

### Q3. How would you say "a dilapidated drum" in your language?

This answer depends on the learner's language. The phrase means an old, worn-out drum in poor condition.

### Q4. Can you think of a song or poem in your language about homecoming?

This answer depends on the learner. The important idea is that homecoming songs often express return, belonging, family, relief, nostalgia, and celebration.

## 25. Grammar Focus: Past Perfect

The past perfect tense is used for an action completed before another past action or past time.

Pattern:

`had + past participle`

Examples:

- had known
- had finished
- had taught
- had lit

Use:

When narrating memories, the writer often moves further back into the past. Past perfect helps show which past event happened earlier.

Example:

The narrator had known his grandmother as old for many years before he wrote about her.

## 26. About A Photograph

A Photograph is a short reflective poem by Shirley Toulson.

The poem presents an old photograph of the poet's mother as a young girl at the beach. The photograph becomes a doorway into memory, time, and loss.

Like The Portrait of a Lady, the poem deals with the death of a loved one. But the poem is more compressed, silent, and indirect.

## 27. Central Idea of A Photograph

The poem explores how a photograph preserves a moment that time has destroyed.

The photograph shows the poet's mother as a young girl with two cousins at the seaside.

Years later, the mother laughs at the photograph and remembers that day.

Later still, after the mother's death, the poet looks at the same photograph and feels the silence of loss.

The sea remains, but human lives pass away.

## 28. Structure of the Poem

The poem has three stages of time.

| Stage | Time | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| First stage | Mother's childhood | The photograph captures a beach scene |
| Second stage | Mother as adult | The mother laughs at the old photograph |
| Third stage | After mother's death | The poet reflects silently on loss |

The poem moves from image to memory to grief.

## 29. The Photograph as a Symbol

The photograph symbolizes:

- preserved memory
- frozen time
- family history
- childhood innocence
- the contrast between past and present
- the pain of loss

A photograph can keep an image, but it cannot bring back the person or the moment.

That is the poem's quiet tragedy.

## 30. The Sea as a Symbol

The sea represents continuity.

The people in the photograph are temporary, but the sea appears almost unchanged.

This contrast shows the difference between nature and human life.

Human beings are transient.

Nature seems more lasting.

## 31. Transience in the Poem

Transient means temporary or short-lived.

The poem shows transience through:

- the mother's childhood passing away
- the mother's later death
- the poet's inability to speak about the loss
- the photograph preserving only an image, not life

The poem does not use loud grief. It shows grief through understatement and silence.

## 32. Meaning of "Laboured Ease of Loss"

The phrase suggests an effort to appear calm after loss.

The mother's laughter at her childhood photograph contains nostalgia because her childhood is gone.

The poet's memory of her mother contains grief because the mother is gone.

Both try to face loss, but the ease is "laboured" because it requires emotional effort.

## 33. Meaning of "This Circumstance"

"This circumstance" refers to the situation of the mother's death and the poet's grief.

The poet cannot say anything adequate about it.

The silence of loss becomes stronger than words.

## 34. Literary Devices in A Photograph

| Device | Example of Use | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Visual imagery | photograph, beach, sea, girls | Creates a vivid scene |
| Contrast | changing human life and unchanged sea | Highlights mortality |
| Alliteration | "terribly transient" | Emphasizes temporary human life |
| Personification | silence appears powerful | Shows grief beyond words |
| Irony | a happy photograph leads to grief | Deepens emotional effect |
| Symbolism | photograph and sea | Connects memory with time |

## 35. Tone of A Photograph

The tone is:

- nostalgic
- quiet
- restrained
- sorrowful
- reflective

The poet does not describe grief dramatically. Instead, she shows how loss can leave a person speechless.

## 36. Think It Out: Answers

### Q1. What does the word "cardboard" denote in the poem? Why has it been used?

"Cardboard" denotes the old photograph mounted or printed on card. The word is used to emphasize the physical object that preserves the image. It also suggests age, fragility, and the distance between the present and the past.

### Q2. What has the camera captured?

The camera has captured the poet's mother as a young girl standing at the beach with two cousins. It has captured a moment of childhood innocence before the poet was born.

### Q3. What has not changed over the years? What does this suggest?

The sea appears to have changed very little. This suggests that nature seems more permanent than human life. Human beings grow old and die, while natural elements continue.

### Q4. What did the mother's laugh at the snapshot indicate?

Her laugh indicates amusement, nostalgia, and affectionate remembrance. She laughs at the old clothes and childhood scene, but the laughter also contains awareness that the past is gone.

### Q5. What is the meaning of "Both wry with the laboured ease of loss"?

The line means that both the mother and the poet experience loss in different ways. The mother has lost her childhood, and the poet has lost her mother. Both try to handle loss with calmness, but that calmness requires effort.

### Q6. What does "this circumstance" refer to?

"This circumstance" refers to the poet's present situation after her mother's death. It refers to the grief and silence caused by that loss.

### Q7. What are the three phases depicted in the poem?

The first phase is the mother's childhood beach holiday captured in the photograph.

The second phase is the mother's adulthood, when she looks at the photograph and laughs at the memory.

The third phase is the poet's present, after the mother's death, when the photograph creates silence and grief.

## 37. Comparison Between the Prose and the Poem

| Point | The Portrait of a Lady | A Photograph |
|---|---|---|
| Main relationship | Grandson and grandmother | Daughter and mother |
| Form | Prose sketch | Poem |
| Memory object | Narrator's recollections | Old photograph |
| Tone | Affectionate, humorous, tender | Quiet, nostalgic, sorrowful |
| Death | Grandmother's death described directly | Mother's death reflected indirectly |
| Symbol of grief | Silent sparrows | Silence after loss |
| Main idea | Love remains despite distance | Memory remains but cannot restore the dead |

Both works show that love survives in memory, but memory also carries pain.

## 38. Common Themes Across Both Works

### Memory

Both works are built from memory. The narrator remembers his grandmother; the poet remembers her mother through a photograph.

### Time

Time changes relationships and people. Childhood becomes adulthood, and the living become memories.

### Loss

Both pieces deal with the death of a loved one.

### Silence

In The Portrait of a Lady, the sparrows are silent.

In A Photograph, silence becomes the final response to grief.

### Love Without Drama

Neither work presents loud emotional expression. The love is quiet, deep, and dignified.

## 39. Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Saying the grandmother was ugly.

Correction: The narrator says she was not pretty, but he clearly presents her as beautiful in a spiritual and emotional sense.

Mistake 2: Saying the narrator stopped loving his grandmother.

Correction: Their daily closeness decreased, but love remained.

Mistake 3: Saying the sparrows came only for food.

Correction: Their silence and refusal to eat suggest grief.

Mistake 4: Treating A Photograph as only a description of a picture.

Correction: The photograph is a starting point for reflection on time, memory, and death.

Mistake 5: Saying the sea has no importance.

Correction: The sea contrasts with temporary human life.

Mistake 6: Explaining "laboured ease" as simple happiness.

Correction: It means an effortful calmness in the face of loss.

## 40. Short Answer Bank

### Why did the grandmother feed sparrows?

Feeding sparrows gave her companionship and joy after she became isolated from the narrator. It became her happiest routine in the city.

### Why was the move to the city a turning point?

It changed the daily closeness between the narrator and his grandmother. He went to an English school by bus, while she could no longer accompany him or help with his lessons.

### Why did the grandmother disapprove of music lessons?

Because in her traditional view, music was associated with beggars and courtesans, not with respectable education.

### Why is the grandmother's death peaceful?

She accepts death calmly, spends her final moments in prayer, and does not show fear or panic.

### Why does the poet say there is nothing to say?

Because her mother's death creates a grief so deep that words feel inadequate.

## 41. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Grandmother's Character

The grandmother in The Portrait of a Lady is a deeply religious, loving, disciplined, and strong person. She is physically old, wrinkled, and bent, but the narrator presents her as spiritually beautiful. In the village, she cares for him completely by waking him, preparing him for school, accompanying him to the temple school, and feeding him. Her religious nature is shown by her prayers, rosary, and reading of scriptures. When the narrator grows up, she accepts loneliness calmly and spends her time spinning, praying, and feeding sparrows. She does not become emotional when he leaves for abroad, but silently kisses his forehead. Even at death, she remains peaceful and chooses prayer over conversation. Her strength lies in quiet acceptance, faith, and self-control.

## 42. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Changing Relationship

The narrator's relationship with his grandmother changes through three phases. In the village, they are very close companions. She looks after him and goes with him to school. In the city, their closeness weakens because he goes to an English school by motor bus, and she cannot share his new education. She becomes unhappy with Western learning and music lessons. When he goes to university, he gets a separate room, and their common link is broken. Yet their affection does not disappear. When he leaves for abroad, she silently blesses him through prayer and a kiss. When he returns, she receives him lovingly. Their outward relationship changes, but their inner bond remains.

## 43. Exam-Ready Long Answer: A Photograph

A Photograph is a poem about memory, time, and loss. The poet looks at an old photograph of her mother as a young girl at the beach with two cousins. The photograph captures a happy childhood moment. Years later, the mother looks at the photograph and laughs at the memory of the beach holiday and their old-fashioned clothes. For the mother, the photograph recalls her lost childhood. For the poet, it recalls her mother, who is now dead. The sea in the photograph appears unchanged, while human life has passed away. The poem ends in silence because grief is too deep for words. Thus the poem shows how photographs preserve images but cannot prevent loss.

## 45. One-Page Exam Summary

The Portrait of a Lady is Khushwant Singh's affectionate memory of his grandmother. She is shown as old, wrinkled, bent, religious, loving, and spiritually beautiful. In the village, she and the narrator are close companions. She prepares him for school, accompanies him to the temple school, and feeds dogs. At university, their distance increases, but affection remains. She spends her time spinning, praying, and feeding sparrows. When he returns from abroad, she celebrates unusually by singing. Soon after, she dies peacefully while praying. The sparrows gather silently and refuse food, showing grief.

The sea remains almost unchanged, while human life passes away. The poem shows that photographs preserve images but cannot prevent loss. Both works show quiet love, memory, and the pain of losing someone deeply loved.
