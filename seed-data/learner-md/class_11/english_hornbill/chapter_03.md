## Part A: Discovering Tut

## 1. About the Prose Article

Discovering Tut: the Saga Continues is a journalistic article about the scientific investigation of King Tutankhamun's mummy.

The article connects:

- ancient Egyptian history
- archaeology
- tomb discovery
- modern medical imaging
- mystery of death
- respect for the dead
- the changing methods of archaeology

It is written in a descriptive and informative style. The article creates atmosphere through details of the desert, tomb, tourists, dust, wind, and technology.

## 2. Who Was King Tut?

King Tutankhamun, commonly called King Tut, was a young Egyptian pharaoh.

Important facts:

- he died in his late teens
- he was the last heir of a powerful dynasty
- he ruled for about nine years
- his tomb was discovered in 1922
- his death remained a mystery
- his tomb contained extraordinary treasures
- his mummy was scanned by CT in 2005

His fame comes partly from his young age, partly from the richness of his tomb, and partly from the mystery of his death.

## 3. Why King Tut Remains Important

King Tut is important because:

- his tomb was one of the richest royal collections ever found
- the tomb was surprisingly complete despite ancient robbery
- the discovery fascinated the modern world
- his death was sudden and unclear
- he belonged to a dynasty undergoing religious and political change
- modern science can reveal details that early archaeology could not

The article shows how the past continues to invite new questions.

## 4. Discovery by Howard Carter

Howard Carter discovered King Tut's tomb in 1922 after years of searching.

The tomb contained:

- gold artefacts
- nested coffins
- ceremonial objects
- jewellery
- amulets
- sandals
- finger and toe sheaths
- food and wine
- everyday objects for the afterlife

The discovery created a sensation because the objects were rich, beautiful, and historically valuable.

## 5. Why Carter's Method Was Criticized

Carter's methods are criticized because the mummy was damaged during his investigation.

The ritual resins had hardened and stuck the mummy to the coffin.

Carter tried to use heat from the sun to loosen the resins, but it did not work.

Finally, the hardened material had to be cut away.

To remove the gold ornaments, Carter's team separated major body parts and later reassembled the remains.

Modern archaeology finds this harsh, but Carter had limited options and less advanced technology. There was also fear that thieves would destroy the mummy to take the gold if the treasures were not removed.

## 6. Why Tut Was Buried with Treasures

Ancient Egyptian royals believed in the afterlife.

They thought the dead ruler would need objects in the next world.

That is why Tut was buried with:

- gold ornaments
- royal mask
- coffin
- jewellery
- amulets
- clothing
- food
- wine
- board games
- personal objects

The treasures were not merely decoration. They reflected religious beliefs about death, rebirth, protection, and royal status.

## 7. Akhenaten and Religious Change

Before Tut, Egypt went through major religious disturbance under Akhenaten.

Akhenaten:

- promoted worship of Aten, the sun disk
- changed his own name from Amenhotep IV to Akhenaten
- moved the religious capital to Akhetaten, now known as Amarna
- attacked the god Amun
- smashed Amun's images
- closed Amun's temples

These actions shocked Egypt because they disrupted older religious traditions.

## 8. Tutankhaten to Tutankhamun

The boy king first took the throne as Tutankhaten.

Later he changed his name to Tutankhamun.

This change was significant.

Tutankhaten was connected with Aten.

Tutankhamun means "living image of Amun."

The name change showed restoration of the old religious order and return to the worship of Amun.

## 9. Why Tut's Body Was Repeatedly Examined

Tut's body has been repeatedly examined because:

- he died young
- his death was sudden
- he was the last heir of his dynasty
- murder had been speculated
- his tomb and treasures made him famous
- early investigations damaged the body
- new technology promised better evidence

Each generation asks new questions and uses better tools.

## 10. The 1968 X-Ray

In 1968, an anatomy professor X-rayed the mummy.

The X-ray revealed that the breastbone and front ribs were missing.

This raised more questions about:

- the condition of the mummy
- damage caused after death
- possible injury
- Carter's handling
- the limits of earlier investigation

The X-ray was an advance, but it could not provide the detailed body images that CT technology later produced.

## 11. CT Scan in 2005

On 5 January 2005, King Tut's mummy was taken from the tomb for a CT scan.

The CT machine created about 1,700 digital cross-section images.

The scan helped specialists study:

- skull
- vertebrae
- rib cage
- bones
- body structure
- possible age clues
- possible death clues

The scan allowed a virtual view of the body without physically cutting or damaging it.

## 12. Why CT Technology Matters

CT technology matters because it is less destructive than earlier methods.

It allows scientists to:

- examine internal structures
- create three-dimensional images
- compare injuries and bone conditions
- support forensic reconstruction
- preserve the mummy physically
- use digital data for future study

This shows how archaeology has changed from treasure hunting to scientific investigation.

## 13. The Scanner Problem

During the scan, the CT machine stopped because sand entered a cooler fan.

Workers brought substitute fans and the procedure continued.

The incident adds tension to the narrative and also reminds readers that modern science still depends on practical conditions.

Some people jokingly connected the failure to the pharaoh's curse, but the real cause was mechanical.

## 14. Archaeology Then and Now

Earlier archaeology often focused on treasure and discovery.

Modern archaeology focuses more on:

- life details
- death mysteries
- scientific analysis
- preservation
- context
- culture
- ethics
- non-destructive technology

This shift is central to the article.

## 15. The Ending Image

The article ends with the constellation Orion over Tut's tomb.

Ancient Egyptians associated Orion with Osiris, the god of the afterlife.

This ending connects:

- science and myth
- modern technology and ancient belief
- death and afterlife
- the boy king and Egyptian cosmology

The image gives the article a solemn and poetic ending.

## 17. Understanding the Text: Reasons

### Q1. Why has King Tut's body been subjected to repeated scrutiny?

King Tut's body has been studied repeatedly because he died young and unexpectedly, and the cause of his death remained mysterious. His tomb was rich and famous, and there were even speculations about murder. Modern technology such as X-rays and CT scans offered new ways to study his body without the damage caused by earlier methods.

### Q2. Why was Howard Carter's investigation resented?

Carter's investigation was resented because his team damaged the mummy while separating it from the coffin and removing treasures. They cut body parts and reassembled the remains. Modern archaeologists consider this rough, though Carter worked with limited technology and under the threat of theft.

### Q3. Why did Carter have to chisel away the solidified resins?

The ritual resins used in burial had hardened and cemented the mummy to the bottom of the gold coffin. Heat from the sun failed to loosen them. Therefore, Carter had to cut away the hardened material to raise the king's remains.

### Q4. Why was Tut's body buried along with gilded treasures?

Tut was buried with gilded treasures because ancient Egyptians believed in an afterlife. Royals were buried with wealth, ornaments, food, clothing, and personal items that they might need in the next world. Gold also symbolized eternal brilliance and royal power.

### Q5. Why did the boy king change his name from Tutankhaten to Tutankhamun?

He changed his name to Tutankhamun to show the restoration of the old god Amun after Akhenaten had promoted Aten worship. The new name means "living image of Amun" and signals a return to traditional religious practices.

## 18. Understanding the Text: Further Answers

### Q1. What deeds led Ray Johnson to call Akhenaten unusual?

Akhenaten promoted the worship of Aten, changed his name, shifted the capital to Akhetaten, attacked the god Amun, smashed Amun's images, and closed Amun's temples. These actions disrupted traditional Egyptian religion and shocked the country.

### Q2. What were the results of the CT scan?

The CT scan produced about 1,700 digital cross-section images of Tut's body. It gave detailed views of the skull, vertebrae, rib cage, and other body parts. It provided data for forensic analysis and reconstruction without physically damaging the mummy.

### Q3. What advances in technology have improved forensic analysis?

Important advances include X-ray imaging, CT scanning, digital cross-section imaging, three-dimensional reconstruction, radiology, forensic science, anatomy-based analysis, and portable scanning equipment.

### Q4. Explain "King Tut is one of the first mummies to be scanned - in death, as in life."

In life, King Tut moved ahead of his countrymen as a pharaoh. In death, he again moved ahead by becoming one of the first Egyptian mummies to be scanned by modern CT technology. The statement is both literal and symbolic.

## 19. Thinking Points from the Prose

### Is scientific intervention necessary?

Scientific intervention is necessary when it helps us understand the past without causing unnecessary damage. It can solve mysteries, correct myths, and preserve historical knowledge.

### Does advanced technology give conclusive evidence?

Advanced technology gives better evidence, but not always final answers. Scientific data must still be interpreted carefully. Technology reduces uncertainty, but it does not remove every mystery.

### Should traditions and funerary practices be respected?

Yes. Archaeology must balance knowledge with respect. Human remains should not be treated only as objects. Cultural beliefs, burial customs, and dignity of the dead matter.

### Why is knowledge of the past useful?

The past helps us understand human society, belief systems, science, art, power, death rituals, and cultural change. It completes our understanding of the world we live in.

## 20. Language Extinction

Languages become extinct when people stop using them across generations.

Reasons include:

- migration
- colonisation
- dominance of powerful languages
- economic pressure
- schooling only in dominant languages
- lack of written records
- social shame attached to minority languages
- death or dispersal of small communities
- globalization and media pressure

## 21. Why Languages Should Be Preserved

Languages should be preserved because they carry:

- identity
- history
- oral traditions
- songs
- stories
- ecological knowledge
- cultural memory
- ways of thinking
- community belonging

When a language dies, a unique view of the world is lost.

## 22. How to Help Preserve Languages

Languages and dialects can be preserved by:

- teaching them to children
- using them at home
- publishing books in them
- recording oral histories
- creating dictionaries and grammars
- using them in songs, theatre, and media
- supporting mother-tongue education
- building digital archives
- respecting speakers instead of mocking accents or dialects

## Part B: The Laburnum Top

## 25. About the Poem

The Laburnum Top by Ted Hughes is a nature poem about a silent tree becoming suddenly alive when a goldfinch arrives.

The poem is short but full of sound, movement, colour, and energy.

It presents the tree and bird as part of one living system.

## 26. Central Idea of The Laburnum Top

At the beginning, the laburnum tree is silent and still in yellow September light.

Then a goldfinch arrives. The tree suddenly fills with sound and movement because the bird's young ones are inside.

The goldfinch feeds them, and the whole tree seems to tremble with life.

When the bird flies away, the tree becomes silent again.

The poem shows how one small bird can transform a quiet tree into a living, musical space.

## 27. Structure of the Poem

| Stage | Mood |
|---|---|
| Beginning | Silence, stillness, yellow light |
| Middle | Sudden movement, sound, energy |
| Ending | Silence and emptiness return |

The poem begins and ends in stillness. The middle is full of life.

This circular structure makes the bird's arrival feel dramatic.

## 28. The Goldfinch's Movement

The bird's movement is compared to a lizard.

The basis of comparison is:

- quickness
- sleek movement
- alertness
- suddenness
- smooth entry into the tree

The comparison helps readers visualize the bird entering the dense tree quickly and silently.

## 29. Image of the Engine

The poet uses the image of an engine because the bird's arrival starts a sudden machine-like burst of sound and movement.

The baby birds inside the tree begin making noises.

The tree seems to vibrate.

The mother bird feeds the young ones as if she is fueling an engine.

The image suggests energy, motion, and a hidden family system.

## 30. Meaning of "Her Barred Face Identity Mask"

The phrase refers to the markings on the goldfinch's face.

"Barred" suggests stripes or bands.

"Identity mask" suggests that these markings help identify the bird.

The phrase creates a vivid image of the goldfinch's face.

## 31. Sound and Movement Words

Sound words include:

- whistle
- whisperings

Movement words include:

- comes
- twitching
- enters
- starts
- trembles
- thrills

- flirts
- launches
- subsides

The dominant colour is yellow or gold.

## 32. Themes in The Laburnum Top

| Theme | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Nature's hidden life | A silent tree contains a lively bird family |
| Motherhood | The goldfinch feeds and energizes her young |
| Transformation | Stillness becomes motion when the bird arrives |
| Sound and silence | The poem contrasts quiet with sudden noise |
| Interdependence | The tree shelters the bird; the bird gives the tree life |

## 33. Answers: The Laburnum Top

### Q1. What do you notice about the beginning and ending of the poem?

The beginning and ending are both silent and still. At first, the tree is quiet before the goldfinch arrives. At the end, after the bird leaves, the tree becomes silent again. The middle of the poem is full of sound and movement.

### Q2. To what is the bird's movement compared? What is the basis?

The bird's movement is compared to that of a lizard. The basis is quickness, sleekness, alertness, and sudden movement.

### Q3. Why is the image of the engine used?

The image of the engine is used because the bird's arrival starts a burst of sound and motion inside the tree. The young birds become active, and the tree seems to tremble like a running machine.

### Q4. What do you like most about the poem?

A learner may answer personally. A strong answer can say that the best part is the sudden transformation of the silent tree into a lively space when the goldfinch arrives. The contrast between stillness and energy makes the poem vivid.

### Q5. What does "her barred face identity mask" mean?

It refers to the striped or marked face of the goldfinch. These markings help identify the bird and make her appearance vivid.

## Part C: The Voice of the Rain

## 34. About the Poem

The Voice of the Rain by Walt Whitman is a nature poem that personifies rain.

The speaker asks the rain who it is, and the rain answers.

Through the rain's answer, the poem explains the water cycle and presents rain as the "poem of Earth."

## 35. Central Idea of The Voice of the Rain

The poem presents rain as a life-giving force.

Rain rises invisibly from land and sea, forms clouds, returns to earth, washes dryness and dust, nourishes seeds, and gives life back to its origin.

The poem compares rain to a song. Just as a song rises from its birthplace, travels, fulfils its purpose, and returns with love, rain also returns to the earth after completing its cycle.

## 36. Voices in the Poem

There are two voices:

1. The voice of the poet or speaker.
2. The voice of the rain.

The speaker asks the rain who it is.

The rain replies by explaining its identity and movement.

This makes the poem conversational.

## 37. Meaning of "Strange to Tell"

The phrase means "strange to say" or "surprisingly."

It shows that the speaker finds it strange that the rain answers his question.

The phrase prepares the reader for personification.

## 38. Rain and Music

The poem draws a parallel between rain and music.

Both:

- arise from a place of origin
- travel outward
- perform a creative function
- return in some form
- bring beauty
- are connected with rhythm

Rain returns to earth and gives life.

A song returns to its birthplace with emotional fulfilment.

## 39. Cyclic Movement of Rain

The rain's movement matches the scientific water cycle.

Steps:

1. Water rises from land and sea through evaporation.
2. Vapour moves upward and forms clouds.
3. Clouds change form.
4. Rain falls back to earth.
5. Rain washes dust and nourishes seeds.
6. Water returns to land and sea.
7. The cycle continues.

The poem presents science in a poetic way.

## 40. Why the Last Lines Are in Brackets

The bracketed lines are a separate reflection.

They compare rain with song.

The brackets show that the poet is adding an explanatory or reflective comment without breaking the rain's main answer.

## 41. Pairs of Opposites in the Poem

Important opposites include:

- rise and descend
- day and night
- land and sea
- changed and same
- reckoned and unreckoned
- hidden and born

These opposites show the cycle and completeness of nature.

## 42. Prose Order of Poetic Sentences

Poetic sentence:

`And who art thou? said I to the soft-falling shower.`

Prose order:

I asked the soft-falling shower who it was.

Poetic sentence:

`I am the poem of Earth, said the voice of the rain.`

Prose order:

The voice of the rain said that it was the poem of Earth.

Poetic sentence:

`Eternal I rise.`

Prose order:

I rise eternally.

Poetic sentence:

`For song ... duly with love returns.`

Prose order:

A song returns with love to its birthplace after fulfilling its purpose, whether noticed or unnoticed.

## 43. Answers: The Voice of the Rain

### Q1. Who are the two voices in the poem?

The two voices are the speaker's voice and the rain's voice. The speaker asks the rain who it is, and the rain answers by explaining its nature and cycle.

### Q2. What does "strange to tell" mean?

It means "strange to say" or "surprisingly." It shows the unusual poetic idea that rain can answer the speaker's question.

### Q3. How are rain and music similar?

Rain and music are similar because both arise from a place of origin, travel outward, fulfil a purpose, and return. Rain rises from earth and sea, returns as showers, and gives life. A song comes from a singer or poet, travels among listeners, and returns with emotional fulfilment.

### Q4. How is the cyclic movement of rain shown?

The rain rises invisibly from land and sea, moves upward, forms clouds, falls back to earth, washes dryness and dust, nourishes seeds, and returns to its origin. This is similar to the scientific water cycle of evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and return flow.

### Q5. Why are the last two lines in brackets?

The last two lines are in brackets because they are an additional reflection comparing rain to song. They explain the main idea without being part of the direct answer of the rain.

### Q6. List pairs of opposites in the poem.

Pairs include rise and descend, day and night, land and sea, changed and same, hidden and born, and reckoned and unreckoned.

## 45. Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Saying Carter damaged the mummy out of carelessness only.

Correction: His method damaged the mummy, but he had limited technology and was trying to recover the remains and treasures before thieves could destroy them.

Mistake 2: Saying CT scan solved every mystery.

Correction: CT scans gave detailed data, but scientific evidence still needs interpretation.

Mistake 3: Saying Akhenaten and Tutankhamun followed the same religion.

Correction: Akhenaten promoted Aten; Tut restored Amun.

Mistake 4: Saying The Laburnum Top is only about a tree.

Correction: It is about the sudden arrival of life, sound, and movement through the goldfinch family.

Mistake 5: Saying The Voice of the Rain is only a science poem.

Correction: It explains the water cycle, but also presents rain as a creative, poetic, life-giving force.

Correction: Silence is important in both poems: the laburnum becomes silent again, and rain's voice emerges from a quiet natural encounter.

## 46. Exam-Ready Long Answer: Discovering Tut and Technology

Discovering Tut shows how modern technology has changed archaeology. Howard Carter discovered Tut's tomb in 1922, but his methods damaged the mummy because ritual resins had hardened and stuck the body to the coffin. Later, X-rays and CT scans allowed scientists to study the mummy more carefully. The CT scan in 2005 created about 1,700 digital images of Tut's body, giving specialists detailed views of bones and body structure. This helped forensic reconstruction and reduced the need for physical damage. The article shows that modern archaeology focuses not only on treasure but also on scientific understanding, preservation, and respect for the past.
