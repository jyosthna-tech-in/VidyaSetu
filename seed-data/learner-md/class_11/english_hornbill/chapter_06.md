## Part A: Silk Road

## 1. About the Travelogue

Silk Road is a travelogue by Nick Middleton.

A travelogue is a written account of a journey. It describes places, people, weather, roads, difficulties, culture, and personal responses.

This travelogue presents the narrator's journey through Tibet toward Mount Kailash. It combines:

- landscape description
- travel difficulty
- humour
- religious geography
- local culture
- high-altitude experience
- human encounters

## 2. Central Idea of Silk Road

The travelogue presents the physical and emotional experience of travelling through the Tibetan plateau toward Mount Kailash.

The narrator faces:

- high altitude
- snow-blocked roads
- rough terrain
- fierce Tibetan mastiffs
- cold and illness
- disappointing towns
- lack of fellow pilgrims
- communication barriers

Yet he also receives help from local people and eventually finds a companion, Norbu, for the pilgrimage.

## 3. Purpose of the Journey

The narrator is heading toward Mount Kailash to complete the kora.

Kora means a ritual circumambulation around a sacred mountain or religious site.

Mount Kailash is sacred in several traditions, especially Hinduism and Buddhism.

The journey is therefore both:

- physical travel through difficult terrain
- spiritual movement toward a sacred destination

## 4. Beginning at Ravu

The journey begins with a farewell from Ravu.

Lhamo gives the narrator a long-sleeved sheepskin coat because he is heading into colder regions.

This gesture shows kindness and the sensitive behaviour of hill people.

Tsetan, the driver, recognizes the narrator's new look and calls him like a drokba, meaning a nomad or herder.

## 5. Crossing the Changtang

Tsetan takes a shortcut across the Changtang toward Mount Kailash.

The route involves high mountain passes and uncertainty about snow.

The landscape includes:

- open plains
- arid pastures
- gazelles
- wild ass called kyang
- rocky wilderness
- nomads
- tents
- flocks
- Tibetan mastiffs

The travelogue creates a vast, remote, and wild atmosphere.

## 6. Tibetan Mastiffs

The narrator sees large Tibetan mastiffs guarding nomad tents.

They are fierce, fearless, and protective.

They rush toward the vehicle and force Tsetan to brake and swerve.

Tibetan mastiffs became popular in Chinese imperial courts as hunting dogs because of their size, strength, courage, and ferocity. They were taken to China along the Silk Road as tribute from Tibet.

## 7. Snow and High Passes

The travellers climb high mountain passes.

At one point, snow blocks the track.

The danger is not only the depth of snow but its icy surface. If the vehicle slips, it could overturn.

Tsetan tests the snow, throws dirt over the ice for grip, and drives carefully across.

At another blockage, he skillfully drives around the snow through rocks and slope.

This shows Tsetan's experience and calm judgment.

## 8. The High Pass

The narrator reaches a pass at about 5,515 metres.

The pass is marked by:

- cairn of rocks
- white silk scarves
- prayer flags
- strong sunlight
- low atmospheric pressure

The narrator suffers a headache because of the altitude.

The lower air pressure also affects the vehicle's fuel tank.

The scene combines danger, ritual, and geography.

## 9. Salt Flats and Tethys Ocean

After descending, the travellers pass salt flats and brackish lakes.

These are described as remains of the ancient Tethys Ocean.

The Tethys Ocean once existed before geological movements lifted Tibet upward.

This part of the travelogue connects present landscape with deep geological history.

## 10. Hor and Lake Manasarovar

The travellers reach Hor, near Lake Manasarovar.

The narrator finds Hor grim and disappointing:

- dusty
- rocky
- full of refuse
- without vegetation
- poorly maintained
- uncomfortable

This contrasts strongly with earlier travellers' accounts of Lake Manasarovar, which described deep spiritual emotion and beauty.

The contrast shows that sacred geography and real travel conditions can be very different.

## 11. Lake Manasarovar

Lake Manasarovar is sacred in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.

It is associated with the origin of great rivers:

- Indus
- Ganges
- Sutlej
- Brahmaputra

The travelogue notes that only the Sutlej actually flows from the lake, while the others rise nearby around Mount Kailash.

This mixture of myth, faith, and geography is important in the chapter.

## 12. Darchen and Illness

The narrator reaches Darchen late at night.

He suffers from:

- cold
- blocked nostril
- breathlessness
- chest heaviness when lying down
- fear of sleeping
- effects of altitude

He spends a sleepless night because he feels that if he sleeps, he may not wake up.

The next morning, Tsetan takes him to a Tibetan doctor.

## 13. Tibetan Medicine

The Tibetan doctor says the narrator has a cold and altitude effects.

He gives him a five-day course of medicine.

After the first full day of medicine, the narrator sleeps soundly.

This restores his confidence and changes his view of Darchen.

## 14. Tsetan's Support

Tsetan is extremely helpful.

He:

- drives skillfully through rough terrain
- handles snow blockages
- checks tyres and vehicle condition
- translates when needed
- takes the narrator to the doctor
- waits until he knows the narrator will recover
- provides practical reassurance

His humorous comment about death and business shows both Buddhist acceptance and practical concern.

## 15. Darchen Without Pilgrims

After recovering, the narrator still feels disappointed.

Darchen has no pilgrims because he has arrived too early in the season.

He does not want to do the kora alone.

He cannot easily get information because few people speak English.

He can only wait.

## 16. Meeting Norbu

The narrator meets Norbu in a cafe.

Norbu is:

- Tibetan
- an academic
- working in Beijing
- connected with ethnic literature
- interested in the Kailash kora
- able to speak English
- also planning to do the kora

The narrator feels relieved because Norbu may become a companion.

Although Norbu is not well-equipped physically, he has enthusiasm and knowledge.

## 17. Why the Positive Thinking Strategy Worked

The narrator had been trying to remain positive but felt discouraged because:

- he was ill
- there were no pilgrims
- he lacked information
- he did not want to go alone

Meeting Norbu changes the situation.

He now has a possible companion who understands the purpose of the journey and can communicate with him.

That is why the narrator feels his positive thinking may have worked.

## 18. Why the Title Silk Road Is Suitable

The title is suitable because the journey follows parts of the old trade route from Lhasa to Kashmir and evokes the historic Silk Road.

The text also refers to cultural and commercial exchanges:

- Tibetan mastiffs taken to Chinese imperial courts
- old trade routes
- movement of goods and people
- modern plastic goods as new trade items
- pilgrims and travellers across Tibet

The title connects the narrator's personal journey with a larger history of travel, trade, and cultural exchange.

## 19. Hill-Folk and Their Behaviour

The text shows hill people as practical, sensitive, and helpful.

Examples:

- Lhamo gives the narrator warm clothing.
- Tsetan drives carefully and helps him during illness.
- The Tibetan doctor treats him calmly.
- Drokbas live in harsh conditions with resilience.

Their kindness is not dramatic. It is practical and suited to the difficult environment.

## 20. Difficult Journeys and Human Motivation

People willingly undergo difficult journeys because of:

- faith
- pilgrimage
- adventure
- self-discovery
- curiosity
- spiritual discipline
- love of travel
- cultural learning
- desire to test oneself

In Silk Road, the journey is both physical and spiritual.

## 21. Legends and Reality

The chapter contrasts legendary or sacred images with real travel experience.

Lake Manasarovar is sacred and described emotionally by earlier travellers.

But the narrator's actual experience at Hor is unpleasant because of dust, rubbish, lack of vegetation, and poor facilities.

This contrast makes the travelogue honest and realistic.

## 22. Language Questions: Answers

### Q1. What kind of English does Tsetan use and how did he pick it up?

Tsetan uses simple, functional English. He probably picked it up through repeated contact with tourists, travellers, pilgrims, and guides. His English is not grammatically perfect, but it is effective for communication.

### Q2. What does "through Daniel" indicate?

It indicates that Daniel acted as an interpreter between the narrator and Lhamo. They did not share enough common language to speak directly.

### Q3. What does "through Tsetan" indicate?

It indicates that Tsetan translated the Tibetan doctor's words for the narrator. This shows the multilingual situation of the journey.

## 27. Understanding the Text: Answers

### Q1. Why is the article titled Silk Road?

The title is suitable because the narrator travels along a region connected with the old trade route from Lhasa to Kashmir. The text also refers to old exchanges, such as Tibetan mastiffs being taken to Chinese courts, and modern trade items moving across the region. The journey is part of a long tradition of travel, trade, pilgrimage, and cultural contact.

### Q2. Why were Tibetan mastiffs popular in China's imperial courts?

Tibetan mastiffs were fierce, strong, fearless dogs. They guarded nomad tents and could chase vehicles aggressively. Their power made them valuable as hunting dogs in China's imperial courts.

### Q3. Why was the author's experience at Hor in contrast to earlier accounts?

Earlier travellers were deeply moved by Lake Manasarovar's sanctity and beauty. But the narrator found Hor dusty, rocky, dirty, without vegetation, and full of rubbish. The sacred lake was nearby, but the town itself was unpleasant.

### Q4. Why was the author disappointed with Darchen?

He was disappointed because there were no pilgrims, he had arrived too early in the season, he did not want to do the kora alone, he lacked information, and few people could speak English. His illness also made the situation worse.

### Q5. Why did the author feel his positive thinking worked?

He felt so because he unexpectedly met Norbu, a Tibetan academic who also wanted to do the kora and could speak English. Norbu became a possible companion and solved the narrator's problem of being alone.

## 28. Brief Comments

The narrator's purpose is to complete the kora around Mount Kailash, a sacred pilgrimage route.

## Part B: Father to Son

## 29. About the Poem

Father to Son by Elizabeth Jennings is a poem about the emotional distance between a father and his son.

The father speaks with sadness and confusion.

Although they have lived in the same house for years, he feels he does not understand his son.

The poem expresses the universal pain of generation gap.

## 30. Central Idea of Father to Son

The poem explores a broken or strained relationship between father and son.

The father wants to rebuild the relationship, but he does not know how.

He remembers the son as a small child, but the grown son has his own world.

Both father and son are hurt, angry, and longing for forgiveness.

The poem suggests that love may still exist, but communication has failed.

## 31. Father's Helplessness

The father's helplessness is shown through:

- his inability to understand his son
- his attempt to build a relationship from old memories
- his feeling that the son has become a stranger
- silence between them
- his inability to share what the son loves
- his longing for the son to return emotionally
- both of them reaching out with empty hands

The father wants closeness but cannot create it alone.

## 32. Meaning of "The Land Is His and None of Mine"

This phrase suggests that the son has his own independent emotional and mental world.

The father may have given him life, but he does not control the son's inner world.

The son belongs to himself.

The father feels excluded from the son's interests, choices, and identity.

## 33. Distance Between Father and Son

Distance is shown through phrases and ideas such as:

- not understanding the child
- living in the same house but knowing nothing of him
- speaking like strangers
- no sign of understanding
- not sharing what the son loves
- silence surrounding them
- empty hands reaching for forgiveness

The distance is emotional, not physical.

## 34. Universal Nature of the Poem

The poem is not only about one father and one son.

It is universal because many families experience:

- generation gap
- lack of communication
- different values
- emotional silence
- parental regret
- children's independence
- desire for forgiveness

The poem speaks to a common human experience.

## 35. Rhyme Scheme

The poem does not have a fully consistent rhyme scheme.

There are some rhyming or near-rhyming sounds, but the pattern is not regular throughout.

The irregular rhyme suits the emotional disorder in the relationship.

## 36. Answers: Father to Son

### Q1. Is the poem personal or universal?

The poem may arise from a personal experience, but it is fairly universal. Many parents and children experience emotional distance, misunderstanding, silence, anger, and the desire for forgiveness.

### Q2. How is the father's helplessness brought out?

The father admits that he does not understand his son despite living in the same house. He tries to rebuild the relationship from memories of the son's childhood. He cannot share what the son loves, and silence surrounds them. His empty hand shows his longing and helplessness.

### Q3. Identify phrases that indicate distance.

Important phrases include "speak like strangers", "no sign of understanding", "what he loves I cannot share", "Silence surrounds us", and "empty hand." These phrases show emotional distance.

### Q4. Does the poem have a consistent rhyme scheme?

No. The poem has some rhymes and near-rhymes, but no consistent rhyme scheme. The irregular pattern reflects the broken relationship and emotional unease.
