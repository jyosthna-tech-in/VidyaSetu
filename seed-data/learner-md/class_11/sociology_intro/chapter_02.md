## Chapter Overview

Sociology studies familiar things in unfamiliar ways. Because family, caste, school, class, friendship, and rules are already part of everyday life, we may think we understand them fully. Sociological concepts help us see them precisely.

Concepts are tools. They sharpen observation and allow comparison across societies and situations.

## 1. Why Concepts Matter

Sociology needs concepts because everyday words are often vague.

Example: the word "family" may mean nuclear family, joint family, single-parent family, chosen family, or extended kin network.

A concept helps us:

1. Define clearly.
2. Compare cases.
3. Identify patterns.
4. Avoid common-sense confusion.
5. Build explanations.
6. Connect evidence to theory.

## 2. Social Group

A social group is a collection of persons who interact, share some sense of belonging, and are recognised as a group.

Features:

1. Interaction.
2. Shared identity.
3. Norms.
4. Roles.
5. Continuity.
6. Mutual expectations.

Examples:

1. Family.
2. Classmates.
3. Workers' union.
4. Caste group.
5. Sports team.
6. Religious congregation.

## 3. Aggregates and Categories

An aggregate is a collection of people in the same place but without meaningful interaction.

Example: people waiting at a bus stop.

A category is a collection of people sharing a characteristic but not necessarily interacting.

Example: all people aged 15-19 in a city.

A social group requires more than physical closeness or shared label.

## 4. Primary Group

A primary group is small, intimate, and emotionally close.

Features:

1. Face-to-face interaction.
2. Emotional depth.
3. Long-term connection.
4. Personal knowledge.
5. Strong influence on identity.

Examples:

1. Family.
2. Close friends.
3. Neighbourhood play group.

## 5. Secondary Group

A secondary group is larger, more formal, and goal-oriented.

Features:

1. Impersonal relations.
2. Formal rules.
3. Specific purpose.
4. Limited emotional intimacy.
5. Membership may change.

Examples:

1. School.
2. Office.
3. Political party.
4. Corporation.
5. University.

## 6. Community and Association

Community usually refers to a group with shared life, belonging, and often locality or identity.

Association refers to a group formed for a specific purpose.

| Community | Association |
|---|---|
| Based on shared life or identity | Based on specific goal |
| Usually more enduring | May be temporary or formal |
| Emotional belonging is stronger | Instrumental purpose is stronger |
| Example: village community | Example: student union |

## 7. Status

Status is the social position a person occupies.

Examples:

1. Student.
2. Teacher.
3. Parent.
4. Sibling.
5. Doctor.
6. Citizen.
7. Member of a caste.

### Ascribed Status

Ascribed status is given by birth or involuntary position.

Examples: caste, age, sex assigned at birth, kinship position.

### Achieved Status

Achieved status is acquired through effort, qualification, or action.

Examples: graduate, athlete, elected representative, engineer.

## 8. Role

Role refers to the expected behaviour attached to a status.

Example: a teacher is expected to teach, guide, evaluate, and maintain classroom order.

A person occupies many statuses and performs many roles.

## 9. Role Conflict and Role Strain

Role conflict occurs when expectations from different roles clash.

Example: a working parent must attend an office meeting and a child's school event at the same time.

Role strain occurs when expectations within the same role are difficult to manage.

Example: a teacher must complete syllabus, support weak students, maintain discipline, and do administrative work.

## 10. Norms

Norms are shared rules of behaviour.

Types:

1. Folkways: everyday customs.
2. Mores: strongly held moral rules.
3. Laws: formal rules enforced by the state.

Norms guide behaviour and create expectations.

## 11. Values

Values are ideas about what is desirable, good, proper, or important.

Examples:

1. Equality.
2. Respect.
3. Success.
4. Family honour.
5. Freedom.
6. Non-violence.

Norms often express values in practical rules.

## 12. Social Stratification

Social stratification refers to structured inequality between groups.

It may be based on:

1. Caste.
2. Class.
3. Gender.
4. Race.
5. Ethnicity.
6. Tribe.
7. Age.

Stratification affects access to wealth, power, status, education, and dignity.

## 13. Social Control

Social control refers to ways society regulates behaviour and maintains norms.

### Informal Social Control

Examples:

1. Praise.
2. Ridicule.
3. Gossip.
4. Family pressure.
5. Peer approval.
6. Shame.

### Formal Social Control

Examples:

1. Law.
2. Police.
3. Courts.
4. School rules.
5. Workplace regulations.

Social control can maintain order, but it can also preserve injustice.

## 14. Deviance

Deviance refers to behaviour that violates accepted norms.

What counts as deviant varies across societies and times.

Example: inter-caste marriage may be punished in some conservative settings but respected as a right in constitutional terms.

Sociology asks who defines deviance and whose power is protected by that definition.

## 15. Social Change and Concepts

Statuses, roles, norms, and values are not fixed forever.

They change through:

1. Education.
2. Law.
3. Social movements.
4. Economic change.
5. Migration.
6. Technology.
7. Media.
8. Political struggle.

Concepts help trace these changes.

## 16. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Concept | Analytical tool for understanding social life |
| Social group | People who interact and share belonging |
| Primary group | Small intimate group |
| Secondary group | Formal goal-oriented group |
| Community | Group with shared life or belonging |
| Association | Group formed for a specific purpose |
| Status | Social position |
| Role | Expected behaviour linked to status |
| Norm | Shared rule of behaviour |
| Value | Shared idea of what is desirable |
| Social control | Regulation of behaviour |
| Deviance | Behaviour violating norms |

## 17. Exam-Oriented Summary

Sociological concepts help us study familiar social life precisely. Groups, communities, associations, statuses, roles, norms, values, stratification, social control, and deviance are basic tools for understanding how society is organised.

Social life is patterned through statuses and roles. Norms and values guide behaviour, while social control enforces conformity. Stratification creates structured inequality. Deviance shows that norms are socially defined and can be challenged.
