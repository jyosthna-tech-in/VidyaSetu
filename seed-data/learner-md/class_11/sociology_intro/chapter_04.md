## Chapter Overview

Culture is the learned way of life of a group. It includes language, values, norms, symbols, beliefs, knowledge, technology, art, rituals, and everyday practices. Human beings are not born with culture; they learn it through socialisation.

Socialisation is the process through which individuals become members of society. It teaches us how to speak, behave, feel, judge, cooperate, and identify ourselves.

## 1. Meaning of Culture

Culture refers to the shared, learned, and symbolic patterns of life of a group or society.

It includes:

1. Language.
2. Food habits.
3. Dress.
4. Music.
5. Values.
6. Norms.
7. Rituals.
8. Beliefs.
9. Technology.
10. Art.
11. Ideas of beauty, respect, purity, honour, and success.

Culture is not inherited biologically. It is learned socially.

## 2. Features of Culture

Culture is:

1. Learned.
2. Shared.
3. Symbolic.
4. Transmitted across generations.
5. Dynamic.
6. Diverse.
7. Connected with power.
8. Both material and non-material.

Culture gives meaning to behaviour.

## 3. Material Culture

Material culture includes physical objects created and used by society.

Examples:

1. Tools.
2. Houses.
3. Clothing.
4. Machines.
5. Books.
6. Roads.
7. Phones.
8. Utensils.
9. Vehicles.
10. Religious objects.

Material culture reflects social values and technologies.

## 4. Non-Material Culture

Non-material culture includes ideas, values, beliefs, norms, language, and symbols.

Examples:

1. Respect for elders.
2. Ideas of purity.
3. Religious beliefs.
4. Gender norms.
5. Moral codes.
6. Marriage rules.
7. National symbols.
8. Concepts of honour.

Non-material culture guides behaviour.

## 5. Dimensions of Culture

Culture may be understood through three dimensions.

### Cognitive Dimension

Knowledge and beliefs about the world.

Example: classification of plants, ideas about health, religious cosmology, scientific knowledge.

### Normative Dimension

Rules, values, and expectations.

Example: manners, marriage rules, gender expectations, ideas of right and wrong.

### Material Dimension

Objects and technologies.

Example: tools, buildings, machines, clothing.

## 6. Symbols

Symbols are things that carry meaning beyond themselves.

Examples:

1. Flag.
2. Wedding ring.
3. Religious sign.
4. School uniform.
5. Gesture.
6. Language word.

Symbols make culture communicable.

## 7. Language

Language is one of the most important parts of culture.

It allows people to:

1. Communicate.
2. Teach.
3. Remember.
4. Classify.
5. Imagine.
6. Transmit culture.
7. Build identity.

Language also carries power because some languages or accents may be valued more than others.

## 8. Norms and Values

Values are ideas about what is good, desirable, or proper.

Norms are rules that express values in behaviour.

Example:

Value: respect for elders.  
Norm: greet elders politely.

Norms may be formal or informal.

## 9. Customs, Mores, and Laws

Customs are established patterns of behaviour.

Mores are strongly held moral norms.

Laws are formal rules enforced by the state.

All three regulate behaviour, but their enforcement differs.

## 10. Cultural Diversity

Cultures vary across regions, classes, castes, religions, tribes, genders, and generations.

Examples:

1. Food practices.
2. Marriage rituals.
3. Languages.
4. Festivals.
5. Dress.
6. Family forms.
7. Work patterns.

Sociology studies this diversity without assuming that one culture is naturally superior.

## 11. Ethnocentrism

Ethnocentrism means judging other cultures by the standards of one's own culture.

It can produce:

1. Prejudice.
2. Stereotypes.
3. Cultural superiority.
4. Misunderstanding.
5. Discrimination.

## 12. Cultural Relativism

Cultural relativism means understanding a culture in its own context before judging it.

It encourages:

1. Empathy.
2. Careful interpretation.
3. Comparative understanding.
4. Avoidance of quick judgment.

But cultural relativism does not require accepting practices that violate dignity or rights. It asks for understanding before evaluation.

## 13. Socialisation

Socialisation is the process through which individuals learn culture and become members of society.

It teaches:

1. Language.
2. Norms.
3. Values.
4. Roles.
5. Gender expectations.
6. Discipline.
7. Identity.
8. Social skills.

Socialisation is lifelong.

## 14. Primary Socialisation

Primary socialisation occurs in early childhood, mainly in the family.

It teaches basic language, emotions, bodily habits, trust, attachment, and early norms.

It is deeply influential because it shapes early personality and identity.

## 15. Secondary Socialisation

Secondary socialisation occurs later through institutions beyond the family.

Agencies include:

1. School.
2. Peer group.
3. Workplace.
4. Media.
5. Religious organisations.
6. State institutions.
7. Digital platforms.

It prepares people for specialised roles.

## 16. Agencies of Socialisation

### Family

Teaches language, care, gender roles, discipline, and early values.

### Peer Group

Teaches cooperation, competition, friendship, style, and youth culture.

### School

Teaches formal knowledge, discipline, citizenship, time routines, and hidden curriculum.

### Media

Shapes aspirations, consumption, identity, gender images, and political awareness.

### Workplace

Teaches professional roles, hierarchy, punctuality, teamwork, and authority.

## 17. Culture and Power

Culture is not always shared equally.

Dominant groups may define their culture as superior or normal.

Examples:

1. Standard language over dialects.
2. Elite taste over popular taste.
3. Patriarchal norms over gender equality.
4. Dominant caste practices over marginalised cultures.

Culture can be a field of struggle.

## 18. Cultural Change

Culture changes through:

1. Migration.
2. Technology.
3. Education.
4. Social movements.
5. Globalisation.
6. Law.
7. Media.
8. Inter-cultural contact.
9. Generational conflict.

Change may be fast in some areas and slow in others.

## 20. Exam-Oriented Summary

Culture is the learned, shared, symbolic way of life of a group. It has material and non-material dimensions and includes values, norms, symbols, language, beliefs, and technology. Culture is diverse, dynamic, and connected with power.

Socialisation is the process through which individuals learn culture and become members of society. Family, peers, school, media, workplace, and other institutions act as agencies of socialisation. Sociology studies culture comparatively and avoids unreflective ethnocentrism.
