## Chapter Overview

Sociology is not only thinking about society. It is systematic investigation of society. Research methods help sociologists move beyond common sense by collecting evidence carefully.

Different questions require different methods. A study of income inequality may need survey data. A study of rituals may need fieldwork and observation. A study of life histories may need interviews.

## 1. Why Research Methods Matter

Research methods matter because they:

1. Make claims evidence-based.
2. Reduce bias.
3. Allow others to check findings.
4. Help compare groups.
5. Reveal hidden patterns.
6. Connect theory with data.
7. Distinguish sociology from common sense.

Without method, sociology becomes opinion.

## 2. Research Question

A research question is the specific question a study tries to answer.

Good research questions are:

1. Clear.
2. Focused.
3. Researchable.
4. Sociological.
5. Linked to concepts.
6. Open to evidence.

Example: "How does access to online classes differ by class background among Class 11 students?"

## 3. Steps in Sociological Research

Common steps:

1. Choose a topic.
2. Frame a research question.
3. Review existing knowledge.
4. Select concepts.
5. Choose method.
6. Select sample or field site.
7. Collect data.
8. Analyse data.
9. Interpret findings.
10. Present results.
11. Reflect on limitations.

## 4. Quantitative Methods

Quantitative methods use numbers.

Examples:

1. Census.
2. Surveys.
3. Structured questionnaires.
4. Statistical tables.
5. Percentages.
6. Graphs.

They are useful for studying scale, distribution, comparison, and trends.

## 5. Qualitative Methods

Qualitative methods study meanings, experiences, processes, and context.

Examples:

1. Interviews.
2. Participant observation.
3. Field notes.
4. Life histories.
5. Case studies.
6. Ethnography.

They are useful for depth and interpretation.

## 6. Survey Method

A survey collects information from many people through standardised questions.

It is useful for:

1. Attitudes.
2. Demographic patterns.
3. Income.
4. Education.
5. Occupation.
6. Access to services.

### Strengths

1. Covers many respondents.
2. Allows comparison.
3. Produces numerical data.
4. Useful for policy.

### Limitations

1. Answers may be superficial.
2. People may misreport.
3. Questions may bias responses.
4. It may miss meaning and context.

## 7. Questionnaire

A questionnaire is a set of questions used to collect data.

Questions may be:

1. Closed-ended: fixed options.
2. Open-ended: respondent answers in own words.

Good questionnaires use clear language and avoid leading questions.

## 8. Interview

An interview is a guided conversation for research.

Types:

1. Structured.
2. Semi-structured.
3. Unstructured.

Interviews help understand meanings, memories, emotions, and explanations.

## 9. Observation

Observation means watching social behaviour systematically.

Types:

1. Participant observation: researcher joins the setting.
2. Non-participant observation: researcher observes from outside.

Observation is useful for studying what people do, not only what they say.

## 10. Fieldwork

Fieldwork means spending time in the social setting being studied.

It may involve:

1. Observation.
2. Interviews.
3. Informal conversation.
4. Mapping.
5. Participation.
6. Field notes.

Fieldwork helps sociologists understand everyday life from within its context.

## 11. Case Study

A case study is an intensive study of one person, group, village, institution, movement, or event.

It provides detail and depth.

Limitation: findings may not be easily generalised to all cases.

## 12. Sampling

Sampling means selecting a smaller group from a larger population for study.

Important types:

1. Random sampling.
2. Stratified sampling.
3. Purposive sampling.
4. Snowball sampling.

Good sampling depends on the research question.

## 13. Objectivity

Objectivity means trying to reduce personal bias and represent evidence fairly.

It does not mean the researcher has no values. It means the researcher must not twist evidence to fit personal beliefs.

Objectivity is supported by:

1. Clear method.
2. Accurate records.
3. Transparent reasoning.
4. Openness to criticism.
5. Careful comparison.

## 14. Reflexivity

Reflexivity means the researcher reflects on their own position and its effect on research.

A researcher's caste, class, gender, language, age, and political views may affect access, trust, interpretation, and interaction.

Reflexivity makes research more honest.

## 15. Ethics in Sociological Research

Ethical research requires:

1. Informed consent.
2. Privacy.
3. Confidentiality.
4. No unnecessary harm.
5. Respect for participants.
6. Honest reporting.
7. Sensitivity to vulnerable groups.
8. Avoiding exploitation.

People being studied are not objects; they are social persons with dignity.

## 16. Comparing Methods

| Method | Best For | Main Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Survey | Large-scale patterns | Limited depth |
| Interview | Meanings and experiences | Time-consuming |
| Observation | Actual behaviour | Observer effect |
| Fieldwork | Context and everyday life | Hard to generalise |
| Case study | Detail of one case | Limited representativeness |
| Census/data analysis | Population trends | May miss lived meanings |

## 17. Combining Methods

Sociologists often combine methods.

Example: to study school inequality, a researcher may use:

1. Survey of students.
2. Interviews with teachers.
3. Observation of classrooms.
4. Analysis of school records.

Combining methods gives a fuller picture.

## 18. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Research method | Systematic way of collecting evidence |
| Research question | Specific question guiding study |
| Survey | Standardised data collection from many respondents |
| Questionnaire | Written or printed set of questions |
| Interview | Guided research conversation |
| Observation | Systematic watching of behaviour |
| Fieldwork | Research in a natural social setting |
| Sampling | Selecting cases from a larger population |
| Objectivity | Fairness toward evidence |
| Reflexivity | Awareness of researcher's own position |

## 19. Exam-Oriented Summary

Sociological research uses systematic methods to study society. The research process begins with a clear question and moves through method selection, data collection, analysis, interpretation, and reflection.

Quantitative methods use numerical data, while qualitative methods study meanings and contexts. Surveys, questionnaires, interviews, observation, fieldwork, case studies, and sampling are important tools. Objectivity, reflexivity, and ethics are essential for responsible research.
