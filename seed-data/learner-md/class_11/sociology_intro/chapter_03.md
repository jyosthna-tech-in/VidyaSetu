## Chapter Overview

Social institutions are established patterns of behaviour organised around basic social needs. They shape how people marry, form families, work, worship, learn, govern, inherit, and cooperate.

Institutions are not natural or identical everywhere. They are social arrangements that vary across culture, history, class, caste, gender, religion, and region.

## 1. Meaning of Social Institution

A social institution is a stable and organised pattern of norms, roles, statuses, and practices that fulfils important social functions.

Examples:

1. Family.
2. Marriage.
3. Kinship.
4. Economy.
5. Polity.
6. Religion.
7. Education.
8. Law.

Institutions give predictability to social life.

## 2. Why Institutions Matter

Institutions:

1. Organise social relationships.
2. Define roles and responsibilities.
3. Regulate behaviour.
4. Transmit culture.
5. Distribute resources.
6. Produce cooperation.
7. Produce and reproduce inequality.
8. Provide identity and belonging.

Institutions are both enabling and constraining.

## 3. Family

Family is a social group connected by marriage, blood, adoption, or care.

Functions often include:

1. Socialisation of children.
2. Emotional support.
3. Care of dependents.
4. Regulation of reproduction.
5. Transmission of property.
6. Formation of identity.
7. Cooperation in daily life.

Family forms vary widely.

## 4. Types of Family

Common forms include:

1. Nuclear family: parents and children.
2. Joint family: multiple generations and related households.
3. Extended family: relatives beyond the nuclear unit.
4. Single-parent family.
5. Matrilineal or patrilineal family patterns.

No single family form is universal.

## 5. Marriage

Marriage is a socially recognised relationship that regulates sexual relations, reproduction, inheritance, alliance, and family formation.

Marriage rules may vary by:

1. Caste.
2. Clan.
3. Religion.
4. Region.
5. Class.
6. Gender norms.

Marriage is personal, but it is also deeply social.

## 6. Forms and Rules of Marriage

Important concepts:

1. Monogamy: one spouse at a time.
2. Polygyny: one man with multiple wives.
3. Polyandry: one woman with multiple husbands.
4. Endogamy: marriage within a group.
5. Exogamy: marriage outside a prohibited group.

In India, caste endogamy has historically been important, while clan exogamy is also found in many communities.

## 7. Kinship

Kinship refers to socially recognised relationships based on blood, marriage, adoption, or descent.

Kinship decides:

1. Who counts as a relative.
2. Inheritance.
3. Marriage rules.
4. Ritual duties.
5. Support networks.
6. Authority relations.

Kinship systems differ across societies.

## 8. Economy as Institution

The economy organises production, distribution, exchange, and consumption.

Economic institutions include:

1. Property.
2. Labour.
3. Markets.
4. Money.
5. Occupations.
6. Firms.
7. Land relations.

Sociology studies how economy is embedded in social relations.

## 9. Work and Division of Labour

Work is organised through division of labour.

Division of labour may be based on:

1. Skill.
2. Gender.
3. Age.
4. Caste.
5. Class.
6. Education.
7. Technology.

Sociology asks whether this division is fair, flexible, and dignified.

## 10. Polity

Polity refers to institutions of power, authority, governance, and decision making.

Examples:

1. State.
2. Government.
3. Panchayat.
4. Political parties.
5. Bureaucracy.
6. Courts.
7. Social movements.

The polity decides how power is organised and legitimised.

## 11. Power and Authority

Power is the ability to influence or control others.

Authority is power considered legitimate.

Examples:

1. A teacher has authority in a classroom.
2. A court has legal authority.
3. A village elder may have customary authority.

Authority depends on social recognition.

## 12. Religion

Religion includes beliefs, rituals, symbols, sacred ideas, and communities of worship.

Sociology does not ask whether a religion is true or false. It asks how religion works in society.

It studies:

1. Rituals.
2. Beliefs.
3. Religious organisations.
4. Social solidarity.
5. Conflict.
6. Identity.
7. Hierarchy.
8. Reform.

## 13. Education

Education is a social institution that transmits knowledge, skills, values, and social norms.

It can:

1. Promote mobility.
2. Create discipline.
3. Spread literacy.
4. Transmit culture.
5. Build citizenship.
6. Reproduce inequality.
7. Prepare people for work.

Schools are not only places of learning. They are social institutions.

## 14. Institutions and Inequality

Institutions can produce inequality.

Examples:

1. Family may reproduce gender roles.
2. Marriage may preserve caste boundaries.
3. Economy may produce class inequality.
4. Education may favour privileged groups.
5. Religion may justify hierarchy.
6. Polity may exclude some groups from power.

Institutions can also be sites of reform and resistance.

## 15. Institutions and Change

Institutions change due to:

1. Law.
2. Social movements.
3. Economic shifts.
4. Education.
5. Urbanisation.
6. Migration.
7. Technology.
8. Inter-group contact.

Change is often uneven. Old and new forms may coexist.

## 16. Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Social institution | Stable pattern of norms and roles organised around social needs |
| Family | Group connected by kinship, marriage, adoption, or care |
| Marriage | Socially recognised union regulating family and reproduction |
| Kinship | Socially recognised relation based on descent, marriage, or adoption |
| Economy | Institution of production, distribution, and consumption |
| Polity | Institution of power and governance |
| Religion | Beliefs and practices around sacred meanings |
| Education | Institution for learning, socialisation, and certification |
| Authority | Legitimate power |

## 17. Exam-Oriented Summary

Social institutions organise social life through stable norms, roles, and practices. Family, marriage, kinship, economy, polity, religion, and education meet social needs but also produce power relations and inequalities.

Institutions vary across societies and change over time. Sociology studies institutions empirically, asking how they function, how they regulate behaviour, how they reproduce inequality, and how they become sites of change.
