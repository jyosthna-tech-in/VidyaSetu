## Learning Purpose

This chapter teaches how to work with statements involving "less than", "greater than", "at most", and "at least".

Equations describe exact equality.

Inequalities describe limits, ranges, restrictions, and possible values.

Many real-life problems are naturally inequalities: budgets, marks, temperatures, mixtures, capacities, dimensions, and minimum or maximum requirements.

The main skill is to convert a condition into an inequality, solve it correctly, and represent the solution on a number line or graph.

## Core Learning Outcomes

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

1. Distinguish equations from inequalities.
2. Recognise the symbols `<`, `>`, `<=`, and `>=`.
3. Identify strict and non-strict inequalities.
4. Understand numerical, literal, and double inequalities.
5. Solve linear inequalities in one variable.
6. Know when the inequality sign reverses.
7. Write solution sets in roster form, interval form, and set-builder form.
8. Represent solutions on a number line.
9. Solve compound inequalities.
10. Solve systems of inequalities.
11. Translate word problems into inequalities.
12. Solve average, consecutive-integer, temperature, mixture, and length problems.
13. Understand the idea of half-planes for inequalities in two variables.

## 1. Equations Versus Inequalities

An equation contains equality.

Example:

$$
2x + 3 = 9
$$

An inequality contains comparison.

Examples:

$$
2x + 3 < 9
$$

$$
2x + 3 \ge 9
$$

In an equation, the solution often gives exact values.

In an inequality, the solution is usually a range of values.

## 2. Meaning Of Inequality Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `<` | less than |
| `>` | greater than |
| `<=` | less than or equal to |
| `>=` | greater than or equal to |

Examples:

$$
3 < 5
$$

$$
7 > 5
$$

$$
x \le 4
$$

$$
y \ge 3
$$

## 3. Strict And Non-Strict Inequalities

Strict inequalities use `<` or `>`.

Examples:

$$
x < 5
$$

$$
3x + 2 > 8
$$

The boundary value is not included.

Non-strict inequalities use `<=` or `>=`.

Examples:

$$
x \le 5
$$

$$
3x + 2 \ge 8
$$

The boundary value is included.

## 4. Numerical, Literal, And Double Inequalities

Numerical inequalities compare numbers:

$$
3 < 5
$$

Literal inequalities involve variables:

$$
x < 5
$$

Double inequalities compare a variable between two bounds:

$$
3 < x < 5
$$

This means:

$$
x > 3
$$

and:

$$
x < 5
$$

Another example:

$$
-1 \le x < 4
$$

means:

$$
x \ge -1
$$

and:

$$
x < 4
$$

## 5. Linear Inequalities

A linear inequality in one variable has the form:

$$
ax + b < 0
$$

or:

$$
ax + b > 0
$$

or:

$$
ax + b \le 0
$$

or:

$$
ax + b \ge 0
$$

where:

$$
a \ne 0
$$

Examples:

$$
5x - 3 < 7
$$

$$
4x + 3 \ge 6x + 7
$$

A linear inequality in two variables has the form:

$$
ax + by < c
$$

or similar forms with `>`, `<=`, or `>=`.

## 6. Solution Of An Inequality

A solution of an inequality is a value of the variable that makes the inequality true.

Example:

$$
30x < 200
$$

If:

$$
x = 6
$$

then:

$$
30(6) = 180 < 200
$$

so 6 is a solution.

If:

$$
x = 7
$$

then:

$$
30(7) = 210
$$

and:

$$
210 < 200
$$

is false.

So 7 is not a solution.

## 7. Main Rules For Solving Inequalities

Rule 1:

The same number may be added to or subtracted from both sides without changing the inequality sign.

Example:

$$
x - 4 < 7
$$

Add 4:

$$
x < 11
$$

Rule 2:

Both sides may be multiplied or divided by the same positive number without changing the sign.

Example:

$$
3x < 12
$$

Divide by 3:

$$
x < 4
$$

Rule 3:

When multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number, reverse the inequality sign.

Example:

$$
-2x < 6
$$

Divide by `-2` and reverse the sign:

$$
x > -3
$$

This is the most important rule in the chapter.

## 8. Why The Sign Reverses

We know:

$$
3 > 2
$$

Multiply both sides by `-1`:

$$
-3 < -2
$$

The order reverses.

This happens because multiplication by a negative number reflects points across zero on the number line.

## 9. Solution Sets In Different Number Systems

The same inequality can have different solution sets depending on the allowed values of x.

Example:

$$
30x < 200
$$

Solving:

$$
x < \frac{20}{3}
$$

If x is a natural number:

$$
\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}
$$

If x is an integer:

$$
\{..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}
$$

If x is a real number:

$$
(-\infty, 20/3)
$$

Always read the domain of the variable carefully.

## 10. Number Line Representation

Use an open point when the boundary is not included.

Examples:

$$
x < 3
$$

means open point at 3 and shading to the left.

$$
x > 3
$$

means open point at 3 and shading to the right.

Use a closed point when the boundary is included.

Examples:

$$
x \le 3
$$

means closed point at 3 and shading to the left.

$$
x \ge 3
$$

means closed point at 3 and shading to the right.

## 11. Interval Notation

| Inequality | Interval |
|---|---|
| `x < a` | `(-infinity, a)` |
| `x <= a` | `(-infinity, a]` |
| `x > a` | `(a, infinity)` |
| `x >= a` | `[a, infinity)` |
| `a < x < b` | `(a, b)` |
| `a <= x <= b` | `[a, b]` |
| `a < x <= b` | `(a, b]` |
| `a <= x < b` | `[a, b)` |

## 12. Worked Example: Simple Linear Inequality

Solve:

$$
5x - 3 < 3x + 1
$$

Move x terms to one side:

$$
5x - 3x < 1 + 3
$$

So:

$$
2x < 4
$$

Divide by positive 2:

$$
x < 2
$$

Solution set in real numbers:

$$
(-\infty, 2)
$$

## 13. Worked Example: Sign Reversal

Solve:

$$
4x + 3 < 6x + 7
$$

Move terms:

$$
4x - 6x < 7 - 3
$$

So:

$$
-2x < 4
$$

Divide by `-2` and reverse the sign:

$$
x > -2
$$

Solution:

$$
(-2, \infty)
$$

## 14. Worked Example: Fractional Inequality

Solve:

$$
\frac{5-2x}{3} \le \frac{x-5}{6}
$$

Multiply both sides by 6, which is positive:

$$
2(5-2x) \le x - 5
$$

Expand:

$$
10 - 4x \le x - 5
$$

Move terms:

$$
-5x \le -15
$$

Divide by `-5` and reverse the sign:

$$
x \ge 3
$$

The raw worked example has a similar form; the main lesson is that clearing positive denominators is safe, but dividing by a negative reverses the sign.

## 15. Compound Inequalities

A compound inequality has two comparisons at once.

Example:

$$
-8 \le 5x - 3 < 7
$$

Add 3 to all three parts:

$$
-5 \le 5x < 10
$$

Divide all parts by 5:

$$
-1 \le x < 2
$$

Solution:

$$
[-1, 2)
$$

## 16. Compound Inequality With Sign Reversal

Solve:

$$
-5 \le 5 - 3x \le 8
$$

Subtract 5 from all three parts:

$$
-10 \le -3x \le 3
$$

Divide by `-3`.

Because this is negative, reverse both inequality signs:

$$
\frac{10}{3} \ge x \ge -1
$$

Write in increasing order:

$$
-1 \le x \le \frac{10}{3}
$$

Solution:

$$
[-1, 10/3]
$$

## 17. System Of Inequalities

A system requires the common solution of two or more inequalities.

Example:

$$
3x - 7 < 5 + x
$$

and:

$$
11 - 5x \le 1
$$

First inequality:

$$
3x - 7 < 5 + x
$$

$$
2x < 12
$$

$$
x < 6
$$

Second inequality:

$$
11 - 5x \le 1
$$

$$
-5x \le -10
$$

Divide by `-5` and reverse:

$$
x \ge 2
$$

Common solution:

$$
2 \le x < 6
$$

Interval:

$$
[2, 6)
$$

## 18. Word Problems: Average Marks

Average means:

$$
\frac{\text{sum of marks}}{\text{number of tests}}
$$

Example:

A student scored 62 and 48 in two tests.

Find minimum marks `x` in the annual exam to have average at least 60.

Set up:

$$
\frac{62 + 48 + x}{3} \ge 60
$$

Multiply by 3:

$$
110 + x \ge 180
$$

So:

$$
x \ge 70
$$

Minimum marks:

$$
70
$$

## 19. Word Problems: Consecutive Odd Integers

Consecutive odd integers differ by 2.

Let the smaller odd integer be:

$$
x
$$

Then the next odd integer is:

$$
x + 2
$$

Example:

Find consecutive odd natural numbers both greater than 10 whose sum is less than 40.

Conditions:

$$
x > 10
$$

and:

$$
x + (x+2) < 40
$$

So:

$$
2x + 2 < 40
$$

$$
x < 19
$$

Thus:

$$
10 < x < 19
$$

Odd natural values:

$$
11, 13, 15, 17
$$

Pairs:

$$
(11,13), (13,15), (15,17), (17,19)
$$

## 20. Word Problems: Consecutive Even Integers

Consecutive even positive integers differ by 2.

Let the smaller even integer be:

$$
x
$$

Then the next is:

$$
x + 2
$$

For conditions such as "both larger than 5" and "sum less than 23":

$$
x > 5
$$

and:

$$
x + (x+2) < 23
$$

So:

$$
2x + 2 < 23
$$

$$
x < \frac{21}{2}
$$

Even positive values satisfying `x > 5` and `x < 10.5` are:

$$
6, 8, 10
$$

Pairs:

$$
(6,8), (8,10), (10,12)
$$

## 21. Word Problems: Triangle Sides

Example pattern:

The longest side is 3 times the shortest side.

The third side is 2 cm shorter than the longest side.

Let shortest side be:

$$
x
$$

Then longest side:

$$
3x
$$

Third side:

$$
3x - 2
$$

If perimeter is at least 61:

$$
x + 3x + (3x-2) \ge 61
$$

So:

$$
7x - 2 \ge 61
$$

$$
7x \ge 63
$$

$$
x \ge 9
$$

Minimum shortest side:

$$
9 \text{ cm}
$$

## 22. Word Problems: Cutting Boards

Let the shortest board length be:

$$
x
$$

Second length:

$$
x + 3
$$

Third length:

$$
2x
$$

Total length at most 91:

$$
x + (x+3) + 2x \le 91
$$

So:

$$
4x + 3 \le 91
$$

$$
x \le 22
$$

Third piece at least 5 cm longer than second:

$$
2x \ge (x+3) + 5
$$

$$
2x \ge x + 8
$$

$$
x \ge 8
$$

Therefore possible shortest length:

$$
8 \le x \le 22
$$

If the board lengths must be whole centimeters, then:

$$
x \in \{8, 9, 10, ..., 22\}
$$

## 23. Word Problems: Temperature Conversion

If Celsius and Fahrenheit are connected by:

$$
F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32
$$

then:

$$
C = \frac{5}{9}(F-32)
$$

Example:

If:

$$
30 < C < 35
$$

then:

$$
30 < \frac{5}{9}(F-32) < 35
$$

Multiply by:

$$
\frac{9}{5}
$$

which is positive:

$$
54 < F - 32 < 63
$$

Add 32:

$$
86 < F < 95
$$

## 24. Word Problems: Mixtures

Mixture problems compare amount of pure substance.

Example:

600 litres of 12 percent acid solution is mixed with x litres of 30 percent acid solution.

Result should be more than 15 percent but less than 18 percent.

Amount of acid after mixing:

$$
0.30x + 0.12(600)
$$

Total mixture:

$$
x + 600
$$

Conditions:

$$
0.30x + 72 > 0.15(x+600)
$$

and:

$$
0.30x + 72 < 0.18(x+600)
$$

First:

$$
0.30x + 72 > 0.15x + 90
$$

$$
0.15x > 18
$$

$$
x > 120
$$

Second:

$$
0.30x + 72 < 0.18x + 108
$$

$$
0.12x < 36
$$

$$
x < 300
$$

Thus:

$$
120 < x < 300
$$

## 25. Linear Inequalities In Two Variables

A linear inequality in two variables looks like:

$$
ax + by < c
$$

or:

$$
ax + by \le c
$$

Its graph is usually a half-plane.

Method:

1. Replace the inequality by equality to draw the boundary line.
2. Use a solid line for `<=` or `>=`.
3. Use a dashed line for `<` or `>`.
4. Pick a test point not on the line, often `(0,0)`.
5. Shade the side that satisfies the inequality.

Example:

For:

$$
x + y \le 4
$$

draw:

$$
x + y = 4
$$

The point `(0,0)` gives:

$$
0 + 0 \le 4
$$

which is true.

So shade the side containing `(0,0)`.

## 28. Key Formulas And Rules

| Idea | Rule |
|---|---|
| Add same number | Inequality sign does not change |
| Subtract same number | Inequality sign does not change |
| Multiply by positive number | Inequality sign does not change |
| Divide by positive number | Inequality sign does not change |
| Multiply by negative number | Inequality sign reverses |
| Divide by negative number | Inequality sign reverses |

| `x <= a` | closed point at a, shade left |
| `x > a` | open point at a, shade right |
| `x >= a` | closed point at a, shade right |
| compound inequality | solve all parts together |

## 29. Common Mistakes And Corrections

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Not reversing sign after dividing by negative | Always reverse the inequality sign |
| Treating `<` and `<=` the same | Boundary is excluded for `<`, included for `<=` |
| Forgetting variable domain | Natural, integer, and real solutions can differ |
| Shading wrong direction | Less than shades left, greater than shades right |
| Losing one part of a double inequality | Operate on all three parts |
| Using equality for "at least" | "At least" means `>=` |
| Using strict inequality for "at most" | "At most" means `<=` |
| Ignoring real-life restrictions | Lengths, marks, and quantities are usually non-negative |

## 31. Fast Problem-Solving Checklist

1. Read the variable domain: natural, integer, or real.
2. Translate words carefully.
3. Move variable terms to one side and constants to the other.
4. Clear positive denominators if fractions appear.
5. Reverse sign when dividing by a negative.
6. For compound inequalities, perform the operation on all parts.
7. For systems, solve each inequality and intersect solutions.
8. Convert final answer to interval, roster, or number-line form as requested.
9. In word problems, check whether the answer makes sense physically.

## 34. Final Chapter Summary

An inequality compares two numbers or expressions using `<`, `>`, `<=`, or `>=`.

Strict inequalities exclude the boundary; non-strict inequalities include it.

Solutions of an inequality are all values that make it true.

You may add or subtract the same number on both sides without changing the inequality sign.

You may multiply or divide by a positive number without changing the sign.

When multiplying or dividing by a negative number, the inequality sign must be reversed.

Solutions can be represented in roster form, interval form, set-builder form, or on a number line.

Compound inequalities and systems require common solutions.

Word problems often use inequalities because they involve limits such as at least, at most, minimum, maximum, more than, or less than.

Linear inequalities in two variables are represented by half-planes bounded by straight lines.
