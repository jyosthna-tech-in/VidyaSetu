# Unit 3A: Meaning of Business Transaction

## What Counts as a Transaction

A business transaction is an economic event between two parties that:

1. affects the financial position of the business,
2. can be measured in money,
3. has evidence or can be supported by a voucher,
4. changes at least two accounts under double entry.

Examples:

| Event | Transaction? | Reason |
|---|---:|---|
| Bought goods for Rs. 20,000 cash | Yes | Cash decreases and purchases increase |
| Sold goods to Rahul for Rs. 15,000 | Yes | Debtor or cash increases and sales increase |
| Owner plans to buy a machine next month | No | No present financial effect |
| Received a cheque from customer | Yes | Bank increases and debtor decreases |
| Employee promises to work harder | No | Cannot be measured reliably in money |
| Rent paid by cheque | Yes | Rent expense increases and bank decreases |

## The Give and Take Aspect

Most transactions have a give side and a take side.

Example: a business buys a computer for Rs. 35,000 cash.

| Side | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Give side | Cash is paid |
| Take side | Computer is received |

This two-sided effect is the heart of double entry accounting. The business never records only one side. If something comes in, something else goes out, or a claim changes.

## Transaction Versus Event

Every transaction is an event, but every event is not a transaction.

Examples:

| Event | Why It May or May Not Be Recorded |
|---|---|
| Goods purchased | Recorded because goods and payment/liability change |
| Fire destroys goods | Recorded because asset is lost and loss is created |
| Market price of building increases | Usually not recorded under historical cost unless accounting rules require revaluation |
| Appointment of a manager | Not recorded unless salary is paid or becomes due |
| Owner withdraws cash | Recorded because business cash and capital both reduce |

# Unit 3B: Evidence Documents and Vouchers

## Why Evidence Is Needed

Accounting records must not be based on memory or guesswork. A transaction should be supported by evidence so that:

1. the entry can be checked later,
2. the amount can be verified,
3. the date can be proved,
4. responsibility can be fixed,
5. audit and tax checking become possible,
6. errors and fraud become easier to detect,
7. the business can prove its claims against customers, suppliers, banks, and employees.

## Common Evidence Documents

| Document | Common Use |
|---|---|
| Cash memo | Cash purchase or cash sale |
| Invoice | Credit purchase or credit sale |
| Sales bill | Goods sold by the business |
| Purchase bill | Goods purchased by the business |
| Pay-in slip | Cash or cheque deposited into bank |
| Cheque counterfoil | Cheque issued |
| Bank statement | Bank transactions confirmed by bank |
| Salary sheet | Salary paid or payable |
| Rent receipt | Rent paid |
| Debit note | Goods returned to supplier or amount debited |
| Credit note | Goods returned by customer or amount credited |
| Internal voucher | Small expenses where external document is not available |

## Voucher Meaning

A voucher is an accounting document prepared to authorise and record a transaction. It may be prepared from an external document such as an invoice, or internally when external proof is not available.

Example:

If petty cash of Rs. 120 is paid for tea for office guests and no formal bill is available, the business may prepare an internal voucher. The responsible person signs it, the authorised person approves it, and the voucher becomes the basis for recording the expense.

## Why Vouchers Are Numbered

Vouchers are usually serially numbered and arranged by date. This helps in:

1. preventing missing records,
2. tracing entries from journal to evidence,
3. checking whether a transaction was recorded twice,
4. locating old records quickly,
5. maintaining internal control.

## Essential Contents of a Voucher

A good voucher normally contains:

| Element | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Name of firm | Identifies the business |
| Voucher number | Gives a unique reference |
| Date of transaction | Shows when the event happened |
| Account debited | Shows receiving or expense side |
| Account credited | Shows giving or income/liability side |
| Amount | Gives monetary value |
| Narration | Explains the reason |
| Supporting document reference | Links voucher with bill or receipt |
| Prepared by | Shows who prepared the voucher |
| Authorised by | Shows approval |

## Types of Vouchers

| Voucher Type | Used When | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Cash voucher | Cash is paid or received | Rent paid in cash |
| Debit voucher | One credit and multiple debits, or a debit-focused entry | Expenses paid under one cheque |
| Credit voucher | One debit and multiple credits, or a credit-focused entry | Cash received from different parties |
| Journal voucher | Non-cash or adjustment entries | Depreciation, bad debts, outstanding expenses |
| Compound voucher | More than one debit or more than one credit | Furniture bought partly in cash and partly on credit |
| Complex voucher | Multiple debits and multiple credits | GST set-off with several input and output accounts |

## Simple, Compound, and Complex Transactions

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Simple transaction | One account debited and one account credited | Paid rent Rs. 5,000 cash |
| Compound transaction | One side has multiple accounts | Bought machine Rs. 50,000; paid Rs. 10,000 cash and balance on credit |
| Complex transaction | Multiple accounts on both debit and credit sides | GST adjustment involving input CGST, input SGST, output CGST, output SGST, and cash ledger |

# Unit 3C: Accounting Equation

## Basic Equation

The accounting equation is:

`Assets = Liabilities + Capital`

Or:

`A = L + C`

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| A | Assets owned by the business |
| L | Liabilities owed by the business to outsiders |
| C | Capital owed by the business to owner |

## Rearranged Forms

The same equation can be rearranged:

| Form | Use |
|---|---|
| `Capital = Assets - Liabilities` | To find owner's claim |
| `Liabilities = Assets - Capital` | To find outsider's claim |
| `Assets = Liabilities + Capital` | To show resources equal claims |

## Why the Equation Always Balances

Every asset of a business must have come from somewhere.

Claims may be funded by:

1. owner contribution,
2. profit earned and retained,
3. loans,
4. credit purchases,
5. outstanding expenses,
6. income received in advance.

Therefore, total business resources must equal total claims against those resources.

## Assets, Liabilities, and Capital

| Category | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Assets | Things owned or controlled by business | Cash, bank, goods, furniture, building, debtors |
| Liabilities | Amounts payable to outsiders | Creditors, bank loan, outstanding salary, rent due |
| Capital | Owner's claim in business | Cash introduced, goods introduced, profit retained |

## Effect of Profit and Loss on Capital

| Item | Effect on Capital |
|---|---|
| Profit | Increases capital |
| Loss | Decreases capital |
| Revenue | Increases capital indirectly |
| Expense | Decreases capital indirectly |
| Drawings | Decreases capital |
| Fresh capital | Increases capital |

## Worked Example: Rohit Starts Business

Rohit starts business with cash Rs. 5,00,000.

| Assets | Amount | Liabilities and Capital | Amount |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Cash | 5,00,000 | Capital | 5,00,000 |
| Total | 5,00,000 | Total | 5,00,000 |

The equation is:

`Assets Rs. 5,00,000 = Liabilities Rs. 0 + Capital Rs. 5,00,000`

## Transaction Analysis: Rohit Example

Assume the following transactions:

1. Opened bank account with Rs. 4,80,000.
2. Bought furniture for Rs. 60,000 by cheque.
3. Bought plant and machinery for Rs. 1,25,000; paid Rs. 10,000 cash and balance on credit from Ramjee Lal.
4. Purchased goods from Sumit Traders for Rs. 55,000 on credit.
5. Sold goods costing Rs. 25,000 to Rajani Enterprises for Rs. 35,000 on credit.

## Analysis of Each Transaction

### Transaction 1: Cash Deposited in Bank

| Item | Effect |
|---|---|
| Bank | Increases by Rs. 4,80,000 |
| Cash | Decreases by Rs. 4,80,000 |
| Total assets | No change |
| Liabilities and capital | No change |

This is only a change in the form of asset: cash becomes bank balance.

### Transaction 2: Furniture Bought by Cheque

| Item | Effect |
|---|---|
| Furniture | Increases by Rs. 60,000 |
| Bank | Decreases by Rs. 60,000 |
| Total assets | No change |

Again, one asset replaces another asset.

### Transaction 3: Plant and Machinery Bought Partly for Cash

| Item | Effect |
|---|---|
| Plant and machinery | Increases by Rs. 1,25,000 |
| Cash | Decreases by Rs. 10,000 |
| Creditor, Ramjee Lal | Increases by Rs. 1,15,000 |

Assets increase by net Rs. 1,15,000 and liabilities also increase by Rs. 1,15,000.

### Transaction 4: Goods Purchased on Credit

| Item | Effect |
|---|---|
| Stock or purchases | Increases by Rs. 55,000 |
| Creditor, Sumit Traders | Increases by Rs. 55,000 |

Assets increase and liabilities increase equally.

### Transaction 5: Goods Sold at Profit on Credit

| Item | Effect |
|---|---|
| Debtor, Rajani Enterprises | Increases by Rs. 35,000 |
| Stock | Decreases by Rs. 25,000 |
| Profit | Increases capital by Rs. 10,000 |

The business has gained Rs. 10,000 because goods costing Rs. 25,000 were sold for Rs. 35,000.

## Final Position in Rohit's Example

| Assets | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Cash | 10,000 |
| Bank | 4,20,000 |
| Debtors | 35,000 |
| Stock | 30,000 |
| Furniture | 60,000 |
| Plant and machinery | 1,25,000 |
| Total assets | 6,80,000 |

| Liabilities and Capital | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Creditors | 1,70,000 |
| Capital | 5,10,000 |
| Total liabilities and capital | 6,80,000 |

Equation:

`Assets Rs. 6,80,000 = Liabilities Rs. 1,70,000 + Capital Rs. 5,10,000`

For exam questions, use a table like this:

| Transaction | Cash | Bank | Stock | Furniture | Debtors | Plant | Liabilities | Capital |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Opening capital | +5,00,000 | - | - | - | - | - | - | +5,00,000 |
| Deposit in bank | -4,80,000 | +4,80,000 | - | - | - | - | - | - |
| Furniture by cheque | - | -60,000 | - | +60,000 | - | - | - | - |
| Plant bought | -10,000 | - | - | - | - | +1,25,000 | +1,15,000 | - |
| Goods on credit | - | - | +55,000 | - | - | - | +55,000 | - |
| Goods sold at profit | - | - | -25,000 | - | +35,000 | - | - | +10,000 |
| Final balance | 10,000 | 4,20,000 | 30,000 | 60,000 | 35,000 | 1,25,000 | 1,70,000 | 5,10,000 |

# Unit 3D: Debit and Credit

## Meaning of Debit and Credit

Debit and credit are not ordinary English words in accounting. They do not always mean increase or decrease.

In accounting:

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Debit | Left side of an account |
| Credit | Right side of an account |

Whether debit increases or decreases an account depends on the type of account.

## T-Account Format

```text
                 Name of Account
Dr.                                             Cr.
Date   Particulars        Amount     Date   Particulars        Amount
```

The left side is debit. The right side is credit.

## Five Categories of Accounts

For this chapter, accounts are grouped as:

1. assets,
2. liabilities,
3. capital,
4. revenues and gains,
5. expenses and losses.

## Debit and Credit Rule Table

| Account Type | Debit Means | Credit Means |
|---|---|---|
| Assets | Increase | Decrease |
| Expenses and losses | Increase | Decrease |
| Liabilities | Decrease | Increase |
| Capital | Decrease | Increase |
| Revenues and gains | Decrease | Increase |

## Memory Rule

Debit the increase in:

1. assets,
2. expenses,
3. losses.

Credit the increase in:

1. liabilities,
2. capital,
3. revenues,
4. gains.

## Why Debit Does Not Always Mean Increase

Example 1:

Cash received from owner increases cash. Cash is an asset, so cash is debited.

Example 2:

Loan repaid decreases loan liability. Liability decreases by debit, so loan account is debited.

Therefore, debit can mean increase for assets and expenses, but decrease for liabilities, capital, and revenues.

## Why Credit Does Not Always Mean Decrease

Example 1:

Goods sold for cash increases sales. Sales is revenue, so sales is credited.

Example 2:

Cash paid for rent decreases cash. Cash is an asset, so cash is credited.

Therefore, credit can mean increase for liabilities, capital, and revenues, but decrease for assets and expenses.

## Transaction Analysis Using Debit and Credit

| Transaction | Account Debited | Reason | Account Credited | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Started business with cash | Cash | Asset increases | Capital | Capital increases |
| Deposited cash into bank | Bank | Asset increases | Cash | Asset decreases |
| Bought furniture by cheque | Furniture | Asset increases | Bank | Asset decreases |
| Bought goods on credit | Purchases | Expense or goods-in-trade increases | Creditor | Liability increases |
| Sold goods on credit | Debtor | Asset increases | Sales | Revenue increases |
| Paid rent | Rent | Expense increases | Cash or bank | Asset decreases |
| Received commission | Cash or bank | Asset increases | Commission | Revenue increases |
| Owner withdrew cash | Drawings | Capital decreases | Cash | Asset decreases |
| Paid creditor | Creditor | Liability decreases | Cash or bank | Asset decreases |
| Salary outstanding | Salary | Expense increases | Outstanding salary | Liability increases |

# Unit 3E: Journal and Journalising

## Meaning of Journal

The journal is the book of original entry in which transactions are first recorded in chronological order after analysing debit and credit.

The journal shows:

1. date of transaction,
2. account debited,
3. account credited,
4. amount debited,
5. amount credited,
6. narration,
7. ledger reference.

## Why Journal Is Needed

The journal is useful because it:

1. records transactions in date order,
2. keeps the complete debit and credit of each transaction together,
3. provides a clear explanation through narration,
4. gives legal and audit evidence when supported by vouchers,
5. helps avoid direct confusion in ledger accounts,
6. makes posting easier.

## Format of Journal

| Date | Particulars | L.F. | Debit Amount Rs. | Credit Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|
| Date | Account to be debited Dr. |  | Amount |  |
|  | To Account to be credited |  |  | Amount |
|  | Narration in brackets |  |  |  |

## Rules for Writing Journal Entry

1. Write the date in the date column.
2. Write the debit account first.
3. Add "Dr." after the debit account.
4. Write the credit account on the next line with "To" before it.
5. Indent the credit account slightly.
6. Write amount in debit and credit columns.
7. Write a short narration in brackets.
8. Leave the ledger folio blank until posting.
9. Ensure total debit equals total credit.

## Simple Journal Entry

Transaction: goods purchased on credit from Govind Traders for Rs. 30,000.

| Date | Particulars | L.F. | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|
| Dec. 24 | Purchases A/c Dr. |  | 30,000 |  |
|  | To Govind Traders A/c |  |  | 30,000 |
|  | Goods purchased on credit |  |  |  |

Why purchases account is debited:

In trading concerns, goods bought for resale are recorded in Purchases A/c, not in a general Goods A/c.

## Compound Journal Entry

Transaction: office furniture purchased from Modern Furniture for Rs. 25,000; Rs. 5,000 paid in cash and Rs. 20,000 still payable.

| Date | Particulars | L.F. | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|
| July 4 | Office Furniture A/c Dr. |  | 25,000 |  |
|  | To Cash A/c |  |  | 5,000 |
|  | To Modern Furniture A/c |  |  | 20,000 |
|  | Furniture bought partly for cash and partly on credit |  |  |  |

Why it is compound:

One account is debited and two accounts are credited.

|  | Business started with cash |  |  |

|  | To Cash A/c |  | 4,80,000 |
|  | Bank account opened |  |  |
|  | Furniture A/c Dr. | 60,000 |  |

|  | Furniture purchased by cheque |  |  |

## Why Cost of Goods Sold Is Not Separately Recorded Here

In early journal practice, the sale entry records the selling price:

`Debtor A/c Dr. to Sales A/c`

The reduction of stock and calculation of gross profit are handled later through trading account and inventory adjustments. The accounting equation examples show the profit effect to explain the logic, while journal practice uses Purchases and Sales accounts.

# Unit 3F: Books of Original Entry

## Meaning

Books of original entry are books where transactions are first recorded from vouchers before being posted to the ledger.

## Main Books of Original Entry

| Book | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Journal proper | Entries not recorded in special books |
| Cash book | Cash and bank transactions |
| Purchases book | Credit purchases of goods |
| Sales book | Credit sales of goods |
| Purchase returns book | Goods returned to suppliers |
| Sales returns book | Goods returned by customers |
| Bills receivable book | Bills receivable accepted by debtors |
| Bills payable book | Bills payable accepted by business |

## Why Special Books Are Used

The journal can record all transactions, but large businesses have repeated transactions. Special books save time by grouping similar transactions.

Example:

If a business has 500 credit sales in a month, entering each in the journal proper may be slow. A sales book collects them together and later posting becomes easier.

# Unit 3G: Ledger

## Meaning of Ledger

The ledger is the principal book of accounts. It contains all individual accounts to which journal entries are posted.

The journal gives transaction-wise information. The ledger gives account-wise information.

## Why Ledger Is Needed

Suppose a business wants to know how much is payable to Ritu. Looking through the entire journal would be slow because entries are date-wise. A ledger collects all entries related to Ritu in one place.

The ledger helps to know:

1. cash balance,
2. bank balance,
3. amount due from each customer,
4. amount due to each supplier,
5. total rent paid,
6. total sales,
7. total purchases,
8. total capital and drawings,
9. balances needed for trial balance.

## Ledger Account Format

```text
                           Name of Account
Dr.                                                          Cr.
Date      Particulars      J.F.      Amount      Date      Particulars      J.F.      Amount
```

## Ledger Columns

| Column | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Date | Date of the transaction |
| Particulars | Name of the opposite account |
| J.F. | Journal folio, meaning page number of journal |
| Amount | Amount posted |

## Important Point About Particulars

In ledger, the particulars column shows the opposite account, not the same account.

Example:

Journal entry:

`Cash A/c Dr. Rs. 10,000`

`To Sales A/c Rs. 10,000`

In Cash Account, debit side particulars will show `Sales`.

In Sales Account, credit side particulars will show `Cash`.

## Classification of Ledger Accounts

| Classification | Accounts Included | Treatment at Year-End |
|---|---|---|
| Permanent accounts | Assets, liabilities, capital | Balanced and carried forward |
| Temporary accounts | Revenues, expenses, gains, losses | Transferred to trading and profit and loss account |

## Examples of Permanent Accounts

1. Cash Account
2. Bank Account
3. Furniture Account
4. Machinery Account
5. Debtors Account
6. Creditors Account
7. Capital Account
8. Loan Account

## Examples of Temporary Accounts

1. Sales Account
2. Purchases Account
3. Rent Account
4. Salary Account
5. Commission Received Account
6. Discount Allowed Account
7. Cartage Account
8. Insurance Account

# Unit 3H: Posting from Journal to Ledger

## Meaning of Posting

Posting means transferring journal entries to the relevant ledger accounts.

Each journal entry has at least two accounts. Every account in the journal must be posted to its separate ledger account.

## Steps for Posting Debit Account

1. Open the account to be debited in the ledger.
2. Write the date on the debit side.
3. In particulars, write the name of the credit account.
4. Write the journal folio reference.
5. Write the amount on the debit side.

## Steps for Posting Credit Account

1. Open the account to be credited in the ledger.
2. Write the date on the credit side.
3. In particulars, write the name of the debit account.
4. Write the journal folio reference.
5. Write the amount on the credit side.

## Posting Example 1: Simple Entry

Journal:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Cash A/c Dr. | 10,000 |  |
| To Sales A/c |  | 10,000 |

Ledger posting:

Cash Account:

| Dr. Date | Dr. Particulars | Dr. Amount Rs. | Cr. Date | Cr. Particulars | Cr. Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---:|---|---|---:|
|  | Sales | 10,000 |  |  |  |

Sales Account:

| Dr. Date | Dr. Particulars | Dr. Amount Rs. | Cr. Date | Cr. Particulars | Cr. Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---:|---|---|---:|
|  |  |  |  | Cash | 10,000 |

## Posting Example 2: Compound Entry

Journal:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Plant and Machinery A/c Dr. | 1,25,000 |  |
| To Cash A/c |  | 10,000 |
| To Ramjee Lal A/c |  | 1,15,000 |

Ledger posting:

Plant and Machinery Account:

| Dr. Particulars | Dr. Amount Rs. | Cr. Particulars | Cr. Amount Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Cash | 10,000 |  |  |
| Ramjee Lal | 1,15,000 |  |  |

Cash Account:

| Dr. Particulars | Dr. Amount Rs. | Cr. Particulars | Cr. Amount Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
|  |  | Plant and Machinery | 10,000 |

Ramjee Lal Account:

| Dr. Particulars | Dr. Amount Rs. | Cr. Particulars | Cr. Amount Rs. |
|---|---:|---|---:|
|  |  | Plant and Machinery | 1,15,000 |

Note:

In a compound entry, the account with total debit may show each credit account separately in the particulars column, or may show a suitable combined reference depending on ledger practice.

# Unit 3I: Journal Versus Ledger

| Basis | Journal | Ledger |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Book of original entry | Principal book of accounts |
| Order | Chronological order | Account-wise order |
| Function | Records complete transaction | Classifies transactions by account |
| Process | Journalising | Posting |
| Evidence value | Stronger because entry is near voucher stage | Supports account balances |
| Basis of classification | Transaction | Account |
| Balancing | Journal entries are totalled, not balanced account-wise | Accounts are balanced |
| Use | Helps write first entry | Helps prepare trial balance and statements |

# Unit 3J: Goods Accounts in Trading Business

## Why Purchases and Sales Are Used

In a trading business, goods are bought and sold repeatedly. Instead of using one Goods Account for everything, accounting uses separate accounts:

| Account | Used For |
|---|---|
| Purchases A/c | Goods bought for resale |
| Sales A/c | Goods sold |
| Purchases Returns A/c | Goods returned to supplier |
| Sales Returns A/c | Goods returned by customer |
| Stock A/c | Goods remaining at a point of time |

## Goods Bought for Use Versus Resale

| Item Bought | Account Debited |
|---|---|
| Goods bought for resale | Purchases A/c |
| Furniture bought for office use | Furniture A/c |
| Computer printer bought for office | Computer Printer A/c |
| Stationery for office | Stationery A/c |
| Goods given as charity | Charity A/c, with Purchases credited |
| Goods withdrawn by owner | Drawings A/c, with Purchases credited |

## Common Entries for Goods

| Transaction | Entry |
|---|---|
| Goods bought for cash | Purchases A/c Dr. To Cash A/c |
| Goods bought on credit | Purchases A/c Dr. To Supplier A/c |
| Goods sold for cash | Cash A/c Dr. To Sales A/c |
| Goods sold on credit | Customer A/c Dr. To Sales A/c |
| Goods returned to supplier | Supplier A/c Dr. To Purchases Returns A/c |
| Goods returned by customer | Sales Returns A/c Dr. To Customer A/c |

## Basic Idea

GST is tax on supply of goods or services. In accounting for a registered business:

1. GST paid on purchases is input GST.
2. GST collected on sales is output GST.
3. Input GST is generally adjusted against output GST.
4. Balance payable is paid through electronic cash ledger.

For beginner-level problems, follow the instructions given in the question. A common rule used in textbook-style questions is:

1. Input IGST is first adjusted against output IGST.

3. Input CGST is adjusted against output CGST.
4. Input SGST is adjusted against output SGST.

# Unit 3Q: Short Answer Solutions

## 1. State the three fundamental steps in the accounting process.

The three fundamental steps are:

1. identifying and analysing business transactions,
2. recording them in books of original entry,
3. classifying and summarising them through ledger and later statements.

In a wider accounting cycle, this is followed by trial balance, financial statements, and communication to users.

## 2. Why is evidence provided by documents important?

Evidence is important because accounting must be verifiable. Documents prove the date, amount, parties, and nature of the transaction. They support audit, tax assessment, internal control, and correction of errors.

Without evidence, the entry may become unreliable.

## 3. Should a transaction be first recorded in journal or ledger? Why?

A transaction is first recorded in the journal or another book of original entry. The ledger is prepared after the journal through posting.

Reason:

The journal records the complete transaction in one place with debit, credit, and narration. The ledger classifies the same information account-wise.

## 4. Are debits or credits listed first in journal entries? Are they indented?

Debit accounts are listed first. Credit accounts are written after the debit line with the word "To" and are indented.

This format makes it easy to identify which account is debited and which account is credited.

## 5. Why are some accounting systems called double entry systems?

They are called double entry systems because every transaction has at least two effects. For every debit, there is an equal credit. The total amount debited must always equal the total amount credited.

## 6. Give a specimen of an account.

```text
                           Cash Account
Dr.                                                          Cr.
Date      Particulars      J.F.      Amount      Date      Particulars      J.F.      Amount
```

The debit side records increases in cash, and the credit side records decreases in cash.

## 7. Why are debit-credit rules same for liabilities and capital?

Liabilities and capital both represent claims against business assets. Liabilities are outsider claims and capital is owner's claim. Since both are on the claims side of the equation, their increase is credited and their decrease is debited.

## 8. What is the purpose of posting folio numbers?

Folio numbers connect journal and ledger records.

They help in:

1. locating the related ledger account from journal,
2. locating the related journal entry from ledger,
3. checking whether posting has been completed,
4. reducing audit and verification time.

## 9. What entry would increase revenue, decrease expense, record drawings, and record fresh capital?

| Situation | Entry Side |
|---|---|
| Increase revenue | Credit revenue account |
| Decrease expense | Credit expense account |
| Record drawings | Debit drawings account |
| Record fresh capital | Credit capital account |

## 10. If an asset decreases, is it debit or credit? If a liability decreases, is it debit or credit?

Decrease in asset is credited.

Decrease in liability is debited.

Example:

Payment to creditor decreases cash and decreases creditor.

Entry:

`Creditor A/c Dr. To Cash A/c`

# Unit 3R: Long Answer Solutions

## 1. Events recorded in accounting systems and importance of documents

Accounting records only those events that affect the business financially and can be measured in money. Examples include purchases, sales, receipts, payments, expenses, income, losses, assets bought, liabilities incurred, and owner withdrawals.

Evidence documents are important because they make accounting reliable. A cash memo proves cash purchase or sale. An invoice proves credit purchase or sale. A cheque counterfoil and bank statement prove bank payment. A salary sheet proves salary expense.

Accounting documents help:

1. verify transaction amount,
2. prove transaction date,
3. confirm parties involved,
4. prevent false entries,
5. support audit,
6. settle disputes,
7. maintain internal control.

## 2. How debits and credits are used to analyse transactions

Debit and credit are used to record the two-sided effect of every transaction. First, the accountant identifies the accounts affected. Then each account is classified as asset, liability, capital, revenue, or expense. After that, the debit-credit rule is applied.

Example:

Paid salary Rs. 5,000 in cash.

Accounts affected:

1. Salary A/c: expense increases.
2. Cash A/c: asset decreases.

Rule:

1. Increase in expense is debited.
2. Decrease in asset is credited.

Entry:

`Salary A/c Dr. Rs. 5,000`

`To Cash A/c Rs. 5,000`

## 3. How accounts record effects of transactions

An account is a classified record of all changes in one item. For example, Cash Account records all cash receipts on the debit side and all cash payments on the credit side. At the end, the difference shows cash balance.

Similarly:

1. debtor accounts show amounts receivable from customers,
2. creditor accounts show amounts payable to suppliers,
3. rent account shows total rent expense,
4. sales account shows total sales,
5. capital account shows owner's claim.

The ledger gathers scattered journal entries into organised account-wise records.

## 4. What is a journal? Give five entries.

A journal is the book of original entry in which transactions are recorded in chronological order after deciding debit and credit.

| Basis | Evidence Document | Voucher |
|---|---|---|

## 6. Accounting equation remains intact under all circumstances.

The accounting equation remains intact because every transaction affects at least two items and the total effect on both sides is equal.

Example:

Business starts with cash Rs. 1,00,000.

`Assets Rs. 1,00,000 = Capital Rs. 1,00,000`

Goods purchased for cash Rs. 20,000:

Cash decreases by Rs. 20,000 and stock increases by Rs. 20,000. Total assets remain Rs. 1,00,000. Claims do not change.

Goods costing Rs. 10,000 sold for Rs. 15,000 cash:

Cash increases by Rs. 15,000, stock decreases by Rs. 10,000, and profit increases capital by Rs. 5,000.

Assets become Rs. 1,05,000 and capital becomes Rs. 1,05,000.

The equation remains balanced.

## 7. Explain double entry mechanism with example.

Double entry means that every transaction is recorded with equal debit and credit. It is based on the idea that every transaction has two aspects.

Example:

Furniture purchased for Rs. 25,000 cash.

Accounts affected:

1. Furniture A/c: asset increases.
2. Cash A/c: asset decreases.

Entry:

`Furniture A/c Dr. Rs. 25,000`

`To Cash A/c Rs. 25,000`

Debit equals credit. The increase in one asset is matched by the decrease in another asset.

# Unit 3U: Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---|
| Treating debit as always increase | First classify the account |
| Treating credit as always decrease | Credit increases liabilities, capital, and revenues |
| Debiting goods for resale to Goods A/c | Use Purchases A/c |
| Crediting Purchases A/c for cash purchase | Credit Cash A/c, debit Purchases A/c |
| Recording only cash side | Every transaction needs equal debit and credit |
| Forgetting narration | Add a short explanation in journal |
| Writing credit account before debit account | Debit account is written first |
| Posting same account name in ledger particulars | Write opposite account name |
| Ignoring discount in full settlement | Record discount allowed or received |
| Treating drawings as expense | Drawings reduce capital, not profit directly |
| Treating outstanding expense as paid | Outstanding expense creates liability |
| Recording personal car purchase in business books | Personal transactions are not business transactions unless business pays or receives |
| Recording GST-inclusive amount fully as purchase | Separate taxable value and GST when input credit is allowed |

## Journal Writing Checklist

1. Identify accounts.
2. Classify each account.
3. Decide increase or decrease.
4. Apply debit-credit rule.
5. Write debit account first.
6. Write credit account with "To".
7. Put equal amounts.
8. Add narration.
9. Check totals.

## Posting Checklist

1. Open every account appearing in the journal.
2. Post debit account on debit side.
3. Post credit account on credit side.
4. In particulars, write the opposite account.
5. Write amount correctly.
6. Add journal folio reference if required.
7. Check that every journal line has been posted.

# Final Study Summary

This chapter connects theory with actual accounting work. A transaction begins with evidence, is supported by a voucher, is analysed through the accounting equation and debit-credit rules, is first recorded in the journal, and is then posted to the ledger.

The accounting equation `Assets = Liabilities + Capital` must always remain balanced. Debit and credit do not automatically mean increase or decrease; their meaning depends on account type. Assets and expenses increase by debit, while liabilities, capital, and revenues increase by credit.

The journal records transactions date-wise with narration. The ledger rearranges the same information account-wise. Posting is the bridge between the two. Once a learner can identify accounts, classify them, decide debit and credit, write journal entries, and post them into ledgers, the foundation for trial balance and financial statements becomes strong.
