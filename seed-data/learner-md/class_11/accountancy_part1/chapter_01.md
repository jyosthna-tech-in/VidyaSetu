# Unit 1A: Why Accounting Matters

For centuries, accounting was mostly seen as financial record-keeping. In modern business, it is much more than that.

Today, accounting helps:

1. Record business transactions.
2. Measure profit or loss.
3. Show what the business owns and owes.
4. Provide information to managers.
5. Help investors and lenders make decisions.
6. Support tax and legal compliance.
7. Compare performance over time.
8. Detect errors, fraud, and inefficiency.
9. Plan future operations.
10. Communicate the condition of the business.

## Modern Role of Accountants

Accountants are no longer only transaction recorders. They may work in:

1. Financial reporting.
2. Cost analysis.
3. Management planning.
4. Auditing.
5. Forensic accounting.
6. E-commerce payment systems.
7. Financial planning.
8. Environmental accounting.
9. Social responsibility reporting.
10. Information system design.

The accountant has become part of the decision-making team.

# Unit 1B: Meaning of Accounting

Accounting can be defined as the process of identifying, measuring, recording, and communicating required information relating to economic events of an organisation to interested users.

## Core Elements of the Definition

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Identifying | Selecting events that should be recorded. |
| Measuring | Expressing those events in monetary terms. |
| Recording | Entering measured transactions in books in chronological order. |
| Communicating | Reporting information to users in a useful form. |
| Economic events | Business happenings that affect financial position or performance. |
| Organisation | The business entity or institution about which information is prepared. |
| Users | Persons or groups who use accounting information for decisions. |

## Accounting Is Broader Than Book-keeping

| Book-keeping | Accounting |
|---|---|
| Mainly recording transactions. | Identifies, measures, records, classifies, summarises, analyses, interprets, and communicates. |
| Mostly clerical. | Analytical and decision-oriented. |
| Ends with proper books. | Ends with useful reports and decisions. |
| A part of accounting. | The wider information system. |

# Unit 1C: Historical Development of Accounting

Accounting has a long history.

## Timeline Highlights

| Period or Place | Development |
|---|---|
| Babylonia and Egypt, around 4000 BC | Transactions such as wages and taxes were recorded on clay tablets. |
| Egypt | Treasury accounts were reported to officials and rulers. |
| Babylonia | Accounting helped commerce and detection of fraud or inefficiency. |
| Greece | Accounting was used for government receipts, payments, and treasury allocation. |
| Rome, 700 BC to 400 AD | Receipts and payments were recorded in daybooks and posted to ledgers. |
| China, around 2000 BC | Sophisticated government accounting existed. |
| India, Kautilya's Arthashastra | Accounting records and administrative control were described. |
| Luca Pacioli, 1494 | His book spread knowledge of double-entry book-keeping. |

## Luca Pacioli and Double Entry

Luca Pacioli did not invent double-entry book-keeping, but his 1494 work helped spread it. He used terms that developed into debit and credit. The basic idea is that every transaction affects at least two accounts.

This idea becomes central in later chapters.

# Unit 1D: Economic Events

An economic event is a happening of consequence to a business organisation that can be measured in monetary terms.

## Example: Purchase and Installation of Machinery

Buying machinery is not only one simple event. It may include several financial transactions:

1. Purchase price of machine.
2. Transport cost.
3. Site preparation cost.
4. Installation cost.
5. Trial run cost.

Accounting identifies and records the transactions connected with the event.

## External and Internal Events

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| External event | Transaction between organisation and outsider. | Sale to customer, purchase from supplier, payment of rent. |
| Internal event | Event occurring within the organisation. | Raw material issued by stores to production, wages paid to employees. |

Only events that can be measured in money and relate to the organisation are recorded in financial accounts.

# Unit 1E: Identification, Measurement, Recording, Communication

Accounting works as a process.

## 1. Identification

Identification means deciding which events should be recorded.

An event is recorded if:

1. It relates to the business entity.
2. It has financial character.
3. It can be measured in monetary terms.

Examples recorded:

1. Cash sales.
2. Credit purchases.
3. Salary paid.
4. Rent paid.
5. Goods sold.

Examples not recorded in financial accounts:

1. Appointment of a new managing director.
2. Change in managerial policy.
3. Skill of employees.
4. Quality of customer relations.

These may be important, but they are not recorded unless they can be measured in money as a transaction.

## 2. Measurement

Measurement means expressing transactions in money.

For Indian books, money is usually measured in rupees and paise.

If an item cannot be quantified in monetary terms, it is normally not recorded in financial accounts.

## 3. Recording

Recording means entering identified and measured transactions in the books of account.

Important points:

1. Transactions are recorded in monetary terms.
2. They are recorded in chronological order.
3. Recording follows established accounting rules.
4. Recorded information becomes evidence.

## 4. Communication

Communication means reporting accounting information to users.

Reports may be:

1. Daily.
2. Weekly.
3. Monthly.
4. Quarterly.
5. Annual.

Good communication gives the right information to the right person at the right time.

# Unit 1F: Organisation

An organisation is the business enterprise or institution for which accounting is done.

It may be:

1. Sole proprietorship.
2. Partnership firm.
3. Cooperative society.
4. Company.
5. Local authority.
6. Municipal corporation.
7. Not-for-profit organisation.

Accounting always belongs to a specific entity. We do not mix the owner's personal transactions with business transactions unless they affect the business.

# Unit 1G: Users of Accounting Information

Accounting is often called the language of business because it communicates financial information to users.

Users are divided into internal and external users.

## Internal Users

Internal users work inside the organisation.

| User | Information Needed |
|---|---|
| Chief executive | Overall performance and strategy information. |
| Financial officer | Cash, funds, loans, profitability, and financial position. |
| Business unit manager | Sales, costs, profit, and operational performance. |
| Plant manager | Production cost, efficiency, inventory, and wastage. |
| Store manager | Stock levels, purchases, and movement of goods. |
| Line supervisor | Labour, material usage, and daily operational data. |
| Employees and unions | Stability, profitability, wage capacity, and bonus potential. |

## External Users

External users are outside the organisation.

| User | Information Needed |
|---|---|
| Owners and shareholders | Return on investment and financial health. |
| Potential investors | Whether investment is worthwhile. |
| Lenders and banks | Ability to repay loans and interest. |
| Creditors and suppliers | Whether amounts due will be paid. |
| Government and tax authorities | Tax, legal compliance, and resource allocation. |
| Regulatory agencies | Compliance with laws and protection of stakeholders. |
| Customers | Whether the business will continue supplying products or services. |
| Social responsibility groups | Environmental and social impact. |
| Competitors | Comparative strength, weakness, and market position. |

## Main Learning

Accounting information is a means to an end. The end is better decision-making.

# Unit 1H: Accounting as an Information System

Accounting begins with business transactions and ends with communication of financial statements and reports.

## Accounting Information Flow

| Step | Output |
|---|---|
| Identify transactions | Relevant economic events selected |
| Measure transactions | Events expressed in money |
| Record transactions | Books of account updated |
| Classify and summarise | Accounts and statements prepared |
| Communicate | Reports sent to users |
| Interpret and decide | Users take decisions |

## What Useful Accounting Information Should Do

Useful accounting information should:

1. Help users make economic decisions.
2. Serve users who rely on financial statements.
3. Help predict and evaluate cash flows.
4. Help judge management's use of resources.
5. Disclose assumptions behind estimates.
6. Provide information about activities affecting society.

# Unit 1I: Branches of Accounting

The growth of business organisations led to specialised branches of accounting.

## Financial Accounting

Financial accounting records all financial transactions and prepares financial reports.

It helps:

1. Calculate profit or loss.
2. Show financial position.
3. Provide information to management and external users.
4. Serve stewardship and legal reporting functions.

It mostly relates to past transactions and is monetary in nature.

## Cost Accounting

Cost accounting analyses expenditure to find cost of products or services.

It helps:

1. Ascertain product cost.
2. Fix prices.
3. Control cost.
4. Provide cost information to management.

## Management Accounting

Management accounting provides information to management for planning, decision-making, and control.

It uses information from financial accounting, cost accounting, and other records.

It may include:

1. Budgets.
2. Profitability analysis.
3. Pricing decisions.
4. Capital expenditure decisions.
5. Sales forecasts.
6. Cash flow planning.
7. Manpower requirements.
8. Environmental and social information.

## Newer Areas

Modern accounting also includes:

1. Human resource accounting.
2. Social accounting.
3. Responsibility accounting.
4. Environmental accounting.
5. Forensic accounting.

# Unit 1J: Qualitative Characteristics of Accounting Information

Accounting information is useful only when it has the right qualities.

## Overview Table

| Characteristic | Meaning | Simple Test |
|---|---|---|
| Reliability | Can users depend on it? | Is it free from bias and verifiable? |
| Relevance | Does it help decisions? | Does it affect prediction, feedback, or choice? |
| Understandability | Can users understand it properly? | Is it clearly presented? |
| Comparability | Can users compare it? | Is the same unit, period, and format used? |

## Reliability

Reliable information faithfully represents what it claims to represent.

Reliable information should be:

1. Free from error.
2. Free from bias.
3. Credible.
4. Verifiable.
5. Neutral.
6. Faithful.

## Relevance

Relevant information influences decisions.

It should:

1. Be available in time.
2. Help predict future outcomes.
3. Confirm or correct past expectations.
4. Affect user decisions.

## Understandability

Understandability means users can interpret the information in the same sense in which it is prepared.

Good presentation helps users understand information without sacrificing relevance or reliability.

## Comparability

Comparability means users can compare information:

1. Over different accounting periods.
2. Across different businesses.
3. Against budgets or industry figures.

To be comparable, reports should use common units, common periods, and common formats.

# Unit 1K: Objectives of Accounting

The basic objective of accounting is to provide useful information to interested users.

## Main Objectives

| Objective | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Maintain records | Keep systematic record of all financial transactions. |
| Calculate profit or loss | Compare revenue and expenses for an accounting period. |
| Show financial position | Report assets, liabilities, and capital through balance sheet. |
| Provide information to users | Communicate reports to internal and external users. |

## 1. Maintenance of Records

Business has many transactions every day: purchases, sales, receipts, payments, expenses, and credit dealings.

No manager can remember all of them accurately. Accounting creates a complete and systematic record.

Records also provide evidence and allow verification.

## 2. Calculation of Profit and Loss

Owners want to know whether business operations earned profit or suffered loss.

Formula:

`Profit = Revenue - Expenses`

If expenses are greater than revenue:

`Loss = Expenses - Revenue`

Example:

| Item | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Revenue | Rs. 6,00,000 |
| Expenses | Rs. 5,40,000 |
| Profit | Rs. 60,000 |

## 3. Depiction of Financial Position

Accounting shows the financial position of a business at the end of an accounting period.

The balance sheet shows:

1. Assets owned by business.
2. Liabilities owed by business.
3. Capital or owner's claim.

At this level, remember:

`Assets = Liabilities + Capital`

This accounting equation will become more important in later chapters.

## 4. Providing Information to Users

Accounting information is communicated through:

1. Financial statements.
2. Reports.
3. Statements.
4. Graphs.
5. Charts.
6. Internal management reports.

Internal users need timely information for planning and control. External users rely mainly on financial statements.

# Unit 1L: Role of Accounting

Accounting has several roles.

| Role | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Language of business | Communicates business information. |
| Historical record | Maintains chronological record of financial transactions. |
| Current economic reality | Helps measure income and financial condition. |
| Information system | Links accountant and users through reports. |
| Service function | Provides specialised information needed by society. |

## Important Limitation

Accounting information usually relates to past transactions and is mostly quantitative and financial. It may not fully capture qualitative and non-financial factors such as employee skill, customer loyalty, brand image, working culture, or environmental impact unless special reporting systems are used.

# Unit 1M: Basic Accounting Terms

Accounting vocabulary must be precise. The same word may have a special meaning in accounting.

## Entity

Entity means a separately identifiable business or organisation.

Examples:

1. Super Bazaar.
2. Hire Jewellers.
3. ITC Limited.
4. A partnership firm.
5. A not-for-profit school.

Accounting records are prepared for one entity at a time.

## Transaction

A transaction is an event involving value between two or more entities.

Examples:

1. Purchase of goods.
2. Receipt of money.
3. Payment to creditor.
4. Payment of rent.
5. Sale of goods on credit.

Transactions may be cash transactions or credit transactions.

## Assets

Assets are economic resources of an enterprise that can be expressed in money and used for business benefit.

Examples:

1. Cash.
2. Bank balance.
3. Inventory.
4. Furniture.
5. Machinery.
6. Buildings.
7. Vehicles.
8. Patents.
9. Computer software.
10. Debtors.

## Current and Non-Current Assets

| Current Assets | Non-Current Assets |
|---|---|
| Expected to be realised, sold, or consumed within 12 months or normal operating cycle. | Held for long-term use or benefit. |
| Cash, bank, inventory, sundry debtors, bills receivable. | Land, building, machinery, furniture, goodwill, patents, vehicles. |

## Liabilities

Liabilities are obligations or debts payable by the business in the future.

Examples:

1. Creditors.
2. Bank loan.
3. Bills payable.
4. Outstanding expenses.
5. Borrowings.
6. Trade payables.

Liabilities are claims of outsiders on the assets of the business.

## Current and Non-Current Liabilities

| Current Liabilities | Non-Current Liabilities |
|---|---|
| Payable within 12 months or normal operating cycle. | Payable after more than 12 months. |
| Sundry creditors, bills payable, short-term borrowings, outstanding expenses. | Long-term loans, debentures, long-term provisions. |

## Capital

Capital is the amount invested by the owner in the business.

It may be brought in cash or assets.

Capital is shown on the liabilities side of the balance sheet because the business entity owes this amount to the owner.

## Sales

Sales are total revenue from goods or services sold to customers.

Sales may be:

1. Cash sales.
2. Credit sales.

## Revenue

Revenue is income earned by the business from selling goods or providing services.

Examples:

1. Sales revenue.
2. Commission received.
3. Interest received.
4. Dividend received.
5. Royalty received.
6. Rent received.

## Expenses

Expenses are costs incurred to earn revenue during an accounting period.

Examples:

1. Rent.
2. Wages.
3. Salaries.
4. Interest.
5. Depreciation.
6. Electricity.
7. Water charges.
8. Telephone expenses.

## Expenditure

Expenditure means spending money or incurring liability for a benefit, service, or property.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue expenditure | Benefit exhausted within a year. | Rent, salaries, electricity. |
| Capital expenditure | Benefit lasts more than one year. | Machinery, furniture, building. |

## Profit

Profit is the excess of revenue over related expenses for an accounting period.

`Profit = Revenue - Expenses`

Profit increases owner's capital.

## Gain

Gain is profit from incidental or non-routine events.

Examples:

1. Gain on sale of fixed asset.
2. Gain from winning a court case.
3. Appreciation in value of an asset, if recognised.

## Loss

Loss means:

1. Expenses exceed revenues for a period; or
2. Money or money's worth is lost without benefit.

Examples:

1. Fire loss.
2. Theft of goods.
3. Loss on sale of fixed asset.
4. Net loss from business operations.

Loss reduces owner's equity.

## Discount

Discount is deduction from price or amount payable.

| Type | Meaning | When Given |
|---|---|---|
| Trade discount | Deduction from list price. | At the time of sale. |
| Cash discount | Deduction for prompt payment. | At the time of payment. |

Trade discount is usually not recorded separately in books. Cash discount is recorded because it affects the amount received or paid.

## Voucher

A voucher is documentary evidence supporting a transaction.

Examples:

1. Cash memo.
2. Invoice.
3. Receipt.
4. Debit note.
5. Credit note.
6. Payment voucher.

## Goods

Goods are items in which the business deals.

Example:

For a furniture dealer, chairs and tables are goods. For an office using chairs and tables, the same items are furniture assets.

The meaning depends on the nature of the business.

## Drawings

Drawings are cash, goods, or assets withdrawn by the owner for personal use.

Drawings reduce owner's capital.

They are not business expenses.

## Purchases

Purchases are goods bought for resale or for processing and sale.

Purchases may be:

1. Cash purchases.
2. Credit purchases.

For a trading business, purchases are merchandise bought for resale. For a manufacturing business, purchases may include raw materials.

## Stock or Inventory

Stock means goods and other inventory on hand.

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Opening stock | Goods on hand at the beginning of accounting period. |
| Closing stock | Goods on hand at the end of accounting period. |
| Raw material | Material held for production. |
| Work-in-progress | Partly completed goods. |
| Finished goods | Completed goods ready for sale. |

## Debtors

Debtors are persons or entities who owe money to the business because they bought goods or services on credit.

Debtors are shown as assets.

## Creditors

Creditors are persons or entities to whom the business owes money because it bought goods or services on credit.

Creditors are shown as liabilities.

# Unit 1N: Common Confusions

## Profit Versus Gain

| Profit | Gain |
|---|---|
| From normal business operations. | From incidental or non-routine events. |
| Example: sales revenue exceeds business expenses. | Example: machinery sold for more than book value. |

## Expense Versus Expenditure

| Expense | Expenditure |
|---|---|
| Cost consumed to earn revenue. | Spending for a benefit, service, or property. |
| Always charged to profit and loss for the period. | May become expense or asset depending on benefit period. |

## Debtor Versus Creditor

| Debtor | Creditor |
|---|---|
| Owes money to the business. | Business owes money to them. |
| Asset. | Liability. |
| Created by credit sale. | Created by credit purchase. |

## Revenue Versus Capital Item

| Revenue Item | Capital Item |
|---|---|
| Benefit generally within one year. | Benefit lasts more than one year. |
| Example: salary, rent, electricity. | Example: machinery, building, furniture. |

# Exercise Solutions: Short Answers

## 1. Define accounting.

Accounting is the process of identifying, measuring, recording, and communicating financial information about economic events of an organisation to interested users.

## 2. State the end product of financial accounting.

The main end products are financial statements, especially the profit and loss account and balance sheet. Other reports may also be prepared.

## 3. Main objectives of accounting.

The main objectives are to maintain records, calculate profit or loss, show financial position, and provide information to users.

## 4. Users of accounting information.

Users include management, employees, owners, investors, lenders, creditors, government, tax authorities, customers, social responsibility groups, and competitors.

## 5. Information needed by long-term lenders.

Long-term lenders need information about solvency, profitability, ability to repay principal and interest, cash flows, assets, liabilities, and long-term stability.

## 6. External users.

External users include shareholders, potential investors, lenders, creditors, suppliers, government, tax authorities, regulators, customers, labour unions, trade associations, stock exchanges, and social groups.

## 7. Information needs of management.

Management needs information about costs, sales, profit, budgets, cash flows, inventory, performance, pricing, capital expenditure, and planning.

## 8. Three examples of revenues.

Examples are sales revenue, commission received, and rent received. Other examples include interest received, dividend received, and royalty received.

## 9. Debtors and creditors; profit and gain.

Debtors owe money to the business and are assets. Creditors are owed money by the business and are liabilities.

Profit arises from normal business operations. Gain arises from incidental transactions, such as sale of an asset above its value.

## 10. Why accounting information should be comparable.

Yes, accounting information should be comparable because users need to compare performance across years and with other firms. Common units, common formats, and consistent policies help meaningful comparison.

## 11. If information is not clearly presented, which characteristic is violated?

Understandability is violated because users cannot interpret the information properly.

## 12. Has the role of accounting changed?

Yes. Earlier accounting was mainly record-keeping. Now it is an information system used for planning, decision-making, control, analysis, legal compliance, and social reporting.

## 13. Accounting terms with examples.

# Exercise Solutions: Long Answer Frameworks

## 1. What is accounting? Define its objectives.

Accounting is the process of identifying, measuring, recording, and communicating financial information about economic events of an organisation to users.

Its objectives are:

1. Maintain systematic records.
2. Calculate profit or loss.
3. Show financial position.
4. Provide information to internal and external users.
5. Help decision-making.
6. Support control and accountability.

## 2. Factors that made systematic accounting necessary.

Systematic accounting became necessary because:

1. Business transactions are numerous.
2. Human memory is limited.
3. Owners need profit or loss information.
4. Managers need information for planning and control.
5. Investors and lenders need reliable reports.
6. Government needs tax and legal compliance information.
7. Business size and complexity increased.
8. Records are needed as evidence.

## 3. Informational needs of external users.

| External User | Need |
|---|---|
| Investors | Profitability, risk, return, share performance. |
| Lenders | Solvency, liquidity, loan repayment ability. |
| Creditors | Whether amounts due will be paid. |
| Government | Tax, regulation, resource allocation. |
| Customers | Continuity of supply and business stability. |
| Social groups | Environmental and social impact. |
| Competitors | Benchmarking and strategic comparison. |

## 4. Meaning and types of assets.

Assets are economic resources owned or controlled by the business and expressible in monetary terms.

Types:

1. Current assets: cash, bank, inventory, debtors.
2. Non-current assets: land, building, machinery, furniture, goodwill, patents.
3. Tangible assets: physical assets such as machinery.
4. Intangible assets: non-physical assets such as patents.

## 5. Gain and profit.

Profit is excess of revenue over expenses from normal business operations. Gain is profit from incidental or non-routine events.

Example: A trader earning more sales revenue than expenses earns profit. Selling old machinery for more than its book value creates gain.

## 6. Qualitative characteristics.

Useful accounting information must be:

1. Reliable: free from error and bias, verifiable, neutral, and faithful.
2. Relevant: timely and useful for prediction, feedback, and decisions.
3. Understandable: clearly presented.
4. Comparable: prepared using common units, periods, and formats so users can compare.

## 7. Role of accounting in the modern world.

Accounting acts as:

1. Language of business.
2. Historical record.
3. Information system.
4. Tool for planning and control.
5. Aid to decision-making.
6. Service to users.
7. Basis for tax and legal compliance.
8. Support for accountability and stewardship.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating accounting as only book-keeping. | Book-keeping is only one part of accounting. |
| Recording non-financial events in financial books. | Only monetary business events are recorded. |
| Confusing debtor and creditor. | Debtor owes us; creditor is owed by us. |
| Treating drawings as expense. | Drawings reduce capital, not profit. |
| Treating every expenditure as expense. | Some expenditure creates assets. |
| Confusing profit and gain. | Profit is normal operation; gain is incidental. |
| Ignoring qualitative characteristics. | Useful information must be reliable, relevant, understandable, and comparable. |
| Mixing owner and business records. | Business entity is separate for accounting. |

# Final Study Summary

Accounting is an information system. It identifies, measures, records, and communicates financial information about economic events to users. Its purpose is not merely to keep books, but to support decisions.

Good accounting information must be reliable, relevant, understandable, and comparable. The main objectives of accounting are to maintain records, calculate profit or loss, show financial position, and provide information to users.

Different users need accounting for different reasons. Managers need planning and control information. Investors need return and risk information. Lenders need repayment ability. Government needs tax and legal compliance information.

The chapter also introduces essential vocabulary: entity, transaction, assets, liabilities, capital, revenue, expenses, expenditure, profit, gain, loss, voucher, goods, drawings, purchases, stock, debtors, and creditors. These terms are the foundation for the rest of accountancy.
