# Chapter 5: Bank Reconciliation Statement
### NCERT Class 11 - Accountancy Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

A business records bank transactions in the bank column of the cash book. The bank records the same transactions in its own books and gives the customer a passbook or bank statement. Ideally, both balances should match. In practice, they often differ because some transactions are recorded in one book before the other, or because errors have been made.

A Bank Reconciliation Statement explains this difference.

| Area | Main Idea |
|---|---|
| Cash book | Business record of bank transactions |
| Passbook or bank statement | Bank record of the customer's account |
| Reconciliation | Explaining why the two balances differ |
| Timing difference | Entry appears in one book now and the other book later |
| Error | Wrong or omitted entry by business or bank |
| Favourable balance | Money available in bank |
| Unfavourable balance | Bank overdraft |

## Learning Outcomes

After studying this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning of a Bank Reconciliation Statement.
2. State why bank reconciliation is prepared.
3. Identify the causes of difference between cash book and passbook.
4. Distinguish timing differences from errors.
5. Understand the cash book and passbook view of debit and credit balances.
6. Prepare a BRS from favourable cash book balance.
7. Prepare a BRS from favourable passbook balance.
8. Prepare a BRS when overdraft is given.
9. Decide whether each item should be added or deducted.
10. Understand how to prepare an adjusted cash book before BRS.
11. Solve numerical reconciliation questions systematically.

# Unit 5A: Cash Book and Passbook Relationship

## Business View: Cash Book

In the business cash book:

| Cash Book Bank Column | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Debit balance | Money available at bank, favourable |
| Credit balance | Bank overdraft, unfavourable |

Why?

The cash book is kept from the business point of view. Bank is an asset when the business has money in the bank. Assets normally show debit balances.

## Bank View: Passbook

In the bank passbook:

| Passbook Balance | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Credit balance | Money payable by bank to customer, favourable |
| Debit balance | Money recoverable by bank from customer, overdraft |

Why?

The bank keeps the customer's account from the bank's point of view. If the customer has deposited money, the bank owes money to the customer. Therefore, the customer's account shows a credit balance in bank books.

## Same Balance, Opposite Language

| Situation | Cash Book | Passbook |
|---|---|---|
| Money available in bank | Debit balance | Credit balance |
| Bank overdraft | Credit balance | Debit balance |

This opposite treatment is a major reason learners get confused. Always ask: whose book is this?

# Unit 5B: Meaning and Need of Bank Reconciliation Statement

## Meaning

A Bank Reconciliation Statement is a statement prepared to reconcile the bank balance shown by the cash book with the bank balance shown by the passbook or bank statement.

It does not create a new account. It only explains the difference.

## Why BRS Is Prepared

BRS is prepared to:

1. identify why cash book and passbook balances differ,
2. detect missing bank entries in cash book,
3. detect cheques not yet cleared,
4. detect cheques issued but not yet presented,
5. identify bank charges, bank interest, and direct payments,
6. discover errors made by business or bank,
7. update the cash book,
8. confirm the correct bank position,
9. prevent fraud or unnoticed withdrawals,
10. support internal control and audit.

## What Information Is Needed

To prepare a BRS, you need:

1. balance as per cash book or passbook,
2. date of reconciliation,
3. list of cheques issued but not presented,
4. list of cheques deposited but not collected,
5. bank charges, interest, or direct payments not recorded,
6. direct deposits or collections by bank not recorded,
7. dishonoured cheques,
8. errors in cash book or passbook.

# Unit 5C: Causes of Difference

The difference between cash book and passbook arises mainly because of:

1. timing differences,
2. errors.

## Timing Differences

Timing differences happen when a transaction is recorded in one book but will appear in the other book later.

### Cheques Issued but Not Presented

When a business issues a cheque:

1. business immediately credits bank column in cash book,
2. bank debits the account only when cheque is presented and paid.

Until presentation:

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Cash book | Balance already reduced |
| Passbook | Balance not yet reduced |

Therefore, passbook balance is higher than cash book balance in a favourable balance case.

### Cheques Deposited but Not Collected

When a business deposits a cheque:

1. business immediately debits bank column in cash book,
2. bank credits the account only after collecting the cheque.

Until collection:

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Cash book | Balance already increased |
| Passbook | Balance not yet increased |

Therefore, cash book balance is higher than passbook balance in a favourable balance case.

### Bank Charges Not Recorded in Cash Book

Bank charges are directly deducted by bank.

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Passbook | Balance reduced |
| Cash book | No entry yet |

Therefore, cash book balance is higher than passbook balance until recorded.

### Direct Deposit by Customer

A customer may deposit money directly into the business bank account.

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Passbook | Balance increased |
| Cash book | No entry yet |

Therefore, passbook balance is higher than cash book balance until recorded.

### Interest or Dividend Collected by Bank

Bank may collect interest or dividend on behalf of the business.

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Passbook | Balance increased |
| Cash book | No entry yet |

This item increases passbook before cash book.

### Direct Payment by Bank

Bank may pay insurance premium, rent, tax, telephone bill, or loan instalment under standing instructions.

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Passbook | Balance reduced |
| Cash book | No entry yet |

This item reduces passbook before cash book.

### Dishonoured Cheque

When a cheque deposited by the business is dishonoured:

1. business may have already recorded bank receipt in cash book,
2. bank later reverses the credit or refuses collection.

Until business records dishonour:

| Book | Effect |
|---|---|
| Passbook | Balance reduced or not credited |
| Cash book | Balance may still be higher |

## Errors

Errors may be made by:

1. business in cash book,
2. bank in passbook.

Examples:

| Error | Effect |
|---|---|
| Cheque issued Rs. 5,000 recorded as Rs. 500 in cash book | Cash book balance overstated by Rs. 4,500 |
| Cheque deposited omitted from cash book | Cash book balance understated |
| Bank wrongly debits customer's account | Passbook balance understated |
| Bank wrongly credits customer's account | Passbook balance overstated |
| Cash book total undercast | Cash book balance wrong |
| Passbook withdrawal column overcast | Passbook balance wrong |

# Unit 5D: Favourable and Unfavourable Balances

## Favourable Balance

A favourable balance means the business has money at bank.

| Book | Name |
|---|---|
| Cash book | Debit balance |
| Passbook | Credit balance |

## Unfavourable Balance

An unfavourable balance means the business has an overdraft.

| Book | Name |
|---|---|
| Cash book | Credit balance |
| Passbook | Debit balance |

## Overdraft Meaning

An overdraft is a facility where the bank allows the business to withdraw more than the amount deposited. In substance, the business has borrowed from the bank.

Example:

If bank column of cash book shows a credit balance of Rs. 8,000, it means the business has an overdraft of Rs. 8,000.

# Unit 5E: Basic BRS Format

## One-Column Format

| Particulars | Amount Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Balance as per cash book | xxx |
| Add: items increasing passbook compared with cash book | xxx |
| Less: items decreasing passbook compared with cash book | xxx |
| Balance as per passbook | xxx |

## Plus-Minus Format

| Particulars | Add Rs. | Less Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Balance as per cash book | xxx |  |
| Cheques issued but not presented | xxx |  |
| Cheques deposited but not collected |  | xxx |
| Bank charges not recorded |  | xxx |
| Balance as per passbook |  | xxx |

The plus-minus format is useful because all figures are placed in columns and both sides can be totalled.

# Unit 5F: Add or Deduct Rules From Cash Book Favourable Balance

When starting with favourable balance as per cash book, ask:

Will this item make passbook balance higher or lower than cash book?

## Add to Cash Book Balance

Add items that make passbook higher than cash book:

| Item | Why Add? |
|---|---|
| Cheques issued but not presented | Cash book reduced, passbook not yet reduced |
| Direct deposit by customer | Passbook increased, cash book not yet increased |
| Interest or dividend collected by bank | Passbook increased first |
| Bank wrongly credited account | Passbook increased wrongly |
| Cheque issued entered twice in cash book | Cash book reduced too much |

## Deduct From Cash Book Balance

Deduct items that make passbook lower than cash book:

| Item | Why Deduct? |
|---|---|
| Cheques deposited but not collected | Cash book increased, passbook not yet increased |
| Bank charges | Passbook reduced first |
| Interest on overdraft | Passbook reduced first |
| Direct payment by bank | Passbook reduced first |
| Cheque deposited dishonoured | Passbook lower than cash book |
| Bank wrongly debited account | Passbook reduced wrongly |
| Cheque deposited entered twice in cash book | Cash book increased too much |

# Unit 5G: Reverse Rules From Passbook Favourable Balance

When starting with favourable balance as per passbook, reverse the treatment used from cash book.

| Item | From Cash Book Start | From Passbook Start |
|---|---|---|
| Cheques issued but not presented | Add | Deduct |
| Cheques deposited but not collected | Deduct | Add |
| Bank charges not in cash book | Deduct | Add |
| Direct deposit not in cash book | Add | Deduct |
| Interest credited by bank | Add | Deduct |
| Direct payment by bank | Deduct | Add |
| Cheque dishonoured | Deduct | Add |

Memory rule:

If starting point changes from cash book to passbook, the sign reverses.

# Unit 5H: Handling Overdraft

## Overdraft Sign Method

The clearest method is to treat overdraft as negative.

Example:

Overdraft as per cash book = Rs. 8,000.

Start with `-8,000`.

Then apply normal add/deduct logic.

At the end:

1. positive answer = favourable balance,
2. negative answer = overdraft.

## Overdraft From Cash Book

Start with overdraft as per cash book:

| Item | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Cheques issued but not presented | Add, because passbook overdraft is lower |
| Cheques deposited but not collected | Deduct, because passbook overdraft is higher |
| Bank charges | Deduct, because bank has increased overdraft |
| Direct deposit by customer | Add, because bank has reduced overdraft |
| Interest credited by bank | Add |
| Direct payment by bank | Deduct |

## Overdraft From Passbook

If starting with overdraft as per passbook, reverse the above.

Use the sign method whenever confused.

# Unit 5K: Solved Example 3 - Overdraft as Per Cash Book

## Question

On March 31, Rakesh had overdraft as per cash book Rs. 8,000.

1. Cheques deposited but not collected Rs. 2,000.
2. Cheques issued but not presented Rs. 800.
3. Interest charged by bank Rs. 60.
4. Bank charges Rs. 100.

Prepare BRS.

## Solution Using Sign Logic

Start:

`Overdraft as per cash book = -8,000`

Adjustments:

1. Cheques deposited but not collected reduce passbook compared with cash book: `-2,000`.
2. Cheques issued but not presented increase passbook compared with cash book: `+800`.
3. Bank interest charged reduces passbook: `-60`.
4. Bank charges reduce passbook: `-100`.

Calculation:

`-8,000 - 2,000 + 800 - 60 - 100 = -9,360`

Answer:

Overdraft as per passbook = Rs. 9,360.

## Statement Format

| Particulars | Add Rs. | Less Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Overdraft as per cash book |  | 8,000 |
| Cheques deposited but not collected |  | 2,000 |
| Bank interest |  | 60 |
| Bank charges |  | 100 |
| Cheques issued but not presented | 800 |  |
| Overdraft as per passbook | 9,360 |  |
| Total | 10,160 | 10,160 |

# Unit 5L: Solved Example 4 - Overdraft Changes Into Favourable Balance

## Question

Cash book of Agrawal Traders showed overdraft Rs. 1,18,100.

1. Cheques received and recorded in cash book but not sent to bank for collection Rs. 12,400.
2. Payment received directly by bank Rs. 27,300, no entry in cash book.
3. Cheques issued but not presented Rs. 1,75,200.
4. Interest on overdraft charged by bank Rs. 8,800, not in cash book.

Prepare BRS.

## Solution Using Sign Logic

Start:

`Overdraft as per cash book = -1,18,100`

Adjustments:

1. Cheques received but not sent to bank: passbook lower, deduct Rs. 12,400.
2. Direct payment received by bank: passbook higher, add Rs. 27,300.
3. Cheques issued not presented: passbook higher, add Rs. 1,75,200.
4. Interest charged by bank: passbook lower, deduct Rs. 8,800.

Calculation:

`-1,18,100 - 12,400 + 27,300 + 1,75,200 - 8,800 = 63,200`

Answer:

Balance as per passbook = Rs. 63,200 favourable.

Prepare BRS from:

# Unit 5N: Adjusted Cash Book Method

## Meaning

Sometimes the cash book is updated first. This updated cash book is called adjusted cash book. After that, BRS is prepared only for remaining timing differences and bank errors.

## Items Usually Entered in Adjusted Cash Book

Enter items that the business should have recorded but did not:

| Item | Cash Book Entry |
|---|---|
| Bank charges | Credit bank column |
| Interest on overdraft | Credit bank column |
| Direct payment by bank | Credit bank column |
| Insurance paid by bank | Credit bank column |
| Direct deposit by customer | Debit bank column |
| Interest collected by bank | Debit bank column |
| Dividend collected by bank | Debit bank column |
| Dishonoured cheque | Credit bank column and debit customer |
| Error in cash book | Correct in cash book |

## Items Not Entered in Adjusted Cash Book

Do not enter pure timing differences if they are already correctly recorded in cash book:

1. cheques issued but not presented,
2. cheques deposited but not collected,
3. cheques deposited but bank will credit later,
4. bank errors, unless bank has corrected them and business must respond.

## Process

1. Start with existing cash book bank balance.
2. Record all missing bank entries that belong in cash book.
3. Correct cash book errors.
4. Find adjusted cash book balance.
5. Prepare BRS from adjusted cash book balance using only remaining items.

# Unit 5O: How to Decide Add or Deduct

## Three-Step Test

For each item, ask:

1. Which book has recorded it?
2. Which book has not recorded it?
3. Are we moving from starting book to target book?

## Example 1

Starting point: cash book favourable balance.

Item: cheque issued but not presented.

Cash book has recorded payment. Passbook has not. Therefore passbook balance is higher. Add.

## Example 2

Starting point: cash book favourable balance.

Item: bank charges not recorded.

Passbook has recorded deduction. Cash book has not. Therefore passbook balance is lower. Deduct.

## Example 3

Starting point: passbook favourable balance.

Item: bank charges not recorded.

Passbook is lower because of charges. Cash book is higher. To move from passbook to cash book, add.

# Unit 5P: Error Treatment Guide

## Errors in Cash Book

If cash book has an error and cash book is the starting point, correct its effect in BRS unless an adjusted cash book is prepared.

Examples:

| Error | Effect | Treatment From Cash Book Favourable Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Cheque issued Rs. 250 recorded as Rs. 205 | Cash book is Rs. 45 too high | Deduct Rs. 45 |
| Cheque issued recorded twice | Cash book is too low | Add |
| Cheque deposited omitted | Cash book too low | Add |
| Payment side undercast | Cash book too high | Deduct |
| Debit balance brought forward as credit balance | Cash book too low by double amount | Add double difference |

## Errors in Passbook

Passbook errors affect the target balance if passbook is being found.

| Error | Meaning | Treatment From Cash Book Favourable Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Bank wrongly debited business | Passbook too low | Deduct |
| Bank wrongly credited business | Passbook too high | Add |
| Withdrawal column undercast | Passbook too high | Add if finding passbook from cash book? No, analyse with target |
| Deposit recorded excess by bank | Passbook too high | Add if passbook target must include bank error; deduct if finding correct balance |

Important:

For bank errors, check whether the question asks for balance as per passbook as shown, or correct bank balance. Most school BRS questions reconcile to the balance shown by the passbook, including bank errors until corrected.

# Unit 5Q: Short Answer Solutions

## 1. Need for preparation of BRS

BRS is needed to explain the difference between bank balance as per cash book and bank balance as per passbook. It helps detect timing differences, missing entries, bank charges, direct deposits, dishonoured cheques, and errors. It also helps the business know the correct bank position.

## 2. What is bank overdraft?

Bank overdraft is the amount by which withdrawals from bank exceed deposits. In cash book, overdraft appears as credit balance in the bank column. In passbook, it appears as debit balance.

## 3. Meaning of "wrongly debited by the bank"

Wrongly debited by the bank means the bank has reduced the customer's balance by mistake.

Example:

Bank debits Rs. 500 to A's account for charges that belong to another customer. A's passbook balance becomes Rs. 500 lower than it should be. This creates a difference between cash book and passbook.

## 4. Causes of difference due to time lag

Time lag differences include:

1. cheques issued but not presented,
2. cheques deposited but not collected,
3. bank charges not yet recorded in cash book,
4. direct deposits not yet recorded in cash book,
5. interest or dividend collected by bank,
6. direct payments by bank,
7. dishonoured cheques known through bank statement.

## 5. Favourable balance as per cash book

Favourable balance as per cash book means debit balance in the bank column of the cash book. It indicates money available in the bank.

## 6. Steps to ascertain correct cash book balance

1. Compare cash book with passbook.
2. Identify items recorded in passbook but missing in cash book.
3. Identify errors in cash book.
4. Record missing bank charges, direct payments, collections, interest, and dishonours.
5. Correct cash book errors.
6. Find adjusted cash book balance.
7. Prepare BRS for remaining timing differences.

# Unit 5R: Long Answer Solutions

## 1. What is BRS and why is it prepared?

A Bank Reconciliation Statement is a statement prepared to reconcile the bank balance shown by the cash book with the balance shown by the passbook or bank statement.

It is prepared because both records are maintained by different parties and entries may be recorded at different times. For example, a business records a cheque issued immediately, but the bank records it only when presented. Similarly, the bank may debit charges or collect interest before the business records it.

BRS is prepared to locate these differences, detect errors, update cash book, prevent fraud, and confirm the correct bank balance.

## 2. Reasons why passbook and cash book balances differ

Balances differ because of timing differences and errors.

Timing differences include cheques issued but not presented, cheques deposited but not collected, bank charges, interest collected by bank, direct deposits, direct payments, and dishonoured cheques.

Errors may be made by the business or by the bank. The business may record wrong amounts, omit entries, record an item in the wrong column, or total the cash book incorrectly. The bank may wrongly debit or credit the account, omit entries, or make posting errors.

## 3. Process of preparing BRS with amended cash balance

When preparing BRS with amended cash balance, first update the cash book. Record all missing items that should appear in cash book, such as bank charges, interest, direct deposits, direct payments, dishonoured cheques, and cash book errors.

After this, compute the adjusted cash book balance. Then prepare BRS using only items that still explain the difference between adjusted cash book and passbook, mainly cheques issued but not presented and cheques deposited but not collected.

# Unit 5U: Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---|
| Treating cash book debit and passbook debit as same | Remember cash book and passbook are opposite viewpoints |
| Adding every unpresented cheque blindly | Add only when starting from cash book favourable balance; reverse from passbook |
| Deducting every bank charge blindly | Deduct from cash book favourable start; reverse from passbook start |
| Forgetting overdraft is negative | Use sign method |
| Recording timing differences in adjusted cash book | Only record items that should update cash book |
| Treating passbook as business book | Passbook is bank's record of customer's account |
| Ignoring direct deposits | They increase passbook before cash book |
| Ignoring dishonoured cheques | They reduce bank balance compared with cash book |
| Confusing uncollected and unpresented cheques | Deposited but not collected reduces passbook; issued but not presented keeps passbook higher |
| Not writing date in BRS heading | BRS is always prepared as on a particular date |

# Unit 5V: Fast Revision Sheet

## From Cash Book Favourable Balance

| Add | Less |
|---|---|
| Cheques issued but not presented | Cheques deposited but not collected |
| Direct deposits by customers | Bank charges |
| Interest or dividend collected by bank | Direct payments by bank |
| Bank wrongly credited | Cheque dishonoured |
| Cheque issued recorded twice in cash book | Bank wrongly debited |

## From Passbook Favourable Balance

| Add | Less |
|---|---|
| Cheques deposited but not collected | Cheques issued but not presented |
| Bank charges | Direct deposits |
| Direct payments by bank | Interest or dividend collected by bank |
| Cheque dishonoured | Bank wrongly credited |

## Balance Meaning

| Balance | Cash Book | Passbook |
|---|---|---|
| Favourable | Debit | Credit |
| Overdraft | Credit | Debit |

## Final Study Summary

Bank reconciliation is not a new accounting entry by itself. It is an explanation of why two records of the same bank account show different balances.

Most differences arise because entries are recorded at different times. Cheques issued may not yet be presented. Cheques deposited may not yet be collected. Banks may debit charges or credit interest before the business records them. Customers may directly deposit money into the bank.

The safest way to solve BRS is to identify the starting balance, decide whether the target book would be higher or lower, and use the sign method for overdrafts. Once the logic is clear, the statement format becomes mechanical.
