# Chapter 7: Depreciation, Provisions and Reserves
### NCERT Class 11 - Accountancy Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

This chapter explains three ideas that are essential for preparing correct financial statements:

1. Depreciation spreads the cost of fixed assets over the years in which they help the business earn revenue.
2. Provisions record expected expenses or losses whose amount is uncertain.
3. Reserves retain part of profit inside the business for strength, stability, legal needs, or future plans.

The central accounting idea is matching. If an asset gives benefit for many years, the whole cost should not be charged in one year. If a future loss is expected from current-period transactions, it should not be ignored. If profit is retained for business strength, it should be clearly separated from provisions.

# Unit 7A: Why Depreciation Is Needed

## The Matching Problem

A fixed asset usually helps the business for more than one accounting year.

Example:

A machine is purchased for Rs. 1,00,000. It is expected to be used for 10 years.

The business should not charge the whole Rs. 1,00,000 to the first year because the machine will not benefit only the first year. The fair approach is to spread the cost over the years that receive benefit.

This yearly charge is called depreciation.

## Simple Idea

Depreciation means:

1. part of the cost of a fixed asset has expired,
2. that expired cost belongs to the current accounting period,
3. it should be charged to profit and loss account,
4. the remaining unexpired cost is carried forward as asset value.

## Depreciation Is Not a Market Valuation

Depreciation is based on cost allocation, not market price.

If a machine was bought for Rs. 5,00,000, depreciation is calculated using its cost, useful life, and estimated residual value. The accountant does not stop charging depreciation merely because the machine's market value temporarily rises.

## Depreciation Is a Non-Cash Expense

Depreciation reduces profit but does not require a fresh cash payment when it is recorded.

Cash usually left the business when the asset was purchased. Depreciation only records the cost consumed during the year.

## Depreciation in One Sentence

Depreciation is the process of allocating the depreciable cost of a fixed asset over its estimated useful life.

# Unit 7B: Meaning and Features of Depreciation

## Meaning of Depreciation

Depreciation is a permanent, continuing, and gradual decrease in the book value of a fixed asset.

It happens because the asset is used, time passes, technology changes, demand changes, or the asset becomes less useful.

In accounting, depreciation is the expired portion of the cost of a fixed asset.

## Important Points in the Meaning

1. It applies to fixed assets, not current assets.
2. It is linked with use, time, and obsolescence.
3. It is based on book value and cost allocation.
4. It is not the same as a fall in market value.
5. It is a charge against profit.
6. It is recorded every accounting period during the asset's useful life.

## Features of Depreciation

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Decline in book value | Depreciation reduces the carrying value of a fixed asset |
| Permanent decrease | It is not treated as a temporary change in market price |
| Continuing process | It is recorded year after year during useful life |
| Gradual process | Normal depreciation happens over time, not suddenly |
| Expense | It is debited to profit and loss account |
| Non-cash item | Recording it does not involve current cash outflow |
| Cost allocation | It spreads historical cost over useful life |
| Not valuation | It does not attempt to show exact current market price |

## Depreciable Assets

A depreciable asset usually has these characteristics:

1. It is expected to be used for more than one accounting period.
2. It has a limited useful life.
3. It is held for production, supply of goods or services, rental, or administration.
4. It is not held for resale in the ordinary course of business.

Examples:

1. plant,
2. machinery,
3. furniture,
4. building,
5. computer,
6. trucks,
7. vans,
8. equipment,
9. office machines,
10. factory tools.

## Assets Normally Not Depreciated in the Same Way

| Item | Reason |
|---|---|
| Stock | It is current asset held for sale |
| Debtors | It is receivable, not a fixed asset |
| Cash | It is money, not a depreciable asset |
| Freehold land | It usually has unlimited useful life unless value is being exhausted or special conditions apply |
| Natural resources | Their expiry is treated as depletion |
| Intangible rights | Their cost is usually written off through amortisation |

# Unit 7C: Depreciation, Depletion, and Amortisation

## Why These Terms Are Similar

Depreciation, depletion, and amortisation all deal with the same broad accounting problem:

An asset gives benefit over time, and its cost must be written off over the period of benefit.

The difference lies in the type of asset.

## Depreciation

Depreciation is used for tangible fixed assets.

Examples:

1. machinery,
2. vehicle,
3. furniture,
4. building,
5. office equipment.

Example:

A printing machine costing Rs. 4,00,000 is expected to be useful for 8 years. A part of its cost is charged every year as depreciation.

## Depletion

Depletion is used for wasting natural resources.

Examples:

1. coal mine,
2. oil well,
3. quarry,
4. mineral deposit.

Example:

A business buys a coal mine. As coal is extracted, the quantity of coal remaining decreases. This reduction in economic resource is depletion.

## Amortisation

Amortisation is used for writing off intangible assets over their useful period.

Examples:

1. patent,
2. copyright,
3. trademark,
4. franchise right,
5. lease right,
6. goodwill, when it has to be written off over a useful period.

Example:

A patent is bought for Rs. 10,00,000 and is useful for 10 years. The business may write off Rs. 1,00,000 each year as amortisation if straight line allocation is used.

## Comparison Table

| Basis | Depreciation | Depletion | Amortisation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Asset type | Tangible fixed assets | Natural resources | Intangible assets |
| Main cause | Use, time, obsolescence | Extraction or exhaustion | Expiry of legal or economic benefit |
| Example | Machinery | Coal mine | Patent |
| Accounting purpose | Allocate cost over useful life | Allocate cost over quantity or period exhausted | Write off intangible cost over useful life |

# Unit 7D: Causes of Depreciation

## Main Causes

Depreciation may arise because of:

1. wear and tear due to use,
2. passage of time,
3. expiry of legal rights,
4. obsolescence,
5. abnormal factors.

## 1. Wear and Tear Due to Use

When an asset is used in business, its physical condition and operating efficiency decline.

Examples:

1. a delivery van loses efficiency after repeated use,
2. a machine part wears out due to production,
3. furniture becomes loose after years of office use,
4. computers slow down or need replacement after heavy use.

This is the most common cause of depreciation for plant, machinery, vehicles, and equipment.

## 2. Passage of Time

Some assets lose usefulness even when not heavily used.

Examples:

1. a building may deteriorate due to weather,
2. furniture may age even if used lightly,
3. a vehicle may lose value as time passes,
4. machines may rust if stored improperly.

Time-related deterioration is especially important for assets exposed to heat, moisture, rain, dust, or other natural conditions.

## 3. Expiry of Legal Rights

Some assets are valuable only for a fixed legal or contractual period.

Examples:

1. patent rights,
2. copyright,
3. leasehold property,
4. franchise rights.

When the legal right expires, the asset may lose its business value even if it still exists as a document or legal record.

## 4. Obsolescence

Obsolescence means becoming outdated.

An asset may still work physically but become uneconomical or unsuitable because better alternatives are available.

Causes of obsolescence include:

1. new technology,
2. improved production methods,
3. changes in customer demand,
4. legal restrictions,
5. change in business process,
6. newer models with lower running cost.

Example:

A business buys a machine. A new machine enters the market that produces double output at lower cost. The old machine may lose usefulness even if it still operates.

## 5. Abnormal Factors

Abnormal factors cause sudden loss in value.

Examples:

1. fire,
2. flood,
3. earthquake,
4. accident,
5. theft,
6. unexpected damage.

Normal depreciation is gradual and continuing. Abnormal loss may be sudden. Still, if it reduces asset value, accounting must record the loss correctly.

# Unit 7E: Need for Charging Depreciation

## 1. Matching Cost with Revenue

Fixed assets help generate revenue over several accounting periods.

Therefore, the cost consumed in each period should be charged to that period.

If depreciation is ignored:

1. profit will be overstated,
2. asset value will be overstated,
3. financial statements will not show a fair position,
4. cost of using fixed assets will be hidden.

## 2. Correct Profit or Loss

Depreciation is an expense. Profit cannot be correctly calculated without deducting all expenses of the period.

Example:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Profit before depreciation | 50,000 |
| Less: Depreciation | 10,000 |
| Profit after depreciation | 40,000 |

If depreciation is ignored, profit appears as Rs. 50,000 instead of Rs. 40,000.

## 3. Correct Balance Sheet Value

If depreciation is not recorded, fixed assets remain at cost even though a part of their service potential has been consumed.

This leads to overstatement of assets.

Example:

A machine costs Rs. 2,00,000. Depreciation for the year is Rs. 20,000.

Correct balance sheet value after one year should be Rs. 1,80,000 if depreciation is charged directly to the asset account.

## 4. Tax Calculation

Depreciation is normally allowed as a deduction while calculating taxable income, subject to tax rules.

Accounting depreciation and tax depreciation may not always be identical, but depreciation remains important for profit and tax computation.

## 5. Legal Compliance

Some forms of business are required by law or accounting rules to provide depreciation before determining distributable profit.

This prevents distribution of capital as if it were profit.

## 6. Capital Maintenance

Depreciation helps the business recognise that part of the asset's cost has been consumed.

Important note:

Depreciation does not automatically create cash for replacing the asset. Cash will be available only if the business earns and retains funds. Depreciation is an accounting charge, not a separate bank deposit.

# Unit 7F: Factors Affecting Amount of Depreciation

The amount of depreciation mainly depends on:

1. cost of asset,
2. estimated net residual value,
3. depreciable cost,
4. estimated useful life,
5. method of depreciation.

## 1. Cost of Asset

Cost means the full amount spent to bring the asset into usable condition.

It includes:

1. purchase price,
2. freight,
3. carriage,
4. transit insurance,
5. installation cost,
6. registration cost,
7. commissioning expenses,
8. initial repair of a second-hand asset needed before use,
9. additions or improvements that increase capacity or useful life,
10. other directly attributable costs needed to make the asset ready for use.

## Example: Cost of Asset

A machine is purchased for Rs. 50,000. Transportation and installation cost Rs. 5,000.

Cost of machine = Rs. 50,000 + Rs. 5,000 = Rs. 55,000

Depreciation should be based on Rs. 55,000, not only on invoice price.

## 2. Estimated Net Residual Value

Residual value is the amount expected to be realised when the asset is sold at the end of its useful life.

Net residual value means:

Estimated selling value - estimated disposal expenses

Example:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Expected sale value at end of life | 6,000 |
| Less: Expected disposal expenses | 1,000 |
| Net residual value | 5,000 |

## 3. Depreciable Cost

Depreciable cost is the amount that must be written off over useful life.

Formula:

`Depreciable cost = Cost of asset - Net residual value`

Example:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Cost of machine | 50,000 |
| Less: Net residual value | 5,000 |
| Depreciable cost | 45,000 |

The total depreciation charged over the useful life should normally equal Rs. 45,000 in this example.

## 4. Estimated Useful Life

Useful life is the period over which the asset is expected to provide economic benefit to the business.

Useful life may be expressed in:

1. number of years,
2. number of units produced,
3. number of working hours,
4. quantity extracted, in case of natural resources.

Useful life is often shorter than physical life.

Example:

A machine may physically run for 12 years, but after 5 years it may become uneconomical because repairs and production cost become too high. Its useful life may be 5 years.

## Factors Affecting Useful Life

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Number of shifts | More shifts usually reduce useful life |
| Maintenance policy | Better maintenance may extend useful life |
| Technological change | Faster change may shorten useful life |
| Legal limits | Lease period or license period may restrict life |
| Market demand | Falling demand may reduce economic usefulness |
| Production method | Better methods may make old assets unsuitable |

# Unit 7G: Straight Line Method

## Meaning

Under straight line method, a fixed and equal amount of depreciation is charged every year over the useful life of the asset.

It is also called:

1. fixed instalment method,
2. fixed percentage on original cost method.

## Core Assumption

The method assumes that the asset gives equal benefit in each year of its useful life.

## Formula

`Annual depreciation = (Cost of asset - Estimated net residual value) / Estimated useful life`

`Rate of depreciation = (Annual depreciation / Cost of asset) x 100`

## Example 1: Straight Line Method

Cost of asset = Rs. 2,50,000

Estimated residual value = Rs. 50,000

Useful life = 10 years

Calculation:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Cost of asset | 2,50,000 |
| Less: Residual value | 50,000 |
| Depreciable cost | 2,00,000 |

Annual depreciation = Rs. 2,00,000 / 10 = Rs. 20,000

Rate of depreciation = Rs. 20,000 / Rs. 2,50,000 x 100 = 8%

## Depreciation Schedule Under Straight Line Method

| Year | Opening Book Value Rs. | Depreciation Rs. | Closing Book Value Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 2,50,000 | 20,000 | 2,30,000 |
| 2 | 2,30,000 | 20,000 | 2,10,000 |
| 3 | 2,10,000 | 20,000 | 1,90,000 |
| 4 | 1,90,000 | 20,000 | 1,70,000 |
| 5 | 1,70,000 | 20,000 | 1,50,000 |
| 6 | 1,50,000 | 20,000 | 1,30,000 |
| 7 | 1,30,000 | 20,000 | 1,10,000 |
| 8 | 1,10,000 | 20,000 | 90,000 |
| 9 | 90,000 | 20,000 | 70,000 |
| 10 | 70,000 | 20,000 | 50,000 |

The asset reaches the estimated residual value of Rs. 50,000 after 10 years.

## Advantages of Straight Line Method

1. It is simple to understand.
2. It is easy to calculate.
3. Equal depreciation makes yearly profit comparison easier.
4. It can write the asset down to residual value or zero.
5. It is useful when asset use is stable each year.
6. It is suitable for assets whose useful life can be estimated reliably.

## Limitations of Straight Line Method

1. It assumes equal benefit every year, which may not be realistic.
2. It ignores increasing repair cost in later years.
3. Total charge of depreciation plus repairs may rise in later years.
4. It may be unsuitable for machinery or vehicles that lose efficiency faster in early years.
5. It may not be accepted for some tax purposes where law requires another basis.

## When Straight Line Method Is Suitable

It is suitable when:

1. asset use is almost uniform,
2. repairs do not rise sharply,
3. obsolescence risk is low,
4. residual value depends mainly on time,
5. benefit is received evenly.

Examples:

1. leasehold property,
2. patents with fixed useful life,
3. some buildings,
4. furniture in stable use.

# Unit 7H: Written Down Value Method

## Meaning

Under written down value method, depreciation is calculated at a fixed percentage on the book value of the asset at the beginning of each year.

It is also called:

1. reducing balance method,
2. diminishing balance method,
3. reducing instalment method.

## Core Assumption

The method assumes that an asset gives higher benefit in earlier years and lower benefit in later years.

Therefore, depreciation is higher in earlier years and gradually decreases.

## Formula

`Depreciation = Opening written down value x Rate of depreciation`

`Closing written down value = Opening written down value - Depreciation`

## Example 2: Written Down Value Method

Cost of asset = Rs. 2,00,000

Rate of depreciation = 10% p.a.

| Year | Opening WDV Rs. | Depreciation at 10% Rs. | Closing WDV Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 2,00,000 | 20,000 | 1,80,000 |
| 2 | 1,80,000 | 18,000 | 1,62,000 |
| 3 | 1,62,000 | 16,200 | 1,45,800 |

The amount of depreciation decreases every year because the base amount decreases every year.

## Rate Formula Under Written Down Value Method

When cost, residual value, and useful life are known, the rate can be found by this formula:

`Rate = [1 - nth root of (Scrap value / Cost)] x 100`

Where:

1. `n` means useful life in years,
2. scrap value means expected residual value,
3. cost means cost of the asset.

## Example 3: WDV Rate

Cost of truck = Rs. 9,00,000

Expected salvage value after 16 years = Rs. 50,000

Useful life = 16 years

Using the WDV rate formula, the rate is approximately 16.6% p.a.

The exact calculation requires taking the 16th root of the ratio Rs. 50,000 / Rs. 9,00,000.

## Advantages of Written Down Value Method

1. It recognises that assets often give more benefit in early years.
2. It charges higher depreciation when the asset is newer and more efficient.
3. It charges lower depreciation in later years when repair cost is usually higher.
4. Depreciation plus repair cost may remain more balanced over time.
5. It reduces risk from obsolescence because more cost is written off early.
6. It is widely used for machinery, vehicles, and equipment.
7. It is accepted for many tax calculations, subject to applicable rules.

## Limitations of Written Down Value Method

1. The asset is never reduced to exact zero if a fixed percentage is applied forever.
2. The suitable rate may be difficult to calculate.
3. Yearly depreciation is not equal, so profit comparison needs care.
4. It may be less simple for beginners than straight line method.
5. It requires tracking opening written down value every year.

## When Written Down Value Method Is Suitable

It is suitable when:

1. repair cost rises over time,
2. the asset loses usefulness faster in early years,
3. technology changes quickly,
4. obsolescence risk is high,
5. asset is machinery, vehicle, computer, or equipment,
6. business wants a more balanced total charge of depreciation plus repairs.

# Unit 7I: Straight Line Method and WDV Method Compared

## Core Difference

Straight line method charges depreciation on original cost or depreciable cost.

Written down value method charges depreciation on opening book value each year.

## Comparison Table

| Basis | Straight Line Method | Written Down Value Method |
|---|---|---|
| Base for depreciation | Original cost or depreciable cost | Opening book value |
| Amount of depreciation | Same every year | Declines every year |
| Book value | Reduces by equal amount | Reduces by decreasing amount |
| Asset value at end | Can reach residual value or zero | Does not reach zero by fixed percentage |
| Repair plus depreciation | Usually increases in later years | Tends to remain more balanced |
| Simplicity | Very simple | Slightly more complex |
| Suitability | Assets with stable use | Assets with rising repairs or fast obsolescence |
| Examples | Leasehold building, patent, furniture | Machinery, vehicles, computers |

## Which Method Should Be Chosen?

Choose straight line method when:

1. asset benefit is equal each year,
2. useful life is predictable,
3. repairs are not expected to rise sharply,
4. simplicity is important.

Choose written down value method when:

1. asset benefit declines over time,
2. repair cost increases with age,
3. obsolescence is likely,
4. higher early depreciation is more realistic.

## Common Exam Point

If a question says that repair and maintenance expenses will rise in later years, written down value method is usually more suitable.

Reason:

Depreciation is high in early years and low in later years. Repairs are low in early years and high in later years. Together, the total charge becomes more even.

# Unit 7J: Recording Depreciation

Depreciation can be recorded in two main ways:

1. charging depreciation directly to asset account,
2. creating provision for depreciation or accumulated depreciation account.

## Method 1: Charging Depreciation Directly to Asset Account

Under this method, depreciation is credited to the asset account itself.

The asset account shows written down value after depreciation.

## Journal Entries

### 1. Purchase of Asset

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Asset A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Bank/Vendor A/c |  | xxx |

Narration:

Asset purchased including directly attributable cost.

### 2. Depreciation at Year End

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Depreciation A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Asset A/c |  | xxx |

Narration:

Depreciation charged on asset.

### 3. Transfer to Profit and Loss Account

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |

Narration:

Depreciation transferred to profit and loss account.

## Balance Sheet Treatment

The asset appears at net book value:

`Cost - depreciation charged till date`

Example:

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Machinery cost | 5,50,000 |
| Less: Depreciation charged | 54,000 |
| Machinery shown in balance sheet | 4,96,000 |

## Method 2: Provision for Depreciation Account

Under this method, depreciation is accumulated in a separate account.

The asset account continues to show original cost. The accumulated depreciation is shown separately.

This accumulated depreciation account may be called:

1. provision for depreciation account,
2. accumulated depreciation account.

## Journal Entries

### 1. Purchase of Asset

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Asset A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Bank/Vendor A/c |  | xxx |

### 2. Recording Depreciation

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Depreciation A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Provision for Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |

### 3. Transfer to Profit and Loss Account

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |

## Balance Sheet Treatment

There are two common presentations:

### Presentation 1: Deduction from Asset

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Machinery at cost | 5,50,000 |
| Less: Provision for depreciation | 54,000 |
| Written down value | 4,96,000 |

### Presentation 2: Provision on Liabilities Side

The asset may appear at cost on the asset side, while provision for depreciation appears separately on the liabilities side.

The modern and clearer method is usually to show accumulated depreciation as a deduction from the asset.

## Difference Between the Two Recording Methods

| Basis | Direct Asset Method | Provision Method |
|---|---|---|
| Asset account | Reduced by depreciation | Remains at original cost |
| Depreciation credit | Credited to asset account | Credited to provision for depreciation |
| Balance sheet | Asset shown at WDV | Asset at cost less provision, or provision shown separately |
| Usefulness | Simple | Shows cost and accumulated depreciation separately |

# Unit 7K: Worked Example - Straight Line With Direct Asset Method

## Problem

Singhania and Bros. purchased a plant for Rs. 5,00,000 on April 1 and spent Rs. 50,000 on installation. Estimated salvage value is Rs. 10,000 after 10 years. Depreciation is charged using straight line method. Books close on March 31.

Prepare journal entries and show plant account for the first three years when depreciation is charged to the asset account.

## Step 1: Calculate Cost

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Purchase price | 5,00,000 |
| Installation cost | 50,000 |
| Total cost | 5,50,000 |

## Step 2: Calculate Annual Depreciation

`Annual depreciation = (Cost - Salvage value) / Useful life`

`Annual depreciation = (Rs. 5,50,000 - Rs. 10,000) / 10`

`Annual depreciation = Rs. 54,000`

# Unit 7R: Additions, Extensions, and Repairs

## Meaning

During use, an existing asset may require:

1. addition,
2. extension,
3. improvement,
4. replacement of an important part,
5. routine maintenance.

The accounting treatment depends on whether the spending improves the asset or only maintains it.

## Capital Expenditure

Spending is capitalised when it:

1. increases capacity,
2. improves efficiency,
3. extends useful life,
4. makes the asset suitable for better operations,
5. becomes an integral part of the asset,
6. creates a separately usable component.

Capitalised amount is added to the asset account and depreciated.

## Revenue Expenditure

Spending is treated as revenue expense when it:

1. only maintains normal working condition,
2. is routine repair,
3. does not improve capacity,
4. does not extend useful life,
5. is recurring maintenance.

Revenue expenditure is debited to profit and loss account.

## Treatment of Addition or Extension

| Situation | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Addition becomes integral part of existing asset | Add to asset cost and depreciate over useful life of asset or at same rate |
| Addition has separate identity and can be used independently | Depreciate separately over its own useful life |
| Routine repair | Charge to profit and loss account |
| Replacement improves efficiency or capacity | Capitalise if it gives future benefit |
| Replacement only restores normal condition | Treat as repair expense |

## Worked Example: Modification and Routine Repair

## Problem

Digital Studio bought a machine for Rs. 8,00,000 on April 1, 2013. Depreciation is 20% p.a. on original cost. On April 1, 2015, a modification costing Rs. 80,000 was made to improve efficiency. Routine maintenance during the year was Rs. 2,000. Books close on March 31, 2016.

## Treatment

Modification cost is capitalised because it improves the asset.

Routine maintenance is revenue expense because it only maintains the asset.

## Depreciation

Depreciation on original machine:

`20% of Rs. 8,00,000 = Rs. 1,60,000`

Depreciation on modification:

`20% of Rs. 80,000 = Rs. 16,000`

Total depreciation for 2015-16:

`Rs. 1,60,000 + Rs. 16,000 = Rs. 1,76,000`

## Amount Charged to Profit and Loss Account

| Particulars | Rs. |
|---|---:|
| Depreciation | 1,76,000 |
| Routine maintenance | 2,000 |
| Total charge | 1,78,000 |

# Unit 7S: Provisions

## Meaning

A provision is an amount set aside by charging profit for a known liability or expected loss whose exact amount is uncertain.

It is made because the loss or liability belongs to the current accounting period, even though the exact amount may be known later.

## Simple Definition

Provision = charge against profit for a known liability or expected loss with uncertain amount.

## Why Provisions Are Needed

Provisions are needed to:

1. follow prudence,
2. avoid overstating profit,
3. recognise expected losses,
4. match expenses with the correct period,
5. show assets and liabilities fairly,
6. prepare reliable financial statements.

## Examples of Provisions

1. provision for doubtful debts,
2. provision for depreciation,
3. provision for taxation,
4. provision for discount on debtors,
5. provision for repairs and renewals,
6. provision for warranty claims, where applicable.

## Features of Provision

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Charge against profit | It is deducted before calculating true profit |
| Created for known liability or expected loss | The reason is known, amount is estimated |
| Created even if there is loss | It is necessary for correct financial statements |
| Reduces profit | It is debited to profit and loss account |
| May reduce asset value | Example: provision for doubtful debts deducted from debtors |
| May appear as liability | Example: provision for tax |

## Balance Sheet Presentation

Provisions may be shown:

1. as deduction from the related asset, or
2. on the liabilities side.

Examples:

| Provision | Balance Sheet Treatment |
|---|---|
| Provision for doubtful debts | Deducted from sundry debtors |
| Provision for depreciation | Deducted from fixed asset or shown separately |
| Provision for taxation | Shown as liability |
| Provision for repairs | Shown as liability |

## Problem

# Unit 7V: Provision and Reserve Compared

## Most Important Difference

Provision is a charge against profit.

Reserve is an appropriation of profit.

This means:

1. provision is deducted before net profit is finalised,
2. reserve is created after net profit is finalised.

## Detailed Comparison

| Basis | Provision | Reserve |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Charge against profit | Appropriation of profit |
| Created for | Known liability or expected loss with uncertain amount | Financial strength, future purpose, legal requirement, or policy |
| Timing | Before calculating true profit | After calculating profit |
| Profit requirement | Created even if there is loss | Usually created only when profit exists |
| Effect on taxable profit | Reduces taxable profit if allowed | Does not reduce taxable profit |
| Balance sheet | Deducted from asset or shown as liability | Shown after capital under reserves and surplus |
| Dividend use | Cannot be distributed as dividend | Some reserves may be used for dividend |
| Compulsion | Necessary under prudence when liability or loss exists | Usually optional unless required by law |
| Example | Provision for doubtful debts | General reserve |

## Memory Aid

Provision protects profit from being overstated.

Reserve protects the business by retaining profit.

# Unit 7W: Types of Reserves

Reserves can be classified in two main ways:

1. general reserve and specific reserve,
2. revenue reserve and capital reserve.

## 1. General Reserve

General reserve is created without a specific earmarked purpose.

It is also called a free reserve when it is available for general use.

It may be used for:

1. strengthening financial position,
2. meeting unexpected needs,
3. supporting future expansion,
4. dividend distribution, subject to rules and prudence.

## 2. Specific Reserve

Specific reserve is created for a particular purpose and should be used only for that purpose.

Examples:

| Specific Reserve | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Dividend equalisation reserve | To maintain stable dividend rate |
| Workmen compensation fund | To meet worker compensation claims |
| Investment fluctuation fund | To absorb fall in investment value |
| Debenture redemption reserve | To support redemption of debentures |
| Capital redemption reserve | To meet legal capital maintenance requirements |

## General Reserve vs Specific Reserve

| Basis | General Reserve | Specific Reserve |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Not earmarked | Earmarked for a specific purpose |
| Use | Flexible | Restricted |
| Example | General reserve | Debenture redemption reserve |
| Nature | Free reserve when available | Purpose-bound reserve |

## 3. Revenue Reserve

Revenue reserve is created from revenue profits.

Revenue profits arise from normal business operations.

Examples:

1. general reserve,
2. dividend equalisation reserve,
3. workmen compensation fund,
4. investment fluctuation fund,
5. debenture redemption reserve when created from revenue profits.

Revenue reserve may be available for dividend if it is not restricted by law or purpose.

## 4. Capital Reserve

Capital reserve is created mainly out of capital profits.

Capital profits do not arise from normal trading operations.

Examples:

1. premium on issue of shares,
2. premium on issue of debentures,
3. profit on sale of fixed assets,
4. profit prior to incorporation,
5. profit on reissue of forfeited shares,
6. capital redemption reserve,
7. revaluation surplus, subject to applicable accounting rules.

Capital reserve is usually not available for dividend distribution.

| Examples | General reserve, dividend equalisation reserve | Securities premium, capital redemption reserve |
| Relation to business operations | Linked with ordinary business profit | Not usually from ordinary trading activities |

## Examples Classified

| Item | Type |

| Capital redemption reserve | Capital reserve |

# Unit 7Y: Importance of Reserves

## 1. Financial Strength

Reserves increase the internal financial strength of a business.

A business with reserves may face difficult periods more comfortably.

## 2. Expansion

Reserves can provide funds for:

1. new plant,
2. more branches,
3. larger stock,
4. new technology,
5. working capital needs.

## 3. Stability

Some reserves help maintain stable dividend or stable operations in years of low profit.

## 4. Meeting Future Contingencies

Business faces uncertainties. Reserves help absorb future shocks, though they are not created for a known liability in the same way as provisions.

## 5. Redemption of Long-Term Liabilities

Specific reserves may support redemption of debentures or other long-term obligations.

## 6. Reducing Dependence on Loans

Retained profits reduce the need for outside borrowing.

This may reduce interest cost and financial risk.

# Unit 7Z: Secret Reserve

## Meaning

Secret reserve is a hidden reserve that does not appear separately in the balance sheet.

It exists when assets are understated or liabilities are overstated, causing disclosed profit and disclosed net worth to appear lower than actual.

## How Secret Reserve May Be Created

Secret reserve may arise through:

1. charging excessive depreciation,
2. undervaluing closing stock,
3. charging capital expenditure to profit and loss account,
4. creating excessive provision for doubtful debts,
5. showing a contingent liability as an actual liability,
6. not recording appreciation in asset value where recognition would otherwise be allowed.

## Example

Actual required depreciation = Rs. 40,000

Depreciation charged = Rs. 70,000

Extra depreciation = Rs. 30,000

This extra charge reduces profit and asset value. If not justified, it creates hidden reserve.

## Effects of Secret Reserve

| Effect | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Profit is understated | Extra charges reduce reported profit |
| Assets may be understated | Excess depreciation or undervaluation lowers asset value |
| Liabilities may be overstated | Excess provisions increase liabilities |
| Return ratios may be distorted | Users cannot judge real performance accurately |
| Future profits may appear better | Hidden reserve can be released in lean years |

## Caution

Prudence allows reasonable caution. It does not justify deliberate concealment or manipulation.

Secret reserves may sometimes be defended as conservative accounting, but excessive hidden reserves reduce transparency and can mislead users of financial statements.

# Unit 7AB: Common Mistakes and Corrections

## Mistake 1: Charging Depreciation on Current Assets

Wrong:

Depreciation is charged on stock or debtors.

Correct:

Depreciation is charged on fixed assets. Expected loss on debtors is recorded through bad debts or provision for doubtful debts.

## Mistake 2: Treating Depreciation as Cash Outflow

Wrong:

Depreciation means cash has gone out this year.

Correct:

Depreciation is a non-cash expense. Cash went out when asset was purchased.

## Mistake 3: Calculating Provision Before Further Bad Debts

Wrong:

Provision is calculated on debtors before writing off further bad debts.

Correct:

First deduct further bad debts, then calculate provision on remaining debtors.

## Mistake 4: Calling Reserve a Charge Against Profit

Wrong:

Reserve is deducted before finding net profit.

Correct:

Reserve is an appropriation of profit after profit is calculated.

## Mistake 5: Ignoring Part-Year Depreciation

Wrong:

Full-year depreciation is charged even when asset is purchased during the year.

Correct:

Charge depreciation for the period of use unless the question gives a different rule.

## Mistake 6: Confusing SLM and WDV Bases

Wrong:

SLM depreciation is calculated on opening book value.

Correct:

SLM uses original cost or depreciable cost. WDV uses opening written down value.

## Mistake 7: Treating All Repairs as Capital Expenditure

Wrong:

Every repair is added to asset cost.

Correct:

Routine repair is revenue expense. Only improvement, extension, or capacity-increasing expenditure is capitalised.

# Unit 7AE: Short Answer Guide

## 1. What Is Depreciation?

Depreciation is the gradual, permanent, and continuing decrease in the book value of a fixed asset due to use, passage of time, obsolescence, or other causes. In accounting, it means allocation of depreciable cost of a fixed asset over its useful life.

## 2. Why Is Depreciation Needed?

Depreciation is needed:

1. to match cost with revenue,
2. to calculate correct profit or loss,
3. to show fixed assets at fair book value,
4. to comply with law and accounting principles,
5. to avoid treating capital cost as profit,
6. to recognise expired cost of fixed assets.

## 3. What Are the Causes of Depreciation?

The main causes are:

1. wear and tear due to use,
2. deterioration due to passage of time,
3. expiry of legal rights,
4. obsolescence due to technology or market change,
5. abnormal factors such as accident, fire, or flood.

## 4. What Factors Affect Depreciation?

The amount of depreciation depends on:

1. cost of asset,
2. estimated net residual value,
3. depreciable cost,
4. estimated useful life,
5. method of depreciation selected.

## 5. Difference Between SLM and WDV

Straight line method charges equal depreciation every year on original cost or depreciable cost.

Written down value method charges depreciation every year at a fixed percentage on opening book value, so depreciation decreases year after year.

## 6. Which Method Is Suitable When Repairs Rise Later?

Written down value method is more suitable.

Reason:

Depreciation is high in early years and low in later years. Repairs are usually low in early years and high in later years. The total charge against profit becomes more balanced.

## 7. Effects of Depreciation on Financial Statements

Profit and loss account:

1. depreciation is debited as expense,
2. net profit decreases.

Balance sheet:

1. fixed asset value decreases, or
2. provision for depreciation increases,
3. total assets or net book value decrease.

## 8. Difference Between Provision and Reserve

Provision is a charge against profit for a known liability or expected loss whose amount is uncertain.

Reserve is an appropriation of profit retained in the business after profit is calculated.

## 9. Four Examples of Provisions

1. provision for doubtful debts,
2. provision for depreciation,
3. provision for taxation,
4. provision for repairs and renewals.

## 10. Four Examples of Reserves

1. general reserve,
2. dividend equalisation reserve,
3. debenture redemption reserve,
4. capital reserve.

## 11. Difference Between Revenue Reserve and Capital Reserve

Revenue reserve is created out of revenue profits arising from normal business operations. It may be available for dividend if not restricted.

Capital reserve is created mainly out of capital profits and is usually not available for dividend.

## 12. Difference Between General Reserve and Specific Reserve

General reserve is created for no particular purpose and may be used generally.

Specific reserve is created for a particular purpose and should be used only for that purpose.

## 13. What Is Secret Reserve?

Secret reserve is a hidden reserve not shown separately in the balance sheet. It may arise when assets are understated or liabilities are overstated, such as by charging excessive depreciation or creating excessive provision.

# Unit 7AF: Long Answer Guide

## 1. Explain Depreciation, Need, and Causes

Depreciation is the allocation of depreciable cost of a fixed asset over its useful life. It is caused by wear and tear, passage of time, expiry of legal rights, obsolescence, and abnormal factors.

Depreciation is needed because fixed assets help earn revenue for more than one accounting period. Charging the whole cost in the year of purchase would violate matching principle. If depreciation is not charged, profit will be overstated and assets will be shown at more than their proper book value.

It is also needed for legal compliance, tax calculation, and fair presentation of financial statements.

## 2. Explain SLM and WDV in Detail

Straight line method charges equal depreciation every year. It is calculated by dividing depreciable cost by useful life. It is simple and useful when asset benefit is uniform.

Written down value method charges depreciation at a fixed rate on opening book value. Depreciation is higher in earlier years and lower in later years. It is suitable for assets whose benefit declines and repair cost increases.

The two methods differ in base, yearly amount, ending value, simplicity, and suitability.

## 3. Explain Two Methods of Recording Depreciation

Under direct asset method, depreciation is credited to asset account. The asset account shows written down value.

Main entries:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Depreciation A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Asset A/c |  | xxx |
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |

Under provision method, depreciation is credited to provision for depreciation account. The asset account remains at cost.

Main entries:

| Entry | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Depreciation A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Provision for Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |
| Profit and Loss A/c Dr. | xxx |  |
| To Depreciation A/c |  | xxx |

## 4. Explain Determinants of Depreciation Amount

The amount of depreciation depends on cost, residual value, useful life, and method.

Cost includes purchase price and all expenses needed to make the asset ready for use. Residual value is expected net amount recoverable at the end of life. Depreciable cost is cost minus residual value. Useful life is the economic period of benefit. The method determines how the depreciable cost is spread across years.

## 5. Explain Types of Reserves

Reserves may be general or specific.

General reserve is not created for a fixed purpose. It strengthens financial position.

Specific reserve is created for a particular purpose, such as dividend equalisation or debenture redemption.

Reserves may also be revenue or capital.

Revenue reserves are created from revenue profits and may be distributable if unrestricted.

Capital reserves are created mainly from capital profits and are usually not distributable as dividend.

## 6. Explain Provisions and Provision for Doubtful Debts

Provision is an amount charged against profit for a known liability or expected loss whose amount is uncertain.

Provision for doubtful debts is created because some debtors may fail to pay. It is calculated on debtors after writing off further bad debts.

Entries:

| Situation | Entry |
|---|---|
| Further bad debts | Bad Debts A/c Dr. To Sundry Debtors A/c |
| Transfer bad debts | Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Bad Debts A/c |
| Create provision | Profit and Loss A/c Dr. To Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c |
| Reduce excess provision | Provision for Doubtful Debts A/c Dr. To Profit and Loss A/c |

## Practice 1: Straight Line Method

## Problem

A machine is purchased for Rs. 1,80,000. Carriage is Rs. 10,000 and installation is Rs. 10,000. Useful life is 10 years. Scrap value is Rs. 20,000. Calculate annual depreciation.

## Practice 2: Part-Year SLM

## Problem

A machine costing Rs. 1,08,000 is purchased on July 1. Installation cost is Rs. 12,000. Useful life is 12 years. Salvage value is Rs. 12,000. Books close on December 31. Calculate depreciation for the first year.

## Solution

Cost:

`Rs. 1,08,000 + Rs. 12,000 = Rs. 1,20,000`

Depreciable cost:

`Rs. 1,20,000 - Rs. 12,000 = Rs. 1,08,000`

Annual depreciation:

`Rs. 1,08,000 / 12 = Rs. 9,000`

Used from July 1 to December 31 = 6 months.

First-year depreciation:

`Rs. 9,000 x 6 / 12 = Rs. 4,500`

## Practice 3: Second-Hand Machine

## Problem

A second-hand machine is purchased for Rs. 56,000. Overhaul and installation cost is Rs. 28,000. Salvage value is Rs. 6,000, but disposal expenses are expected to be Rs. 1,000. Useful life is 15 years. Calculate annual depreciation under straight line method.

## Solution

Cost:

`Rs. 56,000 + Rs. 28,000 = Rs. 84,000`

Net residual value:

`Rs. 6,000 - Rs. 1,000 = Rs. 5,000`

Depreciable cost:

`Rs. 84,000 - Rs. 5,000 = Rs. 79,000`

Annual depreciation:

`Rs. 79,000 / 15 = Rs. 5,266.67`

If the question requires nearest rupee, depreciation is Rs. 5,267 approximately.

## Practice 4: WDV With Sale

## Problem

A truck is purchased for Rs. 8,00,000 on October 1. Depreciation is 15% p.a. on WDV. Books close on March 31. The truck is sold on December 31 of the fourth accounting year for Rs. 5,00,000. Find profit or loss.

## Solution Pattern

The first year has 6 months depreciation:

`Rs. 8,00,000 x 15% x 6 / 12 = Rs. 60,000`

WDV on first March 31:

`Rs. 8,00,000 - Rs. 60,000 = Rs. 7,40,000`

Then calculate full-year depreciation for each next year on opening WDV. For the year of sale, calculate depreciation up to December 31 if required by the question. Compare sale proceeds with WDV on date of sale.

Learning point:

In WDV questions, always update book value year by year.

## Practice 5: Provision for Doubtful Debts

## Problem

Debtors are Rs. 50,000. Bad debts already appear in trial balance Rs. 6,000. Existing provision for doubtful debts is Rs. 4,000. Further bad debts are Rs. 2,000. Provision is required at 8% of debtors. Find profit and loss effect.

## Solution

Adjusted debtors:

`Rs. 50,000 - Rs. 2,000 = Rs. 48,000`

Required provision:

`8% of Rs. 48,000 = Rs. 3,840`

Existing provision:

`Rs. 4,000`

Required provision is lower by Rs. 160, so excess provision of Rs. 160 is credited to profit and loss account.

Profit and loss account:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Bad debts from trial balance | 6,000 |  |
| Further bad debts | 2,000 |  |
| Excess provision |  | 160 |

Net debit to profit and loss:

`Rs. 6,000 + Rs. 2,000 - Rs. 160 = Rs. 7,840`
