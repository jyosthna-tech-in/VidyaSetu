# Chapter 4: Recording of Transactions - II
### NCERT Class 11 - Accountancy Part 1

## Chapter at a Glance

Chapter 3 explained how transactions are recorded in the journal and posted to ledger accounts. Chapter 4 explains what happens when a business has many repeated transactions. Instead of recording every transaction in one large journal, the journal is divided into special books.

These special books are also called subsidiary books or day books.

| Special Book | Records |
|---|---|
| Cash Book | Cash and bank receipts and payments |
| Petty Cash Book | Small regular cash payments |
| Purchases Book | Credit purchases of goods |
| Purchases Return Book | Goods returned to suppliers |
| Sales Book | Credit sales of goods |
| Sales Return Book | Goods returned by customers |
| Journal Proper | Entries that do not fit any special book |

7. Record credit purchases in the purchases book.

9. Record credit sales in the sales book.

# Unit 4A: Why Special Books Are Needed

## The Problem With One Journal

In a small business, all transactions may be recorded in one journal. But as the business grows, transactions become numerous and repetitive.

Examples:

1. cash received every day,
2. cash paid every day,
3. repeated credit sales,
4. repeated credit purchases,
5. goods returned by customers,
6. goods returned to suppliers,
7. small petty expenses.

If all of these are recorded only in the journal proper, the record becomes bulky and slow.

## Benefits of Sub-Dividing the Journal

Special books provide:

| Benefit | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Faster recording | Similar transactions are recorded in one place |
| Division of work | Different clerks can maintain different books |
| Better checking | Totals can be verified periodically |
| Less posting work | Periodic totals are posted to control accounts |
| Clear classification | Purchases, sales, returns, cash, and bank are separated |
| Less repetition | Similar narrations and ledger postings are reduced |
| Stronger control | Missing invoices, return notes, and cash entries are easier to detect |

## Which Book Should Be Used?

| Transaction | Book |
|---|---|
| Cash sale of goods | Cash book |
| Credit sale of goods | Sales book |
| Cash purchase of goods | Cash book |
| Credit purchase of goods | Purchases book |
| Credit purchase of furniture | Journal proper |
| Cash purchase of furniture | Cash book |
| Goods returned to supplier | Purchases return book |
| Goods returned by customer | Sales return book |
| Owner withdraws cash | Cash book |
| Owner withdraws goods | Journal proper |
| Depreciation | Journal proper |
| Outstanding salary | Journal proper |
| Bank charges | Cash book, bank column |

# Unit 4B: Cash Book

## Meaning

The cash book records all cash and bank transactions. It is special because it works both as:

1. a book of original entry, because transactions are first recorded in it,
2. a ledger account, because it also serves as the Cash Account and, where bank column exists, the Bank Account.

When a cash book is maintained:

1. cash transactions are not recorded again in the journal proper,
2. a separate Cash Account is usually not opened in the ledger,
3. if a bank column is used, a separate Bank Account is usually not opened in the ledger.

## Debit and Credit Side of Cash Book

| Side | Records | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Debit side | Receipts | Cash or bank comes in |
| Credit side | Payments | Cash or bank goes out |

Cash in hand cannot normally have a credit balance because a business cannot pay more physical cash than it has.

Bank column can have either:

1. debit balance: favourable bank balance,
2. credit balance: bank overdraft.

## Single Column Cash Book

A single column cash book has one amount column on each side. It records only cash receipts and cash payments.

Format:

| Date | Receipts | L.F. | Amount Rs. | Date | Payments | L.F. | Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---|---:|---|---|---|---:|
|  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |

## When Single Column Cash Book Is Used

It is used when:

1. business has only cash transactions,
2. bank transactions are absent or recorded separately,
3. the exercise asks for simple cash book,
4. learner is practising cash receipts and payments before adding bank column.

## Working of Balance

Receipts:

`30,000 + 12,000 + 28,000 + 18,200 = Rs. 88,200`

Payments excluding balance:

`6,000 + 13,800 + 17,400 + 1,100 + 12,500 + 2,500 + 3,500 + 8,000 = Rs. 64,800`

Balance:

`Rs. 88,200 - Rs. 64,800 = Rs. 23,400`

## Posting From Single Column Cash Book

Rule:

1. Items on debit side of cash book are posted to credit side of the related ledger account.
2. Items on credit side of cash book are posted to debit side of the related ledger account.
3. Cash Account is not opened separately because the cash book itself is Cash Account.

Example:

Cash received from Gurmeet Rs. 12,000 appears on debit side of cash book.

Posting:

Gurmeet Account is credited with Rs. 12,000.

Furniture purchased for cash Rs. 13,800 appears on credit side of cash book.

Posting:

Furniture Account is debited with Rs. 13,800.

# Unit 4D: Double Column Cash Book

## Meaning

A double column cash book has two amount columns on each side:

1. cash column,
2. bank column.

It records both cash and bank transactions.

Format:

| Date | Receipts | L.F. | Cash Rs. | Bank Rs. | Date | Payments | L.F. | Cash Rs. | Bank Rs. |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|---:|---:|
|  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |

## Why Bank Column Is Useful

A business usually makes many bank transactions:

1. cheques issued,
2. cheques received,
3. cash deposited,
4. cash withdrawn,
5. bank charges,
6. interest credited by bank,
7. dishonour of cheque,
8. payments by cheque.

Adding a bank column keeps bank information available without opening a separate Bank Account.

## Bank Terms Learners Must Know

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Pay-in slip | Form used to deposit cash or cheque into bank |
| Cheque | Written order to bank to pay money |
| Bearer cheque | Paid to the person holding the cheque |
| Order cheque | Paid to named person or their order |
| Crossed cheque | Paid through bank account, not directly over counter |
| Account payee crossing | Amount must go only into named person's account |
| Special crossing | Cheque must be collected through a named bank |
| Endorsement | Transfer of cheque by signing on its back |
| Dishonour | Bank refuses payment, usually due to insufficient funds |
| Overdraft | Bank allows withdrawal beyond available balance |

## Contra Entry

A contra entry affects both cash and bank columns of the cash book. It is marked with `C` in the folio column.

Common contra entries:

| Transaction | Debit Side | Credit Side |
|---|---|---|
| Cash deposited into bank | Bank column | Cash column |
| Cash withdrawn from bank for office use | Cash column | Bank column |
| Cheque received earlier and deposited later | Bank column | Cash column |

Contra entries are not posted to ledger because both accounts affected are already inside the cash book.

## Cheque Received: Same Day Versus Later Deposit

| Situation | Recording |
|---|---|
| Cheque received and deposited same day | Debit bank column |
| Cheque received but kept in office | Debit cash column first |
| Later deposited into bank | Contra entry: debit bank column and credit cash column |
| Cheque dishonoured after deposit | Credit bank column and write customer's name |

## Bank Charges and Interest

| Transaction | Cash Book Treatment |
|---|---|
| Bank charges debited by bank | Credit side, bank column |
| Bank interest charged | Credit side, bank column |
| Interest collected by bank for business | Debit side, bank column |
| Dividend collected by bank | Debit side, bank column |

# Unit 4F: Petty Cash Book

## Meaning

Petty cash book records small payments such as:

1. postage,
2. cartage,
3. bus fare,
4. taxi fare,
5. courier charges,
6. stationery,
7. refreshments,
8. small repairs,
9. telephone and communication charges,
10. miscellaneous office expenses.

These expenses are frequent and small. Recording each of them in the main cash book would make it bulky.

## Petty Cashier

A petty cashier is the person who handles small cash payments. The main cashier gives a fixed amount to the petty cashier at the beginning of a period.

## Imprest System

Under the imprest system:

1. a fixed amount is given to petty cashier,
2. petty cashier makes small payments,
3. petty cashier records each payment in petty cash book,
4. at the end of period, total amount spent is calculated,
5. main cashier reimburses exactly the amount spent,
6. petty cashier again starts the next period with the original fixed amount.

Example:

Imprest amount = Rs. 2,000.

Petty expenses during month = Rs. 1,487.

Closing petty cash balance = Rs. 513.

Amount reimbursed = Rs. 1,487.

Opening amount for next month again becomes:

`Rs. 513 + Rs. 1,487 = Rs. 2,000`

## Advantages of Petty Cash Book

| Advantage | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Saves main cashier's time | Chief cashier handles major receipts and payments |
| Better control | Petty cashier is accountable for small cash |
| Convenient recording | Small expenses are grouped in columns |
| Less ledger work | Periodic column totals are posted, not every tiny payment |
| Materiality | Main cash book shows important transactions clearly |
| Reduced errors | Similar small payments are classified immediately |

## Analytical Petty Cash Book Format

| Amount Received Rs. | Date | Particulars | Voucher No. | Total Paid Rs. | Postage Rs. | Conveyance Rs. | Stationery Rs. | Telephone Rs. | Misc. Rs. |
|---:|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
|  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |  |

## Petty Cash Book Posting

At period end:

1. Debit each expense account with its column total.
2. Credit Petty Cash Account with total expenses.
3. Debit Petty Cash Account when cash is given or reimbursed.

# Unit 4G: Purchases Book

## Meaning

The purchases book records only credit purchases of goods meant for resale.

It does not record:

1. cash purchases of goods,
2. credit purchase of assets,
3. cash purchase of assets,
4. services purchased,
5. stationery bought for office use unless the business trades in stationery.

## Purchases Book Rule

| Transaction | Book |
|---|---|
| Goods bought on credit | Purchases book |
| Goods bought for cash | Cash book |
| Furniture bought on credit | Journal proper |
| Furniture bought for cash | Cash book |
| Goods bought by cheque | Cash book, bank column |

## Format

| Date | Invoice No. | Name of Supplier | L.F. | Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---|---|---:|
|  |  | Account to be credited |  |  |

## Trade Discount in Purchases Book

Purchases book records the net invoice amount after trade discount.

Example:

List price = Rs. 1,00,000.

Trade discount = 10%.

Net amount:

`Rs. 1,00,000 - Rs. 10,000 = Rs. 90,000`

Purchases book records Rs. 90,000, not Rs. 1,00,000 and not the discount separately.

# Unit 4H: Purchases Return Book

## Meaning

Purchases return book records goods returned to suppliers. It is also called return outwards book.

Goods may be returned because:

1. they are defective,
2. wrong goods were supplied,
3. goods are not according to sample,
4. goods are damaged,
5. goods are more than ordered,
6. quality is not acceptable.

## Debit Note

When goods are returned to supplier, the buyer prepares a debit note. It informs the supplier that their account is being debited.

## Format

| Date | Debit Note No. | Name of Supplier | L.F. | Amount Rs. |
|---|---|---|---|---:|
|  |  | Account to be debited |  |  |

## Entry Logic

If goods returned to supplier are Rs. 13,200:

| Account | Debit or Credit | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Supplier A/c | Debit | Liability decreases |
| Purchases Return A/c | Credit | Return of goods reduces purchases |

## Posting From Purchases Return Book

1. Supplier accounts are debited individually.
2. Purchases Return Account is credited with total returns.

Example:

Neema Electronics returns = Rs. 13,200.

Neema Electronics Account is debited.

Purchases Return Account is credited.

# Unit 4K: Debit Note Versus Credit Note

| Basis | Debit Note | Credit Note |
|---|---|---|
| Prepared by | Buyer in case of purchase return | Seller in case of sales return |
| Sent to | Supplier | Customer |
| Account effect | Supplier is debited | Customer is credited |
| Used in | Purchases return book | Sales return book |
| Common reason | Defective goods returned to supplier | Goods returned by customer |

## Easy Memory Rule

Return to supplier:

`Supplier A/c Dr. To Purchases Return A/c`

Return from customer:

`Sales Return A/c Dr. To Customer A/c`

# Unit 4L: Trade Discount and Cash Discount

## Trade Discount

Trade discount is deducted from the list price at the time of buying or selling goods. It is not recorded separately in the books.

Example:

List price = Rs. 20,000.

Trade discount = 10%.

Invoice amount = Rs. 18,000.

Only Rs. 18,000 is recorded.

## Cash Discount

Cash discount is allowed or received for prompt payment or full settlement. It is recorded in the books.

Example:

Amount due to supplier = Rs. 7,000.

Paid in full settlement = Rs. 6,850.

Discount received = Rs. 150.

Entry:

| Particulars | Debit Rs. | Credit Rs. |
|---|---:|---:|
| Supplier A/c Dr. | 7,000 |  |
| To Cash A/c |  | 6,850 |
| To Discount Received A/c |  | 150 |

## Difference Table

| Basis | Trade Discount | Cash Discount |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Increase sales or bulk purchase | Encourage quick payment |
| Time | Before invoice amount is recorded | At payment or settlement |
| Recorded separately? | No | Yes |
| Book amount | Net amount after trade discount | Gross amount adjusted with discount |
| Appears in ledger? | No separate account | Discount Allowed or Discount Received Account |

# Unit 4N: Balancing the Accounts

## Meaning

Balancing means finding the difference between total debit and total credit of an account and writing the difference on the shorter side to make both sides equal.

## Steps to Balance an Account

1. Total the debit side.
2. Total the credit side.
3. Find the difference.
4. Write the difference on the smaller side as `Balance c/d`.
5. Write equal totals on both sides.
6. Bring the balance down on the opposite side as `Balance b/d`.

## Debit Balance

If debit side is greater than credit side, the account has a debit balance.

Example:

Cash Account:

Debit total = Rs. 50,000.

Credit total = Rs. 32,000.

Balance = Rs. 18,000.

The balance is written on credit side as Balance c/d, and brought down on debit side as Balance b/d.

## Credit Balance

If credit side is greater than debit side, the account has a credit balance.

Example:

Capital Account:

Debit total = Rs. 10,000.

Credit total = Rs. 1,00,000.

Balance = Rs. 90,000.

The balance is written on debit side as Balance c/d, and brought down on credit side as Balance b/d.

# Unit 4T: Short Answer Solutions

## 1. How is cash book both journal and ledger?

Cash book is a journal because cash and bank transactions are first recorded in it. It is also a ledger because it serves as Cash Account and, if bank column is maintained, Bank Account. Therefore, no separate cash or bank ledger account is normally needed.

## 2. What is the purpose of contra entry?

Contra entry records transfers between cash and bank in the same cash book. It prevents double posting because both cash and bank aspects already appear in the cash book.

Examples:

1. cash deposited into bank,
2. cash withdrawn from bank for office use,
3. cheque received earlier and deposited later.

## 3. What are special purpose books?

Special purpose books are subsidiary books used to record one class of transactions. Examples include cash book, purchases book, sales book, purchases return book, sales return book, and petty cash book.

## 4. What is petty cash book? How is it prepared?

Petty cash book records small cash payments. It is usually prepared under the imprest system. The petty cashier receives a fixed amount, pays small expenses, records them in analytical columns, and receives reimbursement equal to the amount spent.

## 5. What is posting?

Posting means transferring entries from subsidiary books or journal to ledger accounts.

Example:

Total of sales book is posted to credit of Sales Account, while individual customers are debited.

## 6. Why maintain subsidiary journals?

Subsidiary journals are maintained to save time, divide accounting work, reduce journal size, classify similar transactions, improve checking, and simplify ledger posting.

## 7. Difference between return inwards and return outwards.

| Basis | Return Inwards | Return Outwards |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Goods returned by customers | Goods returned to suppliers |
| Book | Sales return book | Purchases return book |
| Document | Credit note | Debit note |
| Account effect | Sales Return A/c debited | Purchases Return A/c credited |
| Customer or supplier | Customer credited | Supplier debited |

## 8. What is ledger folio?

Ledger folio is the page number or reference of the ledger account where the entry is posted. It helps trace entries between subsidiary books and ledger.

## 9. Difference between trade discount and cash discount.

Trade discount is deducted from list price and not recorded separately. Cash discount is allowed or received at payment or settlement and is recorded in Discount Allowed or Discount Received Account.

## 10. Process of preparing ledger from journal.

1. Identify the account debited and credited in the journal.
2. Open each account in the ledger.
3. Post the debit account on debit side.
4. Post the credit account on credit side.
5. In particulars, write the opposite account name.
6. Enter journal folio reference.
7. Balance the account when needed.

## 11. What is imprest amount?

Imprest amount is the fixed amount given to petty cashier at the beginning of a period. At the end of the period, the petty cashier is reimbursed for the amount spent, so the original fixed amount is restored.

# Unit 4U: Long Answer Solutions

## 1. Need for special purpose books

Special purpose books are needed because modern businesses have many repeated transactions. Recording all transactions in one journal would be slow, bulky, and difficult to verify.

Special books make accounting efficient because each book records one type of transaction. Cash book records cash and bank, purchases book records credit purchases of goods, sales book records credit sales of goods, and return books record goods returned.

Advantages include:

1. faster recording,
2. division of labour,
3. easier checking,
4. lower posting burden,
5. clearer classification,
6. better internal control,
7. timely totals for ledger posting.

## 2. Cash book and its types

Cash book records cash and bank receipts and payments. It is both a book of original entry and a ledger account.

Main types:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Single column cash book | Records only cash transactions |
| Double column cash book | Records cash and bank transactions |
| Petty cash book | Records small payments |

Some courses also include triple column cash book with cash, bank, and discount columns, but this chapter focuses mainly on cash, bank, and petty cash formats.

## 3. Contra entry in double column cash book

Contra entry is an entry in which both cash and bank columns are affected. It is marked `C`.

Example:

Cash deposited into bank Rs. 10,000:

1. Debit side bank column: Bank Rs. 10,000.
2. Credit side cash column: Cash Rs. 10,000.

No ledger posting is required because both accounts are already part of the cash book.

## 4. Petty cash book and its advantages

Petty cash book records small expenses such as postage, bus fare, cartage, small stationery, courier, and refreshments.

Under the imprest system, the petty cashier receives a fixed amount. The amount spent is reimbursed at the end of the period.

Advantages:

1. main cashier is not burdened by small payments,
2. petty expenses are classified in columns,
3. control over cash improves,
4. ledger posting becomes easier,
5. main cash book remains less bulky,
6. small expenses are recorded promptly.

## 5. Advantages of sub-dividing journal

Sub-dividing journal means using separate books for separate transaction types. This improves speed, accuracy, responsibility, and control.

For example, one clerk may record purchases, another may record sales, and another may maintain cash book. Totals from each book can then be posted periodically.

This method is especially useful for businesses with many repeated transactions.

## 6. Meaning of balancing an account

Balancing an account means finding the difference between debit and credit sides and carrying forward the balance.

If debit side is greater, the account has a debit balance. If credit side is greater, the account has a credit balance.

Asset accounts usually show debit balances. Liability and capital accounts usually show credit balances. Expense and revenue accounts are normally closed to profit and loss rather than carried forward.

## Final Summary

This chapter teaches how a business handles repeated transactions efficiently. Instead of putting every transaction into one journal, it uses special books. Cash book handles cash and bank. Petty cash book handles small payments. Purchases and sales books handle credit goods transactions. Return books handle goods returned. Journal proper records what does not fit elsewhere.

The most important skill in this chapter is book selection. First decide whether the transaction is cash or credit. Then decide whether it concerns goods or assets. Then choose the correct special book. After that, post individual accounts and periodic totals correctly.

For cash book, remember that receipts are on the debit side and payments on the credit side. For purchases book, suppliers are credited and Purchases Account is debited with total. For sales book, customers are debited and Sales Account is credited with total. For returns, reverse the relationship: supplier is debited for purchases returns, and customer is credited for sales returns.
