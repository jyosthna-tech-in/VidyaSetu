# Unit 2A: Why Accounting Needs a Theory Base

Accounting information is used by owners, managers, employees, investors, creditors, suppliers, government, and tax authorities.

These users need accounting information to be:

1. Reliable.
2. Comparable.
3. Understandable.
4. Consistent.
5. Useful for decisions.

This is possible only when financial statements are prepared using accepted policies, principles, and practices.

## Why Uniformity Is Needed

If every accountant uses different methods, users cannot compare:

1. One firm with another firm.
2. One year with another year.
3. Actual performance with expected performance.
4. Profitability and liquidity across businesses.

The theory base of accounting brings order and discipline to accounting.

# Unit 2B: Generally Accepted Accounting Principles

GAAP stands for Generally Accepted Accounting Principles.

GAAP refers to rules or guidelines adopted for recording and reporting business transactions so that financial statements are prepared and presented uniformly.

## Features of GAAP

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Generally accepted | Accepted by the accounting profession and users. |
| Based on experience | Developed through practice, usage, professional statements, and regulation. |
| Not static | Changes with legal, social, economic, and user needs. |
| Promotes uniformity | Helps businesses prepare comparable reports. |
| Improves reliability | Reduces arbitrary treatment. |

## Principle, Concept, Convention, Assumption

The chapter explains that these terms are often used interchangeably in practice.

| Term | Simple Meaning |
|---|---|
| Principle | General rule or guide to action. |
| Concept | Fundamental accounting idea or assumption. |
| Convention | Custom or tradition followed in accounting. |
| Assumption | Basic belief accepted before preparing accounts. |

For Class 11, focus more on application than on naming disputes.

# Unit 2D: Business Entity Concept

The business entity concept assumes that the business is separate from its owner.

The business and owner are treated as two separate accounting entities.

## Examples

| Situation | Accounting Treatment |
|---|---|
| Owner brings cash into business. | Business records capital as liability to owner. |
| Owner withdraws cash for personal use. | Business records drawings, reducing owner's capital. |
| Owner has personal house loan. | Not recorded in business books unless business is involved. |
| Business pays owner's personal expense. | Recorded as drawings, not business expense. |

## Why It Matters

Without this concept, business profit cannot be measured properly because personal and business transactions would be mixed.

# Unit 2E: Money Measurement Concept

The money measurement concept says that only transactions and events that can be expressed in money are recorded in books of account.

## Recorded

1. Sale of goods.
2. Purchase of machinery.
3. Payment of salary.
4. Receipt of rent.
5. Purchase of raw material.

## Not Recorded in Financial Books

1. Appointment of a talented manager.
2. Skill of employees.
3. Reputation of the business.
4. Quality of research team.
5. Customer loyalty, unless purchased or measurable under accounting rules.

## Why Physical Units Are Not Enough

A business may own:

1. 2 acres of land.
2. 10 rooms.
3. 30 computers.
4. 100 cartons of goods.
5. 20 tons of raw material.

These cannot be added meaningfully in physical units. Money gives a common measurement unit.

## Limitation

Money value changes over time due to inflation. A building bought years ago at Rs. 2 crore and a plant bought later at Rs. 1 crore are both shown at their historical money amounts, even though the rupee's purchasing power may have changed.

# Unit 2F: Going Concern Concept

The going concern concept assumes that a business will continue operating for a long period and will not be liquidated in the near future.

## Why It Matters

If a business is expected to continue, assets can be used over their useful life.

Example:

A computer is purchased for Rs. 50,000 and expected to be used for 5 years.

It would be unfair to charge the full Rs. 50,000 as expense in the year of purchase. Instead, the cost can be spread over useful life, such as Rs. 10,000 per year.

This is possible because the business is assumed to continue.

## If Going Concern Does Not Apply

If the business is about to close, assets may need to be valued differently, such as at sale or liquidation value.

# Unit 2G: Accounting Period Concept

Accounting period means the time span at the end of which financial statements are prepared.

Usually, financial statements are prepared annually.

## Why Needed

Users cannot wait until the business closes to know profit or financial position.

They need periodic information for:

1. Profit measurement.
2. Tax payment.
3. Loan decisions.
4. Dividend decisions.
5. Management planning.
6. Investor evaluation.

## Examples of Accounting Periods

| Period | Use |
|---|---|
| One year | Normal annual financial statements. |
| Quarter | Listed companies may publish quarterly results. |
| Shorter period | Needed for retirement of partner, sale of business, or internal reporting. |

# Unit 2H: Cost Concept

The cost concept requires assets to be recorded at purchase cost.

Cost includes:

1. Purchase price.
2. Transport cost.
3. Installation cost.
4. Repairs needed to bring asset into working condition.
5. Other costs necessary to make the asset ready for use.

## Shiva Enterprise Example

| Item | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Old plant purchase price | Rs. 50,00,000 |
| Transport | Rs. 10,000 |
| Repairs to make plant usable | Rs. 15,000 |
| Installation | Rs. 25,000 |
| Total recorded cost | Rs. 50,50,000 |

## Why Historical Cost Is Used

Historical cost is objective because it can be verified from documents such as invoices, receipts, and payment records.

## Limitation

Historical cost may not show present market value. During rising prices, assets may be worth much more than book value, creating hidden gains.

# Unit 2I: Dual Aspect Concept

Dual aspect is the foundation of accounting.

Every transaction has two effects and must be recorded in at least two accounts.

## Accounting Equation

`Assets = Liabilities + Capital`

This means assets of a business are financed by claims of owners and outsiders.

## Examples

| Transaction | Effect 1 | Effect 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Owner invests Rs. 50,00,000 cash. | Cash asset increases. | Capital increases. |
| Goods bought for cash. | Stock increases. | Cash decreases. |
| Machine bought on credit. | Machinery increases. | Creditor liability increases. |
| Loan taken from bank. | Cash or bank increases. | Loan liability increases. |

The accounting equation must remain balanced after every transaction.

# Unit 2J: Revenue Recognition Concept

Revenue recognition says revenue should be recorded when it is realised.

Revenue is generally realised when:

1. Goods are sold; or
2. Services are rendered; and
3. A legal right to receive money arises.

## Key Rule

Credit sales are recorded as revenue when sales are made, not when cash is received.

## Examples

| Situation | Revenue Recognition |
|---|---|
| Goods sold on credit in March, cash received in April. | Revenue belongs to March. |
| Rent for March received in April. | Revenue belongs to March. |
| Interest for April received in advance in March. | Revenue belongs to April, not March. |

## Exceptions

1. Long-term construction contracts may recognise revenue proportionately based on completion.
2. Hire purchase sales may recognise revenue as instalments are collected, depending on accounting treatment.

# Unit 2K: Matching Concept

The matching concept says expenses of an accounting period should be matched with revenues of the same period.

## Meaning

To find profit correctly:

`Profit = Revenue of the period - Expenses related to that revenue`

Revenue and expenses must belong to the same accounting period.

## Examples

| Situation | Matching Treatment |
|---|---|
| Salary for March paid in April. | Expense belongs to March. |
| Insurance paid for one year. | Only the part relating to current year is expense. |
| Fixed asset used for many years. | Depreciation is charged over useful life. |
| Goods purchased but unsold. | Cost of unsold goods is closing stock, not current expense. |

Matching is the reason we adjust for outstanding expenses, prepaid expenses, accrued income, and income received in advance.

# Unit 2L: Full Disclosure Concept

The full disclosure concept requires that all material and relevant facts about financial performance and position must be disclosed fully and fairly.

## Why It Matters

Financial statements are used by investors, lenders, suppliers, government, and other users. If important information is hidden, users may make wrong decisions.

## Examples of Disclosure

1. Change in accounting policy.
2. Contingent liability.
3. Method of depreciation.
4. Major legal dispute.
5. Basis of inventory valuation.
6. Related party transactions.

Full disclosure does not mean every tiny detail must be shown. It means material information must not be hidden.

# Unit 2M: Consistency Concept

Consistency means using the same accounting policies and methods from one accounting period to another.

## Why Needed

Users compare:

1. This year's profit with last year's profit.
2. One business with another.
3. Actual performance with expected performance.

If methods change every year, comparison becomes misleading.

## Example

If straight-line depreciation is used in one year, the same method should normally be used next year.

## Can Methods Ever Change?

Yes. A method may be changed if it improves reporting or is legally required. But the change and its likely effect should be disclosed.

# Unit 2N: Conservatism Concept

Conservatism is also called prudence.

It means:

1. Do not record profits until they are realised.
2. Provide for expected losses even if not yet final.

## Common Rule

`Anticipate no profits, but provide for all expected losses.`

## Examples

1. Closing stock valued at cost or market value, whichever is lower.
2. Provision for doubtful debts.
3. Provision for discount on debtors.
4. Writing off intangible assets such as goodwill or patents.
5. Recording expected legal loss if probable.

## Why Used

It prevents overstatement of profit and protects creditors and owners from distribution of unrealised profit.

## Limitation

Extreme conservatism can understate assets and profits and create secret reserves. It should not be used to deliberately hide true performance.

# Unit 2O: Materiality Concept

Materiality means accounting should focus on facts that can influence decisions of users.

A fact is material if a reasonable user would consider it important for decision-making.

## Examples of Material Facts

1. Change in depreciation method.
2. Major pending liability.
3. Expansion of theatre capacity.
4. Major loan default.
5. Large inventory loss.

## Immaterial Items

Small items may be treated simply even if strict accounting treatment would be different.

Example:

Stationery such as erasers, pencils, and scales may be treated as expense when bought, even if some remains unused at year end, because the amount is small.

# Unit 2P: Objectivity Concept

Objectivity requires transactions to be recorded in a manner free from personal bias.

Each transaction should be supported by verifiable evidence.

## Evidence Examples

| Transaction | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Cash purchase | Cash memo or receipt |
| Credit purchase | Invoice and delivery challan |
| Machine purchase | Invoice, receipt, installation bill |
| Payment | Payment voucher or bank record |

Historical cost supports objectivity because the amount actually paid can be verified.

Market value can vary from person to person and place to place, so it may be less objective unless supported by reliable valuation.

# Unit 2Q: Systems of Accounting

There are two systems of recording transactions:

1. Double entry system.
2. Single entry system.

## Double Entry System

Double entry is based on dual aspect.

Every transaction has:

1. One debit effect.
2. One credit effect.

Every debit has a corresponding credit.

## Advantages

1. Complete system.
2. Both effects of every transaction are recorded.
3. More reliable.
4. Helps detect arithmetic errors through trial balance.
5. Reduces chances of fraud and misappropriation.
6. Suitable for small and large organisations.

## Single Entry System

Single entry is an incomplete system.

Usually:

1. Personal accounts and cash book may be maintained.
2. Some transactions have one effect recorded.
3. Some transactions may have both effects recorded.
4. No uniform method is followed.

It is simple and flexible but incomplete and less reliable.

# Unit 2R: Basis of Accounting

Basis of accounting decides when revenues and expenses are recorded.

## Cash Basis

Under cash basis, transactions are recorded only when cash is received or paid.

Example:

Office rent for December 2014 paid in January 2015 is recorded in January 2015.

Credit sale made in January 2015 but cash received in April 2015 is recorded in April 2015.

## Limitations of Cash Basis

1. Does not follow matching concept.
2. Ignores outstanding expenses.
3. Ignores accrued income.
4. Profit becomes only difference between cash received and cash paid.
5. Not suitable for most business organisations.

## Accrual Basis

Under accrual basis, revenues and expenses are recognised when they occur, not when cash is received or paid.

Examples:

1. Revenue is recorded when sale is made.
2. Expense is recorded when service or asset is used.
3. Outstanding salary is recorded even if unpaid.
4. Rent earned is recorded even if not received.

Accrual basis is more appropriate for calculating profit because it matches revenue with related expenses.

## Cash Basis Versus Accrual Basis

| Basis | Cash Basis | Accrual Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue recorded | When cash is received | When earned |
| Expense recorded | When cash is paid | When incurred |
| Matching | Weak | Strong |
| Simplicity | Simple | More systematic |
| Accuracy of profit | Lower | Higher |
| Business suitability | Limited | Preferred for most businesses |

# Unit 2S: Accounting Standards

Accounting standards are written policy documents covering:

1. Recognition.
2. Measurement.
3. Treatment.
4. Presentation.
5. Disclosure.

In India, accounting standards are issued by ICAI.

## Objectives

Accounting standards aim to:

1. Bring uniformity in accounting policies.
2. Reduce non-comparability of financial statements.
3. Improve reliability.
4. Improve comparability within and across enterprises.
5. Provide standard accounting policies and disclosure requirements.

## Need for Standards

Different businesses may choose different accounting treatments. Standards reduce arbitrary choices and ensure information is uniform, relevant, reliable, and fully disclosed.

## Benefits

1. Reduce variations in accounting treatment.
2. Require useful disclosures.
3. Improve credibility of accounting data.
4. Make financial statements comparable.
5. Help investors and creditors assess performance.

## Limitations

1. Some standards may reduce flexibility.
2. Choosing among alternatives can still be difficult.
3. Standards cannot override law.
4. Standards must operate within the legal and business environment.

# Unit 2T: Goods and Services Tax

GST is a destination-based tax on consumption of goods and services.

It is levied at stages from manufacture to final consumption, with input tax credit for tax paid at previous stages.

The final burden is borne by the final consumer.

## Destination-Based Meaning

Tax revenue goes to the place where goods or services are consumed, also called place of supply.

## Components of GST

| Component | Full Form | When Used | Revenue Goes To |
|---|---|---|---|
| CGST | Central Goods and Services Tax | Intra-state sale | Central Government |
| SGST | State Goods and Services Tax | Intra-state sale | State Government |
| IGST | Integrated Goods and Services Tax | Inter-state sale or import | Collected by Centre and apportioned as per law |

## Intra-State Sale

If seller and buyer are in the same state:

`GST = CGST + SGST`

Example:

Goods worth Rs. 10,000 are sold within Punjab. GST rate is 18 percent, split as 9 percent CGST and 9 percent SGST.

| Tax | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---:|---:|
| CGST | 10,000 x 9% | Rs. 900 |
| SGST | 10,000 x 9% | Rs. 900 |
| Total GST | 10,000 x 18% | Rs. 1,800 |

## Inter-State Sale

If seller and buyer are in different states:

`GST = IGST`

Example:

Goods worth Rs. 10,00,000 are sold from Madhya Pradesh to Rajasthan. If IGST is 18 percent:

`IGST = 10,00,000 x 18% = Rs. 1,80,000`

## Characteristics from the Chapter

1. GST is a common tax system for goods and services.
2. GST is destination based.
3. GST allows input tax credit.
4. GST reduces multiple indirect taxes.
5. GST removes cascading effect of taxes.
6. GST supports a unified indirect tax structure.

## Advantages

1. Replaces many earlier indirect taxes.
2. Widens tax base.
3. Reduces administrative cost.
4. Reduces compliance cost.
5. Encourages voluntary compliance.
6. Removes cascading effect.
7. Helps improve efficiency in production and distribution.
8. Can strengthen competitiveness of Indian goods and services.

# Applied Case 1: Ruchica and Bank Statements

## Facts

Ruchica prepared financial statements because the accountant was ill. The bank did not rely on them because:

1. Building bought for Rs. 7 lakh was shown at present market value of Rs. 20 lakh.
2. Stock valuation method was changed from last year, increasing value by about 15 percent.
3. Rs. 70,000 spent on a personal computer with expected life of 5 years was fully charged to current year's profit.

## Mistakes and Concepts Violated

| Mistake | Concept Violated | Correct Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Building shown at market value instead of purchase cost. | Cost concept and objectivity. | Record at historical cost, unless standards require otherwise. |
| Stock valuation method changed without proper disclosure. | Consistency and full disclosure. | Use same method or disclose change and effect. |
| Full computer cost charged in one year. | Going concern and matching. | Capitalise computer and charge depreciation over useful life. |

## Explanation

The bank needs reliable and comparable information. By changing values and methods improperly, the statements became less reliable, less objective, and less comparable.

# Applied Case 2: Expected Legal Damages

## Facts

A customer filed a case against a trader for poor quality goods. It is known that the court judgment will favour the customer, but the exact damages are uncertain. The accountant says not to consider the expected loss because final judgment is not yet out.

## Correct View

The accountant is not fully right.

Under conservatism, expected losses should be provided for when they are probable. If the amount can be reasonably estimated, a provision should be made. If the amount cannot be estimated reliably, the matter should at least be disclosed as a contingent liability.

Ignoring the expected loss would overstate profit and liabilities may be understated.

# Exercise Solutions: Short Answers

## 1. Why assume business will remain a going concern?

Going concern is needed because many assets give benefit over several years. If the business is assumed to continue, asset cost can be spread over useful life through depreciation. Without this assumption, assets might need to be charged fully or valued at liquidation value.

## 2. When should revenue be recognised? Are there exceptions?

Revenue should be recognised when goods are sold or services are rendered and a legal right to receive money arises.

Exceptions include long-term construction contracts, where revenue may be recognised according to work completed, and hire purchase transactions, where revenue recognition may depend on instalment collection and accounting rules.

## 3. What is the basic accounting equation?

`Assets = Liabilities + Capital`

It means assets of a business are financed by owner claims and outsider claims.

## 4. When should credit sale be included in sales figure?

The best answer is when goods are delivered or ownership and risk have passed to the customer. Payment is not necessary because credit sales are recorded before cash is received. Dispatch or invoice alone may not be enough if sale is not complete under terms.

# Exercise Solutions: Long Answers

## 1. Accounting concepts and standards as essence of financial accounting.

Accounting concepts and standards are the essence of financial accounting because they provide the rules and assumptions used to record and report transactions.

Concepts such as business entity, money measurement, going concern, accounting period, cost, and dual aspect decide how transactions are identified and recorded. Concepts such as matching, revenue recognition, consistency, conservatism, materiality, objectivity, and full disclosure guide profit measurement and reporting.

Accounting standards provide written guidance on recognition, measurement, presentation, and disclosure. Together, concepts and standards make financial statements reliable, comparable, and useful.

## 2. Importance of consistent basis.

Consistency is important because users compare performance across years and across firms. If methods change frequently, profit and asset values may change because of accounting method rather than real business performance.

Example: If depreciation method changes from straight-line to written-down value without disclosure, current profit cannot be compared fairly with previous year profit.

Consistency does not ban change. It requires that changes be justified and disclosed with their likely effect.

## 3. Concept based on "do not anticipate profits but provide for all losses."

This is the conservatism or prudence concept.

It means expected losses should be recognised, but expected gains should not be recorded until realised.

Examples:

1. Provision for doubtful debts.
2. Closing stock valued at lower of cost and market value.
3. Provision for expected legal damages.
4. Writing off intangible assets.

It prevents overstatement of profit and protects creditors and owners. But it should not be misused to create secret reserves.

## 4. Matching concept and why it should be followed.

Matching concept says expenses incurred in an accounting period should be matched with revenues of the same period.

It should be followed because profit can be correctly measured only when related revenue and expenses belong to the same period.

Examples:

1. Salary outstanding is recorded in the year service is received.
2. Prepaid insurance is not fully charged to current year.
3. Depreciation is charged over useful life.
4. Cost of unsold goods is carried as closing stock.

Without matching, profit may be overstated or understated.

## 5. Money measurement concept and difficulty in comparison.

Money measurement concept says only events measurable in money are recorded. It also says transactions are recorded in monetary units, not physical units.

The difficulty is that value of money changes over time due to inflation or deflation. A rupee today may not have the same purchasing power as a rupee ten years ago. Therefore, monetary values of assets bought in different years may not be fully comparable.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating owner and business as same. | Business entity concept keeps them separate. |
| Recording every important non-financial fact. | Money measurement records only measurable events. |
| Charging full cost of long-term asset in one year. | Going concern and matching require allocation over useful life. |
| Recording market value increase as profit before sale. | Conservatism avoids unrealised gains. |
| Changing methods without disclosure. | Consistency permits justified change with disclosure. |
| Including order received as sales. | Revenue is recognised when sale is complete, not when order is received. |
| Treating single entry as complete. | It is incomplete and less reliable. |
| Confusing cash and accrual basis. | Cash basis follows cash movement; accrual follows earning and incurring. |
| Applying CGST and SGST to inter-state sale. | Inter-state sale attracts IGST. |

# Final Study Summary

The theory base of accounting gives accounting its rules, structure, and reliability. GAAP, accounting concepts, and accounting standards help accountants prepare financial statements consistently.

The basic concepts guide every stage of accounting. Business entity separates owner and business. Money measurement limits recording to monetary events. Going concern allows assets to be used over future periods. Accounting period makes periodic reporting possible. Cost and objectivity support verifiable recording. Dual aspect creates the accounting equation. Revenue recognition and matching help calculate correct profit. Full disclosure, consistency, conservatism, and materiality improve usefulness of financial statements.

Double entry is the complete system of recording. Accrual basis gives a better measure of profit than cash basis for most businesses. Accounting standards improve uniformity, reliability, and comparability.

GST is introduced as a destination-based tax with CGST and SGST for intra-state supply and IGST for inter-state supply.
