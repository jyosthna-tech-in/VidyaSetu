## Chapter Overview

Psychology studies behaviour and experience scientifically. This requires careful methods, not guesswork. A psychologist must define a problem, collect evidence, analyse data, and draw conclusions cautiously.

Because human behaviour is complex, psychology uses many methods. No single method is perfect. Good enquiry often combines methods to understand a phenomenon more completely.

## 1. Goals of Psychological Enquiry

Psychological enquiry usually has four major goals.

### Description

Description means identifying what is happening.

Example: describing symptoms of anxiety among students before examinations.

### Explanation

Explanation means identifying why something happens.

Example: explaining anxiety through evaluation pressure, preparation, expectations, and coping style.

### Prediction

Prediction means estimating what may happen under certain conditions.

Example: predicting that poor sleep may reduce attention during a test.

### Control or Change

Control means influencing behaviour in a desired direction.

Example: using study planning and relaxation to reduce exam anxiety.

In psychology, control should be ethical and aimed at well-being.

## 2. Steps in Scientific Enquiry

The scientific method generally includes:

1. Identifying a problem.
2. Reviewing existing knowledge.
3. Formulating a hypothesis.
4. Choosing a method.
5. Collecting data.
6. Analysing data.
7. Drawing conclusions.
8. Revising theory if needed.
9. Reporting findings honestly.

## 3. Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a tentative explanation or prediction that can be tested.

Example:

"Students who sleep at least seven hours before a test will recall more words than students who sleep less than five hours."

A good hypothesis is:

1. Clear.
2. Testable.
3. Based on reasoning or earlier findings.
4. Open to being supported or rejected.

## 4. Variables

A variable is any factor that can change or take different values.

Examples:

1. Age.
2. Gender.
3. Anxiety level.
4. Sleep hours.
5. Test score.
6. Reaction time.
7. Social support.

### Independent Variable

The independent variable is the factor manipulated or selected to see its effect.

Example: hours of sleep.

### Dependent Variable

The dependent variable is the outcome measured.

Example: recall score.

### Extraneous Variables

Extraneous variables are unwanted factors that may influence results.

Example: prior knowledge, noise, health, motivation.

## 5. Experimental Method

The experimental method studies cause and effect.

It involves:

1. Manipulating an independent variable.
2. Measuring a dependent variable.
3. Controlling extraneous variables.
4. Comparing groups or conditions.

Example: testing whether practice improves memory by giving one group practice trials and another group no practice.

### Strengths

1. Best method for cause-effect inference.
2. Allows control.
3. Can be replicated.

### Limitations

1. Artificial laboratory settings may reduce real-life validity.
2. Some variables cannot be ethically manipulated.
3. Human behaviour may change because participants know they are being studied.

## 6. Control Group and Experimental Group

The experimental group receives the treatment or manipulation.

The control group does not receive it or receives a neutral condition.

Comparing the two groups helps determine whether the independent variable had an effect.

## 7. Observational Method

Observation involves watching and recording behaviour systematically.

Types:

1. Naturalistic observation: behaviour observed in natural settings.
2. Participant observation: researcher becomes part of the group.
3. Non-participant observation: researcher observes without joining.

### Strengths

1. Useful for real-life behaviour.
2. Good when experiments are not possible.
3. Captures context.

### Limitations

1. Observer bias.
2. People may change behaviour when watched.
3. Cause-effect conclusions are limited.

## 8. Survey Method

Surveys collect information from many people through questionnaires or interviews.

They are useful for studying:

1. Attitudes.
2. Opinions.
3. Habits.
4. Preferences.
5. Self-reported behaviour.

### Strengths

1. Covers large samples.
2. Efficient.
3. Useful for population trends.

### Limitations

1. People may give socially desirable answers.
2. Questions may be misunderstood.
3. Poor sampling can distort results.

## 9. Interview Method

An interview is a conversation designed to collect information.

Types:

1. Structured interview: fixed questions.
2. Unstructured interview: flexible conversation.
3. Semi-structured interview: planned themes with flexibility.

Interviews are useful when depth and meaning are important.

## 10. Case Study Method

A case study is an intensive study of one person, group, institution, or event.

It may use:

1. Interviews.
2. Observation.
3. Records.
4. Tests.
5. Family history.
6. Medical history.

### Strengths

1. Rich detail.
2. Useful for rare conditions.
3. Good for clinical understanding.

### Limitations

1. Findings may not generalise.
2. Researcher bias is possible.
3. Cause-effect conclusions are weak.

## 11. Correlational Method

Correlation studies the relationship between variables.

Example: relation between study time and marks.

Correlation can be:

1. Positive: both variables increase together.
2. Negative: one increases while the other decreases.
3. Zero: no systematic relation.

Important caution: correlation does not prove causation.

## 12. Psychological Testing

Psychological tests measure abilities, traits, attitudes, interests, or symptoms.

Good tests should have:

1. Reliability: consistency of results.
2. Validity: measures what it claims to measure.
3. Norms: comparison standards.
4. Standardisation: uniform procedure.

Tests must be used responsibly and interpreted by trained persons.

## 13. Quantitative and Qualitative Data

### Quantitative Data

Quantitative data are numerical.

Examples:

1. Test scores.
2. Reaction time.
3. Rating scales.
4. Number of errors.

These can be analysed statistically.

### Qualitative Data

Qualitative data are descriptive.

Examples:

1. Interview narratives.
2. Field notes.
3. Personal accounts.
4. Open-ended responses.

These are analysed for themes, meanings, and patterns.

## 14. Data Analysis

Data analysis means organising evidence to answer the research question.

Quantitative analysis may use:

1. Mean.
2. Median.
3. Correlation.
4. Percentages.
5. Statistical comparison.

Qualitative analysis may use:

1. Thematic categories.
2. Narrative analysis.
3. Content analysis.
4. Interpretation of meaning.

## 15. Ethical Principles

Psychological research must protect participants.

Important ethical principles:

1. Informed consent.
2. Confidentiality.
3. No unnecessary harm.
4. Right to withdraw.
5. Debriefing after research.
6. Respect for dignity.
7. Special care with children and vulnerable groups.
8. Honest reporting of results.

## 16. Limitations of Psychological Enquiry

Psychological research faces challenges:

1. Behaviour is complex and multi-caused.
2. People change across situations.
3. Self-reports may be inaccurate.
4. Researcher bias can enter.
5. Ethical limits prevent some experiments.
6. Cultural differences affect behaviour.
7. Laboratory findings may not always fit real life.

These limits do not make psychology unscientific. They make careful method necessary.

## 17. Key Terms

## 18. Exam-Oriented Summary

Psychological enquiry aims to describe, explain, predict, and influence behaviour ethically. It uses scientific steps such as problem identification, hypothesis formation, data collection, analysis, and conclusion.

Major methods include experiment, observation, survey, interview, case study, correlational method, and psychological testing. Quantitative methods use numerical data, while qualitative methods study meanings and narratives. Ethics and awareness of limitations are essential in all psychological research.
