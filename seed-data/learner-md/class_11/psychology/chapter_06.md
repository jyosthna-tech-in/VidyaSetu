## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how behaviour changes through experience, practice, observation, and understanding. Learning includes conditioning, imitation, insight, verbal learning, skill learning, and concept learning.

The central idea is:

> Learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour or mental processes produced by experience and practice.

## 1. Meaning of Learning

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behaviour or behavioural potential due to experience or practice.

Important features:

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Change | Learning produces some change |
| Relatively permanent | Temporary fatigue or illness is not learning |
| Based on experience | Practice, training, observation, or consequences matter |
| Behaviour or potential | Learning may not always be immediately visible |

Example:

A child learns to ride a bicycle through practice. The improved balance and control show learning.

---

## 2. Classical Conditioning

Classical conditioning is learning by association between two stimuli.

It was demonstrated by Ivan Pavlov with dogs.

### Basic Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Unconditioned stimulus | Naturally produces a response |
| Unconditioned response | Natural response to US |
| Conditioned stimulus | Previously neutral stimulus that gains power through association |
| Conditioned response | Learned response to CS |

### Example

Before conditioning:

```text
Food -> Salivation
Bell -> No salivation
```

During conditioning:

```text
Bell + Food -> Salivation
```

After conditioning:

```text
Bell -> Salivation
```

Classical conditioning explains many emotional and automatic responses.

---

## 3. Processes in Classical Conditioning

| Process | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Acquisition | Learning of conditioned response |
| Extinction | Conditioned response weakens when CS appears without US |
| Spontaneous recovery | Extinguished response reappears after rest |
| Generalisation | Similar stimuli produce similar response |
| Discrimination | Learner responds differently to different stimuli |

Example:

A child afraid of one dog may fear similar dogs. That is generalisation. Learning to fear only aggressive dogs is discrimination.

---

## 4. Operant Conditioning

Operant conditioning is learning in which behaviour is shaped by its consequences.

It is associated with B. F. Skinner.

If a behaviour is followed by reinforcement, it is more likely to occur again. If followed by punishment or no reinforcement, it may reduce.

### Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Reinforcer | Consequence that increases behaviour |
| Punishment | Consequence that decreases behaviour |
| Positive reinforcement | Adding pleasant consequence |
| Negative reinforcement | Removing unpleasant condition |
| Shaping | Reinforcing successive approximations to desired behaviour |

Example:

A student studies regularly because praise and good marks follow studying.

---

## 5. Reinforcement Schedules

| Schedule | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Continuous reinforcement | Every correct response is reinforced | Reward every correct answer |
| Partial reinforcement | Some responses are reinforced | Reward sometimes |
| Fixed ratio | Reinforcement after fixed number of responses | Reward after every 5 tasks |
| Variable ratio | Reinforcement after varying number of responses | Unpredictable reward |
| Fixed interval | Reinforcement after fixed time | Weekly test |
| Variable interval | Reinforcement after varying time | Surprise quiz |

Variable schedules often produce strong and persistent behaviour.

---

## 6. Classical vs Operant Conditioning

| Basis | Classical conditioning | Operant conditioning |
|---|---|---|
| Learning basis | Association between stimuli | Consequences of behaviour |
| Response type | Reflexive or automatic | Voluntary or emitted |
| Learner role | More passive | More active |
| Key term | CS, US, CR, UR | Reinforcement, punishment |
| Example | Bell causes salivation | Lever pressing produces food |

---

## 7. Observational Learning

Observational learning occurs by watching others.

It is also called:

- Imitation
- Modelling
- Social learning

Example:

A child learns polite greetings by watching parents.

Observational learning is important in:

- Language
- Social behaviour
- Aggression
- Gender roles
- Skills
- Attitudes

---

## 8. Cognitive Learning

Cognitive learning emphasises mental processes such as thinking, understanding, memory, and problem solving.

It is not limited to stimulus-response connections.

### Insight Learning

Insight learning occurs when the solution to a problem suddenly becomes clear.

Example:

Kohler's chimpanzee suddenly uses sticks or boxes to reach a banana.

### Latent Learning

Latent learning is learning that occurs but is not shown until there is a reason to use it.

Example:

You may learn the route to a place by travelling with someone, but show that learning only later when you travel alone.

---

## 9. Verbal Learning

Verbal learning involves learning words, lists, pairs, and language material.

Methods:

| Method | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Paired-associates learning | Learning pairs of words or items |
| Serial learning | Learning items in a fixed order |
| Free recall | Recalling items in any order |

---

## 10. Skill Learning

Skill learning involves acquiring coordinated motor or cognitive performance through practice.

Examples:

- Typing
- Driving
- Playing an instrument
- Solving math problems
- Playing a sport

Skill learning improves with feedback, practice, motivation, and correction of errors.

---

## 11. Concept Learning

A concept is a category based on common features or rules.

Examples:

- Fruit
- Vehicle
- Triangle
- Democracy
- Mammal

Concept learning helps us organise the world and think efficiently.

---

## 12. Learning Disabilities

Learning disabilities are difficulties in acquiring skills such as reading, writing, reasoning, speaking, or mathematics despite adequate opportunity.

They may affect:

- Reading
- Writing
- Spelling
- Mathematical calculation
- Attention
- Language processing

Learners with disabilities need support, diagnosis, patience, and appropriate teaching methods.

---

## 13. Applications of Learning

Learning principles are used in:

| Area | Application |
|---|---|
| Education | Reinforcement, feedback, practice |
| Therapy | Reducing phobias through counter-conditioning |
| Parenting | Encouraging desirable behaviour |
| Skill training | Step-by-step practice |
| Behaviour modification | Reinforcing target behaviours |
| Social learning | Modelling positive behaviour |

---

## 14. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating all behaviour change as learning | Temporary changes due to fatigue are not learning. |
| Confusing classical and operant conditioning | Classical links stimuli; operant depends on consequences. |
| Thinking punishment teaches desired behaviour | Punishment may suppress behaviour but does not teach replacement by itself. |
| Confusing negative reinforcement with punishment | Negative reinforcement increases behaviour by removing unpleasant condition. |
| Ignoring cognition | Learning can involve insight, memory, and understanding. |
