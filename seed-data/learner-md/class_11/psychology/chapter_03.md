# Chapter 3: The Bases of Human Behaviour
### NCERT Class 11 Psychology

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains the biological and cultural foundations of human behaviour. Human actions, emotions, thoughts, and social practices are shaped by the body, brain, nervous system, endocrine system, heredity, evolution, culture, socialisation, and acculturation.

The central idea is:

> Human behaviour is shaped by the interaction of biological systems and cultural experience.

## 1. Two Bases of Behaviour

Human behaviour has two major bases:

| Base | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Biological base | Body, brain, genes, nervous system, hormones | Reflexes, emotions, sensory responses |
| Cultural base | Learning through society, family, language, customs | Greeting habits, values, gender roles |

Neither base works alone. Biology gives capacities; culture shapes how those capacities are expressed.

---

## 2. Evolutionary Basis of Behaviour

Evolution refers to gradual biological changes in a species across generations in response to environmental demands.

Natural selection favours traits that help survival and reproduction.

Examples of human evolutionary advantages:

- Upright walking
- Large and developed brain
- Opposable thumb
- Language capacity
- Social cooperation
- Tool use

Evolution explains why humans have certain basic capacities, but culture explains how those capacities develop in specific societies.

---

## 3. Heredity and Genes

Heredity is the transmission of characteristics from parents to offspring through genes.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gene | Unit of heredity |
| Genotype | Genetic makeup of a person |
| Phenotype | Observable characteristics produced by genes and environment |
| Mutation | Change in a gene |

Example:

A child may inherit a capacity for musical sensitivity, but actual musical skill develops only if the environment provides exposure, practice, and encouragement.

---

## 4. Neurons

Neurons are nerve cells. They are the basic units of the nervous system.

They receive, process, and transmit information in electrochemical form.

### Parts of a Neuron

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Dendrites | Receive incoming signals |
| Soma | Cell body; contains nucleus |
| Axon | Carries impulse away from soma |
| Myelin sheath | Speeds up neural transmission |
| Terminal buttons | Pass signal to another neuron or muscle |
| Synapse | Gap between two neurons |
| Neurotransmitter | Chemical messenger crossing the synapse |

---

## 5. Neural Transmission

Neural transmission is the process by which a message passes from one neuron to another.

Basic sequence:

1. Dendrites receive signal.
2. Signal passes to soma.
3. Axon carries the impulse.
4. Terminal button releases neurotransmitter.
5. Neurotransmitter crosses synapse.
6. Next neuron receives the message.

Important learner point:

> Neural messages are electrical within a neuron and chemical between neurons.

## 6. Nervous System

The nervous system controls and coordinates behaviour and bodily activity.

```text
Nervous System
        |
        +-- Central Nervous System
        |       +-- Brain
        |       +-- Spinal cord
        |
        +-- Peripheral Nervous System
                +-- Somatic nervous system
                +-- Autonomic nervous system
                        +-- Sympathetic
                        +-- Parasympathetic
```

---

## 7. Central Nervous System

The Central Nervous System, or CNS, consists of the brain and spinal cord.

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Brain | Controls cognition, emotion, movement, memory, language, and regulation |
| Spinal cord | Carries messages between brain and body; controls many reflexes |

---

## 8. Peripheral Nervous System

The Peripheral Nervous System, or PNS, connects the CNS with the rest of the body.

### Somatic Nervous System

Controls voluntary movement and carries sensory information.

Examples:

- Moving hand
- Walking
- Seeing and hearing
- Touch sensation

### Autonomic Nervous System

Controls internal organs and involuntary functions.

| Division | Function |
|---|---|
| Sympathetic system | Prepares body for action and emergency |
| Parasympathetic system | Calms body and restores normal functioning |

Example:

During fear, the sympathetic system increases heartbeat. After safety returns, the parasympathetic system helps the body relax.

---

## 9. Major Parts of the Brain

### Hindbrain

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Medulla | Controls vital functions like breathing and heartbeat |
| Pons | Helps relay signals and regulate sleep/arousal |
| Cerebellum | Coordinates balance, posture, and movement |

### Midbrain

The midbrain helps in sensory processing, attention, and movement coordination.

### Forebrain

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Hypothalamus | Regulates hunger, thirst, temperature, sleep, and endocrine activity |
| Thalamus | Relay station for sensory information |
| Limbic system | Emotion, motivation, and memory |
| Cerebrum/cerebral cortex | Higher mental functions: thinking, language, memory, reasoning |

---

## 10. Cerebral Cortex and Lobes

| Lobe | Main functions |
|---|---|
| Frontal lobe | Planning, decision-making, voluntary movement |
| Parietal lobe | Touch, body position, spatial processing |
| Temporal lobe | Hearing, language, memory |
| Occipital lobe | Vision |

The cerebral cortex supports complex human behaviour such as reasoning, problem solving, imagination, and communication.

---

## 11. Spinal Cord and Reflex Action

The spinal cord carries sensory messages to the brain and motor messages from the brain to the body.

It also controls reflex actions.

Reflex action is a quick automatic response to a stimulus.

Examples:

- Pulling hand away from hot object
- Knee jerk
- Eye blinking
- Pupil constriction

Reflexes protect the body and often occur without conscious decision.

---

## 12. Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of glands that release hormones into the bloodstream.

Hormones affect growth, emotion, metabolism, stress, and behaviour.

| Gland | Important role |
|---|---|
| Pituitary | Master gland; regulates other glands |
| Thyroid | Metabolism and energy regulation |
| Adrenal glands | Stress response; adrenaline |
| Pancreas | Blood sugar regulation |
| Gonads | Sex hormones and reproductive development |
| Pineal | Sleep-related rhythms |

The nervous system acts quickly through neural signals. The endocrine system acts more slowly through hormones but may have longer-lasting effects.

---

## 13. Culture

Culture is the shared way of life of a group. It includes values, beliefs, customs, language, practices, tools, symbols, and social rules.

Culture shapes:

- Food habits
- Dress
- Family roles
- Ideas of politeness
- Emotional expression
- Gender expectations
- Religious practices
- Social responsibilities

Human behaviour cannot be fully understood without cultural context.

---

## 14. Enculturation, Socialisation, and Acculturation

### Enculturation

Enculturation is the process of learning culture through everyday participation, often without direct teaching.

Example:

A child learns how to greet elders by watching family members.

### Socialisation

Socialisation is the process through which individuals acquire knowledge, skills, values, and behaviours needed to participate in society.

Agents of socialisation:

- Family
- School
- Peers
- Media
- Community

### Acculturation

Acculturation occurs when individuals or groups come into contact with another culture and undergo change.

Example:

Migrants may learn a new language and lifestyle while retaining parts of their original culture.

---

## 15. Biology and Culture Together

| Behaviour | Biological part | Cultural part |
|---|---|---|
| Eating | Hunger regulation | Food choices and eating rules |
| Emotion | Brain and hormones | How emotions are expressed |
| Language | Brain capacity | Specific language learned |
| Gender behaviour | Biological sex | Gender roles and expectations |
| Aggression | Arousal and neural systems | Social norms about conflict |

The best psychological explanation usually considers both nature and nurture.

---

## 16. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying biology alone explains behaviour | Culture also shapes behaviour. |
| Saying culture alone explains behaviour | Biological systems provide basic capacities. |
| Confusing neuron and nerve | Neuron is a nerve cell; nerves are bundles of fibres. |
| Confusing CNS and PNS | CNS is brain and spinal cord; PNS connects body to CNS. |
| Treating hormones as instant like nerve impulses | Hormones are usually slower and longer-lasting. |
| Confusing enculturation and acculturation | Enculturation is learning one's culture; acculturation involves cultural contact and change. |

Human behaviour is neither purely biological nor purely cultural. The brain, nervous system, hormones, heredity, learning, family, society, and culture all interact to produce the behaviour we observe.
