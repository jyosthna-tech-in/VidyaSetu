## Chapter Overview

Psychology studies how people think, feel, experience, learn, remember, behave, relate, and change. It asks questions about ordinary life as well as extraordinary events: why people help, why they become aggressive, why they dream, why they forget, why they suffer, and how they adapt.

Modern psychology is scientific because it collects data systematically and tries to make explanations verifiable. At the same time, it studies subjective experience, culture, relationships, and meaning. That is why psychology stands between natural sciences and social sciences.

## 1. Meaning of Psychology

The word psychology comes from two Greek roots:

1. Psyche: soul or mind.
2. Logos: study or science.

Earlier, psychology was often described as the study of soul or mind. Modern psychology defines itself more carefully.

Psychology is the scientific study of mental processes, experiences, and behaviour in different contexts.

## 2. Mental Processes

Mental processes are internal activities of the mind.

Examples:

1. Thinking.
2. Remembering.
3. Learning.
4. Perceiving.
5. Imagining.
6. Feeling.
7. Deciding.
8. Solving problems.

Mental processes cannot always be seen directly, but they can be studied through behaviour, verbal reports, brain activity, experiments, and observation.

## 3. Experiences

Experiences are subjective states of awareness.

Examples:

1. Pain.
2. Joy.
3. Anxiety.
4. Grief.
5. Meditation.
6. Dreaming.
7. Love.
8. Fear.

Only the experiencing person directly knows the experience. Psychologists study experiences through reports, interviews, observation, physiological measures, and careful interpretation.

## 4. Behaviour

Behaviour refers to responses, actions, and reactions.

Behaviour can be:

1. Overt: visible to others, such as walking, speaking, writing, smiling.
2. Covert: internal or hidden, such as thinking silently, imagining, feeling tension.

Behaviour is usually connected with stimuli, motives, emotions, learning, social context, and biological processes.

## 5. Psychology as a Discipline

Psychology is defined by both:

1. What it studies.
2. How it studies.

It studies mental processes, experiences, and behaviour. It uses methods such as experiments, observation, case studies, interviews, tests, surveys, and biological measures.

Psychologists try to reduce bias, collect evidence, and explain behaviour in a systematic way.

## 6. Psychology as a Natural Science

Psychology works as a natural science when it studies behaviour through biological and experimental methods.

It uses:

1. Hypotheses.
2. Controlled observation.
3. Measurement.
4. Experiments.
5. Brain imaging.
6. Physiological recording.
7. Cause-effect analysis.

Examples:

1. Studying attention using reaction time.
2. Studying memory through recall tests.
3. Studying emotion through heart rate and brain activity.
4. Studying learning in controlled conditions.

## 7. Psychology as a Social Science

Psychology is also a social science because human behaviour occurs in social and cultural contexts.

People are shaped by:

1. Family.
2. Language.
3. Culture.
4. Class.
5. Gender.
6. Religion.
7. Education.
8. Group membership.
9. Historical conditions.

The same person may behave differently in different social settings. Therefore, psychology studies both the individual and the social environment.

## 8. Mind, Brain, and Behaviour

The mind depends on the brain, but mind and brain are not identical.

The brain is the biological organ. The mind refers to organised mental processes and experiences.

Examples that show complexity:

1. Brain injury can change perception, memory, emotion, or self-recognition.
2. Mental imagery can influence bodily states.
3. Stress can affect immunity.
4. Positive visualisation may affect health behaviour and coping.

The field of psychoneuroimmunology studies links among mind, nervous system, and immune system.

## 9. Common-Sense Psychology

Everyone uses everyday explanations of behaviour. But common sense can be misleading.

Example: one saying claims "absence makes the heart grow fonder"; another claims "out of sight, out of mind." Both cannot be generally true in the same way.

Common-sense explanations often:

1. Depend on hindsight.
2. Ignore evidence.
3. Overgeneralise.
4. Reflect personal bias.
5. Fail to predict behaviour reliably.

Scientific psychology tests explanations systematically.

## 10. Evolution of Modern Psychology

Modern psychology developed through several schools and approaches.

### Structuralism

Wilhelm Wundt established the first psychology laboratory in 1879 at Leipzig.

Structuralists studied conscious experience by trying to identify its basic elements.

Method: introspection.

Limitation: introspective reports were difficult to verify objectively.

### Functionalism

William James focused on what the mind does and how behaviour helps adaptation.

Functionalists asked how consciousness and behaviour help people adjust to their environment.

### Gestalt Psychology

Gestalt psychologists argued that experience is organised as a whole.

The whole is more than the sum of parts.

This approach deeply influenced perception research.

### Behaviourism

John Watson argued that psychology should study observable behaviour.

Behaviourists focused on stimulus-response relations, learning, conditioning, reinforcement, and measurable behaviour.

B.F. Skinner extended behaviourism through operant conditioning.

### Psychoanalysis

Sigmund Freud emphasised unconscious motives, conflicts, childhood experiences, and personality dynamics.

Psychoanalysis influenced clinical psychology and ideas about hidden causes of behaviour.

### Humanistic Psychology

Humanistic psychologists stressed free will, personal growth, meaning, and self-actualisation.

They argued that human beings are not only driven by instincts or conditioning.

### Cognitive Psychology

Cognitive psychology studies mental processes such as attention, memory, problem solving, language, and decision making.

It brought the mind back into scientific study.

## 11. Development of Psychology in India

Psychology in India has roots in philosophical traditions that studied consciousness, self, emotion, suffering, and well-being.

Modern academic psychology in India developed through universities, research institutions, clinical settings, and applied fields.

Indian psychology also draws attention to:

1. Self-reflection.
2. Yoga and meditation.
3. Social context.
4. Family and community.
5. Cultural meanings of behaviour.

## 12. Branches of Psychology

Major branches include:

| Branch | Focus |
|---|---|
| Cognitive psychology | Attention, memory, thinking, language |
| Biological psychology | Brain, nervous system, hormones, behaviour |
| Developmental psychology | Changes across lifespan |
| Social psychology | Behaviour in social contexts |
| Clinical psychology | Assessment and treatment of mental disorders |
| Counselling psychology | Adjustment, relationships, career, stress |
| Educational psychology | Learning, teaching, motivation, testing |
| Industrial-organisational psychology | Work behaviour, leadership, productivity |
| Health psychology | Behaviour, health, illness, coping |
| Environmental psychology | Human relation with physical environment |

## 13. Psychology and Other Disciplines

Psychology connects with:

1. Biology and neuroscience.
2. Medicine.
3. Sociology.
4. Anthropology.
5. Philosophy.
6. Education.
7. Computer science.
8. Economics.
9. Law.

Examples:

1. Artificial intelligence uses knowledge of cognition.
2. Medicine uses psychology in pain, stress, and adherence to treatment.
3. Education uses psychology to improve learning.
4. Law uses psychology in eyewitness testimony and criminal behaviour.

## 14. Psychology in Everyday Life

Psychology helps us understand:

2. Motivation.
3. Memory.
4. Stress.
5. Relationships.
6. Prejudice.
7. Communication.
8. Conflict.
9. Mental health.

## 16. Exam-Oriented Summary

Psychology is the scientific study of mental processes, experiences, and behaviour. It uses systematic methods to understand human and animal behaviour. It is both a natural science and a social science because it studies biological bases as well as social and cultural contexts.

Psychology developed from early philosophical ideas to modern scientific schools such as structuralism, functionalism, Gestalt psychology, behaviourism, psychoanalysis, humanistic psychology, and cognitive psychology. It has many branches and strong connections with everyday life.
