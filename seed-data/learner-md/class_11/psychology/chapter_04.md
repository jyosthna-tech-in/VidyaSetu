# Chapter 4: Human Development
### NCERT Class 11 Psychology

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how human beings change from conception to old age. Development includes physical, cognitive, social, emotional, and moral changes. It is shaped by heredity, environment, culture, history, family, peers, and personal choices.

The central idea is:

> Human development is lifelong, multidimensional, and shaped by the interaction of biological, psychological, and socio-cultural factors.

## 1. Meaning of Development

Development is the pattern of progressive, orderly, and predictable changes that begin at conception and continue throughout life.

Development includes:

- Growth
- Decline
- Learning
- Adaptation
- Social change
- Emotional change
- Cognitive change

It is not limited to childhood. Adults and older people also continue to develop.

---

## 2. Growth, Maturation, and Development

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Growth | Increase in size or quantity | Height, weight |
| Maturation | Biological unfolding according to inner timetable | Infant sitting, standing, walking when physically ready |
| Development | Overall change across life | Language, thinking, identity, relationships |

Growth is mainly quantitative. Development is broader and includes qualitative change.

---

## 3. Life-Span Perspective

The life-span perspective studies development from conception to death.

Key assumptions:

| Assumption | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Development is lifelong | Change occurs at every age |
| Development is multidimensional | Physical, cognitive, emotional, and social aspects interact |
| Development includes gains and losses | Ageing may bring wisdom but physical decline |
| Development is plastic | Abilities can improve with support and training |
| Development is contextual | Family, culture, history, and environment matter |
| Development is multidisciplinary | Psychology, sociology, biology, anthropology, and neuroscience contribute |

---

## 4. Factors Influencing Development

### 4.1 Heredity

Heredity refers to genetic inheritance from parents.

Examples:

- Height potential
- Body structure
- Temperament tendencies
- Certain abilities or vulnerabilities

### 4.2 Environment

Environment includes physical, social, cultural, and economic surroundings.

Examples:

- Nutrition
- Parenting
- Schooling
- Peer group
- Books and toys
- Healthcare
- Neighbourhood safety

### 4.3 Heredity and Environment Interaction

Development results from the interaction of heredity and environment.

Example:

A child may have a genetic potential for athletic ability, but training, nutrition, encouragement, and opportunity are needed to develop that potential.

---

## 5. Bronfenbrenner's Ecological Systems

Bronfenbrenner explained development through nested environmental systems.

| System | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Microsystem | Immediate setting | Family, school, peers |
| Mesosystem | Connections between microsystems | Parent-teacher interaction |
| Exosystem | Indirect setting affecting child | Parent's workplace |
| Macrosystem | Culture, values, laws, customs | Social beliefs about childhood |
| Chronosystem | Time and life events | Divorce, migration, historical change |

This view shows that development is affected by both close relationships and larger social conditions.

---

## 6. Developmental Stages

| Stage | Approximate period | Main focus |
|---|---|---|
| Prenatal | Conception to birth | Physical formation |
| Infancy | Birth to about 2 years | Sensory, motor, attachment, early language |
| Childhood | Early and middle years | Cognitive growth, play, school, self-concept |
| Adolescence | Puberty to young adulthood | Identity, abstract thinking, peer relations |
| Adulthood | Mature life period | Career, relationships, family, responsibility |
| Old age | Later life | Adjustment, health, reflection, social support |

These stages are useful for study, but real development varies across individuals and cultures.

---

## 7. Prenatal Development

Prenatal development is the period from conception to birth.

Important influences:

- Mother's nutrition
- Health
- Stress
- Substance use
- Medical care
- Environmental risks

Healthy prenatal conditions support later development.

---

## 8. Infancy

Infants are not helpless in every way. They are born with basic reflexes and capacities.

Examples:

- Breathing
- Sucking
- Swallowing
- Crying
- Responding to sound
- Recognising familiar voices

Infancy includes rapid physical growth, sensory development, motor development, attachment, and early communication.

---

## 9. Attachment

Attachment is the close emotional bond between an infant and caregiver.

It gives the child:

- Security
- Comfort
- Emotional regulation
- Base for exploration
- Trust in relationships

Responsive caregiving supports healthy attachment.

---

## 10. Piaget's Cognitive Development

Jean Piaget believed children actively construct knowledge and pass through stages of thinking.

| Stage | Approximate age | Main feature |
|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | 0-2 years | Learning through senses and actions |
| Preoperational | 2-7 years | Symbolic thought, egocentrism, limited logic |
| Concrete operational | 7-11 years | Logical thinking about concrete objects |
| Formal operational | 11 years onward | Abstract, hypothetical, and systematic thinking |

Piaget's theory shows that children do not simply know less than adults; they think differently.

---

## 11. Socio-Emotional Development

Socio-emotional development includes self, emotions, relationships, gender identity, and moral understanding.

Important areas:

| Area | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Self-concept | Understanding of oneself |
| Gender identity | Knowing oneself as boy, girl, or gendered person in society |
| Emotional regulation | Managing feelings |
| Peer relationships | Learning cooperation and conflict |
| Moral development | Understanding right and wrong |

---

## 12. Adolescence

Adolescence is the transition from childhood to adulthood.

Major changes:

| Area | Change |
|---|---|
| Physical | Puberty, growth spurt, sexual maturation |
| Cognitive | Abstract and logical thinking improves |
| Emotional | Strong emotions and identity exploration |
| Social | Peer influence increases |
| Moral | More complex ideas of fairness and responsibility |

Adolescence is experienced differently across cultures. In some cultures it is seen as a difficult period; in others it is treated as the beginning of adult responsibility.

---

## 13. Adulthood

Adulthood involves responsibilities such as:

- Work
- Higher education or training
- Marriage or partnership, for many people
- Parenting, for some people
- Economic independence
- Social responsibility
- Care for family members

Entry into adulthood varies across cultures and historical periods.

---

## 14. Old Age

Old age includes both losses and gains.

Possible changes:

- Physical decline
- Retirement
- Health concerns
- Loss of loved ones
- Increased reflection
- Wisdom and life experience
- Need for social support

Healthy ageing depends on physical health, emotional resilience, family support, social engagement, and meaningful activity.

---

## 15. Developmental Tasks

Developmental tasks are achievements expected at particular stages of life.

Examples:

| Stage | Developmental task |
|---|---|
| Infancy | Attachment and basic trust |
| Childhood | School skills and peer interaction |
| Adolescence | Identity formation |
| Adulthood | Work and relationships |
| Old age | Adjustment and life review |

These tasks vary across cultures.

---

## 16. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Equating development with growth | Growth is only one part of development. |
| Thinking development stops after childhood | Development is lifelong. |
| Saying heredity or environment alone determines development | Both interact. |
| Treating all children as developing at the same rate | Development varies across individuals. |
| Ignoring culture | Culture shapes developmental expectations and experiences. |
| Seeing old age only as decline | Old age may include wisdom, reflection, and social contribution. |

Human development is a lifelong process shaped by the continuous interaction of biology, environment, culture, history, and personal experience. People change across the entire life span, and every stage brings both challenges and possibilities.
