# Chapter 9: Motivation and Emotion
### NCERT Class 11 Psychology

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains why people act and how they feel. Motivation energises and directs behaviour toward goals. Emotion gives behaviour intensity, meaning, and personal experience. Both motivation and emotion involve biological, psychological, and social processes.

The central idea is:

> Motivation moves behaviour toward goals, while emotion colours experience and prepares the person for action.

## 1. Meaning of Motivation

Motivation is the process that energises, directs, and sustains behaviour.

It answers questions such as:

- Why do people study?
- Why do people work?
- Why do people seek friends?
- Why do people compete?
- Why do people eat, drink, explore, or achieve?

### Need, Drive, and Goal

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Need | A state of deficiency | Lack of food |
| Drive | Tension or arousal created by need | Hunger discomfort |
| Goal-directed behaviour | Action taken to reduce need | Searching for food |
| Goal | Object or condition that satisfies need | Eating a meal |

---

## 2. Motivation Cycle

```text
Need -> Drive/Arousal -> Goal-directed behaviour -> Goal achievement -> Drive reduction
```

Example:

Thirst creates tension. The person searches for water, drinks it, and returns to a balanced state.

---

## 3. Types of Motives

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Biological motives | Based mainly on bodily needs | Hunger, thirst, sex |
| Psychosocial motives | Developed through social interaction | Achievement, affiliation, power, curiosity |

Human motives often include both biological and social elements.

---

## 4. Biological Motives

### Hunger

Hunger motivates food-seeking and eating. It is influenced by:

- Body energy needs
- Brain mechanisms
- Blood sugar level
- Smell and sight of food
- Eating habits
- Culture and social timing

### Thirst

Thirst is triggered by water loss and bodily imbalance.

It may occur due to:

- Dehydration of cells
- Reduction in blood volume
- Heat
- Physical activity

### Sex

Sex is biologically rooted but strongly shaped by social norms, culture, values, and personal meaning.

---

## 5. Psychosocial Motives

### Achievement Motivation

Achievement motivation is the desire to meet standards of excellence.

People high in achievement motivation usually:

- Prefer moderately difficult tasks
- Want feedback
- Take responsibility
- Try to improve performance
- Set challenging but realistic goals

### Affiliation Motivation

Affiliation is the need to be close to others physically and psychologically.

It appears in:

- Friendship
- Belongingness
- Group membership
- Emotional support

### Power Motivation

Power motivation is the desire to influence, control, or have impact on others.

It may be expressed through:

- Leadership
- Persuasion
- Competition
- Social influence
- Organisational roles

### Curiosity and Exploration

Curiosity motivates people to seek new information and experiences.

It supports:

- Learning
- Creativity
- Problem solving
- Scientific inquiry

---

## 6. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow arranged human needs in a hierarchy.

```text
Self-actualisation
Esteem needs
Belongingness needs
Safety needs
Physiological needs
```

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Physiological needs | Hunger, thirst, sleep |
| Safety needs | Protection, security, stability |
| Belongingness needs | Love, friendship, group membership |
| Esteem needs | Respect, achievement, recognition |
| Self-actualisation | Realising one's full potential |

Lower needs usually demand attention first, but real life may show exceptions. People may sometimes risk safety for values, duty, or others.

## 7. Frustration and Conflict

### Frustration

Frustration occurs when a desired goal is blocked.

Example:

A student works hard for admission but does not get selected.

Common responses:

- Anger
- Sadness
- Trying alternative paths
- Withdrawal
- Aggression
- Problem solving

### Conflict

Conflict occurs when two or more motives are active at the same time.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Approach-approach | Choice between two desirable goals | Two good colleges |
| Avoidance-avoidance | Choice between two undesirable options | Dentist or toothache |
| Approach-avoidance | Same goal has positive and negative aspects | Wanting a job but fearing interview |

---

## 8. Meaning of Emotion

Emotion is an internal state involving feeling, bodily changes, and behaviour.

Examples:

- Joy
- Anger
- Fear
- Sadness
- Disgust
- Surprise

### Components of Emotion

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Physiological | Bodily changes such as heartbeat, sweating |
| Cognitive | Interpretation of the situation |
| Behavioural | Facial expressions, actions, gestures |
| Subjective | Personal feeling experience |

---

## 9. Biological Basis of Emotion

Emotion involves:

- Brain
- Autonomic nervous system
- Hormones
- Muscles
- Bodily arousal

The sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for action during strong emotion. For example, fear may increase heart rate and muscle readiness.

Lie detectors, or polygraphs, record bodily arousal indicators, but bodily arousal alone cannot perfectly reveal truth or emotion.

---

## 10. Cultural Influences on Emotion

Culture affects emotional expression.

Examples:

- Some cultures encourage open expression.
- Some cultures value emotional restraint.
- Gender roles may shape how anger or sadness is expressed.
- Public and private emotional expressions may differ.

Important distinction:

> Culture may influence expression more visibly than the inner experience itself.

---

## 11. Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is the ability to understand, manage, and use emotions effectively in oneself and others.

### Intrapersonal Components

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Self-awareness | Knowing one's emotions |
| Self-regulation | Managing impulses and negative emotions |
| Self-motivation | Continuing effort despite setbacks |

### Interpersonal Components

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Social awareness | Understanding others' feelings |
| Social competence | Managing relationships and communication |

Emotionally intelligent people can respond thoughtfully rather than react impulsively.

---

## 12. Managing Emotions

Useful strategies:

- Recognise the emotion.
- Name it accurately.
- Understand the trigger.
- Pause before reacting.
- Reframe the situation.
- Use relaxation or breathing.
- Seek social support.
- Express feelings respectfully.
- Work toward problem solving.

Negative emotions are not always bad. Fear can protect, anger can signal injustice, and sadness can invite reflection. The problem arises when emotions are excessive, inappropriate, or unmanaged.

---

## 13. Motivation and Emotion Together

Motivation and emotion influence each other.

Examples:

| Situation | Motivation | Emotion |
|---|---|---|
| Preparing for exam | Achievement motive | Anxiety or confidence |
| Seeking friends | Affiliation motive | Joy or loneliness |
| Facing threat | Safety motive | Fear |
| Winning competition | Achievement/power | Pride |

Emotions can energise motives, and motives can create emotional reactions when fulfilled or blocked.

---

## 14. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Treating motivation and emotion as separate | They interact strongly. |
| Saying all motives are biological | Many motives are psychosocial. |
| Confusing need and drive | Need is deficiency; drive is tension/arousal. |
| Treating Maslow's hierarchy as rigid | Real-life behaviour may show exceptions. |
| Saying negative emotions are useless | They can protect and guide action. |
| Thinking emotional intelligence means no emotion | It means understanding and regulating emotion. |

## Final Takeaway

Motivation explains why behaviour begins and continues. Emotion explains how behaviour is felt, expressed, and energised. Together, they help us understand goals, effort, conflict, relationships, and self-control.
