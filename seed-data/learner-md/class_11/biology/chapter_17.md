## 1. Movement As A Living Feature

Movement is a basic feature of living organisms.

Examples:

- protoplasmic streaming in Amoeba;
- ciliary movement;
- flagellar movement;
- tentacle movement;
- movement of limbs;
- movement of jaws;
- movement of eyelids;
- movement of tongue.

Movement can occur at many levels:

- within a cell;
- by a cell;
- by an organ;
- by the whole organism.

## 2. Locomotion

Locomotion is voluntary movement that changes the location of the organism.

Examples:

- walking;
- running;
- climbing;
- flying;
- swimming.

Important relation:

```text
all locomotion is movement;
all movement is not locomotion.
```

Example:

Moving the tongue is movement but not locomotion.

Walking is both movement and locomotion.

## 3. Why Animals Move

Animals move for:

- food;
- shelter;
- mate;
- breeding grounds;
- favourable climate;
- escape from predators;
- exploration;
- protection.

The type of locomotion depends on habitat and need.

## 4. Same Structure Can Serve Movement And Locomotion

One structure may serve more than one function.

Examples:

- Paramecium uses cilia for food movement and locomotion;
- Hydra uses tentacles for prey capture and locomotion;
- humans use limbs for posture changes and locomotion.

Therefore movement and locomotion are linked.

## 5. Types Of Movement In Human Cells

Human cells show three main movement types:

1. amoeboid movement;
2. ciliary movement;
3. muscular movement.

## 6. Amoeboid Movement

Amoeboid movement occurs through pseudopodia.

Pseudopodia are temporary cytoplasmic projections.

They form by streaming of protoplasm.

Human examples:

- macrophages;
- leucocytes.

Cytoskeletal elements, especially microfilaments, help this movement.

## 7. Ciliary Movement

Ciliary movement occurs by coordinated beating of cilia.

It occurs in many internal tubular organs lined by ciliated epithelium.

Examples:

- cilia in trachea help remove dust and foreign particles;
- cilia in female reproductive tract help movement of ova.

## 8. Muscular Movement

Muscular movement is produced by muscle contraction.

Examples:

- limb movement;
- jaw movement;
- tongue movement;
- posture changes;
- locomotion.

Locomotion requires coordination of:

- muscular system;
- skeletal system;
- neural system.

## 9. Flagellar Movement

Flagella are cell membrane outgrowths.

Flagellar movement helps:

- spermatozoa swim;
- sponges maintain water current in canal system;
- protists such as Euglena move.

## 10. Muscle Tissue

Muscle is specialised tissue of mesodermal origin.

In adult humans, muscles form about:

```text
40 to 50 percent of body weight
```

## 10.1 Properties Of Muscle

Muscle has four key properties:

- excitability;
- contractility;
- extensibility;
- elasticity.

Excitability allows response to stimuli.

Contractility allows shortening and force generation.

Extensibility allows stretching.

Elasticity allows return to original form.

## 11. Types Of Muscles

Muscles can be classified by:

- location;
- appearance;
- control.

Three major types:

- skeletal muscle;
- visceral muscle;
- cardiac muscle.

## 12. Skeletal Muscle

Skeletal muscles are associated with skeletal components.

They appear striped under microscope.

Therefore they are called striated muscles.

They are under voluntary control.

Therefore they are voluntary muscles.

Functions:

- locomotion;
- posture;
- body movement;
- facial expression in many cases;
- breathing movements through diaphragm and intercostals.

## 13. Visceral Muscle

Visceral muscles occur in walls of hollow internal organs.

Examples:

- alimentary canal;
- reproductive tract;
- blood vessels in many contexts;
- urinary tract.

They do not show striations.

They are smooth muscles.

They are involuntary.

Functions:

- movement of food in digestive tract;
- movement of gametes in reproductive tract;
- movement of materials in internal tubes.

## 14. Cardiac Muscle

Cardiac muscle occurs in the heart.

It is:

- striated;
- branched;
- involuntary;
- rhythmic.

Cardiac muscle cells form a branching network that allows coordinated contraction of the heart.

## 15. Muscle Types Compared

| Feature | Skeletal Muscle | Visceral Muscle | Cardiac Muscle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Location | Attached to bones | Walls of internal organs | Heart |
| Appearance | Striated | Non-striated | Striated |
| Control | Voluntary | Involuntary | Involuntary |
| Cell shape | Long cylindrical fibres | Spindle-shaped cells | Branched cells |
| Main role | Locomotion and posture | Internal organ movement | Pumping blood |

## 16. Organisation Of Skeletal Muscle

Skeletal muscle is organised in levels.

```text
whole muscle
-> fascicles
-> muscle fibres
-> myofibrils
-> myofilaments
-> contractile proteins
```

## 16.1 Fascicles

A skeletal muscle is made of many muscle bundles.

These bundles are called fascicles.

Fascicles are held together by collagenous connective tissue called fascia.

## 16.2 Muscle Fibre

Each fascicle contains many muscle fibres.

A muscle fibre is a muscle cell.

It is covered by sarcolemma.

Its cytoplasm is sarcoplasm.

It contains many nuclei.

Therefore skeletal muscle fibre is a syncytium.

## 16.3 Sarcoplasmic Reticulum

Sarcoplasmic reticulum is the endoplasmic reticulum of muscle fibre.

It stores calcium ions.

Ca++ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum is essential for contraction.

## 16.4 Myofibrils

Muscle fibres contain many parallel myofibrils.

Myofibrils have alternate dark and light bands.

This produces the striated appearance.

## 17. Myofilaments

Myofibrils contain two main myofilaments:

- actin;
- myosin.

Actin filaments are thin.

Myosin filaments are thick.

Both are arranged parallel to the long axis of the myofibril.

## 18. Bands And Lines In Sarcomere

## 18.1 I Band

I band is the light band.

It contains actin.

It is called isotropic band.

It is bisected by Z line.

## 18.2 A Band

A band is the dark band.

It contains myosin.

It is called anisotropic band.

The length of A band remains constant during contraction.

## 18.3 Z Line

Z line bisects the I band.

Thin filaments are attached to Z line.

During contraction, Z lines move closer.

## 18.4 M Line

M line lies in the middle of A band.

It holds thick filaments together.

## 18.5 H Zone

H zone is the central region of A band containing only thick filaments in a resting sarcomere.

It becomes narrower during contraction.

## 19. Sarcomere

Sarcomere is the functional unit of contraction.

It is the region between two successive Z lines.

Basic layout:

```text
Z line | I band | A band with H zone | I band | Z line
```

During contraction:

- sarcomere shortens;
- Z lines move closer;
- I band shortens;
- H zone shortens;
- A band remains same length;
- actin slides over myosin.

## 20. Actin Filament Structure

Each actin filament is made of:

- two F-actin strands;
- tropomyosin;
- troponin.

## 20.1 F-Actin And G-Actin

F-actin means filamentous actin.

Each F-actin is a polymer of G-actin.

G-actin means globular actin.

Two F-actin strands are helically wound around each other.

## 20.2 Tropomyosin

Tropomyosin runs along the F-actin strands.

In resting muscle, it helps cover myosin-binding sites on actin.

## 20.3 Troponin

Troponin is distributed at regular intervals on tropomyosin.

In resting state, a troponin subunit masks the active binding sites for myosin on actin.

When Ca++ binds troponin, the masking is removed.

## 21. Myosin Filament Structure

Each myosin filament is a polymerised protein.

It is made of many meromyosin units.

Each meromyosin has:

- globular head with short arm;
- tail.

## 21.1 Heavy And Light Meromyosin

Heavy meromyosin includes:

- globular head;
- short arm.

Light meromyosin forms the tail.

## 21.2 Myosin Head

The myosin head:

- projects outward as a cross arm;
- has ATPase activity;
- has ATP-binding site;
- has actin-binding site.

This head is the active part in cross-bridge formation.

## 22. Sliding Filament Theory

Sliding filament theory states that muscle contraction occurs by sliding of thin filaments over thick filaments.

The filaments themselves do not shorten.

Instead:

```text
actin slides inward over myosin;
sarcomere shortens.
```

## 23. Neuromuscular Junction

Muscle contraction begins with a signal from the central nervous system.

The signal travels through a motor neuron.

A motor neuron and all muscle fibres supplied by it form a motor unit.

The junction between motor neuron and muscle fibre is the neuromuscular junction.

It is also called motor-end plate.

## 24. Initiation Of Contraction

Sequence:

```text
nerve impulse reaches neuromuscular junction
-> acetylcholine is released
-> action potential forms in sarcolemma
-> action potential spreads through muscle fibre
-> Ca++ is released into sarcoplasm
```

## 25. Role Of Calcium

Ca++ binds to troponin.

Troponin changes position.

Tropomyosin shifts away from myosin-binding sites.

Active sites on actin become exposed.

Now myosin heads can bind to actin.

## 26. Cross-Bridge Cycle

## 26.1 Cross-Bridge Formation

Myosin head uses energy from ATP hydrolysis.

It binds to exposed active site on actin.

This forms a cross-bridge.

## 26.2 Power Stroke

The myosin head bends and pulls actin toward the centre of A band.

Z lines move inward.

Sarcomere shortens.

## 26.3 Cross-Bridge Breakage

ADP and inorganic phosphate are released.

A new ATP binds to myosin head.

The cross-bridge breaks.

## 26.4 Reactivation Of Myosin

ATP is hydrolysed by myosin ATPase.

The myosin head returns to an energised state.

It can form another cross-bridge.

The cycle repeats as long as:

- Ca++ remains in sarcoplasm;
- ATP is available;
- actin sites remain exposed.

## 27. Relaxation

Relaxation occurs when Ca++ is pumped back into sarcoplasmic reticulum.

Ca++ leaves troponin.

Tropomyosin again masks binding sites on actin.

Cross-bridge cycling stops.

Z lines return toward original position.

Muscle fibre relaxes.

## 28. Changes During Contraction

| Sarcomere Region | Change During Contraction |
|---|---|
| Sarcomere length | Decreases |
| Z lines | Move closer |
| I band | Shortens |
| H zone | Shortens |
| A band | Remains same length |
| Actin and myosin filament length | Remains same |

## 29. Muscle Fatigue

Repeated activation of muscles can lead to fatigue.

A major cause is accumulation of lactic acid due to anaerobic breakdown of glycogen.

Fatigue reduces muscle performance.

## 30. Red And White Muscle Fibres

Muscles differ in myoglobin and mitochondria content.

## 30.1 Red Fibres

Red fibres have:

- high myoglobin;
- many mitochondria;
- high oxygen storage;
- aerobic ATP production;
- red appearance.

They resist fatigue better.

They are useful for sustained activity.

## 30.2 White Fibres

White fibres have:

- low myoglobin;
- fewer mitochondria;
- high sarcoplasmic reticulum;
- more anaerobic energy dependence;
- pale appearance.

They contract rapidly but fatigue faster.

## 30.3 Red And White Fibres Compared

| Feature | Red Fibres | White Fibres |
|---|---|---|
| Myoglobin | High | Low |
| Colour | Red | Pale |
| Mitochondria | Many | Few |
| Main metabolism | Aerobic | Anaerobic |
| Fatigue resistance | High | Lower |
| Best suited for | Sustained work | Quick bursts |

## 31. Skeletal System

The skeletal system consists of:

- bones;
- cartilage.

It supports:

- movement;
- posture;
- protection;
- attachment for muscles.

Human skeletal system has:

```text
206 bones
```

## 32. Bone And Cartilage

Bone and cartilage are specialised connective tissues.

Bone has hard matrix because of calcium salts.

Cartilage has slightly pliable matrix because of chondroitin salts.

## 33. Divisions Of Skeleton

Human skeleton has two main divisions:

- axial skeleton;
- appendicular skeleton.

## 33.1 Axial Skeleton

Axial skeleton has 80 bones.

It lies along the main axis of body.

It includes:

- skull;
- vertebral column;
- sternum;
- ribs.

## 33.2 Appendicular Skeleton

Appendicular skeleton includes:

- limb bones;
- pectoral girdle;
- pelvic girdle.

It has 126 bones.

## 34. Skull

The skull has 22 bones.

It includes:

- 8 cranial bones;
- 14 facial bones.

Cranial bones form cranium, the protective covering for brain.

Facial bones form the front part of skull.

## 34.1 Hyoid Bone

Hyoid is a single U-shaped bone at the base of buccal cavity.

## 34.2 Ear Ossicles

Each middle ear contains three tiny bones:

- malleus;
- incus;
- stapes.

Together they are called ear ossicles.

## 34.3 Dicondylic Skull

Human skull has two occipital condyles.

It articulates with vertebral column through these condyles.

Therefore human skull is dicondylic.

## 35. Vertebral Column

Human vertebral column has 26 serially arranged units.

It extends from base of skull.

It forms main framework of trunk.

It is dorsally placed.

Each vertebra has a neural canal for spinal cord.

## 35.1 Regions Of Vertebral Column

| Region | Number |
|---|---:|
| Cervical | 7 |
| Thoracic | 12 |
| Lumbar | 5 |
| Sacral | 1 fused |
| Coccygeal | 1 fused |

Total:

```text
26 vertebral units
```

## 35.2 Atlas

Atlas is the first cervical vertebra.

It articulates with occipital condyles.

## 35.3 Functions Of Vertebral Column

Vertebral column:

- protects spinal cord;
- supports head;
- supports trunk;
- provides attachment for ribs;
- provides attachment for back muscles.

## 36. Sternum And Ribs

Sternum is a flat bone on ventral midline of thorax.

Humans have 12 pairs of ribs.

Each rib is a thin flat bone.

Each rib connects dorsally to vertebral column.

Many ribs connect ventrally to sternum.

Ribs are bicephalic because each has two articulation surfaces on dorsal end.

## 36.1 True Ribs

First seven pairs are true ribs.

They attach:

- dorsally to thoracic vertebrae;
- ventrally to sternum through hyaline cartilage.

## 36.2 False Ribs

Eighth, ninth, and tenth pairs do not connect directly to sternum.

They join the seventh rib through hyaline cartilage.

They are vertebrochondral ribs.

## 36.3 Floating Ribs

Eleventh and twelfth pairs are floating ribs.

They do not connect ventrally to sternum.

## 36.4 Rib Cage

Rib cage is formed by:

- thoracic vertebrae;
- ribs;
- sternum.

It protects thoracic organs and supports breathing movements.

## 37. Appendicular Skeleton

Appendicular skeleton includes limb bones and girdles.

Each limb has 30 bones.

## 37.1 Forelimb Bones

Each upper limb has:

- humerus;
- radius;
- ulna;
- 8 carpals;
- 5 metacarpals;
- 14 phalanges.

Total:

```text
30 bones per upper limb
```

## 37.2 Hindlimb Bones

Each lower limb has:

- femur;
- tibia;
- fibula;
- 7 tarsals;
- 5 metatarsals;
- 14 phalanges;
- patella.

Femur is the longest bone.

Patella is the kneecap.

Total:

```text
30 bones per lower limb
```

## 38. Pectoral Girdle

Each half of pectoral girdle has:

- clavicle;
- scapula.

Scapula is a large triangular flat bone on dorsal thorax.

It lies between second and seventh ribs.

The scapular spine projects as acromion.

Clavicle articulates with acromion.

Glenoid cavity articulates with head of humerus to form shoulder joint.

Clavicle is also called collar bone.

## 39. Pelvic Girdle

Pelvic girdle has two coxal bones.

Each coxal bone is formed by fusion of:

- ilium;
- ischium;
- pubis.

At the fusion point is acetabulum.

Acetabulum articulates with femur.

The two halves meet ventrally at pubic symphysis.

Pubic symphysis contains fibrous cartilage.

## 40. Joints

Joints are points of contact:

- between bones;
- between bone and cartilage.

Joints allow movement by acting as fulcrums.

Muscle force produces movement through joints.

## 41. Types Of Joints

Three structural types:

- fibrous joints;
- cartilaginous joints;
- synovial joints.

## 41.1 Fibrous Joints

Fibrous joints do not allow movement.

Example:

- sutures between skull bones.

Skull bones fuse end-to-end with dense fibrous connective tissue.

## 41.2 Cartilaginous Joints

Cartilaginous joints allow limited movement.

Bones are joined by cartilage.

Example:

- joint between adjacent vertebrae.

Pubic symphysis is also a cartilaginous joint.

## 41.3 Synovial Joints

Synovial joints allow considerable movement.

They have a fluid-filled synovial cavity between articulating bone surfaces.

They are important in locomotion.

## 42. Synovial Joint Types

| Joint Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Ball and socket | Humerus and pectoral girdle, femur and acetabulum |
| Hinge | Knee, elbow, between phalanges |
| Pivot | Atlas and axis |
| Gliding | Between carpals |
| Saddle | Carpal and metacarpal of thumb |

## 43. Disorders Of Muscular And Skeletal System

## 43.1 Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder.

It affects neuromuscular junction.

Effects:

- fatigue;
- weakening of skeletal muscle;
- paralysis in severe cases.

## 43.2 Muscular Dystrophy

Muscular dystrophy is progressive degeneration of skeletal muscle.

It is mostly due to genetic disorder.

## 43.3 Tetany

Tetany means rapid spasms or wild contractions of muscles.

It occurs due to low Ca++ in body fluid.

## 43.4 Arthritis

Arthritis is inflammation of joints.

It causes pain and restricted movement.

## 43.5 Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is age-related decrease in bone mass.

It increases chance of fractures.

Decreased estrogen level is a common cause.

## 43.6 Gout

Gout is inflammation of joints due to accumulation of uric acid crystals.

## 44. Common Misconceptions

## 44.1 Misconception: Locomotion And Movement Are Same

Correct idea:

Locomotion changes place.

Movement may or may not change place.

## 44.2 Misconception: Skeletal Muscle Fibres Have One Nucleus

Correct idea:

Skeletal muscle fibres are multinucleate and form a syncytium.

## 44.3 Misconception: A Band Shortens During Contraction

Correct idea:

A band remains same length.

I band and H zone shorten.

## 44.4 Misconception: Actin And Myosin Shorten

Correct idea:

The filaments slide over each other.

They do not themselves shorten.

## 44.5 Misconception: Calcium Gives Energy For Contraction

Correct idea:

Calcium exposes actin binding sites.

ATP provides energy for cross-bridge cycling.

## 44.6 Misconception: All Joints Allow Free Movement

Correct idea:

Fibrous joints are immovable.

Cartilaginous joints allow limited movement.

Synovial joints allow considerable movement.

## 45. Solved Concept Checks

## 45.1 Why Are All Locomotions Movements But Not All Movements Locomotions?

Locomotion changes the location of the whole organism.

Movement may involve only a body part or cell component.

Therefore locomotion is a type of movement.

## 45.2 What Are The Three Types Of Movement In Human Cells?

Human cells show:

- amoeboid movement;
- ciliary movement;
- muscular movement.

## 45.3 Why Is Skeletal Muscle Voluntary?

Skeletal muscle activity is under conscious control of the nervous system.

Therefore it is voluntary.

## 45.4 Why Is Cardiac Muscle Involuntary?

Cardiac muscle contracts rhythmically without conscious control.

It is not directly controlled voluntarily.

## 45.5 What Is A Sarcomere?

Sarcomere is the functional contractile unit of myofibril.

It lies between two successive Z lines.

## 45.6 What Happens To I Band And A Band During Contraction?

I band shortens.

A band remains the same length.

This supports sliding filament theory.

## 45.7 Why Is ATP Needed In Muscle Contraction?

ATP provides energy for myosin head movement.

ATP binding also helps detach myosin from actin.

ATP hydrolysis re-energises the myosin head.

## 45.8 Why Do Muscles Fatigue?

Repeated activation can cause lactic acid accumulation due to anaerobic breakdown of glycogen.

This contributes to fatigue.

## 45.9 Why Are Red Fibres Aerobic?

Red fibres contain high myoglobin and many mitochondria.

They store oxygen and use aerobic respiration efficiently.

## 45.10 Why Are Synovial Joints Important?

Synovial joints have a fluid-filled cavity and allow considerable movement.

They are essential for locomotion.

## 46. Exam-Oriented Answers

## 46.1 Sliding Filament Theory

Sliding filament theory states that muscle contraction occurs when thin actin filaments slide over thick myosin filaments.

The sarcomere shortens because Z lines move closer.

The I band and H zone reduce, while A band remains the same length.

## 46.2 Steps In Muscle Contraction

1. CNS sends signal through motor neuron.
2. Acetylcholine is released at neuromuscular junction.
3. Action potential spreads over sarcolemma.
4. Ca++ is released from sarcoplasmic reticulum.
5. Ca++ binds troponin.
6. Tropomyosin shifts and exposes actin sites.
7. Myosin head binds actin and forms cross-bridge.
8. Myosin head pulls actin toward centre of A band.
9. New ATP binds and cross-bridge breaks.
10. ATP hydrolysis re-energises myosin head.
11. Cycle repeats until Ca++ is pumped back.
12. Binding sites are masked and muscle relaxes.

## 46.3 Actin And Myosin Difference

| Associated proteins | Troponin and tropomyosin | Meromyosin units |
| Main role | Provides binding sites for myosin | Pulls actin using ATP |
| ATPase activity | No major ATPase head | Myosin head has ATPase |

## 49. Practice Answers

## 49.1 Skeletal Muscle Versus Cardiac Muscle

Skeletal muscle is attached to bones, voluntary, striated, and mainly helps in locomotion and posture.

Cardiac muscle is found in heart, involuntary, striated, branched, and pumps blood rhythmically.

## 49.2 Sliding Filament Theory

During contraction, thin actin filaments slide over thick myosin filaments.

Sarcomere shortens, Z lines move closer, I band and H zone shorten, and A band remains unchanged.

## 49.3 Role Of Calcium

Ca++ binds troponin and shifts tropomyosin away from actin binding sites.

This allows myosin heads to bind actin.

## 49.4 Role Of ATP

ATP provides energy for myosin head movement.

ATP binding breaks cross-bridge.

ATP hydrolysis re-energises the myosin head.
