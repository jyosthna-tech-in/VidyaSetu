## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter links chemistry with biology. The goal is not to memorise every molecule, but to understand how living tissues are analysed and how biomolecules are grouped.

Study in this order:

1. Learn how chemical composition is analysed.
2. Separate acid-soluble and acid-insoluble fractions.
3. Understand micromolecules and macromolecules.
4. Study amino acids, lipids, sugars, nucleotides, proteins, polysaccharides, and nucleic acids.
5. Study protein structure levels.
6. Study enzymes as biological catalysts.

The most important exam areas are macromolecules, protein structure, enzymes, cofactors, and enzyme factors such as temperature, pH, substrate concentration, and inhibition.

## 1. Chemical Composition Of Living Matter

Living organisms and non-living matter are made of elements. Qualitatively, many elements present in living tissues are also present in the earth's crust.

The difference lies mainly in relative abundance.

Living organisms have higher relative abundance of carbon and hydrogen compared with non-living crustal matter.

## 1.1 Common Elements In Living Tissues

Important elements include:

- carbon;
- hydrogen;
- oxygen;
- nitrogen;
- sulphur;
- sodium;
- potassium;
- calcium;
- magnesium;
- phosphorus;
- chlorine.

Water is the most abundant chemical in living organisms.

## 2. How To Analyse Chemical Composition

To analyse living tissue chemically, one can grind tissue in trichloroacetic acid, usually written as Cl3CCOOH.

The tissue forms a thick slurry.

When this slurry is strained through cotton or cheesecloth, two fractions are obtained.

## 2.1 Two Fractions

| Fraction | Also Called | What It Contains |
|---|---|---|
| Filtrate | Acid-soluble pool | Small organic compounds and inorganic ions |
| Retentate | Acid-insoluble fraction | Biomacromolecules and membrane fragments |

## 2.2 Why This Separation Matters

The acid-soluble pool contains many small molecules.

The acid-insoluble fraction contains large molecules such as proteins, nucleic acids, and polysaccharides. Lipids also appear here because they are part of membranes, which break into insoluble vesicles during grinding.

## 2.3 Wet Weight, Dry Weight, And Ash

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Wet weight | Weight of tissue before drying |
| Dry weight | Weight after water evaporates |
| Ash | Inorganic residue left after complete burning |

When tissue is fully burnt:

- carbon compounds oxidise to gases such as CO2 and water vapour;
- remaining ash contains inorganic elements such as calcium and magnesium.

## 2.4 Organic And Inorganic Constituents

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Organic constituents | Amino acids, sugars, fatty acids, nucleotides, proteins |
| Inorganic constituents | Water, sodium ions, potassium ions, calcium ions, magnesium ions, chloride, phosphate, sulphate |

## 3. Small Organic Biomolecules

From a biological point of view, small organic molecules include:

- amino acids;
- sugars;
- fatty acids;
- glycerol;
- nitrogen bases;
- nucleosides;
- nucleotides.

## 4. Amino Acids

Amino acids are organic compounds containing:

- amino group;
- carboxyl group;
- hydrogen;
- variable R group.

All four are attached to the alpha-carbon.

## 4.1 General Amino Acid Structure

```text
       H
       |
H2N - C - COOH
       |
       R
```

The R group determines the type and properties of the amino acid.

## 4.2 Protein Amino Acids

Although many amino acids exist, proteins are mainly built from 20 types of amino acids.

Examples:

- glycine, where R is hydrogen;
- alanine, where R is methyl group;
- serine, where R is hydroxymethyl group.

## 4.3 Classification Of Amino Acids

| Type | Basis | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acidic amino acid | More acidic groups | Glutamic acid |
| Basic amino acid | More basic groups | Lysine |
| Neutral amino acid | Balanced amino and carboxyl groups | Valine |
| Aromatic amino acid | Aromatic ring in R group | Tyrosine, phenylalanine, tryptophan |

## 4.4 Zwitterionic Nature

Amino acids have ionisable amino and carboxyl groups.

In different pH conditions, their charge state changes.

The zwitterionic form contains both positive and negative charges in the same molecule.

## 5. Lipids

Lipids are generally water-insoluble biomolecules.

They include:

- fatty acids;
- glycerol;
- fats;
- oils;
- phospholipids;
- complex lipids.

## 5.1 Fatty Acids

A fatty acid has a carboxyl group attached to an R group.

The R group may be a hydrocarbon chain of different length.

Examples:

- palmitic acid has 16 carbon atoms including carboxyl carbon;
- arachidonic acid has 20 carbon atoms including carboxyl carbon.

## 5.2 Saturated And Unsaturated Fatty Acids

| Type | Feature |
|---|---|
| Saturated fatty acid | No carbon-carbon double bond |
| Unsaturated fatty acid | One or more carbon-carbon double bonds |

## 5.3 Glycerol And Glycerides

Glycerol is trihydroxy propane.

When fatty acids esterify with glycerol, they form:

- monoglycerides;
- diglycerides;
- triglycerides.

Fats and oils are glycerides.

Oils have lower melting points and remain liquid more easily.

## 5.4 Phospholipids

Phospholipids contain:

- fatty acid components;
- phosphate;
- phosphorylated organic compound.

They are important components of cell membranes.

Lecithin is an example.

## 6. Nitrogen Bases, Nucleosides, And Nucleotides

Living organisms contain nitrogen bases with heterocyclic rings.

## 6.1 Nitrogen Bases

| Base | Type |
|---|---|
| Adenine | Purine |
| Guanine | Purine |
| Cytosine | Pyrimidine |
| Uracil | Pyrimidine |
| Thymine | Pyrimidine |

## 6.2 Nucleoside And Nucleotide

| Term | Components |
|---|---|
| Nucleoside | Nitrogen base plus sugar |
| Nucleotide | Nitrogen base plus sugar plus phosphate |

Examples of nucleosides:

- adenosine;
- guanosine;
- thymidine;
- uridine;
- cytidine.

Examples of nucleotides:

- adenylic acid;
- thymidylic acid;
- guanylic acid;
- uridylic acid;
- cytidylic acid.

DNA and RNA are made of nucleotides.

## 7. Primary And Secondary Metabolites

Biomolecules involved in metabolism are called metabolites.

## 7.1 Primary Metabolites

Primary metabolites have identifiable roles in normal physiological processes.

Examples:

- amino acids;
- sugars;
- fatty acids;
- nucleotides;
- proteins;
- nucleic acids.

These are common in animal tissues and other living cells.

## 7.2 Secondary Metabolites

Secondary metabolites are often found in plants, fungi, and microbes.

Their role in the host organism may not always be fully understood, but many are useful to humans or ecologically important.

## 7.3 Examples Of Secondary Metabolites

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Pigments | Carotenoids, anthocyanins |
| Alkaloids | Morphine, codeine |
| Terpenoids | Monoterpenes, diterpenes |

| Lectins | Concanavalin A |
| Drugs | Vinblastine, curcumin |
| Polymeric substances | Rubber, gums, cellulose |

## 8. Biomacromolecules

Biomolecules are grouped by molecular size and solubility fraction.

## 8.1 Micromolecules

Micromolecules are small biomolecules with molecular weight usually below 1000 Da.

They are commonly found in the acid-soluble pool.

Examples:

- amino acids;
- sugars;
- fatty acids;
- glycerol;
- nucleotides.

## 8.2 Macromolecules

Macromolecules generally have molecular weight around 10000 Da or more.

They are found in the acid-insoluble fraction.

Major true biomacromolecules:

- proteins;
- nucleic acids;
- polysaccharides.

## 8.3 Why Lipids Appear In The Macromolecular Fraction

Lipids usually have molecular weights below 800 Da, so they are not true macromolecules.

However, lipids are major parts of cell membranes and organelle membranes.

When tissue is ground, membranes break into vesicles. These vesicles are not water soluble, so they separate with the acid-insoluble fraction.

Therefore, lipids appear in the macromolecular fraction because of membrane association, not because they are large polymers.

## 8.4 Average Cell Composition

| Component | Approximate Share Of Cellular Mass |
|---|---|
| Water | 70 to 90 percent |
| Proteins | 10 to 15 percent |
| Carbohydrates | About 3 percent |
| Lipids | About 2 percent |
| Nucleic acids | 5 to 7 percent |
| Ions | About 1 percent |

## 9. Proteins

Proteins are polypeptides.

They are linear chains of amino acids joined by peptide bonds.

## 9.1 Proteins As Heteropolymers

Proteins are heteropolymers because they are made of different kinds of amino acids.

A homopolymer has only one kind of repeating monomer.

Since proteins may contain many of the 20 amino acids in different sequences, they are heteropolymers.

## 9.2 Essential And Non-Essential Amino Acids

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Essential amino acids | Must be obtained through diet |
| Non-essential amino acids | Can be made by the body |

Dietary proteins are important because they provide essential amino acids.

## 9.3 Functions Of Proteins

| Protein | Function |
|---|---|
| Collagen | Intercellular ground substance |
| Trypsin | Enzyme |
| Insulin | Hormone |
| Antibody | Defence against infectious agents |
| Receptor | Sensory reception such as smell, taste, or hormone response |
| GLUT-4 | Enables glucose transport into cells |

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the animal world.

RuBisCO is the most abundant protein in the biosphere.

## 10. Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides are long chains of sugars.

They are carbohydrates found in the acid-insoluble fraction.

## 10.1 Important Polysaccharides

| Polysaccharide | Building Block | Function Or Occurrence |
|---|---|---|
| Cellulose | Glucose | Plant cell wall |
| Starch | Glucose | Storage form in plants |
| Glycogen | Glucose | Storage form in animals |
| Inulin | Fructose | Plant storage polysaccharide |
| Chitin | Amino-sugars and modified sugars | Arthropod exoskeleton |

## 10.2 Cellulose

Cellulose is a homopolymer of glucose.

Plant cell walls are made of cellulose.

Paper made from plant pulp and cotton fibre are cellulosic.

## 10.3 Starch And Iodine

Starch forms helical secondary structures.

The helix can hold iodine molecules, producing a blue colour.

Cellulose does not form the same complex helices and does not give this blue iodine complex.

## 10.4 Glycogen

Glycogen is an animal storage polysaccharide.

It is branched and has reducing and non-reducing ends.

## 11. Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are polynucleotides.

They are found in the acid-insoluble fraction.

## 11.1 Components Of A Nucleotide

Each nucleotide has:

1. Nitrogen base
2. Pentose sugar
3. Phosphate group

## 11.2 DNA And RNA

| Nucleic Acid | Sugar | Full Name |
|---|---|---|
| DNA | Deoxyribose | Deoxyribonucleic acid |
| RNA | Ribose | Ribonucleic acid |

DNA and RNA function as genetic material or participate in genetic processes.

## 12. Structure Of Proteins

Proteins are described at four structural levels.

## 12.1 Primary Structure

Primary structure is the sequence of amino acids in a protein.

The first amino acid is the N-terminal amino acid.

The last amino acid is the C-terminal amino acid.

## 12.2 Secondary Structure

Secondary structure refers to local folding patterns of the polypeptide chain.

Examples include:

- alpha helix;
- beta-pleated sheet.

In proteins, right-handed helices are commonly observed.

## 12.3 Tertiary Structure

Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain.

It is essential for many biological activities of proteins.

## 12.4 Quaternary Structure

Quaternary structure occurs when a protein has more than one polypeptide subunit.

The arrangement of these subunits forms the quaternary structure.

Example: adult human haemoglobin has four subunits, two alpha-type and two beta-type.

## 12.5 Protein Structure Summary

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Primary | Amino acid sequence |
| Secondary | Local folding such as helix or sheet |
| Tertiary | Overall 3D structure of one polypeptide |
| Quaternary | Arrangement of multiple subunits |

## 13. Enzymes

Almost all enzymes are proteins.

Some nucleic acids also behave like enzymes. These are called ribozymes.

## 13.1 Active Site

An enzyme has a three-dimensional structure with pockets or crevices.

One such pocket is the active site.

The substrate fits into the active site.

## 13.2 Enzymes As Catalysts

Enzymes increase the rate of biochemical reactions.

They are highly specific and work under mild biological conditions.

Inorganic catalysts often work at high temperature and pressure, while many enzymes get damaged at high temperature.

Some enzymes from thermophilic organisms remain stable even at high temperatures such as 80 to 90 degree C.

## 14. Chemical Reactions And Reaction Rate

A chemical reaction involves breaking and forming bonds.

Physical changes involve changes in shape or state without forming new chemical substances.

## 14.1 Reaction Rate

Reaction rate refers to amount of product formed per unit time.

It may be written simply as:

```text
rate = change in product / change in time
```

Rate may also be called velocity when direction is specified.

## 14.2 Temperature Effect On Rate

A general rule is that reaction rate may approximately double or halve for every 10 degree C change in temperature, depending on direction of change.

## 14.3 Carbonic Anhydrase Example

The reaction is:

```text
CO2 + H2O -> H2CO3
```

Without enzyme, the reaction is very slow.

With carbonic anhydrase, the reaction rate becomes extremely high.

This shows how powerful enzyme catalysis can be.

## 14.4 Metabolic Pathway

A metabolic pathway is a multistep chemical process in which each step is catalysed by an enzyme or enzyme complex.

Example:

```text
Glucose -> 2 pyruvic acid
```

In different conditions, the same broad pathway may produce different end products:

| Condition | Product |
|---|---|
| Aerobic condition | Pyruvic acid proceeds into aerobic respiration |
| Anaerobic skeletal muscle | Lactic acid |
| Yeast fermentation | Ethanol |

## 15. How Enzymes Increase Reaction Rate

An enzyme converts substrate into product.

Symbolically:

```text
S -> P
```

Where:

- S means substrate;
- P means product.

The substrate binds at the enzyme active site.

## 15.1 Enzyme-Substrate Complex

The enzyme-substrate complex is written as ES.

It is temporary.

Simplified pathway:

```text
E + S -> ES -> EP -> E + P
```

Where:

- E is enzyme;
- S is substrate;
- ES is enzyme-substrate complex;
- EP is enzyme-product complex;
- P is product.

## 15.2 Transition State And Activation Energy

The substrate must pass through a high-energy transition state before it becomes product.

Activation energy is the energy difference between the substrate state and the transition state.

Enzymes lower this activation energy barrier.

This makes conversion of substrate to product easier and faster.

## 16. Catalytic Cycle Of Enzyme Action

The catalytic cycle can be described in four steps:

1. Substrate binds to the active site.
2. Binding induces the enzyme to fit more tightly around the substrate.
3. The active site helps break and form bonds, producing an enzyme-product complex.
4. Product is released and the free enzyme can bind another substrate.

## 17. Factors Affecting Enzyme Rate

Enzyme activity depends on conditions that affect protein structure and substrate binding.

Major factors:

- temperature;
- pH;
- substrate concentration;
- inhibitors.

## 17.1 Temperature

Each enzyme has an optimum temperature.

Below the optimum, activity is low.

At very high temperature, enzymes may be denatured and lose activity.

## 17.2 pH

Each enzyme has an optimum pH.

The rate decreases on either side of this optimum.

Changes in pH may alter enzyme structure and active site charge.

## 17.3 Substrate Concentration

As substrate concentration increases, reaction velocity first increases.

Eventually, the reaction reaches maximum velocity, Vmax.

Beyond Vmax, adding more substrate does not increase rate because enzyme active sites are saturated.

## 17.4 Enzyme Inhibition

An inhibitor is a chemical that reduces or stops enzyme activity.

In competitive inhibition, the inhibitor resembles the substrate and competes for the active site.

Example:

Malonate inhibits succinic dehydrogenase because it resembles succinate.

Competitive inhibitors are useful in controlling some bacterial pathogens.

## 18. Classification Of Enzymes

Enzymes are classified according to the type of reaction they catalyse.

## 18.1 Major Classes Of Enzymes

| Class | Reaction Type |
|---|---|
| Oxidoreductases or dehydrogenases | Oxidation-reduction reactions |
| Transferases | Transfer of a group other than hydrogen |
| Hydrolases | Hydrolysis of bonds |
| Lyases | Removal of groups without hydrolysis, often forming double bonds |
| Isomerases | Interconversion of isomers |
| Ligases | Joining of two compounds using energy |

## 19. Cofactors

Some enzymes require non-protein components called cofactors.

The protein part of such an enzyme is called apoenzyme.

The complete active enzyme requires the cofactor.

## 19.1 Types Of Cofactors

| Type | Nature | Binding | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prosthetic group | Organic compound | Tightly bound | Haem in peroxidase and catalase |
| Coenzyme | Organic compound | Transient association | NAD and NADP |
| Metal ion | Inorganic ion | Coordinates with enzyme and substrate | Zinc in carboxypeptidase |

## 19.2 Importance Of Cofactors

If the cofactor is removed, catalytic activity may be lost.

This shows that cofactors are crucial for enzyme activity.

Many coenzymes contain vitamins. For example, NAD and NADP contain niacin.

## 21. Common Learner Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling all acid-insoluble molecules true macromolecules | Lipids are in that fraction due to membrane association, not polymeric size |
| Confusing nucleoside and nucleotide | Nucleoside lacks phosphate; nucleotide has phosphate |
| Saying all proteins are enzymes | Many enzymes are proteins, but proteins also act as hormones, receptors, antibodies, and structural molecules |
| Forgetting RuBisCO | RuBisCO is the most abundant protein in the biosphere |
| Calling cellulose a heteropolymer | Cellulose is a homopolymer of glucose |
| Saying starch and cellulose both give iodine blue colour | Starch does; cellulose does not |
| Thinking enzymes change reaction equilibrium by being used up | Enzymes lower activation energy and are released unchanged |
| Confusing coenzyme and apoenzyme | Apoenzyme is protein part; coenzyme is non-protein cofactor |

## 22. Exercise Answers

## 22.1 What Are Macromolecules? Give Examples

Macromolecules are large biological molecules with high molecular weight, usually above 10000 Da.

They are found in the acid-insoluble fraction of living tissues.

Examples:

- proteins;
- nucleic acids;
- polysaccharides.

Lipids appear in the acid-insoluble fraction because they are associated with membranes, but they are not true polymeric macromolecules.

## 22.2 What Is Meant By Tertiary Structure Of Proteins?

Tertiary structure is the overall three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain.

It forms when the protein chain folds upon itself into a compact shape.

Tertiary structure is essential for many biological activities of proteins because it creates specific shapes such as active sites and binding regions.

Many such compounds are manufactured or purified industrially. Buyers include pharmaceutical companies, food industries, research laboratories, diagnostic companies, cosmetics industries, and biotechnology companies.

Examples of therapeutic proteins:

- insulin;
- growth hormone;
- interferons;
- clotting factors;
- monoclonal antibodies;
- therapeutic enzymes;
- vaccines based on protein antigens.

Other uses of proteins:

- collagen in biomedical and cosmetic products;
- keratin in hair-care products;
- enzymes in detergents and food processing;
- antibodies in diagnostic kits;
- albumin in medical preparations.

## 22.5 Composition Of Triglyceride

A triglyceride is made of one glycerol molecule esterified with three fatty acid molecules.

Text structure:

```text
Glycerol + fatty acid 1 + fatty acid 2 + fatty acid 3 -> triglyceride + water
```

Triglycerides are common fats and oils.

## 22.6 Building Models Of Biomolecules

Ball-and-stick models can be used to build biomolecules such as:

- alanine;
- glucose;
- glycerol;
- fatty acids;
- nucleotides;
- peptide bonds.

When building a model:

- identify atoms first;
- connect atoms according to valency;
- show functional groups clearly;
- compare the model with a structural formula.

## 22.7 Structure Of Alanine

Alanine has a methyl group as its R group.

```text
       H
       |
H2N - C - COOH
       |
      CH3
```

Alanine is an alpha-amino acid.

## 22.8 What Are Gums Made Of? Is Fevicol Different?

Natural gums are usually complex polysaccharides. They are polymeric substances produced by plants or microbes.

Fevicol is different from natural gums. It is a synthetic adhesive mainly based on polyvinyl acetate emulsion, not a natural polysaccharide gum.

## 22.9 Qualitative Tests For Proteins, Fats, Oils, And Amino Acids

Common qualitative tests:

| Substance | Test | Positive Indication |
|---|---|---|
| Proteins | Biuret test | Violet colour |
| Amino acids | Ninhydrin test | Purple or blue colour |
| Fats and oils | Sudan dye test | Red-stained oily layer |
| Fats and oils | Grease spot test | Translucent spot on paper |
| Reducing sugars | Benedict's test | Green, yellow, orange, or red precipitate depending on amount |

Samples such as fruit juice, saliva, sweat, and urine can be tested only with proper lab supervision, clean handling, and safe disposal.

## 22.10 Cellulose Production And Paper Manufacture

This is a data-finding question, so exact values should be checked from updated scientific and industrial reports.

A reasonable biological answer is:

Plants synthesize an enormous amount of cellulose every year because cellulose is the major structural component of plant cell walls. The annual natural production of cellulose is far greater than the amount converted into paper by humans.

Paper manufacture still consumes a very large amount of plant material. This is why recycling paper, reducing waste, and using sustainable forestry matter.

## 22.11 Important Properties Of Enzymes

Important properties of enzymes:

- most enzymes are proteins;
- some RNA molecules called ribozymes are catalytic;
- enzymes are highly specific;
- enzymes have active sites;
- enzymes form enzyme-substrate complexes;
- enzymes lower activation energy;
- enzymes increase reaction rate;
- enzymes are not consumed in the reaction;
- enzymes work best at optimum temperature and pH;
- high temperature may denature enzymes;
- enzyme activity increases with substrate concentration up to Vmax;
- inhibitors can reduce enzyme activity;
- some enzymes require cofactors.

## 23. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 23.1 Why Is Water Important In Living Cells?

Water is the most abundant chemical in living organisms. It acts as a solvent, supports biochemical reactions, and forms the medium for cellular processes.

## 23.2 Why Are Proteins Heteropolymers?

Proteins are made of different kinds of amino acids arranged in specific sequences. Therefore, they are heteropolymers.

## 23.3 Why Is Cellulose A Homopolymer?

Cellulose is made of repeating glucose units only. Therefore, it is a homopolymer.

## 23.4 Why Are Enzymes Sensitive To Temperature And pH?

Enzymes are mostly proteins. Changes in temperature or pH can alter their tertiary structure and active site, reducing activity.

## 23.5 Why Does Reaction Velocity Reach Vmax?

At high substrate concentration, all enzyme active sites become occupied. Once enzymes are saturated, adding more substrate cannot increase reaction rate further.
