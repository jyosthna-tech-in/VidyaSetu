
```text
food energy is not released all at once;
```

## 1. Why Respiration Is Needed

All living organisms need energy.

Energy is needed for:

- absorption;
- transport;
- movement;
- biosynthesis;
- reproduction;
- growth;
- maintenance;
- active transport;
- repair;
- breathing itself.

Food contains chemical energy.

Cells release this energy by oxidising food molecules.

The released energy is captured mainly in ATP.

## 2. Photosynthesis And Respiration Are Connected

Green plants make carbohydrates during photosynthesis.

Photosynthesis traps light energy and stores it in chemical bonds of molecules such as:

- glucose;
- sucrose;
- starch.

Respiration breaks down these molecules to release usable energy.

Thus:

```text
photosynthesis stores energy;
respiration releases usable energy.
```

Both occur in plants.

## 2.1 Why Green Plants Still Need Respiration

Plants make food, but they still need to break it down for ATP.

Not every plant cell photosynthesises.

Only cells with chloroplasts can photosynthesise.

Non-green parts need food supplied to them, such as:

- roots;
- inner stem tissues;
- flowers;
- fruits;
- seeds;
- growing regions without chlorophyll.

These tissues use respiration to obtain ATP.

## 2.2 Food Origin In Living Organisms

Green plants and cyanobacteria produce food by photosynthesis.

Animals obtain food directly or indirectly from plants.

Saprophytes such as many fungi obtain food from dead and decaying matter.

Ultimately, most food oxidised in respiration originates from photosynthesis.

## 3. Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is the breaking of C-C bonds of complex organic compounds through oxidation inside cells, releasing energy that is trapped as ATP.

Respiration is not the same as simple burning.

It is controlled, stepwise, and enzyme-mediated.

## 3.1 Respiratory Substrates

Respiratory substrates are compounds oxidised during respiration.

Common respiratory substrates:

- carbohydrates;
- fats;
- proteins;
- organic acids in some conditions.

Glucose is the favoured respiratory substrate.

## 3.2 Why Energy Is Released Step By Step

If glucose were oxidised completely in one step, most energy would be lost as heat.

Cells cannot efficiently use such sudden heat release.

Instead, glucose is oxidised in many enzyme-controlled steps.

This allows cells to:

- release energy gradually;
- trap energy in ATP;
- avoid heat damage;
- control pathway speed;
- use intermediates for biosynthesis.

## 3.3 Overall Oxidation Of Glucose

Complete oxidation of glucose can be represented as:

```text
C6H12O6 + 6O2 -> 6CO2 + 6H2O + energy
```

This equation is useful, but it hides the pathway details.

Inside cells, this does not happen in one direct step.

## 3.4 Respiration And ATP

ATP acts as the energy currency of the cell.

Respiration releases energy from food and stores part of it in ATP.

ATP is later broken down wherever energy is needed.

Basic idea:

```text
respiratory substrate energy -> ATP -> cellular work
```

## 3.5 Carbon Skeletons From Respiration

Respiration also produces intermediate carbon skeletons.

These intermediates can be used to synthesise:

- amino acids;
- fatty acids;
- other organic molecules.

This is one reason respiration is linked with both breakdown and synthesis.

## 4. Do Plants Breathe?

Plants need oxygen for aerobic respiration.

They release carbon dioxide during respiration.

However, plants do not have specialised breathing organs like lungs or gills.

Gas exchange occurs mainly by diffusion through:

- stomata;
- lenticels;
- general surface of young tissues;
- intercellular air spaces.

## 4.1 Stomata

Stomata are pores mostly present in leaves.

They allow exchange of:

- CO2;
- O2;
- water vapour.

During photosynthesis, leaves exchange large volumes of gases.

During respiration, oxygen enters and carbon dioxide leaves.

## 4.2 Lenticels

Lenticels are openings in woody stems and some other parts.

They allow gases to diffuse between external air and internal tissues.

They are important because thick bark can otherwise reduce gas exchange.

## 4.3 Why Plants Do Not Need Special Respiratory Organs

Plants manage without special respiratory organs for several reasons.

First, each plant part mostly handles its own gas exchange.

There is little long-distance transport of gases from one part to another.

Second, plant respiration rate is generally lower than animal respiration rate.

Roots, stems, and leaves usually demand less gas exchange than active animals.

Third, living plant cells are often close to air spaces or external surfaces.

Fourth, parenchyma cells are loosely packed, creating intercellular spaces.

These spaces form an internal air network.

## 4.4 Gas Exchange In Thick Stems And Roots

In thick woody stems, many inner cells are dead and provide mechanical support.

Living cells are often arranged in thinner layers near or beneath the bark.

Lenticels allow gas diffusion into these tissues.

Roots also exchange gases through the soil air spaces and root surface.

Poorly aerated soil can reduce root respiration.

## 5. Respiration Versus Combustion

| Feature | Respiration | Combustion |
|---|---|---|
| Site | Living cells | Outside living control |
| Control | Enzyme-controlled | Not enzyme-controlled |
| Temperature | Normal cellular temperature | High temperature |
| Energy release | Stepwise | Sudden |
| Energy capture | Partly trapped as ATP | Mostly lost as heat and light |
| Intermediates | Many useful intermediates | No regulated metabolic intermediates |
| Cell safety | Compatible with life | Would damage cells if uncontrolled |

## 6. Glycolysis

Glycolysis means sugar splitting.

The term comes from:

- glycos = sugar;
- lysis = splitting.

Glycolysis is also called the EMP pathway.

EMP stands for:

- Embden;
- Meyerhof;
- Parnas.

## 6.1 Location Of Glycolysis

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm.

It is present in all living organisms.

In anaerobic organisms, glycolysis may be the only energy-yielding respiratory pathway.

## 6.2 Main Outcome Of Glycolysis

One glucose molecule is partially oxidised into two pyruvate molecules.

Simple view:

```text
glucose -> 2 pyruvate
```

Glucose has 6 carbon atoms.

Each pyruvate has 3 carbon atoms.

## 6.3 Glucose Entry In Plants

In plants, glucose may come from:

- sucrose produced by photosynthesis;
- starch stored in tissues.

Sucrose is broken by invertase into:

- glucose;
- fructose.

These monosaccharides can enter glycolysis.

## 6.4 Phosphorylation At The Start

Glucose is phosphorylated by hexokinase.

It becomes glucose-6-phosphate.

This uses ATP.

Fructose can also be converted into glycolytic intermediates.

Glucose-6-phosphate is converted to fructose-6-phosphate.

Then another ATP is used to form fructose-1,6-bisphosphate.

## 6.5 Energy Investment Phase

Two ATP molecules are used early in glycolysis:

1. glucose -> glucose-6-phosphate;
2. fructose-6-phosphate -> fructose-1,6-bisphosphate.

This is called the energy investment phase.

The cell spends ATP to prepare glucose for later energy extraction.

## 6.6 Splitting Of Fructose-1,6-Bisphosphate

Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate is split into two three-carbon molecules:

- dihydroxyacetone phosphate;
- glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, also called PGAL.

Only PGAL continues directly in glycolysis.

Dihydroxyacetone phosphate is converted into PGAL.

Therefore one glucose gives two PGAL molecules.

## 6.7 Oxidation Of PGAL

PGAL is oxidised to 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate.

During this step:

- NAD+ is reduced to NADH + H+;
- inorganic phosphate is added;
- energy is conserved in a high-energy intermediate.

Because one glucose gives two PGAL molecules:

```text
2 NADH + 2H+ are formed per glucose
```

## 6.8 ATP Formation In Glycolysis

ATP is formed at two substrate-level phosphorylation steps.

First:

```text
1,3-bisphosphoglycerate -> 3-phosphoglycerate
```

Second:

```text
phosphoenolpyruvate -> pyruvate
```

Each step occurs twice per glucose because there are two three-carbon molecules.

Therefore:

- 4 ATP are produced gross;
- 2 ATP were used;
- net gain is 2 ATP.

## 6.9 Net Products Of Glycolysis

Per glucose:

| Product | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Pyruvate | 2 |
| ATP used | 2 |
| ATP formed | 4 |
| Net ATP | 2 |
| NADH | 2 |

Net summary:

```text
glucose -> 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP net + 2 NADH
```

## 6.10 Schematic Flow Of Glycolysis

```text
Glucose
-> glucose-6-phosphate
-> fructose-6-phosphate
-> fructose-1,6-bisphosphate
-> PGAL and DHAP
-> 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate
-> 3-phosphoglycerate
-> 2-phosphoglycerate
-> phosphoenolpyruvate
-> pyruvate
```

## 6.11 Why Glycolysis Is Partial Oxidation

Glycolysis does not fully oxidise glucose to CO2 and water.

It only converts glucose to pyruvate.

Much energy remains in pyruvate.

Complete energy extraction requires aerobic respiration.

## 7. Fate Of Pyruvate

Pyruvate is the key product of glycolysis.

Its fate depends on:

- oxygen availability;
- organism type;
- cell condition;
- energy need.

Three major fates are:

1. alcoholic fermentation;
2. lactic acid fermentation;
3. aerobic respiration.

## 8. Fermentation

Fermentation is incomplete oxidation of glucose under anaerobic conditions.

It occurs in many:

- prokaryotes;
- unicellular eukaryotes;
- some plant tissues under low oxygen;
- animal muscles during oxygen shortage.

Fermentation does not require oxygen.

It allows glycolysis to continue by regenerating NAD+.

## 8.1 Why NAD+ Regeneration Matters

Glycolysis requires NAD+ during PGAL oxidation.

If NAD+ is not regenerated, glycolysis stops.

Fermentation converts NADH back to NAD+.

This allows continued ATP production by glycolysis.

## 8.2 Alcoholic Fermentation

Yeast commonly performs alcoholic fermentation.

Steps:

```text
pyruvate -> acetaldehyde + CO2
acetaldehyde + NADH + H+ -> ethanol + NAD+
```

Enzymes involved:

- pyruvate decarboxylase;
- alcohol dehydrogenase.

Final products:

- ethanol;
- carbon dioxide;
- NAD+ regenerated.

## 8.3 Lactic Acid Fermentation

Some bacteria and animal muscles can perform lactic acid fermentation.

Step:

```text
pyruvate + NADH + H+ -> lactic acid + NAD+
```

Enzyme:

```text
lactate dehydrogenase
```

No CO2 is released in lactic acid fermentation from pyruvate.

## 8.4 Net ATP In Fermentation

Fermentation itself does not add extra ATP beyond glycolysis.

The ATP gain comes from glycolysis.

Per glucose:

```text
net ATP = 2
```

This is much lower than aerobic respiration.

## 8.5 Why Fermentation Is Inefficient

Fermentation releases less than seven percent of the energy stored in glucose.

Most energy remains in ethanol or lactic acid.

It is also potentially harmful because:

- alcohol can poison cells;
- acid can disturb pH.

Yeast can die when alcohol concentration becomes high, around 13 percent in the raw text.

Alcoholic beverages with higher alcohol content require additional processing such as distillation or fortification.

## 9. Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration is complete oxidation of organic substrates in the presence of oxygen.

It produces:

- CO2;
- H2O;
- much more ATP than fermentation.

Aerobic respiration is common in higher organisms.

In eukaryotes, it occurs mainly in mitochondria after glycolysis.

## 9.1 Main Events Of Aerobic Respiration

After glycolysis:

1. pyruvate enters the mitochondrion;
2. pyruvate is converted to acetyl CoA;
3. acetyl CoA enters Krebs cycle;
4. NADH and FADH2 carry electrons to ETS;
5. oxygen accepts electrons and hydrogen;
6. ATP is formed by oxidative phosphorylation.

## 9.2 Location Of Main Events

| Event | Location |
|---|---|
| Glycolysis | Cytoplasm |
| Pyruvate transport | From cytoplasm to mitochondrial matrix |
| Pyruvate oxidation | Mitochondrial matrix |
| Krebs cycle | Mitochondrial matrix |
| ETS | Inner mitochondrial membrane |
| Proton accumulation | Intermembrane space |
| ATP synthesis | Inner mitochondrial membrane, matrix side catalytic head |

## 10. Pyruvate Oxidation

Pyruvate from glycolysis enters the mitochondrial matrix.

There it undergoes oxidative decarboxylation.

The enzyme complex is pyruvate dehydrogenase.

It requires coenzymes including:

- NAD+;
- coenzyme A;
- metal ion cofactors such as Mg2+ in the raw treatment.

## 10.1 Reaction Idea

For one pyruvate:

```text
pyruvate + CoA + NAD+ -> acetyl CoA + CO2 + NADH + H+
```

For one glucose, there are two pyruvate molecules.

Therefore per glucose:

```text
2 pyruvate -> 2 acetyl CoA + 2 CO2 + 2 NADH
```

## 10.2 Why This Step Is Important

Pyruvate oxidation links glycolysis with Krebs cycle.

It converts a 3-carbon pyruvate into a 2-carbon acetyl group.

The lost carbon leaves as CO2.

The acetyl group enters Krebs cycle attached to CoA.

## 11. Krebs Cycle

Krebs cycle is also called:

- citric acid cycle;
- tricarboxylic acid cycle;
- TCA cycle.

It occurs in the mitochondrial matrix.

It was elucidated by Hans Krebs.

## 11.1 Entry Of Acetyl CoA

The cycle begins when acetyl CoA combines with oxaloacetic acid.

Oxaloacetic acid is a 4-carbon compound.

Acetyl group has 2 carbons.

The first product is citrate, a 6-carbon compound.

Reaction idea:

```text
acetyl CoA + OAA + H2O -> citrate + CoA
```

Enzyme:

```text
citrate synthase
```

## 11.2 Why The Cycle Is Cyclic

OAA is used at the start.

OAA is regenerated at the end.

Therefore the pathway can continue as long as:

- acetyl CoA enters;
- NAD+ and FAD are regenerated;
- enzymes remain active.

## 11.3 Major Steps Of Krebs Cycle

Simplified flow:

```text
acetyl CoA + OAA
-> citrate
-> isocitrate
-> alpha-ketoglutarate
-> succinyl CoA
-> succinate
-> fumarate
-> malate
-> OAA
```

## 11.4 Decarboxylation Steps

Two CO2 molecules are released per acetyl CoA.

They are released during conversions leading to:

- alpha-ketoglutarate;
- succinyl CoA.

This completes oxidation of the acetyl carbon skeleton.

## 11.5 Reduced Coenzymes Formed

Per acetyl CoA, Krebs cycle forms:

- 3 NADH;
- 1 FADH2.

These reduced coenzymes carry electrons to ETS.

## 11.6 ATP Or GTP Formation

During conversion of succinyl CoA to succinate, GTP is formed.

This is substrate-level phosphorylation.

GTP can be converted to ATP.

So per acetyl CoA:

```text
1 ATP equivalent is formed
```

## 11.7 Products Per Acetyl CoA

| Product | Amount Per Acetyl CoA |
|---|---:|
| CO2 | 2 |
| NADH | 3 |
| FADH2 | 1 |
| ATP or GTP | 1 |
| OAA | Regenerated |

## 11.8 Products Per Glucose From Krebs Cycle

One glucose gives two acetyl CoA.

Therefore Krebs cycle per glucose gives:

| Product | Amount Per Glucose |
|---|---:|
| CO2 | 4 |
| NADH | 6 |
| FADH2 | 2 |
| ATP or GTP | 2 |

## 11.9 Pyruvate Oxidation Plus Krebs Cycle Per Glucose

When pyruvate oxidation is included, per glucose:

| Product | Amount |
|---|---:|
| CO2 | 6 |
| NADH | 8 |
| FADH2 | 2 |
| ATP or GTP | 2 |

These 8 NADH include:

- 2 from pyruvate oxidation;
- 6 from Krebs cycle.

## 12. Why Oxygen Has Not Appeared Earlier

Glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and Krebs cycle generate reduced coenzymes.

They do not directly use oxygen in each individual step.

However, they depend on NAD+ and FAD being regenerated.

The electron transport system regenerates these coenzymes using oxygen as final acceptor.

Without oxygen, ETS stops.

Then NADH and FADH2 cannot be efficiently oxidised.

Then aerobic respiration stops.

## 13. Electron Transport System

The electron transport system is a chain of electron carriers in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

It transfers electrons from NADH and FADH2 to oxygen.

As electrons move, energy is used to build a proton gradient.

This gradient drives ATP synthesis.

## 13.1 Main Electron Donors

Electron donors:

- NADH + H+;
- FADH2.

NADH usually donates electrons to complex I.

FADH2 donates through complex II.

## 13.2 Main Electron Carriers

Important components include:

- complex I, NADH dehydrogenase;
- complex II, linked with FADH2 entry;
- ubiquinone;
- complex III, cytochrome bc1 complex;
- cytochrome c;
- complex IV, cytochrome c oxidase;
- complex V, ATP synthase.

## 13.3 Electron Flow From NADH

Simplified sequence:

```text
NADH -> complex I -> ubiquinone -> complex III -> cytochrome c -> complex IV -> O2
```

Oxygen combines with electrons and protons to form water.

## 13.4 Electron Flow From FADH2

Simplified sequence:

```text
FADH2 -> complex II -> ubiquinone -> complex III -> cytochrome c -> complex IV -> O2
```

FADH2 enters later than NADH.

Therefore it generally produces less ATP.

## 13.5 Oxygen As Final Acceptor

Oxygen is the final electron and hydrogen acceptor in aerobic respiration.

It is reduced to water.

This terminal role is vital.

Even though oxygen appears at the end, it drives the whole process by removing electrons and hydrogen from the system.

## 14. Oxidative Phosphorylation

Oxidative phosphorylation is ATP synthesis coupled with electron transport and oxidation-reduction reactions.

It differs from photophosphorylation.

| Feature | Photophosphorylation | Oxidative Phosphorylation |
|---|---|---|
| Energy input | Light energy | Energy from oxidation-reduction reactions |
| Organelle in eukaryotes | Chloroplast | Mitochondrion |
| Membrane | Thylakoid membrane | Inner mitochondrial membrane |
| Final electron acceptor | NADP+ in non-cyclic flow | O2 |
| Product emphasis | ATP and NADPH | ATP and water |

## 14.1 Proton Gradient In Mitochondria

As electrons move through ETS, protons are pumped from the matrix to the intermembrane space.

This creates:

- high H+ concentration in intermembrane space;
- lower H+ concentration in matrix;
- electrochemical proton gradient.

Protons flow back to the matrix through ATP synthase.

## 14.2 ATP Synthase Structure

Mitochondrial ATP synthase is complex V.

It has two major components:

| Part | Location | Role |
|---|---|---|
| F0 | Integral membrane part | Proton channel |
| F1 | Peripheral headpiece | ATP synthesis catalytic site |

The F1 headpiece faces the mitochondrial matrix.

As protons pass through F0, F1 catalyses:

```text
ADP + Pi -> ATP
```

## 14.3 Proton Requirement

The raw chapter states that for each ATP produced:

```text
4H+ pass through F0 from intermembrane space to matrix
```

This proton movement down the electrochemical gradient powers ATP synthesis.

## 14.4 ATP Yield From NADH And FADH2

In the textbook treatment:

| Reduced Coenzyme | ATP Yield |
|---|---:|
| 1 NADH | 3 ATP |
| 1 FADH2 | 2 ATP |

This convention is used in the chapter balance sheet.

## 15. Respiratory Balance Sheet

The respiratory balance sheet estimates ATP gain from complete oxidation of one glucose molecule.

The chapter uses the classical value:

```text
38 ATP per glucose
```

This is a theoretical calculation.

## 15.1 ATP Accounting

Using the chapter convention:

| Stage | Direct ATP | Reduced Coenzymes | ATP From Coenzymes |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Glycolysis | 2 net | 2 NADH | 6 |
| Pyruvate oxidation | 0 | 2 NADH | 6 |
| Krebs cycle | 2 | 6 NADH + 2 FADH2 | 18 + 4 |
| Total | 4 direct | 10 NADH + 2 FADH2 | 34 |

Total:

```text
4 direct ATP + 34 ATP from ETS = 38 ATP
```

## 15.2 Why The Balance Sheet Is Theoretical

The ATP calculation depends on assumptions.

The raw chapter lists important assumptions.

## 15.2.1 Assumption Of Orderly Sequence

It assumes pathways occur in a neat sequence:

```text
glycolysis -> Krebs cycle -> ETS
```

In living cells, many pathways operate simultaneously.

## 15.2.2 Assumption That Glycolytic NADH Enters Mitochondria

It assumes NADH made in glycolysis is transferred into mitochondria and undergoes oxidative phosphorylation.

In real cells, transfer systems can vary.

## 15.2.3 Assumption That No Intermediates Are Withdrawn

It assumes intermediates are not used for making other compounds.

In living cells, intermediates are often withdrawn for biosynthesis.

## 15.2.4 Assumption That Only Glucose Is Respired

It assumes glucose is the only substrate.

In reality, fats, proteins, and organic acids may also enter the respiratory pathway.

## 15.2.5 Assumption That ATP Is Not Used During The Process

Cells use ATP continuously.

Therefore measured ATP yield in living systems may differ from ideal calculation.

## 15.3 Why The Calculation Is Still Useful

The balance sheet helps learners understand:

- respiration is efficient;
- aerobic respiration yields much more ATP than fermentation;
- reduced coenzymes store most extractable energy before ETS;
- oxygen is vital for full ATP yield.

## 20. Respiratory Pathway As Amphibolic

Respiration is often called a catabolic process because it breaks down substrates.

But this is incomplete.

Respiratory intermediates are also used for synthesis.

Therefore the respiratory pathway is amphibolic.

Amphibolic means:

```text
both catabolic and anabolic
```

## 20.1 Catabolic Role

Respiration breaks down:

- glucose;
- fats;
- proteins;
- organic acids.

This releases energy and forms ATP.

## 20.2 Anabolic Role

Respiratory intermediates can be withdrawn to make other molecules.

Examples:

- acetyl CoA can be used to synthesise fatty acids;
- Krebs cycle intermediates can help form amino acids;
- PGAL can connect carbohydrate metabolism with other pathways.

## 20.3 Entry Of Fats

Fats are first broken into:

- glycerol;
- fatty acids.

Glycerol can enter after conversion to PGAL.

Fatty acids are degraded to acetyl CoA.

Acetyl CoA then enters Krebs cycle.

## 20.4 Entry Of Proteins

Proteins are broken down by proteases into amino acids.

Amino acids undergo deamination.

Depending on structure, they can enter respiration as:

- pyruvate;
- acetyl CoA;
- Krebs cycle intermediates.

## 20.5 Why Amphibolic Nature Matters

The same pathway can:

- break molecules for energy;
- provide building blocks for synthesis.

Thus respiration is central metabolism, not merely energy extraction.

## 21. Respiratory Quotient

Respiratory quotient is the ratio of volume of CO2 evolved to volume of O2 consumed during respiration.

Formula:

```text
RQ = volume of CO2 evolved / volume of O2 consumed
```

RQ depends on the respiratory substrate.

## 21.1 RQ For Carbohydrates

For glucose:

```text
C6H12O6 + 6O2 -> 6CO2 + 6H2O + energy
```

Therefore:

```text
RQ = 6CO2 / 6O2 = 1.0
```

Carbohydrate RQ is usually 1 when completely oxidised.

## 21.2 RQ For Fats

Fats require more oxygen for complete oxidation.

Therefore their RQ is less than 1.

For tripalmitin in the raw chapter:

```text
2(C51H98O6) + 145O2 -> 102CO2 + 98H2O + energy
```

So:

```text
RQ = 102 / 145 = 0.72
```

Thus fat RQ is about 0.7.

## 21.3 RQ For Proteins

When proteins are respiratory substrates, RQ is about 0.9.

In living organisms, pure protein or pure fat is rarely used alone.

Usually a mixture of substrates is respired.

## 21.4 Why RQ Is Useful

RQ gives clues about the respiratory substrate being used.

| RQ Value | Likely Substrate |
|---|---|
| 1.0 | Carbohydrate |
| Less than 1 | Fat or protein-rich substrate |
| About 0.7 | Fat |
| About 0.9 | Protein |

## 22. Carbon Tracking Through Respiration

Start:

```text
glucose has 6 carbons
```

Glycolysis:

```text
glucose -> 2 pyruvate
```

Each pyruvate has 3 carbons.

Pyruvate oxidation:

```text
2 pyruvate -> 2 acetyl CoA + 2 CO2
```

Each acetyl CoA has 2 carbons.

Krebs cycle:

```text
2 acetyl CoA -> 4 CO2
```

Total per glucose:

```text
6 carbons leave as 6 CO2
```

## 23. Electron Tracking Through Respiration

Electrons removed during oxidation are first stored in reduced coenzymes.

Main reduced coenzymes:

- NADH;
- FADH2.

They donate electrons to ETS.

ETS transfers electrons to oxygen.

Oxygen becomes water.

Flow:

```text
food substrate -> NADH and FADH2 -> ETS -> O2 -> H2O
```

## 24. ATP Tracking Through Respiration

ATP is produced in three main ways in this chapter.

## 24.1 Direct ATP In Glycolysis

Net:

```text
2 ATP per glucose
```

## 24.2 Direct ATP Equivalent In Krebs Cycle

Per glucose:

```text
2 ATP or GTP equivalent
```

## 24.3 ATP From Oxidative Phosphorylation

Using textbook convention:

```text
10 NADH x 3 ATP = 30 ATP
2 FADH2 x 2 ATP = 4 ATP
```

Total from ETS:

```text
34 ATP
```

Grand total:

```text
2 + 2 + 34 = 38 ATP
```

## 25. Stepwise Release Of Energy

Stepwise release is significant because:

- it prevents sudden heat loss;
- it allows ATP synthesis;
- it permits enzymatic control;
- it allows pathway regulation;
- it produces useful intermediates;
- it protects living cells from damage.

This is why respiration is biologically useful whereas uncontrolled combustion is not.

## 26. Common Misconceptions

## 26.1 Misconception: Plants Do Not Respire

Correct idea:

Plants respire continuously.

Photosynthesis makes food; respiration releases ATP from food.

## 26.2 Misconception: Photosynthesis Replaces Respiration

Correct idea:

Photosynthesis and respiration serve different roles.

Photosynthesis stores energy.

Respiration releases usable energy.

## 26.3 Misconception: Oxygen Is Needed For Glycolysis

Correct idea:

Glycolysis does not directly require oxygen.

It occurs in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions.

## 26.4 Misconception: Fermentation Produces A Lot Of ATP

Correct idea:

Fermentation yields only 2 ATP net per glucose, through glycolysis.

## 26.5 Misconception: Krebs Cycle Directly Uses Oxygen

Correct idea:

Krebs cycle does not directly use oxygen in its reactions.

But it depends on oxygen indirectly because ETS needs oxygen to regenerate NAD+ and FAD.

## 26.6 Misconception: The 38 ATP Value Is Always Exact

Correct idea:

The 38 ATP value is theoretical under textbook assumptions.

Living cells may show different actual yields.

## 26.7 Misconception: Respiration Is Only Catabolic

Correct idea:

Respiration is amphibolic because it participates in both breakdown and synthesis.

## 27. Solved Concept Checks

## 27.1 Why Is Breathing Connected To Energy Release?

Breathing supplies oxygen and removes carbon dioxide.

Oxygen is needed as the final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration.

Aerobic respiration releases energy from food and stores it as ATP.

## 27.2 Why Do Non-Green Plant Parts Need Food?

Non-green parts cannot photosynthesise because they lack functional chloroplasts.

They still need ATP for living processes.

Therefore food must be transported to them and oxidised in respiration.

## 27.3 Why Do Plants Lack Special Respiratory Organs?

Plant cells usually have lower gas exchange demands than animal cells.

Most living cells are near surfaces or air spaces.

Each part can exchange gases locally through stomata, lenticels, and diffusion.

## 27.4 Why Is ATP Called Energy Currency?

ATP can store energy in its phosphate bonds and release it when hydrolysed.

Cells use ATP to power many processes.

## 27.5 What Is The Net ATP Yield Of Glycolysis?

Glycolysis forms 4 ATP and uses 2 ATP.

Net gain:

```text
2 ATP per glucose
```

## 27.6 How Many NADH Are Formed In Glycolysis?

Two NADH are formed per glucose.

This happens because one glucose forms two PGAL molecules, and each PGAL oxidation reduces one NAD+.

## 27.7 What Are The Three Fates Of Pyruvate?

Pyruvate can enter:

- alcoholic fermentation;
- lactic acid fermentation;
- aerobic respiration through acetyl CoA.

## 27.8 Why Is Fermentation Anaerobic?

Fermentation occurs without oxygen.

It regenerates NAD+ from NADH so glycolysis can continue.

## 27.9 Why Is Fermentation Less Efficient Than Aerobic Respiration?

Fermentation only partially breaks down glucose.

Much energy remains in ethanol or lactic acid.

It yields only 2 ATP net per glucose.

## 27.10 What Happens To Pyruvate Before Krebs Cycle?

Pyruvate enters the mitochondrial matrix.

It is converted to acetyl CoA by pyruvate dehydrogenase.

CO2 and NADH are formed.

## 27.11 Why Is Krebs Cycle Called A Cycle?

OAA combines with acetyl CoA at the start.

OAA is regenerated at the end.

Thus the pathway can repeat.

## 27.12 What Is The Role Of NADH And FADH2?

They carry electrons and hydrogen from oxidation reactions to ETS.

Their oxidation in ETS helps produce ATP.

## 27.13 Why Is Oxygen Vital In Aerobic Respiration?

Oxygen accepts electrons and hydrogen at the end of ETS.

This forms water and keeps electron flow going.

Without oxygen, ETS stops and aerobic respiration cannot continue.

## 27.14 Why Does FADH2 Produce Less ATP Than NADH?

FADH2 donates electrons through complex II, entering ETS later than NADH.

Less proton pumping occurs, so less ATP is formed.

## 27.15 Why Is Respiration Amphibolic?

Respiration breaks down substrates to release energy.

It also provides intermediates for synthesising fats, amino acids, and other molecules.

Therefore it is both catabolic and anabolic.

## 27.16 Why Is RQ Of Fat Less Than One?

Fats contain less oxygen relative to carbon and hydrogen.

They require more oxygen for complete oxidation.

Thus O2 consumed is greater than CO2 evolved.

RQ becomes less than 1.

## 28.1 What Are Respiratory Substrates?

## 28.2 Main Steps In Aerobic Respiration

Main steps:

## 28.5 Explain ETS

ETS is a system of electron carriers in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

It receives electrons from NADH and FADH2.

Electrons move through complexes and carriers to oxygen.

Oxygen is reduced to water.

Energy released during electron transport builds a proton gradient.

The gradient powers ATP synthase to form ATP.

## 28.6 What Is Oxidative Phosphorylation?

Oxidative phosphorylation is ATP synthesis using energy released during oxidation of NADH and FADH2 through ETS.

It occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor.

## 28.7 Assumptions In ATP Balance Sheet

The ATP balance sheet assumes:

- pathways occur sequentially and orderly;
- NADH from glycolysis enters mitochondria and undergoes oxidative phosphorylation;
- no intermediates are withdrawn for biosynthesis;
- only glucose is respired;
- substrates do not enter midway;
- ATP is not being simultaneously consumed;
- all reduced coenzymes give ideal ATP yields.

## 28.8 Why Is The Respiratory Pathway Amphibolic?

It is amphibolic because it is involved in both catabolism and anabolism.

It breaks down glucose, fats, and proteins to release energy.

It also supplies intermediates such as acetyl CoA and Krebs cycle acids for synthesis of other molecules.
