## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is about two linked jobs:

```text
remove wastes;
maintain internal balance.
```

Do not study the kidney as only a waste-removal organ.

The kidney also regulates:

- water balance;
- salt balance;
- pH;
- blood volume;
- blood pressure;
- osmolarity.

The most important pathway is:

```text
blood -> glomerular filtrate -> tubular fluid -> urine
```

Track every substance by asking:

1. Is it filtered?
2. Is it reabsorbed?
3. Is it secreted?
4. Does it finally leave in urine?

## 1. Need For Excretion

Animals produce many waste substances through metabolism.

They may also accumulate extra substances through food and water intake.

Important substances that must be removed or regulated include:

- ammonia;
- urea;
- uric acid;
- carbon dioxide;
- excess water;
- excess ions;
- Na+;
- K+;
- Cl-;
- phosphate;
- sulphate.

If these substances accumulate beyond safe limits, body functions are disturbed.

## 2. Nitrogenous Wastes

Nitrogenous wastes arise mainly from breakdown of:

- amino acids;
- proteins;
- nucleic acids;
- nitrogen-containing compounds.

The three major nitrogenous wastes are:

- ammonia;
- urea;
- uric acid.

## 3. Ammonia

Ammonia is the most toxic of the three major nitrogenous wastes.

It is highly soluble in water.

It requires a large amount of water for elimination.

Animals that excrete ammonia are called ammonotelic.

## 3.1 Ammonotelic Animals

Examples:

- many bony fishes;
- aquatic amphibians;
- aquatic insects.

Ammonia is often excreted by diffusion:

- across body surface;
- across gill surface in fishes.

It may be excreted as ammonium ions.

Kidneys do not play a major role in ammonia removal in many ammonotelic animals.

## 4. Urea

Urea is less toxic than ammonia.

It requires less water for excretion than ammonia.

Animals that excrete urea are called ureotelic.

## 4.1 Ureotelic Animals

Examples:

- mammals;
- many terrestrial amphibians;
- marine fishes.

In ureotelic animals:

- ammonia formed by metabolism is converted to urea in liver;
- urea enters blood;
- kidneys filter and excrete urea.

Some urea may be retained in the kidney matrix of some animals to help maintain osmolarity.

## 5. Uric Acid

Uric acid is the least toxic of the three major nitrogenous wastes.

It can be removed with minimal water loss.

It is often excreted as a paste or pellet.

Animals that excrete uric acid are called uricotelic.

## 5.1 Uricotelic Animals

Examples:

- reptiles;
- birds;
- land snails;
- insects.

Uricotelism is useful for water conservation.

It is also useful in shelled eggs because uric acid can be stored with low toxicity.

## 6. Comparison Of Nitrogenous Waste Types

| Feature | Ammonia | Urea | Uric Acid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Toxicity | Highest | Moderate | Lowest |
| Water need | Very high | Moderate | Very low |
| Excretion type | Ammonotelism | Ureotelism | Uricotelism |
| Common habitat link | Aquatic | Terrestrial or marine | Dry terrestrial, egg-laying |
| Examples | Bony fishes, aquatic amphibians | Mammals, many terrestrial amphibians | Birds, reptiles, insects |

## 7. Excretory Structures In Animals

Animal groups show different excretory structures.

The structures may remove nitrogenous wastes and also regulate water and ions.

## 7.1 Protonephridia

Protonephridia are also called flame cells in many contexts.

They occur in:

- Platyhelminthes such as Planaria;
- rotifers;
- some annelids;
- cephalochordate Amphioxus.

Main role:

- osmoregulation;
- ionic and fluid volume regulation;
- some excretory function.

## 7.2 Nephridia

Nephridia occur in earthworms and other annelids.

Functions:

- remove nitrogenous wastes;
- maintain fluid balance;
- maintain ionic balance.

## 7.3 Malpighian Tubules

Malpighian tubules are found in most insects, including cockroaches.

Functions:

- nitrogenous waste removal;
- osmoregulation.

They are adapted to water conservation.

## 7.4 Antennal Or Green Glands

Antennal glands, also called green glands, are excretory structures in crustaceans such as prawns.

## 8. Human Excretory System

The human excretory system consists of:

- two kidneys;
- two ureters;
- urinary bladder;
- urethra.

## 8.1 Kidneys

Kidneys are:

- reddish brown;
- bean-shaped;
- located near the dorsal inner wall of abdominal cavity;
- placed between last thoracic and third lumbar vertebrae.

Each adult human kidney is about:

- 10 to 12 cm long;
- 5 to 7 cm wide;
- 2 to 3 cm thick;
- 120 to 170 g in weight.

## 8.2 Hilum And Renal Pelvis

The inner concave surface of the kidney has a notch called hilum.

Through the hilum pass:

- ureter;
- blood vessels;
- nerves.

Inner to hilum is the renal pelvis.

Renal pelvis is a broad funnel-shaped space with projections called calyces.

## 8.3 Kidney Zones

The kidney has:

- outer tough capsule;
- outer cortex;
- inner medulla.

The medulla is divided into conical medullary pyramids.

The pyramids project into calyces.

The cortex extends between medullary pyramids as renal columns.

These are called columns of Bertini.

## 9. Nephron

Each kidney contains about one million nephrons.

Nephron is the functional unit of kidney.

Each nephron has:

- glomerulus;
- renal tubule.

## 9.1 Glomerulus

Glomerulus is a tuft of capillaries.

It is formed by the afferent arteriole, a branch of renal artery.

Blood leaves the glomerulus through efferent arteriole.

## 9.2 Bowman Capsule

Bowman capsule is a double-walled cup-like structure.

It encloses the glomerulus.

Glomerulus plus Bowman capsule forms:

```text
renal corpuscle or Malpighian body
```

## 9.3 Renal Tubule Parts

The renal tubule begins from Bowman capsule and continues as:

```text
PCT -> loop of Henle -> DCT -> collecting duct
```

PCT means proximal convoluted tubule.

DCT means distal convoluted tubule.

## 9.4 Loop Of Henle

The loop of Henle is hairpin-shaped.

It has:

- descending limb;
- ascending limb.

It dips into the medulla.

It plays a major role in urine concentration.

## 9.5 Collecting Duct

DCTs of many nephrons open into collecting ducts.

Many collecting ducts converge and open into renal pelvis through medullary pyramids in calyces.

## 10. Cortical And Juxtamedullary Nephrons

## 10.1 Cortical Nephrons

Most nephrons are cortical nephrons.

They have short loops of Henle that extend only a little into medulla.

Vasa recta is absent or highly reduced in them.

## 10.2 Juxtamedullary Nephrons

Some nephrons are juxtamedullary nephrons.

They have long loops of Henle that run deep into medulla.

They are important in concentrating urine.

## 11. Blood Supply Around Nephron

The efferent arteriole from glomerulus forms peritubular capillaries around renal tubules.

A minute vessel runs parallel to loop of Henle and forms vasa recta.

Vasa recta is U-shaped.

It is important in counter-current exchange.

## 12. Urine Formation

Urine formation has three major processes:

1. glomerular filtration;
2. reabsorption;
3. secretion.

These occur in different parts of nephron.

## 13. Glomerular Filtration

Glomerular filtration is the first step in urine formation.

It occurs in the renal corpuscle.

Blood pressure in glomerular capillaries forces fluid from blood into Bowman capsule.

## 13.1 Filtration Layers

Blood is filtered through three layers:

1. endothelium of glomerular blood vessels;
2. basement membrane;
3. epithelium of Bowman capsule.

## 13.2 Podocytes And Slit Pores

The epithelial cells of Bowman capsule are called podocytes.

Podocytes leave minute spaces called filtration slits or slit pores.

These allow fine filtration.

## 13.3 Ultrafiltration

Almost all plasma constituents except proteins pass into Bowman capsule.

Because filtration is very fine, it is called ultrafiltration.

Proteins and formed elements normally remain in blood.

## 13.4 Blood Filtered Per Minute

On average, kidneys filter:

```text
1100 to 1200 mL blood per minute
```

This is roughly one-fifth of blood pumped by each ventricle per minute.

## 14. Glomerular Filtration Rate

Glomerular filtration rate is the amount of filtrate formed by kidneys per minute.

In a healthy individual:

```text
GFR = about 125 mL per minute
```

Per day:

```text
125 mL/min x 1440 min/day = 180000 mL/day = 180 L/day
```

Only about 1 to 1.5 L urine is excreted daily.

Therefore nearly 99 percent of filtrate is reabsorbed.

## 15. Juxta Glomerular Apparatus

The juxta glomerular apparatus, or JGA, regulates GFR.

It is formed by cellular modifications at the contact region between:

- distal convoluted tubule;
- afferent arteriole.

If GFR falls, JG cells release renin.

Renin helps restore glomerular blood flow and GFR.

## 16. Reabsorption

Reabsorption is movement of useful substances from filtrate back into blood.

Nearly 99 percent of filtrate is reabsorbed.

Reabsorption may be:

- active;
- passive.

## 16.1 Actively Reabsorbed Substances

Examples:

- glucose;
- amino acids;
- Na+.

## 16.2 Passively Reabsorbed Substances

Examples:

- water in many segments;
- some nitrogenous wastes.

Passive movement depends on concentration or osmotic gradients.

## 17. Secretion

Secretion is movement of substances from blood or tubule cells into filtrate.

Important secreted substances:

- H+;
- K+;
- ammonia.

Secretion helps maintain:

- ionic balance;
- acid-base balance;
- blood pH.

## 18. Function Of PCT

PCT is lined by simple cuboidal brush border epithelium.

Brush border increases surface area for reabsorption.

PCT reabsorbs:

- nearly all essential nutrients;
- 70 to 80 percent of electrolytes;
- 70 to 80 percent of water.

PCT also helps maintain pH and ionic balance.

It secretes:

- H+;
- ammonia.

It reabsorbs:

- HCO3-.

## 19. Function Of Loop Of Henle

Loop of Henle is critical for medullary osmotic gradient and urine concentration.

## 19.1 Descending Limb

Descending limb is permeable to water.

It is almost impermeable to electrolytes.

Water leaves the filtrate.

Filtrate becomes concentrated as it moves down.

## 19.2 Ascending Limb

Ascending limb is impermeable to water.

It allows transport of electrolytes, actively or passively.

As filtrate moves upward, electrolytes leave.

Filtrate becomes diluted.

## 19.3 Core Contrast

```text
descending limb: water leaves, filtrate concentrates
ascending limb: salts leave, filtrate dilutes
```

## 20. Function Of DCT

DCT performs conditional reabsorption of:

- Na+;
- water.

It also reabsorbs:

- HCO3-.

It secretes:

- H+;
- K+;
- NH3.

DCT helps maintain:

- pH;
- sodium-potassium balance;
- ionic balance.

## 21. Function Of Collecting Duct

Collecting duct extends from cortex to inner medulla.

It can reabsorb large amounts of water.

This helps produce concentrated urine.

It allows passage of small amounts of urea into medullary interstitium.

This helps maintain medullary osmolarity.

It also secretes H+ and K+ ions and helps maintain pH and ionic balance.

## 22. Segment Summary

| Segment | Major Functions |
|---|---|
| PCT | Reabsorbs nutrients, most water and electrolytes; secretes H+ and ammonia |
| Descending limb | Water leaves; filtrate becomes concentrated |
| Ascending limb | Electrolytes leave; water does not; filtrate becomes diluted |
| DCT | Conditional Na+ and water reabsorption; pH and ion regulation |
| Collecting duct | Water reabsorption, urea recycling, final urine concentration |

## 23. Concentration Of Filtrate

Mammals can produce concentrated urine.

This conserves water.

Important structures:

- loop of Henle;
- vasa recta;
- collecting duct;
- medullary interstitium.

## 24. Counter-Current Mechanism

Counter-current means fluids flow in opposite directions in nearby limbs.

In kidney:

- filtrate flows in opposite directions in descending and ascending limbs of Henle loop;
- blood flows in opposite directions in descending and ascending limbs of vasa recta.

The close arrangement of Henle loop and vasa recta helps maintain a medullary osmotic gradient.

## 24.1 Medullary Osmotic Gradient

Osmolarity increases from cortex to inner medulla.

Approximate values:

```text
cortex: about 300 mOsmol/L
inner medulla: about 1200 mOsmol/L
```

This gradient is mainly caused by:

- NaCl;
- urea.

## 24.2 Role Of NaCl

NaCl is transported out by ascending limb of Henle loop.

It contributes to high osmolarity in medullary interstitium.

NaCl is exchanged with vasa recta and returned to interstitium, helping preserve the gradient.

## 24.3 Role Of Urea

Small amounts of urea enter the thin segment of ascending limb of Henle loop.

Urea is also transported back to interstitium from collecting tubule.

This urea recycling helps maintain medullary osmolarity.

## 24.4 Role Of Vasa Recta

Vasa recta runs parallel to loop of Henle.

Its counter-current blood flow prevents washout of the medullary gradient.

It allows exchange while preserving high osmolarity in inner medulla.

## 24.5 How Concentrated Urine Forms

The medullary interstitium becomes hyperosmotic.

As filtrate passes through collecting duct, water moves out into the hyperosmotic medulla.

This concentrates the urine.

Human kidneys can produce urine nearly four times more concentrated than initial filtrate.

## 25. Regulation Of Kidney Function

Kidney function is regulated by hormonal feedback mechanisms involving:

- hypothalamus;
- JGA;
- heart.

Key mechanisms:

- ADH mechanism;
- renin-angiotensin-aldosterone mechanism;
- ANF mechanism.

## 26. ADH Regulation

ADH is antidiuretic hormone.

It is also called vasopressin.

It is released from neurohypophysis under control of hypothalamus.

## 26.1 Trigger For ADH

Osmoreceptors detect changes in:

- blood volume;
- body fluid volume;
- ionic concentration.

Excessive fluid loss activates osmoreceptors.

They stimulate ADH release.

## 26.2 Effect Of ADH

ADH increases water reabsorption from later parts of nephron.

This reduces water loss in urine.

It prevents diuresis.

Diuresis means excessive urine formation.

ADH can also constrict blood vessels.

This may raise blood pressure.

## 26.3 Negative Feedback

When body fluid volume increases, osmoreceptors are switched off.

ADH release is suppressed.

Less water is reabsorbed.

Urine becomes more dilute.

## 27. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone Mechanism

This mechanism is activated when:

- glomerular blood flow falls;
- glomerular blood pressure falls;
- GFR falls.

## 27.1 Renin Release

JG cells release renin.

Renin converts angiotensinogen in blood to angiotensin I.

Angiotensin I is converted to angiotensin II.

## 27.2 Angiotensin II

Angiotensin II is a powerful vasoconstrictor.

It increases glomerular blood pressure.

This helps restore GFR.

## 27.3 Aldosterone

Angiotensin II stimulates adrenal cortex to release aldosterone.

Aldosterone increases reabsorption of:

- Na+;
- water.

This occurs mainly in distal parts of tubule.

The result is increased blood volume, blood pressure, and GFR.

## 27.4 RAAS Summary

```text
low GFR -> renin -> angiotensin II -> vasoconstriction + aldosterone
-> Na+ and water reabsorption -> blood pressure rises -> GFR restored
```

## 28. ANF Regulation

ANF means atrial natriuretic factor.

It is released by atria of heart when blood flow to atria increases.

ANF causes vasodilation.

Vasodilation lowers blood pressure.

ANF acts as a check on the renin-angiotensin mechanism.

## 29. Micturition

Urine formed by nephrons passes into urinary bladder.

The bladder stores urine until release.

Urine release is called micturition.

## 29.1 Micturition Reflex

As bladder fills, its wall stretches.

Stretch receptors send signals to CNS.

CNS sends motor signals that cause:

- contraction of bladder smooth muscle;
- relaxation of urethral sphincter.

Urine is released through urethra.

The neural mechanism is called micturition reflex.

## 29.2 Normal Urine

An adult human excretes about:

```text
1 to 1.5 L urine per day
```

Urine is:

- light yellow;
- watery;
- slightly acidic;
- about pH 6.0;
- characteristically odoured.

Average urea excreted:

```text
25 to 30 g per day
```

## 29.3 Urine Analysis

Urine analysis helps diagnose metabolic and kidney disorders.

Examples:

- glucose in urine is glycosuria;
- ketone bodies in urine is ketonuria.

Both may indicate diabetes mellitus.

## 30. Other Organs In Excretion

Kidneys are main excretory organs, but other organs also eliminate wastes.

Important supporting organs:

- lungs;
- liver;
- skin;
- salivary glands in small amount.

## 30.1 Lungs

Lungs remove:

- CO2;
- water vapour.

CO2 removal is large, about:

```text
200 mL per minute
```

## 30.2 Liver

Liver secretes bile containing:

- bilirubin;
- biliverdin;
- cholesterol;
- degraded steroid hormones;
- vitamins;
- drugs.

Many of these substances leave the body with digestive wastes.

## 30.3 Skin

Sweat glands produce sweat.

Sweat contains:

- water;
- NaCl;
- small amounts of urea;
- lactic acid.

Primary function of sweat is cooling, but it also removes small amounts of wastes.

Sebaceous glands produce sebum.

Sebum may eliminate:

- sterols;
- hydrocarbons;
- waxes.

Sebum also forms a protective oily covering on skin.

## 30.4 Saliva

Small amounts of nitrogenous wastes may be eliminated through saliva.

## 31. Disorders Of Excretory System

## 31.1 Uremia

Uremia is accumulation of urea in blood.

It occurs due to kidney malfunction.

It is harmful and may lead to kidney failure.

## 31.2 Haemodialysis

Haemodialysis removes nitrogenous wastes from blood using an artificial kidney.

It is useful for uremic patients.

## 31.3 Steps Of Haemodialysis

Basic steps:

1. Blood is taken from a convenient artery.
2. Anticoagulant such as heparin is added.
3. Blood passes through a coiled cellophane tube.
4. The tube is surrounded by dialysing fluid.
5. Dialysing fluid has plasma-like composition but lacks nitrogenous wastes.
6. Wastes diffuse from blood into dialysing fluid.
7. Cleansed blood is returned through a vein after anti-heparin is added.

The cellophane membrane is porous and allows movement based on concentration gradient.

## 31.4 Kidney Transplantation

Kidney transplantation is the ultimate correction method for acute renal failure in suitable cases.

A functioning kidney is taken from a donor.

A close relative is preferred to reduce rejection risk.

The host immune system may reject foreign tissue, so matching and medical management are important.

## 31.5 Renal Calculi

Renal calculi are kidney stones.

They are insoluble masses of crystallised salts such as oxalates.

They form within kidney.

## 31.6 Glomerulonephritis

Glomerulonephritis is inflammation of glomeruli.

It can reduce filtration efficiency and disturb kidney function.

## 32. Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation means regulation of water and ion concentration in body fluids.

It maintains proper osmotic balance.

Kidneys perform osmoregulation by adjusting:

- water reabsorption;
- salt reabsorption;
- ion secretion;
- urine concentration.

## 33. Why Terrestrial Animals Are Not Usually Ammonotelic

Ammonia is highly toxic and requires much water for excretion.

Terrestrial animals need to conserve water.

Therefore they usually convert ammonia into less toxic wastes such as:

- urea;
- uric acid.

Urea and uric acid can be excreted with less water loss.

## 34. Common Misconceptions

## 34.1 Misconception: Excretion Only Removes Urine

Correct idea:

Excretion removes metabolic wastes through kidneys, lungs, liver, skin, and other routes.

## 34.2 Misconception: Filtration Removes Only Wastes

Correct idea:

Glomerular filtration is non-selective for small molecules.

Useful substances such as glucose and amino acids are filtered and then reabsorbed.

## 34.3 Misconception: All Filtered Fluid Becomes Urine

Correct idea:

About 180 L filtrate forms daily, but only 1 to 1.5 L urine is excreted.

Nearly 99 percent is reabsorbed.

## 34.4 Misconception: Descending And Ascending Limbs Have Same Permeability

Correct idea:

Descending limb is permeable to water.

Ascending limb is impermeable to water and transports electrolytes.

## 34.5 Misconception: ADH Eliminates Water

Correct idea:

ADH increases water reabsorption and reduces water loss.

It makes urine more concentrated, not more dilute.

## 34.6 Misconception: Dialysing Fluid Contains Urea

Correct idea:

Dialysing fluid is similar to plasma but lacks nitrogenous wastes.

This allows urea and other wastes to diffuse out of blood.

## 35. Solved Concept Checks

## 35.1 Define GFR

GFR is the amount of filtrate formed by kidneys per minute.

In a healthy individual, it is about 125 mL per minute.

This equals about 180 L per day.

## 35.2 Explain Autoregulation Of GFR

JGA helps regulate GFR.

When GFR falls, JG cells release renin.

Renin starts the renin-angiotensin mechanism.

Angiotensin II increases glomerular blood pressure and aldosterone increases Na+ and water reabsorption.

Together these help restore GFR.

## 35.3 Why Is Protein-Free Fluid Filtered Into Bowman Capsule?

The filtration membrane allows small plasma constituents to pass.

Large proteins and formed elements normally remain in blood.

Therefore filtrate in Bowman capsule is almost protein-free.

## 35.4 Why Is Henle Loop Important?

Henle loop helps maintain medullary osmotic gradient.

Descending limb loses water.

Ascending limb loses electrolytes.

This supports concentration of urine.

## 35.5 Why Is PCT Important?

PCT reabsorbs nearly all essential nutrients and most water and electrolytes.

It also secretes H+ and ammonia and reabsorbs bicarbonate.

Thus it maintains pH and ionic balance.

## 35.6 What Is Counter-Current Mechanism?

Counter-current mechanism is the opposite direction flow in Henle loop and vasa recta that maintains the medullary osmotic gradient.

This gradient allows water reabsorption from collecting duct and concentration of urine.

## 35.7 What Is JGA Significance?

JGA detects changes related to GFR.

When GFR decreases, it releases renin.

Renin helps restore blood pressure, glomerular blood flow, and GFR.

## 35.8 What Is Micturition?

Micturition is release of urine from urinary bladder.

It is controlled by micturition reflex involving bladder stretch receptors, CNS, bladder muscle contraction, and urethral sphincter relaxation.

## 40. Exam-Oriented Answers

## 40.1 Role Of Liver, Lungs, And Skin In Excretion

Lungs remove CO2 and water vapour.

Liver excretes bile pigments, cholesterol, degraded steroid hormones, vitamins, and drugs through bile.

Skin removes small amounts of wastes through sweat and sebum.

Sweat contains water, NaCl, small amounts of urea, and lactic acid.

## 40.2 Terrestrial Animals And Nitrogenous Waste

Terrestrial animals need to conserve water.

Ammonia is highly toxic and needs much water for removal.

Therefore terrestrial animals are usually ureotelic or uricotelic.

Urea and uric acid are less toxic and require less water.

## 40.3 Counter-Current Mechanism Brief Account

Filtrate flows in opposite directions in the two limbs of Henle loop.

Blood flows in opposite directions in vasa recta.

NaCl and urea help create a gradient from 300 mOsmol/L in cortex to 1200 mOsmol/L in inner medulla.

Vasa recta preserves this gradient.

Water leaves collecting duct into the hyperosmotic medulla.

Thus urine becomes concentrated.

## 40.4 Micturition Explanation

Urine fills the urinary bladder.

Stretch receptors in bladder wall send signals to CNS.

CNS sends motor signals to contract bladder smooth muscle and relax urethral sphincter.

Urine is released.

This reflex is called micturition reflex.

## 43. Practice Answers

## 43.1 Osmoregulation

Osmoregulation is regulation of water and ion concentration in body fluids.

Kidneys maintain osmotic balance by adjusting reabsorption and secretion of water and ions.

## 43.2 JGA Significance

JGA monitors conditions related to GFR.

When GFR falls, JG cells release renin.

Renin activates the renin-angiotensin mechanism, restoring glomerular pressure and GFR.

## 43.3 Counter-Current Mechanism

Counter-current flow in Henle loop and vasa recta maintains increasing osmolarity from cortex to inner medulla.

NaCl and urea create the gradient.

The gradient allows water to leave collecting duct, concentrating urine.

## 43.4 Role Of Other Organs

Lungs remove CO2 and water.

Liver removes bile pigments, cholesterol, degraded hormones, vitamins, and drugs through bile.

Skin removes small amounts of urea, salts, lactic acid, sterols, hydrocarbons, and waxes.
