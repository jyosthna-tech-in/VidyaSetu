## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter uses frog as a representative vertebrate. The aim is not to memorise isolated organ names. The aim is to see how organ systems cooperate.

Study in this order:

1. Understand the hierarchy: cell to tissue to organ to organ system.
2. Learn the external features of frog.
3. Study the digestive system as a continuous pathway.
4. Study respiration by habitat: water, land, aestivation, hibernation.
5. Study circulation, excretion, nervous control, and reproduction.
6. Keep male and female reproductive differences separate.

## 1. From Cells To Organ Systems

Unicellular organisms perform all life functions within one cell. Digestion, respiration, excretion, and reproduction are handled by the same cell.

Multicellular animals are different. Their bodies contain many cells, and groups of cells specialise for particular functions.

This creates division of labour.

## 1.1 Biological Organisation

| Level | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Cell | Basic structural and functional unit | Muscle cell, nerve cell |
| Tissue | Similar cells plus intercellular substances performing a function | Epithelial tissue |
| Organ | Different tissues organised for a specific function | Heart, stomach, lung |
| Organ system | Organs working together | Digestive system, respiratory system |

## 1.2 Four Basic Animal Tissues

Complex animals have four basic tissue types:

1. Epithelial tissue
2. Connective tissue
3. Muscular tissue
4. Neural tissue

An organ may contain more than one tissue type. For example, the heart contains epithelial, connective, muscular, and neural tissues.

## 1.3 Why Organ Systems Matter

Organ systems allow efficient coordination of millions or billions of cells.

For example:

- digestive system obtains and processes food;
- respiratory system exchanges gases;
- circulatory system transports materials;
- excretory system removes wastes;
- nervous and endocrine systems coordinate body functions.

## 2. Frog As A Representative Vertebrate

Frogs can live both on land and in freshwater. They belong to:

| Taxonomic Position | Group |
|---|---|
| Phylum | Chordata |
| Class | Amphibia |
| Common Indian example | Rana tigrina |

## 2.1 Poikilothermy

Frogs do not maintain a constant body temperature.

Their body temperature changes with the temperature of the environment.

Such animals are called poikilothermous or cold-blooded.

## 2.2 Colour Change And Protection

Frogs may show colour changes when present in grass or on dry land.

This helps them blend with surroundings and hide from enemies. This protective colour matching is called camouflage.

## 2.3 Aestivation And Hibernation

Frogs are not usually seen during peak summer and peak winter.

| Condition | Meaning | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Aestivation | Summer sleep | Protection from extreme heat |
| Hibernation | Winter sleep | Protection from extreme cold |

During these periods, frogs take shelter in deep burrows.

## 3. Morphology Of Frog

Morphology means external visible features.

## 3.1 Skin

Frog skin is smooth and slippery because mucus is present.

The skin is always kept moist.

| Surface | Colour |
|---|---|
| Dorsal side | Usually olive green with dark irregular spots |
| Ventral side | Pale yellow |

Frogs do not drink water in the usual way. They absorb water through the skin.

## 3.2 Body Regions

The body of frog is divided into:

- head;
- trunk.

Neck and tail are absent in adult frog.

## 3.3 Head Features

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| Nostrils | Air entry |
| Eyes | Vision |
| Nictitating membrane | Protects eyes in water |
| Tympanum | Receives sound signals |

The eyes are bulged and protected by a nictitating membrane.

The tympanum is present on each side behind the eyes.

## 3.4 Limbs

Frogs have forelimbs and hind limbs.

These help in:

- swimming;
- walking;
- leaping;
- burrowing.

## 3.5 Forelimbs And Hind Limbs

| Feature | Forelimbs | Hind Limbs |
|---|---|---|
| Digits | Four | Five |
| Size | Smaller | Larger and more muscular |
| Main role | Support during landing and movement | Jumping and swimming |
| Webbing | Less prominent | Webbed digits help swimming |

## 3.6 Sexual Dimorphism

Sexual dimorphism means visible differences between male and female individuals.

Male frogs can be identified by:

- sound-producing vocal sacs;
- copulatory pad on the first digit of forelimbs.

These are absent in female frogs.

## 4. Anatomy Of Frog

Anatomy means internal structure.

The frog body cavity contains well-developed organ systems:

- digestive system;
- respiratory system;
- circulatory system;
- excretory system;
- nervous system;
- endocrine system;
- reproductive system.

## 5. Digestive System

The digestive system consists of:

- alimentary canal;
- digestive glands.

Because frogs are carnivores, their alimentary canal is relatively short and the intestine is reduced compared with herbivorous animals.

## 5.1 Alimentary Canal Pathway

The pathway is:

```text
Mouth
Buccal cavity
Pharynx
Oesophagus
Stomach
Intestine
Rectum
Cloaca
Exterior
```

## 5.2 Digestive Glands

| Gland | Secretion Or Role |
|---|---|
| Liver | Secretes bile |
| Gall bladder | Stores bile |
| Pancreas | Produces pancreatic juice containing digestive enzymes |

## 5.3 Food Capture

Frogs capture food using a bilobed tongue.

The tongue helps catch prey quickly.

## 5.4 Digestion In Stomach

The stomach secretes:

- hydrochloric acid;
- gastric juices.

These help digest food.

Partially digested food is called chyme.

## 5.5 Digestion In Intestine

Chyme moves from stomach to duodenum, the first part of the small intestine.

The duodenum receives:

- bile from gall bladder;
- pancreatic juice from pancreas.

Both enter through a common bile duct.

## 5.6 Role Of Bile And Pancreatic Juice

| Secretion | Function |
|---|---|
| Bile | Emulsifies fats |
| Pancreatic juice | Digests carbohydrates and proteins |

Final digestion occurs in the intestine.

## 5.7 Absorption

Digested food is absorbed by finger-like folds in the intestinal wall.

These folds are called villi and microvilli.

They increase the surface area for absorption.

## 5.8 Egestion

Undigested solid waste moves into the rectum.

It passes out through the cloaca.

## 6. Respiratory System

Frogs respire differently in water and on land.

## 6.1 Types Of Respiration In Frog

| Type | Organ Or Surface | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Cutaneous respiration | Skin | In water, during aestivation and hibernation |
| Buccal respiration | Buccal cavity | On land |
| Pulmonary respiration | Lungs | On land |

## 6.2 Cutaneous Respiration

In water, frog skin acts as the respiratory surface.

Dissolved oxygen diffuses through moist skin.

Carbon dioxide also diffuses out through the skin.

## 6.3 Pulmonary Respiration

Respiration through lungs is called pulmonary respiration.

The lungs are paired, elongated, pink, sac-like structures in the upper trunk region.

Air pathway:

```text
Nostrils
Buccal cavity
Lungs
```

## 6.4 Respiration During Aestivation And Hibernation

During summer sleep and winter sleep, gaseous exchange mainly occurs through skin.

This is possible because the skin is moist and richly supplied with blood.

## 7. Circulatory System

Frog has a well-developed closed circulatory system.

It also has a lymphatic system.

## 7.1 Blood Vascular System

The blood vascular system includes:

- heart;
- blood vessels;
- blood.

## 7.2 Lymphatic System

The lymphatic system includes:

- lymph;
- lymph channels;
- lymph nodes.

Lymph differs from blood because it lacks RBCs and has fewer proteins.

## 7.3 Heart

The frog heart is muscular and located in the upper part of the body cavity.

It is covered by a membrane called pericardium.

## 7.4 Chambers Of Frog Heart

Frog heart has three chambers:

- two atria;
- one ventricle.

## 7.5 Associated Heart Structures

| Structure | Role |
|---|---|
| Sinus venosus | Receives blood into right atrium through major veins |
| Vena cava | Major veins bringing blood to heart |
| Conus arteriosus | Receives blood from ventricle and leads to arterial system |

## 7.6 Arteries And Veins

Arteries carry blood from the heart to body parts.

Veins collect blood from body parts and return it to the heart.

## 7.7 Portal Systems

Frogs have special venous connections.

| Portal System | Connection |
|---|---|
| Hepatic portal system | Between intestine and liver |
| Renal portal system | Between lower body parts and kidneys |

## 7.8 Blood

Blood is made of:

- plasma;
- RBCs;
- WBCs;
- platelets.

Frog RBCs are nucleated and contain haemoglobin.

Blood transports nutrients, gases, and water.

## 8. Excretory System

The excretory system eliminates nitrogenous wastes.

## 8.1 Parts Of Excretory System

The frog excretory system consists of:

- pair of kidneys;
- ureters;
- cloaca;
- urinary bladder.

## 8.2 Kidneys

Kidneys are compact, dark red, bean-like structures.

They are located posteriorly in the body cavity, on both sides of the vertebral column.

Each kidney contains structural and functional units called uriniferous tubules or nephrons.

## 8.3 Ureters In Male Frog

In male frogs, two ureters emerge from the kidneys.

They act as urinogenital ducts.

They carry urine as well as reproductive materials and open into the cloaca.

## 8.4 Ureters In Female Frog

In female frogs, ureters and oviducts open separately into the cloaca.

The ureters mainly carry urine from kidneys to cloaca.

## 8.5 Urinary Bladder

The urinary bladder is thin-walled and lies ventral to the rectum.

It opens into the cloaca.

## 8.6 Ureotelic Nature

Frog excretes nitrogenous waste mainly as urea.

Therefore, frog is ureotelic.

## 9. Control And Coordination

Frog has a highly developed system for control and coordination.

It includes:

- nervous system;
- endocrine glands.

## 9.1 Endocrine Glands

Chemical coordination is achieved by hormones secreted by endocrine glands.

Prominent endocrine glands include:

- pituitary;
- thyroid;
- parathyroid;
- thymus;
- pineal body;
- pancreatic islets;
- adrenals;
- gonads.

## 9.2 Nervous System

The nervous system is divided into:

| Division | Components |
|---|---|
| Central nervous system | Brain and spinal cord |
| Peripheral nervous system | Cranial and spinal nerves |
| Autonomic nervous system | Sympathetic and parasympathetic systems |

Frog has ten pairs of cranial nerves.

## 9.3 Brain

The brain is enclosed in the cranium.

It has three main regions.

| Brain Region | Parts |
|---|---|
| Forebrain | Olfactory lobes, cerebral hemispheres, diencephalon |
| Midbrain | Optic lobes |
| Hindbrain | Cerebellum, medulla oblongata |

The medulla oblongata passes through the foramen magnum and continues as the spinal cord.

The spinal cord is enclosed in the vertebral column.

## 10. Sense Organs

Frog has sense organs for:

- touch;
- taste;
- smell;
- vision;
- hearing;
- balance.

## 10.1 Main Sense Structures

| Sense | Structure |
|---|---|
| Touch | Sensory papillae |
| Taste | Taste buds |
| Smell | Nasal epithelium |
| Vision | Eyes |
| Hearing and balance | Tympanum with internal ears |

Eyes and internal ears are well-organised structures.

Other sense organs are cellular aggregations around nerve endings.

## 10.2 Eyes

Frog eyes are spherical and located in orbits of the skull.

They are simple eyes, meaning each eye has one visual unit.

## 10.3 Ears

External ear is absent.

Only tympanum is visible externally.

The ear functions in hearing and balance.

## 11. Male Reproductive System

Male reproductive organs consist of a pair of yellowish, ovoid testes.

## 11.1 Position Of Testes

Testes are attached to the upper part of kidneys by a double fold of peritoneum called mesorchium.

## 11.2 Sperm Duct Pathway

Vasa efferentia arise from testes.

They enter the kidneys and open into Bidder's canal.

Finally, the pathway communicates with the urinogenital duct, which opens into the cloaca.

## 11.3 Cloaca In Male Frog

The cloaca is a small median chamber used to pass:

- faecal matter;
- urine;
- sperms.

## 12. Female Reproductive System

Female reproductive organs include a pair of ovaries.

## 12.1 Position Of Ovaries

Ovaries are located near the kidneys.

There is no functional connection between ovaries and kidneys.

## 12.2 Oviducts

A pair of oviducts arises from the ovaries and opens separately into the cloaca.

## 12.3 Ova And Fertilisation

A mature female can lay about 2500 to 3000 ova at a time.

Fertilisation is external and occurs in water.

Development includes a larval stage called tadpole.

The tadpole undergoes metamorphosis to become an adult frog.

## 13. Importance Of Frogs

Frogs are useful because:

- they eat insects;
- they help protect crops;
- they maintain ecological balance;
- they are important links in food chains and food webs;
- in some countries, muscular legs of frogs are used as food.

## 15. Common Learner Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Saying frog drinks water through mouth | Frog absorbs water through skin |
| Calling frog warm-blooded | Frog is poikilothermous |
| Confusing aestivation and hibernation | Aestivation is summer sleep; hibernation is winter sleep |
| Saying skin respiration occurs only in water | Skin respiration also occurs during aestivation and hibernation |
| Forgetting frog heart has three chambers | Frog heart has two atria and one ventricle |
| Calling frog RBCs non-nucleated | Frog RBCs are nucleated |
| Saying male and female ureters function identically | Male ureters act as urinogenital ducts; female ureters are separate from oviducts |
| Forgetting fertilisation is external | Frog fertilisation occurs in water |

```text
                   |

                   |

```

Essential labels:

- mouth;
- buccal cavity;
- pharynx;
- oesophagus;
- stomach;
- liver;
- gall bladder;
- pancreas;
- intestine;
- rectum;
- cloaca.

Short explanation:

Food is captured by the bilobed tongue. It passes through the alimentary canal to the stomach, where gastric juices act on it. Chyme then enters the duodenum and receives bile and pancreatic juice. Final digestion and absorption occur in the intestine. Waste passes through rectum and cloaca.

## 16.2 Function Of Ureters In Frog

The ureters carry urine from kidneys to cloaca.

In male frogs, ureters also function as urinogenital ducts. They carry urine and also provide a passage connected with the reproductive system.

In female frogs, ureters and oviducts open separately into the cloaca, so the ureters mainly transport urine.

## 17. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 17.1 Why Is Frog Called Amphibian?

Frog can live both on land and in freshwater. Therefore, it belongs to class Amphibia.

## 17.2 Why Is Frog Called Poikilothermous?

Frog body temperature varies with environmental temperature. It does not maintain a constant internal body temperature.

## 17.3 Why Is Frog Skin Always Moist?

Frog skin has mucus and remains moist. Moist skin helps in cutaneous respiration and water absorption.

## 17.4 What Is The Role Of Nictitating Membrane?

The nictitating membrane protects the eyes when frog is in water.

## 17.5 What Is The Role Of Tympanum?

Tympanum receives sound signals and is associated with hearing.

## 17.6 Why Is Frog Intestine Short?

Frog is carnivorous. Carnivores generally have shorter intestines than herbivores because animal food is easier to digest than cellulose-rich plant food.

## 17.7 Why Is Frog Ureotelic?

Frog excretes nitrogenous waste mainly as urea. Therefore, it is ureotelic.

## 17.8 Why Is Frog Ecologically Important?

Frogs eat insects, help protect crops, and form an important link in food chains and food webs.
