## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is not only a list of kingdoms. It teaches a way of thinking.

Classification changes when scientists use better criteria. Early systems used simple visible features. Modern systems use cell type, body organisation, nutrition, reproduction, and evolutionary relationships. Once these criteria are understood, most details in the chapter become easier to remember.

Read the chapter in this order:

1. Learn why two kingdoms were inadequate.
2. Learn the five kingdoms and the criteria used to separate them.
3. Study Monera, Protista, and Fungi in detail.
4. Keep Plantae and Animalia as short revision points, because later chapters expand them.
5. Study viruses, viroids, prions, and lichens separately because they do not fit neatly into the five kingdoms.

## 1. Why Biological Classification Was Needed

Living organisms are extremely diverse. They differ in size, shape, cell type, habitat, food habit, reproduction, and evolutionary history. Without classification, the study of living organisms would become a collection of isolated facts.

Classification helps biology by:

- organising diversity into meaningful groups;
- making identification easier;
- showing similarities and differences among organisms;
- helping us understand evolutionary relationships;
- allowing scientists to communicate using shared categories;
- predicting features of organisms based on their group.

For example, if an organism is placed under Fungi, we expect it to be eukaryotic, heterotrophic, and to have a chitinous cell wall. A kingdom name therefore carries biological meaning.

## 2. Early Classification Systems

## 2.1 Aristotle's Classification

Aristotle was among the earliest thinkers to classify organisms on a more scientific basis. His system used simple visible characters.

| Group | Basis Used |
|---|---|
| Plants | Divided into herbs, shrubs, and trees |
| Animals | Divided into animals with red blood and animals without red blood |

This was an important beginning, but it was limited because it used only a few external characters. Organisms that look similar may be very different internally, and organisms that look different may share deep evolutionary relationships.

## 2.2 Two-Kingdom Classification

During Linnaeus' time, organisms were mainly placed into two kingdoms:

| Kingdom | General Idea |
|---|---|
| Plantae | Plants and plant-like organisms |
| Animalia | Animals and animal-like organisms |

This system was simple and easy to use, but it could not handle many organisms properly.

## 2.3 Why the Two-Kingdom System Was Inadequate

The two-kingdom system grouped together organisms that were very different in important ways.

| Problem | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Prokaryotes and eukaryotes were not separated | Bacteria and blue-green algae were grouped with eukaryotic plants |
| Unicellular and multicellular forms were mixed | Chlamydomonas and large plants could be treated under broad plant groups |
| Photosynthetic and non-photosynthetic forms were mixed | Fungi were grouped with plants even though fungi do not photosynthesise |
| Cell wall composition was ignored | Fungi have chitin in their walls, while green plants have cellulose |
| Evolutionary relationships were not clear | The system was not strongly phylogenetic |

The major issue was this: having a cell wall alone is not enough to place organisms together. Bacteria, fungi, algae, and plants may all have walls, but their cell structure, nutrition, and evolutionary relationships differ strongly.

## 3. Criteria Used In Modern Classification

As biology advanced, scientists began using more reliable criteria.

| Criterion | Question It Answers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Cell structure | Is the cell prokaryotic or eukaryotic? | Bacteria are prokaryotic; fungi are eukaryotic |
| Cell wall | Is a wall present, and what is it made of? | Fungi have chitin; plants have cellulose |
| Body organisation | Is the body unicellular, colonial, filamentous, or multicellular? | Protists are mostly unicellular; animals are multicellular |
| Mode of nutrition | Is nutrition autotrophic or heterotrophic? | Plants are photosynthetic; fungi are absorptive heterotrophs |
| Reproduction | How does the organism reproduce? | Fungi form spores; bacteria divide by fission |
| Habitat | Where does it live? | Archaebacteria can live in extreme habitats |
| Phylogenetic relationship | What is its evolutionary relationship? | Modern systems aim to reflect evolutionary lines |

The more criteria we use, the more natural and useful classification becomes.

## 4. Whittaker's Five-Kingdom Classification

R. H. Whittaker proposed the five-kingdom classification in 1969. His five kingdoms were:

1. Monera
2. Protista
3. Fungi
4. Plantae
5. Animalia

## 4.1 Main Criteria Used By Whittaker

Whittaker used these major criteria:

- cell structure;
- body organisation;
- mode of nutrition;
- reproduction;
- phylogenetic relationships.

This was a major improvement because it separated prokaryotes, unicellular eukaryotes, fungi, plants, and animals into more meaningful groups.

## 4.2 Quick Comparison Of The Five Kingdoms

| Character | Monera | Protista | Fungi | Plantae | Animalia |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cell type | Prokaryotic | Eukaryotic | Eukaryotic | Eukaryotic | Eukaryotic |
| Nuclear membrane | Absent | Present | Present | Present | Present |
| Cell wall | Usually present; non-cellulosic | Present in some | Present; chitin | Present; cellulose | Absent |
| Body organisation | Cellular, mostly unicellular | Cellular, mostly unicellular | Multicellular or loose tissue; yeast unicellular | Tissue and organ level | Tissue, organ, and organ system level |
| Nutrition | Autotrophic or heterotrophic | Autotrophic or heterotrophic | Heterotrophic by absorption | Mostly photosynthetic autotrophic | Heterotrophic, mainly holozoic |
| Examples | Bacteria, cyanobacteria | Amoeba, Euglena, diatoms | Rhizopus, yeast, mushrooms | Algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, seed plants | Sponges, insects, fishes, humans |

## 4.3 Why Fungi Became A Separate Kingdom

Fungi were once placed with plants because both often have cell walls and are generally stationary. But this grouping was artificial.

| Feature | Green Plants | Fungi |
|---|---|---|
| Nutrition | Photosynthetic autotrophs | Heterotrophs by absorption |
| Main wall material | Cellulose | Chitin and polysaccharides |
| Chlorophyll | Present in green plants | Absent |
| Food reserve | Commonly starch | Commonly glycogen and oils |
| Ecological role | Producers | Decomposers, parasites, symbionts |

The separation of fungi shows an important principle: classification must use internal and functional features, not only outward appearance.

## 4.4 The Three-Domain Idea

Later classification systems proposed a three-domain framework. In this idea, organisms once placed in Monera are divided into two major prokaryotic domains, while eukaryotes form a third domain.

For this chapter, the detailed three-domain system is not the focus. The important point is that classification continues to change as knowledge improves.

## 5. Kingdom Monera

Kingdom Monera includes prokaryotic organisms. Bacteria are the main members of this kingdom.

## 5.1 General Features Of Monera

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell type | Prokaryotic |
| Nucleus | True nucleus absent |
| Membrane-bound organelles | Absent |
| Body organisation | Mostly unicellular |
| Cell wall | Usually present, but not made of cellulose |
| Nutrition | Autotrophic or heterotrophic |
| Reproduction | Mainly by fission |
| Habitat | Nearly everywhere, including extreme habitats |

Monerans are simple in cell structure but very diverse in metabolism. They can live in soil, water, air, hot springs, deserts, snow, deep oceans, and inside other organisms.

## 5.2 Bacterial Abundance

Bacteria are among the most abundant microorganisms. A small handful of soil can contain hundreds of bacterial forms. Their success is due to:

- small size;
- rapid reproduction;
- ability to use many kinds of food;
- survival in harsh habitats;
- ability to form resistant structures in unfavourable conditions;
- metabolic diversity.

## 5.3 Main Shapes Of Bacteria

| Shape | Singular Name | Plural Name | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spherical | Coccus | Cocci | Round bacteria |
| Rod-shaped | Bacillus | Bacilli | Cylindrical or rod-like bacteria |
| Comma-shaped | Vibrio | Vibrios | Curved comma-like bacteria |
| Spiral | Spirillum | Spirilla | Spiral or twisted bacteria |

Memory aid: cocci are circular, bacilli are bars, vibrios are curved, spirilla are spiral.

## 5.4 Nutrition In Bacteria

Bacteria show great metabolic diversity.

| Type | Meaning | Examples Or Role |
|---|---|---|
| Photosynthetic autotrophs | Use light energy to prepare food | Cyanobacteria |
| Chemosynthetic autotrophs | Use energy from oxidation of inorganic substances | Bacteria involved in nutrient cycles |
| Heterotrophs | Depend on organic matter or other organisms | Decomposers, parasites, useful bacteria |

Most bacteria are heterotrophic, but bacterial metabolism is broader than that of most other organism groups.

## 6. Archaebacteria

Archaebacteria are special bacteria that often live in extreme habitats. Their cell wall structure differs from that of other bacteria, and this helps them survive harsh conditions.

## 6.1 Types Of Archaebacteria

| Type | Habitat | Important Point |
|---|---|---|
| Halophiles | Extremely salty areas | Adapted to high salt concentration |
| Thermoacidophiles | Hot acidic environments such as hot springs | Tolerate heat and acidity |
| Methanogens | Marshes and gut of ruminant animals | Produce methane |

## 6.2 Methanogens And Biogas

Methanogens occur in the gut of ruminant animals such as cows and buffaloes. They help break down organic matter and produce methane.

This is why cattle dung can be used for biogas production. Methane is a major component of biogas.

## 6.3 Why Archaebacteria Matter

Archaebacteria are important because:

- they show that life can exist in extreme environments;
- they reveal deep evolutionary diversity among prokaryotes;
- methanogens are linked with biogas production;
- they help students understand why prokaryotes are not all identical.

## 7. Eubacteria

Eubacteria are often called true bacteria. They are more common in ordinary environments than archaebacteria.

## 7.1 General Features Of Eubacteria

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell wall | Rigid |
| Motility | Motile forms may have flagella |
| Cell type | Prokaryotic |
| Nutrition | Autotrophic or heterotrophic |
| Reproduction | Mainly fission |
| Importance | Decomposition, disease, curd formation, antibiotics, nitrogen fixation |

## 7.2 Cyanobacteria

Cyanobacteria are photosynthetic eubacteria. They are also called blue-green algae, although they are prokaryotes and not true algae in the eukaryotic sense.

## 7.3 Features Of Cyanobacteria

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Pigment | Chlorophyll a |
| Nutrition | Photosynthetic autotrophic |
| Organisation | Unicellular, colonial, or filamentous |
| Habitat | Freshwater, marine water, or terrestrial habitats |
| Colony covering | Often surrounded by a gelatinous sheath |
| Water bloom | Can multiply rapidly in polluted water bodies |
| Nitrogen fixation | Some forms fix atmospheric nitrogen |

Examples include Nostoc and Anabaena.

## 7.4 Heterocysts

Some cyanobacteria have specialised cells called heterocysts. These cells help in nitrogen fixation.

Nitrogen fixation means conversion of atmospheric nitrogen into usable nitrogen compounds. This is ecologically important because living organisms need nitrogen for proteins, nucleic acids, and other biomolecules.

## 7.5 Chemosynthetic Autotrophic Bacteria

Chemosynthetic autotrophic bacteria obtain energy by oxidising inorganic substances such as:

- nitrates;
- nitrites;
- ammonia;
- compounds of iron;
- compounds of sulphur.

They use the released energy for ATP production.

## 7.6 Role In Nutrient Cycling

Chemosynthetic bacteria help recycle nutrients such as:

- nitrogen;
- phosphorus;
- iron;
- sulphur.

These cycles are essential because nutrients must be reused in ecosystems. Without microbial recycling, nutrients would remain locked in waste and dead matter.

## 7.7 Heterotrophic Bacteria

Heterotrophic bacteria are the most abundant bacteria in nature. They depend on organic matter for nutrition.

## 7.8 Useful Roles Of Heterotrophic Bacteria

| Role | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Decomposition | Break down dead organic matter |
| Curd formation | Convert milk into curd |
| Antibiotic production | Some bacteria help produce antibiotics |
| Nitrogen fixation | Some bacteria fix nitrogen in legume roots |
| Nutrient recycling | Return minerals to the environment |

## 7.9 Harmful Roles Of Bacteria

Some bacteria are pathogens. They cause diseases in humans, animals, and plants.

| Disease Or Damage | Type |
|---|---|
| Cholera | Human bacterial disease |
| Typhoid | Human bacterial disease |
| Tetanus | Human bacterial disease |
| Citrus canker | Plant bacterial disease |

## 7.10 Reproduction In Bacteria

Bacteria reproduce mainly by fission.

In fission, one bacterial cell divides into two daughter cells. Under favourable conditions, this can happen very quickly, which explains rapid bacterial multiplication.

Under unfavourable conditions, some bacteria form spores. Spores help them survive heat, dryness, starvation, or other stress.

Bacteria can also exchange genetic material through primitive forms of DNA transfer. This is not sexual reproduction in the same way as in higher organisms, but it creates genetic variation.

## 7.11 Mycoplasma

Mycoplasma are unusual prokaryotic organisms.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell wall | Completely absent |
| Size | Among the smallest living cells known |
| Oxygen need | Can survive without oxygen |
| Pathogenicity | Many forms cause disease in plants and animals |

Mycoplasma are important because they show that not all bacteria-like organisms have a cell wall.

## 8. Kingdom Protista

Kingdom Protista includes single-celled eukaryotic organisms. It is a connecting kingdom because protists show plant-like, animal-like, and fungus-like features.

## 8.1 General Features Of Protista

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell type | Eukaryotic |
| Body organisation | Mostly unicellular |
| Habitat | Mainly aquatic |
| Nucleus | Well-defined nucleus present |
| Organelles | Membrane-bound organelles present |
| Locomotion | Some have cilia or flagella |
| Nutrition | Autotrophic or heterotrophic |
| Reproduction | Asexual and sexual methods |

Protista has flexible boundaries. A photosynthetic protist may look plant-like, while a protozoan may look animal-like in behaviour.

## 8.2 Major Groups Of Protista

| Group | Main Character |
|---|---|
| Chrysophytes | Diatoms and golden algae |
| Dinoflagellates | Mostly marine, often photosynthetic, two flagella |
| Euglenoids | Flexible pellicle, photosynthetic in light, heterotrophic in dark |
| Slime moulds | Saprophytic protists with plasmodium stage |
| Protozoans | Heterotrophic, predator or parasite forms |

## 9. Chrysophytes

Chrysophytes include diatoms and golden algae, including desmids.

## 9.1 Main Features Of Chrysophytes

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Members | Diatoms and golden algae |
| Habitat | Freshwater and marine water |
| Size | Microscopic |
| Movement in water | Float passively as plankton |
| Nutrition | Mostly photosynthetic |
| Importance | Chief producers in oceans |

## 9.2 Diatom Cell Wall

Diatoms have a unique wall made of two overlapping halves, like the two parts of a soap box.

The wall is impregnated with silica. Because silica is hard and resistant, diatom walls do not decay easily.

## 9.3 Diatomaceous Earth

Over long periods, dead diatoms leave behind large deposits of their silica-rich walls. This material is called diatomaceous earth.

## 9.4 Uses Of Diatomaceous Earth

| Property | Use |
|---|---|
| Gritty texture | Polishing |
| Fine porous nature | Filtration of oils and syrups |
| Silica-rich composition | Industrial filtering and abrasive uses |

## 9.5 Exam Point On Diatoms

If asked about the nature of the diatom cell wall, write:

Diatom walls are made of two overlapping silica-embedded halves. Their silica-rich walls are resistant to decay and accumulate as diatomaceous earth.

## 10. Dinoflagellates

Dinoflagellates are mostly marine protists. Many are photosynthetic.

## 10.1 Main Features Of Dinoflagellates

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Habitat | Mostly marine |
| Nutrition | Mostly photosynthetic |
| Pigments | May appear yellow, green, brown, blue, or red |
| Cell covering | Stiff cellulose plates |
| Flagella | Usually two |
| Flagellar arrangement | One longitudinal and one transverse flagellum |

The two flagella help dinoflagellates move in a spinning or whirling manner.

## 10.2 Red Tides

Some red dinoflagellates, such as Gonyaulax, multiply rapidly and make seawater appear red. This phenomenon is called red tide.

Red tides can be harmful because toxins released by large populations may kill fish and other marine animals.

## 10.3 Algal Bloom

An algal bloom is rapid multiplication of algae or algae-like organisms in a water body. It often occurs when water contains excess nutrients.

In this chapter, cyanobacteria are also mentioned as organisms that may form blooms in polluted water.

## 11. Euglenoids

Euglenoids are interesting because they show both plant-like and animal-like features.

## 11.1 Main Features Of Euglenoids

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Habitat | Mostly freshwater, especially stagnant water |
| Cell wall | Absent |
| Outer covering | Protein-rich pellicle |
| Body | Flexible |
| Flagella | Two flagella, one short and one long |
| Nutrition in sunlight | Photosynthetic |
| Nutrition without sunlight | Heterotrophic |
| Pigments | Similar to higher plants |
| Example | Euglena |

## 11.2 Why Euglenoids Are Important

Euglenoids show that classification is complex. They can photosynthesise in sunlight, but in the absence of light they behave like heterotrophs by feeding on smaller organisms.

This dual nature is one reason protists are treated as a separate kingdom rather than being forced into plants or animals.

## 12. Slime Moulds

Slime moulds are saprophytic protists. They feed on decaying organic material.

## 12.1 Main Features Of Slime Moulds

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Nutrition | Saprophytic |
| Habitat | Decaying twigs and leaves |
| Movement | Body moves over decaying matter |
| Feeding | Engulfs organic material |
| Favourable condition | Forms plasmodium |
| Unfavourable condition | Forms fruiting bodies with spores |
| Spore survival | Spores are resistant and may survive for years |
| Dispersal | Spores are dispersed by air currents |

## 12.2 Plasmodium

Under suitable conditions, slime moulds form an aggregation called plasmodium. It may spread over a large area.

When conditions become unfavourable, the plasmodium forms fruiting bodies that bear spores. The spores have true walls and are resistant.

## 13. Protozoans

Protozoans are heterotrophic protists. They live as predators or parasites and are considered primitive relatives of animals.

## 13.1 Four Major Groups Of Protozoans

| Group | Locomotion Or Main Feature | Habitat Or Role | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amoeboid protozoans | Pseudopodia | Freshwater, seawater, moist soil; some parasitic | Amoeba, Entamoeba |
| Flagellated protozoans | Flagella | Free-living or parasitic | Trypanosoma |
| Ciliated protozoans | Cilia | Aquatic and actively moving | Paramoecium |
| Sporozoans | Infectious spore-like stage | Mostly parasitic | Plasmodium |

## 13.2 Amoeboid Protozoans

Amoeboid protozoans move and capture food using pseudopodia.

Pseudopodia means false feet. These are temporary extensions of the cell surface.

Some marine amoeboid protozoans have silica shells. Some forms, such as Entamoeba, are parasites.

## 13.3 Flagellated Protozoans

Flagellated protozoans have flagella. They may be free-living or parasitic.

Trypanosoma is a parasitic example associated with sleeping sickness.

## 13.4 Ciliated Protozoans

Ciliated protozoans have many cilia. Cilia help in movement and feeding.

Paramoecium has a cavity called a gullet. Coordinated ciliary movement pushes food-laden water into the gullet.

## 13.5 Sporozoans

Sporozoans include organisms with an infectious spore-like stage in their life cycle.

Plasmodium is the most important example in this chapter. It causes malaria.

## 14. Kingdom Fungi

Fungi form a separate kingdom of heterotrophic eukaryotes.

## 14.1 Common Examples Of Fungi

| Example | Context |
|---|---|
| Bread mould | Grows on moist bread |
| Rotten fruit mould | Common on decaying fruits |
| Mushroom | Edible fungal body in many species |
| Toadstools | Mushroom-like fungi, often not edible |
| Yeast | Used in bread and beer |
| Puccinia | Causes wheat rust |
| Penicillium | Linked with antibiotic production |

## 14.2 General Features Of Fungi

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell type | Eukaryotic |
| Nutrition | Heterotrophic by absorption |
| Cell wall | Chitin and polysaccharides |
| Body | Usually filamentous |
| Habitat | Air, water, soil, plants, animals |
| Preferred condition | Warm and humid places |
| Reproduction | Vegetative, asexual, and sexual |

Fungi are cosmopolitan, meaning they occur in many regions and habitats.

## 14.3 Why Food Is Refrigerated

Food is kept in a refrigerator because low temperature slows the growth of bacteria and fungi. This delays spoilage.

Fungi grow well in warm and humid conditions. Reducing temperature makes growth slower.

## 14.4 Fungal Body Structure

Most fungi are filamentous. Yeast is an exception because it is unicellular.

| Structure | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Hypha | Long thread-like fungal filament |
| Mycelium | Network of hyphae |
| Coenocytic hypha | Continuous multinucleate hypha without cross walls |
| Septate hypha | Hypha divided by cross walls |

## 14.5 Nutrition In Fungi

Fungi are heterotrophic. They absorb soluble organic matter.

| Mode | Meaning | Example Or Context |
|---|---|---|
| Saprophytic | Feed on dead and decaying organic matter | Bread mould, decomposer fungi |
| Parasitic | Obtain nutrition from living hosts | Puccinia on wheat |
| Symbiotic | Live in mutual association | Lichens and mycorrhiza |

## 14.6 Symbiotic Fungi

Fungi may form mutual associations:

| Association | Partners | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Lichen | Fungus with algae or cyanobacteria | Food and shelter exchange |
| Mycorrhiza | Fungus with roots of higher plants | Mineral absorption support for plant, food for fungus |

## 15. Reproduction In Fungi

Fungi reproduce by vegetative, asexual, and sexual methods.

## 15.1 Vegetative Reproduction

| Method | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Fragmentation | Mycelium breaks into pieces, each piece grows |
| Fission | A cell divides into two |
| Budding | A small outgrowth forms and separates |

Yeast commonly reproduces by budding.

## 15.2 Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction in fungi occurs by spores.

| Asexual Spore | General Meaning |
|---|---|
| Conidia | Non-motile spores often produced externally |
| Sporangiospores | Spores produced inside sporangia |
| Zoospores | Motile spores |

## 15.3 Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction in fungi produces sexual spores such as:

- oospores;
- ascospores;
- basidiospores.

These spores are produced in distinct structures called fruiting bodies.

## 15.4 Three Steps In The Fungal Sexual Cycle

| Step | Name | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Plasmogamy | Cytoplasm of two compatible cells or gametes fuses |
| 2 | Karyogamy | The nuclei fuse |
| 3 | Meiosis | Reduction division produces haploid spores |

## 15.5 Dikaryotic Stage

In many fungi, plasmogamy is not immediately followed by karyogamy. The two haploid nuclei remain separate in the same cell.

This condition is called dikaryon and is written as n + n.

The phase is called dikaryophase.

This stage is especially important in ascomycetes and basidiomycetes.

## 15.6 Order Of Events In Fungal Sexual Reproduction

| Order | Event | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Compatible haploid cells come together | Beginning of sexual process |
| 2 | Plasmogamy occurs | Cytoplasm fuses |
| 3 | Dikaryotic stage may occur | Two unfused nuclei remain in one cell |
| 4 | Karyogamy occurs | Diploid nucleus forms |
| 5 | Meiosis occurs | Haploid spores form |

## 16. Classification Of Fungi

The kingdom Fungi is divided into classes based on:

- morphology of mycelium;
- mode of spore formation;
- type of fruiting body;
- sexual and asexual reproductive structures.

The four classes in this chapter are:

1. Phycomycetes
2. Ascomycetes
3. Basidiomycetes
4. Deuteromycetes

## 17. Phycomycetes

## 17.1 Main Features Of Phycomycetes

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Habitat | Aquatic habitats, decaying wood, moist places |
| Other occurrence | Some are obligate parasites on plants |
| Mycelium | Aseptate and coenocytic |
| Asexual reproduction | Zoospores or aplanospores |
| Spore production | Spores produced inside sporangia |
| Sexual reproduction | Zygospore formation |
| Gamete fusion | Isogamous, anisogamous, or oogamous |
| Examples | Mucor, Rhizopus, Albugo |

## 17.2 Important Terms In Phycomycetes

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Zoospore | Motile asexual spore |
| Aplanospore | Non-motile asexual spore |
| Sporangium | Structure in which spores are produced |
| Zygospore | Thick-walled sexual spore formed by gamete fusion |
| Isogamous | Gametes similar in shape and size |
| Anisogamous | Gametes dissimilar in size |
| Oogamous | Large non-motile female gamete and small motile male gamete |

## 18. Ascomycetes

Ascomycetes are commonly called sac fungi.

## 18.1 Main Features Of Ascomycetes

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Common name | Sac fungi |
| Body | Mostly multicellular; yeast is unicellular |
| Nutrition | Saprophytic, decomposer, parasitic, or coprophilous |
| Mycelium | Branched and septate |
| Asexual spores | Conidia |
| Asexual spore formation | Exogenously on conidiophores |
| Sexual spores | Ascospores |
| Sexual spore formation | Endogenously in asci |
| Fruiting body | Ascocarp |
| Examples | Aspergillus, Claviceps, Neurospora, Penicillium, Saccharomyces |

Although plants are generally autotrophic, some are partially heterotrophic.

## 23.2 Holozoic Nutrition

Animals usually show holozoic nutrition. This means food is ingested, digested internally, absorbed, and assimilated.

This differs from fungi, which absorb soluble food from outside their body.

## 24. Why Viruses, Viroids, Prions, And Lichens Are Separate Topics

Whittaker's five-kingdom system does not clearly place:

- viruses;
- viroids;
- prions;
- lichens.

Viruses, viroids, and prions are not cellular organisms. Lichens are associations of two partners rather than single independent organisms in the usual sense.

## 25. Viruses

Viruses are acellular infectious agents. They are not made of cells.

## 25.1 Why Viruses Are Difficult To Classify

Viruses show both living and non-living features.

| Living-Like Feature | Non-Living Feature |
|---|---|
| They reproduce inside host cells | They cannot reproduce independently |
| They contain genetic material | They lack cellular structure |
| They can mutate | Outside the host they are inert |
| They infect specific hosts | They have no cytoplasm or organelles |

Because of this, viruses are not placed in the five kingdoms.

## 25.2 Important Historical Points

| Scientist | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Dmitri Ivanowsky | Studied the agent causing tobacco mosaic disease; found it passed through bacteria-proof filters |
| M. W. Beijerinck | Called the infectious agent a virus and described it as an infectious living fluid |
| W. M. Stanley | Crystallised viruses and showed that the crystals largely consisted of protein |

## 25.3 Structure Of A Virus

A virus is a nucleoprotein. It contains:

- genetic material;
- protein coat.

The protein coat is called capsid. The capsid is made of smaller units called capsomeres.

Capsomeres are arranged in definite patterns such as helical or polyhedral forms.

## 25.4 Genetic Material In Viruses

| Point | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Genetic material | Either DNA or RNA |
| Both DNA and RNA together | Not found in one virus |
| Plant viruses | Usually single-stranded RNA |
| Animal viruses | Single-stranded RNA, double-stranded RNA, or double-stranded DNA |
| Bacteriophages | Usually double-stranded DNA |

## 25.5 Viral Lifestyle

Viruses are obligate parasites. They can multiply only inside living host cells.

Outside the host cell, they are inert and may have a crystalline form.

Inside the host cell, they take over the host machinery to produce more virus particles.

## 27. Prions

Prions are infectious agents made of abnormally folded proteins.

## 27.1 Features Of Prions

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Composition | Protein only |
| Genetic material | No DNA or RNA mentioned in this chapter |
| Disease type | Neurological diseases |

Prions are unusual because the infectious agent is not a nucleic acid-based organism. It is an abnormal protein form that can affect normal proteins.

## 28. Lichens

Lichens are symbiotic associations between an algal partner and a fungal partner.

## 28.1 Components Of A Lichen

| Component | Meaning | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Phycobiont | Algal component | Prepares food by photosynthesis |
| Mycobiont | Fungal component | Provides shelter and absorbs water and minerals |

## 28.2 Nature Of The Association

The association is mutually useful.

The algal partner prepares food. The fungal partner provides shelter and helps absorb water and mineral nutrients.

The association is so close that a lichen may look like one organism, although it contains two partners.

## 28.3 Lichens As Pollution Indicators

Lichens are good indicators of air pollution because they do not grow well in polluted areas.

If lichens are absent from an area where they would normally be expected, it may indicate pollution.

## 32. Exercise Answers

## 32.1 Explain How Classification Systems Changed Over Time

Early classification systems were based on simple visible characters and practical needs. Aristotle classified plants as herbs, shrubs, and trees, and divided animals based on the presence or absence of red blood.

Later, the two-kingdom system placed organisms into Plantae and Animalia. This system was simple but inadequate because it did not separate prokaryotes from eukaryotes, unicellular forms from multicellular forms, or photosynthetic organisms from non-photosynthetic organisms.

Whittaker's five-kingdom classification improved this by using cell structure, body organisation, nutrition, reproduction, and phylogenetic relationships. It separated Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia.

Classification continues to change because scientific understanding of cells, genetics, and evolutionary relationships keeps improving.

## 32.2 State Two Economically Important Uses Of Heterotrophic Bacteria

Two economically important uses of heterotrophic bacteria are:

1. They help convert milk into curd.
2. Some are used in the production of antibiotics.

Other valid points include decomposition and nitrogen fixation in legume roots.

## 32.3 State Two Economically Important Uses Of Archaebacteria

Two economically important points related to archaebacteria are:

1. Methanogens help produce methane, an important component of biogas.
2. Methanogens present in the gut of ruminants help break down organic matter in dung, supporting biogas production.

## 32.4 What Is The Nature Of Cell Walls In Diatoms?

Diatom cell walls are made of two thin overlapping halves that fit together like a soap box.

The walls are embedded with silica, making them hard and resistant to decay. Over time, these walls accumulate as diatomaceous earth.

## 32.5 What Do Algal Bloom And Red Tide Signify?

An algal bloom is a rapid increase in algae or algae-like organisms in a water body, often due to excess nutrients.

A red tide is caused by rapid multiplication of red dinoflagellates such as Gonyaulax. It can make seawater appear red and may release toxins that kill fish and other marine animals.

## 32.6 How Are Viroids Different From Viruses?

| Feature | Virus | Viroid |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Larger than viroids | Smaller than viruses |
| Composition | Genetic material plus protein coat | Free RNA only |
| Protein coat | Present | Absent |
| Genetic material | DNA or RNA | RNA |
| Example | Tobacco mosaic virus | Potato spindle tuber disease agent |

Viroids are simpler than viruses because they lack the protein coat found in viruses.

## 32.7 Describe The Four Major Groups Of Protozoa

| Group | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Amoeboid protozoans | Move and capture prey using pseudopodia; live in freshwater, seawater, or moist soil | Amoeba, Entamoeba |
| Flagellated protozoans | Have flagella; may be free-living or parasitic | Trypanosoma |
| Ciliated protozoans | Move actively using cilia; cilia also help feed | Paramoecium |
| Sporozoans | Have an infectious spore-like stage in the life cycle | Plasmodium |

## 32.8 Give Examples Of Partially Heterotrophic Plants

Some plants are partially heterotrophic even though plants are generally autotrophic.

Examples:

- Bladderwort is an insectivorous plant.
- Venus flytrap is an insectivorous plant.
- Cuscuta is a parasitic plant.

Insectivorous plants trap organisms to supplement their nutrition. Cuscuta obtains food from a host plant.

## 32.9 What Do Phycobiont And Mycobiont Signify?

In a lichen:

- phycobiont means the algal component;
- mycobiont means the fungal component.

The phycobiont prepares food by photosynthesis. The mycobiont provides shelter and absorbs water and mineral nutrients.

## 32.10 Give A Comparative Account Of Fungal Classes Based On Nutrition And Reproduction

| Class | Mode Of Nutrition | Mode Of Reproduction |
|---|---|---|
| Phycomycetes | Saprophytic or parasitic | Asexual by zoospores or aplanospores; sexual by zygospore |
| Ascomycetes | Saprophytic, decomposer, parasitic, or coprophilous | Asexual by conidia; sexual by ascospores in asci |
| Basidiomycetes | Saprophytic or parasitic | Vegetative reproduction by fragmentation; sexual by basidiospores on basidia |
| Deuteromycetes | Saprophytic or parasitic | Asexual by conidia; sexual stage not known in the classic grouping |

## 32.11 What Are The Characteristic Features Of Euglenoids?

Euglenoids are mostly freshwater protists found in stagnant water.

Their main features are:

- they lack a cell wall;
- they have a protein-rich pellicle;
- the pellicle makes the body flexible;
- they have two flagella, one short and one long;
- they are photosynthetic in sunlight;
- in the absence of sunlight, they behave like heterotrophs;
- their pigments are similar to those of higher plants;
- Euglena is an example.

## 32.12 Give A Brief Account Of Viruses With Structure, Genetic Material, And Diseases

Viruses are acellular infectious agents. They are obligate parasites and can multiply only inside living host cells.

Structurally, a virus is a nucleoprotein. It has genetic material surrounded by a protein coat called capsid. The capsid is made of smaller units called capsomeres.

The genetic material of a virus may be DNA or RNA, but never both in the same virus. Plant viruses usually have single-stranded RNA. Animal viruses may have single-stranded RNA, double-stranded RNA, or double-stranded DNA. Bacteriophages usually have double-stranded DNA.

Four viral diseases in humans are:

- mumps;
- smallpox;
- herpes;
- influenza.

AIDS is also caused by a virus.

## 32.13 Reasoned Note: Are Viruses Living Or Non-Living?

Viruses can be considered living only when they are inside a host cell, because they use host machinery to replicate and can show heredity and variation.

Outside the host cell, viruses behave like non-living particles. They are inert, acellular, and may exist in crystalline form.

Therefore, viruses are best described as borderline biological agents. They are not placed in Whittaker's five kingdoms because they lack cellular structure and independent metabolism.

## 33. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 33.1 Why Was Kingdom Monera Created?

Kingdom Monera was created to group all prokaryotic organisms separately. Bacteria and cyanobacteria lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, so they should not be placed with eukaryotic plants or animals.

## 33.2 Why Was Kingdom Protista Created?

Kingdom Protista was created for mostly unicellular eukaryotes. It includes organisms that may show plant-like, animal-like, or fungus-like features, such as diatoms, Euglena, Amoeba, and slime moulds.

## 33.3 Why Is Kingdom Fungi Separate From Plantae?

Fungi are heterotrophic and have chitinous cell walls. Plants are mostly autotrophic and have cellulose cell walls. Because of these major differences, fungi are placed in a separate kingdom.

## 33.4 Why Are Archaebacteria Able To Live In Extreme Habitats?

Archaebacteria have a different cell wall structure from other bacteria. This helps them survive in harsh conditions such as extreme salt, high temperature, acidity, and marshy habitats.

## 33.5 What Are Heterocysts?

Heterocysts are specialised cells found in some cyanobacteria. They help fix atmospheric nitrogen.

## 33.6 Why Are Diatoms Called Chief Producers In Oceans?

Diatoms are photosynthetic plankton and occur abundantly in marine environments. Because they produce large amounts of organic matter through photosynthesis, they are considered chief producers in oceans.

## 33.7 Why Are Dinoflagellates Important?

Dinoflagellates are important marine photosynthetic protists. Some cause red tides when they multiply rapidly. These red tides may release toxins that kill fish and other marine animals.

## 33.8 Why Are Slime Mould Spores Resistant?

Slime mould spores have true walls and can survive for many years under adverse conditions. They are dispersed by air currents.

## 33.9 Why Are Fungi Important As Decomposers?

Many fungi feed on dead organic matter. They break down complex organic material and return minerals to the environment, helping nutrient cycling.

## 33.10 Why Are Lichens Useful Pollution Indicators?

Lichens are sensitive to pollution and do not grow well in polluted areas. Their absence can indicate air pollution.
