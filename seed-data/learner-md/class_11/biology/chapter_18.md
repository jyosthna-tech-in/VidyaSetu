## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is about fast communication.

The basic flow is:

```text
stimulus -> receptor -> sensory pathway -> CNS -> motor pathway -> effector
```

At cell level, learn this flow:

```text
resting membrane -> depolarisation -> action potential -> repolarisation
```

At synapse level, learn this flow:

```text
impulse reaches axon terminal -> neurotransmitter release
-> receptor binding -> new signal in next cell
```

The chapter becomes easier if you keep two systems separate:

- neural system gives quick point-to-point coordination;
- endocrine system gives slower chemical coordination through hormones.

## 1. Coordination

Coordination is the process by which two or more organs interact and complement each other's functions.

The body needs coordination to maintain homeostasis.

Example during exercise:

- muscles require more energy;
- oxygen demand increases;
- breathing rate increases;
- heart rate increases;
- blood flow increases;
- kidneys and other organs adjust internal balance.

When exercise stops, these activities gradually return to normal.

## 2. Neural And Endocrine Coordination

The body uses two major coordination systems:

- neural system;
- endocrine system.

## 2.1 Neural System

The neural system provides rapid coordination.

It works through:

- neurons;
- nerves;
- point-to-point connections;
- electrical impulses;
- synaptic transmission.

It is fast and targeted.

## 2.2 Endocrine System

The endocrine system provides chemical coordination through hormones.

It is generally slower but can have longer-lasting effects.

Hormones travel through blood to target tissues.

## 2.3 Neural And Endocrine Systems Compared

| Feature | Neural System | Endocrine System |
|---|---|---|
| Signal | Nerve impulse and neurotransmitter | Hormone |
| Speed | Fast | Usually slower |
| Pathway | Neurons and synapses | Blood |
| Effect duration | Usually short | Often longer |
| Targeting | Point-to-point | Depends on receptors in target tissues |

## 3. Neural System In Animals

All animal neural systems are made of specialised cells called neurons.

Neurons can:

- detect stimuli;
- receive signals;
- transmit signals.

## 3.1 Hydra

Hydra has a simple neural network.

There is no complex brain.

Neurons are arranged as a net.

## 3.2 Insects

Insects have a better-organised neural system.

They have:

- brain;
- ganglia;
- neural tissues.

## 3.3 Vertebrates

Vertebrates have highly developed neural systems.

They have:

- brain;
- spinal cord;
- cranial nerves;
- spinal nerves;
- specialised sense organs.

## 4. Human Neural System

Human neural system has two major divisions:

1. central neural system;
2. peripheral neural system.

## 5. Central Neural System

CNS includes:

- brain;
- spinal cord.

It is the main site of:

- information processing;
- control;
- integration;
- decision-making for many responses.

## 6. Peripheral Neural System

PNS includes all nerves associated with brain and spinal cord.

It connects CNS with body tissues and organs.

PNS includes:

- cranial nerves;
- spinal nerves;
- peripheral ganglia;
- nerve fibres.

## 6.1 Afferent Fibres

Afferent fibres carry impulses from tissues and organs to CNS.

They are sensory pathways.

Direction:

```text
periphery -> CNS
```

## 6.2 Efferent Fibres

Efferent fibres carry regulatory impulses from CNS to peripheral tissues and organs.

They are motor pathways.

Direction:

```text
CNS -> periphery
```

## 7. Somatic And Autonomic Neural Systems

PNS has two major functional divisions:

- somatic neural system;
- autonomic neural system.

## 7.1 Somatic Neural System

Somatic neural system relays impulses from CNS to skeletal muscles.

It controls voluntary movements.

Example:

- moving arm;
- walking;
- writing;
- changing posture.

## 7.2 Autonomic Neural System

Autonomic neural system transmits impulses from CNS to involuntary organs and smooth muscles.

It regulates:

- heart;
- glands;
- smooth muscles;
- blood vessels;
- digestive activity;
- many visceral functions.

## 7.3 Sympathetic And Parasympathetic Divisions

Autonomic neural system has:

- sympathetic division;
- parasympathetic division.

In simple terms:

- sympathetic responses prepare body for stress or action;
- parasympathetic responses support rest, digestion, and maintenance.

## 8. Visceral Nervous System

The visceral nervous system is part of PNS.

It includes nerves, fibres, ganglia, and plexuses by which impulses travel:

- from CNS to viscera;
- from viscera to CNS.

Viscera means internal organs.

## 9. Neuron

Neuron is the structural and functional unit of the neural system.

A neuron has three major parts:

- cell body;
- dendrites;
- axon.

## 10. Cell Body

The cell body contains:

- cytoplasm;
- nucleus;
- typical organelles;
- Nissl granules.

Nissl granules are important granular bodies in neurons.

## 11. Dendrites

Dendrites are short, branched fibres projecting from cell body.

They contain Nissl granules.

Function:

```text
carry impulses toward cell body
```

## 12. Axon

Axon is a long fibre.

Its distal end is branched.

Each branch ends in a synaptic knob.

Function:

```text
carry impulses away from cell body
```

Axon may transmit impulse to:

- another neuron;
- muscle;
- gland;
- synapse;
- neuromuscular junction.

## 13. Synaptic Knob

Synaptic knob is a bulb-like axon terminal.

It contains synaptic vesicles.

Synaptic vesicles contain neurotransmitters.

These chemicals help transmit impulses across chemical synapses.

## 14. Types Of Neurons By Processes

## 14.1 Multipolar Neuron

Multipolar neuron has:

- one axon;
- two or more dendrites.

Example location:

- cerebral cortex.

## 14.2 Bipolar Neuron

Bipolar neuron has:

- one axon;
- one dendrite.

Example location:

- retina of eye.

## 14.3 Unipolar Neuron

Unipolar neuron has:

- cell body with one axon only.

It is usually found in embryonic stage.

## 15. Myelinated And Non-Myelinated Axons

## 15.1 Myelinated Nerve Fibre

Myelinated nerve fibres are enveloped by Schwann cells.

Schwann cells form myelin sheath around axon.

Gaps between adjacent myelin segments are nodes of Ranvier.

Myelinated fibres occur in spinal and cranial nerves.

Myelin increases conduction speed.

## 15.2 Non-Myelinated Nerve Fibre

Non-myelinated nerve fibre is enclosed by a Schwann cell.

The Schwann cell does not form a myelin sheath around the axon.

These fibres occur commonly in autonomic and somatic neural systems in the raw chapter wording.

Conduction is generally slower than in myelinated fibres.

## 15.3 Myelinated And Non-Myelinated Fibres Compared

| Feature | Myelinated Fibre | Non-Myelinated Fibre |
|---|---|---|
| Myelin sheath | Present | Absent |
| Schwann cell role | Forms myelin | Encloses axon without myelin |
| Nodes of Ranvier | Present | Absent |
| Conduction speed | Faster | Slower |
| Common location | Spinal and cranial nerves | Autonomic fibres and some somatic fibres |

## 16. Resting Neuron Is Polarised

Neurons are excitable because their membranes can maintain and change electrical differences.

At rest, the axonal membrane is polarised.

This means:

```text
outside is positive;
inside is negative.
```

## 17. Ion Distribution At Rest

At resting state:

- axonal membrane is more permeable to K+;
- axonal membrane is nearly impermeable to Na+;
- negatively charged proteins remain inside axoplasm.

Inside axon:

- high K+;
- high negatively charged proteins;
- low Na+.

Outside axon:

- low K+;
- high Na+.

## 18. Sodium-Potassium Pump

The sodium-potassium pump maintains ionic gradients.

It actively transports:

```text
3 Na+ out
2 K+ in
```

This helps maintain resting potential.

## 19. Resting Potential

Resting potential is the electrical potential difference across the resting neural membrane.

At rest:

- outer surface is positively charged;
- inner surface is negatively charged.

This is polarisation.

## 20. Depolarisation And Action Potential

When a stimulus reaches a site on the polarised membrane, the membrane becomes freely permeable to Na+ for a very short time.

Na+ rushes inward.

This reverses polarity.

At that site:

- inside becomes positive;
- outside becomes negative.

This is depolarisation.

The electrical potential difference during this event is action potential.

Action potential is the nerve impulse.

## 21. Conduction Of Nerve Impulse

After one site depolarises, current flows to the next site.

The next site depolarises.

Then the next site after that depolarises.

Thus the action potential travels along the axon.

Simple flow:

```text
site A depolarises -> local current affects site B
-> site B depolarises -> impulse moves forward
```

## 22. Repolarisation

Na+ permeability increases only briefly.

Soon K+ permeability increases.

K+ diffuses outward.

This restores resting polarity at the excited site.

The membrane becomes responsive to another stimulus.

This return toward resting state is repolarisation.

## 23. Impulse Conduction Summary

| Stage | Main Event |
|---|---|
| Resting state | Outside positive, inside negative |
| Stimulus | Na+ channels open |
| Depolarisation | Na+ enters, inside becomes positive |
| Local current | Adjacent membrane region is stimulated |
| Propagation | Depolarisation moves along axon |
| Repolarisation | K+ exits, resting state returns |

## 24. Synapse

A synapse is a junction between:

- one neuron and another neuron;
- neuron and muscle;
- neuron and gland.

In neuron-to-neuron synapse, it is formed by:

- pre-synaptic membrane;
- post-synaptic membrane.

There may or may not be a gap called synaptic cleft.

## 25. Electrical Synapse

At electrical synapse, pre-synaptic and post-synaptic membranes are very close.

Electrical current flows directly from one neuron to another.

Transmission is similar to impulse conduction along an axon.

It is faster than chemical synapse.

Electrical synapses are rare in humans.

## 26. Chemical Synapse

At chemical synapse, the two membranes are separated by synaptic cleft.

Neurotransmitters carry the signal across the cleft.

Chemical synapses are common in humans.

## 27. Chemical Synaptic Transmission

Sequence:

1. Action potential reaches axon terminal.
2. Synaptic vesicles move toward pre-synaptic membrane.
3. Vesicles fuse with membrane.
4. Neurotransmitter is released into synaptic cleft.
5. Neurotransmitter binds receptors on post-synaptic membrane.
6. Ion channels open.
7. A new potential forms in post-synaptic neuron.

The new potential may be:

- excitatory;
- inhibitory.

## 28. Electrical And Chemical Synapses Compared

| Feature | Electrical Synapse | Chemical Synapse |
|---|---|---|
| Gap | Very small or direct contact | Synaptic cleft present |
| Signal | Electrical current | Neurotransmitter |
| Speed | Faster | Slower |
| Direction | Often direct current flow | Usually one-way in typical synapse |
| Commonness in humans | Rare | Common |

## 29. Central Neural System

The brain is the central information processing organ.

It acts as command and control system.

It controls:

- voluntary movement;
- balance;
- vital involuntary organs;
- thermoregulation;
- hunger;
- thirst;
- circadian rhythm;
- endocrine activity;
- behaviour;
- vision;
- hearing;
- speech;
- memory;
- intelligence;
- emotions;
- thoughts.

## 30. Protection Of Brain

Brain is protected by:

- skull;
- cranial meninges.

## 30.1 Meninges

Cranial meninges have three layers:

| Layer | Position |
|---|---|
| Dura mater | Outer layer |
| Arachnoid | Thin middle layer |
| Pia mater | Inner layer in contact with brain tissue |

## 31. Major Parts Of Brain

The brain has three major parts:

- forebrain;
- midbrain;
- hindbrain.

Brain stem includes:

- midbrain;
- pons;
- medulla oblongata.

Brain stem connects brain and spinal cord.

## 32. Forebrain

Forebrain consists of:

- cerebrum;
- thalamus;
- hypothalamus.

## 33. Cerebrum

Cerebrum forms the major part of human brain.

It is the most developed part of the human brain.

A deep cleft divides it into:

- left cerebral hemisphere;
- right cerebral hemisphere.

The hemispheres are connected by corpus callosum.

## 33.1 Cerebral Cortex

Cerebral cortex is the outer layer covering cerebral hemispheres.

It has prominent folds.

It is called grey matter because neuron cell bodies are concentrated there.

## 33.2 Cerebral Cortex Functions

The cortex contains:

- motor areas;
- sensory areas;
- association areas.

Association areas are involved in complex functions such as:

- intersensory association;
- memory;
- communication;
- reasoning in broader understanding;
- learning.

## 33.3 White Matter

Inner fibres of tracts are covered with myelin sheath.

They appear opaque white.

Therefore this region is called white matter.

## 34. Thalamus

Thalamus is wrapped by cerebrum.

It is a major coordinating centre for sensory and motor signalling.

It acts as an important relay station.

## 35. Hypothalamus

Hypothalamus lies at the base of thalamus.

It contains centres controlling:

- body temperature;
- urge for eating;
- urge for drinking.

It also contains neurosecretory cells.

These cells secrete hypothalamic hormones.

## 35.1 Master Clock

The central neural system's master clock is in the hypothalamus, especially the suprachiasmatic nucleus.

It helps regulate circadian rhythms.

## 36. Limbic System

The limbic system includes inner parts of cerebral hemispheres and associated deep structures such as:

- amygdala;
- hippocampus.

Along with hypothalamus, it regulates:

- sexual behaviour;
- emotional reactions;
- excitement;
- pleasure;
- rage;
- fear;
- motivation.

## 37. Midbrain

Midbrain lies between:

- thalamus and hypothalamus of forebrain;
- pons of hindbrain.

A canal called cerebral aqueduct passes through midbrain.

Dorsal midbrain has four rounded swellings called corpora quadrigemina.

Midbrain helps process and integrate visual, auditory, and other sensory information in broad terms.

## 38. Hindbrain

Hindbrain includes:

- pons;
- cerebellum;
- medulla oblongata.

## 38.1 Pons

Pons contains fibre tracts.

These tracts interconnect different regions of brain.

## 38.2 Cerebellum

Cerebellum has highly convoluted surface.

This provides more space for neurons.

It helps coordinate:

- posture;
- balance;
- fine movement;
- learned motor activity.

## 38.3 Medulla Oblongata

Medulla connects to spinal cord.

It contains centres controlling:

- respiration;
- cardiovascular reflexes;
- gastric secretions.

## 40. Common Misconceptions

## 40.1 Misconception: Neural And Endocrine Systems Work Separately

Correct idea:

They work together to coordinate body functions.

Neural system is fast and targeted.

Endocrine system is chemical and often longer-lasting.

## 40.2 Misconception: Dendrites Carry Impulses Away From Cell Body

Correct idea:

Dendrites carry impulses toward cell body.

Axons carry impulses away from cell body.

## 40.3 Misconception: Resting Neuron Has No Electrical Difference

Correct idea:

Resting neuron is polarised.

Outside is positive and inside is negative.

## 40.4 Misconception: Na+ Moves Out During Depolarisation

Correct idea:

Na+ moves into the axon during depolarisation.

## 40.5 Misconception: Chemical Synapse Is Faster Than Electrical Synapse

Correct idea:

Electrical synapse is faster.

Chemical synapse is more common in humans.

## 40.6 Misconception: Cerebellum Is The Largest Part Of Human Brain

Correct idea:

Cerebrum is the major and most developed part of human brain.

## 41. Solved Concept Checks

## 41.1 What Is Coordination?

Coordination is interaction of two or more organs so that their functions complement each other.

It maintains homeostasis and efficient body function.

## 41.2 What Is The Difference Between CNS And PNS?

CNS includes brain and spinal cord.

PNS includes nerves associated with CNS.

CNS processes information.

PNS carries impulses to and from CNS.

## 41.3 What Is Polarisation?

Polarisation is the resting state of neuron membrane where outside is positive and inside is negative.

It is maintained by ion distribution and sodium-potassium pump.

## 41.4 What Is Depolarisation?

Depolarisation is reversal of membrane polarity when Na+ enters the axon.

Inside becomes positive and outside becomes negative at the stimulated site.

## 41.5 What Is Action Potential?

Action potential is the electrical potential difference during depolarisation.

It is the nerve impulse.

## 41.6 How Does Repolarisation Occur?

After brief Na+ influx, K+ permeability increases.

K+ moves out.

Resting polarity is restored.

## 41.7 What Happens At Chemical Synapse?

Impulse reaches axon terminal.

Neurotransmitter is released into synaptic cleft.

It binds receptors on post-synaptic membrane.

Ion channels open and a new potential is generated.

## 41.8 Which Brain Part Is Most Developed?

Cerebrum is the most developed part of human brain.

## 41.9 Which CNS Part Acts As Master Clock?

The hypothalamus acts as master clock region, especially the suprachiasmatic nucleus.

It regulates circadian rhythms.

## 43.2 Neural Coordination

Neural coordination is rapid coordination through neurons and nerves.

## 43.3 Forebrain Note

Forebrain includes cerebrum, thalamus, and hypothalamus.

## 43.6 Synapse Note

Synapse is junction between two neurons or neuron and effector.

Electrical synapses transmit current directly and are faster.

Chemical synapses use neurotransmitters released into synaptic cleft.

## 43.7 Role Of Na+ In Action Potential

At resting state, Na+ concentration is high outside axon.

When stimulated, membrane permeability to Na+ increases.

Na+ rushes into axon.

This reverses polarity and generates action potential.

Thus Na+ influx causes depolarisation.

## 43.8 Chemical Synapse Mechanism

An impulse reaches the pre-synaptic axon terminal.

Synaptic vesicles fuse with membrane.

Neurotransmitter is released into synaptic cleft.

It binds receptors on post-synaptic membrane.

Ion channels open.

A new excitatory or inhibitory potential develops.

## 46. Practice Answers

## 46.1 Most Developed Brain Part

Cerebrum is the most developed part of the human brain.

It forms the major part of the brain and contains sensory, motor, and association areas.

## 46.2 Master Clock

The master clock is located in the hypothalamus, especially the suprachiasmatic nucleus.

It helps regulate circadian rhythms.

## 46.3 Polarisation

At rest, the neuron membrane is more permeable to K+ and nearly impermeable to Na+.

The sodium-potassium pump moves 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in.

Outside becomes positive and inside becomes negative.

This is polarisation.

## 46.4 Depolarisation

During stimulation, Na+ channels open.

Na+ enters the axon.

Inside becomes positive and outside becomes negative at that site.

This is depolarisation.

## 46.5 Chemical Synapse

Chemical synapse uses neurotransmitters.

Impulse causes vesicles to release neurotransmitter into synaptic cleft.

The neurotransmitter binds receptors and generates a new potential in the post-synaptic cell.
