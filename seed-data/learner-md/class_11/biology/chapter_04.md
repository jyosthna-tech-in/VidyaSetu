## How To Study This Chapter

Animal Kingdom is a comparison-heavy chapter. The best method is to first master the classification criteria, then use those criteria to identify each phylum.

Do not begin by memorising examples. Start with these questions:

1. What is the level of organisation?
2. What type of symmetry is present?
3. Is the animal diploblastic or triploblastic?
4. Is the coelom absent, false, or true?
5. Is segmentation present?
6. Is a notochord present?
7. What special structure makes the group identifiable?

Once these questions are clear, the phyla become much easier to separate.

## 1. Why Animal Classification Is Needed

More than a million animal species have been described. Animals vary greatly in form, body plan, habitat, nutrition, movement, and reproduction. Without classification, it would be difficult to identify newly discovered animals or understand their relationships.

Classification helps by:

- arranging animal diversity into meaningful groups;
- identifying new species systematically;
- comparing body plans;
- understanding evolutionary relationships;
- predicting features from group identity;
- reducing confusion caused by superficial similarity.

For example, a worm-like body alone is not enough for classification. A flatworm, roundworm, annelid, and hemichordate can all look worm-like, but they differ in coelom, segmentation, organ systems, and developmental characters.

## 2. Basis Of Animal Classification

Animal classification uses fundamental features such as:

- level of organisation;
- symmetry;
- germ layers;
- coelom;
- segmentation;
- notochord;
- digestive system;
- circulatory system;
- respiratory system;
- reproductive pattern;
- special diagnostic structures.

## 3. Levels Of Organisation

Although all animals are multicellular, they do not all show the same organisation of cells.

## 3.1 Cellular Level

At the cellular level, cells are arranged as loose aggregates. True tissues are absent.

Example: Porifera.

Sponges show some division of labour among cells, but their cells are not organised into true tissues.

## 3.2 Tissue Level

At the tissue level, similar cells performing the same function are arranged into tissues.

Example: Cnidaria.

## 3.3 Organ Level

At the organ level, tissues combine to form organs specialised for particular functions.

Example: Platyhelminthes.

## 3.4 Organ-System Level

At the organ-system level, organs work together as functional systems.

Examples include annelids, arthropods, molluscs, echinoderms, and chordates.

## 3.5 Comparison Of Organisation Levels

| Level | Meaning | Example Group |
|---|---|---|
| Cellular | Cells form loose aggregates | Porifera |
| Tissue | Cells form tissues | Cnidaria, Ctenophora |
| Organ | Tissues form organs | Platyhelminthes |
| Organ-system | Organs form systems | Annelida onward in many groups |

## 4. Digestive And Circulatory Patterns

## 4.1 Digestive System

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Incomplete digestive system | One opening acts as both mouth and anus |
| Complete digestive system | Separate mouth and anus are present |

An incomplete digestive system is seen in groups such as cnidarians and flatworms. A complete digestive system is seen in many higher groups.

## 4.2 Circulatory System

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Open circulatory system | Blood is pumped out of the heart and directly bathes tissues |
| Closed circulatory system | Blood flows through vessels such as arteries, veins, and capillaries |

Open circulation is found in arthropods and molluscs. Closed circulation is found in annelids and chordates.

## 5. Symmetry

Symmetry refers to how body parts are arranged.

## 5.1 Asymmetry

An animal is asymmetrical if no plane passing through the centre divides it into equal halves.

Example: many sponges.

## 5.2 Radial Symmetry

An animal is radially symmetrical if any plane passing through the central axis divides the body into two equal halves.

Examples:

- cnidarians;
- ctenophores;
- adult echinoderms.

Radial symmetry is useful in animals that are sessile, slow-moving, or interact with the environment from many directions.

## 5.3 Bilateral Symmetry

An animal is bilaterally symmetrical if only one plane divides it into equal left and right halves.

Examples:

- annelids;
- arthropods;
- molluscs;
- chordates.

Bilateral symmetry is usually linked with directed movement and development of a head region.

## 6. Germ Layers

During embryonic development, body tissues arise from germ layers.

## 6.1 Diploblastic Animals

Diploblastic animals have two germ layers:

- ectoderm;
- endoderm.

A jelly-like layer called mesoglea may be present between them.

Example: cnidarians.

## 6.2 Triploblastic Animals

Triploblastic animals have three germ layers:

- ectoderm;
- mesoderm;
- endoderm.

Animals from Platyhelminthes to Chordata are triploblastic.

## 7. Coelom

The presence, absence, or type of body cavity is important in classification.

## 7.1 Acoelomate

Acoelomates lack a body cavity.

Example: Platyhelminthes.

## 7.2 Pseudocoelomate

Pseudocoelomates have a body cavity, but it is not completely lined by mesoderm.

Example: Aschelminthes.

## 7.3 Coelomate

Coelomates have a true body cavity lined by mesoderm.

Examples:

- annelids;
- molluscs;
- arthropods;
- echinoderms;
- hemichordates;
- chordates.

## 7.4 Why Coelom Matters

A true coelom provides space for internal organs, allows organ systems to develop more complexity, and helps separate the body wall from the gut wall. It is therefore a major feature in animal classification.

## 8. Segmentation

Segmentation means division of the body into repeated parts.

Metameric segmentation means external and internal segmentation with serial repetition of at least some organs.

Example: earthworm.

Segmentation first becomes clearly seen in Annelida.

## 9. Notochord

Notochord is a mesodermally derived rod-like structure formed on the dorsal side during embryonic development in some animals.

Animals with a notochord are called chordates.

Animals without a notochord are called non-chordates.

Non-chordates include Porifera to Echinodermata, and Hemichordata is treated separately under non-chordates in this chapter.

## 10. Quick Classification Logic

| Feature Asked | If Present | If Absent Or Different |
|---|---|---|
| Notochord | Chordata | Non-chordate groups |
| True coelom | Annelida and higher coelomate groups | Acoelomate or pseudocoelomate groups |
| Metamerism | Annelida and Arthropoda show clear segmentation | Many lower groups lack it |
| Cnidoblasts | Cnidaria | Other phyla |
| Comb plates | Ctenophora | Other phyla |
| Jointed appendages | Arthropoda | Other phyla |
| Radula | Mollusca | Other phyla |
| Water vascular system | Echinodermata | Other phyla |

## 11. Phylum Porifera

Members of Porifera are commonly called sponges.

## 11.1 Main Features Of Porifera

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Common name | Sponges |
| Habitat | Mostly marine; some freshwater |
| Symmetry | Mostly asymmetrical |
| Organisation | Cellular level |
| Body pores | Present |
| Canal system | Present |
| Digestion | Intracellular |
| Skeleton | Spicules or spongin fibres |
| Sexes | Not separate in most forms |
| Fertilisation | Internal |
| Development | Indirect with larval stage |

## 11.2 Canal System In Sponges

Water enters through small pores called ostia.

It moves into a central cavity called spongocoel.

It exits through a larger opening called osculum.

This water transport helps in:

- food collection;
- respiratory exchange;
- waste removal.

## 11.3 Choanocytes

Choanocytes are collar cells that line the spongocoel and canals.

They help generate water current and capture food particles.

## 11.4 Reproduction In Porifera

Sponges reproduce:

- asexually by fragmentation;
- sexually by formation of gametes.

Most sponges are hermaphrodites, meaning eggs and sperms may be produced by the same individual.

## 11.5 Examples Of Porifera

Examples:

- Sycon;
- Spongilla;
- Euspongia.

In such forms:

## 17. Phylum Arthropoda

Arthropoda is the largest phylum of Animalia.

It includes insects and many other forms. More than two-thirds of all named animal species are arthropods.

## 17.1 Main Features Of Arthropoda

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Organisation | Organ-system level |
| Symmetry | Bilateral |
| Germ layers | Triploblastic |
| Segmentation | Present |
| Coelom | Coelomate |

| Body regions | Head, thorax, abdomen in many forms |
| Appendages | Jointed |
| Circulation | Open |
| Excretion | Malpighian tubules |
| Sexes | Mostly separate |
| Fertilisation | Usually internal |
| Development | Direct or indirect |

## 17.2 Respiratory Organs In Arthropods

Arthropods may respire through:

- gills;
- book gills;
- book lungs;
- tracheal system.

## 17.3 Sensory Organs

Arthropods may have:

- antennae;
- compound eyes;
- simple eyes;
- statocysts for balance.

## 17.4 Why Arthropods Are So Successful

Important reasons include:

- chitinous exoskeleton for protection and water conservation;
- jointed appendages for diverse movement and feeding;
- body segmentation and specialisation;
- wide range of respiratory organs;
- well-developed sensory organs;
- internal fertilisation in many forms;
- high reproductive potential;
- metamorphosis in many insects;
- ability to occupy land, water, and air habitats.

## 18.2 Mantle And Mantle Cavity

The space between mantle and visceral hump is the mantle cavity.

The mantle cavity contains feather-like gills that help in respiration and excretion.

## 18.3 Radula

Radula is a file-like rasping organ used in feeding.

It is a key feature of molluscs.

## 21. Phylum Chordata

Chordates are animals that show a notochord at least during some stage of development.

## 21.1 Fundamental Features Of Chordates

Chordates have:

- notochord;
- dorsal hollow nerve cord;
- paired pharyngeal gill slits;
- post-anal tail;
- closed circulatory system.

They are bilaterally symmetrical, triploblastic, coelomate animals with organ-system level of organisation.

## 21.2 Chordates Versus Non-Chordates

| Feature | Chordates | Non-Chordates |
|---|---|---|
| Notochord | Present at least during some stage | Absent |
| Nerve cord | Dorsal, hollow, single | Usually ventral, solid, double |
| Pharyngeal gill slits | Present at least during some stage | Absent |
| Heart | Ventral | Dorsal if present |
| Post-anal tail | Present at least in some stage | Absent |

## 21.3 Subphyla Of Chordata

Phylum Chordata is divided into:

1. Urochordata or Tunicata
2. Cephalochordata
3. Vertebrata

Urochordata and Cephalochordata are often called protochordates. They are exclusively marine.

## 21.4 Urochordata

In Urochordata, the notochord is present only in the larval tail.

Examples:

- Ascidia;
- Salpa;
- Doliolum.

## 21.5 Cephalochordata

In Cephalochordata, the notochord extends from head to tail region and persists throughout life.

Example:

- Branchiostoma, also called Amphioxus or Lancelet.

## 22. Vertebrata

Vertebrates possess a notochord during the embryonic period.

In adults, the notochord is replaced by a cartilaginous or bony vertebral column.

Therefore:

- all vertebrates are chordates;
- all chordates are not vertebrates.

## 22.1 General Features Of Vertebrates

Vertebrates have:

- vertebral column in adult stage;
- ventral muscular heart;
- kidneys for excretion and osmoregulation;
- paired appendages as fins or limbs;
- closed circulatory system.

## 22.2 Major Vertebrate Groups

| Division Or Superclass | Group | Main Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Agnatha | Cyclostomata | Jawless vertebrates |
| Gnathostomata | Pisces | Jawed vertebrates with fins |
| Gnathostomata | Tetrapoda | Jawed vertebrates with limbs |

Pisces includes:

- Chondrichthyes;
- Osteichthyes.

Tetrapoda includes:

- Amphibia;
- Reptilia;
- Aves;
- Mammalia.

## 33. Exercise Answers

## 33.1 Difficulties Without Common Fundamental Features

If common fundamental features are not used, animal classification becomes confusing and unreliable.

The main difficulties would be:

- animals with similar outward appearance but different body plans may be grouped together;
- closely related animals may be separated because of superficial differences;
- newly discovered species would be hard to place systematically;
- evolutionary relationships would remain unclear;
- important features such as coelom, symmetry, germ layers, and notochord would be ignored;
- classification would become based on guesswork rather than biological organisation.

For example, a flatworm, roundworm, and annelid may all look worm-like, but they differ in coelom, segmentation, and organ-system development.

## 33.2 Steps To Classify A Given Specimen

To classify an animal specimen, follow these steps:

1. Observe whether it is multicellular and animal-like.
2. Check the level of organisation: cellular, tissue, organ, or organ-system.
3. Identify symmetry: asymmetrical, radial, or bilateral.
4. Determine germ layers if developmental information is available: diploblastic or triploblastic.
5. Check the body cavity: acoelomate, pseudocoelomate, or coelomate.
6. Look for segmentation.
7. Check for notochord or chordate characters.
8. Observe special structures such as pores, cnidoblasts, comb plates, flame cells, jointed appendages, radula, water vascular system, or vertebral column.
9. Compare the features with known phyla and classes.
10. Use diagnostic examples and keys to confirm the group.

## 33.3 Usefulness Of Coelom In Animal Classification

Coelom is highly useful in classification because it reflects internal body organisation.

Animals can be grouped as:

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acoelomate | Body cavity absent | Platyhelminthes |
| Pseudocoelomate | False body cavity not fully lined by mesoderm | Aschelminthes |
| Coelomate | True body cavity lined by mesoderm | Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca, Echinodermata, Chordata |

The presence of coelom allows better organ development and separation of gut and body wall. It is therefore an important evolutionary and classification feature.

## 33.4 Distinguish Between Intracellular And Extracellular Digestion

| Feature | Intracellular Digestion | Extracellular Digestion |
|---|---|---|
| Site | Inside cells | In a cavity or digestive tract |
| Complexity | Simpler | More advanced |
| Seen in | Sponges and part of cnidarian digestion | Cnidarians and higher animals |
| Food handling | Food particles are engulfed by cells | Enzymes digest food outside cells |

In sponges, digestion is intracellular. In cnidarians, digestion is both extracellular and intracellular.

## 33.5 Difference Between Direct And Indirect Development

| Feature | Direct Development | Indirect Development |
|---|---|---|
| Larval stage | Absent | Present |
| Young one | Resembles adult | Does not resemble adult |
| Metamorphosis | Not required | Required |
| Example context | Many reptiles, birds, mammals | Many amphibians, echinoderms, some invertebrates |

In indirect development, the larva is morphologically distinct from the adult.

## 33.6 Peculiar Features Of Parasitic Platyhelminthes

Parasitic platyhelminthes show features that help them live inside hosts.

Important features include:

- dorsoventrally flattened body;
- hooks and suckers for attachment;
- nutrient absorption through body surface in some forms;
- reduced or modified digestive structures in some parasites;
- flame cells for excretion and osmoregulation;
- hermaphroditic condition in many forms;
- internal fertilisation;
- high reproductive output;
- complex development with larval stages.

Examples include Taenia and Fasciola.

## 33.7 Reasons For Arthropods Being The Largest Animal Group

Arthropods form the largest animal phylum due to several successful features:

- jointed appendages for walking, feeding, sensing, and mating;
- chitinous exoskeleton for protection and support;
- segmentation with specialised body regions;
- diverse respiratory organs for different habitats;
- well-developed sense organs;
- open circulation supporting active lifestyles;
- high reproductive capacity;
- metamorphosis in many insects, reducing competition between young and adult stages;
- ability to live in aquatic, terrestrial, and aerial environments.

## 33.8 Water Vascular System Is Characteristic Of Which Group?

Correct answer: Echinodermata.

The water vascular system helps echinoderms in locomotion, food capture, transport of food, and respiration.

## 33.9 Justify: All Vertebrates Are Chordates But All Chordates Are Not Vertebrates

All vertebrates show chordate characters at least during embryonic life, such as notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, and pharyngeal gill slits. Therefore, all vertebrates are chordates.

However, not all chordates develop a vertebral column. Urochordates and cephalochordates are chordates but not vertebrates.

In vertebrates, the embryonic notochord is replaced by a cartilaginous or bony vertebral column in the adult.

## 33.10 Importance Of Air Bladder In Pisces

Air bladder is important in bony fishes because it regulates buoyancy.

It helps the fish stay at a particular depth without continuous swimming. This saves energy and prevents sinking.

Cartilaginous fishes lack an air bladder, so many must swim constantly to avoid sinking.

## 33.11 Modifications In Birds That Help In Flight

Birds show several flight adaptations:

- forelimbs modified into wings;
- feathers present;
- streamlined body;
- hollow bones with air cavities;
- powerful flight muscles;
- lungs with air sacs;
- four-chambered heart;
- warm-blooded metabolism;
- beak instead of heavy toothed jaws in modern birds;
- hind limbs adapted for landing, walking, swimming, or perching.

## 33.12 Can The Number Of Eggs Or Young Ones Produced By Oviparous And Viviparous Mothers Be Equal?

Usually, the number is not equal.

Oviparous animals often produce many eggs because the eggs develop outside the mother's body and face greater risk from predators and environmental conditions.

Viviparous animals usually produce fewer young ones because the young develop inside the mother's body, receive better protection, and require more parental investment.

So, while exceptions can occur, in general oviparous animals produce more offspring than viviparous animals.

## 33.13 Segmentation In The Body Is First Observed In Which Group?

Correct answer: Annelida.

Annelids show metameric segmentation, where the body is divided into repeated segments.

## 34. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 34.1 Why Are Sponges Considered Primitive Multicellular Animals?

Sponges are multicellular but show only cellular level of organisation. Their cells perform some division of labour, but true tissues and organs are absent.

## 34.2 Why Are Cnidarians Named So?

Cnidarians have special stinging cells called cnidoblasts or cnidocytes. These contain nematocysts used for anchorage, defence, and prey capture.

## 34.3 Why Are Ctenophores Easy To Identify?

Ctenophores have eight external rows of ciliated comb plates used for locomotion. They also show well-marked bioluminescence.

## 34.4 Why Are Flatworms Called Acoelomates?

Flatworms lack a body cavity between body wall and gut wall. Therefore, they are called acoelomates.

## 34.5 Why Are Roundworms Pseudocoelomates?

Roundworms have a body cavity, but it is not completely lined by mesoderm. Therefore, the cavity is called pseudocoelom.

## 34.6 Why Are Annelids More Advanced Than Roundworms?

Annelids have a true coelom, metameric segmentation, closed circulatory system, and nephridia. Roundworms are pseudocoelomate and unsegmented.

## 34.7 What Is The Key Feature Of Arthropoda?

The key feature is jointed appendages. Arthropods also have a chitinous exoskeleton and segmented body.

## 34.8 What Is The Key Feature Of Mollusca?

Molluscs usually have a soft unsegmented body, mantle, mantle cavity, muscular foot, and radula.

## 34.9 What Is The Key Feature Of Echinodermata?

The key feature is the water vascular system.

## 34.10 Why Is Hemichordata Not Placed Under Chordata Here?

Hemichordates have a stomochord, not a true notochord. Therefore, they are treated as a separate non-chordate phylum in this chapter.
