This chapter is easier if you separate three linked ideas.

First:

```text

```

Second:

```text

```

Third:

```text
blood transports gases between lungs and tissues
```

Keep the direction clear:

```text
O2: atmosphere -> alveoli -> blood -> tissues -> cells
CO2: cells -> tissues -> blood -> alveoli -> atmosphere
```

Also remember:

- breathing is ventilation;
- cellular respiration is energy release inside cells;
- the respiratory system connects external air with blood;
- the circulatory system connects blood with tissues.

## 1. Why Gas Exchange Is Needed

Cells need oxygen for aerobic metabolism.

Oxygen helps cells release energy from molecules such as:

- glucose;
- amino acids;
- fatty acids.

These catabolic reactions produce carbon dioxide.

Carbon dioxide must be removed because excess CO2 affects blood pH and body function.

Therefore the body must continuously:

- supply O2 to cells;
- remove CO2 from cells.

## 2. Breathing And Cellular Respiration

Breathing is the exchange of atmospheric oxygen with carbon dioxide-rich air from the lungs.

Cellular respiration is the intracellular oxidation of food molecules to release energy.

They are connected, but they are not the same.

| Feature | Breathing | Cellular Respiration |
|---|---|---|
| Level | Organ-system level | Cellular level |
| Main site | Lungs and air passages | Cells, especially mitochondria |
| Main role | Gas exchange with atmosphere | ATP production |
| O2 role | Taken into body | Used as final acceptor in aerobic respiration |
| CO2 role | Removed from body | Produced during catabolism |

## 3. Respiratory Organs In Animals

Different animals use different respiratory structures.

The structure depends on:

- habitat;
- body size;
- level of organisation;
- metabolic demand;
- body surface availability.

## 3.1 Simple Diffusion Across Body Surface

Lower invertebrates can exchange gases through their body surface.

Examples:

- sponges;
- coelenterates;
- flatworms.

This works because their bodies are thin or organised so diffusion distances are small.

## 3.2 Moist Cuticle

Earthworms exchange gases through moist cuticle.

The body surface must remain moist because gases diffuse through a thin water film.

## 3.3 Tracheal System In Insects

Insects have a network of tracheal tubes.

These tubes carry air directly inside the body.

The site of gas exchange in insects is the tracheal system, especially fine tracheoles near tissues.

## 3.4 Gills

Many aquatic animals use gills.

Gills are vascularised structures suited for gas exchange in water.

Examples:

- many aquatic arthropods;
- many molluscs;
- fishes.

This is called branchial respiration.

## 3.5 Lungs

Terrestrial vertebrates usually use lungs.

Examples:

- amphibians;
- reptiles;
- birds;
- mammals.

This is called pulmonary respiration.

Frogs can also exchange gases through moist skin.

That is cutaneous respiration.

## 4. Human Respiratory System

The human respiratory system includes:

- external nostrils;
- nasal passages;
- nasal chamber;
- pharynx;
- larynx;
- trachea;
- bronchi;
- bronchioles;
- terminal bronchioles;
- alveoli;
- lungs;
- pleura.

## 4.1 Air Pathway

Air flows through this route:

```text
external nostrils
-> nasal passages
-> nasal chamber
-> pharynx
-> larynx
-> trachea
-> primary bronchi
-> secondary bronchi
-> tertiary bronchi
-> bronchioles
-> terminal bronchioles
-> alveoli
```

## 4.2 Pharynx

The pharynx is a common passage for food and air.

Air passes from nasal chamber to pharynx and then toward the larynx.

Food passes from pharynx toward oesophagus.

## 4.3 Larynx

The larynx is a cartilaginous box.

It helps in sound production.

It is therefore called the sound box.

The opening into the larynx is the glottis.

## 4.4 Epiglottis

The epiglottis is a thin elastic cartilaginous flap.

During swallowing, it covers the glottis.

This prevents food from entering the larynx.

## 4.5 Trachea

The trachea is a straight tube extending into the thoracic cavity.

At about the fifth thoracic vertebra, it divides into:

- right primary bronchus;
- left primary bronchus.

The trachea and bronchi are supported by incomplete cartilaginous rings.

These rings prevent collapse of the airway.

## 4.6 Bronchi And Bronchioles

Each primary bronchus divides repeatedly.

Branching sequence:

```text
primary bronchus -> secondary bronchus -> tertiary bronchus -> bronchioles -> terminal bronchioles
```

Terminal bronchioles lead to alveolar regions.

## 4.7 Alveoli

Alveoli are very thin, irregular-walled, vascularised sacs.

They are the main sites of gas exchange.

They provide:

- large surface area;
- thin diffusion membrane;
- close contact with capillaries;
- moist surface;
- suitable partial pressure gradients.

## 4.8 Lungs And Pleura

Humans have two lungs.

Each lung is covered by double-layered pleura.

Pleural fluid lies between the two pleural layers.

Pleural fluid reduces friction during breathing movements.

Outer pleura is in contact with thoracic lining.

Inner pleura is in contact with lung surface.

## 5. Conducting Part And Exchange Part

The respiratory system has two functional parts.

## 5.1 Conducting Part

The conducting part extends from external nostrils to terminal bronchioles.

Functions:

- transports air to alveoli;
- clears air of foreign particles;
- humidifies air;
- brings air close to body temperature.

No major gas exchange occurs here.

## 5.2 Exchange Part

The exchange part includes alveoli and their ducts.

Function:

- diffusion of O2 from alveoli to blood;
- diffusion of CO2 from blood to alveoli.

## 6. Thoracic Chamber

Lungs lie in the thoracic chamber.

The thoracic chamber is anatomically airtight.

Boundaries:

| Side | Boundary |
|---|---|
| Dorsal | Vertebral column |
| Ventral | Sternum |
| Lateral | Ribs |
| Lower side | Diaphragm |

The diaphragm is dome-shaped.

Because of the lung-thorax arrangement, changes in thoracic volume affect pulmonary volume.

This is essential because we cannot directly pull lungs open.

## 7. Steps Of Respiration In Humans

Respiration involves five broad steps:

1. Pulmonary ventilation, or breathing.
2. Diffusion of O2 and CO2 across alveolar membrane.
3. Transport of gases by blood.
4. Diffusion of O2 and CO2 between blood and tissues.
5. Cellular use of O2 and release of CO2.

## 8. Mechanism Of Breathing

Breathing has two stages:

- inspiration;
- expiration.

Air moves because of pressure gradients.

Air flows from higher pressure to lower pressure.

## 8.1 Intrapulmonary Pressure

Intrapulmonary pressure is pressure inside the lungs.

If intrapulmonary pressure is less than atmospheric pressure:

```text
air enters lungs
```

If intrapulmonary pressure is greater than atmospheric pressure:

```text
air leaves lungs
```

## 9. Inspiration

Inspiration is drawing air into lungs.

It occurs when lung pressure becomes lower than atmospheric pressure.

Normal inspiration is an active process because muscles contract.

## 9.1 Diaphragm In Inspiration

During inspiration, diaphragm contracts.

It becomes flatter.

This increases thoracic volume in the antero-posterior axis.

## 9.2 External Intercostal Muscles

External intercostal muscles contract.

They lift ribs and sternum upward and outward.

This increases thoracic volume in the dorso-ventral axis.

## 9.3 Pressure Change During Inspiration

Sequence:

```text
diaphragm contracts
external intercostals contract
thoracic volume increases
pulmonary volume increases
intrapulmonary pressure decreases
air enters lungs
```

## 10. Expiration

Expiration is release of alveolar air out of lungs.

Normal expiration is mostly passive.

It occurs when inspiratory muscles relax.

## 10.1 Muscle Relaxation

During normal expiration:

- diaphragm relaxes and becomes dome-shaped again;
- external intercostals relax;
- ribs and sternum return toward normal position.

## 10.2 Pressure Change During Expiration

Sequence:

```text
thoracic volume decreases
pulmonary volume decreases
intrapulmonary pressure increases
air moves out
```

## 10.3 Forceful Breathing

During forceful inspiration and expiration, additional muscles help.

Abdominal muscles and internal intercostal muscles are especially useful in forceful expiration.

## 10.4 Normal Breathing Rate

A healthy human breathes about:

```text
12 to 16 times per minute
```

Breathing rate changes with:

- exercise;
- CO2 level;
- emotional state;
- altitude;
- disease;
- metabolic demand.

## 11. Spirometer

A spirometer measures volumes of air involved in breathing movements.

It is useful in clinical assessment of pulmonary function.

It can help evaluate:

- lung capacity;
- airway obstruction;
- restrictive lung disease;
- respiratory fitness.

## 12. Respiratory Volumes

## 12.1 Tidal Volume

Tidal volume is the volume of air inspired or expired during normal breathing.

Approximate value:

```text
500 mL
```

Minute ventilation at rest:

```text
500 mL x 12 to 16 breaths per minute = 6000 to 8000 mL per minute
```

## 12.2 Inspiratory Reserve Volume

Inspiratory reserve volume is the additional air that can be inspired by forceful inspiration after a normal inspiration.

Approximate value:

```text
2500 to 3000 mL
```

## 12.3 Expiratory Reserve Volume

Expiratory reserve volume is the additional air that can be expired by forceful expiration after a normal expiration.

Approximate value:

```text
1000 to 1100 mL
```

## 12.4 Residual Volume

Residual volume is the air remaining in lungs after forceful expiration.

Approximate value:

```text
1100 to 1200 mL
```

Residual volume prevents complete lung collapse and keeps some air available in lungs.

## 13. Respiratory Capacities

Respiratory capacities are combinations of respiratory volumes.

## 13.1 Inspiratory Capacity

Inspiratory capacity is the total air a person can inspire after normal expiration.

Formula:

```text
IC = TV + IRV
```

Approximate range:

```text
3000 to 3500 mL
```

## 13.2 Expiratory Capacity

Expiratory capacity is total air a person can expire after normal inspiration.

Formula:

```text
EC = TV + ERV
```

Approximate range:

```text
1500 to 1600 mL
```

## 13.3 Functional Residual Capacity

Functional residual capacity is the air remaining in lungs after normal expiration.

Formula:

```text
FRC = ERV + RV
```

Approximate range:

```text
2100 to 2300 mL
```

This is the likely answer when asked for air remaining in lungs after normal breathing out.

## 13.4 Vital Capacity

Vital capacity is the maximum volume of air a person can breathe out after forced inspiration.

It can also be described as the maximum air a person can breathe in after forced expiration.

Formula:

```text
VC = IRV + TV + ERV
```

Approximate range:

```text
4000 to 4600 mL
```

Significance:

- indicates maximum ventilatory ability;
- helps assess lung health;
- decreases in many restrictive or obstructive lung conditions.

## 13.5 Total Lung Capacity

Total lung capacity is the total air accommodated in lungs at the end of forced inspiration.

Formula:

```text
TLC = VC + RV
```

or:

```text
TLC = IRV + TV + ERV + RV
```

Approximate range:

```text
5100 to 5800 mL
```

## 14. Volume Summary Table

| Symbol | Name | Meaning | Approximate Value |
|---|---|---|---:|
| TV | Tidal volume | Normal breath in or out | 500 mL |
| IRV | Inspiratory reserve volume | Extra inspired after normal inspiration | 2500-3000 mL |
| ERV | Expiratory reserve volume | Extra expired after normal expiration | 1000-1100 mL |
| RV | Residual volume | Air left after forced expiration | 1100-1200 mL |
| IC | Inspiratory capacity | TV + IRV | 3000-3500 mL |
| EC | Expiratory capacity | TV + ERV | 1500-1600 mL |
| FRC | Functional residual capacity | ERV + RV | 2100-2300 mL |
| VC | Vital capacity | IRV + TV + ERV | 4000-4600 mL |
| TLC | Total lung capacity | VC + RV | 5100-5800 mL |

## 15. Tidal Volume Per Hour

At rest:

```text
TV = 500 mL per breath
breathing rate = 12 to 16 breaths per minute
```

Air per minute:

```text
500 x 12 = 6000 mL
500 x 16 = 8000 mL
```

Air per hour:

```text
6000 x 60 = 360000 mL = 360 L
8000 x 60 = 480000 mL = 480 L
```

Approximate tidal air moved in one hour:

```text
360 to 480 L
```

## 16. Exchange Of Gases

Gas exchange occurs mainly in:

- alveoli;
- tissues.

In alveoli:

```text
O2 diffuses from alveoli to blood;
CO2 diffuses from blood to alveoli.
```

In tissues:

```text
O2 diffuses from blood to tissues;
CO2 diffuses from tissues to blood.
```

## 16.1 Diffusion Basis

O2 and CO2 move by simple diffusion.

Diffusion depends on:

- partial pressure gradient;
- solubility of gases;
- thickness of membrane;
- surface area;
- diffusion distance.

## 16.2 Partial Pressure

Partial pressure is pressure contributed by an individual gas in a gas mixture.

Symbols:

- pO2 for oxygen;
- pCO2 for carbon dioxide.

Gases diffuse from higher partial pressure to lower partial pressure.

## 16.3 Partial Pressures In Key Sites

| Site | pO2 mm Hg | pCO2 mm Hg |
|---|---:|---:|
| Atmospheric air | 159 | 0.3 |
| Alveoli | 104 | 40 |
| Deoxygenated blood | 40 | 45 |
| Oxygenated blood | 95 | 40 |
| Tissues | 40 | 45 |

## 16.4 Oxygen Gradient

At alveoli:

```text
alveolar pO2 = 104 mm Hg
deoxygenated blood pO2 = 40 mm Hg
```

So O2 diffuses:

```text
alveoli -> blood
```

At tissues:

```text
oxygenated blood pO2 = 95 mm Hg
tissue pO2 = 40 mm Hg
```

So O2 diffuses:

```text
blood -> tissues
```

## 16.5 Carbon Dioxide Gradient

At tissues:

```text
tissue pCO2 = 45 mm Hg
oxygenated blood pCO2 = 40 mm Hg
```

So CO2 diffuses:

```text
tissues -> blood
```

At alveoli:

```text
deoxygenated blood pCO2 = 45 mm Hg
alveolar pCO2 = 40 mm Hg
```

So CO2 diffuses:

```text
blood -> alveoli
```

## 16.6 Why CO2 Diffuses Readily

CO2 is 20 to 25 times more soluble than O2.

Therefore, for the same partial pressure difference, CO2 diffuses more readily than O2.

## 17. Diffusion Membrane

The alveolar diffusion membrane is very thin.

It has three major components:

1. thin squamous epithelium of alveolus;
2. endothelium of alveolar capillary;
3. basement substance between them.

Total thickness is much less than one millimetre.

This thinness supports rapid diffusion.

## 17.1 Why Gas Exchange Occurs Mainly In Alveoli

Alveoli are specialised for diffusion.

They have:

- very thin walls;
- rich capillary supply;
- huge surface area;
- moist surface;
- close contact between air and blood;
- suitable pO2 and pCO2 gradients.

Conducting parts such as trachea and bronchi mainly transport and condition air.

They are not designed for major diffusion.

## 18. Transport Of Gases

Blood transports both oxygen and carbon dioxide.

## 18.1 Oxygen Transport Overview

Oxygen transport:

- about 97 percent by RBCs through haemoglobin;
- about 3 percent dissolved in plasma.

## 18.2 Carbon Dioxide Transport Overview

Carbon dioxide transport:

- about 70 percent as bicarbonate;
- about 20 to 25 percent as carbaminohaemoglobin;
- about 7 percent dissolved in plasma.

## 19. Transport Of Oxygen

Haemoglobin is a red iron-containing pigment in RBCs.

Oxygen binds reversibly with haemoglobin.

Product:

```text
oxyhaemoglobin
```

Each haemoglobin molecule can carry up to four oxygen molecules.

## 19.1 Factors Affecting O2 Binding To Haemoglobin

O2 binding depends mainly on pO2.

It is also affected by:

- pCO2;
- H+ concentration;
- temperature.

## 19.2 Conditions In Alveoli

In alveoli:

- pO2 is high;
- pCO2 is low;
- H+ concentration is lower;
- temperature is lower than active tissues.

These conditions favour oxyhaemoglobin formation.

So oxygen loads onto haemoglobin in lungs.

## 19.3 Conditions In Tissues

In tissues:

- pO2 is low;
- pCO2 is high;
- H+ concentration is high;
- temperature is higher.

These conditions favour dissociation of oxygen from oxyhaemoglobin.

So oxygen unloads into tissues.

## 19.4 Oxygen Dissociation Curve

The oxygen dissociation curve plots percentage saturation of haemoglobin with oxygen against pO2.

It is sigmoid, or S-shaped.

This curve shows how haemoglobin binds oxygen at different pO2 values.

## 19.5 Why The Curve Is Sigmoid

Haemoglobin shows cooperative binding.

When one O2 molecule binds, haemoglobin's affinity for the next O2 molecule increases.

This produces the S-shaped curve.

At high pO2 in alveoli, haemoglobin becomes highly saturated.

At lower pO2 in tissues, oxygen is released.

## 19.6 Effect Of pCO2 On Oxygen Transport

High pCO2 reduces haemoglobin's affinity for oxygen.

This helps oxygen unload in tissues.

Tissues have high pCO2 due to metabolism.

Therefore high pCO2 supports O2 delivery where it is needed.

This effect is commonly called the Bohr effect.

## 19.7 Oxygen Delivered To Tissues

Under normal physiological conditions:

```text
100 mL oxygenated blood delivers about 5 mL O2 to tissues.
```

## 20. Transport Of Carbon Dioxide

CO2 is transported in three main forms.

## 20.1 Dissolved CO2

About 7 percent of CO2 is transported dissolved in plasma.

## 20.2 Carbaminohaemoglobin

About 20 to 25 percent of CO2 is transported by haemoglobin as carbaminohaemoglobin.

Binding depends on pCO2.

At tissues:

- pCO2 is high;
- pO2 is low;
- CO2 binds more with haemoglobin.

At alveoli:

- pCO2 is low;
- pO2 is high;
- CO2 dissociates from haemoglobin.

## 20.3 Bicarbonate Transport

About 70 percent of CO2 is transported as bicarbonate.

RBCs contain high concentration of carbonic anhydrase.

Plasma contains a small amount of the enzyme.

Reaction:

```text
CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> HCO3- + H+
```

Carbonic anhydrase speeds the reaction in both directions.

## 20.4 At Tissue Level

At tissues, pCO2 is high because of catabolism.

CO2 diffuses into blood and RBCs.

Carbonic anhydrase helps form carbonic acid.

Carbonic acid dissociates into bicarbonate and hydrogen ions.

Sequence:

```text
CO2 + H2O -> H2CO3 -> HCO3- + H+
```

Thus CO2 is trapped mainly as bicarbonate.

## 20.5 At Alveolar Level

At alveoli, pCO2 is low.

The reaction reverses.

Bicarbonate and H+ form carbonic acid.

Carbonic acid forms CO2 and water.

Sequence:

```text
HCO3- + H+ -> H2CO3 -> CO2 + H2O
```

CO2 diffuses into alveoli and is expired.

## 20.6 CO2 Delivered To Alveoli

Under normal conditions:

```text
100 mL deoxygenated blood delivers about 4 mL CO2 to alveoli.
```

## 21. Regulation Of Respiration

Respiration is regulated mainly by the nervous system.

Humans can adjust respiratory rhythm according to tissue demands.

Important control centres:

- respiratory rhythm centre;
- pneumotaxic centre;
- chemosensitive area;
- peripheral chemoreceptors.

## 21.1 Respiratory Rhythm Centre

The respiratory rhythm centre is in the medulla region of the brain.

It is primarily responsible for maintaining respiratory rhythm.

It sends signals to respiratory muscles.

## 21.2 Pneumotaxic Centre

The pneumotaxic centre is in the pons.

It moderates the function of the respiratory rhythm centre.

Signals from this centre can reduce duration of inspiration.

This can alter respiratory rate.

## 21.3 Chemosensitive Area

A chemosensitive area lies near the rhythm centre.

It is highly sensitive to:

- CO2;
- H+ concentration.

Increase in CO2 or H+ activates this area.

It signals the rhythm centre to adjust breathing so CO2 can be eliminated.

## 21.4 Peripheral Chemoreceptors

Receptors in the aortic arch and carotid artery detect changes in:

- CO2;
- H+ concentration.

They send signals to the respiratory rhythm centre.

## 21.5 Role Of Oxygen In Regulation

In normal conditions, oxygen has a smaller role in regulating respiratory rhythm than CO2 and H+.

CO2 and H+ are the major drivers of normal respiratory adjustment.

## 22. Respiratory Changes At High Altitude

When a person goes up a hill or to high altitude, atmospheric pressure decreases.

The pO2 of inspired air decreases.

Less oxygen diffuses into blood.

This can cause hypoxia.

The body responds by:

- increasing breathing rate;
- increasing depth of breathing;
- increasing heart rate;
- gradually increasing RBC production during acclimatisation.

The immediate respiratory response is faster and deeper breathing.

## 23. Hypoxia

Hypoxia means insufficient oxygen availability to tissues.

Possible causes:

- high altitude;
- lung disease;
- anaemia;
- poor circulation;
- carbon monoxide poisoning;
- airway obstruction.

Symptoms may include:

- breathlessness;
- fatigue;
- confusion;
- bluish skin in severe cases;
- rapid breathing.

## 24. Disorders Of Respiratory System

## 24.1 Asthma

Asthma causes difficulty in breathing and wheezing.

It occurs due to inflammation and narrowing of bronchi and bronchioles.

Airflow becomes obstructed.

## 24.2 Emphysema

Emphysema is a chronic disorder in which alveolar walls are damaged.

This reduces respiratory surface area.

Gas exchange becomes less efficient.

Cigarette smoking is a major cause.

## 24.3 Occupational Respiratory Disorders

Some industries produce large amounts of dust.

Examples include:

- grinding;
- stone-breaking;
- mining-related work in some settings.

Long exposure may cause inflammation and fibrosis.

Fibrosis means proliferation of fibrous tissue.

This can cause serious lung damage.

Workers in dusty industries should use protective masks and proper ventilation systems.

## 25. Common Misconceptions

## 25.1 Misconception: Respiration Only Means Breathing

Correct idea:

Breathing is ventilation.

Cellular respiration is energy release inside cells.

The word respiration may be used broadly, but the processes are different.

## 25.2 Misconception: Air Is Forced Into Lungs By Positive Pressure

Correct idea:

During normal inspiration, lung pressure falls below atmospheric pressure.

Air enters because of this pressure gradient.

## 25.3 Misconception: Alveoli Act Like Simple Air Bags Only

Correct idea:

Alveoli are specialised exchange surfaces with thin walls and rich capillary supply.

## 25.4 Misconception: Oxygen Is Mainly Dissolved In Plasma

Correct idea:

About 97 percent of oxygen is transported by haemoglobin in RBCs.

Only about 3 percent is dissolved in plasma.

## 25.5 Misconception: CO2 Is Mainly Carried By Haemoglobin

Correct idea:

Most CO2 is transported as bicarbonate.

Only 20 to 25 percent is carried as carbaminohaemoglobin.

## 25.6 Misconception: Oxygen Is The Main Normal Breathing Regulator

Correct idea:

CO2 and H+ are more important in normal regulation of respiratory rhythm.

## 26. Solved Concept Checks

## 26.1 Why Is CO2 Removed Continuously?

CO2 is produced during cellular catabolism.

If it accumulates, it changes H+ concentration and affects blood pH.

Therefore it must be removed continuously.

## 26.2 Why Are Alveoli Good For Gas Exchange?

Alveoli have thin walls, large surface area, rich capillary supply, and moist surfaces.

They maintain partial pressure gradients for O2 and CO2.

## 26.3 Why Does Inspiration Occur?

Inspiration occurs when thoracic and pulmonary volumes increase.

This lowers intrapulmonary pressure below atmospheric pressure.

Air moves into lungs.

## 26.4 Why Does Expiration Occur?

Normal expiration occurs when inspiratory muscles relax.

Thoracic and pulmonary volumes decrease.

Intrapulmonary pressure rises above atmospheric pressure.

Air moves out.

## 26.5 Why Is Residual Volume Important?

Residual volume keeps some air in lungs after forced expiration.

It helps prevent lung collapse and maintains continuous gas exchange.

## 26.6 Why Does O2 Move From Alveoli To Blood?

Alveolar pO2 is about 104 mm Hg.

Deoxygenated blood pO2 is about 40 mm Hg.

O2 diffuses from higher pO2 to lower pO2.

## 26.7 Why Does CO2 Move From Blood To Alveoli?

Deoxygenated blood pCO2 is about 45 mm Hg.

Alveolar pCO2 is about 40 mm Hg.

CO2 diffuses from blood to alveoli.

## 26.8 Why Does High pCO2 Help Oxygen Delivery?

High pCO2 lowers haemoglobin affinity for oxygen.

This promotes oxygen unloading in active tissues.

## 26.9 Why Is Carbonic Anhydrase Important?

Carbonic anhydrase rapidly converts CO2 and water into carbonic acid and reverses the reaction in alveoli.

This allows efficient CO2 transport as bicarbonate.

## 26.10 Why Does Breathing Increase At High Altitude?

High altitude lowers inspired pO2.

Less oxygen enters blood.

Chemoreceptor responses increase breathing rate and depth to improve oxygen uptake.

## 27. Exam-Oriented Answers

## 27.1 Define Vital Capacity And Its Significance

Vital capacity is the maximum volume of air a person can breathe out after forced inspiration.

It equals:

```text
VC = IRV + TV + ERV
```

Significance:

- measures maximum ventilatory capacity;
- helps assess pulmonary function;
- decreases in many lung diseases.

## 27.2 Volume Remaining After Normal Expiration

The volume remaining in lungs after normal expiration is functional residual capacity.

Formula:

```text
FRC = ERV + RV
```

Approximate value:

```text
2100 to 2300 mL
```

## 27.3 Why Diffusion Occurs In Alveoli Only

Alveoli are specialised for gas exchange.

They have:

- thin diffusion membrane;
- extensive capillary network;
- large surface area;
- moist surface;
- suitable partial pressure gradients.

Other parts mainly conduct, clean, humidify, and warm air.

They lack the full structural adaptation for efficient gas exchange.

## 27.4 Major Transport Mechanisms For CO2

CO2 is transported in three forms:

- about 70 percent as bicarbonate;
- about 20 to 25 percent as carbaminohaemoglobin;
- about 7 percent dissolved in plasma.

At tissues, CO2 forms bicarbonate with help of carbonic anhydrase.

At alveoli, bicarbonate is converted back to CO2 and released.

## 27.5 Atmospheric Air Compared With Alveolar Air

Atmospheric air has:

- higher pO2 than alveolar air;
- lower pCO2 than alveolar air.

Correct option:

```text
pO2 higher, pCO2 lesser
```

## 27.6 Inspiration Under Normal Conditions

During inspiration:

1. Diaphragm contracts and flattens.
2. External intercostal muscles contract.
3. Ribs and sternum move upward and outward.
4. Thoracic volume increases.
5. Pulmonary volume increases.
6. Intrapulmonary pressure falls below atmospheric pressure.
7. Air enters lungs.

## 27.7 Regulation Of Respiration

Respiration is regulated by neural centres.

The respiratory rhythm centre in medulla maintains breathing rhythm.

The pneumotaxic centre in pons modifies inspiration duration.

A chemosensitive area near the rhythm centre responds to CO2 and H+.

Aortic arch and carotid artery receptors also detect CO2 and H+ changes.

Oxygen has a relatively minor role in normal regulation.

## 27.8 Effect Of pCO2 On Oxygen Transport

High pCO2 reduces oxygen affinity of haemoglobin.

This promotes oxygen unloading in tissues.

Low pCO2 in alveoli favours oxygen binding to haemoglobin.

## 27.9 Respiratory Process While Going Up A Hill

At higher altitude, pO2 decreases.

Less oxygen enters blood.

The person breathes faster and deeper to increase oxygen intake.

With time, acclimatisation can increase RBC production and improve oxygen transport.

## 27.10 Site Of Gaseous Exchange In Insects

In insects, gas exchange occurs through the tracheal system.

Fine tracheoles deliver gases close to tissues.

## 27.11 Oxygen Dissociation Curve

The oxygen dissociation curve shows the relationship between pO2 and percentage saturation of haemoglobin with oxygen.

It is sigmoid because haemoglobin shows cooperative binding.

Binding of one oxygen molecule increases affinity for the next.

## 27.12 Hypoxia

Hypoxia is low oxygen availability to tissues.

It may occur due to high altitude, lung disease, anaemia, poor circulation, or toxic gases such as carbon monoxide.

## 28. Important Comparisons

## 28.1 IRV And ERV

| Full form | Inspiratory reserve volume | Expiratory reserve volume |
| Meaning | Extra air inspired after normal inspiration | Extra air expired after normal expiration |
| Approximate value | 2500-3000 mL | 1000-1100 mL |
| Direction | Inward | Outward |

## 28.2 Inspiratory Capacity And Expiratory Capacity

| Feature | Inspiratory Capacity | Expiratory Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Total air inspired after normal expiration | Total air expired after normal inspiration |
| Formula | TV + IRV | TV + ERV |
| Approximate value | 3000-3500 mL | 1500-1600 mL |

## 28.3 Vital Capacity And Total Lung Capacity

| Feature | Vital Capacity | Total Lung Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Maximum usable air moved after forced breathing effort | Total air in lungs after forced inspiration |
| Formula | IRV + TV + ERV | IRV + TV + ERV + RV |
| Includes RV | No | Yes |
| Approximate value | 4000-4600 mL | 5100-5800 mL |

## 28.4 Oxygen And Carbon Dioxide Transport

| Feature | O2 | CO2 |
|---|---|---|
| Main transport form | Oxyhaemoglobin | Bicarbonate |
| Dissolved in plasma | About 3 percent | About 7 percent |
| Hb-linked form | About 97 percent as oxyhaemoglobin | About 20-25 percent as carbaminohaemoglobin |
| Enzyme needed for main form | Not carbonic anhydrase | Carbonic anhydrase |
