## 1. Biology, Unity, And The Cell

Living organisms show enormous diversity. They differ in shape, size, behaviour, habitat, and complexity.

Cell theory explains the unity behind this diversity.

All living organisms are made of cells or products of cells. This makes the cell the basic unit underlying all life forms.

## 2. What Is A Cell?

Unicellular organisms are capable of:

- independent existence;
- performing essential life functions.

Anything less than a complete cell does not normally show independent living.

Therefore, the cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of living organisms.

## 2.1 Unicellular And Multicellular Organisms

| Type | Meaning | Example Context |
|---|---|---|
| Unicellular organism | Body made of one cell | Many bacteria, Amoeba |
| Multicellular organism | Body made of many cells | Plants, animals, many fungi |

In unicellular organisms, one cell performs all life activities.

In multicellular organisms, cells specialise and cooperate.

## 2.2 Discovery Milestones

| Scientist | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Antonie van Leeuwenhoek | First saw and described a living cell |
| Robert Brown | Discovered the nucleus |
| Schleiden | Studied plants and concluded that plants are made of cells |
| Schwann | Studied animal cells and helped formulate cell theory |
| Virchow | Explained that new cells arise from pre-existing cells |

## 3. Cell Theory

Schleiden and Schwann together formulated the cell theory.

Virchow later modified it by explaining cell origin.

## 3.1 Modern Cell Theory

Modern cell theory states:

1. All living organisms are composed of cells and products of cells.
2. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Virchow expressed this idea as "Omnis cellula e cellula", meaning every cell arises from a cell.

## 4. Overview Of Cell Structure

All cells have some common features.

| Feature | Present In |
|---|---|
| Plasma membrane | All cells |
| Cytoplasm | All cells |
| Genetic material | All cells |
| Ribosomes | All cells |

Eukaryotic cells have additional membrane-bound organelles.

## 4.1 Prokaryotic Versus Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Membrane-bound nucleus | Absent | Present |
| Nuclear membrane | Absent | Present |
| Membrane-bound organelles | Absent | Present |
| Genetic material | Naked, usually circular DNA | Organised into chromosomes |
| Ribosomes | 70S | 80S in cytoplasm |
| Size | Usually smaller | Usually larger |
| Examples | Bacteria, cyanobacteria, mycoplasma | Protists, fungi, plants, animals |

## 4.2 Cytoplasm

Cytoplasm is the semi-fluid matrix inside the cell.

It is the main site of many cellular reactions.

In both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, cytoplasm helps maintain the living state of the cell.

## 4.3 Cell Size And Shape

Cells vary in size and shape depending on function.

Examples:

- Mycoplasma are among the smallest cells.
- Bacteria are generally larger than mycoplasma but smaller than typical eukaryotic cells.
- Ostrich egg is a very large isolated single cell.
- Human red blood cells are small and biconcave.
- Nerve cells are among the longest cells.

## 4.4 Cell Shape And Function

| Cell Shape | Functional Meaning |
|---|---|
| Biconcave | Helps red blood cells exchange gases efficiently |
| Long and branched | Helps nerve cells transmit impulses |
| Columnar | Useful for lining and absorption |
| Irregular | Useful for movement or engulfing particles |
| Elongated | Useful for support or conduction in plants |

## 5. Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotic cells include:

- bacteria;
- cyanobacteria;
- mycoplasma;
- PPLO.

They are generally smaller and multiply more rapidly than eukaryotic cells.

## 5.1 Basic Bacterial Shapes

| Shape | Name |
|---|---|
| Rod-like | Bacillus |
| Spherical | Coccus |
| Comma-shaped | Vibrio |
| Spiral | Spirillum |

## 5.2 General Features Of Prokaryotic Cells

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Nucleus | Not well-defined |
| Nuclear membrane | Absent |
| Genomic DNA | Naked, usually single circular chromosome |
| Plasmids | Small circular extra DNA in many bacteria |
| Organelles | Membrane-bound organelles absent |
| Ribosomes | Present |
| Cell wall | Present in most, absent in mycoplasma |
| Cytoplasm | Semi-fluid matrix |

## 5.3 Plasmids

Plasmids are small circular DNA molecules outside genomic DNA.

They can provide unique traits to bacteria, such as antibiotic resistance.

Plasmids are also useful in biotechnology for bacterial transformation.

## 6. Prokaryotic Cell Envelope

Most bacterial cells have a chemically complex cell envelope.

It has three layers.

## 6.1 Layers Of Bacterial Cell Envelope

| Layer | Position |
|---|---|
| Glycocalyx | Outermost |
| Cell wall | Middle |
| Plasma membrane | Innermost |

These layers work together as a protective unit.

## 6.2 Glycocalyx

Glycocalyx differs in thickness and composition among bacteria.

| Type | Feature |
|---|---|
| Slime layer | Loose sheath |
| Capsule | Thick and tough |

## 6.3 Cell Wall

The bacterial cell wall:

- determines cell shape;
- provides structural support;
- prevents bursting or collapsing.

## 6.4 Gram Positive And Gram Negative Bacteria

Bacteria are classified based on response to Gram staining.

| Type | Response |
|---|---|
| Gram positive | Take up Gram stain |
| Gram negative | Do not take up Gram stain in the same way |

This difference is linked to cell envelope structure.

## 6.5 Plasma Membrane

The bacterial plasma membrane is selectively permeable and interacts with the environment.

It is structurally similar to the eukaryotic plasma membrane.

## 7. Mesosome And Other Prokaryotic Extensions

## 7.1 Mesosome

Mesosome is a specialised infolding of the plasma membrane in prokaryotic cells.

It may appear as:

- vesicles;
- tubules;
- lamellae.

## 7.2 Functions Of Mesosome

Mesosome helps in:

- cell wall formation;
- DNA replication;
- distribution of DNA to daughter cells;
- respiration;
- secretion;
- increasing surface area of plasma membrane;
- increasing enzymatic content.

## 7.3 Chromatophores

In some prokaryotes, such as cyanobacteria, membranous extensions called chromatophores contain pigments.

## 8. Prokaryotic Flagella, Pili, And Fimbriae

## 8.1 Bacterial Flagella

Motile bacteria may have thin filamentous extensions called flagella.

Bacterial flagellum has three parts:

1. Filament
2. Hook
3. Basal body

The filament is the longest part and extends outside the cell surface.

## 8.2 Pili

Pili are elongated tubular surface structures made of special protein.

They are not primarily for motility.

## 8.3 Fimbriae

Fimbriae are small bristle-like fibres.

In some bacteria, they help attachment to surfaces such as rocks or host tissues.

## 9. Prokaryotic Ribosomes And Inclusion Bodies

## 9.1 Prokaryotic Ribosomes

Prokaryotic ribosomes are about 70S.

They are made of:

- 50S larger subunit;
- 30S smaller subunit.

They are sites of protein synthesis.

## 9.2 Polysome

Several ribosomes may attach to one mRNA and form a chain called a polyribosome or polysome.

Each ribosome translates the mRNA to produce protein.

## 9.3 Inclusion Bodies

Inclusion bodies store reserve materials in prokaryotic cells.

They are not membrane-bound and lie free in cytoplasm.

Examples:

- phosphate granules;
- cyanophycean granules;
- glycogen granules.

Gas vacuoles occur in blue-green, purple, and green photosynthetic bacteria.

## 10. Eukaryotic Cells

Eukaryotes include:

- protists;
- plants;
- animals;
- fungi.

## 10.1 Main Features Of Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Nucleus | Organised nucleus with nuclear envelope |
| Organelles | Membrane-bound organelles present |
| Cytoplasm | Compartmentalised |
| Genetic material | Organised into chromosomes |
| Cytoskeleton | Present |
| Locomotory structures | Cilia and flagella in many cells |

## 10.2 Plant Cell Versus Animal Cell

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Cell wall | Present | Absent |
| Plastids | Present | Absent |
| Large central vacuole | Usually present | Usually absent |
| Centrioles | Absent in most plant cells | Present |
| Plasma membrane | Present | Present |
| Nucleus | Present | Present |
| Mitochondria | Present | Present |

## 11. Cell Membrane

The plasma membrane is the living boundary of the cell.

It is mainly made of:

- lipids;
- proteins;
- carbohydrates.

## 11.1 Phospholipid Bilayer

The major lipids are phospholipids arranged in a bilayer.

| Part | Orientation |
|---|---|
| Polar heads | Toward watery outer and inner sides |
| Hydrophobic tails | Toward the inner part of the bilayer |

This arrangement protects nonpolar hydrocarbon tails from the aqueous environment.

## 11.2 Membrane Proteins

Membrane proteins are of two broad types.

| Type | Position |
|---|---|
| Peripheral proteins | On membrane surface |
| Integral proteins | Partly or fully embedded in membrane |

## 11.3 Fluid Mosaic Model

Singer and Nicolson proposed the fluid mosaic model in 1972.

According to this model:

- lipids form a quasi-fluid bilayer;
- proteins are embedded in or associated with the bilayer;
- proteins can move laterally within the bilayer.

This fluidity is important for:

- cell growth;
- intercellular junction formation;
- secretion;
- endocytosis;
- cell division.

## 11.4 Transport Across Plasma Membrane

The plasma membrane is selectively permeable.

It allows some molecules to cross more easily than others.

## 11.5 Passive Transport

Passive transport does not require energy.

Neutral solutes may move by simple diffusion along the concentration gradient, from higher concentration to lower concentration.

## 11.6 Osmosis

Osmosis is diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.

Water moves from higher water concentration to lower water concentration.

## 11.7 Facilitated Transport

Polar molecules cannot easily pass through the nonpolar lipid bilayer.

They require carrier proteins or channel proteins for facilitated transport.

## 11.8 Active Transport

Active transport uses energy, usually ATP.

It moves ions or molecules against their concentration gradient, from lower concentration to higher concentration.

Example: Na+/K+ pump.

## 12. Cell Wall

Cell wall is a non-living rigid structure outside the plasma membrane in plants and fungi.

## 12.1 Functions Of Cell Wall

Cell wall:

- gives shape to the cell;
- protects from mechanical damage;
- protects from infection;
- helps cell-to-cell interaction;
- acts as a barrier to undesirable macromolecules.

## 12.2 Composition Of Cell Wall

| Organism Group | Cell Wall Composition |
|---|---|
| Algae | Cellulose, galactans, mannans, minerals such as calcium carbonate |
| Higher plants | Cellulose, hemicellulose, pectins, proteins |

## 12.3 Primary Wall, Secondary Wall, And Middle Lamella

| Structure | Description |
|---|---|
| Primary wall | Wall of young plant cell, capable of growth |
| Secondary wall | Formed on inner side of primary wall as cell matures |
| Middle lamella | Calcium pectate layer that glues neighbouring cells |

## 12.4 Plasmodesmata

Plasmodesmata are cytoplasmic connections passing through cell walls and middle lamella.

They connect neighbouring plant cells.

## 13. Endomembrane System

The endomembrane system includes organelles whose functions are coordinated.

It includes:

- endoplasmic reticulum;
- Golgi complex;
- lysosomes;
- vacuoles.

Mitochondria, chloroplasts, and peroxisomes are not considered part of the endomembrane system because their functions are not coordinated with the above components in the same way.

## 14. Endoplasmic Reticulum

Endoplasmic reticulum, or ER, is a network of tiny tubular structures scattered in cytoplasm.

It divides intracellular space into:

- luminal compartment inside ER;
- extra-luminal compartment in cytoplasm.

## 14.1 Rough ER And Smooth ER

| Type | Feature | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Rough ER | Ribosomes attached | Protein synthesis and secretion |
| Smooth ER | Ribosomes absent | Lipid synthesis |

RER is often continuous with the outer nuclear membrane.

In animal cells, SER helps synthesise steroidal hormones.

## 15. Golgi Apparatus

Golgi apparatus was first observed by Camillo Golgi.

It consists of flat disc-shaped sacs called cisternae.

## 15.1 Structure Of Golgi Apparatus

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cisternae | Flat stacked sacs |
| Cis face | Forming face |
| Trans face | Maturing face |
| Location | Often near nucleus and ER |

## 15.2 Functions Of Golgi Apparatus

Golgi apparatus:

- packages materials for delivery;
- modifies proteins made on ER;
- forms glycoproteins;
- forms glycolipids;
- helps secretion.

Materials from ER fuse with the cis face and move toward the trans face.

## 16. Lysosomes

Lysosomes are membrane-bound vesicles formed by packaging in the Golgi apparatus.

They contain hydrolytic enzymes such as:

- lipases;
- proteases;
- carbohydrases.

These enzymes work best at acidic pH.

Lysosomes digest carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.

## 17. Vacuoles

A vacuole is a membrane-bound space in cytoplasm.

It may contain:

- water;
- sap;
- excretory products;
- other materials not useful for the cell.

## 17.1 Tonoplast

The vacuole is bound by a single membrane called tonoplast.

In plant cells, vacuoles can occupy up to 90 percent of cell volume.

The tonoplast helps transport ions and materials into the vacuole, often against concentration gradients.

## 17.2 Special Vacuoles

| Type | Function |
|---|---|
| Contractile vacuole | Osmoregulation and excretion in Amoeba |
| Food vacuole | Formed by engulfing food particles in many protists |

## 18. Mitochondria

Mitochondria are double membrane-bound organelles.

Their number varies depending on cell activity.

They may be sausage-shaped or cylindrical.

## 18.1 Structure Of Mitochondrion

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| Outer membrane | Continuous limiting boundary |
| Inner membrane | Forms infoldings called cristae |
| Cristae | Increase surface area |
| Matrix | Dense inner compartment |
| Intermembrane space | Space between outer and inner membranes |

## 18.2 Functions Of Mitochondria

Mitochondria are sites of aerobic respiration.

They produce ATP, the cellular energy currency.

Therefore, they are called powerhouses of the cell.

## 18.3 Semi-Autonomous Features

Mitochondrial matrix contains:

- circular DNA;
- RNA;
- 70S ribosomes;
- components for protein synthesis.

Mitochondria divide by fission.

## 19. Plastids

Plastids are found in plant cells and euglenoids.

They are large and often visible under a microscope.

They contain pigments or stored nutrients.

## 19.1 Types Of Plastids

| Plastid Type | Pigment Or Content | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Chloroplast | Chlorophyll and carotenoids | Photosynthesis |
| Chromoplast | Carotene, xanthophylls, other pigments | Yellow, orange, red colours |
| Leucoplast | Colourless, stored nutrients | Storage |

## 19.2 Types Of Leucoplasts

| Type | Stored Material | Example Context |
|---|---|---|
| Amyloplast | Starch | Potato |
| Elaioplast | Oils and fats | Storage tissues |
| Aleuroplast | Proteins | Protein storage |

## 20. Chloroplast

Chloroplasts are double membrane-bound plastids.

They are common in mesophyll cells of leaves.

## 20.1 Chloroplast Shapes

Chloroplasts may be:

- lens-shaped;
- oval;
- spherical;
- discoid;
- ribbon-like.

## 20.2 Structure Of Chloroplast

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| Outer membrane | Outer boundary |
| Inner membrane | Less permeable than outer membrane |
| Stroma | Matrix inside inner membrane |
| Thylakoids | Flattened membranous sacs |
| Grana | Stacks of thylakoids |
| Stroma lamellae | Tubules connecting grana |
| Lumen | Space inside thylakoid |

## 20.3 Functions Of Chloroplast Parts

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Thylakoid membrane | Contains chlorophyll pigments for light reactions |
| Stroma | Contains enzymes for synthesis of carbohydrates and proteins |
| Grana | Site associated with light reaction organisation |
| Stroma | Site associated with dark reaction enzymes |

## 20.4 Semi-Autonomous Features

Chloroplasts contain:

- circular DNA;
- 70S ribosomes;
- enzymes for synthesis of carbohydrates and proteins.

## 21. Ribosomes

Ribosomes were observed as dense particles by George Palade.

They are made of RNA and proteins.

They are not surrounded by a membrane.

## 21.1 Ribosome Types

| Ribosome | Subunits | Location |
|---|---|---|
| 70S | 50S and 30S | Prokaryotes, mitochondria, chloroplasts |
| 80S | 60S and 40S | Eukaryotic cytoplasm |

The letter S refers to Svedberg unit, a measure related to sedimentation, size, and density.

## 21.2 Function Of Ribosomes

Ribosomes are sites of protein synthesis.

They may be free in cytoplasm or attached to rough ER.

## 22. Cytoskeleton

Cytoskeleton is a network of protein filaments in cytoplasm.

It includes:

- microtubules;
- microfilaments;
- intermediate filaments.

## 22.1 Functions Of Cytoskeleton

Cytoskeleton helps in:

- mechanical support;
- cell motility;
- maintenance of cell shape.

## 23. Cilia And Flagella

Cilia and flagella are hair-like outgrowths of the cell membrane.

## 23.1 Cilia Versus Flagella

| Feature | Cilia | Flagella |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Shorter | Longer |
| Function | Move cell or surrounding fluid | Mainly cell movement |
| Motion comparison | Work like oars | Usually whip-like |

Prokaryotic flagella are structurally different from eukaryotic flagella.

## 23.2 Eukaryotic Axoneme

The core of cilium or flagellum is called axoneme.

It usually has:

- nine peripheral doublets of microtubules;
- two central microtubules.

This is called the 9+2 arrangement.

## 23.3 Basal Body

Cilia and flagella arise from centriole-like structures called basal bodies.

## 24. Centrosome And Centrioles

The centrosome usually contains two centrioles.

The centrioles lie perpendicular to each other.

## 24.1 Structure Of Centriole

Each centriole:

- is cylindrical;
- has a cartwheel-like organisation;
- has nine evenly spaced peripheral fibrils;
- each fibril is a triplet of tubulin microtubules;
- has a central proteinaceous hub;
- has radial spokes connecting hub to peripheral triplets.

## 24.2 Functions Of Centrioles

Centrioles:

- form basal bodies of cilia and flagella;
- help form spindle apparatus during cell division in animal cells.

## 25. Nucleus

The nucleus was described by Robert Brown.

It controls cell activities and plays a major role in heredity.

## 25.1 Components Of Interphase Nucleus

An interphase nucleus has:

- nuclear envelope;
- nucleoplasm or nuclear matrix;
- chromatin;
- one or more nucleoli.

## 25.2 Nuclear Envelope

The nuclear envelope has two parallel membranes.

The space between them is called perinuclear space.

The outer membrane is often continuous with endoplasmic reticulum and may bear ribosomes.

## 25.3 Nuclear Pores

Nuclear pores are openings formed by fusion of the two nuclear membranes at certain points.

They allow movement of RNA and protein molecules between nucleus and cytoplasm.

## 25.4 Nucleolus

Nucleolus is a spherical, non-membranous structure in nucleoplasm.

It is the site of active ribosomal RNA synthesis.

Cells actively synthesising proteins often have larger or more numerous nucleoli.

## 25.5 Chromatin

Chromatin is a network of nucleoprotein fibres in the interphase nucleus.

It contains:

- DNA;
- histone proteins;
- non-histone proteins;
- RNA.

During cell division, chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes.

## 26. Chromosomes

Each visible chromosome has a primary constriction called centromere.

Disc-shaped kinetochores occur on the sides of centromere.

Centromere holds the two chromatids together.

## 26.1 Chromosome Types Based On Centromere Position

| Type | Centromere Position | Arms |
|---|---|---|
| Metacentric | Middle | Two equal arms |
| Submetacentric | Slightly away from middle | One short arm, one long arm |
| Acrocentric | Close to one end | One extremely short arm, one very long arm |
| Telocentric | Terminal | One main arm |

## 26.2 Satellite

Some chromosomes have secondary constrictions at constant locations.

This can produce a small fragment-like region called a satellite.

## 27. Microbodies

Microbodies are small membrane-bound vesicles containing enzymes.

They occur in both plant and animal cells.

Examples commonly studied in biology include peroxisomes and glyoxysomes, though this chapter only introduces microbodies generally.

## 28. Organelle Summary By Membrane Type

| Membrane Type | Organelles Or Structures |
|---|---|
| No membrane | Ribosomes, cytoskeleton, centrioles |
| Single membrane | ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, vacuoles, microbodies |
| Double membrane | Nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts |

- hub;
- radial spokes.

## 32.5 What Is A Mesosome? Mention Its Functions

Mesosome is an infolding of the plasma membrane in prokaryotic cells.

It may occur as vesicles, tubules, or lamellae.

Functions:

- helps cell wall formation;
- helps DNA replication;
- helps distribution of DNA to daughter cells;
- helps respiration;
- helps secretion;
- increases surface area of plasma membrane;
- increases enzymatic content.

## 32.6 How Do Neutral Solutes And Polar Molecules Move Across Plasma Membrane?

Neutral solutes move by simple diffusion along the concentration gradient, from higher concentration to lower concentration.

Polar molecules cannot pass easily through the nonpolar lipid bilayer. They require carrier proteins or channels for facilitated transport.

If molecules move against their concentration gradient, energy is required. This is active transport and uses ATP.

## 32.7 Name Two Double Membrane-Bound Organelles And State Their Features

Two double membrane-bound organelles are:

1. Mitochondria
2. Chloroplasts

## 32.7.1 Mitochondria

Characteristics:

- double membrane-bound;
- outer membrane forms boundary;
- inner membrane forms cristae;
- matrix contains circular DNA, RNA, 70S ribosomes;
- divides by fission.

Function:

- site of aerobic respiration;
- produces ATP.

## 32.7.2 Chloroplasts

Characteristics:

- double membrane-bound;
- contain stroma, thylakoids, grana, and stroma lamellae;
- contain chlorophyll pigments;
- contain circular DNA and 70S ribosomes.

Function:

- site of photosynthesis;
- traps light energy and helps synthesize carbohydrates.

## 32.8 Characteristics Of Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotic cells have:

- no membrane-bound nucleus;
- no nuclear envelope;
- naked genetic material;
- usually circular genomic DNA;
- plasmids in many bacteria;
- no membrane-bound organelles;
- 70S ribosomes;
- cell wall in most forms except mycoplasma;
- cell envelope in bacteria;
- mesosomes in prokaryotes;
- inclusion bodies for storage;
- smaller size and rapid multiplication compared with eukaryotic cells.

## 32.9 Multicellular Organisms Have Division Of Labour

In multicellular organisms, different cells specialise for different functions.

Cells form tissues, tissues form organs, and organs form organ systems.

For example, in humans, nerve cells conduct impulses, muscle cells contract, epithelial cells line surfaces, and blood cells transport materials.

This division of labour makes body functions more efficient.

## 32.10 Cell Is The Basic Unit Of Life: Brief Explanation

The cell is the basic unit of life because:

- all living organisms are made of cells;
- a unicellular organism performs all life functions in one cell;
- cells contain genetic material;
- cells carry out metabolism;
- new cells arise from pre-existing cells;
- tissues, organs, and organ systems are built from cells.

Therefore, cell is both the structural and functional unit of life.

## 32.11 What Are Nuclear Pores? State Their Function

Nuclear pores are openings in the nuclear envelope formed where the two nuclear membranes fuse.

Function:

- allow movement of RNA and proteins between nucleus and cytoplasm;
- help communication between nucleus and cytoplasm.

## 32.12 Lysosomes And Vacuoles Differ In Function

Both lysosomes and vacuoles are single membrane-bound endomembrane structures, but their functions differ.

| Feature | Lysosomes | Vacuoles |
|---|---|---|
| Main content | Hydrolytic enzymes | Water, sap, waste, stored materials |
| Main function | Digestion of macromolecules | Storage, osmoregulation, excretion |
| Example | Digestion of proteins, lipids, nucleic acids | Large central vacuole in plants |

## 32.13 Structure Of Nucleus

The nucleus has:

- double membrane nuclear envelope;
- perinuclear space;
- nuclear pores;
- nucleoplasm;
- chromatin;
- nucleolus.

The nuclear envelope separates nuclear contents from cytoplasm. Nuclear pores allow exchange of RNA and proteins. Nucleolus synthesizes rRNA. Chromatin contains DNA and proteins and condenses into chromosomes during cell division.

Use Section 30.3 for a labelled diagram guide.

## 32.14 Structure Of Centrosome

The centrosome usually contains two centrioles placed perpendicular to each other.

Each centriole:

- is cylindrical;
- has cartwheel organisation;
- has nine peripheral triplet fibrils of tubulin;
- has a central hub;
- has radial spokes.

Centrioles form basal bodies of cilia and flagella and help form spindle apparatus during cell division in animal cells.

Use Section 30.4 for a labelled diagram guide.

## 32.15 What Is A Centromere?

Centromere is the primary constriction of a chromosome.

It holds two chromatids together.

Kinetochores are disc-like structures present on the sides of the centromere.

## 32.16 Classification Of Chromosomes By Centromere Position

| Metacentric | Middle | Two equal arms |
| Submetacentric | Slightly away from middle | One short and one long arm |
| Acrocentric | Close to one end | One very short and one very long arm |
| Telocentric | At terminal end | One main arm |

## 33. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 33.1 Why Are Ribosomes Found In Both Prokaryotic And Eukaryotic Cells?

All cells need proteins. Ribosomes are the sites of protein synthesis, so they occur in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

## 33.2 Why Are Mitochondria Called Powerhouses?

Mitochondria produce ATP through aerobic respiration. ATP provides usable energy for cellular processes.

## 33.3 Why Are Chloroplasts Important?

Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll and carry out photosynthesis. They trap light energy and help form carbohydrates.

## 33.4 Why Is Plasma Membrane Selectively Permeable?

The plasma membrane allows some substances to pass more easily than others. Its lipid bilayer, proteins, and channels regulate movement across it.

## 33.5 Why Is Cell Wall Non-Living But Useful?

Cell wall is non-living but provides shape, mechanical support, protection, and cell-to-cell interaction.

## 33.6 Why Is Nucleolus Important?

Nucleolus is the site of rRNA synthesis and is involved in ribosome formation.
