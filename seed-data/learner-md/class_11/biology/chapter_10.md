## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is about sequence. Every stage makes sense if you ask three questions:

1. Has DNA replicated?
2. Are homologous chromosomes separating or sister chromatids separating?
3. Is chromosome number maintained or reduced?

Learn the flow first:

```text
Interphase -> M phase -> cytokinesis
```

Then learn meiosis:

```text
DNA replication -> meiosis I -> meiosis II -> four haploid cells
```

The most important distinction is this:

- mitosis separates sister chromatids and keeps chromosome number same;
- meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes and reduces chromosome number;
- meiosis II separates sister chromatids.

## 1. Why Cells Divide

All organisms begin life from a single cell.

Growth, repair, and reproduction require cell division.

When a parent cell divides, it gives rise to daughter cells. These daughter cells may grow and divide again, creating a population of cells.

Cell division must be coordinated with:

- DNA replication;
- cell growth;
- chromosome separation;
- cytoplasm division.

## 2. Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the sequence of events by which a cell:

1. Duplicates its genome.
2. Synthesises cellular components.
3. Divides into two daughter cells.

## 2.1 Two Basic Phases

| Phase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Interphase | Preparation phase between two M phases |
| M phase | Actual division phase |

In a typical human cell cycle of about 24 hours, M phase lasts about one hour, while interphase takes more than 95 percent of the cycle.

Yeast cells may complete the cell cycle in about 90 minutes.

## 2.2 Interphase Is Not Rest

Interphase is sometimes called a resting phase, but it is actually metabolically active.

During interphase, the cell:

- grows;
- replicates DNA;
- prepares proteins and organelles for division.

## 3. Phases Of Interphase

Interphase has three phases:

1. G1 phase
2. S phase
3. G2 phase

## 3.1 G1 Phase

G1 is the interval between mitosis and DNA replication.

During G1:

- cell is metabolically active;
- cell grows continuously;
- DNA is not replicated.

## 3.2 S Phase

S phase is the period of DNA synthesis or replication.

During S phase:

- DNA content doubles;
- chromosome number does not double;
- in animal cells, centriole duplicates in cytoplasm.

If a diploid cell has 2n chromosomes in G1, it still has 2n chromosomes after S phase, but each chromosome has two sister chromatids.

If DNA content is 2C in G1, it becomes 4C after S phase.

## 3.3 G2 Phase

During G2:

- proteins required for mitosis are synthesised;
- cell growth continues;
- DNA replication has already occurred.

## 4. G0 Phase

Some cells exit G1 and enter an inactive stage called G0 or quiescent phase.

These cells:

- remain metabolically active;
- do not proliferate;
- may divide later if required.

Examples include many adult animal cells that divide only occasionally or not at all under normal conditions.

## 5. M Phase

M phase is the phase of actual cell division.

It includes:

- karyokinesis, division of nucleus;
- cytokinesis, division of cytoplasm.

Mitosis is called equational division because daughter cells receive the same chromosome number as the parent cell.

## 6. Mitosis

Mitosis is divided into four stages:

1. Prophase
2. Metaphase
3. Anaphase
4. Telophase

These stages are continuous. The boundaries between them are not completely sharp in living cells.

## 7. Prophase

Prophase follows S and G2 phases.

## 7.1 Events Of Prophase

| Event | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Chromatin condensation | Chromosomal material condenses into visible chromosomes |
| Sister chromatids visible | Each chromosome has two chromatids joined at centromere |
| Centrosome movement | Duplicated centrosomes move toward opposite poles |
| Aster formation | Microtubules radiate from centrosomes |
| Spindle formation | Asters and spindle fibres form mitotic apparatus |
| Organelle changes | Golgi complex, ER, nucleolus, and nuclear envelope disappear by end of prophase |

## 7.2 Prophase Diagnostic Point

If chromosomes are condensing and spindle apparatus is forming, the cell is in prophase.

## 8. Metaphase

Metaphase begins after nuclear envelope disintegrates.

Chromosomes are highly condensed and clearly visible.

This is the best stage for studying chromosome morphology.

## 8.1 Events Of Metaphase

| Event | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Chromosomes align | Chromosomes move to the equator |
| Metaphase plate forms | Chromosomes lie along the equatorial plane |
| Kinetochore attachment | Spindle fibres attach to kinetochores |
| Opposite pole connection | Sister chromatids attach to spindle fibres from opposite poles |

## 8.2 Metaphase Diagnostic Point

If chromosomes are arranged at the equator and spindle fibres attach to kinetochores, the cell is in metaphase.

## 9. Anaphase

Anaphase begins when centromeres split.

## 9.1 Events Of Anaphase

| Event | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Centromeres split | Sister chromatids separate |
| Daughter chromosomes form | Separated chromatids are now called daughter chromosomes |
| Movement to poles | Daughter chromosomes move to opposite poles |
| Centromere leads | Centromere is directed toward pole; arms trail behind |

## 9.2 Anaphase Diagnostic Point

If sister chromatids are separating and moving to opposite poles, the cell is in anaphase.

## 10. Telophase

Telophase begins when chromosomes reach opposite poles.

## 10.1 Events Of Telophase

| Event | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Chromosomes decondense | Chromosomes lose distinct individuality |
| Chromatin clusters | Chromatin collects at two poles |
| Nuclear envelope reforms | Daughter nuclei form |
| Nucleolus reforms | Nucleoli reappear |
| Golgi and ER reform | Cytoplasmic organisation returns |

## 10.2 Telophase Diagnostic Point

If two daughter nuclei are reforming at opposite poles, the cell is in telophase.

## 11. Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis is division of cytoplasm.

Mitosis separates duplicated chromosomes into daughter nuclei. Cytokinesis separates the cytoplasm to form two daughter cells.

## 11.1 Cytokinesis In Animal Cells

In animal cells:

- a furrow appears in the plasma membrane;
- the furrow deepens;
- it joins in the centre;
- the cytoplasm divides into two.

## 11.2 Cytokinesis In Plant Cells

Plant cells have a rigid cell wall, so they cannot form a deep cleavage furrow.

Instead:

- wall formation begins at the centre;
- a cell plate forms;
- the cell plate grows outward;
- it meets the existing lateral walls;
- the cell plate becomes the middle lamella between daughter cells.

## 11.3 Cytokinesis Comparison

| Feature | Animal Cell | Plant Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Cleavage furrow | Cell plate |
| Direction | Outside inward | Centre outward |
| Reason | Flexible plasma membrane | Rigid cell wall |

## 11.4 Karyokinesis Without Cytokinesis

Sometimes nuclear division is not followed by cytoplasmic division.

This forms a multinucleate condition called syncytium.

Example: liquid endosperm in coconut.

## 12. Significance Of Mitosis

Mitosis is important because:

- it produces genetically identical daughter cells;
- it maintains chromosome number;
- it supports growth of multicellular organisms;
- it restores nucleo-cytoplasmic ratio;
- it replaces damaged or lost cells;
- it helps repair tissues;
- it supports continuous plant growth in meristematic tissues.

Examples of tissues replaced by mitosis:

- upper epidermal cells;
- gut lining cells;
- blood cells.

In plants, apical meristem and lateral cambium divide mitotically throughout life.

## 13. Meiosis

Meiosis is a specialised cell division that reduces chromosome number by half.

It produces haploid cells from diploid cells.

## 13.1 Why Meiosis Is Needed

Sexual reproduction involves fusion of two gametes.

If gametes were diploid, chromosome number would double every generation.

Meiosis produces haploid gametes. Fertilisation restores diploid condition.

## 13.2 Key Features Of Meiosis

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Two divisions | Meiosis I and meiosis II |
| One DNA replication | DNA replicates only once before meiosis I |
| Homologous pairing | Homologous chromosomes pair during prophase I |
| Recombination | Crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids |
| Final result | Four haploid cells |

## 13.3 Phases Of Meiosis

| Meiosis I | Meiosis II |
|---|---|
| Prophase I | Prophase II |
| Metaphase I | Metaphase II |
| Anaphase I | Anaphase II |
| Telophase I | Telophase II |

## 14. Meiosis I

Meiosis I is reductional division.

Homologous chromosomes separate, and chromosome number becomes half.

## 14.1 Prophase I

Prophase I is longer and more complex than mitotic prophase.

It has five stages:

1. Leptotene
2. Zygotene
3. Pachytene
4. Diplotene
5. Diakinesis

## 14.2 Leptotene

During leptotene:

- chromosomes become gradually visible;
- chromosome compaction begins and continues.

## 14.3 Zygotene

During zygotene:

- homologous chromosomes pair;
- pairing is called synapsis;
- synaptonemal complex forms.

The paired homologous chromosomes form a bivalent.

Because each homolog has two chromatids after replication, the bivalent has four chromatids and is also called a tetrad.

## 14.4 Pachytene

During pachytene:

- four chromatids of each bivalent become distinct;
- recombination nodules appear;
- crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes.

Crossing over is enzyme-mediated.

The enzyme involved is recombinase.

Crossing over creates recombination of genetic material.

## 14.5 Diplotene

During diplotene:

- synaptonemal complex dissolves;
- homologous chromosomes tend to separate;
- they remain attached at crossover points.

These X-shaped crossover points are called chiasmata.

In oocytes of some vertebrates, diplotene may last for months or years.

## 14.6 Diakinesis

During diakinesis:

- chiasmata undergo terminalisation;
- chromosomes are fully condensed;
- meiotic spindle forms;
- nucleolus disappears;
- nuclear envelope breaks down.

Diakinesis marks transition to metaphase I.

## 14.7 Metaphase I

During metaphase I:

- bivalent chromosomes align on the equatorial plate;
- spindle microtubules from opposite poles attach to kinetochores of homologous chromosomes.

## 14.8 Anaphase I

During anaphase I:

- homologous chromosomes separate;
- sister chromatids remain joined at centromeres;
- chromosome number is reduced.

## 14.9 Telophase I

During telophase I:

- nuclear membrane reappears;
- nucleolus reappears;
- cytokinesis follows;
- a dyad of cells forms.

Chromosomes may disperse partly but do not return fully to interphase condition.

## 14.10 Interkinesis

Interkinesis is the short stage between meiosis I and meiosis II.

There is no DNA replication during interkinesis.

## 15. Meiosis II

Meiosis II resembles mitosis.

It separates sister chromatids.

## 15.1 Prophase II

During prophase II:

- chromosomes become compact again;
- nuclear membrane disappears by the end.

## 15.2 Metaphase II

During metaphase II:

- chromosomes align at the equator;
- spindle microtubules attach to kinetochores of sister chromatids from opposite poles.

## 15.3 Anaphase II

During anaphase II:

- centromeres split;
- sister chromatids separate;
- chromatids move to opposite poles.

## 15.4 Telophase II

During telophase II:

- chromosomes are enclosed by nuclear envelopes;
- cytokinesis follows;
- four haploid daughter cells are formed.

## 16. Significance Of Meiosis

Meiosis is important because:

- it produces haploid gametes or spores;
- it maintains species-specific chromosome number across generations;
- fertilisation restores diploid number;
- crossing over creates genetic recombination;
- independent assortment increases variation;
- variation supports evolution.

## 17. High-Yield Comparison Tables

## 17.1 Mitosis Versus Meiosis

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| Number of divisions | One | Two |
| DNA replication | Once before division | Once before meiosis I |
| Daughter cells | Two | Four |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent | Half of parent |
| Genetic similarity | Daughter cells usually identical | Daughter cells genetically different |
| Homologous pairing | Absent | Present in prophase I |
| Crossing over | Absent | Present in pachytene |
| Main role | Growth, repair, replacement | Gamete or spore formation |

## 17.2 Anaphase Of Mitosis Versus Anaphase I Of Meiosis

| Feature | Anaphase Of Mitosis | Anaphase I Of Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| What separates | Sister chromatids | Homologous chromosomes |
| Centromere | Splits | Does not split |
| Sister chromatids | Separate | Remain together |
| Chromosome number effect | Maintained | Reduced by half |

## 17.3 Meiosis I Versus Meiosis II

| Feature | Meiosis I | Meiosis II |
|---|---|---|
| Type | Reductional division | Equational division |
| Main separation | Homologous chromosomes | Sister chromatids |
| DNA replication before it | Yes, before meiosis I | No |
| Crossing over | Occurs in prophase I | Absent |
| Result | Two haploid cells with duplicated chromosomes | Four haploid cells |

## 18. Chromosome Number And DNA Content

Use `n` for chromosome sets and `C` for DNA content.

## 18.1 Mitotic Cell Cycle In A Diploid Cell

| Stage | Chromosome Number | DNA Content | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| G1 | 2n | 2C | DNA not yet replicated |
| After S | 2n | 4C | DNA doubled, chromosome number same |
| G2 | 2n | 4C | Cell prepares for mitosis |
| Metaphase | 2n | 4C | Chromosomes have sister chromatids |
| Anaphase | Daughter chromosomes separating | 4C in one dividing cell | Centromeres split; each chromatid becomes chromosome |
| After cytokinesis | 2n in each daughter cell | 2C in each daughter cell | Original condition restored |

## 18.2 Meiosis In A Diploid Cell

| Stage | Chromosome Number Per Cell | DNA Content Per Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Before S phase | 2n | 2C |
| After S phase | 2n | 4C |
| After meiosis I | n | 2C |
| After meiosis II | n | C |

## 19. Text-Based Diagrams

## 19.1 Synapsis, Bivalent, And Chiasma

```text
Before synapsis:
Homolog 1: A - B - C
Homolog 2: a - b - c

After synapsis:
Homologs pair closely to form a bivalent.

Bivalent or tetrad:
Two homologous chromosomes
Four chromatids total

After crossing over:
X-shaped point of contact is chiasma.
```

## 19.2 Mitosis Flow

```text
Prophase -> Metaphase -> Anaphase -> Telophase -> Cytokinesis

Condense -> Align -> Separate chromatids -> Reform nuclei -> Divide cytoplasm
```

## 19.3 Meiosis Flow

```text
DNA replication
Meiosis I: homologous chromosomes separate
Interkinesis: no DNA replication
Meiosis II: sister chromatids separate
Result: four haploid cells
```

## 20. Common Learner Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling interphase inactive | Interphase is metabolically active |
| Saying DNA and chromosome number both double in S phase | DNA doubles, chromosome number remains same |
| Confusing karyokinesis with cytokinesis | Karyokinesis is nuclear division; cytokinesis is cytoplasm division |
| Saying centromere splits in anaphase I | Centromere does not split in anaphase I |
| Confusing bivalent and tetrad | A bivalent is a pair of homologous chromosomes; it contains four chromatids, so it is also a tetrad |
| Saying crossing over occurs in zygotene | Crossing over occurs in pachytene |
| Saying meiosis II reduces chromosome number | Reduction occurs in meiosis I |
| Forgetting interkinesis has no DNA replication | DNA is not replicated between meiosis I and meiosis II |

## 21. Exercise Answers

## 21.1 Average Cell Cycle Span For A Mammalian Cell

The average cell cycle span for a typical mammalian cell such as a human cell in culture is about 24 hours.

## 21.2 Distinguish Cytokinesis From Karyokinesis

| Feature | Karyokinesis | Cytokinesis |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Division of nucleus | Division of cytoplasm |
| Occurs during | M phase | Usually after nuclear division |
| Result | Daughter nuclei | Daughter cells |
| Example event | Chromosome separation | Cleavage furrow or cell plate |

## 21.3 Events During Interphase

Interphase has G1, S, and G2 phases.

| Phase | Events |
|---|---|
| G1 | Cell grows, metabolism continues, DNA not replicated |
| S | DNA replication occurs, DNA content doubles, centriole duplicates in animal cells |
| G2 | Cell grows, proteins for mitosis are synthesised |

## 21.4 What Is G0 Phase?

G0 phase is the quiescent phase of the cell cycle.

Cells in G0:

- leave G1;
- remain metabolically active;
- do not divide;
- may re-enter cell cycle if required.

## 21.5 Why Is Mitosis Called Equational Division?

Mitosis is called equational division because daughter cells receive the same chromosome number as the parent cell.

The genetic complement remains identical in normal mitosis.

## 21.6 Name The Stages For Specific Events

| Event | Stage |
|---|---|
| Chromosomes move to spindle equator | Metaphase |
| Centromere splits and chromatids separate | Anaphase |
| Pairing of homologous chromosomes | Zygotene of prophase I |
| Crossing over between homologous chromosomes | Pachytene of prophase I |

## 21.7 Describe Synapsis

Synapsis is pairing of homologous chromosomes during zygotene of prophase I.

It is accompanied by formation of the synaptonemal complex.

## 21.8 Describe Bivalent

A bivalent is a pair of synapsed homologous chromosomes.

Since each homolog has two sister chromatids after DNA replication, a bivalent has four chromatids and is also called a tetrad.

## 21.9 Describe Chiasmata

Chiasmata are X-shaped points where homologous chromosomes remain attached after crossing over.

They become visible during diplotene.

Use Section 19.1 for a text-based diagram guide.

## 21.10 Plant Cytokinesis Versus Animal Cytokinesis

| Feature | Animal Cell | Plant Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Method | Cleavage furrow | Cell plate |
| Direction | Outside inward | Centre outward |
| Cause | Flexible membrane | Rigid cell wall |
| Final structure | Two separated cells | New cell wall and middle lamella |

## 21.11 Equal And Unequal Daughter Cells From Meiosis

Equal-sized meiotic products:

- spermatogenesis in many animals produces four functional sperms of similar size;
- microspore formation in many plants may produce four microspores.

Unequal-sized meiotic products:

- oogenesis in animals produces one large ovum and smaller polar bodies;
- megaspore formation in many angiosperms often results in one functional megaspore and degenerating others.

## 21.12 Anaphase Of Mitosis Versus Anaphase I Of Meiosis

In mitotic anaphase, centromeres split and sister chromatids separate.

In anaphase I of meiosis, homologous chromosomes separate, but sister chromatids remain joined at their centromeres.

## 21.13 Main Differences Between Mitosis And Meiosis

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| Divisions | One | Two |
| Daughter cells | Two | Four |
| Chromosome number | Maintained | Reduced by half |
| Synapsis | Absent | Present |
| Crossing over | Absent | Present |
| Genetic variation | Usually absent | Produced |
| Function | Growth and repair | Gamete or spore formation |

## 21.14 Significance Of Meiosis

Meiosis is significant because:

- it produces haploid gametes or spores;
- it maintains chromosome number across generations after fertilisation;
- it creates genetic variation through crossing over;
- it contributes to evolution.

## 21.15 Teacher Extension: Haploid Cells That Divide And Do Not Divide

Examples where haploid cells divide:

- male honey bees are haploid and their cells divide by mitosis;
- haploid gametophyte stages of plants divide by mitosis.

Examples where haploid cells may not divide further:

- egg cell in higher plants generally does not divide before fertilisation;
- some specialised gametophytic cells have fixed roles and do not keep dividing.

## 21.16 Can There Be Mitosis Without DNA Replication In S Phase?

In a normal cell cycle, mitosis should not occur without DNA replication in S phase.

If mitosis occurred without DNA replication, daughter cells would not receive a complete genome.

Therefore, proper S phase completion is required before mitosis.

## 21.17 Can There Be DNA Replication Without Cell Division?

Yes, DNA replication can occur without immediate cell division in some cases.

This may lead to increased DNA content or polyploid cells.

Examples include endoreduplication and cells that replicate DNA but do not complete mitosis or cytokinesis.

## 21.18 Change In Chromosome Number And DNA Content

For a diploid mitotic cell:

| Stage | Chromosome Number | DNA Content |
|---|---|---|
| G1 | 2n | 2C |
| After S | 2n | 4C |
| G2 | 2n | 4C |
| After M phase and cytokinesis | 2n per daughter cell | 2C per daughter cell |

For meiosis:

| Stage | Chromosome Number Per Cell | DNA Content Per Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Before S | 2n | 2C |
| After S | 2n | 4C |
| After meiosis I | n | 2C |
| After meiosis II | n | C |

## 22. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 22.1 Why Does Chromosome Number Not Increase After S Phase?

During S phase, DNA replicates and sister chromatids form. The centromere still holds the sister chromatids together, so chromosome number remains the same.

## 22.2 Why Is Metaphase Best For Studying Chromosomes?

At metaphase, chromosomes are maximally condensed and aligned at the metaphase plate, making their morphology easiest to observe.

## 22.3 Why Is Prophase I Long?

Prophase I includes pairing of homologous chromosomes, synaptonemal complex formation, crossing over, recombination, chiasma formation, and spindle preparation.

## 22.4 Why Is Meiosis I Reductional?

Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes, reducing chromosome number from diploid to haploid.

## 22.5 Why Is Meiosis II Equational?

Meiosis II separates sister chromatids without further reducing chromosome set number.
