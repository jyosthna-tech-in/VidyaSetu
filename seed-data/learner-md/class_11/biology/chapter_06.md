## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is about internal arrangement, not external shape. Learn it as a set of tissue positions.

Use this order:

1. Learn the three tissue systems.
2. Learn vascular bundle types: radial, conjoint, open, closed.
3. Study root anatomy first, then stem anatomy.
4. Compare dicot and monocot organs side by side.
5. Study leaf anatomy by location: epidermis, mesophyll, vascular bundles.

For exams, the most common skill is diagnosis. If a section shows scattered vascular bundles and no phloem parenchyma, it is a monocot stem. If a root has two to four xylem patches and a small pith, it is a dicot root.

## 1. What Is Plant Anatomy?

Anatomy is the study of internal structure.

In plants:

- cells are the basic units;
- cells form tissues;
- tissues form organs;
- different organs show different internal organisation.

Angiosperms show anatomical differences between dicots and monocots. Internal structure also reveals adaptations to environment.

## 2. Tissue Systems In Flowering Plants

On the basis of structure and location, flowering plants have three tissue systems.

| Tissue System | Main Components | Main Function |
|---|---|---|
| Epidermal tissue system | Epidermis, stomata, trichomes, root hairs | Protection, absorption, gas exchange |
| Ground tissue system | Parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma | Storage, support, photosynthesis |
| Vascular tissue system | Xylem and phloem | Conduction of water, minerals, and food |

## 3. Epidermal Tissue System

The epidermal tissue system forms the outermost covering of the whole plant body.

It includes:

- epidermal cells;
- stomata;
- trichomes;
- root hairs.

## 3.1 Epidermis

The epidermis is the outermost layer of the primary plant body.

## 3.2 Features Of Epidermal Cells

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Arrangement | Compactly arranged |
| Layering | Usually single-layered |
| Cell type | Parenchymatous |
| Cytoplasm | Small amount lining the wall |
| Vacuole | Large |
| Cuticle | Often present outside, absent in roots |

## 3.3 Cuticle

The cuticle is a waxy layer outside the epidermis.

It helps prevent water loss.

Cuticle is absent in roots because roots must absorb water and minerals.

## 3.4 Stomata

Stomata are pores present in the epidermis of leaves.

They regulate:

- transpiration;
- gaseous exchange.

## 3.5 Structure Of A Stoma

Each stoma has:

- stomatal pore;
- two guard cells;
- sometimes subsidiary cells around guard cells.

Guard cells are bean-shaped in many plants.

In grasses, guard cells are dumbbell-shaped.

## 3.6 Guard Cells

Guard cells regulate opening and closing of stomata.

| Part Of Guard Cell | Feature |
|---|---|
| Outer wall away from pore | Thin |
| Inner wall toward pore | Thick |
| Chloroplasts | Present |

The unequal wall thickness helps guard cells change shape during opening and closing.

## 3.7 Subsidiary Cells

Some epidermal cells near guard cells become specialised in shape and size. These are called subsidiary cells.

## 3.8 Stomatal Apparatus

The stomatal apparatus includes:

- stomatal pore;
- guard cells;
- subsidiary cells.

## 3.10 Root Hairs

Root hairs are unicellular elongations of epidermal cells.

They absorb water and minerals from soil.

## 3.11 Trichomes

Trichomes are epidermal hairs on the stem or shoot system.

They are usually multicellular.

They may be:

- branched or unbranched;
- soft or stiff;
- secretory or non-secretory.

Trichomes help reduce water loss due to transpiration.

## 3.12 Root Hairs Versus Trichomes

| Feature | Root Hairs | Trichomes |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Root epidermis | Shoot epidermis |
| Cell number | Unicellular | Usually multicellular |
| Function | Absorption of water and minerals | Protection and reduction of water loss |
| Cuticle relation | Roots lack cuticle | Shoots often have cuticle |

## 4. Ground Tissue System

The ground tissue system includes all tissues except epidermis and vascular bundles.

It consists mainly of simple tissues:

- parenchyma;
- collenchyma;
- sclerenchyma.

## 4.1 Locations Of Ground Tissue

| Organ Or Region | Ground Tissue Examples |
|---|---|
| Primary stem | Cortex, pericycle, pith, medullary rays |
| Root | Cortex, pericycle, pith |
| Leaf | Mesophyll |

## 4.2 Mesophyll

In leaves, the ground tissue is called mesophyll.

Mesophyll cells are thin-walled and contain chloroplasts.

Their main function is photosynthesis.

## 5. Vascular Tissue System

The vascular tissue system is made of complex tissues:

- xylem;
- phloem.

Together, xylem and phloem form vascular bundles.

## 5.1 Xylem And Phloem

| Tissue | Main Function |
|---|---|
| Xylem | Conducts water and minerals |
| Phloem | Conducts food materials |

## 5.2 Open And Closed Vascular Bundles

| Type | Cambium | Secondary Growth | Common Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Open vascular bundle | Present | Possible | Dicot stem |
| Closed vascular bundle | Absent | Not possible through bundle cambium | Monocot stem |

In dicot stems, cambium is present between xylem and phloem. Such bundles can form secondary xylem and secondary phloem.

In monocots, cambium is absent in vascular bundles, so the bundles are closed.

## 5.3 Radial And Conjoint Vascular Bundles

| Type | Arrangement | Common Location |
|---|---|---|
| Radial | Xylem and phloem on alternate radii | Roots |
| Conjoint | Xylem and phloem on same radius | Stems and leaves |

In conjoint bundles, phloem is usually on the outer side of xylem.

## 6. Anatomy Of Roots, Stems, And Leaves

Transverse sections of mature zones are used to study internal organisation.

The key is to note:

- outermost layer;
- cortex;
- endodermis;
- pericycle;
- vascular bundle arrangement;
- pith;
- special features.

## 7. Dicotyledonous Root

The sunflower root is a common example of dicot root anatomy.

## 7.1 Tissue Arrangement In Dicot Root

| Region | Description |
|---|---|
| Epiblema | Outermost layer; many cells form unicellular root hairs |
| Cortex | Several layers of thin-walled parenchyma with intercellular spaces |
| Endodermis | Innermost cortex; single layer of barrel-shaped cells |
| Casparian strips | Suberin deposits on radial and tangential walls of endodermis |
| Pericycle | Few layers of thick-walled parenchymatous cells |
| Vascular tissue | Usually two to four xylem and phloem patches |
| Conjunctive tissue | Parenchyma between xylem and phloem |
| Pith | Small or inconspicuous |
| Stele | All tissues inside endodermis |

## 7.2 Role Of Pericycle In Dicot Root

Pericycle gives rise to:

- lateral roots;
- vascular cambium during secondary growth.

## 7.3 Casparian Strips

Casparian strips are suberin deposits in endodermal cell walls.

Suberin is water-impermeable and helps regulate movement of water and solutes into the stele.

## 7.4 Dicot Root Diagnostic Points

To identify a dicot root, look for:

- epiblema with root hairs;
- radial vascular bundles;
- two to four xylem patches in many forms;
- small or inconspicuous pith;
- possible secondary growth.

## 8. Monocotyledonous Root

Monocot root anatomy is similar to dicot root anatomy in many basic layers, but it differs in vascular bundle number and pith.

## 8.1 Tissue Arrangement In Monocot Root

| Region | Description |
|---|---|
| Epidermis or epiblema | Outermost layer |
| Cortex | Parenchymatous |
| Endodermis | Present |
| Pericycle | Present |
| Vascular bundles | More than six xylem bundles, called polyarch |
| Pith | Large and well developed |
| Secondary growth | Absent |

## 8.2 Monocot Root Diagnostic Points

To identify a monocot root, look for:

- radial vascular bundles;
- many xylem bundles, usually more than six;
- large pith;
- no secondary growth.

## 9. Dicot Root Versus Monocot Root

| Feature | Dicot Root | Monocot Root |
|---|---|---|
| Xylem bundles | Usually 2 to 4 | Usually more than 6 |
| Xylem condition | Diarch to tetrarch common | Polyarch |
| Pith | Small or inconspicuous | Large and well developed |
| Secondary growth | Usually possible | Absent |
| Pericycle role | Lateral roots and cambium initiation | Lateral roots, but no normal secondary growth |

## 10. Dicotyledonous Stem

A young dicot stem shows a ring arrangement of vascular bundles.

## 10.1 Tissue Arrangement In Dicot Stem

| Region | Description |
|---|---|
| Epidermis | Outermost protective layer |
| Cuticle | Thin layer outside epidermis |
| Trichomes and stomata | May be present |
| Cortex | Multiple layers between epidermis and pericycle |
| Hypodermis | Few layers of collenchyma below epidermis |
| General cortex | Rounded parenchyma with intercellular spaces |
| Endodermis | Innermost cortex, rich in starch grains |
| Pericycle | Semilunar patches of sclerenchyma above phloem |
| Medullary rays | Radial parenchyma between vascular bundles |
| Vascular bundles | Arranged in a ring |
| Bundle type | Conjoint, open, endarch |
| Pith | Large central parenchymatous region |

## 10.2 Starch Sheath

In dicot stem, the endodermis contains starch grains and is called the starch sheath.

## 10.3 Vascular Bundles In Dicot Stem

The vascular bundles are:

- arranged in a ring;
- conjoint;
- open;
- endarch.

Open means cambium is present.

Endarch means protoxylem is toward the centre and metaxylem is toward the outside.

## 10.4 Dicot Stem Diagnostic Points

To identify a dicot stem, look for:

- epidermis with cuticle;
- collenchymatous hypodermis;
- cortex differentiated into zones;
- vascular bundles in a ring;
- open conjoint bundles;
- medullary rays;
- large central pith.

## 11. Monocotyledonous Stem

Monocot stem has scattered vascular bundles and undifferentiated ground tissue.

## 11.1 Tissue Arrangement In Monocot Stem

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Hypodermis | Sclerenchymatous |
| Ground tissue | Large, conspicuous, parenchymatous |
| Vascular bundles | Numerous and scattered |
| Bundle sheath | Sclerenchymatous |
| Bundle type | Conjoint and closed |
| Peripheral bundles | Smaller |
| Central bundles | Larger |
| Phloem parenchyma | Absent |
| Water-containing cavities | Present in vascular bundles |

## 11.2 Monocot Stem Diagnostic Points

To identify a monocot stem, look for:

- scattered vascular bundles;
- sclerenchymatous hypodermis;
- sclerenchymatous bundle sheath;
- closed vascular bundles;
- absence of phloem parenchyma;
- large ground tissue;
- no ring arrangement of vascular bundles.

## 12. Dicot Stem Versus Monocot Stem

| Feature | Dicot Stem | Monocot Stem |
|---|---|---|
| Hypodermis | Collenchymatous | Sclerenchymatous |
| Ground tissue | Differentiated into cortex, endodermis, pericycle, pith | Generally undifferentiated |
| Vascular bundles | Arranged in a ring | Scattered |
| Bundle type | Conjoint, open | Conjoint, closed |
| Cambium | Present | Absent |
| Medullary rays | Present | Absent |
| Pith | Distinct | Not distinct |
| Phloem parenchyma | Present | Absent |

## 13. Dorsiventral Dicotyledonous Leaf

A dorsiventral leaf has different upper and lower surfaces.

It is typical of many dicot leaves.

## 13.1 Main Parts Of Dorsiventral Leaf

The vertical section through the lamina shows:

1. Epidermis
2. Mesophyll
3. Vascular system

## 13.2 Epidermis In Dicot Leaf

| Surface | Feature |
|---|---|
| Adaxial epidermis | Upper surface |
| Abaxial epidermis | Lower surface |
| Cuticle | Conspicuous |
| Stomata | More on lower epidermis; upper may have few or none |

## 13.3 Mesophyll In Dicot Leaf

Mesophyll contains chloroplasts and carries out photosynthesis.

It is differentiated into:

- palisade parenchyma;
- spongy parenchyma.

## 13.4 Palisade Parenchyma

Palisade parenchyma is placed near the upper epidermis.

Cells are elongated, vertical, and parallel to each other.

They contain chloroplasts and are important for photosynthesis.

## 13.5 Spongy Parenchyma

Spongy parenchyma is located below palisade cells and extends toward the lower epidermis.

Cells are oval or round and loosely arranged.

Large air spaces are present between the cells.

## 13.6 Vascular System In Dicot Leaf

Vascular bundles occur in veins and midrib.

Bundle size depends on vein size.

In dicot leaves, reticulate venation creates veins of varying thickness.

Vascular bundles are surrounded by thick-walled bundle sheath cells.

In a typical leaf vascular bundle:

- xylem is toward the upper side;
- phloem is toward the lower side.

## 14. Isobilateral Monocotyledonous Leaf

An isobilateral leaf has similar upper and lower surfaces.

It is typical of many monocot leaves.

## 14.1 Main Features Of Isobilateral Leaf

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Stomata | Present on both epidermal surfaces |
| Mesophyll | Not differentiated into palisade and spongy parenchyma |
| Venation | Parallel |
| Vascular bundle size | Nearly similar except in main veins |
| Bulliform cells | Present in grasses |

## 14.2 Bulliform Cells

In grasses, some upper epidermal cells along veins become large, empty, and colourless. These are called bulliform cells.

When bulliform cells are turgid, the leaf surface remains exposed.

When water stress makes them flaccid, the leaf curls inward. This reduces water loss.

## 15. Dorsiventral Versus Isobilateral Leaf

| Feature | Dorsiventral Leaf | Isobilateral Leaf |
|---|---|---|
| Common in | Dicots | Monocots |
| Upper and lower surfaces | Different | Similar |
| Stomata | More on lower surface | On both surfaces |
| Mesophyll | Palisade and spongy layers | Not differentiated |
| Venation | Reticulate | Parallel |
| Bulliform cells | Absent | Present in grasses |

## 18.3 How To Identify Whether A Young Stem Is Monocot Or Dicot

Observe the transverse section under a microscope.

If the vascular bundles are arranged in a ring, cambium is present, bundles are open, medullary rays are present, and pith is distinct, it is a dicot stem.

If the vascular bundles are scattered, each bundle has a sclerenchymatous bundle sheath, cambium is absent, bundles are closed, phloem parenchyma is absent, and the ground tissue is not differentiated into cortex and pith, it is a monocot stem.

## 18.4 Identification From Given Features

Given features:

- vascular bundles are conjoint;
- vascular bundles are scattered;
- vascular bundles are surrounded by sclerenchymatous bundle sheaths;
- phloem parenchyma is absent.

Identification: monocotyledonous stem.

Reason: scattered closed conjoint vascular bundles with sclerenchymatous bundle sheath and absence of phloem parenchyma are characteristic of monocot stem.

## 18.5 What Is Stomatal Apparatus?

The stomatal apparatus includes:

- stomatal pore;
- two guard cells;
- surrounding subsidiary cells.

Stomata regulate transpiration and gaseous exchange.

## 18.6 Structure Of Stomata

Each stoma has a pore surrounded by two guard cells.

Guard cells are bean-shaped in many dicots and dumbbell-shaped in grasses.

The outer walls of guard cells are thin, while inner walls facing the stomatal pore are thick.

Guard cells contain chloroplasts and control the opening and closing of the pore.

Subsidiary cells are specialised epidermal cells near guard cells.

Use the text-based labelled stoma in Section 3.9 for diagram labels.

## 18.7 Three Basic Tissue Systems In Flowering Plants

| Tissue System | Tissues Or Structures Included |
|---|---|
| Epidermal tissue system | Epidermis, stomata, root hairs, trichomes |
| Ground tissue system | Parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma; cortex, pericycle, pith, medullary rays, mesophyll |
| Vascular tissue system | Xylem and phloem |

## 18.8 How Is Plant Anatomy Useful?

Plant anatomy is useful because it helps us:

- understand internal organisation of plant organs;
- distinguish monocots and dicots;
- identify roots, stems, and leaves from sections;
- understand functions such as conduction, photosynthesis, storage, support, and protection;
- study adaptations to environments;
- understand secondary growth and wood formation;
- support plant classification;
- connect structure with physiology.

## 18.9 Internal Structure Of A Dorsiventral Leaf

A dorsiventral leaf has three main parts: epidermis, mesophyll, and vascular system.

The epidermis covers both upper and lower surfaces. The upper surface is called adaxial epidermis, and the lower surface is called abaxial epidermis. The cuticle is conspicuous. Stomata are generally more numerous on the lower epidermis.

The mesophyll lies between the two epidermal layers. It is divided into palisade parenchyma and spongy parenchyma. Palisade cells are elongated and arranged vertically near the upper epidermis. Spongy cells are loosely arranged with air spaces and extend toward the lower epidermis.

The vascular system occurs in veins and midrib. Vascular bundles are surrounded by bundle sheath cells. Xylem is usually toward the upper side and phloem toward the lower side.

For diagram labels, include upper epidermis, cuticle, palisade parenchyma, spongy parenchyma, air spaces, vascular bundle, bundle sheath, xylem, phloem, lower epidermis, and stomata.
