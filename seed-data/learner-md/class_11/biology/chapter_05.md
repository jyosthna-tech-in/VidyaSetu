## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is vocabulary-heavy, but the terms are not random. Each term describes a visible structure or a structural relationship.

Study in this order:

1. Learn the root system and shoot system.
2. Study each vegetative part: root, stem, leaf.
3. Study reproductive parts: inflorescence, flower, fruit, seed.
4. Learn how floral parts are described in a fixed sequence.
5. Practise comparing similar terms such as racemose and cymose, apocarpous and syncarpous, reticulate and parallel venation.

The chapter rewards exact language. For example, "pinnately compound leaf" and "palmately compound leaf" are not interchangeable. The same is true for "superior ovary", "inferior ovary", "hypogynous", "perigynous", and "epigynous".

## 1. Why Morphology Matters

Morphology means the study of external form and structure. In flowering plants, morphology helps us identify plant parts, compare species, and classify plants.

Flowering plants show great diversity in size, shape, habitat, life span, and structure. Even with this diversity, most angiosperms have:

- roots;
- stems;
- leaves;
- flowers;
- fruits;
- seeds.

To describe plants scientifically, standard terms are needed. These terms help avoid vague descriptions.

For example, "leaf divided into many parts" is vague. "Pinnately compound leaf" is precise.

## 2. Root System And Shoot System

A flowering plant body can be divided into two major systems.

| System | Position | Main Parts |
|---|---|---|
| Root system | Usually underground | Primary root, lateral roots, root hairs |
| Shoot system | Usually above ground | Stem, branches, leaves, flowers, fruits |

The root system mainly absorbs and anchors. The shoot system mainly supports, conducts, photosynthesises, and reproduces.

## 3. The Root

The root usually develops from the radicle of the embryo.

## 3.1 Types Of Root Systems

## 3.1.1 Tap Root System

In most dicotyledonous plants, the radicle elongates directly to form the primary root.

The primary root gives rise to lateral roots of different orders, such as secondary and tertiary roots.

Together, these form the tap root system.

Example: mustard.

## 3.1.2 Fibrous Root System

In monocotyledonous plants, the primary root is short-lived.

It is replaced by many roots that arise from the base of the stem.

Together, these form a fibrous root system.

Example: wheat.

## 3.1.3 Adventitious Root System

Adventitious roots arise from plant parts other than the radicle.

Examples:

- grass;
- Monstera;
- banyan.

## 3.2 Comparison Of Root Systems

| Feature | Tap Root | Fibrous Root | Adventitious Root |
|---|---|---|---|
| Origin | Radicle | Base of stem after primary root becomes short-lived | Any part other than radicle |
| Common in | Dicots | Monocots | Many plants depending on adaptation |
| Main structure | One main primary root with branches | Many similar roots | Roots from unusual plant parts |
| Example | Mustard | Wheat | Banyan, Monstera, grass |

## 3.3 Functions Of Roots

Roots perform several functions:

- absorb water and minerals from soil;
- anchor the plant body;
- store reserve food in some plants;
- synthesise plant growth regulators;
- provide mechanical support in modified roots;
- help respiration in some specialised roots.

## 3.4 Regions Of The Root Tip

The root tip has distinct regions.

| Region | Position | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Root cap | Covers the root apex | Protects tender root tip as it grows through soil |
| Region of meristematic activity | Just above root cap | Cells divide repeatedly |
| Region of elongation | Above meristematic zone | Cells elongate and increase root length |
| Region of maturation | Above elongation zone | Cells mature and differentiate |
| Root hair zone | Part of maturation region | Root hairs absorb water and minerals |

## 3.5 Root Hairs

Root hairs are fine, delicate, thread-like structures formed by some epidermal cells in the region of maturation.

They greatly increase the surface area for absorption.

## 3.6 Root Modifications

The raw chapter summary mentions that roots may be modified for storage, support, and respiration.

| Modification | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Storage roots | Store food | Carrot, turnip, sweet potato |
| Prop roots | Support heavy branches | Banyan |
| Stilt roots | Support stem from lower nodes | Maize, sugarcane |
| Pneumatophores | Help respiration in swampy habitats | Rhizophora |

## 4. The Stem

The stem develops from the plumule of the embryo.

It is the ascending part of the plant axis and bears branches, leaves, flowers, and fruits.

## 4.1 Features Of Stem

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Origin | Plumule |
| Direction | Usually ascending |
| Nodes | Points where leaves arise |
| Internodes | Regions between nodes |
| Buds | Terminal or axillary |
| Young stem | Often green |
| Older stem | May become woody and dark brown |

## 4.2 Nodes And Internodes

Nodes are regions where leaves are borne.

Internodes are portions of stem between two nodes.

This node-internode pattern is a key way to distinguish a stem from a root.

## 4.3 Buds

| Bud Type | Position |
|---|---|
| Terminal bud | At the tip of the stem or branch |
| Axillary bud | In the axil of a leaf |

Axillary buds may develop into branches or flowers.

## 4.4 Functions Of Stem

The stem:

- spreads branches bearing leaves, flowers, and fruits;
- conducts water and minerals upward;
- conducts photosynthates from leaves to other parts;
- supports the shoot system;
- stores food in some plants;
- protects the plant in modified forms;
- helps vegetative propagation in some plants.

## 4.5 Stem Modifications

| Modification | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Underground stem | Food storage and perennation | Potato, ginger, turmeric |
| Stem tendril | Climbing | Cucumber, grapevine |
| Stem thorn | Protection | Citrus, Bougainvillea |
| Phylloclade | Photosynthesis in dry habitats | Opuntia |
| Runner or stolon | Vegetative propagation | Grasses, strawberry |

## 5. The Leaf

The leaf is a lateral, usually flattened structure borne on the stem.

It develops at the node and bears a bud in its axil.

Leaves arise from shoot apical meristems and are arranged in acropetal order.

The leaf is the main vegetative organ for photosynthesis.

## 5.1 Parts Of A Typical Leaf

A typical leaf has three main parts:

1. Leaf base
2. Petiole
3. Lamina

## 5.2 Leaf Base

The leaf base attaches the leaf to the stem.

It may bear two small lateral leaf-like structures called stipules.

In many monocotyledons, the leaf base expands into a sheath that partly or completely covers the stem.

In some leguminous plants, the leaf base becomes swollen and is called pulvinus.

## 5.3 Petiole

The petiole is the stalk of the leaf.

It helps hold the leaf blade toward light.

Long, thin, flexible petioles allow leaf blades to flutter in wind. This helps cooling and brings fresh air to the leaf surface.

## 5.4 Lamina

The lamina is the green expanded part of the leaf.

It contains veins and veinlets.

The prominent middle vein is called the midrib.

Veins:

- provide rigidity;
- transport water and minerals;
- transport food materials.

The shape, margin, apex, surface, and incision of lamina vary among plants.

## 6. Venation

Venation is the arrangement of veins and veinlets in the leaf lamina.

## 6.1 Reticulate Venation

In reticulate venation, veinlets form a network.

It is common in dicotyledonous plants.

## 6.2 Parallel Venation

In parallel venation, veins run parallel to each other within the lamina.

It is common in monocotyledonous plants.

## 6.3 Reticulate Versus Parallel Venation

| Feature | Reticulate Venation | Parallel Venation |
|---|---|---|
| Vein pattern | Network-like | Parallel |
| Common in | Dicots | Monocots |
| Example context | Many broad leaves | Grasses and many monocot leaves |

## 7. Types Of Leaves

Leaves may be simple or compound.

## 7.1 Simple Leaf

A leaf is simple if:

- the lamina is entire; or
- the lamina is incised, but the incisions do not reach the midrib.

## 7.2 Compound Leaf

A leaf is compound if the incisions of lamina reach the midrib and divide it into leaflets.

Important rule:

A bud is present in the axil of the whole leaf, not in the axil of individual leaflets.

## 7.3 Pinnately Compound Leaf

In a pinnately compound leaf, leaflets are arranged on a common axis called rachis.

The rachis represents the midrib of the leaf.

Example: neem.

## 7.4 Palmately Compound Leaf

In a palmately compound leaf, leaflets are attached at a common point, usually at the tip of the petiole.

Example: silk cotton.

## 7.5 Leaf Type Comparison

| Feature | Simple Leaf | Pinnately Compound Leaf | Palmately Compound Leaf |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lamina | Single | Divided into leaflets | Divided into leaflets |
| Midrib or rachis | Lamina not broken into separate leaflets | Leaflets on rachis | Leaflets from one common point |
| Bud | At leaf axil | At leaf axil, not leaflet axils | At leaf axil, not leaflet axils |
| Example | Mango leaf | Neem | Silk cotton |

## 8. Phyllotaxy

Phyllotaxy is the arrangement of leaves on the stem or branch.

## 8.1 Alternate Phyllotaxy

In alternate phyllotaxy, one leaf arises at each node in an alternate manner.

Examples:

- China rose;
- mustard;
- sunflower.

## 8.2 Opposite Phyllotaxy

In opposite phyllotaxy, a pair of leaves arises at each node and the two leaves lie opposite each other.

Examples:

- Calotropis;
- guava.

## 8.3 Whorled Phyllotaxy

In whorled phyllotaxy, more than two leaves arise at a node and form a whorl.

Example:

- Alstonia.

## 8.4 Phyllotaxy Comparison

| Type | Leaf Arrangement | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Alternate | One leaf per node | China rose, mustard, sunflower |
| Opposite | Two leaves per node | Calotropis, guava |
| Whorled | More than two leaves per node | Alstonia |

## 9. Inflorescence

An inflorescence is the arrangement of flowers on the floral axis.

A flower is a modified shoot. During flower formation, the shoot apical meristem changes into a floral meristem. Internodes do not elongate, and the axis becomes condensed.

## 9.1 Racemose Inflorescence

In racemose inflorescence:

- the main axis continues to grow;
- flowers are borne laterally;
- flowers are arranged in acropetal succession.

Acropetal succession means older flowers are lower or outer, while younger flowers are toward the apex or centre.

## 9.2 Cymose Inflorescence

In cymose inflorescence:

- the main axis ends in a flower;
- growth is limited;
- flowers are borne in basipetal order.

Basipetal order means older flowers are near the apex or centre, while younger ones are lower or outer.

## 9.3 Racemose Versus Cymose

| Feature | Racemose | Cymose |
|---|---|---|
| Main axis | Continues to grow | Ends in a flower |
| Growth | Unlimited | Limited |
| Flower position | Lateral | Terminal flower appears first |
| Succession | Acropetal | Basipetal |

## 10. The Flower

The flower is the reproductive unit of angiosperms. It is meant for sexual reproduction.

A typical flower has four whorls arranged on the thalamus or receptacle.

## 10.1 Four Floral Whorls

| Whorl | Members | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Calyx | Sepals | Protect flower in bud stage |
| Corolla | Petals | Often attract pollinators |
| Androecium | Stamens | Male reproductive whorl |
| Gynoecium | Carpels | Female reproductive whorl |

Calyx and corolla are accessory organs.

Androecium and gynoecium are reproductive organs.

## 10.2 Perianth

In some flowers, calyx and corolla are not distinct.

Together they are called perianth.

Example: lily.

## 10.3 Bisexual And Unisexual Flowers

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Bisexual | Both androecium and gynoecium present |
| Unisexual | Only stamens or only carpels present |

## 10.4 Floral Symmetry

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Actinomorphic | Can be divided into two equal halves by any radial plane | Mustard, datura, chilli |
| Zygomorphic | Can be divided into two equal halves only in one vertical plane | Pea, gulmohur, bean, Cassia |
| Asymmetric | Cannot be divided into two equal halves by any vertical plane | Canna |

## 10.5 Number Of Floral Parts

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Trimerous | Floral parts in multiples of 3 |
| Tetramerous | Floral parts in multiples of 4 |
| Pentamerous | Floral parts in multiples of 5 |

## 10.6 Bracteate And Ebracteate

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Bracteate flower | Flower has a reduced leaf called bract at base of pedicel |
| Ebracteate flower | Bract absent |

## 11. Position Of Floral Parts On Thalamus

Flowers are described as hypogynous, perigynous, or epigynous based on the position of calyx, corolla, and androecium in relation to the ovary.

## 11.1 Hypogynous Flower

In a hypogynous flower:

- gynoecium occupies the highest position;
- other floral parts are below it;
- ovary is superior.

Examples:

- mustard;
- China rose;
- brinjal.

## 11.2 Perigynous Flower

In a perigynous flower:

- gynoecium is in the centre;
- other floral parts are on the rim of thalamus;
- ovary is half inferior.

Examples:

- plum;
- rose;
- peach.

## 11.3 Epigynous Flower

In an epigynous flower:

- thalamus grows upward and encloses the ovary;
- other floral parts arise above the ovary;
- ovary is inferior.

Examples:

- guava;
- cucumber;
- ray florets of sunflower.

## 11.4 Comparison Of Ovary Position

| Flower Type | Position Of Ovary | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Hypogynous | Superior | Mustard, China rose, brinjal |
| Perigynous | Half inferior | Plum, rose, peach |
| Epigynous | Inferior | Guava, cucumber, sunflower ray florets |

## 12. Parts Of A Flower

## 12.1 Calyx

Calyx is the outermost whorl of the flower.

Its members are called sepals.

Sepals are usually green and protect the flower in bud stage.

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gamosepalous | Sepals united |
| Polysepalous | Sepals free |

## 12.2 Corolla

Corolla is made of petals.

Petals are often brightly coloured and help attract pollinators.

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Gamopetalous | Petals united |
| Polypetalous | Petals free |

Corolla shape may be:

- tubular;
- bell-shaped;
- funnel-shaped;
- wheel-shaped.

## 13. Aestivation

Aestivation is the arrangement of sepals or petals in a floral bud with respect to members of the same whorl.

## 13.1 Types Of Aestivation

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Valvate | Margins just touch, no overlap | Calotropis |
| Twisted | One margin overlaps the next in the same direction | China rose, lady's finger, cotton |
| Imbricate | Margins overlap but not in a fixed direction | Cassia, gulmohur |
| Vexillary | Standard overlaps wings; wings overlap keel | Pea, bean |

## 14. Androecium

Androecium is the male reproductive whorl of the flower. It is made of stamens.

## 14.1 Structure Of A Stamen

Each stamen has:

- filament;
- anther.

The anther is usually bilobed. Each lobe has two chambers called pollen sacs.

Pollen grains are produced in pollen sacs.

A sterile stamen is called staminode.

## 14.2 Attachment Of Stamens

| Condition | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Epipetalous | Stamens attached to petals | Brinjal |
| Epiphyllous | Stamens attached to perianth | Lily |

## 14.3 Union Of Stamens

| Condition | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Polyandrous | Stamens free | Many flowers |
| Monoadelphous | Stamens united into one bundle | China rose |
| Diadelphous | Stamens united into two bundles | Pea |
| Polyadelphous | Stamens united into more than two bundles | Citrus |

## 15. Gynoecium

Gynoecium is the female reproductive part of the flower.

It is made of one or more carpels.

## 15.1 Parts Of A Carpel

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Stigma | Receptive surface for pollen grains |
| Style | Tube-like part connecting stigma and ovary |
| Ovary | Enlarged basal part containing ovules |

Ovules are attached to a flattened cushion-like placenta.

After fertilisation:

- ovules become seeds;
- ovary becomes fruit.

## 15.2 Apocarpous And Syncarpous

| Condition | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Apocarpous | Carpels free | Lotus, rose |
| Syncarpous | Carpels fused | Mustard, tomato |

## 16. Placentation

Placentation is the arrangement of ovules within the ovary.

## 16.1 Types Of Placentation

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Marginal | Placenta forms a ridge along ventral suture; ovules in two rows | Pea |
| Axile | Ovules attached to central axis in a multilocular ovary | China rose, tomato, lemon |
| Parietal | Ovules develop on inner wall or peripheral part of ovary | Mustard, Argemone |
| Free central | Ovules borne on central axis; septa absent | Dianthus, Primrose |
| Basal | Placenta develops at base; usually one ovule attached | Sunflower, marigold |

## 17. The Fruit

Fruit is the mature or ripened ovary formed after fertilisation.

If a fruit forms without fertilisation, it is called a parthenocarpic fruit.

## 17.1 Parts Of A Fruit

A fruit generally consists of:

- pericarp;
- seeds.

Pericarp is the fruit wall.

It may be dry or fleshy.

## 17.2 Fleshy Pericarp Layers

When the pericarp is thick and fleshy, it may be divided into:

| Layer | Position |
|---|---|
| Epicarp | Outer layer |
| Mesocarp | Middle layer |
| Endocarp | Inner layer |

## 17.3 Drupe

Mango and coconut are examples of drupes.

Drupes develop from monocarpellary superior ovaries and are usually one-seeded.

In mango:

- epicarp is thin and outer;
- mesocarp is fleshy and edible;
- endocarp is hard and stony.

In coconut, the mesocarp is fibrous.

## 18. The Seed

After fertilisation, ovules develop into seeds.

A seed has:

- seed coat;
- embryo.

The embryo has:

- radicle;
- embryonal axis;
- one or two cotyledons.

Monocots usually have one cotyledon. Dicots usually have two cotyledons.

## 19. Structure Of A Dicotyledonous Seed

## 19.1 Main Parts

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| Seed coat | Outermost covering |
| Testa | Outer layer of seed coat |
| Tegmen | Inner layer of seed coat |
| Hilum | Scar where seed was attached to fruit |
| Micropyle | Small pore above hilum |
| Embryonal axis | Axis of embryo |
| Cotyledons | Two seed leaves, often food-rich |
| Radicle | Embryonic root |
| Plumule | Embryonic shoot |

## 19.2 Endospermic And Non-Endospermic Seeds

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Endospermic seed | Endosperm present in mature seed | Castor |
| Non-endospermic seed | Endosperm absent in mature seed | Bean, gram, pea |

In non-endospermic seeds, stored food is mainly in cotyledons.

## 20. Structure Of A Monocotyledonous Seed

Monocot seeds are generally endospermic, but some, such as orchids, are non-endospermic.

The cereal grain maize is a useful example.

## 20.1 Main Parts Of Maize Seed

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| Seed coat and fruit wall | Fused and membranous |
| Endosperm | Bulky food-storing tissue |
| Aleurone layer | Protein-rich layer separating embryo from endosperm |
| Embryo | Small, placed in a groove at one end |
| Scutellum | Large shield-shaped cotyledon |
| Plumule | Embryonic shoot |
| Radicle | Embryonic root |
| Coleoptile | Protective sheath around plumule |
| Coleorhiza | Protective sheath around radicle |

## 21. Dicot Seed Versus Monocot Seed

| Feature | Dicot Seed | Monocot Seed |
|---|---|---|
| Cotyledons | Two | One |
| Food storage | Often in cotyledons if non-endospermic | Usually in endosperm |
| Example | Gram, pea, bean | Maize, wheat |
| Protective sheaths | Coleoptile and coleorhiza absent | Coleoptile and coleorhiza present in cereals |
| Seed coat | Testa and tegmen distinct | Often fused with fruit wall in cereals |

## 23.3 Floral Diagram

A floral diagram shows:

- number of floral parts;
- arrangement of floral parts;
- relationship among whorls;
- position of mother axis;
- fusion and adhesion patterns.

Calyx is drawn outside, followed by corolla, androecium, and gynoecium at the centre.

## 24. Family Solanaceae

Solanaceae is commonly called the potato family.

It is widely distributed in tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions.

## 24.1 Vegetative Characters Of Solanaceae

| Character | Description |
|---|---|
| Habit | Mostly herbs and shrubs, rarely small trees |
| Stem | Herbaceous, rarely woody; aerial; erect; cylindrical; branched; solid or hollow; hairy or smooth |
| Underground stem | Present in potato |
| Leaves | Alternate, simple, rarely pinnately compound |
| Stipules | Absent |
| Venation | Reticulate |

## 26. Common Learner Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling any underground part a root | Some underground parts are modified stems, such as potato |
| Confusing leaflets with leaves | Leaflets do not have axillary buds |
| Calling all networked leaves dicots without exception | Reticulate venation is typical of dicots, but classification uses multiple features |
| Mixing racemose and cymose | Racemose has continuing main axis; cymose has terminal flower |
| Confusing actinomorphic and zygomorphic | Actinomorphic has many possible radial planes; zygomorphic has one plane |
| Confusing superior ovary with inferior ovary | Superior ovary is above other whorls; inferior ovary is enclosed below them |
| Calling apocarpous and syncarpous the same | Apocarpous carpels are free; syncarpous carpels are fused |
| Forgetting maize is usually endospermic | Maize has bulky endosperm |
| Forgetting gram seed has two cotyledons | Gram is dicotyledonous |

## 27. Exercise Answers

## 27.1 Difference Between Pinnately Compound And Palmately Compound Leaf

| Feature | Pinnately Compound Leaf | Palmately Compound Leaf |
|---|---|---|
| Leaflet arrangement | Leaflets arise on a common axis called rachis | Leaflets arise from one common point |
| Axis | Rachis represents midrib | No elongated rachis |
| Example | Neem | Silk cotton |

In both types, the axillary bud is present at the base of the whole leaf, not at the base of each leaflet.

## 27.2 Types Of Phyllotaxy With Examples

Phyllotaxy is the arrangement of leaves on the stem or branch.

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Alternate | One leaf arises at each node | China rose, mustard, sunflower |
| Opposite | Two leaves arise at each node opposite each other | Calotropis, guava |
| Whorled | More than two leaves arise at each node | Alstonia |

## 27.3 Define Aestivation

Aestivation is the arrangement of sepals or petals in a floral bud with respect to other members of the same whorl.

Types include valvate, twisted, imbricate, and vexillary.

## 27.4 Define Placentation

Placentation is the arrangement of ovules inside the ovary.

Types include marginal, axile, parietal, free central, and basal placentation.

## 27.5 Define Actinomorphic

An actinomorphic flower can be divided into two equal halves by any radial plane passing through the centre.

Examples: mustard, datura, chilli.

## 27.6 Define Zygomorphic

A zygomorphic flower can be divided into two equal halves only by one particular vertical plane.

Examples: pea, gulmohur, bean, Cassia.

## 27.7 Define Superior Ovary

A superior ovary is positioned above the attachment of other floral whorls.

It is seen in hypogynous flowers.

Examples: mustard, China rose, brinjal.

## 27.8 Define Perigynous Flower

In a perigynous flower, the gynoecium is in the centre and other floral parts are placed on the rim of the thalamus at nearly the same level.

The ovary is half inferior.

Examples: plum, rose, peach.

## 27.9 Define Epipetalous Stamen

An epipetalous stamen is attached to the petal.

Example: brinjal.

## 27.10 Difference Between Racemose And Cymose Inflorescence

| Feature | Racemose | Cymose |
|---|---|---|
| Growth of main axis | Continues | Stops in a terminal flower |
| Flower arrangement | Lateral | Terminal flower appears first |
| Order | Acropetal | Basipetal |
| Growth | Indefinite | Definite |

## 27.11 Difference Between Apocarpous And Syncarpous Ovary

| Feature | Apocarpous | Syncarpous |
|---|---|---|
| Carpels | Free | Fused |
| Example | Lotus, rose | Mustard, tomato |

## 27.14 Semi-Technical Description Of Solanaceae

Habit: mostly herbs or shrubs, rarely small trees.

Stem: herbaceous, rarely woody, aerial, erect, cylindrical, branched, solid or hollow, hairy or smooth. Potato has an underground stem.

Leaves: alternate, simple, rarely pinnately compound, exstipulate, reticulate venation.

Inflorescence: solitary, axillary, or cymose.

Flower: bisexual and actinomorphic.

Calyx: five sepals, united, persistent, valvate aestivation.

Corolla: five petals, united, valvate aestivation.

Androecium: five stamens, epipetalous.

Gynoecium: bicarpellary, obliquely placed, syncarpous; ovary superior and bilocular; placentation axile; many ovules.

Fruit: berry or capsule.

Seeds: many and endospermous.

Floral formula in ASCII words: actinomorphic, bisexual, K(5), C(5), A5 epipetalous, G(2) superior.

## 27.15 Types Of Placentation In Flowering Plants

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Marginal | Ovules borne along ventral suture in two rows | Pea |
| Axile | Ovules attached to central axis in multilocular ovary | China rose, tomato, lemon |
| Parietal | Ovules develop on inner wall or peripheral part | Mustard, Argemone |
| Free central | Ovules borne on central axis without septa | Dianthus, Primrose |
| Basal | Single ovule attached at base of ovary | Sunflower, marigold |

## 27.16 What Is A Flower? Describe Parts Of A Typical Angiosperm Flower

A flower is a modified shoot meant for sexual reproduction in angiosperms.

A typical flower has four whorls:

| Whorl | Members | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Calyx | Sepals | Protects flower in bud stage |
| Corolla | Petals | Attracts pollinators |
| Androecium | Stamens | Produces pollen grains |
| Gynoecium | Carpels | Bears ovules and forms fruit after fertilisation |

The whorls are arranged on the thalamus or receptacle.

## 27.17 Define Inflorescence And Explain Its Basis

Inflorescence is the arrangement of flowers on the floral axis.

The main basis for classifying inflorescence is whether the main axis keeps growing or ends in a flower.

| Type | Basis |
|---|---|
| Racemose | Main axis continues to grow; flowers arise laterally in acropetal order |
| Cymose | Main axis ends in a flower; flowers appear in basipetal order |

## 27.18 Arrangement Of Floral Members On Thalamus

Based on insertion of floral members on thalamus, flowers are:

| Type | Description | Ovary Position | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hypogynous | Gynoecium highest; other parts below it | Superior | Mustard, China rose, brinjal |
| Perigynous | Gynoecium in centre; other parts on rim of thalamus | Half inferior | Plum, rose, peach |
| Epigynous | Thalamus encloses ovary; other parts arise above it | Inferior | Guava, cucumber, sunflower ray florets |
