## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is about plant change through time.

The main flow is:

```text
zygote -> embryo -> seed -> seedling -> mature plant -> flowering -> fruiting -> senescence
```

Do not treat growth and development as the same word.

Use this distinction:

- growth means irreversible increase in size or mass;
- differentiation means cells become specialised;
- development includes growth plus differentiation plus ageing changes.

Also keep this idea in mind:

```text
plants remain open systems of growth because meristems keep producing new cells.
```

Plant growth regulators do not act alone.

They interact with:

- genes;
- tissue position;
- light;
- temperature;
- water;
- nutrition;
- gravity;
- developmental stage.

## 1. Big Idea Of Plant Development

A flowering plant begins as a zygote.

The zygote forms an embryo.

The embryo becomes part of the seed.

Under favourable conditions, the seed germinates.

The seedling becomes a mature plant.

The mature plant forms flowers, fruits, and seeds.

Eventually organs senesce and die.

This ordered sequence is plant development.

## 2. Growth And Differentiation

Development is the result of two linked processes:

- growth;
- differentiation.

Growth increases size, mass, volume, length, or cell number.

Differentiation makes cells structurally and functionally specialised.

Together, they create organs such as:

- roots;
- stems;
- leaves;
- flowers;
- fruits;
- seeds.

## 3. Seed Germination As The Beginning

Seed germination begins when conditions become favourable.

Important conditions include:

- water;
- oxygen;
- suitable temperature;
- sometimes light;
- viable embryo;
- suitable internal hormone balance.

If conditions are unfavourable, seeds may remain dormant.

Dormancy is a period of suspended growth.

When favourable conditions return, metabolic activity resumes.

## 4. Definition Of Growth

Growth is an irreversible permanent increase in size of an organ, organ part, whole organism, or individual cell.

Examples:

- expansion of a leaf;
- elongation of a root;
- increase in stem girth;
- enlargement of a fruit;
- increase in cell number in a meristem.

## 4.1 What Growth Is Not

Swelling of a dry piece of wood in water is not true growth.

Reason:

- it may be reversible;
- it does not involve living protoplasm increase;
- it does not require metabolism in the way biological growth does;
- it does not involve cell division or cell enlargement as a living process.

This swelling is mainly physical water absorption.

## 4.2 Growth Requires Energy

Growth is accompanied by metabolic processes.

These include:

- anabolism;
- catabolism;
- synthesis of protoplasm;
- cell wall formation;
- enlargement;
- cell division.

Because these processes need energy, respiration is important for growth.

## 5. Plant Growth Is Generally Indeterminate

Plants can continue growing throughout life.

This is because plants retain meristems.

Meristems contain cells that:

- divide repeatedly;
- self-perpetuate;
- produce daughter cells;
- add new cells to the plant body.

This is called open growth.

## 5.1 Meristems And Open Growth

In open growth, meristems keep adding cells.

Newly formed cells may:

- remain meristematic;
- elongate;
- differentiate;
- become part of permanent tissues.

If a meristem stops dividing, growth from that meristem stops.

But plants often have multiple meristems, so other regions may continue growth.

## 5.2 Primary Growth

Primary growth increases length.

It is caused mainly by:

- root apical meristem;
- shoot apical meristem;
- intercalary meristem in some plants.

Primary growth produces:

- longer roots;
- longer stems;
- new leaves;
- new branches.

## 5.3 Secondary Growth

Secondary growth increases girth.

It is common in dicotyledons and gymnosperms.

It is caused by lateral meristems such as:

- vascular cambium;
- cork cambium.

Secondary growth produces:

- secondary xylem;
- secondary phloem;
- cork;
- increased stem and root thickness.

## 5.4 Determinate Growth

Some plant organs show determinate growth.

They grow to a limited size and then stop.

Examples:

- leaves;
- flowers;
- fruits;
- many floral organs.

Thus the whole plant may show indeterminate growth, while many organs show determinate growth.

## 6. Growth Is Measurable

Growth at cellular level mainly reflects increase in protoplasm.

Direct measurement of protoplasm is difficult.

So growth is measured through related parameters.

Common parameters:

- fresh weight;
- dry weight;
- length;
- area;
- volume;
- cell number.

## 6.1 Why One Parameter Is Not Enough

No single parameter measures growth throughout the whole life of a flowering plant.

Reason:

- roots may show growth as length increase;
- leaves may show growth as area increase;
- fruits may show growth as volume or fresh weight increase;
- meristems may show growth as cell number increase;
- storage organs may show growth as dry weight increase;
- pollen tubes are measured by length.

Different organs grow in different ways.

## 6.2 Examples Of Growth Measurement

| Plant Part Or Situation | Best Growth Parameter |
|---|---|
| Root tip | Cell number or length |
| Pollen tube | Length |
| Leaf | Area |
| Fruit | Volume or fresh weight |
| Storage tissue | Dry weight |
| Meristem | Cell division rate |

## 7. Phases Of Growth

Growth is divided into three phases:

1. meristematic phase;
2. elongation phase;
3. maturation phase.

These phases are clearly seen near root and shoot tips.

## 7.1 Meristematic Phase

This phase contains actively dividing cells.

Cells in this phase have:

- dense protoplasm;
- large conspicuous nuclei;
- thin primary cell walls;
- cellulosic walls;
- many plasmodesmatal connections.

They are found at root apex and shoot apex.

## 7.2 Elongation Phase

This phase lies just behind the meristematic zone.

Cells show:

- increased vacuolation;
- cell enlargement;
- new cell wall deposition;
- rapid increase in length.

Elongation is responsible for much of root and shoot extension.

## 7.3 Maturation Phase

This phase lies farther away from the apex.

Cells reach maximum size and begin specialisation.

Features:

- wall thickening;
- protoplasmic modification;
- development of mature tissue features;
- functional specialisation.

Most permanent tissues belong to this phase.

## 8. Growth Rate

Growth rate is increase in growth per unit time.

It can be expressed mathematically.

Growth rate may be:

- arithmetic;
- geometric;
- absolute;
- relative.

## 9. Arithmetic Growth

In arithmetic growth, after mitosis:

- one daughter cell continues to divide;
- the other daughter cell differentiates and matures.

This leads to a constant rate of increase.

Root elongation at a constant rate is a simple example.

When length is plotted against time, a straight line is obtained.

## 9.1 Arithmetic Growth Formula

```text
Lt = L0 + rt
```

Where:

- Lt = length at time t;
- L0 = length at time zero;
- r = growth rate or elongation per unit time;
- t = time.

## 10. Geometric Growth

In geometric growth, after mitosis:

- both daughter cells retain ability to divide;
- cell number increases rapidly;
- growth becomes exponential for a period.

This type of growth is common in many living systems during favourable conditions.

## 10.1 Phases Of Geometric Growth

Geometric growth usually has three phases:

1. lag phase;
2. log or exponential phase;
3. stationary phase.

## 10.2 Lag Phase

Growth begins slowly.

Cells are preparing for rapid division.

Metabolism is active, but visible growth is limited.

## 10.3 Log Or Exponential Phase

Growth becomes rapid.

Cells divide actively.

Both daughter cells may continue division.

The growth curve rises steeply.

## 10.4 Stationary Phase

Growth slows down.

Reasons include:

- limited nutrients;
- limited space;
- accumulated wastes;
- developmental control;
- environmental limits.

## 10.5 Sigmoid Growth Curve

When growth is plotted against time, geometric growth often forms an S-shaped curve.

This is called a sigmoid growth curve.

It is typical of many:

- cells;
- tissues;
- organs;
- whole plants.

The curve shows:

```text
lag phase -> exponential phase -> stationary phase
```

## 10.6 Exponential Growth Formula

```text
W1 = W0 e^(rt)
```

Where:

- W1 = final size;
- W0 = initial size;
- r = relative growth rate;
- t = time;
- e = base of natural logarithms.

In this formula, r is also called an efficiency index.

It measures the ability to produce new plant material.

## 11. Absolute And Relative Growth Rates

## 11.1 Absolute Growth Rate

Absolute growth rate is total growth per unit time.

Formula idea:

```text
absolute growth rate = increase in size / time
```

Example:

If two leaves each increase by 5 cm2 in the same time, both have the same absolute growth rate.

## 11.2 Relative Growth Rate

Relative growth rate expresses growth relative to initial size.

Formula idea:

```text
relative growth rate = growth per unit time / initial size
```

Example:

If a small leaf and a large leaf both increase by 5 cm2, the small leaf has a higher relative growth rate.

Reason:

```text
5 cm2 is a larger fraction of a small initial area.
```

## 11.3 Why Relative Rate Matters

Relative growth rate allows fair comparison between differently sized organs or organisms.

Absolute growth alone can hide the efficiency of growth.

## 12. Conditions Required For Growth

Growth requires both internal and external conditions.

Essential external conditions include:

- water;
- oxygen;
- nutrients;
- optimum temperature;
- light in certain stages;
- gravity signals in certain stages.

## 12.1 Water

Water is essential because:

- cell enlargement requires water uptake;
- turgidity helps extension growth;
- enzymes work in aqueous medium;
- transport of nutrients depends on water;
- photosynthesis and metabolism require water.

Growth is closely linked to plant water status.

## 12.2 Oxygen

Oxygen is needed for aerobic respiration.

Respiration releases ATP.

ATP is required for:

- biosynthesis;
- active transport;
- cell division;
- growth-related metabolism.

## 12.3 Nutrients

Nutrients are needed for:

- protoplasm synthesis;
- nucleic acid synthesis;
- enzyme formation;
- chlorophyll formation;
- energy metabolism;
- structural components.

Both macronutrients and micronutrients are required.

## 12.4 Temperature

Every plant has an optimum temperature range for growth.

Too low or too high temperature can reduce enzyme function and damage tissues.

## 12.5 Light And Gravity

Light affects:

- photosynthesis;
- photomorphogenesis;
- flowering;
- seed germination in some species.

Gravity affects:

- root growth direction;
- shoot growth direction;
- orientation of organs.

## 13. Differentiation

Differentiation is the process by which cells derived from meristems mature and become specialised for specific functions.

During differentiation, cells may change:

- shape;
- size;
- wall thickness;
- wall composition;
- organelle content;
- protoplasm amount;
- metabolic activity.

## 13.1 Example: Tracheary Element

A tracheary element is specialised for water conduction.

During differentiation:

- the cell loses protoplasm;
- strong secondary wall develops;
- lignocellulosic thickening appears;
- the cell becomes suited for water transport under tension.

Structure matches function.

## 14. Dedifferentiation

Dedifferentiation is the process by which mature living cells regain the ability to divide.

Examples:

- formation of interfascicular cambium from mature parenchyma;
- formation of cork cambium from mature tissues;
- callus formation in tissue culture.

Dead cells cannot dedifferentiate.

Only living mature cells can regain division capacity.

## 15. Redifferentiation

Redifferentiation occurs when cells produced by dedifferentiated tissue again mature into specialised cells.

Examples in woody dicot plants:

- secondary xylem;
- secondary phloem;
- cork cells;
- secondary cortex-like tissues;
- ray cells.

The cells divide first, then specialise again.

## 16. Tumour And Callus

A tumour is an abnormal mass of cells produced by uncontrolled cell division.

In plants, uncontrolled growth can form tumour-like masses.

A callus is a mass of undifferentiated cells produced under controlled laboratory conditions in tissue culture.

Callus cells are living cells induced to divide.

They can later be guided to form roots, shoots, or whole plants under suitable hormone conditions.

## 17. Open Differentiation

Differentiation in plants is open.

This means cells from the same meristem can mature into different structures depending on position and signals.

Example:

- cells away from root apex may become root cap cells;
- cells toward the periphery may become epidermis;
- cells in inner positions may become vascular tissues.

Position matters.

The final fate of a cell depends on:

- gene expression;
- local signals;
- hormones;
- neighbouring cells;
- organ location.

## 18. Development

Development includes all changes from seed germination to senescence.

It includes:

- growth;
- differentiation;
- maturation;
- flowering;
- fruiting;
- ageing;
- death of organs.

Broadly:

```text
development = growth + differentiation
```

This is a simplified but useful idea.

## 19. Plasticity

Plasticity is the ability of a plant to produce different structures in response to environment or life stage.

Plants are highly plastic because they cannot move away from environmental conditions.

They adjust growth and development instead.

## 19.1 Heterophylly

Heterophylly means different leaf forms in the same plant.

Examples:

- juvenile and mature leaves may differ in cotton;
- juvenile and mature leaves may differ in coriander;
- juvenile and mature leaves may differ in larkspur;
- leaves produced in air and water may differ in buttercup.

Heterophylly shows plasticity.

## 19.2 Developmental Plasticity

Plasticity may be controlled by:

- age of plant;
- environmental conditions;
- water availability;
- light;
- internal hormones.

This allows one genotype to produce different forms under different conditions.

## 20. Control Of Development

Plant development is controlled by intrinsic and extrinsic factors.

## 20.1 Intrinsic Factors

Intrinsic factors include:

- genetic control;
- intracellular signals;
- intercellular chemical signals;
- plant growth regulators.

## 20.2 Extrinsic Factors

Extrinsic factors include:

- light;
- temperature;
- water;
- oxygen;
- nutrition;
- gravity.

External factors often act through internal chemical signals.

## 21. Plant Growth Regulators

Plant growth regulators are small molecules that regulate plant growth and development.

They are also called:

- plant growth substances;
- plant hormones;
- phytohormones.

## 21.1 Chemical Diversity Of PGRs

PGRs have diverse chemical nature.

Examples:

| PGR Type | Chemical Nature Example |
|---|---|
| Auxin | Indole compound such as IAA |
| Cytokinin | Adenine derivative such as kinetin |
| ABA | Carotenoid derivative |
| Gibberellin | Terpene such as GA3 |
| Ethylene | Gas, C2H4 |

## 21.2 Functional Groups Of PGRs

PGRs can be broadly grouped by major effects.

Growth promoters:

- auxins;
- gibberellins;
- cytokinins.

Growth inhibitors or stress-related regulators:

- abscisic acid;
- ethylene in many situations.

Ethylene is special because it can show both promotory and inhibitory effects depending on process and tissue.

## 22. Discovery Of Plant Growth Regulators

The major PGR groups were discovered through observations and experiments.

## 22.1 Discovery Of Auxin

Charles Darwin and Francis Darwin studied coleoptiles of canary grass.

They observed phototropism.

Phototropism is bending toward unilateral light.

Their work showed that the coleoptile tip receives the light stimulus and sends a transmittable influence to the lower region.

F. W. Went later isolated auxin from oat coleoptile tips.

## 22.2 Discovery Of Gibberellins

The rice disease called bakanae, or foolish seedling disease, caused abnormal elongation in rice seedlings.

It was caused by the fungus Gibberella fujikuroi.

E. Kurosawa showed that sterile filtrates from the fungus could produce the symptoms.

The active substances were later identified as gibberellins.

Gibberellic acid, GA3, became one of the best studied gibberellins.

## 22.3 Discovery Of Cytokinins

F. Skoog and coworkers studied tobacco stem callus.

They found callus proliferation required auxin plus certain additional substances.

Miller and coworkers identified a cytokinesis-promoting substance called kinetin.

Kinetin is a modified adenine-like compound.

Kinetin does not naturally occur in plants.

Natural cytokinins such as zeatin were later isolated from corn kernels and coconut milk.

## 22.4 Discovery Of ABA

In the 1960s, three inhibitory substances were described:

- inhibitor-B;
- abscission II;
- dormin.

They were later shown to be chemically identical.

The compound was named abscisic acid, or ABA.

## 22.5 Discovery Of Ethylene

H. H. Cousins observed that a volatile substance from ripened oranges hastened ripening of stored unripe bananas.

The volatile substance was later identified as ethylene.

Ethylene is a gaseous plant growth regulator.

## 23. Auxins

Auxins are growth regulators linked strongly with cell elongation and apical dominance.

The word auxin is related to the idea "to grow".

Natural auxins include:

- IAA, indole-3-acetic acid;
- IBA, indole butyric acid.

Synthetic auxins include:

- NAA, naphthalene acetic acid;
- 2,4-D, 2,4-dichlorophenoxyacetic acid.

## 23.1 Production And Movement

Auxins are generally produced in growing apices of:

- stems;
- roots.

They move from production regions to regions of action.

## 23.2 Physiological Effects Of Auxins

Auxins:

- promote cell elongation;
- initiate rooting in stem cuttings;
- promote flowering in pineapple;
- prevent early fruit and leaf drop;
- promote abscission of older mature leaves and fruits;
- maintain apical dominance;
- induce parthenocarpy in tomato;
- act as herbicides in synthetic forms;
- influence xylem differentiation;
- help in cell division.

## 23.3 Auxins In Plant Propagation

Auxins are used to initiate roots in stem cuttings.

This is useful for vegetative propagation.

Cuttings treated with auxin can form adventitious roots more readily.

## 23.4 Auxins And Apical Dominance

In many higher plants, the growing apical bud suppresses the growth of lateral buds.

This is apical dominance.

Auxin produced by the apical bud contributes to this inhibition.

When the shoot tip is removed, lateral buds grow.

This is used in:

- tea plantations;
- hedge-making;
- pruning for bushy growth.

## 23.5 Auxins As Herbicides

2,4-D is used to kill dicot weeds.

It generally does not affect mature monocot plants as strongly.

This is why it can help maintain weed-free lawns.

## 24. Gibberellins

Gibberellins are promotory PGRs.

More than 100 gibberellins are known.

They are named:

- GA1;
- GA2;
- GA3;
- and so on.

GA3 is one of the earliest discovered and most studied forms.

All gibberellins are acidic.

## 24.1 Physiological Effects Of Gibberellins

Gibberellins:

- increase stem length;
- elongate grape stalks;
- improve apple shape by elongation;
- delay senescence;
- extend market period of fruits;
- speed up malting in brewing;
- increase sugarcane stem length and yield;
- hasten maturity in juvenile conifers;
- promote bolting in rosette plants.

## 24.2 Gibberellins And Bolting

Bolting means rapid internode elongation just before flowering.

It occurs in plants such as:

- beet;
- cabbage;
- many rosette plants.

Gibberellins can induce or promote bolting.

## 24.3 Gibberellins In Agriculture

Examples of use:

- increase grape stalk length;
- increase sugarcane yield by stem elongation;
- promote early seed production in conifers;
- speed malting in brewing industry.

## 25. Cytokinins

Cytokinins promote cytokinesis.

They are linked with cell division and shoot formation.

Natural cytokinins are produced in regions of rapid cell division, such as:

- root apices;
- developing shoot buds;
- young fruits.

## 25.1 Physiological Effects Of Cytokinins

Cytokinins:

- promote cell division;
- help produce new leaves;
- promote chloroplast development in leaves;
- promote lateral shoot growth;
- promote adventitious shoot formation;
- help overcome apical dominance;
- promote nutrient mobilisation;
- delay leaf senescence.

## 25.2 Cytokinins In Tissue Culture

In plant tissue culture, cytokinin balance with auxin controls organ formation.

General idea:

- more auxin often favours rooting;
- more cytokinin often favours shoot formation;
- balanced levels may maintain callus.

Exact response depends on species and culture conditions.

## 26. Ethylene

Ethylene is a gaseous plant growth regulator.

It is produced in large amounts by:

- ripening fruits;
- senescing tissues.

## 26.1 Physiological Effects Of Ethylene

Ethylene:

- promotes fruit ripening;
- enhances respiration rate during ripening;
- causes respiratory climacteric;
- promotes senescence;
- promotes abscission of leaves and flowers;
- causes horizontal seedling growth;
- causes swelling of seedling axis;
- promotes apical hook formation in dicot seedlings;
- breaks seed and bud dormancy;
- initiates germination in peanut seeds;
- promotes sprouting of potato tubers;
- promotes internode or petiole elongation in deepwater rice;
- promotes root growth and root hair formation;
- initiates flowering and synchronised fruit set in pineapple;
- induces flowering in mango in some treatments.

## 26.2 Respiratory Climacteric

Respiratory climacteric is the rise in respiration rate during ripening of certain fruits.

Ethylene is highly effective in promoting this process.

## 26.3 Ethephon

Ethephon is a widely used compound that releases ethylene slowly inside plants.

Uses:

- hastens ripening in tomatoes and apples;
- accelerates abscission in flowers and fruits;
- helps thinning in cotton, cherry, and walnut;
- promotes female flowers in cucumber, increasing yield.

## 27. Abscisic Acid

Abscisic acid is commonly abbreviated ABA.

It was first linked with abscission and dormancy.

It is a major growth inhibitor and stress-related hormone.

## 27.1 Physiological Effects Of ABA

ABA:

- inhibits growth;
- inhibits plant metabolism in many contexts;
- inhibits seed germination;
- promotes seed dormancy;
- promotes seed maturation;
- helps seeds tolerate desiccation;
- closes stomata;
- increases tolerance to stress;
- acts antagonistically to gibberellins in many situations.

## 27.2 ABA As Stress Hormone

ABA is called the stress hormone because it helps plants tolerate adverse conditions.

During water stress, ABA promotes stomatal closure.

This reduces water loss by transpiration.

ABA also helps seeds remain dormant until conditions become favourable.

## 28. PGR Interactions

PGRs rarely act alone.

Their effects may be:

- complementary;
- antagonistic;
- individual;
- synergistic.

## 28.1 Antagonistic Effects

ABA and gibberellins often show opposite effects.

Example:

- gibberellins promote seed germination;
- ABA promotes seed dormancy.

## 28.2 Synergistic Effects

Two hormones may cooperate to produce a stronger response.

Example:

- auxin and cytokinin together influence tissue culture growth;
- hormone balance can guide root or shoot formation.

## 28.3 Event-Level Control

Many events involve more than one PGR:

- dormancy;
- germination;
- abscission;
- senescence;
- apical dominance;
- flowering;
- fruit ripening.

## 29. Extrinsic Factors And PGRs

External factors can control development through PGRs.

Important factors:

- light;
- temperature;
- water;
- nutrition;
- oxygen;
- gravity.

Examples:

- light affects flowering through photoperiodism;
- temperature affects flowering through vernalisation;
- water stress increases ABA response;
- gravity affects directional growth.

## 30. Photoperiodism

Photoperiodism is the response of plants to the relative lengths of day and night.

It is especially important in flowering.

The critical factor is often the duration of darkness, not simply the duration of light.

## 30.1 Short-Day Plants

Short-day plants flower when day length is shorter than a critical limit.

Equivalently, they require a long uninterrupted night.

Examples commonly taught:

- chrysanthemum;
- tobacco varieties;
- soybean in many contexts.

## 30.2 Long-Day Plants

Long-day plants flower when day length is longer than a critical limit.

Equivalently, they require a short night.

Examples commonly taught:

- wheat;
- spinach;
- radish in many contexts.

## 30.3 Day-Neutral Plants

Day-neutral plants flower without a strict photoperiod requirement.

Flowering depends more on age, nutrition, or other signals.

Examples:

- tomato;
- cucumber in many treatments.

## 30.4 Leaves Perceive Photoperiod

Leaves are the main organs that perceive photoperiod.

After perception, a floral signal is transported to the shoot apex.

The shoot apex then changes from vegetative growth to flowering.

This is why a defoliated plant often does not respond properly to photoperiodic cycles.

## 30.5 Short-Day And Long-Day Plants In Same Place

A short-day plant and a long-day plant can flower at the same place if their required photoperiodic conditions are met.

This may happen due to:

- different critical day lengths;
- different seasons;
- artificial light control;
- genetic variation;
- local microclimate.

The label short-day or long-day does not mean they can never flower together.

It means they have different photoperiodic requirements.

## 31. Vernalisation

Vernalisation is the promotion of flowering by exposure to low temperature.

It is seen in many:

- winter cereals;
- biennial plants;
- temperate plants.

## 31.1 Why Vernalisation Matters

Vernalisation prevents flowering before the plant has passed through a suitable vegetative period or winter period.

It helps plants flower in the correct season.

It can ensure that flowering happens after cold winter conditions have passed.

## 31.2 Site Of Vernalisation Response

Low temperature is often perceived by:

- shoot apex;
- embryo in some seeds;
- actively dividing regions.

After vernalisation, the plant becomes competent to flower when other conditions are suitable.

## 33. Common Misconceptions

## 33.1 Misconception: Any Increase In Size Is Growth

Correct idea:

True growth is irreversible and living.

Swelling of dry wood in water is not true growth.

## 33.2 Misconception: Plants Grow Everywhere Equally

Correct idea:

Growth occurs mainly in meristematic regions.

Different organs grow at different rates and for different durations.

## 33.3 Misconception: Differentiated Cells Can Never Divide Again

Correct idea:

Some living differentiated plant cells can dedifferentiate and regain division capacity.

## 33.4 Misconception: Development Means Only Increase In Size

Correct idea:

Development includes growth, differentiation, maturation, senescence, and other life-cycle changes.

## 33.5 Misconception: PGRs Always Have One Fixed Effect

Correct idea:

PGR effects depend on concentration, tissue, developmental stage, and interaction with other PGRs.

## 33.6 Misconception: Ethylene Only Inhibits Growth

Correct idea:

Ethylene often inhibits or promotes senescence, but it also promotes fruit ripening, root hair formation, deepwater rice elongation, and flowering in some plants.

## 33.7 Misconception: ABA Only Causes Abscission

Correct idea:

ABA is important in dormancy, stomatal closure, seed maturation, and stress tolerance.

## 34. Solved Concept Checks

## 34.1 Why Is Swelling Of Wood Not Growth?

Swelling of wood by water is a physical process.

It is not an irreversible increase in living protoplasm.

It does not involve metabolic growth.

Therefore it is not true biological growth.

## 34.2 Why Is Plant Growth Called Open?

Plant growth is called open because meristems keep adding new cells throughout life.

These cells can later elongate and differentiate.

## 34.3 What Happens If A Meristem Stops Dividing?

Growth from that meristem stops.

However, other meristems in the plant may continue growth.

For example, lateral meristems may still contribute to girth.

## 34.4 Why Is No One Parameter Enough To Measure Growth?

Different plant parts grow differently.

Root growth may be measured by length.

Leaf growth may be measured by area.

Fruit growth may be measured by volume or fresh weight.

Meristem growth may be measured by cell number.

Therefore one parameter cannot represent all growth.

## 34.5 Which Leaf Has Higher Relative Growth?

If two leaves increase by the same area, the smaller initial leaf has higher relative growth.

Reason:

The same increase is a larger proportion of its original size.

## 34.6 What Is Differentiation?

Differentiation is maturation of cells to perform specific functions.

It involves structural and biochemical changes.

## 34.7 What Is Dedifferentiation?

Dedifferentiation is regain of division capacity by mature living cells.

Example:

Mature parenchyma cells can form interfascicular cambium.

## 34.8 What Is Redifferentiation?

Redifferentiation is maturation of newly divided cells into specialised tissues after dedifferentiation.

Example:

Cambium-derived cells becoming secondary xylem or secondary phloem.

## 34.9 Why Is Development Flexible In Plants?

Plants show plasticity.

The same plant can form different structures depending on age or environment.

Heterophylly is an example.

## 34.10 Why Is ABA Called Stress Hormone?

ABA helps plants respond to stress.

It closes stomata during water stress and promotes dormancy.

This helps reduce water loss and survive unfavourable conditions.

## 34.11 Why Does Decapitation Promote Lateral Branching?

Removing the shoot tip reduces apical dominance.

Lateral buds are released from inhibition.

They grow into branches.

This is useful in tea plantations and hedge-making.

## 34.12 Why Does Rotten Fruit Ripen Unripe Fruits?

Rotten or ripening fruit releases ethylene.

Ethylene promotes ripening in nearby fruits.

Therefore unripe fruits ripen faster.

## 34.13 Why Does A Defoliated Plant Not Respond Well To Photoperiod?

Leaves perceive photoperiod.

If leaves are removed, the plant cannot properly detect day-night length.

The floral signal may not be produced or transported effectively.

## 35. Exam-Oriented Answers

## 35.1 Define Growth

Growth is an irreversible permanent increase in size of an organ, organ part, organism, or cell.

It is usually accompanied by metabolic processes and energy use.

## 35.2 Define Differentiation

Differentiation is the process by which cells derived from meristems mature and become structurally and functionally specialised.

## 35.3 Define Development

Development includes all changes an organism undergoes during its life cycle, from seed germination to senescence.

It includes growth and differentiation.

## 35.4 Define Dedifferentiation

Dedifferentiation is the process by which mature living cells regain the capacity to divide.

## 35.5 Define Redifferentiation

Redifferentiation is the process by which cells formed after dedifferentiation mature again into specialised cells.

## 35.6 Define Determinate Growth

Determinate growth is growth that stops after a certain stage.

Leaves, flowers, and fruits usually show determinate growth.

## 35.7 Define Meristem

A meristem is a plant tissue made of actively dividing cells that produce new cells for growth.

## 35.8 Define Growth Rate

Growth rate is increase in growth per unit time.

It may be measured as absolute or relative growth rate.

## 35.9 Arithmetic Growth

In arithmetic growth, one daughter cell continues to divide while the other differentiates.

It produces a linear growth curve.

Formula:

```text
Lt = L0 + rt
```

## 35.10 Geometric Growth

In geometric growth, both daughter cells continue to divide for a time.

It shows lag, exponential, and stationary phases.

The curve is sigmoid under natural conditions.

Formula:

```text
W1 = W0 e^(rt)
```

## 35.11 Sigmoid Growth Curve

A sigmoid growth curve is S-shaped.

It has:

- lag phase;
- log or exponential phase;
- stationary phase.

It is typical of many living systems.

## 35.12 Absolute And Relative Growth

Absolute growth rate is total increase per unit time.

Relative growth rate is increase per unit time relative to initial size.

A smaller organ can show higher relative growth even when absolute increase is the same.

## 35.13 Five Groups Of Natural PGRs

The five main groups are:

- auxins;
- gibberellins;
- cytokinins;
- ethylene;
- abscisic acid.

## 35.14 Auxin Note

Auxin was studied through coleoptile phototropism experiments by Darwin and Darwin, and isolated by F. W. Went from oat coleoptile tips.

Functions and uses:

- cell elongation;
- rooting in cuttings;
- apical dominance;
- parthenocarpy in tomato;
- prevention of early fruit drop;
- 2,4-D as herbicide;
- pineapple flowering.

## 35.15 Gibberellin Note

Gibberellins were linked with bakanae disease of rice caused by Gibberella fujikuroi.

Functions and uses:

- stem elongation;
- grape stalk elongation;
- bolting in rosette plants;
- delaying senescence;
- sugarcane yield increase;
- malting in brewing;
- early seed production in conifers.

## 35.16 Cytokinin Note

Cytokinins were discovered through tissue culture work on cell division.

Functions and uses:

- cytokinesis;
- shoot formation;
- chloroplast development;
- nutrient mobilisation;
- delayed senescence;
- overcoming apical dominance.

## 35.17 Ethylene Note

Ethylene is a gaseous PGR discovered through fruit ripening observations.

Functions and uses:

- fruit ripening;
- respiratory climacteric;
- senescence;
- abscission;
- breaking dormancy;
- ethephon use in ripening and thinning;
- female flower promotion in cucumber.

## 35.18 ABA Note

ABA was identified from inhibitors linked with abscission and dormancy.

Functions:

- seed dormancy;
- stomatal closure;
- stress tolerance;
- inhibition of seed germination;
- seed maturation;
- antagonism to gibberellins.

## 35.19 Open Growth And Open Differentiation

Growth in higher plants is open because meristems keep adding new cells.

Differentiation is open because cells from the same meristem can mature into different tissue types depending on position and signals.

## 35.20 Short-Day And Long-Day Plants Flowering Together

Short-day and long-day plants can flower in the same place if their specific critical photoperiod requirements are met.

This can occur naturally in suitable seasons or through controlled light conditions.

## 36. Applied Questions

## 36.1 Which PGR Induces Rooting In A Twig?

Auxin.

Common examples:

- IBA;
- NAA.

## 36.2 Which PGR Quickly Ripens A Fruit?

Ethylene.

Ethephon may be used because it releases ethylene in plant tissues.

## 36.3 Which PGR Delays Leaf Senescence?

Cytokinin.

Cytokinins mobilise nutrients and delay ageing of leaves.

## 36.4 Which PGR Induces Growth In Axillary Buds?

Cytokinin promotes lateral shoot growth.

Reducing auxin effect from the shoot tip also helps overcome apical dominance.

## 36.5 Which PGR Bolts A Rosette Plant?

Gibberellin, especially GA3.

## 36.6 Which PGR Causes Immediate Stomatal Closure?

Abscisic acid.

ABA promotes stomatal closure during water stress.

## 36.7 What Happens If GA3 Is Applied To Rice Seedlings?

Rice seedlings show excessive elongation.

This resembles the bakanae or foolish seedling effect.

## 36.8 What Happens If Dividing Cells Stop Differentiating?

They may remain as an undifferentiated mass.

Normal tissue and organ formation will be disturbed.

In tissue culture this can appear as callus.

In uncontrolled conditions it may resemble tumour-like growth.

## 36.9 What Happens If Rotten Fruit Is Mixed With Unripe Fruits?

Rotten or ripening fruit releases ethylene.

Ethylene hastens ripening of unripe fruits nearby.

## 36.10 What Happens If Cytokinin Is Not Added To Culture Medium?

Cell division and shoot formation may be poor.

Callus proliferation may reduce depending on hormone balance.

Auxin alone may favour rooting or fail to produce desired shoots.
