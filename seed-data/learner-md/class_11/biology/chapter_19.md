## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is best studied as a map.

For each hormone, learn five things:

1. Which gland or tissue produces it?
2. What chemical type is it?
3. Which target does it act on?
4. What does it do?
5. What happens when it is too high or too low?

Use this broad structure:

```text
hypothalamus controls pituitary;
pituitary controls several peripheral endocrine glands;
peripheral glands regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, stress, calcium, and glucose.
```

Also separate hormone action into two patterns:

- membrane receptor and second messenger;
- intracellular receptor and gene regulation.

## 1. Why Chemical Coordination Is Needed

The neural system provides rapid point-to-point coordination.

However, neural effects are often short-lived.

Also, nerve fibres do not directly innervate every cell.

Many cellular functions must be regulated continuously.

Hormones provide this chemical coordination.

The neural and endocrine systems work together to regulate body functions.

## 2. Endocrine Glands

Endocrine glands are ductless glands.

They release hormones directly into blood or surrounding fluid.

Hormones travel to target tissues.

Only target cells with specific receptors respond.

## 3. Exocrine Glands

Exocrine glands release secretions through ducts.

Examples:

- salivary glands;
- sweat glands;
- digestive glands;
- exocrine pancreas.

Difference:

```text
endocrine = ductless, hormone into blood
exocrine = ducts, secretion to surface or cavity
```

## 4. Hormone Definition

Classically, hormones were defined as chemicals produced by endocrine glands, released into blood, and transported to distant target organs.

Modern idea:

```text
hormones are non-nutrient chemicals that act as intercellular messengers and are produced in trace amounts.
```

This includes hormones from organised endocrine glands and hormone-like chemicals from other tissues.

## 5. Human Endocrine System

The human endocrine system includes organised endocrine bodies and hormone-producing tissues.

Organised endocrine bodies include:

- hypothalamus;
- pituitary;
- pineal;
- thyroid;
- parathyroid;
- thymus;
- adrenal glands;
- pancreas;
- testis;
- ovary.

Other hormone-producing organs include:

- heart;
- kidney;
- gastrointestinal tract;
- liver in some regulatory contexts;
- other tissues producing growth factors.

## 6. Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus is the basal part of diencephalon in forebrain.

It regulates many body functions.

It contains groups of neurosecretory cells called nuclei.

These cells produce hormones that regulate pituitary secretion.

## 6.1 Releasing And Inhibiting Hormones

Hypothalamic hormones are of two broad types:

- releasing hormones;
- inhibiting hormones.

Releasing hormones stimulate pituitary secretion.

Inhibiting hormones suppress pituitary secretion.

## 6.2 Examples

GnRH means gonadotrophin releasing hormone.

It stimulates pituitary release of gonadotrophins.

Somatostatin inhibits growth hormone release from pituitary.

## 6.3 Link With Pituitary

Hypothalamic hormones pass through axons and are released from nerve endings.

They reach anterior pituitary through a portal circulatory system.

Posterior pituitary is under direct neural regulation of hypothalamus.

## 7. Pituitary Gland

The pituitary gland is located in a bony cavity called sella tursica.

It is attached to hypothalamus by a stalk.

It has two main anatomical parts:

- adenohypophysis;
- neurohypophysis.

## 7.1 Adenohypophysis

Adenohypophysis includes:

- pars distalis;
- pars intermedia.

Pars distalis is commonly called anterior pituitary.

Pars intermedia is almost merged with pars distalis in humans.

## 7.2 Anterior Pituitary Hormones

Anterior pituitary secretes:

- GH;
- PRL;
- TSH;
- ACTH;
- LH;
- FSH.

## 7.3 Pars Intermedia Hormone

Pars intermedia secretes:

- MSH.

In humans, pars intermedia is almost merged with pars distalis.

## 7.4 Neurohypophysis

Neurohypophysis is posterior pituitary.

It stores and releases:

- oxytocin;
- vasopressin, also called ADH.

These hormones are actually synthesised in hypothalamus and transported axonally to posterior pituitary.

## 8. Growth Hormone

Growth hormone stimulates body growth.

Over-secretion in childhood causes gigantism.

Low secretion in childhood causes pituitary dwarfism.

Excess GH in adults, especially middle age, can cause acromegaly.

Acromegaly causes severe disfigurement, especially of face, and may lead to serious complications if unchecked.

## 9. Prolactin

Prolactin regulates:

- growth of mammary glands;
- formation of milk.

## 10. TSH

TSH means thyroid stimulating hormone.

It stimulates thyroid gland to synthesize and secrete thyroid hormones.

## 11. ACTH

ACTH means adrenocorticotrophic hormone.

It stimulates adrenal cortex to synthesize and secrete glucocorticoids.

## 12. LH And FSH

LH and FSH are gonadotrophins.

They regulate gonadal activity.

## 12.1 In Males

LH stimulates Leydig cells of testis to secrete androgens.

FSH, along with androgens, regulates spermatogenesis.

## 12.2 In Females

FSH stimulates growth and development of ovarian follicles.

LH induces ovulation of mature Graafian follicle.

LH maintains corpus luteum after ovulation.

## 13. MSH

MSH acts on melanocytes.

Melanocytes contain melanin.

MSH regulates skin pigmentation.

## 14. Oxytocin

Oxytocin acts on smooth muscles.

In females, it stimulates:

- strong uterine contractions during childbirth;
- milk ejection from mammary gland.

## 15. Vasopressin Or ADH

Vasopressin acts mainly on kidney.

It stimulates reabsorption of water and electrolytes by distal tubules.

It reduces water loss through urine.

Therefore it is called antidiuretic hormone.

Defective synthesis or release of ADH causes diabetes insipidus.

Diabetes insipidus causes excessive water loss and dehydration.

## 16. Pineal Gland

Pineal gland is located on dorsal side of forebrain.

It secretes melatonin.

## 16.1 Melatonin

Melatonin regulates 24-hour rhythms.

It helps maintain:

- sleep-wake cycle;
- body temperature rhythm;
- metabolism;
- pigmentation;
- menstrual cycle;
- defence capability.

## 17. Thyroid Gland

Thyroid gland has two lobes.

The lobes are located on either side of trachea.

They are connected by a thin flap of connective tissue called isthmus.

Thyroid gland contains follicles and stromal tissue.

Follicular cells enclose a cavity and synthesize thyroid hormones.

## 17.1 Thyroid Hormones

Thyroid follicular cells synthesize:

- T4, tetraiodothyronine or thyroxine;
- T3, triiodothyronine.

Iodine is essential for normal thyroid hormone synthesis.

## 17.2 Thyroid Hormone Functions

Thyroid hormones:

- regulate basal metabolic rate;
- support RBC formation;
- regulate carbohydrate metabolism;
- regulate protein metabolism;
- regulate fat metabolism;
- influence water and electrolyte balance;
- support growth and maturation of central neural system.

## 17.3 Hypothyroidism And Goitre

Iodine deficiency reduces thyroid hormone synthesis.

This can cause hypothyroidism.

Thyroid gland may enlarge.

This enlargement is called goitre.

## 17.4 Cretinism

Hypothyroidism during pregnancy or early development can cause defective development of baby.

Effects may include:

- stunted growth;
- mental retardation;
- low intelligence quotient;
- abnormal skin;
- deaf-mutism.

This condition is called cretinism.

## 17.5 Hypothyroidism In Adult Women

In adult women, hypothyroidism may cause irregular menstrual cycle.

## 17.6 Hyperthyroidism

Hyperthyroidism means abnormally high thyroid hormone secretion.

It may occur due to:

- thyroid cancer;
- thyroid nodules;
- autoimmune stimulation in Graves disease.

It adversely affects body physiology.

## 17.7 Graves Disease

Exophthalmic goitre is a form of hyperthyroidism.

It is also called Graves disease.

Features:

- enlarged thyroid gland;
- protruding eyeballs;
- increased basal metabolic rate;
- weight loss.

## 17.8 Thyrocalcitonin

Thyroid gland also secretes thyrocalcitonin, or TCT.

TCT regulates blood calcium level.

It lowers blood calcium.

## 18. Parathyroid Glands

Humans have four parathyroid glands.

They are located on the back side of thyroid gland.

There is one pair in each thyroid lobe region.

## 18.1 Parathyroid Hormone

Parathyroid glands secrete PTH.

PTH is a peptide hormone.

Its secretion is regulated by circulating Ca2+ level.

## 18.2 PTH Functions

PTH increases blood Ca2+.

It acts by:

- stimulating bone resorption;
- increasing Ca2+ reabsorption by renal tubules;
- increasing Ca2+ absorption from digested food.

PTH is a hypercalcemic hormone.

Together, PTH and TCT maintain calcium balance.

## 19. Thymus

Thymus is a lobular structure located between lungs behind sternum on ventral side of aorta.

It plays a major role in immune system development.

## 19.1 Thymosins

Thymus secretes peptide hormones called thymosins.

Functions:

- differentiation of T lymphocytes;
- cell-mediated immunity;
- promotion of antibody production;
- support of humoral immunity.

Thymus degenerates in old individuals.

Thymosin production decreases.

Immune responses become weaker.

## 20. Adrenal Glands

Humans have one pair of adrenal glands.

Each adrenal gland is located above a kidney.

The gland has two regions:

- adrenal medulla;
- adrenal cortex.

## 21. Adrenal Medulla

Adrenal medulla is centrally located.

It secretes catecholamines:

- adrenaline, also called epinephrine;
- noradrenaline, also called norepinephrine.

## 21.1 Catecholamine Functions

Catecholamines are rapidly secreted during stress and emergency.

They are called emergency hormones or fight-or-flight hormones.

Effects:

- increased alertness;
- pupil dilation;
- piloerection;
- sweating;
- increased heart rate;
- increased strength of heart contraction;
- increased respiration rate;
- glycogen breakdown;
- increased blood glucose;
- lipid breakdown;
- protein breakdown.

## 22. Adrenal Cortex

Adrenal cortex is outer adrenal region.

It has three layers:

- zona glomerulosa;
- zona fasciculata;
- zona reticularis.

It secretes corticoids.

Underproduction of adrenal cortex hormones can cause Addison disease, with altered carbohydrate metabolism, weakness, and fatigue.

## 22.1 Glucocorticoids

Glucocorticoids regulate carbohydrate metabolism.

Cortisol is the main glucocorticoid in humans.

Functions:

- stimulates gluconeogenesis;
- stimulates lipolysis;
- stimulates proteolysis;
- inhibits cellular uptake and use of amino acids;
- maintains cardiovascular system;
- supports kidney function;
- produces anti-inflammatory reactions;
- suppresses immune response;
- stimulates RBC production.

## 22.2 Mineralocorticoids

Mineralocorticoids regulate water and electrolyte balance.

Aldosterone is the main mineralocorticoid.

It acts mainly on renal tubules.

Functions:

- reabsorption of Na+;
- reabsorption of water;
- excretion of K+;
- excretion of phosphate ions;
- maintenance of body fluid volume;
- maintenance of osmotic pressure;
- maintenance of blood pressure.

## 22.3 Adrenal Androgens

Adrenal cortex secretes small amounts of androgenic steroids.

They help in growth of:

- axillary hair;
- pubic hair;
- facial hair during puberty.

## 23. Pancreas

Pancreas is a composite gland.

It has both:

- exocrine function;
- endocrine function.

The endocrine pancreas consists of islets of Langerhans.

Normal human pancreas has about 1 to 2 million islets.

They form only about 1 to 2 percent of pancreatic tissue.

## 23.1 Alpha And Beta Cells

Main endocrine cell types:

- alpha cells;
- beta cells.

Alpha cells secrete glucagon.

Beta cells secrete insulin.

## 24. Glucagon

Glucagon is a peptide hormone.

It helps maintain blood glucose.

It acts mainly on liver cells.

Effects:

- stimulates glycogenolysis;
- stimulates gluconeogenesis;
- reduces cellular glucose uptake and use;
- raises blood glucose.

Glucagon is hyperglycemic.

## 25. Insulin

Insulin is a peptide hormone.

It is important for glucose homeostasis.

It acts mainly on:

- hepatocytes;
- adipocytes.

Effects:

- enhances cellular glucose uptake;
- enhances glucose use;
- stimulates glycogenesis;
- lowers blood glucose.

Insulin is hypoglycemic.

## 26. Diabetes Mellitus

Prolonged hyperglycemia leads to diabetes mellitus.

It is associated with:

- loss of glucose through urine;
- formation of ketone bodies.

Diabetic patients may be treated with insulin therapy when needed.

## 27. Testis

Males have a pair of testes in scrotal sac outside abdomen.

Testis has dual function:

- primary sex organ;
- endocrine gland.

It contains:

- seminiferous tubules;
- stromal or interstitial tissue.

## 27.1 Leydig Cells And Androgens

Leydig cells are interstitial cells in intertubular spaces.

They produce androgens, mainly testosterone.

## 27.2 Androgen Functions

Androgens regulate:

- development of male accessory sex organs;
- maturation of epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate gland, urethra;
- male secondary sexual characters;
- facial and axillary hair growth;
- muscular growth;
- low-pitched voice;
- aggressiveness in raw chapter wording;
- spermatogenesis;
- male sexual behaviour;
- anabolic effects on protein and carbohydrate metabolism.

## 28. Ovary

Females have a pair of ovaries in abdomen.

Ovary is the primary female sex organ.

It produces one ovum during each menstrual cycle.

It also functions as an endocrine gland.

## 28.1 Ovarian Hormones

Ovary produces:

- estrogens;
- progesterone.

Estrogens are mainly secreted by growing ovarian follicles.

After ovulation, ruptured follicle becomes corpus luteum.

Corpus luteum secretes mainly progesterone.

## 28.2 Estrogen Functions

Estrogens stimulate:

- growth and activity of female secondary sex organs;
- development of ovarian follicles;
- female secondary sexual characters;
- high-pitched voice in raw chapter example;
- mammary gland development;
- female sexual behaviour.

## 28.3 Progesterone Functions

Progesterone supports pregnancy.

It acts on mammary glands and stimulates:

- formation of alveoli;
- milk secretion preparation.

## 29. Hormones From Heart, Kidney, And GI Tract

Some organs that are not classical endocrine glands also secrete hormones.

## 29.1 Heart

Atrial wall of heart secretes atrial natriuretic factor.

ANF is a peptide hormone.

When blood pressure increases, ANF is secreted.

ANF causes vasodilation.

This lowers blood pressure.

## 29.2 Kidney

Juxtaglomerular cells of kidney produce erythropoietin.

Erythropoietin stimulates erythropoiesis.

Erythropoiesis means formation of RBCs.

## 29.3 Gastrointestinal Tract

Endocrine cells in GI tract secrete peptide hormones:

- gastrin;
- secretin;
- cholecystokinin, or CCK;
- gastric inhibitory peptide, or GIP.

## 29.4 Gastrin

Gastrin acts on gastric glands.

It stimulates secretion of:

- hydrochloric acid;
- pepsinogen.

## 29.5 Secretin

Secretin acts on exocrine pancreas.

It stimulates secretion of:

- water;
- bicarbonate ions.

## 29.6 Cholecystokinin

CCK acts on:

- pancreas;
- gall bladder.

It stimulates:

- pancreatic enzyme secretion;
- bile juice release.

## 29.7 GIP

GIP inhibits:

- gastric secretion;
- gastric motility.

## 29.8 Growth Factors

Several non-endocrine tissues secrete growth factors.

Growth factors are essential for:

- tissue growth;
- repair;
- regeneration.

## 30. Chemical Classes Of Hormones

Hormones can be classified by chemical nature.

Main groups:

- peptide, polypeptide, and protein hormones;
- steroids;
- iodothyronines;
- amino-acid derivatives.

## 30.1 Examples

| Hormone Class | Examples |
|---|---|
| Peptide, polypeptide, protein | Insulin, glucagon, pituitary hormones, hypothalamic hormones |
| Steroids | Cortisol, testosterone, estradiol, progesterone |
| Iodothyronines | T3, T4 |
| Amino-acid derivatives | Epinephrine |

## 31. Hormone Receptors

Hormones act only on target tissues.

Target tissues have specific receptors.

Each receptor is specific for a hormone.

When hormone binds receptor, hormone-receptor complex forms.

This triggers biochemical changes in the target cell.

## 32. Membrane-Bound Receptor Mechanism

Many peptide and protein hormones bind membrane receptors.

They usually do not enter the target cell.

Instead, they produce second messengers inside the cell.

Examples of second messengers:

- cyclic AMP;
- IP3;
- Ca++.

Second messengers regulate cellular metabolism.

## 32.1 FSH Action As Example

FSH is a protein or glycoprotein hormone.

It binds to membrane receptors on target gonadal cells.

It activates second messenger pathways.

These pathways change cellular activity.

In females, FSH stimulates ovarian follicle growth.

In males, FSH helps regulate spermatogenesis along with androgens.

## 33. Intracellular Receptor Mechanism

Steroid hormones and iodothyronines can interact with intracellular receptors.

These receptors are often nuclear.

Hormone-receptor complex interacts with the genome.

It regulates gene expression or chromosome function.

Cumulative biochemical actions produce physiological and developmental effects.

## 34. Membrane And Intracellular Mechanisms Compared

| Feature | Membrane Receptor Hormones | Intracellular Receptor Hormones |
|---|---|---|
| Receptor location | Cell membrane | Cytoplasm or nucleus |
| Hormone entry into cell | Usually no | Usually yes |
| Signal method | Second messenger | Gene regulation |
| Examples | Insulin, glucagon, FSH, pituitary hormones | Steroids, thyroid hormones |
| Effect speed | Often faster metabolic effects | Often slower genomic effects |

## 36. Common Misconceptions

## 36.1 Misconception: Only Endocrine Glands Produce Hormones

Correct idea:

Classical endocrine glands produce hormones, but heart, kidney, GI tract, and other tissues also produce hormone-like messengers.

## 36.2 Misconception: All Hormones Travel To Distant Organs Only

Correct idea:

Many hormones act through blood at distant targets, but modern hormone definition includes intercellular messengers produced in trace amounts.

## 36.3 Misconception: Posterior Pituitary Synthesises ADH And Oxytocin

Correct idea:

ADH and oxytocin are synthesised by hypothalamus.

Posterior pituitary stores and releases them.

## 36.4 Misconception: Insulin Raises Blood Glucose

Correct idea:

Insulin lowers blood glucose.

Glucagon raises blood glucose.

## 36.5 Misconception: PTH Lowers Calcium

Correct idea:

PTH raises blood calcium.

TCT lowers blood calcium.

## 36.6 Misconception: Steroid Hormones Use Only Membrane Second Messengers

Correct idea:

Steroid hormones usually bind intracellular receptors and regulate gene expression.

## 37. Solved Concept Checks

## 37.1 Why Are Endocrine Glands Called Ductless?

They lack ducts.

They release hormones into blood or surrounding fluid instead of through ducts.

## 37.2 Why Is Hormone Action Specific?

Only target cells with specific receptors can bind a hormone.

Without receptor, the cell does not respond.

## 37.3 Why Does Hypothalamus Control Pituitary?

Hypothalamus secretes releasing and inhibiting hormones that regulate anterior pituitary.

It also directly controls posterior pituitary through neural connections.

## 37.4 Why Is ADH Called Antidiuretic?

ADH increases water reabsorption in kidney tubules.

This reduces water loss in urine.

Therefore it prevents diuresis.

## 37.5 Why Is Glucagon Hyperglycemic?

Glucagon stimulates glycogen breakdown and gluconeogenesis in liver.

This raises blood glucose.

## 37.6 Why Is Insulin Hypoglycemic?

Insulin increases glucose uptake and glycogen formation.

This lowers blood glucose.

## 37.7 Why Is PTH Hypercalcemic?

PTH increases blood calcium by bone resorption, renal calcium reabsorption, and intestinal calcium absorption.

## 37.8 Why Does Thymus Weakening Affect Old People?

Thymus degenerates with age.

Thymosin production decreases.

T lymphocyte differentiation and immune response weaken.

## 38.6 PTH Function

PTH increases blood Ca2+ level.

It stimulates bone resorption, calcium reabsorption in kidney tubules, and calcium absorption from digested food.

## 38.7 Thyroid Hormone Function

Thyroid hormones regulate basal metabolic rate, RBC formation, metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins and fats, water-electrolyte balance, and nervous system development.

## 38.8 Thymosin Function

Thymosins promote T lymphocyte differentiation and cell-mediated immunity.

They also promote antibody production for humoral immunity.

## 38.9 Androgen Function

Androgens regulate male accessory sex organs, male secondary sex characters, spermatogenesis, libido, and anabolic metabolism.

## 38.10 Estrogen Function

Estrogens stimulate female accessory sex organ growth, ovarian follicle development, female secondary sex characters, mammary gland development, and female sexual behaviour.

## 38.11 Insulin And Glucagon

Insulin lowers blood glucose by increasing glucose uptake and glycogenesis.

Glucagon raises blood glucose by stimulating glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.

Together they maintain glucose homeostasis.

## 38.14 Mechanism Of FSH Action

FSH is a protein or glycoprotein hormone.

It binds to membrane-bound receptors on gonadal target cells.

It generates second messengers inside the cell.

These second messengers alter cellular metabolism and gene expression pathways.

In females, FSH stimulates ovarian follicle development.

In males, it supports spermatogenesis along with androgens.

## 41. Practice Answers

## 41.1 Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes mellitus is caused by insulin deficiency or insulin resistance.

It produces prolonged hyperglycemia.

It may cause glucose loss in urine and ketone body formation.

## 41.2 Goitre

Goitre is enlargement of thyroid gland.

It commonly occurs due to iodine deficiency, which reduces thyroid hormone synthesis.

## 41.3 Cretinism

Cretinism occurs due to thyroid hormone deficiency during development.

It causes stunted growth and impaired mental development.

## 41.4 ANF

ANF is secreted by atrial wall of heart.

It causes vasodilation and lowers blood pressure.

## 41.5 Erythropoietin

Erythropoietin is produced by kidney.

It stimulates erythropoiesis, the formation of RBCs.
