## 1. Need For Transport

All living cells require continuous supply of:

- nutrients;
- oxygen;
- hormones;
- ions;
- water;
- other essential substances.

Cells also produce wastes and harmful substances that must be removed.

In simple organisms, diffusion or water circulation through body cavities may be enough.

In complex organisms, special body fluids transport materials.

## 2. Body Fluids

The two important transport fluids in humans are:

- blood;
- lymph.

Blood is the main transport fluid.

Lymph supports transport, immune function, and return of tissue fluid.

## 3. Blood

Blood is a special connective tissue.

It has:

- fluid matrix called plasma;
- formed elements suspended in plasma.

Approximate composition:

| Component | Percentage Of Blood |
|---|---:|
| Plasma | 55 percent |
| Formed elements | 45 percent |

## 3.1 Why Blood Is A Connective Tissue

Blood is considered connective tissue because:

- it has a matrix, plasma;
- it has cells and cell fragments;
- it is mesodermal in origin;
- it connects body tissues by transporting materials;
- it carries gases, nutrients, wastes, hormones, and immune components.

It is a fluid connective tissue.

## 4. Plasma

Plasma is straw-coloured and viscous.

It forms about 55 percent of blood.

Composition:

- 90 to 92 percent water;
- 6 to 8 percent proteins;
- minerals;
- nutrients;
- gases;
- hormones;
- waste products;
- inactive clotting factors.

## 4.1 Major Plasma Proteins

Major plasma proteins:

- fibrinogen;
- globulins;
- albumins.

## 4.2 Fibrinogen

Fibrinogen is required for blood clotting.

During coagulation, fibrinogen is converted into fibrin.

Fibrin forms the thread-like clot network.

## 4.3 Globulins

Globulins are mainly involved in defence mechanisms.

Many antibodies are globulin proteins.

They help protect the body from pathogens and foreign substances.

## 4.4 Albumins

Albumins help maintain osmotic balance of blood.

They help retain water in blood plasma.

This supports blood volume and pressure.

## 4.5 Other Plasma Components

Plasma contains minerals such as:

- Na+;
- Ca2+;
- Mg2+;
- HCO3-;
- Cl-.

It also transports:

- glucose;
- amino acids;
- lipids;
- hormones;
- wastes.

## 4.6 Serum

Serum is plasma without clotting factors.

Simple distinction:

```text
plasma = fluid part of unclotted blood
serum = plasma minus clotting factors
```

## 5. Formed Elements

Formed elements include:

- erythrocytes, or RBCs;
- leucocytes, or WBCs;
- platelets, or thrombocytes.

They form about 45 percent of blood.

## 6. Erythrocytes

Erythrocytes are red blood cells.

They are the most abundant blood cells.

In a healthy adult male:

```text
about 5 to 5.5 million RBCs per mm3 of blood
```

## 6.1 RBC Formation

In adults, RBCs are formed in red bone marrow.

## 6.2 RBC Structure

In most mammals, RBCs:

- lack nucleus;
- are biconcave;
- contain haemoglobin.

Biconcave shape increases surface area for gas exchange.

Absence of nucleus provides more space for haemoglobin.

## 6.3 Haemoglobin

Haemoglobin is a red, iron-containing protein.

It transports respiratory gases.

A healthy person has about:

```text
12 to 16 g haemoglobin per 100 mL blood
```

## 6.4 RBC Life Span

RBCs live for about 120 days.

Old RBCs are destroyed mainly in the spleen.

The spleen is called the graveyard of RBCs.

## 7. Leucocytes

Leucocytes are white blood cells.

They are colourless because they lack haemoglobin.

They are nucleated.

They are fewer than RBCs.

Normal count:

```text
6000 to 8000 WBCs per mm3 of blood
```

They are generally short-lived.

## 7.1 Types Of Leucocytes

Two major categories:

- granulocytes;
- agranulocytes.

Granulocytes:

- neutrophils;
- eosinophils;
- basophils.

Agranulocytes:

- lymphocytes;
- monocytes.

## 7.2 Neutrophils

Neutrophils are the most abundant WBCs.

They form about 60 to 65 percent of total WBCs.

They are phagocytic.

They destroy foreign organisms.

## 7.3 Monocytes

Monocytes form about 6 to 8 percent of WBCs.

They are phagocytic.

They can destroy pathogens and cellular debris.

## 7.4 Basophils

Basophils are the least abundant WBCs.

They form about 0.5 to 1 percent of total WBCs.

They secrete:

- histamine;
- serotonin;
- heparin.

They are involved in inflammatory reactions.

## 7.5 Eosinophils

Eosinophils form about 2 to 3 percent of WBCs.

They resist infections.

They are also associated with allergic reactions.

## 7.6 Lymphocytes

Lymphocytes form about 20 to 25 percent of WBCs.

Major types:

- B lymphocytes;
- T lymphocytes.

They are responsible for immune responses.

## 8. Platelets

Platelets are also called thrombocytes.

They are cell fragments.

They are produced from megakaryocytes in bone marrow.

Normal count:

```text
150000 to 350000 platelets per mm3 of blood
```

Platelets release substances involved in coagulation.

Low platelet count can cause clotting disorders and excessive blood loss.

## 9. Formed Elements Summary

| Element | Count Or Share | Main Function |
|---|---|---|
| RBC | 5 to 5.5 million per mm3 in healthy adult male | Gas transport |
| Neutrophils | 60 to 65 percent of WBCs | Phagocytosis |
| Eosinophils | 2 to 3 percent of WBCs | Resist infections, allergy role |
| Basophils | 0.5 to 1 percent of WBCs | Inflammation mediators |
| Monocytes | 6 to 8 percent of WBCs | Phagocytosis |
| Lymphocytes | 20 to 25 percent of WBCs | Immune responses |
| Platelets | 150000 to 350000 per mm3 | Clotting |

## 10. Blood Groups

Human blood differs in surface antigens on RBCs.

Two important systems:

- ABO grouping;
- Rh grouping.

Matching blood groups is essential before transfusion.

Mismatched blood can cause clumping and destruction of RBCs.

## 11. ABO Blood Grouping

ABO grouping depends on presence or absence of A and B antigens on RBCs.

Plasma contains natural antibodies against absent antigens.

## 11.1 ABO Table

| Blood Group | Antigen On RBC | Antibody In Plasma | Can Receive From |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | A | anti-B | A, O |
| B | B | anti-A | B, O |
| AB | A and B | none | AB, A, B, O |
| O | none | anti-A and anti-B | O |

## 11.2 Universal Donor And Recipient

Group O has no A or B antigen on RBCs.

Therefore group O is called universal donor in the basic ABO system.

Group AB has no anti-A or anti-B antibody in plasma.

Therefore group AB is called universal recipient in the basic ABO system.

In actual medical practice, cross-matching is still essential.

## 12. Rh Blood Grouping

Rh grouping depends on Rh antigen on RBCs.

Most humans, about 80 percent in the raw chapter, are Rh positive.

Rh positive means Rh antigen is present.

Rh negative means Rh antigen is absent.

## 12.1 Rh In Transfusion

An Rh negative person exposed to Rh positive blood may form anti-Rh antibodies.

Therefore Rh compatibility must be checked before transfusion.

## 12.2 Erythroblastosis Foetalis

Erythroblastosis foetalis can occur when:

- mother is Rh negative;
- fetus is Rh positive;
- maternal blood becomes sensitised to Rh antigen.

In first pregnancy, fetal and maternal blood are usually separated by placenta.

During delivery, some fetal Rh positive blood may enter maternal circulation.

The mother may produce anti-Rh antibodies.

In later Rh positive pregnancies, these antibodies can cross placenta and destroy fetal RBCs.

Consequences may include:

- severe anaemia;
- jaundice;
- fetal damage;
- death in severe cases.

Prevention:

```text
anti-Rh antibodies are given to the mother soon after first delivery
```

This prevents sensitisation.

## 13. Coagulation Of Blood

Coagulation is blood clotting.

It prevents excessive blood loss after injury.

At the site of injury, a clot appears as a dark reddish-brown scum.

## 13.1 What A Clot Contains

A clot is mainly a network of fibrin threads.

The network traps:

- dead formed elements;
- damaged formed elements;
- blood cells;
- platelets.

## 13.2 Fibrin Formation

Fibrin is formed from fibrinogen.

Fibrinogen is inactive and present in plasma.

Thrombin converts fibrinogen to fibrin.

```text
fibrinogen -> fibrin
```

## 13.3 Thrombin Formation

Thrombin is formed from prothrombin.

Prothrombin is inactive and present in plasma.

Thrombokinase enzyme complex helps convert prothrombin to thrombin.

```text
prothrombin -> thrombin
```

## 13.4 Cascade Process

Coagulation involves a cascade of linked enzymatic reactions.

Many clotting factors circulate in inactive forms.

Injury activates platelets and tissue factors.

These activate the clotting cascade.

Calcium ions play an important role in clotting.

## 14. Lymph

As blood passes through capillaries, some fluid leaves blood and enters tissue spaces.

This fluid is interstitial fluid or tissue fluid.

It contains:

- water;
- small water-soluble substances;
- minerals similar to plasma.

Large proteins and most formed elements remain in blood vessels.

## 14.1 Role Of Tissue Fluid

Exchange between blood and tissue cells occurs through tissue fluid.

Materials moving through tissue fluid include:

- nutrients;
- gases;
- wastes;
- hormones;
- ions.

## 14.2 Lymph Formation

The lymphatic system collects excess tissue fluid.

Once inside lymphatic vessels, the fluid is called lymph.

Lymph is eventually returned to major veins.

## 14.3 Composition And Functions Of Lymph

Lymph is colourless.

It contains specialised lymphocytes.

Functions:

- immune response;
- return of tissue fluid to blood;
- transport of nutrients;
- transport of hormones;
- absorption and transport of fats.

Fats are absorbed through lacteals in intestinal villi.

## 15. Blood And Lymph Compared

| Feature | Blood | Lymph |
|---|---|---|
| Colour | Red due to haemoglobin | Colourless |
| RBCs | Present | Absent normally |
| Platelets | Present | Very few or absent normally |
| Proteins | More plasma proteins | Less protein |
| Main cells | RBCs, WBCs, platelets | Mainly lymphocytes |
| Flow | Pumped by heart | Slow flow through lymphatic vessels |
| Main role | Transport gases, nutrients, wastes | Return tissue fluid, immunity, fat transport |

## 16. Circulatory Pathways

Circulatory systems are of two main types:

- open circulation;
- closed circulation.

## 16.1 Open Circulatory System

In open circulation, blood pumped by the heart passes through vessels into open spaces or sinuses.

It is found in:

- arthropods;
- many molluscs.

Blood is not always confined to vessels.

## 16.2 Closed Circulatory System

In closed circulation, blood remains within a closed network of vessels.

It is found in:

- annelids;
- chordates.

Advantage:

- flow can be precisely regulated;
- pressure can be maintained;
- delivery to tissues is more controlled.

## 17. Vertebrate Hearts

All vertebrates have muscular chambered hearts.

## 17.1 Fish Heart

Fishes have a two-chambered heart:

- one atrium;
- one ventricle.

The heart pumps deoxygenated blood to gills.

Blood is oxygenated in gills and then goes to body tissues.

This is single circulation.

## 17.2 Amphibians And Most Reptiles

Amphibians and reptiles, except crocodiles, generally have three-chambered hearts:

- two atria;
- one ventricle.

Oxygenated and deoxygenated blood may mix in the single ventricle.

This is incomplete double circulation.

## 17.3 Crocodiles, Birds, And Mammals

Crocodiles, birds, and mammals have four-chambered hearts:

- two atria;
- two ventricles.

Oxygenated and deoxygenated blood remain separated.

This is complete double circulation.

## 18. Human Circulatory System

The human circulatory system is also called the blood vascular system.

It consists of:

- heart;
- blood vessels;
- blood.

## 18.1 Position And Covering Of Heart

The heart is:

- mesodermal in origin;
- located in thoracic cavity;
- between the two lungs;
- slightly tilted to the left;
- about the size of a clenched fist.

It is protected by the pericardium.

The pericardium is a double-walled membranous bag.

Pericardial fluid reduces friction and protects the heart.

## 19. Chambers Of Human Heart

Human heart has four chambers:

- right atrium;
- left atrium;
- right ventricle;
- left ventricle.

Atria are smaller upper chambers.

Ventricles are larger lower chambers.

## 19.1 Septa

Inter-atrial septum separates right and left atria.

Inter-ventricular septum separates right and left ventricles.

Atrio-ventricular septum separates atria from ventricles.

Openings in atrio-ventricular septa connect atria and ventricles of the same side.

## 19.2 Valves

Valves maintain one-way blood flow.

| Valve | Location | Main Role |
|---|---|---|
| Tricuspid valve | Right atrium to right ventricle | Prevents backflow to right atrium |
| Bicuspid or mitral valve | Left atrium to left ventricle | Prevents backflow to left atrium |
| Semilunar valves | Openings to pulmonary artery and aorta | Prevent backflow into ventricles |

## 19.3 Why Ventricles Have Thicker Walls

Ventricles pump blood out of the heart.

They need greater force than atria.

Left ventricle is especially thick because it pumps blood to the whole body.

## 20. Nodal Tissue

Nodal tissue is specialised cardiac musculature.

It can generate action potentials without external stimuli.

This property is called autoexcitability.

## 20.1 Sino-Atrial Node

The sino-atrial node is located in the right upper corner of right atrium.

It is abbreviated SAN.

SAN can generate about 70 to 75 action potentials per minute.

It initiates and maintains rhythmic contraction of the heart.

Therefore it is called the pacemaker.

## 20.2 Atrio-Ventricular Node

The atrio-ventricular node is located in the lower left region of right atrium near the atrio-ventricular septum.

It is abbreviated AVN.

It receives impulse from SAN and passes it toward ventricles.

## 20.3 AV Bundle And Purkinje Fibres

From AVN, the atrio-ventricular bundle continues through the septum.

It divides into right and left bundles.

These branches form Purkinje fibres in ventricular muscles.

They distribute impulse through ventricles for coordinated contraction.

## 21. Myogenic Heart

Human heart is myogenic.

This means normal heartbeat originates in specialised cardiac muscle tissue, not directly from external nerves.

Nerves can modify heart rate and force, but the rhythm begins in nodal tissue.

## 22. Cardiac Cycle

Cardiac cycle is one complete sequence of events in the heart during one heartbeat.

It includes:

- atrial systole;
- ventricular systole;
- joint diastole.

At an average heart rate of 72 beats per minute:

```text
duration of one cardiac cycle = about 0.8 second
```

## 22.1 Joint Diastole

All four chambers are relaxed.

Tricuspid and bicuspid valves are open.

Semilunar valves are closed.

Blood flows:

- from vena cavae to right atrium and right ventricle;
- from pulmonary veins to left atrium and left ventricle.

## 22.2 Atrial Systole

SAN generates an action potential.

Both atria contract simultaneously.

This pushes additional blood into ventricles.

Atrial systole increases ventricular filling by about 30 percent.

## 22.3 Ventricular Systole

Impulse reaches ventricles through:

```text
AVN -> AV bundle -> bundle branches -> Purkinje fibres
```

Ventricles contract.

Atria relax.

Ventricular pressure rises.

Tricuspid and bicuspid valves close.

This closure produces the first heart sound:

```text
lub
```

As pressure rises further, semilunar valves open.

Blood is pumped:

- from right ventricle to pulmonary artery;
- from left ventricle to aorta.

## 22.4 Ventricular Diastole

Ventricles relax.

Ventricular pressure falls.

Semilunar valves close.

This closure produces the second heart sound:

```text
dub
```

As ventricular pressure falls further, AV valves open again.

Blood again flows into ventricles.

The cycle repeats.

## 23. Stroke Volume And Cardiac Output

Stroke volume is the blood pumped by each ventricle per beat.

Approximate value:

```text
70 mL
```

Cardiac output is the blood pumped by each ventricle per minute.

Formula:

```text
cardiac output = stroke volume x heart rate
```

Using normal values:

```text
70 mL x 72 beats per minute = 5040 mL per minute
```

So cardiac output is about:

```text
5 L per minute
```

Athletes can have much higher cardiac output during exercise.

## 24. Heart Sounds

Two main heart sounds occur in each cardiac cycle.

| Sound | Cause |
|---|---|
| Lub | Closure of tricuspid and bicuspid valves |
| Dub | Closure of semilunar valves |

These sounds are heard using a stethoscope.

They have clinical diagnostic value.

## 25. Electrocardiogram

An electrocardiogram records electrical events of the heart during a cardiac cycle.

The instrument is an electrocardiograph.

Standard ECG uses electrical leads attached to:

- right wrist;
- left wrist;
- left ankle.

For detailed evaluation, multiple chest leads may be used.

## 25.1 P Wave

P wave represents atrial depolarisation.

It leads to atrial contraction.

## 25.2 QRS Complex

QRS complex represents ventricular depolarisation.

It initiates ventricular contraction.

Ventricular systole begins shortly after Q.

## 25.3 T Wave

T wave represents ventricular repolarisation.

It marks return of ventricles to normal electrical state.

End of T wave marks end of systole.

## 25.4 ECG Significance

Counting QRS complexes in a given time gives heart rate.

Abnormal ECG shape may indicate heart disease or conduction problems.

ECG is clinically important.

## 26. Blood Vessels

Blood flows through arteries, veins, and capillaries.

Arteries and veins have three layers:

1. tunica intima;
2. tunica media;
3. tunica externa.

## 26.1 Tunica Intima

Tunica intima is the inner lining.

It is made of squamous endothelium.

## 26.2 Tunica Media

Tunica media is the middle layer.

It contains smooth muscle and elastic fibres.

It is thinner in veins than in arteries.

## 26.3 Tunica Externa

Tunica externa is the outer layer.

It contains fibrous connective tissue with collagen fibres.

## 27. Double Circulation

Double circulation means blood passes through the heart twice during one complete circuit of the body.

The two circuits are:

- pulmonary circulation;
- systemic circulation.

## 27.1 Pulmonary Circulation

Pulmonary circulation carries blood between heart and lungs.

Route:

```text
right ventricle
-> pulmonary artery
-> lungs
-> pulmonary veins
-> left atrium
```

Function:

- sends deoxygenated blood to lungs;
- returns oxygenated blood to heart.

## 27.2 Systemic Circulation

Systemic circulation carries blood between heart and body tissues.

Route:

```text
left ventricle
-> aorta
-> arteries
-> arterioles
-> capillaries
-> venules
-> veins
-> vena cavae
-> right atrium
```

Function:

- supplies O2, nutrients, and essential substances to tissues;
- removes CO2 and wastes from tissues.

## 27.3 Significance Of Double Circulation

Double circulation is significant because:

- oxygenated and deoxygenated blood remain separate;
- lungs receive blood at suitable pressure;
- body tissues receive oxygenated blood at high pressure;
- oxygen delivery is efficient;
- high metabolic demands can be supported.

## 28. Portal And Coronary Circulation

## 28.1 Hepatic Portal System

The hepatic portal system connects digestive tract and liver.

The hepatic portal vein carries blood from intestine to liver before it enters systemic circulation.

This allows the liver to process absorbed nutrients and detoxify harmful substances.

## 28.2 Coronary Circulation

Coronary circulation supplies the heart muscle itself.

The heart needs its own blood supply because cardiac muscle is active continuously.

Coronary vessels deliver oxygen and nutrients and remove wastes from cardiac tissue.

## 29. Regulation Of Heart Function

The heart is intrinsically regulated by nodal tissue.

It is myogenic.

However, its function is moderated by:

- autonomic nervous system;
- adrenal medullary hormones.

## 29.1 Sympathetic Effect

Sympathetic nerves increase:

- heart rate;
- force of ventricular contraction;
- cardiac output.

## 29.2 Parasympathetic Effect

Parasympathetic nerves decrease:

- heart rate;
- speed of conduction;
- cardiac output.

## 29.3 Adrenal Medullary Hormones

Adrenal medullary hormones such as adrenaline and noradrenaline increase cardiac output.

They prepare the body for stress or emergency responses.

## 30. Disorders Of Circulatory System

## 30.1 Hypertension

Normal blood pressure is about:

```text
120/80 mm Hg
```

120 mm Hg is systolic pressure.

80 mm Hg is diastolic pressure.

Repeated blood pressure of:

```text
140/90 mm Hg or higher
```

indicates hypertension.

Hypertension can damage:

- heart;
- brain;
- kidneys;
- blood vessels.

## 30.2 Coronary Artery Disease

Coronary artery disease affects blood vessels supplying heart muscle.

It is often linked with atherosclerosis.

Atherosclerosis involves deposits of:

- calcium;
- fat;
- cholesterol;
- fibrous tissue.

These deposits narrow the arterial lumen.

Blood supply to heart muscle is reduced.

## 30.3 Angina

Angina pectoris is acute chest pain caused by insufficient oxygen reaching heart muscle.

It can occur in men and women.

It is more common in middle-aged and elderly people.

It occurs due to conditions that reduce blood flow to the heart.

## 30.4 Heart Failure

Heart failure is a state in which the heart cannot pump blood effectively enough to meet body needs.

It may be called congestive heart failure when lung congestion is a major symptom.

Heart failure is not the same as:

- cardiac arrest, where heart stops beating;
- heart attack, where heart muscle is suddenly damaged by poor blood supply.

## 31. Common Misconceptions

## 31.1 Misconception: Plasma And Serum Are Same

Correct idea:

Serum is plasma without clotting factors.

## 31.2 Misconception: All WBCs Have Same Function

Correct idea:

Different WBCs perform different immune roles.

Neutrophils and monocytes are phagocytic.

Lymphocytes are central to immune responses.

## 31.3 Misconception: O Blood Group Can Receive Any Blood

Correct idea:

O group is universal donor in basic ABO donation logic.

O group can receive only O in the basic ABO system.

AB group is universal recipient.

## 31.4 Misconception: Heartbeat Requires Nerve Impulse From Brain

Correct idea:

The heart is myogenic.

SAN initiates heartbeat.

Nerves only modify the rhythm and force.

## 31.5 Misconception: Pulmonary Artery Carries Oxygenated Blood

Correct idea:

Pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from right ventricle to lungs.

Pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood from lungs to left atrium.

## 31.6 Misconception: Cardiac Output Is Blood Pumped Per Beat

Correct idea:

Blood pumped per beat is stroke volume.

Blood pumped per minute is cardiac output.

## 32. Solved Concept Checks

## 32.1 Why Are Albumins Important?

Albumins maintain osmotic balance.

They help retain water in plasma and support blood volume.

## 32.2 Why Are Platelets Important?

Platelets release factors that start coagulation.

Low platelet count can lead to excessive bleeding.

## 32.3 Why Is Rh Matching Needed?

An Rh negative person exposed to Rh positive blood can form anti-Rh antibodies.

Future exposure may cause immune destruction of Rh positive RBCs.

## 32.4 Why Is SAN Called Pacemaker?

SAN generates the maximum number of action potentials per minute.

It initiates and maintains rhythmic heart contraction.

Therefore it sets the pace of heartbeat.

## 32.5 Why Is AVN Important?

AVN receives impulse from atria and conducts it to ventricles through AV bundle.

This coordinates ventricular contraction after atrial contraction.

## 32.6 Why Is Double Circulation Efficient?

It keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood separate.

It sends blood to lungs and body through separate circuits.

This supports efficient oxygen delivery.

## 32.7 Why Are Heart Valves Needed?

Valves ensure one-way blood flow.

They prevent backflow from ventricles to atria and from arteries to ventricles.

## 32.8 Why Is The Left Ventricle Thick?

The left ventricle pumps blood to the entire body.

It needs high pressure.

Therefore its muscular wall is thick.

## 33.4 Blood As Connective Tissue

Blood is a connective tissue because it has plasma as matrix and formed elements as cells or cell fragments.

It transports substances between different tissues and organs.

It is mesodermal in origin.

## 33.5 Difference Between Lymph And Blood

Blood contains RBCs, WBCs, platelets, and high protein content.

Lymph is colourless, has fewer proteins, normally lacks RBCs, and contains lymphocytes.

Blood flows in blood vessels under heart pressure.

Lymph flows in lymphatic vessels and returns tissue fluid to veins.

## 33.6 Double Circulation And Significance

Double circulation means blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circuit:

- once through pulmonary circulation;
- once through systemic circulation.

Significance:

- separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood;
- maintains efficient oxygen supply;
- supports high metabolic rate.

## 33.7 Open And Closed Circulation

Open circulation allows blood to flow into open spaces or sinuses.

Closed circulation keeps blood inside vessels.

Closed circulation allows more precise control of flow and pressure.

## 33.8 Systole And Diastole

Systole is contraction of heart chambers.

Diastole is relaxation of heart chambers.

Systole pumps blood forward.

Diastole allows filling.

## 33.9 P Wave And T Wave

P wave represents atrial depolarisation and leads to atrial contraction.

T wave represents ventricular repolarisation and marks return of ventricles to normal electrical state.

## 33.10 Evolutionary Pattern Of Vertebrate Heart

Fishes have two chambers and single circulation.

Amphibians and most reptiles have three chambers and incomplete double circulation.

Crocodiles, birds, and mammals have four chambers and complete double circulation.

## 33.11 Why Heart Is Myogenic

The heart is myogenic because its normal rhythm originates in specialised cardiac muscle, especially SAN.

It does not require external nerve impulse to start each beat.

## 33.12 SAN As Pacemaker

SAN generates action potentials at the highest rate, about 70 to 75 per minute.

It initiates and maintains rhythmic contraction.

Therefore it is called the pacemaker.

## 33.13 Significance Of AVN And AV Bundle

AVN receives impulse from atria and passes it to AV bundle.

AV bundle carries impulse through ventricular septum to Purkinje fibres.

This ensures coordinated ventricular contraction after atrial systole.

## 33.14 Cardiac Cycle And Cardiac Output

Cardiac cycle is the sequence of systole and diastole repeated in each heartbeat.

At 72 beats per minute, one cycle lasts about 0.8 second.

Cardiac output is blood pumped by each ventricle per minute.

```text
cardiac output = stroke volume x heart rate
```

Normal value:

```text
70 mL x 72 = about 5 L per minute
```

## 33.15 Heart Sounds

The first sound, lub, is due to closure of tricuspid and bicuspid valves.

The second sound, dub, is due to closure of semilunar valves.

## 33.16 ECG Peaks

P wave shows atrial depolarisation.

QRS complex shows ventricular depolarisation.

T wave shows ventricular repolarisation.

Counting QRS complexes gives heart rate.
