## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter becomes easier if you separate photosynthesis into two linked phases.

First learn the light reaction.

```text
Light energy -> excited electrons -> ATP + NADPH + O2
```

Then learn the carbon fixation reactions.

```text
CO2 + ATP + NADPH -> carbohydrate
```

Keep one central rule in mind:

- the light reaction happens in thylakoid membranes;
- the Calvin cycle happens in the stroma;
- the C4 pathway separates initial CO2 fixation and the Calvin cycle into different cell types;
- photorespiration is reduced when CO2 concentration near RuBisCO is high.

The chapter is not mainly about memorising names. It is about following matter and energy.

Track these five things throughout:

1. where light is absorbed;
2. where electrons come from;
3. where oxygen comes from;
4. where ATP and NADPH are formed;
5. where CO2 becomes carbohydrate.

## 1. Big Idea Of Plant Physiology

Plant physiology studies how plants function.

In this unit, flowering plants are studied through processes such as:

- photosynthesis;
- respiration;
- plant growth;
- plant development.

These processes occur at molecular and cellular levels, but they affect the whole plant.

Photosynthesis is one of the most important plant processes because it links:

- chloroplast structure;
- light absorption;
- energy conversion;
- carbon fixation;
- food production;
- atmospheric oxygen.

## 2. What Photosynthesis Means

Photosynthesis is a physico-chemical process.

It is physical because light energy is captured.

It is chemical because captured energy is used to form chemical bonds in organic compounds.

Green plants use photosynthesis to synthesise their own food.

Because they make their own organic food, green plants are autotrophs.

Most animals, fungi, and many microorganisms depend directly or indirectly on green plants. They are heterotrophs.

## 2.1 Why Photosynthesis Is Essential

Photosynthesis is essential for two major reasons.

First, it is the primary basis of food production on Earth.

Green plants make carbohydrates. Other organisms eat plants or eat organisms that have eaten plants.

Second, photosynthesis releases oxygen into the atmosphere.

Most aerobic organisms require oxygen for cellular respiration.

Therefore photosynthesis supports both:

- food chains;
- oxygen availability.

## 2.2 Core Summary Of Photosynthesis

In simple terms:

```text
carbon dioxide + water + light energy -> carbohydrate + oxygen
```

In green plants, the final overall equation is commonly written as:

```text
6CO2 + 12H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6H2O + 6O2
```

Important points:

- CO2 provides carbon for carbohydrate.
- H2O provides electrons and protons.
- O2 released during photosynthesis comes from water.
- Light energy is converted into chemical energy.
- Glucose can be converted to starch for storage.

## 3. What Was Already Known From Earlier Classes

Earlier experiments show that photosynthesis requires:

- chlorophyll;
- light;
- carbon dioxide;
- water;
- suitable living green tissue.

## 3.1 Variegated Leaf Test

A variegated leaf has green and non-green regions.

If the leaf is exposed to light and later tested for starch:

- green regions give a positive starch test;
- non-green regions do not give a positive starch test.

This shows that chlorophyll-containing tissue is needed for photosynthesis.

The conclusion is:

```text
green part + light -> starch formation
```

The non-green part lacks enough chlorophyll, so it cannot carry out photosynthesis effectively.

## 3.2 Covered Leaf Test

If part of a leaf is covered with black paper and the leaf is exposed to light:

- uncovered region receives light and forms starch;
- covered region does not receive light and does not form starch.

This shows that light is needed for photosynthesis.

## 3.3 KOH Test For Carbon Dioxide

Potassium hydroxide absorbs carbon dioxide.

If part of a leaf is enclosed in a test tube containing KOH-soaked cotton:

- the enclosed part receives little or no CO2;
- the exposed part receives normal air;
- after light exposure, the exposed part forms starch;
- the enclosed part does not form starch.

The conclusion is:

```text
CO2 is required for starch formation during photosynthesis.
```

## 4. Early Experiments On Photosynthesis

The understanding of photosynthesis developed gradually.

Each experiment answered one part of the larger question:

```text
How do green plants use light, air, and water to make food?
```

## 4.1 Joseph Priestley

Joseph Priestley studied air, burning, animals, and plants.

His observations:

- a candle in a closed bell jar eventually goes out;
- a mouse in a closed bell jar eventually suffocates;
- a mint plant in the same closed space allows the air to become suitable again;
- after some time, the candle can burn again and the mouse can survive.

Priestley's interpretation:

```text
burning candles and breathing animals damage air;
green plants restore air.
```

Modern interpretation:

- burning and respiration use oxygen and release CO2;
- green plants release oxygen during photosynthesis in light;
- plants help restore oxygen balance.

## 4.2 Jan Ingenhousz

Jan Ingenhousz improved the understanding by testing plants in light and dark.

His observations:

- green aquatic plants release bubbles in bright sunlight;
- bubbles do not form in darkness;
- bubbles form mainly around green parts.

The bubbles were later identified as oxygen.

His conclusions:

- sunlight is essential for oxygen release;
- green parts of plants perform this oxygen-releasing process;
- photosynthesis is linked with light.

## 4.3 Julius Von Sachs

Julius von Sachs provided evidence that green plants produce glucose.

His key findings:

- glucose is produced in green parts of plants;
- glucose is usually stored as starch;
- chlorophyll is located in special bodies inside cells;
- those bodies are now known as chloroplasts.

His work connected:

```text
green tissue -> chloroplasts -> glucose formation -> starch storage
```

## 4.4 T. W. Engelmann

Engelmann studied which wavelengths of light are most effective for photosynthesis.

His method:

- he split white light into different colours using a prism;
- he illuminated the green alga Cladophora;
- he placed aerobic bacteria near the alga;
- bacteria gathered where oxygen production was highest.

His observation:

- bacteria accumulated mainly in blue and red regions of the spectrum.

His conclusion:

```text
blue and red light are most effective for photosynthesis.
```

This produced an early action spectrum of photosynthesis.

## 4.5 Cornelius Van Niel

Van Niel studied photosynthesis in bacteria.

He proposed that photosynthesis is a light-dependent process in which hydrogen from an oxidisable compound reduces CO2.

General form:

```text
2H2A + CO2 + light energy -> 2A + [CH2O] + H2O
```

Here:

- H2A is a hydrogen donor;
- CO2 is reduced to carbohydrate;
- A is the oxidised product.

In green plants:

```text
H2O is the hydrogen donor.
```

When water is the donor:

```text
O2 is released.
```

In purple and green sulphur bacteria:

```text
H2S may be the hydrogen donor.
```

Then the product is sulphur or sulphate, not oxygen.

Van Niel's major inference:

```text
oxygen released by green plants comes from water, not from carbon dioxide.
```

This was later proved using radioisotopic experiments.

## 4.6 Melvin Calvin

Melvin Calvin traced carbon during photosynthesis using radioactive carbon.

He and his coworkers used C14-labelled CO2 to follow where carbon atoms went in algae.

His work identified:

- the first stable product of CO2 fixation in C3 plants;
- the pathway by which CO2 becomes carbohydrate;
- the cyclic nature of carbon fixation.

The pathway is called the Calvin cycle.

The first stable product in C3 plants is:

```text
3-phosphoglyceric acid, or 3-PGA
```

## 5. Equations Of Photosynthesis

## 5.1 Simple Empirical Equation

An older simplified equation represented carbohydrate as [CH2O].

```text
CO2 + H2O + light energy -> [CH2O] + O2
```

This is useful as a basic idea, but it hides important details.

It does not show clearly that oxygen comes from water.

## 5.2 Correct Overall Equation For Green Plants

The more accurate overall equation is:

```text
6CO2 + 12H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6H2O + 6O2
```

This means:

- 6 molecules of carbon dioxide enter;
- 12 molecules of water are used;
- one molecule of glucose is formed;
- 6 molecules of water appear as product;
- 6 molecules of oxygen are released.

## 5.3 Why Twelve Water Molecules Are Written

The equation writes 12H2O on the reactant side to represent water as the hydrogen and electron donor.

During photosynthesis:

- water is split;
- electrons replace those lost by PS II;
- protons contribute to proton gradient and reduction reactions;
- oxygen is released from water.

Some water is regenerated during sugar formation, so the net simplified version is often written as:

```text
6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2
```

But for understanding oxygen evolution, the fuller equation is better.

## 6. Where Photosynthesis Occurs

Photosynthesis occurs mainly in green leaves.

It can also occur in other green plant parts, such as:

- young green stems;
- green sepals;
- green fruits in some plants;
- any tissue containing functional chloroplasts.

## 6.1 Mesophyll Cells

Leaves contain mesophyll cells.

Mesophyll cells have many chloroplasts.

These chloroplasts often align along cell walls to receive suitable light.

Chloroplast positioning can change with light intensity:

- in low light, broad surfaces may face incoming light to absorb more;
- in intense light, chloroplasts may reduce direct exposure to avoid damage.

## 6.2 Chloroplast Structure

A chloroplast has:

- outer membrane;
- inner membrane;
- grana;
- stroma lamellae;
- stroma;
- ribosomes;
- starch granules;
- lipid droplets.

The most important parts for this chapter are:

| Chloroplast Part | Main Role In Photosynthesis |
|---|---|
| Grana thylakoids | Light absorption, electron transport, ATP and NADPH formation |
| Stroma lamellae | Cyclic electron flow in some conditions |
| Stroma | Calvin cycle and carbohydrate synthesis |

## 6.3 Division Of Labour In Chloroplast

The chloroplast has a clear functional division.

Membrane system:

- traps light energy;
- contains photosystems;
- carries electron transport proteins;
- helps make ATP;
- helps make NADPH.

Stroma:

- contains enzymes for CO2 fixation;
- runs Calvin cycle;
- synthesises sugar;
- converts sugar into starch.

## 6.4 Light Reaction And Carbon Reaction

Photosynthesis has two linked phases.

Light reaction:

- directly driven by light;
- occurs in thylakoid membranes;
- produces ATP, NADPH, and oxygen.

Carbon reaction:

- occurs in stroma;
- fixes CO2 into carbohydrates;
- uses ATP and NADPH from light reaction;
- is not directly driven by light at the exact moment of each enzyme step.

The term dark reaction can be misleading.

It does not mean that the reaction normally happens only in darkness.

It means the steps do not directly require photons.

In a living plant, carbon fixation depends on products made by the light reaction.

## 7. Photosynthetic Pigments

Leaves appear green because of pigments in chloroplasts.

Paper chromatography can separate leaf pigments.

The major pigments are:

- chlorophyll a;
- chlorophyll b;
- xanthophylls;
- carotenoids.

## 7.1 Pigment Colours

| Pigment | Typical Colour |
|---|---|
| Chlorophyll a | Bright green or blue green |
| Chlorophyll b | Yellow green |
| Xanthophylls | Yellow |
| Carotenoids | Yellow to yellow-orange |

The visible colour of a leaf is a combined effect of these pigments.

## 7.2 What Pigments Do

Pigments absorb light of specific wavelengths.

They do not absorb all wavelengths equally.

The energy of absorbed light can be used to excite electrons.

Excited electrons begin the energy conversion process of photosynthesis.

## 7.3 Absorption Spectrum

An absorption spectrum shows how strongly a pigment absorbs different wavelengths.

Chlorophyll a absorbs strongly in:

- blue region;
- red region.

It absorbs poorly in green region.

That is why much green light is reflected or transmitted, giving leaves their green appearance.

## 7.4 Action Spectrum

An action spectrum shows how effective different wavelengths are in driving photosynthesis.

For photosynthesis, maximum effectiveness is usually seen in:

- blue light;
- red light.

This matches the absorption behaviour of chlorophyll a.

## 7.5 Chlorophyll A As Chief Pigment

Chlorophyll a is called the chief pigment because:

- it is directly involved in reaction centres;
- it can donate excited electrons;
- its absorption peaks match high photosynthetic rate regions;
- without it, photosynthesis cannot proceed efficiently.

## 7.6 Role Of Accessory Pigments

Accessory pigments include:

- chlorophyll b;
- xanthophylls;
- carotenoids.

They help photosynthesis in three major ways.

First, they broaden the range of usable light.

They absorb wavelengths that chlorophyll a does not absorb strongly.

Second, they transfer absorbed energy to chlorophyll a.

Third, they protect chlorophyll a from photo-oxidation.

Photo-oxidation means light-induced oxidative damage to pigment molecules.

## 8. Light Reaction

The light reaction is also called the photochemical phase.

It includes:

- absorption of light;
- excitation of electrons;
- splitting of water;
- release of oxygen;
- electron transport;
- formation of ATP;
- formation of NADPH.

## 8.1 Photosystems

Photosynthetic pigments are organised into photosystems.

Higher plants have two main photosystems:

- photosystem I;
- photosystem II.

They were named in the order of discovery.

They do not act in that numerical order during non-cyclic electron transport.

In the Z scheme:

```text
PS II works before PS I.
```

## 8.2 Light Harvesting Complex

Each photosystem contains a light harvesting complex.

The light harvesting complex has many pigment molecules bound to proteins.

These pigment molecules form an antenna system.

The antenna system:

- catches light;
- absorbs different wavelengths;
- transfers energy toward the reaction centre.

## 8.3 Reaction Centre

The reaction centre is a special chlorophyll a molecule.

It is the molecule that actually donates the excited electron.

In PS I:

```text
reaction centre = P700
```

P700 absorbs best near 700 nm.

In PS II:

```text
reaction centre = P680
```

P680 absorbs best near 680 nm.

## 8.4 Antenna Versus Reaction Centre

| Feature | Antenna Pigments | Reaction Centre |
|---|---|---|
| Number | Many molecules | One special chlorophyll a pair or molecule |
| Function | Absorb and transfer energy | Donates excited electron |
| Pigment types | Chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, carotenoids, xanthophylls | Chlorophyll a |
| Direct electron donation | No | Yes |

## 9. Electron Transport

Electron transport converts light excitation into chemical energy.

In non-cyclic electron flow:

```text
water -> PS II -> electron carriers -> PS I -> NADP+
```

Final reduced product:

```text
NADPH + H+
```

## 9.1 Excitation In PS II

PS II reaction centre P680 absorbs red light.

The electron in chlorophyll a becomes excited.

It moves to a higher energy level.

The excited electron is captured by a primary electron acceptor.

P680 becomes electron-deficient.

This missing electron is later replaced by electrons from water.

## 9.2 Downhill Transport From PS II To PS I

The electron from PS II passes through an electron transport chain.

It moves through carriers such as cytochromes.

This movement is downhill in terms of redox potential.

Energy released during this flow helps build a proton gradient.

The electron finally reaches PS I.

## 9.3 Excitation In PS I

PS I reaction centre P700 absorbs light near 700 nm.

Its electron becomes excited.

The excited electron is transferred to another acceptor.

The electron then moves through carriers to NADP+.

NADP+ accepts electrons and protons to form NADPH.

## 9.4 Z Scheme

The whole non-cyclic electron pathway is called the Z scheme.

It is called this because if electron energy levels are drawn on a redox potential scale, the path resembles the letter Z.

Basic sequence:

```text
H2O -> PS II -> electron transport chain -> PS I -> NADP+ -> NADPH
```

Main products:

- ATP;
- NADPH;
- O2.

## 10. Splitting Of Water

PS II loses electrons during light excitation.

Those electrons must be replaced.

They are replaced by splitting water.

This process is associated with PS II.

Water splitting can be represented as:

```text
2H2O -> 4H+ + O2 + 4e-
```

Products:

- electrons replace lost electrons in PS II;
- protons help create a proton gradient;
- oxygen is released as a by-product.

## 10.1 Where Products Are Released

The water-splitting complex is located on the inner side of the thylakoid membrane.

Therefore:

- protons are released into the thylakoid lumen;
- oxygen is released from the chloroplast and later from the leaf;
- electrons enter PS II.

The accumulation of protons in the lumen is important for ATP synthesis.

## 10.2 Oxygen Origin

A common misconception is that oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from carbon dioxide.

It does not.

The released oxygen comes from water.

Radioisotope experiments proved this.

## 11. Photophosphorylation

Phosphorylation means formation of ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate.

Photophosphorylation means ATP formation using light-driven electron transport.

In chloroplasts:

```text
ADP + Pi -> ATP
```

The energy comes indirectly from light.

Light moves electrons.

Electron movement builds a proton gradient.

The proton gradient powers ATP synthase.

## 11.1 Non-Cyclic Photophosphorylation

Non-cyclic photophosphorylation uses both PS II and PS I.

Electron flow:

```text
H2O -> PS II -> electron carriers -> PS I -> NADP+
```

Why it is non-cyclic:

- electrons do not return to the starting photosystem;
- electrons from water replace electrons lost by PS II;
- electrons finally reduce NADP+.

Products:

- ATP;
- NADPH;
- O2.

## 11.2 Cyclic Photophosphorylation

Cyclic photophosphorylation uses PS I.

Electron flow:

```text
PS I -> electron acceptor -> electron transport chain -> PS I
```

Why it is cyclic:

- the excited electron returns to PS I;
- NADP+ is not reduced;
- water is not split.

Product:

- ATP only.

No NADPH is formed.

No oxygen is released.

## 11.3 Site Of Cyclic Photophosphorylation

Cyclic photophosphorylation can occur in stroma lamellae.

Stroma lamellae:

- contain PS I;
- lack PS II;
- lack NADP reductase.

Because NADP reductase is absent, the excited electron does not reduce NADP+.

Instead, it returns through carriers to PS I.

## 11.4 When Cyclic Flow Is Useful

The Calvin cycle needs more ATP than NADPH.

For each CO2 fixed:

```text
3 ATP and 2 NADPH are needed
```

Non-cyclic flow alone may not supply the ATP to NADPH ratio needed.

Cyclic photophosphorylation helps provide extra ATP.

## 11.5 Cyclic Versus Non-Cyclic Photophosphorylation

| Feature | Cyclic Photophosphorylation | Non-Cyclic Photophosphorylation |
|---|---|---|
| Photosystem involved | PS I only | PS II and PS I |
| Electron path | Returns to PS I | Moves from water to NADP+ |
| Water splitting | Absent | Present |
| Oxygen release | No | Yes |
| NADPH formation | No | Yes |
| ATP formation | Yes | Yes |
| Main role | Extra ATP supply | ATP, NADPH, and oxygen formation |
| Likely site | Stroma lamellae | Grana thylakoids |

## 12. Chemiosmotic Hypothesis

Chemiosmosis explains ATP synthesis in chloroplasts.

The principle is:

```text
proton gradient across membrane -> proton flow through ATP synthase -> ATP formation
```

In chloroplasts, the membrane is the thylakoid membrane.

The high proton concentration develops inside the thylakoid lumen.

## 12.1 How Proton Gradient Forms

Three events help create the proton gradient.

## 12.1.1 Water Splitting Adds Protons To Lumen

Water is split on the inner side of the thylakoid membrane.

This releases H+ into the lumen.

Result:

```text
lumen H+ concentration increases
```

## 12.1.2 Electron Transport Moves Protons Into Lumen

As electrons move through carriers, protons are transported from stroma to lumen.

Some carriers pick up protons from the stroma and release them into the lumen.

Result:

```text
lumen gains protons;
stroma loses protons.
```

## 12.1.3 NADP+ Reduction Removes Protons From Stroma

NADP reductase is located on the stroma side of the thylakoid membrane.

To form NADPH, protons from the stroma are used.

Result:

```text
stroma H+ concentration decreases
```

This strengthens the proton gradient.

## 12.2 Direction Of Proton Gradient

In chloroplasts:

```text
high H+ concentration = thylakoid lumen
low H+ concentration = stroma
```

Therefore protons tend to move:

```text
lumen -> stroma
```

They move through ATP synthase.

## 12.3 ATP Synthase In Chloroplast

Chloroplast ATP synthase has two major parts.

| Part | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| CF0 | Embedded in thylakoid membrane | Forms proton channel |
| CF1 | Extends toward stroma | Catalyses ATP formation |

As protons pass through CF0, CF1 changes shape and catalyses:

```text
ADP + Pi -> ATP
```

ATP is formed on the stroma side.

This is useful because the Calvin cycle also occurs in the stroma.

## 12.4 Requirements For Chemiosmosis

Chemiosmosis requires:

- a membrane;
- a proton pump or proton-moving system;
- a proton gradient;
- ATP synthase.

In chloroplasts:

- thylakoid membrane acts as the membrane;
- electron transport helps move protons;
- lumen becomes proton-rich;
- ATP synthase uses the gradient.

## 13. Where ATP And NADPH Are Used

The products of light reaction are:

- ATP;
- NADPH;
- oxygen.

Oxygen diffuses out.

ATP and NADPH are used in the stroma for carbon fixation.

The carbon fixation phase produces sugars.

## 13.1 Why The Term Dark Reaction Is Misleading

The biosynthetic phase is often called the dark reaction.

This name can mislead learners.

It does not mean that the phase normally happens only in darkness.

It means the reactions do not directly require light photons.

However, they depend on:

- ATP from light reaction;
- NADPH from light reaction;
- CO2;
- enzymes in the stroma.

If light stops, ATP and NADPH supply soon stops.

Then carbon fixation also stops after a short time.

## 13.2 Carbon Fixation Question

Scientists asked:

```text
What is the first stable product after CO2 is fixed?
```

In many plants, the first stable product is a 3-carbon compound.

That compound is:

```text
3-PGA
```

Such plants are called C3 plants.

In some plants, the first stable product is a 4-carbon compound.

That compound is:

```text
OAA
```

Such plants are called C4 plants.

## 14. Primary Acceptor Of CO2

The primary CO2 acceptor in the Calvin cycle is RuBP.

RuBP means ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate.

It is a 5-carbon sugar phosphate.

This was not obvious at first.

Scientists initially expected a 2-carbon acceptor because CO2 plus a 2-carbon compound would give a 3-carbon product.

However, experiments showed that the real acceptor is 5-carbon RuBP.

When CO2 combines with RuBP, the unstable 6-carbon intermediate splits into two 3-carbon molecules.

Result:

```text
RuBP + CO2 -> 2 molecules of 3-PGA
```

## 15. Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma.

It occurs in all photosynthetic plants.

This includes:

- C3 plants;
- C4 plants;
- other photosynthetic plants.

The difference is where and how CO2 is first fixed before the Calvin cycle.

## 15.1 Three Stages Of Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle has three major stages:

1. carboxylation;
2. reduction;
3. regeneration.

## 15.2 Stage 1 - Carboxylation

Carboxylation means addition of CO2 to an organic compound.

In the Calvin cycle:

```text
CO2 combines with RuBP
```

The enzyme is RuBisCO.

Full name:

```text
ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase
```

The reaction forms two molecules of 3-PGA.

```text
RuBP + CO2 -> 2 x 3-PGA
```

Why the enzyme name includes oxygenase:

- it can add CO2 to RuBP;
- it can also react RuBP with O2.

This dual nature is important in photorespiration.

## 15.3 Stage 2 - Reduction

Reduction converts 3-PGA into carbohydrate intermediates.

This stage uses:

- ATP;
- NADPH.

For each CO2 fixed, reduction needs:

```text
2 ATP + 2 NADPH
```

The energy and reducing power come from the light reaction.

## 15.4 Stage 3 - Regeneration

RuBP must be regenerated for the cycle to continue.

If RuBP is not regenerated, CO2 fixation stops.

Regeneration uses ATP.

For each CO2 fixed, regeneration needs:

```text
1 ATP
```

## 15.5 Energy Requirement Per CO2

For each CO2 entering the Calvin cycle:

| Stage | ATP Used | NADPH Used |
|---|---:|---:|
| Reduction | 2 | 2 |
| Regeneration | 1 | 0 |
| Total | 3 | 2 |

Therefore:

```text
1 CO2 fixed -> 3 ATP + 2 NADPH required
```

## 15.6 Energy Requirement For One Glucose

One glucose contains 6 carbon atoms.

Therefore 6 molecules of CO2 must be fixed.

The Calvin cycle must turn 6 times for one glucose.

For one glucose:

```text
6 CO2 -> 18 ATP + 12 NADPH required
```

Summary:

| Product Goal | CO2 Needed | ATP Needed | NADPH Needed |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| One CO2 fixed | 1 | 3 | 2 |
| One glucose | 6 | 18 | 12 |

## 15.7 Calvin Cycle Inputs And Outputs

Input:

- 6 CO2;
- 18 ATP;
- 12 NADPH.

Output:

- one glucose or carbohydrate equivalent;
- 18 ADP;
- 12 NADP+.

Simple view:

```text
6CO2 + 18ATP + 12NADPH -> carbohydrate + 18ADP + 12NADP+
```

The real pathway has many intermediates, but this summary helps track energy.

## 16. C3 Pathway

The C3 pathway is named because the first stable product of CO2 fixation is a 3-carbon compound.

That compound is 3-PGA.

In C3 plants:

- initial CO2 fixation occurs in mesophyll cells;
- Calvin cycle occurs in mesophyll cells;
- RuBisCO is present in mesophyll cells;
- photorespiration can be significant.

Examples include:

- wheat;
- rice;
- beans;
- many temperate plants.

## 17. C4 Pathway

C4 plants are often adapted to dry tropical environments.

The first stable product of CO2 fixation is a 4-carbon compound.

That compound is usually OAA.

C4 plants still use the Calvin cycle.

The Calvin cycle remains the main carbohydrate-forming pathway.

The special feature is that C4 plants first concentrate CO2 near RuBisCO.

## 17.1 Special Features Of C4 Plants

C4 plants commonly show:

- special leaf anatomy;
- high tolerance to temperature;
- strong response to high light intensity;
- little photorespiration;
- high productivity;
- efficient CO2 concentration near RuBisCO.

## 17.2 Kranz Anatomy

C4 leaves have Kranz anatomy.

Kranz means wreath-like arrangement.

In such leaves:

- vascular bundles are surrounded by large bundle sheath cells;
- bundle sheath cells have many chloroplasts;
- bundle sheath cell walls are thick and less permeable to gases;
- intercellular spaces are reduced;
- mesophyll cells surround the bundle sheath region.

This arrangement creates two functional compartments:

| Cell Type | Main Function |
|---|---|
| Mesophyll cell | Initial CO2 fixation by PEPcase |
| Bundle sheath cell | Calvin cycle using RuBisCO |

## 17.3 Hatch-Slack Pathway

The C4 pathway is also called the Hatch-Slack pathway.

It is a cyclic process.

It begins in mesophyll cells and continues in bundle sheath cells.

## 17.4 Step 1 - CO2 Fixation In Mesophyll

The primary CO2 acceptor in C4 plants is PEP.

PEP is a 3-carbon molecule.

The enzyme is PEP carboxylase, or PEPcase.

Reaction idea:

```text
PEP + CO2 -> OAA
```

OAA has 4 carbons.

Important point:

```text
mesophyll cells of C4 plants lack RuBisCO
```

They use PEPcase for initial fixation.

## 17.5 Step 2 - Formation Of C4 Acids

OAA may be converted into other 4-carbon acids such as:

- malic acid;
- aspartic acid.

These acids move from mesophyll cells to bundle sheath cells.

## 17.6 Step 3 - CO2 Release In Bundle Sheath Cells

Inside bundle sheath cells, the 4-carbon acids are broken down.

This releases:

- CO2;
- a 3-carbon compound.

The 3-carbon compound returns to mesophyll cells.

It is converted back into PEP.

This completes the C4 cycle.

## 17.7 Step 4 - Calvin Cycle In Bundle Sheath Cells

The released CO2 enters the Calvin cycle.

Bundle sheath cells contain abundant RuBisCO.

They lack PEPcase.

Therefore:

```text
PEPcase works in mesophyll;
RuBisCO works in bundle sheath.
```

## 17.8 Why C4 Pathway Helps

The C4 pathway raises CO2 concentration around RuBisCO.

When CO2 concentration is high, RuBisCO acts mainly as carboxylase.

This reduces oxygenase activity.

Therefore photorespiration becomes negligible.

This is why many C4 plants are productive in high light and high temperature conditions.

## 17.9 Examples Of C4 Plants

Common C4 examples:

- maize;
- sugarcane;
- sorghum;
- some grasses.

## 18. Photorespiration

Photorespiration is a light-associated process in which RuBisCO reacts with oxygen instead of carbon dioxide.

It is important mainly in C3 plants.

## 18.1 RuBisCO Has Two Activities

RuBisCO can act as:

- carboxylase;
- oxygenase.

Carboxylase activity:

```text
RuBP + CO2 -> 2 x 3-PGA
```

Oxygenase activity:

```text
RuBP + O2 -> 3-PGA + phosphoglycolate
```

## 18.2 What Controls RuBisCO Behaviour

RuBisCO binding is competitive.

The outcome depends on relative concentrations of:

- CO2;
- O2.

If CO2 is high near RuBisCO:

```text
carboxylation is favoured
```

If O2 competes strongly and CO2 is relatively low:

```text
oxygenation increases
```

## 18.3 Why Photorespiration Is Wasteful

Photorespiration is considered wasteful because:

- no sugar is formed;
- no ATP is formed;
- no NADPH is formed;
- ATP is used;
- CO2 is released;
- carbon fixation efficiency decreases.

The raw chapter notes that its biological function is not known in that treatment.

## 18.4 Photorespiration In C3 Plants

In C3 plants:

- RuBisCO is in mesophyll cells;
- CO2 concentration may fall when stomata close;
- O2 can bind to RuBisCO;
- photorespiration may become high.

This reduces photosynthetic efficiency.

## 18.5 Why C4 Plants Avoid Photorespiration

In C4 plants:

- CO2 is first fixed in mesophyll cells by PEPcase;
- C4 acids carry carbon to bundle sheath cells;
- CO2 is released in bundle sheath cells;
- RuBisCO is exposed to high CO2 concentration;
- oxygenase activity is minimised.

Therefore photorespiration is negligible.

## 19. C3 And C4 Plants Compared

| Characteristic | C3 Plants | C4 Plants |
|---|---|---|
| First stable product | 3-PGA | OAA |
| Carbon atoms in first product | 3 | 4 |
| Primary CO2 acceptor | RuBP | PEP |
| Carbon atoms in acceptor | 5 | 3 |
| First carboxylating enzyme | RuBisCO | PEPcase |
| Initial fixation site | Mesophyll cell | Mesophyll cell |
| Calvin cycle site | Mesophyll cell | Bundle sheath cell |
| Cell types involved in CO2 fixation | One main type, mesophyll | Two types, mesophyll and bundle sheath |
| Kranz anatomy | Absent | Present |
| RuBisCO location | Mesophyll cell | Bundle sheath cell |
| PEPcase presence | Absent | Present in mesophyll |
| Photorespiration | High or significant | Negligible |
| High light response | Lower than C4 | High |
| Temperature optimum | Usually 20-25 degree C | Usually 30-40 degree C |
| Productivity in hot bright conditions | Lower | Higher |
| Examples | Wheat, rice, beans | Maize, sorghum, sugarcane |

## 20. Anatomy Of C3 And C4 Leaves

## 20.1 C3 Leaf Anatomy

In C3 plants:

- mesophyll cells perform the Calvin cycle;
- bundle sheath cells are not specialised for CO2 concentration;
- Kranz anatomy is absent;
- intercellular spaces are common;
- RuBisCO is present in mesophyll cells.

## 20.2 C4 Leaf Anatomy

In C4 plants:

- large bundle sheath cells surround vascular bundles;
- bundle sheath cells contain many chloroplasts;
- thick walls restrict gas exchange;
- mesophyll cells fix CO2 first;
- bundle sheath cells run Calvin cycle;
- Kranz anatomy is present.

## 20.3 Quick Visual Logic

Remember:

```text
C3 = one main photosynthetic cell type
C4 = two cooperating photosynthetic cell types
```

For C4:

```text
mesophyll captures CO2 first;
bundle sheath uses CO2 in Calvin cycle.
```

## 21. Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

The rate of photosynthesis affects plant growth and crop yield.

Photosynthetic rate is influenced by both internal and external factors.

## 21.1 Internal Factors

Internal plant factors include:

- number of leaves;
- size of leaves;
- age of leaves;
- orientation of leaves;
- number of mesophyll cells;
- number of chloroplasts;
- internal CO2 concentration;
- amount of chlorophyll;
- genetic constitution;
- growth stage of the plant.

Internal factors decide how well a plant can use available external conditions.

## 21.2 External Factors

External factors include:

- light;
- temperature;
- CO2 concentration;
- water availability.

These factors act together.

At any moment, one factor may become limiting.

## 22. Blackman's Law Of Limiting Factors

Blackman's law states that when a process is affected by more than one factor, its rate is controlled by the factor nearest to its minimum value.

In simple words:

```text
photosynthesis runs only as fast as the most limiting required factor allows
```

Example:

If light and CO2 are optimal but temperature is very low, photosynthesis remains low.

Increasing light further will not help much.

Increasing temperature to a suitable level will increase the rate.

## 22.1 How To Apply The Law

Ask:

1. Which factors are needed?
2. Which one is currently least available relative to need?
3. If that factor increases, will the rate increase?
4. If not, another factor has become limiting.

This explains why photosynthesis graphs often rise at first and then level off.

## 23. Light As A Factor

Light affects photosynthesis by:

- quality;
- intensity;
- duration.

Light quality means wavelength or colour.

Light intensity means amount of light.

Light duration means time of exposure.

## 23.1 Low Light Intensity

At low light intensity, photosynthetic rate increases almost linearly with light.

Reason:

```text
light is limiting
```

More light excites more electrons and increases ATP and NADPH formation.

## 23.2 High Light Intensity

At high light intensity, the rate stops increasing.

Reason:

```text
another factor becomes limiting
```

That factor may be:

- CO2 concentration;
- temperature;
- enzyme capacity;
- water status.

## 23.3 Light Saturation

Light saturation occurs when further increase in light does not increase photosynthesis.

The raw chapter notes that light saturation may occur at about 10 percent of full sunlight.

Therefore, in nature, light is rarely limiting except in places such as:

- shade;
- dense forests;
- lower leaf layers in a canopy.

## 23.4 Excess Light

Very high light can damage photosynthetic pigments.

It can cause breakdown of chlorophyll.

Then photosynthetic rate may decrease.

Accessory pigments help protect chlorophyll a from photo-oxidation.

## 24. Carbon Dioxide As A Factor

CO2 is a major limiting factor for photosynthesis.

Atmospheric CO2 concentration is low, about 0.03 to 0.04 percent in the raw textbook treatment.

Increasing CO2 up to about 0.05 percent can increase CO2 fixation.

Higher levels over long periods may become damaging.

## 24.1 C3 And C4 Response To CO2

At low light:

- C3 plants do not respond strongly to high CO2;
- C4 plants also do not respond strongly;
- light is limiting.

At high light:

- both C3 and C4 plants can increase photosynthesis when CO2 rises;
- C3 plants show a stronger response to extra CO2 because present CO2 can be limiting for them.

## 24.2 CO2 Saturation

C4 plants reach CO2 saturation at a lower CO2 concentration, around 360 uL L-1.

C3 plants may continue responding until beyond 450 uL L-1.

This shows:

```text
current CO2 availability is often more limiting for C3 plants
```

## 24.3 Greenhouse Enrichment

Some greenhouse crops are grown in CO2-enriched air.

Examples:

- tomatoes;
- bell pepper.

Higher CO2 can increase photosynthesis and yield when other conditions are suitable.

## 25. Temperature As A Factor

Temperature mainly affects enzyme-controlled reactions.

The carbon fixation reactions are strongly enzymatic.

Therefore temperature affects them significantly.

Light reactions are also temperature-sensitive, but generally less so.

## 25.1 C3 And C4 Temperature Response

C4 plants often show higher photosynthetic rates at higher temperatures.

C3 plants usually have lower temperature optima.

Typical values:

| Plant Type | Temperature Optimum |
|---|---|
| C3 plants | About 20-25 degree C |
| C4 plants | About 30-40 degree C |

The exact optimum depends on habitat.

Tropical plants usually have higher optima than temperate plants.

## 26. Water As A Factor

Water is a reactant in photosynthesis.

However, water usually affects photosynthesis more through plant condition than through direct shortage as a reactant.

Water stress causes:

- stomatal closure;
- reduced CO2 entry;
- wilting;
- reduced leaf surface exposure;
- reduced metabolic activity.

When stomata close, internal CO2 concentration falls.

Then photosynthesis decreases.

## 27. Light Reaction To Calvin Cycle Link

The light reaction and Calvin cycle are tightly connected.

Light reaction provides:

- ATP;
- NADPH.

Calvin cycle uses:

- ATP;
- NADPH;
- CO2.

Calvin cycle returns:

- ADP;
- NADP+.

These return to the light reaction.

Simplified cycle:

```text
light reaction -> ATP + NADPH
Calvin cycle -> ADP + NADP+
ADP + NADP+ return to light reaction
```

This makes photosynthesis an integrated process, not two isolated reactions.

## 28. Matter And Energy Tracking

## 28.1 Carbon Tracking

Carbon enters as:

```text
CO2
```

Carbon becomes:

- 3-PGA in C3 pathway;
- OAA first in C4 pathway;
- sugar after Calvin cycle.

## 28.2 Oxygen Tracking

Oxygen released as O2 comes from:

```text
H2O
```

Not from CO2.

## 28.3 Electron Tracking

Electrons begin from:

```text
water
```

They move through:

```text
PS II -> electron transport chain -> PS I
```

They finally reduce:

```text
NADP+ -> NADPH
```

## 28.4 Proton Tracking

Protons accumulate in:

```text
thylakoid lumen
```

They move back to:

```text
stroma
```

through ATP synthase.

This movement forms ATP.

## 29. Common Misconceptions

## 29.1 Misconception: Oxygen Comes From CO2

Correct idea:

```text
oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from water
```

CO2 supplies carbon for carbohydrate.

## 29.2 Misconception: Dark Reaction Happens Only In Dark

Correct idea:

The carbon reaction is not directly light-driven, but it depends on ATP and NADPH produced in light.

It usually operates in illuminated leaves.

## 29.3 Misconception: PS I Acts Before PS II

Correct idea:

In non-cyclic electron flow, PS II acts before PS I.

The names reflect discovery order, not functional order.

## 29.4 Misconception: Accessory Pigments Are Unimportant

Correct idea:

Accessory pigments broaden light absorption and protect chlorophyll a.

## 29.5 Misconception: C4 Plants Do Not Use Calvin Cycle

Correct idea:

C4 plants do use Calvin cycle.

They run it mainly in bundle sheath cells.

## 29.6 Misconception: Photorespiration Is A Type Of Useful Respiration

Correct idea:

Photorespiration does not produce ATP.

It uses ATP and releases CO2.

In this chapter, it is treated as a wasteful process.

## 30. Solved Concept Checks

## 30.1 Why Are Green Plants Called Autotrophs?

Green plants are called autotrophs because they synthesise their own organic food from inorganic substances.

They use CO2, water, and light energy to form carbohydrates.

## 30.2 Why Is Photosynthesis Called The Basis Of Life?

Photosynthesis is the basis of life because it provides food and oxygen.

Food made by plants supports food chains.

Oxygen released by plants supports aerobic respiration.

## 30.3 What Did The Variegated Leaf Experiment Prove?

It proved that chlorophyll-containing green parts form starch in light.

Therefore chlorophyll is required for photosynthesis.

## 30.4 What Did The KOH Experiment Prove?

KOH absorbs CO2.

The leaf part enclosed with KOH does not form starch.

Therefore CO2 is required for photosynthesis.

## 30.5 What Did Priestley Show?

Priestley showed that plants can restore air made unsuitable by burning candles or breathing animals.

Modern explanation: plants release oxygen during photosynthesis.

## 30.6 What Did Ingenhousz Add To Priestley's Finding?

Ingenhousz showed that sunlight and green plant parts are necessary for oxygen release.

## 30.7 Why Was Engelmann's Experiment Important?

It showed that blue and red light are most effective in photosynthesis.

It linked oxygen production with specific wavelengths.

## 30.8 What Did Van Niel Conclude?

Van Niel concluded that oxygen evolved by green plants comes from water.

He used bacterial photosynthesis to infer the role of hydrogen donors.

## 30.9 Why Is Chlorophyll A The Chief Pigment?

Chlorophyll a is the chief pigment because it forms reaction centres and directly donates excited electrons.

Other pigments mainly collect light and transfer energy to it.

## 30.10 Why Do Plants Need Accessory Pigments?

Plants need accessory pigments because they:

- absorb additional wavelengths;
- transfer energy to chlorophyll a;
- protect chlorophyll a from photo-oxidation.

## 30.11 Why Does Non-Cyclic Flow Produce Oxygen?

Non-cyclic flow involves PS II.

PS II replaces lost electrons by splitting water.

Water splitting releases oxygen.

## 30.12 Why Does Cyclic Flow Not Produce NADPH?

In cyclic flow, excited electrons from PS I return to PS I.

They do not pass to NADP+.

Therefore NADPH is not produced.

## 30.13 Why Is The Proton Gradient In Chloroplasts Directed Toward Lumen?

Water splitting releases protons into the lumen.

Electron transport also moves protons into the lumen.

NADP+ reduction removes protons from the stroma.

Together these events make the lumen proton-rich.

## 30.14 Why Is ATP Formed On The Stroma Side?

CF1 of ATP synthase faces the stroma.

Protons move from lumen to stroma through CF0.

CF1 uses this energy to form ATP on the stroma side.

## 30.15 Why Does Calvin Cycle Need Cyclic Photophosphorylation?

For each CO2 fixed, Calvin cycle needs 3 ATP and 2 NADPH.

Extra ATP may be supplied by cyclic photophosphorylation.

## 30.16 Why Is RuBP A Surprising CO2 Acceptor?

Because the first stable product is 3-PGA, scientists initially expected a 2-carbon acceptor.

Experiments showed the acceptor is actually 5-carbon RuBP.

CO2 plus RuBP forms an unstable 6-carbon compound that splits into two 3-carbon molecules.

## 30.17 Why Are Six Turns Needed For One Glucose?

One glucose has 6 carbon atoms.

Each Calvin cycle turn fixes one CO2.

Therefore six turns fix six carbons.

## 30.18 Why Are C4 Plants Highly Productive?

C4 plants concentrate CO2 near RuBisCO.

This reduces photorespiration.

They also perform well under high light and high temperature.

As a result, carbon fixation is efficient.

## 30.19 Why Does RuBisCO Carry Out More Carboxylation In C4 Plants?

In C4 plants, C4 acids release CO2 in bundle sheath cells.

This raises CO2 concentration around RuBisCO.

High CO2 favours carboxylase activity over oxygenase activity.

## 30.20 Can A Plant Lacking Chlorophyll A Photosynthesise Efficiently?

No.

Chlorophyll a is essential as the reaction centre pigment.

A plant with chlorophyll b but no chlorophyll a cannot run normal light reactions efficiently.

## 30.21 Why Do Leaves Kept In Darkness Become Yellow?

In darkness, chlorophyll synthesis decreases and chlorophyll breaks down.

Yellow carotenoids are more stable and become more visible.

Therefore leaves appear yellow or pale green.

## 30.22 Why Are Shade Leaves Often Darker Green?

Shade leaves often have more chlorophyll to capture limited light.

This makes them darker green than leaves exposed to strong sunlight.

## 31. Graph Interpretation: Light And Photosynthesis

In the light intensity graph:

- early rising region means light is limiting;
- later plateau means light is no longer limiting;
- another factor controls the rate.

## 31.1 Point A Or Early Region

At point A or in the early rising part:

```text
light intensity is limiting
```

Increasing light increases photosynthetic rate.

## 31.2 Region Near Saturation

At the saturation point:

```text
light is enough for maximum rate under current conditions
```

Beyond this, increasing light gives little or no increase.

## 31.3 Plateau Region

In the plateau region:

```text
another factor is limiting
```

Possible factors:

- CO2 concentration;
- temperature;
- enzyme availability;
- water status.

## 31.4 Very High Light

At very high light, photosynthesis can decline because chlorophyll may be damaged.

## 33. Exam-Oriented Answers

## 33.1 Can You Identify C3 And C4 Plants Externally?

No, external appearance alone is not reliable.

C3 and C4 plants are best identified by internal anatomy and physiology.

The presence of Kranz anatomy strongly indicates a C4 plant.

## 33.2 Which Internal Structure Helps Identify C4 Plants?

Kranz anatomy helps identify C4 plants.

The key feature is large bundle sheath cells around vascular bundles.

These cells contain many chloroplasts and are surrounded by mesophyll cells.

## 33.3 Why Are C4 Plants Productive Despite Fewer Calvin Cycle Cells?

Only bundle sheath cells perform the Calvin cycle in typical C4 plants.

However, these cells receive concentrated CO2 from mesophyll cells.

High CO2 reduces photorespiration.

The Calvin cycle therefore works efficiently.

## 33.4 Why Does RuBisCO Work More As Carboxylase In C4 Plants?

CO2 released from C4 acids accumulates in bundle sheath cells.

This creates high CO2 concentration near RuBisCO.

High CO2 reduces competition from O2.

Thus RuBisCO favours carboxylation.

## 33.5 Why Is Chlorophyll A Essential?

Chlorophyll a forms the reaction centre of photosystems.

It donates excited electrons.

Accessory pigments cannot replace this role completely.

## 33.6 Why Are Accessory Pigments Present?

Accessory pigments increase photosynthetic efficiency.

They absorb light wavelengths that chlorophyll a absorbs poorly.

They pass energy to chlorophyll a.

They also protect chlorophyll a from light damage.

## 33.7 Why Do Dark-Kept Leaves Become Pale?

Darkness prevents normal chlorophyll maintenance.

Chlorophyll breaks down.

Carotenoids remain more stable, so yellow colour becomes visible.

## 33.8 Why Are Shaded Leaves Darker?

Shade leaves often develop more chlorophyll per unit area.

This helps capture weak light.

Therefore they look darker green.

## 33.9 What Do C And D Represent On A Light Curve?

On the common light intensity curve:

- C often represents the light saturation region or near-maximum rate;
- D represents the plateau where light no longer increases photosynthesis.

At D, another factor limits the rate.

## 36. Practice Answers

## 36.1 Photosynthesis Definition

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants use light energy to synthesise carbohydrates from carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen.

## 36.2 Oxygen Origin

The oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from water.

Water is split near PS II.

The reaction releases electrons, protons, and oxygen.

## 36.3 Chief Pigment

Chlorophyll a is the chief pigment.

It forms the reaction centre of photosystems and directly donates excited electrons.

## 36.4 Accessory Pigments

Accessory pigments absorb extra wavelengths and transfer energy to chlorophyll a.

They also protect chlorophyll a from photo-oxidation.

## 36.5 Calvin Cycle Energy Need

For one CO2:

```text
3 ATP + 2 NADPH
```

For one glucose:

```text
18 ATP + 12 NADPH
```

## 36.6 C4 Advantage

C4 plants concentrate CO2 in bundle sheath cells.

This favours RuBisCO carboxylation and reduces photorespiration.

That is why C4 plants often have higher productivity in hot, bright conditions.

## 36.7 Limiting Factor

A limiting factor is the factor present at the lowest effective level relative to need.

It controls the rate of photosynthesis even when other factors are sufficient.
