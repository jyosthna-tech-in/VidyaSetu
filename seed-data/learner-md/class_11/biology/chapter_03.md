## How To Study This Chapter

This chapter is best understood as an evolutionary ladder of plant organisation.

Start with algae, which are simple, thalloid, mostly aquatic, and mostly autotrophic. Then move to bryophytes, which live on land but still need water for fertilisation. Pteridophytes are more advanced because they have vascular tissue and a dominant sporophyte. Gymnosperms and angiosperms are seed-bearing plants, but gymnosperms have naked seeds while angiosperms have seeds enclosed in fruits.

The chapter has two layers:

1. Classification of plant groups.
2. Alternation of generations and plant life cycles.

Both layers are important for exams. Many questions ask not only "what is this group?" but also "which phase is dominant?", "where does meiosis occur?", and "what is the ploidy of this structure?"

## 1. Plant Kingdom In Context

In the previous chapter, living organisms were arranged into five kingdoms: Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia. This chapter focuses on Kingdom Plantae.

Modern Plantae does not include all organisms that have cell walls. Earlier systems often placed fungi, bacteria, and cyanobacteria with plants because they had walls or seemed plant-like. Modern classification excludes them because their cell structure, nutrition, and evolutionary relationships are different.

For this chapter, the main groups under Plantae are:

1. Algae
2. Bryophytes
3. Pteridophytes
4. Gymnosperms
5. Angiosperms

## 2. Changing Ideas In Plant Classification

Plant classification has changed as scientists began using more reliable characters.

## 2.1 Artificial Classification Systems

Artificial systems use only a few easily visible characters, such as:

- habit;
- colour;
- number of leaves;
- shape of leaves;
- structure of androecium.

Linnaeus used an artificial system based strongly on floral characters.

The weakness of artificial systems is that they may separate closely related species or group unrelated species together. They also give equal weight to characters that may not be equally important.

## 2.2 Natural Classification Systems

Natural systems use a wider range of characters and try to reflect natural affinities.

They consider:

- external morphology;
- internal anatomy;
- embryology;
- ultrastructure;
- phytochemistry.

Bentham and Hooker gave an important natural classification system for flowering plants.

## 2.3 Phylogenetic Classification Systems

Phylogenetic systems classify organisms according to evolutionary relationships.

The main assumption is that organisms placed in the same taxon share a common ancestor.

Modern classification favours phylogenetic systems because they try to show how organisms are related through evolution, not merely how similar they look.

## 2.4 Numerical Taxonomy

Numerical taxonomy uses observable characters and computer-based analysis.

Each character is assigned a number or code. The data are processed, and many characters can be considered at once.

Key point: each character is given equal importance in numerical taxonomy.

## 2.5 Cytotaxonomy

Cytotaxonomy uses cytological information, such as:

- chromosome number;
- chromosome structure;
- chromosome behaviour.

This helps resolve classification problems when external characters are insufficient.

## 2.6 Chemotaxonomy

Chemotaxonomy uses chemical constituents of plants to resolve classification problems.

For example, the presence or absence of certain chemicals can help compare plant groups or species.

## 4. Algae

Algae are chlorophyll-bearing, simple, thalloid, autotrophic organisms. Most are aquatic, occurring in freshwater or marine environments.

## 4.1 General Features Of Algae

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Body | Simple and thalloid |
| Chlorophyll | Present |
| Nutrition | Autotrophic |
| Habitat | Mostly aquatic; also moist stones, soil, wood, and associations |
| Organisation | Unicellular, colonial, filamentous, or large marine forms |
| Vascular tissue | Absent |
| True root, stem, leaves | Absent |

Algae may also occur in association with fungi as lichens. Some may occur on animals.

## 4.2 Forms And Size Of Algae

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| Colonial | Volvox |
| Filamentous | Ulothrix, Spirogyra |
| Large marine body | Kelps |

Some marine algae can form massive plant bodies.

## 4.3 Reproduction In Algae

Algae reproduce by vegetative, asexual, and sexual methods.

## 4.4 Vegetative Reproduction

Vegetative reproduction usually occurs by fragmentation.

In fragmentation, the thallus breaks into pieces. Each fragment develops into a new thallus.

## 4.5 Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction occurs through spores. The most common spores are zoospores.

Zoospores are motile because they have flagella. On germination, they give rise to new plants.

## 4.6 Sexual Reproduction

Sexual reproduction occurs through fusion of gametes.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Isogamy | Fusion of similar gametes | Ulothrix, Spirogyra |
| Anisogamy | Fusion of gametes dissimilar in size | Eudorina |
| Oogamy | Fusion of large non-motile female gamete and small motile male gamete | Volvox, Fucus |

## 4.7 Economic Importance Of Algae

Algae are important ecologically and economically.

| Importance | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Carbon dioxide fixation | A major portion of total photosynthetic carbon fixation on Earth is carried out by algae |
| Oxygen release | Algae increase dissolved oxygen in water |
| Primary production | They form the base of many aquatic food chains |
| Food | Porphyra, Laminaria, and Sargassum are eaten in some regions |
| Hydrocolloids | Brown algae yield algin; red algae yield carrageen |
| Agar | Gelidium and Gracilaria yield agar |
| Microbial culture | Agar is used to grow microbes |
| Food products | Agar is used in ice creams and jellies |
| Food supplement | Chlorella is protein-rich and used as a supplement |

## 5. Main Classes Of Algae

The three main classes of algae in this chapter are:

1. Chlorophyceae
2. Phaeophyceae
3. Rhodophyceae

## 5.1 Comparison Of Algal Classes

| Character | Chlorophyceae | Phaeophyceae | Rhodophyceae |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common name | Green algae | Brown algae | Red algae |
| Major pigments | Chlorophyll a and b | Chlorophyll a and c, carotenoids, xanthophylls, fucoxanthin | Chlorophyll a and d, phycoerythrin |

## 6. Chlorophyceae

Chlorophyceae are commonly called green algae.

## 6.1 Main Features Of Chlorophyceae

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Colour | Grass green |
| Pigments | Chlorophyll a and b |
| Body | Unicellular, colonial, or filamentous |
| Chloroplasts | Definite and varied in shape |
| Stored food | Starch, often associated with pyrenoids |
| Cell wall | Inner cellulose layer and outer pectose layer |
| Vegetative reproduction | Fragmentation |
| Asexual reproduction | Flagellated zoospores in zoosporangia |
| Sexual reproduction | Isogamous, anisogamous, or oogamous |

## 6.2 Chloroplast Shapes In Green Algae

Chloroplasts may be:

- discoid;
- plate-like;
- reticulate;
- cup-shaped;
- spiral;
- ribbon-shaped.

## 6.3 Pyrenoids

Pyrenoids are storage bodies located in chloroplasts.

They contain protein besides starch. Some green algae may also store food as oil droplets.

## 6.4 Examples Of Chlorophyceae

Examples include:

- Chlamydomonas;
- Volvox;
- Ulothrix;
- Spirogyra;
- Chara.

## 7. Phaeophyceae

Phaeophyceae are commonly called brown algae. They are found primarily in marine habitats.

## 7.1 Main Features Of Phaeophyceae

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Common name | Brown algae |
| Habitat | Mostly marine |
| Size range | Simple filamentous forms to giant kelps |
| Pigments | Chlorophyll a, chlorophyll c, carotenoids, xanthophylls |
| Brown colour | Due to fucoxanthin |
| Stored food | Laminarin and mannitol |
| Cell wall | Cellulose with gelatinous algin coating |
| Body parts in many forms | Holdfast, stipe, frond |
| Asexual reproduction | Biflagellate zoospores |
| Sexual reproduction | Isogamous, anisogamous, or oogamous |

## 7.2 Holdfast, Stipe, And Frond

Many brown algae show body differentiation into:

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Holdfast | Attaches body to substratum |
| Stipe | Stalk-like part |
| Frond | Leaf-like photosynthetic part |

These are not true roots, stems, or leaves, but they show functional differentiation.

## 7.3 Zoospores And Gametes In Brown Algae

In many brown algae, zoospores are pear-shaped and have two unequal laterally attached flagella.

Gametes are also often pear-shaped and bear two laterally attached flagella.

## 7.4 Examples Of Phaeophyceae

Examples include:

- Ectocarpus;
- Dictyota;
- Laminaria;
- Sargassum;
- Fucus.

## 8. Rhodophyceae

Rhodophyceae are commonly called red algae.

## 8.1 Main Features Of Rhodophyceae

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Common name | Red algae |
| Red pigment | r-phycoerythrin |
| Habitat | Mostly marine, especially warmer areas |
| Light range | Found near surface and at greater depths |
| Body | Mostly multicellular thalli |
| Stored food | Floridean starch |
| Vegetative reproduction | Fragmentation |
| Asexual reproduction | Non-motile spores |
| Sexual reproduction | Non-motile gametes; oogamous |
| Post-fertilisation | Complex developments |

## 8.2 Floridean Starch

Floridean starch is the stored food of red algae.

It is structurally similar to amylopectin and glycogen.

## 8.3 Examples Of Rhodophyceae

Examples include:

- Polysiphonia;
- Porphyra;
- Gracilaria;
- Gelidium.

## 9. Bryophytes

Bryophytes include mosses and liverworts.

They are called amphibians of the plant kingdom because they can live on land but require water for sexual reproduction.

## 9.1 General Features Of Bryophytes

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Habitat | Damp, humid, shaded places |
| Vascular tissue | Absent |
| True roots, stems, leaves | Absent |
| Attachment | Rhizoids |
| Main plant body | Haploid gametophyte |
| Sex organs | Multicellular |
| Male sex organ | Antheridium |
| Female sex organ | Archegonium |
| Male gametes | Biflagellate antherozoids |
| Water need | Required for fertilisation |
| Sporophyte | Attached to gametophyte and dependent on it |

## 9.2 Body Organisation In Bryophytes

The bryophyte body is more differentiated than that of algae, but it still lacks true roots, stems, and leaves.

It may have:

- root-like structures;
- stem-like structures;
- leaf-like structures.

These are not true organs in the vascular plant sense.

## 9.3 Reproduction In Bryophytes

The main bryophyte plant is the gametophyte.

The gametophyte produces gametes:

- antheridia produce antherozoids;
- archegonia produce eggs.

Water carries the antherozoids to the archegonium. Fertilisation produces a zygote.

The zygote develops into a multicellular sporophyte. The sporophyte remains attached to the gametophyte and obtains nourishment from it.

Some sporophyte cells undergo meiosis to produce haploid spores. Spores germinate to form new gametophytes.

## 9.4 Economic And Ecological Importance Of Bryophytes

| Importance | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Food | Some mosses provide food for herbaceous mammals, birds, and other animals |
| Peat | Sphagnum forms peat used as fuel |
| Packing material | Sphagnum can hold water and has been used for packing living material |
| Plant succession | Mosses and lichens help colonise bare rocks |
| Soil formation | They help break down rocks and prepare substratum for higher plants |
| Soil protection | Dense moss mats reduce rain impact and help prevent soil erosion |

## 10. Liverworts

Liverworts are bryophytes that usually grow in moist, shady habitats.

## 10.1 Habitat Of Liverworts

Liverworts may grow on:

- stream banks;
- marshy ground;
- damp soil;
- bark of trees;
- deep wooded areas.

## 10.2 Body Of Liverworts

The plant body of a typical liverwort such as Marchantia is thalloid.

The thallus is dorsiventral, meaning it has distinct upper and lower surfaces.

It remains closely attached to the substratum.

Leafy liverworts have tiny leaf-like appendages arranged in two rows on stem-like structures.

## 10.3 Asexual Reproduction In Liverworts

Asexual reproduction occurs by:

- fragmentation of thalli;
- formation of gemmae.

Gemmae are green multicellular asexual buds. They develop in small receptacles called gemma cups.

When gemmae detach from the parent body, they germinate and form new individuals.

## 10.4 Sexual Reproduction In Liverworts

Male and female sex organs may be produced on the same thallus or on different thalli.

The sporophyte is differentiated into:

- foot;
- seta;
- capsule.

Meiosis occurs in the capsule and produces spores. Spores germinate into free-living gametophytes.

## 11. Mosses

Mosses are bryophytes with a more distinct gametophyte structure than many liverworts.

## 11.1 Two Stages Of The Moss Gametophyte

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| Protonema stage | Creeping, green, branched, often filamentous stage that develops from a spore |
| Leafy stage | Upright slender axes with spirally arranged leaves; develops from a bud on secondary protonema |

## 11.2 Leafy Stage Of Mosses

The leafy stage has:

- upright slender axes;
- spirally arranged leaves;
- multicellular branched rhizoids;
- sex organs at the apex of leafy shoots.

## 11.3 Reproduction In Mosses

Vegetative reproduction occurs by fragmentation and budding in the secondary protonema.

Sexual reproduction involves antheridia and archegonia produced at the apex of leafy shoots.

After fertilisation, the zygote develops into a sporophyte.

## 11.4 Moss Sporophyte

The sporophyte consists of:

- foot;
- seta;
- capsule.

The moss sporophyte is more elaborate than the liverwort sporophyte.

The capsule contains spores. Spores are formed after meiosis. Mosses also have an elaborate mechanism for spore dispersal.

## 11.5 Examples Of Mosses

Examples include:

- Funaria;
- Polytrichum;
- Sphagnum.

## 12. Liverworts Versus Mosses

| Feature | Liverworts | Mosses |
|---|---|---|
| Common body form | Thalloid in forms like Marchantia | Leafy gametophyte common |
| Body orientation | Often dorsiventral and attached to substratum | Upright leafy shoots common |
| Asexual structures | Gemmae in gemma cups common in Marchantia | Fragmentation and budding in protonema |
| Gametophyte stages | Thalloid or leafy | Protonema stage and leafy stage |
| Sporophyte | Foot, seta, capsule | Foot, seta, capsule, more elaborate |
| Examples | Marchantia | Funaria, Polytrichum, Sphagnum |

## 13. Pteridophytes

Pteridophytes include horsetails and ferns.

They are evolutionarily important because they are the first terrestrial plants to possess vascular tissues: xylem and phloem.

## 13.1 General Features Of Pteridophytes

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Habitat | Cool, damp, shady places; some in sandy soil |
| Vascular tissue | Present |
| Dominant phase | Sporophyte |
| Main plant body | Differentiated into true root, stem, and leaves |
| Leaves | Small microphylls or large macrophylls |
| Reproduction | Spores formed in sporangia |
| Gametophyte | Small, multicellular, free-living, often photosynthetic |
| Water need | Needed for fertilisation |

## 13.2 Importance Of Pteridophytes

Pteridophytes are useful:

- for medicinal purposes;
- as soil binders;
- as ornamental plants;
- as an evolutionary step toward seed plants.

## 13.3 Leaves In Pteridophytes

| Leaf Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Microphyll | Small leaf | Selaginella |
| Macrophyll | Large leaf | Ferns |

## 13.4 Sporangia, Sporophylls, And Strobili

The sporophyte bears sporangia. These sporangia are often associated with leaf-like structures called sporophylls.

In some pteridophytes, sporophylls form compact structures called strobili or cones.

Examples with strobili:

- Selaginella;
- Equisetum.

## 13.5 Pteridophyte Gametophyte

Spores are produced by meiosis in spore mother cells.

The spores germinate into small, multicellular, free-living, mostly photosynthetic gametophytes.

In many ferns, the gametophyte is called prothallus.

The prothallus bears:

- antheridia;
- archegonia.

Water is required for transfer of antherozoids to the archegonium.

## 13.6 Dominant Phase In Pteridophytes

The dominant phase is the sporophyte.

After fertilisation, the zygote develops into a young sporophyte. This sporophyte becomes the main plant body.

## 13.7 Homospory And Heterospory

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Homospory | Production of one kind of spore | Many pteridophytes |
| Heterospory | Production of two kinds of spores, microspores and megaspores | Selaginella, Salvinia |

## 13.8 Significance Of Heterospory

In heterosporous plants:

- microspores give rise to male gametophytes;
- megaspores give rise to female gametophytes;
- female gametophytes may be retained on the parent sporophyte;
- young embryos can develop inside the female gametophyte.

This condition is considered a precursor to the seed habit. It represents an important step in plant evolution.

## 13.9 Classes Of Pteridophytes

| Class | Example |
|---|---|
| Psilopsida | Psilotum |
| Lycopsida | Selaginella, Lycopodium |
| Sphenopsida | Equisetum |
| Pteropsida | Dryopteris, Pteris, Adiantum |

## 14. Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are seed plants in which ovules are not enclosed by an ovary wall.

The term gymnosperm means naked seed.

## 14.1 General Features Of Gymnosperms

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Ovules | Exposed, not enclosed in ovary |
| Seeds | Naked, not covered by fruit |
| Plant body | Root, stem, leaves |
| Size | Medium-sized trees, tall trees, or shrubs |
| Vascular tissue | Present |
| Dominant phase | Sporophyte |
| Spore type | Heterosporous |
| Gametophytes | Highly reduced and dependent |
| Reproductive structures | Cones or strobili |

## 14.2 Examples Of Gymnosperms

Examples include:

- Cycas;
- Pinus;
- Cedrus;
- Ginkgo;
- Sequoia.

Sequoia, the giant redwood, is one of the tallest tree species.

## 14.3 Roots In Gymnosperms

Gymnosperm roots are generally tap roots.

Special root associations:

| Plant | Association |
|---|---|
| Pinus | Mycorrhiza, a fungal association |
| Cycas | Coralloid roots with N2-fixing cyanobacteria |

## 14.4 Stems And Leaves In Gymnosperms

Stems may be:

- unbranched, as in Cycas;
- branched, as in Pinus and Cedrus.

Leaves may be simple or compound.

In conifers, needle-like leaves reduce surface area. Thick cuticle and sunken stomata reduce water loss. These features help gymnosperms withstand extremes of temperature, humidity, and wind.

## 14.5 Reproduction In Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are heterosporous. They produce:

- microspores;
- megaspores.

The spores are formed in sporangia borne on sporophylls. Sporophylls are arranged into cones or strobili.

## 14.6 Male Cone

Male cones are also called microsporangiate strobili.

They bear microsporophylls and microsporangia.

Microspores develop into highly reduced male gametophytes called pollen grains.

## 14.7 Female Cone

Female cones are also called macrosporangiate strobili.

They bear megasporophylls with ovules.

The ovule contains nucellus protected by envelopes. The megaspore mother cell undergoes meiosis to form megaspores. One megaspore develops into the female gametophyte.

The female gametophyte bears archegonia.

## 14.8 Same Tree Or Different Trees

| Plant | Arrangement |
|---|---|
| Pinus | Male and female cones may occur on the same tree |
| Cycas | Male cones and megasporophylls occur on different trees |

## 14.9 Pollination And Fertilisation In Gymnosperms

Pollen grains are released from microsporangia and carried by air currents.

They reach the ovule. A pollen tube grows toward the archegonia and carries male gametes.

After fertilisation:

- zygote develops into embryo;
- ovule develops into seed;
- seed remains uncovered.

## 14.10 Economic Importance Of Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are useful in many ways.

| Use | Example Or Explanation |
|---|---|
| Timber | Conifers such as Pinus and Cedrus provide wood |
| Paper industry | Softwood from conifers is useful for pulp |
| Resins | Some gymnosperms yield resins |
| Turpentine | Obtained from resinous gymnosperms such as Pinus |
| Ornamentals | Cycas and conifers are grown for ornamental value |
| Ecological value | Forest-forming trees support habitats and soil stability |

## 15. Angiosperms

Angiosperms are flowering plants.

Unlike gymnosperms, their ovules are enclosed. After fertilisation, seeds are enclosed in fruits.

## 15.1 General Features Of Angiosperms

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Reproductive structure | Flower |
| Ovules | Enclosed in ovary |
| Seeds | Enclosed in fruits |
| Size range | From tiny Wolffia to tall Eucalyptus |
| Habitat | Very wide range |
| Vascular tissue | Present |
| Dominant phase | Sporophyte |
| Gametophytes | Highly reduced |

## 15.2 Importance Of Angiosperms

Angiosperms provide:

- food;
- fodder;
- fuel;
- medicines;
- fibres;
- timber;
- oils;
- spices;
- many commercial products.

## 15.3 Dicotyledons And Monocotyledons

Angiosperms are divided into two main classes:

1. Dicotyledons
2. Monocotyledons

## 15.4 Dicotyledons Versus Monocotyledons

| Feature | Dicotyledons | Monocotyledons |
|---|---|---|
| Cotyledons | Two | One |
| Leaf venation | Usually reticulate | Usually parallel |
| Root system | Usually tap root | Usually fibrous root |
| Floral parts | Often in fours or fives | Often in threes |
| Vascular bundles in stem | Usually arranged in a ring | Usually scattered |
| Examples | Pea, mustard, mango | Wheat, maize, rice |

## 15.5 Double Fertilisation In Angiosperms

The raw chapter exercise asks about the primary endosperm nucleus, so this concept must be clear.

In angiosperms, the pollen grain reaches the stigma and produces a pollen tube. The pollen tube carries two male gametes into the embryo sac.

Two fusion events occur:

| Fusion | Product |
|---|---|
| One male gamete plus egg | Zygote, which is diploid |
| Other male gamete plus two polar nuclei | Primary endosperm nucleus, which is triploid |

This is called double fertilisation.

The primary endosperm nucleus later gives rise to endosperm, which nourishes the developing embryo.

## 15.6 After Fertilisation In Angiosperms

After fertilisation:

- zygote develops into embryo;
- primary endosperm nucleus develops into endosperm;
- ovule develops into seed;
- ovary develops into fruit.

## 16. Alternation Of Generations

Plants show alternation between two phases:

| Phase | Ploidy | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Gametophyte | Haploid, n | Produces gametes |
| Sporophyte | Diploid, 2n | Produces spores by meiosis |

The relative dominance of these phases differs across plant groups.

## 16.1 Important Rule For Ploidy

Use this rule:

- gametes are haploid;
- spores are haploid;
- gametophyte is haploid;
- zygote is diploid;
- sporophyte is diploid;
- endosperm in angiosperms is usually triploid.

## 16.2 Haplontic Life Cycle

In a haplontic life cycle, the dominant plant body is haploid.

The zygote is the only diploid stage. It undergoes meiosis to restore the haploid phase.

Examples include many green algae such as Chlamydomonas, Volvox, and Spirogyra.

## 16.3 Diplontic Life Cycle

In a diplontic life cycle, the dominant plant body is diploid.

Gametes are the only haploid stage.

Gymnosperms and angiosperms show a diplontic pattern with highly reduced gametophytes.

Some algae, such as Fucus, also show a diplontic life cycle.

## 16.4 Haplo-Diplontic Life Cycle

In a haplo-diplontic life cycle, both gametophyte and sporophyte are multicellular.

Bryophytes and pteridophytes show this pattern, but dominance differs:

| Group | Dominant Phase |
|---|---|
| Bryophytes | Gametophyte dominant |
| Pteridophytes | Sporophyte dominant |

Some algae also show haplo-diplontic life cycles.

## 17. Trend In Plant Evolution

The broad trend from algae to angiosperms is:

| Trend | Direction |
|---|---|
| Body organisation | Thalloid to organ-level differentiation |
| Vascular tissue | Absent in algae and bryophytes, present from pteridophytes onward |
| Dominant phase | Gametophyte dominance in bryophytes to sporophyte dominance in vascular plants |
| Water for fertilisation | Strong dependence in bryophytes and pteridophytes, reduced in seed plants |
| Seed habit | Absent in algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes; present in gymnosperms and angiosperms |
| Gametophyte | Free-living in lower groups, reduced and dependent in seed plants |

## 20. Exercise Answers

## 20.1 What Is The Basis Of Classification Of Algae?

Algae are classified mainly on the basis of:

- major photosynthetic pigments;
- nature of stored food;
- cell wall composition;
- number and position of flagella;
- habitat;
- reproductive features.

The three main classes are Chlorophyceae, Phaeophyceae, and Rhodophyceae.

| Class | Pigments | Stored Food | Main Habitat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chlorophyceae | Chlorophyll a and b | Starch | Freshwater, brackish water, marine water |
| Phaeophyceae | Chlorophyll a and c, fucoxanthin | Laminarin and mannitol | Mostly marine |
| Rhodophyceae | Chlorophyll a and d, phycoerythrin | Floridean starch | Mostly marine |

## 20.2 When And Where Does Reduction Division Take Place?

Reduction division means meiosis. It reduces chromosome number from diploid to haploid.

| Plant Group | Where Meiosis Occurs | When It Occurs |
|---|---|---|
| Liverwort | Capsule of sporophyte | During spore formation |
| Moss | Capsule of sporophyte | During spore formation |
| Fern | Sporangia of sporophyte | During spore formation |
| Gymnosperm | Microsporangia and megasporangia | During microspore and megaspore formation |
| Angiosperm | Anther and ovule | During microspore and megaspore formation |

## 20.3 Name Three Groups Of Plants That Bear Archegonia

The three groups of plants that bear archegonia are:

1. Bryophytes
2. Pteridophytes
3. Gymnosperms

Angiosperms do not have archegonia.

## 20.4 Brief Life Cycle Of A Moss

The main plant body of a moss is the haploid gametophyte.

The moss gametophyte has two stages:

1. Protonema stage
2. Leafy stage

The leafy gametophyte bears antheridia and archegonia. Antheridia produce motile antherozoids. Archegonia produce eggs.

Water is required for the male gamete to reach the egg. Fertilisation forms a diploid zygote.

The zygote develops into a sporophyte consisting of foot, seta, and capsule. The sporophyte remains attached to the gametophyte.

Meiosis occurs in the capsule and produces haploid spores. Spores germinate to form protonema, and the cycle continues.

## 20.5 Mention The Ploidy Of The Given Structures

| Structure | Ploidy | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Protonemal cell of a moss | n | It is part of the moss gametophyte |
| Primary endosperm nucleus in dicot | 3n | Formed by fusion of one male gamete with two polar nuclei |
| Leaf cell of a moss | n | Moss leafy plant is gametophytic |
| Prothallus cell of a fern | n | Prothallus is the fern gametophyte |
| Gemma cell in Marchantia | n | Gemma belongs to gametophyte |
| Meristem cell of monocot | 2n | It is part of sporophytic plant body |
| Ovum of a liverwort | n | Gamete is haploid |
| Zygote of a fern | 2n | Formed by fusion of male gamete and egg |

## 20.6 Write A Note On Economic Importance Of Algae And Gymnosperms

## 20.6.1 Economic Importance Of Algae

Algae are important because:

- they fix carbon dioxide by photosynthesis;
- they increase dissolved oxygen in aquatic habitats;
- they form the base of aquatic food chains;
- Porphyra, Laminaria, and Sargassum are used as food;
- brown algae provide algin;
- red algae provide carrageen;
- Gelidium and Gracilaria provide agar;
- agar is used for microbial culture and food preparations;
- Chlorella is used as a protein-rich food supplement.

## 20.6.2 Economic Importance Of Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are important because:

- conifers provide timber;
- softwood is used in paper and pulp industries;
- some gymnosperms yield resins;
- turpentine can be obtained from resinous forms such as Pinus;
- Cycas and conifers are grown as ornamentals;
- gymnosperm forests support ecosystems and help soil stability.

## 20.7 Why Are Gymnosperms And Angiosperms Classified Separately Even Though Both Bear Seeds?

Gymnosperms and angiosperms both produce seeds, but they differ in how ovules and seeds are protected.

| Feature | Gymnosperms | Angiosperms |
|---|---|---|
| Ovules | Exposed | Enclosed in ovary |
| Seeds | Naked | Enclosed in fruit |
| Flowers | Absent | Present |
| Fruits | Absent | Present |
| Reproductive structures | Cones | Flowers |
| Double fertilisation | Not a typical feature | Present |

Therefore, they are classified separately.

## 20.8 What Is Heterospory? Give Its Significance And Examples

Heterospory is the production of two kinds of spores:

- microspores;
- megaspores.

Microspores give rise to male gametophytes. Megaspores give rise to female gametophytes.

Significance:

- it separates male and female gametophyte development;
- the female gametophyte may remain retained on the parent sporophyte;
- the embryo can develop within the female gametophyte;
- it is considered a precursor to seed habit.

Examples:

- Selaginella;
- Salvinia.

## 20.9 Explain Protonema

Protonema is the first stage in the gametophyte of a moss.

It develops directly from a spore. It is creeping, green, branched, and often filamentous.

The leafy stage of the moss develops from buds on the protonema.

## 20.10 Explain Antheridium

Antheridium is the male sex organ in bryophytes and pteridophytes.

It produces male gametes called antherozoids.

In bryophytes and pteridophytes, antherozoids are motile and need water to reach the egg.

## 20.11 Explain Archegonium

Archegonium is the flask-shaped female sex organ found in bryophytes, pteridophytes, and gymnosperms.

It contains the egg. After fertilisation, the zygote develops inside or near the archegonium.

## 20.12 Explain Diplontic

Diplontic refers to a life cycle in which the diploid sporophyte is the dominant plant body.

The gametophyte is highly reduced, and gametes are the main haploid structures.

Gymnosperms and angiosperms show a diplontic pattern.

## 20.13 Explain Sporophyll

A sporophyll is a leaf-like structure that bears sporangia.

In pteridophytes and gymnosperms, sporophylls may be arranged into cone-like structures called strobili.

## 20.14 Explain Isogamy

Isogamy is sexual reproduction involving fusion of gametes that are similar in size and shape.

Examples include Ulothrix and Spirogyra.

## 20.15 Differentiate Between Red Algae And Brown Algae

| Feature | Red Algae | Brown Algae |
|---|---|---|
| Class | Rhodophyceae | Phaeophyceae |
| Main pigment | r-phycoerythrin | Fucoxanthin |

| Flagella | Absent | Present in zoospores and gametes |
| Habitat | Mostly marine, often warm seas | Mostly marine |
| Examples | Porphyra, Polysiphonia, Gelidium | Laminaria, Sargassum, Fucus |

| Sporophyte | Foot, seta, capsule | Foot, seta, capsule, more elaborate |

| Feature | Homosporous Pteridophytes | Heterosporous Pteridophytes |

## 20.19 Describe Important Characteristics Of Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are seed-bearing plants with naked ovules and naked seeds.

Important characteristics:

- ovules are not enclosed by ovary wall;
- seeds are not enclosed in fruits;
- plant body is differentiated into root, stem, and leaves;
- roots are generally tap roots;
- Pinus shows mycorrhizal roots;
- Cycas has coralloid roots associated with N2-fixing cyanobacteria;
- stems may be branched or unbranched;
- leaves may be simple or compound;
- conifers have needle-like leaves, thick cuticle, and sunken stomata;
- gymnosperms are heterosporous;
- they produce microspores and megaspores;
- reproductive structures are cones or strobili;
- male gametophyte is pollen grain;
- female gametophyte is retained within the ovule;
- fertilisation produces a zygote;
- ovule develops into naked seed.

## 21. Exam-Oriented Short Answers

## 21.1 Why Are Bryophytes Called Amphibians Of The Plant Kingdom?

Bryophytes can live on land, but they need water for sexual reproduction. Their motile male gametes swim through water to reach the egg. Hence they are called amphibians of the plant kingdom.

## 21.2 Why Are Pteridophytes More Advanced Than Bryophytes?

Pteridophytes have true roots, stems, leaves, and vascular tissues. Their dominant plant body is an independent sporophyte. Bryophytes lack true vascular tissue and have a dominant gametophyte with a dependent sporophyte.

## 21.3 Why Is Water Needed For Fertilisation In Bryophytes And Pteridophytes?

Their male gametes are motile antherozoids. Water is required for these gametes to move from antheridia to archegonia.

## 21.4 Why Is Heterospory Important In Evolution?

Heterospory produces separate microspores and megaspores. Megaspores can give rise to female gametophytes retained on the parent sporophyte. This retention and embryo development within the female gametophyte are important steps toward seed habit.

## 21.5 Why Are Gymnosperm Seeds Called Naked?

Gymnosperm ovules are not enclosed inside an ovary. After fertilisation, the seeds remain exposed and are not covered by fruit. Therefore, the seeds are called naked.

## 21.6 Why Are Angiosperms So Successful?

Angiosperms have flowers for reproduction and fruits for seed protection and dispersal. They occupy a wide range of habitats and provide many useful products such as food, fodder, fuel, and medicines.

## 21.7 What Is The Difference Between A Spore And A Gamete?

| Feature | Spore | Gamete |
|---|---|---|
| Function | Germinates to form gametophyte or new individual | Fuses with another gamete |
| Fusion needed | No fusion needed for germination | Fusion required for zygote formation |
| Ploidy | Usually haploid in plant life cycles | Haploid |
| Formed by | Often meiosis in sporophyte | Gametophyte |

## 21.8 What Is The Difference Between Gametophyte And Sporophyte?

| Feature | Gametophyte | Sporophyte |
|---|---|---|
| Ploidy | Haploid | Diploid |
| Produces | Gametes | Spores |
| Production process | Gametes by mitosis | Spores by meiosis |
| Dominant in | Bryophytes | Pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms |
