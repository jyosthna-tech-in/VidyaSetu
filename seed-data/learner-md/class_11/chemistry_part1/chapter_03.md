# Chapter 3: Classification of Elements and Periodicity in Properties

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

This chapter explains why the periodic table is not just a chart of element names. It is a system that arranges elements so that chemical behaviour, atomic structure, valence, reactivity, and physical properties can be understood as patterns.

The central idea is:

```text
Properties of elements repeat periodically because valence shell electronic configurations repeat periodically.
```

The chapter joins three major themes:

1. Historical development of the periodic table.
2. Modern periodic law based on atomic number.
3. Periodic trends explained through electronic configuration.

## 5. Why Elements Need Classification

If every element were studied separately, chemistry would become a long list of facts.

Classification is needed because:

1. Many elements exist.
2. Elements form a very large number of compounds.
3. Similar elements show similar reactions.
4. A good arrangement helps predict properties of unknown or less familiar elements.
5. It reduces memory load by converting separate facts into patterns.

In 1800, only about 31 elements were known. By 1865, 63 elements were known. Today, elements up to atomic number 118 have official names.

The more elements were discovered, the more necessary a systematic table became.

## 6. The Meaning of Periodicity

Periodicity means regular repetition.

In chemistry, periodicity means:

```text
Similar properties appear again and again when elements are arranged in a suitable order.
```

For example:

1. Lithium, sodium, and potassium are soft reactive metals.
2. Fluorine, chlorine, bromine, and iodine form a family of reactive non-metals.
3. Helium, neon, and argon are very unreactive gases.

These repetitions are not accidental. They come from similar valence shell electronic configurations.

## 7. Dobereiner's Triads

Johann Dobereiner observed that certain groups of three elements had similar properties.

Such groups were called triads.

In a triad:

1. Three elements had similar chemical behaviour.
2. The atomic weight of the middle element was approximately the average of the other two.
3. The properties of the middle element were intermediate.

Example:

| Triad | Approximate idea |
|---|---|
| Li, Na, K | Sodium lies between lithium and potassium |
| Ca, Sr, Ba | Strontium lies between calcium and barium |
| Cl, Br, I | Bromine lies between chlorine and iodine |

Limitation:

Dobereiner's rule worked only for a few groups, so it could not classify all known elements.

## 8. de Chancourtois Arrangement

A.E.B. de Chancourtois arranged elements in increasing atomic weight on a cylindrical form.

The idea showed that properties recur periodically, but it did not become widely accepted because:

1. It was not easy for chemists to use.
2. It did not clearly connect many chemical properties.
3. It did not become a practical table for regular study.

## 9. Newlands' Law of Octaves

John Newlands arranged elements in increasing order of atomic weight.

He observed:

```text
Every eighth element had properties similar to the first.
```

This was compared with octaves in music.

Why it mattered:

It was one of the first clear statements that element properties repeat.

Limitations:

1. It worked mainly up to calcium.
2. It failed for heavier elements.
3. It left little room for undiscovered elements.
4. It sometimes placed dissimilar elements together.

## 10. Lothar Meyer

Lothar Meyer arranged elements and studied periodic variation in physical properties such as atomic volume.

He showed that when atomic volume was plotted against atomic weight, a repeated pattern appeared.

His work supported the idea that properties vary periodically, but Mendeleev's work became more famous because Mendeleev used the table more powerfully for prediction.

## 11. Mendeleev's Periodic Law

Mendeleev arranged elements mainly in increasing order of atomic weight.

His periodic law was:

```text
The properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic weights.
```

Mendeleev placed elements with similar properties in the same vertical columns.

His table was powerful because it used:

1. Atomic weights.
2. Similarity in chemical properties.
3. Formulas of oxides and hydrides.
4. Valence patterns.

## 12. Mendeleev's Strengths

Mendeleev's table was important because he did more than arrange known elements.

He also:

1. Left gaps for undiscovered elements.
2. Predicted properties of undiscovered elements.
3. Corrected doubtful atomic weights in some cases.
4. Placed some elements according to properties instead of strictly following atomic mass.

Classic examples:

| Mendeleev name | Later element |
|---|---|
| Eka-aluminium | Gallium |
| Eka-silicon | Germanium |

Mendeleev predicted their approximate atomic weights, densities, and formulas of oxides and chlorides.

## 13. Why Mendeleev Sometimes Broke Atomic Weight Order

Mendeleev wanted similar elements to remain in the same group.

If strict atomic weight order separated chemically similar elements, he sometimes changed the order.

Example:

Iodine has a lower atomic weight than tellurium, but iodine was placed with fluorine, chlorine, and bromine because iodine behaves like a halogen.

This showed that chemical property was more important than blindly following atomic weight.

## 14. Limitations of Mendeleev's Table

Mendeleev's table had some weaknesses:

1. Atomic mass could not explain all positions.
2. Isotopes could not be placed properly because isotopes have different masses but same chemical properties.
3. Hydrogen had no completely fixed position.
4. Some pairs were not in increasing atomic mass order.
5. The reason for periodicity was not fully known because electronic structure was not yet understood.

These problems were later solved by using atomic number.

## 15. Moseley and Atomic Number

Henry Moseley studied characteristic X-ray spectra of elements.

He showed that atomic number is more fundamental than atomic mass.

The atomic number is equal to:

1. Number of protons in the nucleus.
2. Nuclear charge.
3. Number of electrons in a neutral atom.

This discovery gave the correct basis for periodic classification.

## 16. Modern Periodic Law

Modern periodic law states:

```text
The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.
```

This law is stronger than Mendeleev's law because atomic number directly connects with electronic configuration.

Atomic number controls:

1. Number of electrons in a neutral atom.
2. Electronic configuration.
3. Valence electrons.
4. Chemical properties.

## 17. Modern Periodic Table

The modern periodic table is the long form of the periodic table.

It contains:

1. 7 periods.
2. 18 groups.
3. s-block, p-block, d-block, and f-block.
4. Lanthanoids and actinoids placed separately at the bottom.

Groups are numbered from 1 to 18 according to IUPAC recommendation.

## 18. Periods

A period is a horizontal row.

The period number generally corresponds to the highest principal quantum number `n` of the valence shell.

| Period | Shell being filled mainly | Number of elements |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | n = 1 | 2 |
| 2 | n = 2 | 8 |
| 3 | n = 3 | 8 |
| 4 | n = 4 with 3d filling | 18 |
| 5 | n = 5 with 4d filling | 18 |
| 6 | n = 6 with 4f and 5d filling | 32 |
| 7 | n = 7 with 5f and 6d filling | 32 maximum |

## 19. Why Periods Have 2, 8, 18, and 32 Elements

The number of elements in a period depends on the number of orbitals being filled.

Each orbital can hold 2 electrons.

| Orbitals involved | Number of orbitals | Maximum electrons |
|---|---:|---:|
| s | 1 | 2 |
| s + p | 1 + 3 = 4 | 8 |
| s + d + p | 1 + 5 + 3 = 9 | 18 |
| s + f + d + p | 1 + 7 + 5 + 3 = 16 | 32 |

So a period can contain 2, 8, 18, or 32 elements depending on the subshells involved.

## 20. Groups

A group is a vertical column.

Elements in the same group usually have:

1. Same number of valence electrons.
2. Similar valence shell electronic configuration.
3. Similar chemical properties.
4. Similar formulas of many compounds.

Example:

Group 1 elements have outer configuration:

```text
ns1
```

So lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, caesium, and francium show similar chemical behaviour.

## 21. Electronic Configuration as the Basis of the Table

Electronic configuration explains the structure of the periodic table.

The last electron entering an atom tells us:

1. Which shell is being filled.
2. Which subshell is being filled.
3. Which block the element belongs to.
4. The likely valence pattern.
5. The likely chemical family.

Example:

```text
Na: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s1
```

The highest shell is `n = 3`, so sodium is in period 3.

The last electron enters s subshell, so sodium is an s-block element.

The valence configuration is `3s1`, so sodium belongs to group 1.

## 22. IUPAC Temporary Naming for Elements Above 100

Elements with very high atomic number are often produced in tiny amounts and may be highly unstable.

Before an official name is accepted, a temporary systematic name can be made from the atomic number.

Digit roots:

| Digit | Root | Symbol letter |
|---:|---|---|
| 0 | nil | n |
| 1 | un | u |
| 2 | bi | b |
| 3 | tri | t |
| 4 | quad | q |
| 5 | pent | p |
| 6 | hex | h |
| 7 | sept | s |
| 8 | oct | o |
| 9 | enn | e |

Rules:

1. Write the roots in the order of the digits.
2. Add `ium` at the end.
3. Make a three-letter symbol from root letters.
4. Capitalize only the first letter of the symbol.

Example:

Atomic number 120 has digits 1, 2, 0.

```text
1 -> un
2 -> bi
0 -> nil
```

Name:

```text
un + bi + nil + ium = unbinilium
```

Symbol:

```text
Ubn
```

## 24. Blocks of the Periodic Table

The periodic table is divided into blocks according to the subshell that receives the last electron.

| Block | Last electron enters | General area |
|---|---|---|
| s-block | s subshell | Groups 1 and 2 |
| p-block | p subshell | Groups 13 to 18 |
| d-block | d subshell | Groups 3 to 12 |
| f-block | f subshell | Lanthanoids and actinoids |

## 25. s-Block Elements

s-block elements include:

1. Group 1: alkali metals.
2. Group 2: alkaline earth metals.

General outer configurations:

| Group | General configuration |
|---|---|
| Group 1 | ns1 |
| Group 2 | ns2 |

Key features:

1. They are metals.
2. They have low ionization enthalpy.
3. They lose valence electrons easily.
4. Group 1 forms `M+` ions.
5. Group 2 forms `M2+` ions.
6. Reactivity generally increases down the group.
7. Except lithium and beryllium, their compounds are mainly ionic.

## 26. p-Block Elements

p-block elements belong to groups 13 to 18.

General outer configuration:

```text
ns2 np1 to ns2 np6
```

Important families:

| Group | Common family |
|---:|---|
| 13 | Boron family |
| 14 | Carbon family |
| 15 | Nitrogen family |
| 16 | Chalcogens |
| 17 | Halogens |
| 18 | Noble gases |

Key features:

1. They include metals, non-metals, and metalloids.
2. Non-metallic character increases from left to right.
3. Metallic character increases down a group.
4. Halogens have high tendency to gain one electron.
5. Noble gases have closed valence shell and very low reactivity.

## 27. d-Block Elements

d-block elements are groups 3 to 12.

They are also called transition elements.

General outer configuration:

```text
(n - 1)d1-10 ns0-2
```

Key features:

1. They are metals.
2. They often show variable oxidation states.
3. Many form coloured ions.
4. Many are paramagnetic.
5. Many are useful catalysts.
6. They form a bridge between reactive s-block metals and less reactive p-block elements.

Important note:

Zinc, cadmium, and mercury have `(n - 1)d10 ns2` configuration and do not show many typical transition element properties.

## 28. f-Block Elements

f-block elements are inner-transition elements.

They include:

1. Lanthanoids: cerium to lutetium.
2. Actinoids: thorium to lawrencium.

General outer configuration:

```text
(n - 2)f1-14 (n - 1)d0-1 ns2
```

Key features:

1. They are metals.
2. Their properties within a series are often similar.
3. Actinoids are radioactive.
4. Many actinoids are synthetic or present in very small quantities.
5. They are placed separately to keep the periodic table compact.

## 29. Special Position of Hydrogen

Hydrogen has configuration:

```text
1s1
```

It resembles group 1 because it has one electron and can form `H+`.

It resembles group 17 because it can gain one electron and form `H-`.

But hydrogen is not a typical alkali metal or a typical halogen.

Therefore, it is often placed separately.

## 30. Special Position of Helium

Helium has configuration:

```text
1s2
```

Strictly by subshell filling, helium belongs to s-block.

But it is placed in group 18 because:

1. Its valence shell is completely filled.
2. It behaves like a noble gas.
3. Its chemical reactivity is extremely low.

Chemical behaviour decides its practical position.

## 31. Metals, Non-Metals, and Metalloids

Elements can also be classified by broad physical and chemical character.

| Type | Main position | Common properties |
|---|---|---|
| Metals | Left and centre | Conductive, malleable, ductile, tend to lose electrons |
| Non-metals | Upper right | Poor conductors, often gain or share electrons |
| Metalloids | Border region | Intermediate properties |

Common metalloids include:

1. Boron.
2. Silicon.
3. Germanium.
4. Arsenic.
5. Antimony.
6. Tellurium.

## 32. Metallic Character Trend

Metallic character means tendency to lose electrons.

Trend:

```text
Across a period: metallic character decreases.
Down a group: metallic character increases.
```

Reason across a period:

1. Nuclear charge increases.
2. Atomic size decreases.
3. Valence electrons are held more strongly.
4. Loss of electrons becomes harder.

Reason down a group:

1. Atomic size increases.
2. Shielding increases.
3. Valence electron is less strongly held.
4. Loss of electron becomes easier.

## 33. Non-Metallic Character Trend

Non-metallic character means tendency to gain electrons or attract shared electrons.

Trend:

```text
Across a period: non-metallic character increases.
Down a group: non-metallic character decreases.
```

Fluorine is highly non-metallic because it is small and strongly attracts electrons.

## 34. Shielding and Effective Nuclear Charge

The nucleus attracts electrons, but inner electrons repel outer electrons.

Shielding means inner electrons reduce the attraction felt by valence electrons.

Effective nuclear charge is the net positive pull experienced by an outer electron.

Conceptually:

```text
Effective nuclear charge = nuclear pull - shielding effect
```

Across a period:

1. Nuclear charge increases.
2. Electrons are added to the same shell.
3. Shielding does not increase enough to cancel nuclear charge.
4. Effective nuclear charge increases.

Down a group:

1. New shells are added.
2. Shielding increases strongly.
3. Valence electrons are farther away.
4. Effective pull on outer electrons becomes weaker.

## 35. Atomic Radius

Atomic radius means approximate size of an atom.

Atoms do not have sharp boundaries, so atomic radius is estimated from distances between atoms.

Two useful forms:

| Radius type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Covalent radius | Half the distance between nuclei of two bonded identical non-metal atoms |
| Metallic radius | Half the distance between adjacent metal atom cores in metallic crystal |

Example:

If the distance between two chlorine atoms in `Cl2` is 198 pm, the covalent radius of chlorine is:

$$
\text{Radius of Cl} = \frac{198}{2} = 99 \text{ pm}
$$

## 36. Atomic Radius Trend Across a Period

Atomic radius generally decreases from left to right across a period.

Reason:

1. Electrons enter the same main shell.
2. Nuclear charge increases.
3. Shielding changes only slightly.
4. Effective nuclear charge increases.
5. Electron cloud is pulled closer to nucleus.

Example in period 3:

```text
Na > Mg > Al > Si > P > S > Cl
```

## 37. Atomic Radius Trend Down a Group

Atomic radius generally increases down a group.

Reason:

1. A new shell is added at each step.
2. Valence electrons are farther from the nucleus.
3. Inner shells shield the outer electrons.
4. Size increases.

Example in group 1:

```text
Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs
```

## 38. Noble Gas Radii Note

Noble gases are monoatomic.

Their radii are usually measured as van der Waals radii, not covalent radii.

Therefore, noble gas radii should not be directly compared with covalent radii of non-metals without care.

## 39. Ionic Radius

Ionic radius is the approximate size of an ion.

Formation of ions changes size:

1. Cation forms by loss of electron.
2. Anion forms by gain of electron.

General rule:

```text
Cation is smaller than parent atom.
Anion is larger than parent atom.
```

## 40. Why Cations Are Smaller

A cation is smaller than its parent atom because:

1. It has fewer electrons.
2. Electron-electron repulsion decreases.
3. Nuclear charge remains the same.
4. Remaining electrons are pulled closer.

Example:

```text
Na atom > Na+ ion
```

## 41. Why Anions Are Larger

An anion is larger than its parent atom because:

1. It has extra electrons.
2. Electron-electron repulsion increases.
3. Nuclear charge remains the same.
4. Effective attraction per electron decreases.

Example:

```text
F- ion > F atom
```

## 42. Isoelectronic Species

Isoelectronic species have the same number of electrons.

Examples with 10 electrons:

```text
N3-, O2-, F-, Ne, Na+, Mg2+, Al3+
```

For isoelectronic species:

```text
Higher nuclear charge means smaller radius.
```

Because the same number of electrons is pulled by a stronger nucleus.

## 43. Isoelectronic Radius Order

For:

```text
N3-, O2-, F-, Na+, Mg2+, Al3+
```

All have 10 electrons.

Nuclear charge increases as:

```text
N = 7, O = 8, F = 9, Na = 11, Mg = 12, Al = 13
```

Increasing ionic radius:

```text
Al3+ < Mg2+ < Na+ < F- < O2- < N3-
```

Largest species: `N3-`.

Smallest species: `Al3+`.

## 44. Ionization Enthalpy

Ionization enthalpy is the energy required to remove an electron from an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state.

General process:

$$
X(g) \rightarrow X^+(g) + e^-
$$

First ionization enthalpy removes the first electron.

Second ionization enthalpy removes the second electron:

$$
X^+(g) \rightarrow X^{2+}(g) + e^-
$$

Successive ionization enthalpies always increase:

```text
Delta_i H1 < Delta_i H2 < Delta_i H3
```

Reason:

After an electron is removed, the positive ion holds remaining electrons more strongly.

## 45. Meaning of Isolated Gaseous Atom

Ionization enthalpy and electron gain enthalpy are defined for isolated gaseous atoms so that comparisons are fair.

This avoids complications due to:

1. Intermolecular forces.
2. Bonding in molecules.
3. Crystal lattice forces.
4. Solvent interactions.

The atom must also be in ground state because excited atoms already have extra energy.

## 46. Ionization Enthalpy Trend Across a Period

Ionization enthalpy generally increases across a period.

Reason:

1. Atomic size decreases.
2. Effective nuclear charge increases.
3. Valence electrons are held more strongly.
4. More energy is required to remove an electron.

Example:

```text
Na < Mg < Al < Si < P < S < Cl < Ar
```

This trend has some important exceptions.

## 47. Ionization Enthalpy Trend Down a Group

Ionization enthalpy generally decreases down a group.

Reason:

1. Atomic size increases.
2. Shielding increases.
3. Valence electron is farther from nucleus.
4. Electron removal becomes easier.

Example:

```text
Li > Na > K > Rb > Cs
```

## 48. Exception: Beryllium and Boron

Expected trend from left to right suggests boron should have higher ionization enthalpy than beryllium.

Actual:

```text
Be > B
```

Reason:

```text
Be: 1s2 2s2
B:  1s2 2s2 2p1
```

The electron removed from boron is a 2p electron.

A 2p electron is:

1. Less penetrating than 2s.
2. More shielded.
3. Less tightly held.

So boron has lower first ionization enthalpy than beryllium.

## 49. Exception: Nitrogen and Oxygen

Expected trend suggests oxygen should have higher ionization enthalpy than nitrogen.

Actual:

```text
N > O
```

Configurations:

```text
N: 1s2 2s2 2p3
O: 1s2 2s2 2p4
```

Nitrogen has half-filled `2p3`, which is relatively stable.

Oxygen has one paired 2p orbital, causing electron-electron repulsion.

So it is easier to remove one electron from oxygen than from nitrogen.

## 50. Sodium and Magnesium: First and Second Ionization

Sodium:

```text
Na: [Ne] 3s1
```

Magnesium:

```text
Mg: [Ne] 3s2
```

First ionization:

```text
Na < Mg
```

Reason:

Sodium loses its single 3s electron easily.

Second ionization:

```text
Na > Mg
```

Reason:

After losing one electron, sodium becomes `[Ne]`. Removing the second electron means breaking a noble gas core, which requires very high energy.

For magnesium, the second electron is still a valence 3s electron.

## 51. Electron Gain Enthalpy

Electron gain enthalpy is the enthalpy change when an electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom.

General process:

$$
X(g) + e^- \rightarrow X^-(g)
$$

If energy is released, electron gain enthalpy is negative.

If energy is absorbed, electron gain enthalpy is positive.

Halogens usually have highly negative electron gain enthalpies because they need one electron to reach noble gas configuration.

## 52. Electron Gain Enthalpy Across a Period

Electron gain enthalpy generally becomes more negative from left to right across a period.

Reason:

1. Atomic size decreases.
2. Effective nuclear charge increases.
3. Added electron is attracted more strongly.
4. Energy release becomes larger.

This trend is less regular than ionization enthalpy.

## 53. Electron Gain Enthalpy Down a Group

Electron gain enthalpy generally becomes less negative down a group.

Reason:

1. Atomic size increases.
2. Added electron enters a shell farther from nucleus.
3. Nuclear attraction is weaker.
4. Less energy is released.

But oxygen and fluorine show important exceptions.

## 54. Exception: Oxygen and Sulfur

Oxygen is smaller than sulfur.

When an electron is added to oxygen, it enters compact 2p orbitals and faces strong electron-electron repulsion.

When an electron is added to sulfur, it enters larger 3p orbitals with less repulsion.

Therefore:

```text
Electron gain enthalpy of sulfur is more negative than oxygen.
```

## 55. Exception: Fluorine and Chlorine

Fluorine is very small.

An added electron enters compact 2p orbitals and experiences strong repulsion.

Chlorine is larger, so the added electron enters 3p orbitals with less repulsion.

Therefore:

```text
Chlorine has more negative electron gain enthalpy than fluorine.
```

This is a common exam point.

## 56. Second Electron Gain Enthalpy

Adding an electron to a neutral atom may release energy.

Adding another electron to an already negative ion requires energy because the incoming electron is repelled by the negative ion.

Example:

First electron addition:

$$
O(g) + e^- \rightarrow O^-(g)
$$

Second electron addition:

$$
O^-(g) + e^- \rightarrow O^{2-}(g)
$$

The second electron gain enthalpy of oxygen is positive.

## 57. Electron Gain Enthalpy and Electron Affinity

In some books, electron affinity is defined as the negative of electron gain enthalpy.

For this chapter, use the NCERT convention carefully:

```text
Electron gain enthalpy is an enthalpy change.
More negative value means more energy released.
```

Approximate relationship sometimes given:

$$
\Delta_{eg}H = -A_e - \frac{5}{2}RT
$$

For exam reasoning, the sign convention matters more than the formula.

## 58. Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom in a chemical compound to attract shared electrons toward itself.

Important points:

1. It is not directly measured like ionization enthalpy.
2. It depends on the atom and its bonding environment.
3. Pauling scale is commonly used.
4. Fluorine has the highest Pauling electronegativity value, about 4.0.

## 59. Electronegativity Trend

Trend:

```text
Across a period: electronegativity increases.
Down a group: electronegativity decreases.
```

Reason across a period:

1. Atomic size decreases.
2. Effective nuclear charge increases.
3. Bonding electrons are attracted more strongly.

Reason down a group:

1. Atomic size increases.
2. Shielding increases.
3. Bonding electrons are farther from nucleus.

## 60. Electron Gain Enthalpy vs Electronegativity

| Point | Electron gain enthalpy | Electronegativity |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Enthalpy change when isolated gaseous atom gains electron | Tendency of bonded atom to attract shared electrons |
| Applies to | Isolated gaseous atom | Atom in a compound |
| Nature | Thermodynamic quantity | Relative tendency |
| Units | kJ mol^-1 | Usually unitless scale |
| Sign used | Can be negative or positive | No sign |

## 61. Electronegativity Is Not Constant in All Compounds

A statement such as "nitrogen has electronegativity 3.0 in all compounds" is not fully correct.

Reason:

Electronegativity can vary with:

1. Oxidation state.
2. Hybridisation.
3. Bonding partner.
4. Molecular environment.

The Pauling value is a useful approximate guide, not an unchanging physical constant.

## 62. Valence

Valence is the combining capacity of an element.

For representative elements, valence often relates to valence electrons:

| Group | Valence electrons | Common valence |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 2 | 2 |
| 13 | 3 | 3 |
| 14 | 4 | 4 |
| 15 | 5 | 3 or 5 |
| 16 | 6 | 2 or 6 |
| 17 | 7 | 1 or 7 |
| 18 | 8 | 0 or 8 |

## 63. Oxidation State

Oxidation state is the charge assigned to an atom in a compound by assuming electrons belong more to the more electronegative atom.

Example in `Na2O`:

1. Oxygen is more electronegative.
2. Each sodium is assigned `+1`.
3. Oxygen is assigned `-2`.

Example in `OF2`:

1. Fluorine is more electronegative than oxygen.
2. Each fluorine is `-1`.
3. Oxygen is `+2`.

## 64. Predicting Formulas from Valence

To predict formulas:

1. Identify common valence or charge.
2. Balance total positive and negative valence.
3. Write simplest whole-number ratio.

Examples:

| Elements | Valence pattern | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Silicon and bromine | Si = 4, Br = 1 | SiBr4 |
| Aluminium and sulfur | Al = 3, S = 2 | Al2S3 |
| Lithium and oxygen | Li = 1, O = 2 | Li2O |
| Magnesium and nitrogen | Mg = 2, N = 3 | Mg3N2 |

## 65. Anomalous Behaviour of Second Period Elements

The first element of many groups behaves differently from later members.

Examples:

1. Lithium differs from other alkali metals.
2. Beryllium differs from other alkaline earth metals.
3. Boron, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and fluorine show special behaviour in their groups.

Reasons:

1. Very small atomic size.
2. High charge to radius ratio.
3. High electronegativity.
4. Absence of d orbitals in the valence shell.
5. Strong ability to form p-p multiple bonds.

## 66. Maximum Covalency of Second Period Elements

Second period elements have only 2s and 2p orbitals in the valence shell.

Available valence orbitals:

```text
2s, 2px, 2py, 2pz
```

Total orbitals: 4.

So maximum covalency is usually 4.

Example:

```text
B can form BF4-
```

But elements in period 3 and beyond may use more available valence orbitals in many bonding descriptions.

## 67. Diagonal Relationship

Some second period elements resemble the element diagonally below and to the right.

Examples:

1. Lithium resembles magnesium.
2. Beryllium resembles aluminium.

Reason:

The diagonal pair often has similar size and charge density.

## 68. Periodic Trends and Chemical Reactivity

Chemical reactivity depends strongly on:

1. Atomic radius.
2. Ionization enthalpy.
3. Electron gain enthalpy.
4. Electronegativity.
5. Valence shell configuration.

Across a period:

1. Group 1 metals are highly reactive because they lose electrons easily.
2. Middle elements are less reactive.
3. Group 17 non-metals are highly reactive because they gain electrons easily.
4. Noble gases are least reactive due to closed shells.

## 69. Reactivity of Group 1

Group 1 reactivity increases down the group.

```text
Li < Na < K < Rb < Cs
```

Reason:

1. Atomic size increases.
2. Ionization enthalpy decreases.
3. The outer electron is lost more easily.
4. Metallic character increases.

## 70. Reactivity of Group 17

Group 17 reactivity decreases down the group.

```text
F > Cl > Br > I
```

Reason:

1. Atomic size increases down the group.
2. Attraction for an incoming electron decreases.
3. Oxidizing power decreases.
4. Non-metallic character decreases.

## 71. Oxides Across a Period

Oxides show periodic trends.

Across a period:

1. Left-side metal oxides are basic.
2. Middle oxides may be amphoteric or neutral.
3. Right-side non-metal oxides are acidic.

Examples:

| Type | Example | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| Metal oxide | Na2O | Basic |
| Amphoteric oxide | Al2O3 | Acidic with bases, basic with acids |
| Neutral oxide | CO | Neither acidic nor basic |
| Non-metal oxide | Cl2O7 | Acidic |

## 72. Basic and Acidic Oxides with Water

Sodium oxide forms a base with water:

$$
Na_2O + H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH
$$

Chlorine heptoxide forms an acid with water:

$$
Cl_2O_7 + H_2O \rightarrow 2HClO_4
$$

This shows:

```text
Na2O is basic.
Cl2O7 is acidic.
```

## 76. Worked Example: Fifth Period Has 18 Elements

Question:

Why does the fifth period contain 18 elements?

Solution:

In the fifth period, filling involves:

```text
5s, 4d, 5p
```

Number of orbitals:

```text
5s = 1 orbital
4d = 5 orbitals
5p = 3 orbitals
```

Total orbitals:

```text
1 + 5 + 3 = 9
```

Each orbital holds 2 electrons.

Maximum electrons:

```text
9 x 2 = 18
```

So the fifth period has 18 elements.

## 77. Worked Example: Sixth Period Has 32 Elements

Question:

Justify using quantum numbers that the sixth period can have 32 elements.

Solution:

In the sixth period, the relevant filling pattern is:

```text
6s, 4f, 5d, 6p
```

Number of orbitals:

```text
6s = 1
4f = 7
5d = 5
6p = 3
```

Total orbitals:

```text
1 + 7 + 5 + 3 = 16
```

Maximum electrons:

```text
16 x 2 = 32
```

Therefore, the sixth period can contain 32 elements.

## 85. Worked Example: Ionization Enthalpy of Hydrogen

Given:

Energy of electron in ground state of hydrogen:

$$
E = -2.18 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J per atom}
$$

Energy needed to remove electron from one atom:

$$
2.18 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J}
$$

For one mole:

$$
2.18 \times 10^{-18} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23}
$$

$$
= 1.312 \times 10^6 \text{ J mol}^{-1}
$$

Convert to kJ:

$$
= 1312 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

Answer:

```text
Ionization enthalpy of hydrogen = 1312 kJ mol^-1
```

## 86. Worked Example: Aluminium Ionization Enthalpy

Given first ionization enthalpies:

```text
Na = 496 kJ mol^-1
Mg = 737 kJ mol^-1
Si = 786 kJ mol^-1
```

Question:

Will aluminium be closer to 575 or 760 kJ mol^-1?

Answer:

```text
Closer to 575 kJ mol^-1
```

Reason:

```text
Al: [Ne] 3s2 3p1
Mg: [Ne] 3s2
```

The electron removed from aluminium is a 3p electron, which is more shielded and less penetrating than a 3s electron. So aluminium has lower first ionization enthalpy than magnesium.

## 87. Worked Example: Be and B Ionization

Question:

Why is first ionization enthalpy of Be higher than B?

Answer:

```text
Be: 1s2 2s2
B:  1s2 2s2 2p1
```

The electron removed from Be is a 2s electron. The electron removed from B is a 2p electron.

The 2p electron is less penetrating and more shielded, so it is easier to remove.

Therefore:

```text
Be has higher first ionization enthalpy than B.
```

## 88. Worked Example: N and O Ionization

Question:

Why is first ionization enthalpy of oxygen lower than nitrogen?

Answer:

```text
N: 2p3
O: 2p4
```

Nitrogen has a half-filled p subshell, which is relatively stable.

Oxygen has one paired p orbital, causing extra electron-electron repulsion.

So oxygen loses one electron more easily than nitrogen.

## 89. Worked Example: Electron Gain Enthalpy of P, S, Cl, F

3. Phosphorus has half-filled `3p3`, so adding an electron is less favourable.

Answer:

```text
Most negative: Cl
Least negative: P
```

## 91. Worked Example: Oxidation State and Covalency of Aluminium

Question:

Are oxidation state and covalency of Al in `[AlCl(H2O)5]2+` the same?

Answer:

No.

Oxidation state:

Water is neutral and chloride is `-1`.

Let oxidation state of Al be `x`.

$$

$$

$$

$$

Covalency:

Al is bonded to 1 chloride and 5 water molecules.

```text
Covalency = 6
```

So oxidation state is `+3`, but covalency is `6`.

## 97. Exercise Support: Cations and Anions

Cations are smaller because electrons are removed.

Effects:

1. Repulsion among electrons decreases.
2. Same nuclear charge pulls fewer electrons.
3. Sometimes an entire shell is lost.

Anions are larger because electrons are added.

Effects:

1. Repulsion among electrons increases.
2. Same nuclear charge pulls more electrons.

## 107. Exam Answer: Need for Classification

Elements need classification because there are many elements and a vast number of compounds. Studying each element separately would be difficult. Classification groups elements with similar properties, makes study systematic, helps compare elements, and allows prediction of properties of unknown or less familiar elements.

## 108. Exam Answer: Mendeleev's Contribution

Mendeleev arranged elements mainly in increasing atomic weight and placed elements with similar chemical properties in the same groups. He left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties accurately. His table showed that properties of elements repeat periodically, although the modern basis was later changed from atomic weight to atomic number.

## 109. Exam Answer: Modern Periodic Law

Modern periodic law states that the physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers. This is because atomic number determines electronic configuration, and electronic configuration determines valence electrons and chemical behaviour.

## 110. Exam Answer: Atomic Radius Trend

Atomic radius decreases across a period because nuclear charge increases while electrons are added to the same shell. Shielding does not increase enough to cancel the stronger nuclear pull, so the electron cloud is pulled closer. Atomic radius increases down a group because new shells are added and shielding increases.

## 111. Exam Answer: Ionization Enthalpy Trend

Ionization enthalpy generally increases across a period due to increasing effective nuclear charge and decreasing atomic radius. It generally decreases down a group because atomic size and shielding increase, so the valence electron is less strongly held.

## 112. Exam Answer: Electron Gain Enthalpy Trend

Electron gain enthalpy generally becomes more negative across a period because effective nuclear charge increases and the added electron is more strongly attracted. It generally becomes less negative down a group because the added electron enters a shell farther from the nucleus. Exceptions occur for oxygen and fluorine due to strong repulsions in compact 2p orbitals.

## 113. Exam Answer: Electronegativity Trend

Electronegativity increases from left to right across a period because atomic size decreases and effective nuclear charge increases. It decreases down a group because atomic size and shielding increase, reducing attraction for shared electrons.

## 114. Exam Answer: Anomalous Second Period Elements

Second period elements often differ from heavier members of their groups because they are small, have high electronegativity, high charge density, no d orbitals in the valence shell, and strong ability to form p-p multiple bonds. Their maximum covalency is usually limited to 4.

## 122. End Summary

The periodic table developed from early attempts to organize elements into a modern arrangement based on atomic number. Mendeleev's table was historically important because it grouped similar elements and predicted undiscovered ones, but modern periodic law is based on atomic number because atomic number determines electronic configuration. The long form of the periodic table has periods, groups, and blocks. Elements in the same group show similar properties because of similar valence shell configurations. Periodic trends in atomic radius, ionic radius, ionization enthalpy, electron gain enthalpy, electronegativity, metallic character, and chemical reactivity can be explained by effective nuclear charge, shielding, atomic size, and valence shell structure.
