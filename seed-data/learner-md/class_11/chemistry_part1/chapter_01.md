## 9. Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.

Examples:

1. Water.
2. Air.
3. Book.
4. Pencil.
5. Soil.
6. Metal.
7. Living beings.

Chemistry begins with matter because all substances are forms of matter.

## 10. States of Matter

Matter commonly exists in three physical states:

1. Solid.
2. Liquid.
3. Gas.

| State | Shape | Volume | Particle arrangement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solid | Definite | Definite | Particles closely packed and orderly |
| Liquid | Not definite | Definite | Particles close but able to move |
| Gas | Not definite | Not definite | Particles far apart and move freely |

## 11. Interconversion of States

Matter can change state by changing temperature or pressure.

```text
solid -> liquid -> gas
gas -> liquid -> solid
```

Heating usually changes solid to liquid and liquid to gas.

Cooling usually changes gas to liquid and liquid to solid.

## 12. Classification of Matter

At the bulk level, matter can be classified as:

1. Pure substances.
2. Mixtures.

Pure substances are further classified as:

1. Elements.
2. Compounds.

Mixtures are further classified as:

1. Homogeneous mixtures.
2. Heterogeneous mixtures.

## 13. Pure Substances

A pure substance has a fixed composition and characteristic properties.

Examples:

1. Pure water.
2. Sodium chloride.
3. Oxygen.
4. Copper.

Pure substances are not separated into simpler substances by ordinary physical methods.

## 14. Elements

An element is a pure substance containing only one kind of atom.

Examples:

1. Hydrogen.
2. Oxygen.
3. Carbon.
4. Iron.
5. Copper.
6. Gold.

Elements may exist as atoms or molecules.

Example:

Oxygen gas exists as `O2` molecules.

## 15. Compounds

A compound is a pure substance formed when atoms of two or more elements combine in a fixed ratio.

Examples:

1. Water, `H2O`.
2. Carbon dioxide, `CO2`.
3. Ammonia, `NH3`.
4. Sodium chloride, `NaCl`.
5. Sugar.

Compounds can be decomposed into simpler substances by chemical methods, not by ordinary physical separation.

## 16. Mixtures

A mixture is a physical combination of two or more substances.

The components:

1. May be present in variable proportions.
2. Retain their individual properties.
3. Can often be separated by physical methods.

Examples:

1. Air.
2. Soil.
3. Salt solution.
4. Sugar solution.
5. Sand and salt.

## 17. Homogeneous and Heterogeneous Mixtures

| Basis | Homogeneous mixture | Heterogeneous mixture |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | Uniform throughout | Not uniform throughout |
| Appearance | Single phase | Two or more visible phases |
| Example | Salt solution | Sand and iron filings |
| Separation | Physical methods | Physical methods |

## 18. Element, Compound, and Mixture Compared

| Basis | Element | Compound | Mixture |
|---|---|---|---|
| Composition | One type of atom | Fixed ratio of elements | Variable ratio |
| Separation | Cannot be broken chemically into simpler elements | Can be decomposed chemically | Can often be separated physically |
| Properties | Characteristic of element | Different from constituent elements | Components retain properties |
| Example | Iron | Water | Air |

## 25. Mass and Weight

Mass is the amount of matter in a substance.

Weight is the force exerted by gravity on the substance.

| Basis | Mass | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Amount of matter | Gravitational force |
| SI unit | kilogram | newton |
| Changes with place | No | Yes |
| Measured by | Balance | Spring balance |

In chemistry laboratories, mass is often measured in grams because chemical samples are small.

## 26. Volume

Volume is the amount of space occupied by a substance.

SI unit of volume is cubic metre, `m^3`.

Common laboratory units:

1. `cm^3`.
2. `dm^3`.
3. `mL`.
4. `L`.

Important conversions:

```text
1 L = 1000 mL
1 L = 1 dm^3
1 mL = 1 cm^3
1 m^3 = 1000 L
```

## 27. Density

Density is mass per unit volume.

$$
density = mass / volume
$$

SI unit:

$$
kg m^{-3}
$$

Common chemistry unit:

$$
g cm^{-3}
$$

Higher density means particles are packed more closely.

## 28. Temperature Scales

Common temperature scales:

1. Celsius, written here as `deg C`.
2. Fahrenheit, written here as `deg F`.
3. Kelvin, `K`.

Kelvin is the SI unit of thermodynamic temperature.

Kelvin and Celsius relation:

$$
K = deg C + 273.15
$$

Fahrenheit and Celsius relation:

$$
deg F = (9/5)(deg C) + 32
$$

## 29. Scientific Notation

Scientific notation writes very large or very small numbers in the form:

$$
N x 10^n
$$

where `1 <= N < 10` and `n` is an integer.

Examples:

| Ordinary number | Scientific notation |
|---:|---:|
| 232.508 | `2.32508 x 10^2` |
| 0.00016 | `1.6 x 10^-4` |
| 602200000000000000000000 | `6.022 x 10^23` |
| 0.00000000000000000000000166 | `1.66 x 10^-24` |

## 30. Rules for Scientific Notation

If the decimal moves left, the exponent is positive.

If the decimal moves right, the exponent is negative.

Examples:

1. `4500 = 4.5 x 10^3`.
2. `0.0045 = 4.5 x 10^-3`.

## 31. Measurement Uncertainty

Every measurement has some uncertainty because:

1. Instruments have limits.
2. Observers may read scales differently.
3. Experimental conditions may vary.
4. Calibration may not be perfect.

Measurements should be reported with appropriate significant figures.

## 32. Significant Figures

Significant figures are meaningful digits in a measured value.

They include:

1. Digits known with certainty.
2. One estimated or uncertain digit.

Example:

If a reading is `11.2 mL`, then `11` is certain and `2` is uncertain in the last digit.

## 33. Rules for Significant Figures

1. All non-zero digits are significant.
2. Zeros before the first non-zero digit are not significant.
3. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
4. Zeros at the end of a number are significant if they are to the right of a decimal point.
5. Exact counting numbers have infinite significant figures.
6. In scientific notation, all digits in the coefficient are significant.

## 34. Significant Figures Examples

| Number | Significant figures | Reason |
|---:|---:|---|
| `285` | 3 | All non-zero digits count |
| `0.25` | 2 | Leading zero does not count |
| `0.0052` | 2 | Leading zeros do not count |
| `2.005` | 4 | Captive zeros count |
| `0.200` | 3 | Trailing decimal zeros count |
| `100` | 1, unless specified otherwise | Trailing zeros without decimal are ambiguous |
| `100.0` | 4 | Decimal shows zeros are significant |

## 35. Accuracy and Precision

Accuracy means closeness to the true value.

Precision means closeness of repeated measurements to one another.

Example:

If the true value is `2.00 g`:

| Measurements | Accuracy | Precision |
|---|---|---|
| `1.95 g`, `1.93 g` | Not accurate | Precise |
| `1.94 g`, `2.05 g` | Not accurate | Not precise |
| `2.01 g`, `1.99 g` | Accurate | Precise |

## 36. Significant Figures in Addition and Subtraction

For addition and subtraction, the result should have the same number of decimal places as the value with the fewest decimal places.

Example:

```text
12.11 + 18.0 + 1.012 = 31.122
```

Since `18.0` has only one decimal place, answer is:

```text
31.1
```

## 37. Significant Figures in Multiplication and Division

For multiplication and division, the result should have the same number of significant figures as the factor with the fewest significant figures.

Example:

```text
2.5 x 1.25 = 3.125
```

`2.5` has two significant figures, so answer is:

```text
3.1
```

## 38. Rounding Rules

1. If the digit removed is greater than 5, increase the previous digit by 1.
2. If the digit removed is less than 5, keep the previous digit unchanged.
3. If the digit removed is exactly 5, round to make the preceding digit even in the textbook rule.

Examples:

1. `1.386` to three significant figures becomes `1.39`.
2. `4.334` to three significant figures becomes `4.33`.
3. `6.35` to two significant figures becomes `6.4`.
4. `6.25` to two significant figures becomes `6.2`.

## 39. Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is also called:

1. Factor-label method.
2. Unit-factor method.

It converts units by multiplying by conversion factors equal to 1.

Example:

$$
1 \ inch = 2.54 \ cm
$$

So:

$$
3 \ inch x (2.54 \ cm / 1 \ inch) = 7.62 \ cm
$$

## 40. Dimensional Analysis Method

Steps:

1. Write the given value with unit.
2. Write the required unit.
3. Choose a conversion factor.
4. Arrange the factor so unwanted units cancel.
5. Multiply numbers.
6. Report the answer with correct unit and significant figures.

## 41. Laws of Chemical Combination

Chemical combination is governed by five important laws:

1. Law of conservation of mass.
2. Law of definite proportions.
3. Law of multiple proportions.
4. Gay Lussac's law of gaseous volumes.
5. Avogadro's law.

## 42. Law of Conservation of Mass

The law states that matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

In a chemical reaction:

$$
total \ mass \ of \ reactants = total \ mass \ of \ products
$$

This law was established through careful experimental work by Antoine Lavoisier.

## 43. Law of Definite Proportions

The law states that a given compound always contains the same elements combined in the same fixed proportion by mass.

Example:

Pure water always contains hydrogen and oxygen in a fixed mass ratio.

This law is also called law of definite composition.

## 44. Law of Multiple Proportions

The law states that when two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other element are in a ratio of small whole numbers.

Example:

Hydrogen and oxygen form water and hydrogen peroxide.

```text
Water: 2 g hydrogen combines with 16 g oxygen
Hydrogen peroxide: 2 g hydrogen combines with 32 g oxygen
```

Oxygen masses are in ratio:

$$
16 : 32 = 1 : 2
$$

## 45. Gay Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes

The law states that when gases combine or are produced in a chemical reaction, they do so in simple whole-number ratios by volume, provided temperature and pressure are the same.

Example:

```text
100 mL hydrogen + 50 mL oxygen -> 100 mL water vapour
```

Volume ratio:

$$
100 : 50 : 100 = 2 : 1 : 2
$$

## 46. Avogadro's Law

Avogadro's law states that equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

This law helped explain gaseous volume relationships and the distinction between atoms and molecules.

## 47. Dalton's Atomic Theory

Dalton proposed:

1. Matter consists of indivisible atoms.
2. All atoms of a given element have identical properties and identical mass.
3. Atoms of different elements differ in mass.
4. Compounds form when atoms of different elements combine in fixed ratios.
5. Chemical reactions involve reorganisation of atoms.
6. Atoms are neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions.

Dalton's theory explained laws of chemical combination but could not fully explain gaseous volumes.

## 48. Atomic Mass

Atomic mass is the mass of an atom relative to a standard.

Modern atomic masses are based on carbon-12.

One atomic mass unit, now commonly called unified mass unit `u`, is:

$$
1 \ u = (1/12) \ of \ mass \ of \ one \ carbon-12 \ atom
$$

Also:

$$
1 \ u = 1.66056 x 10^{-24} \ g
$$

## 49. Average Atomic Mass

Many elements exist as isotopes.

Average atomic mass depends on:

1. Isotopic masses.
2. Natural abundance of each isotope.

Formula:

$$
average \ atomic \ mass = sum[(fractional \ abundance) x (isotopic \ mass)]
$$

Example for carbon:

`C-12`, `C-13`, and `C-14` occur in different abundances, so the average atomic mass is approximately `12.011 u`.

## 50. Molecular Mass

Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.

Example:

For methane, `CH4`:

$$
molecular \ mass = 12.011 + 4(1.008) = 16.043 \ u
$$

For water, `H2O`:

$$
molecular \ mass = 2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 \ u
$$

## 51. Formula Mass

Ionic substances such as sodium chloride do not exist as separate molecules in the solid state.

For such compounds, formula mass is used.

For `NaCl`:

$$
formula \ mass = atomic \ mass \ of \ Na + atomic \ mass \ of \ Cl
$$

$$
= 23.0 + 35.5 = 58.5 \ u
$$

## 52. Mole Concept

Atoms and molecules are extremely small, so chemists use mole to count them conveniently.

One mole contains exactly:

$$
6.02214076 x 10^{23}
$$

elementary entities.

This number is Avogadro constant, written as `N_A`.

Examples:

1. `1 mol` hydrogen atoms contains `6.022 x 10^23` hydrogen atoms.
2. `1 mol` water molecules contains `6.022 x 10^23` water molecules.
3. `1 mol` sodium chloride contains `6.022 x 10^23` formula units.

## 53. Molar Mass

Molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance.

Its unit is:

$$
g \ mol^{-1}
$$

The numerical value of molar mass in grams per mole is equal to molecular mass or formula mass in `u`.

Examples:

1. Molecular mass of water = `18.016 u`, so molar mass = `18.016 g mol^-1`.
2. Formula mass of sodium chloride = `58.5 u`, so molar mass = `58.5 g mol^-1`.

## 54. Mole Conversion Triangle

Use these three relationships:

$$
moles = mass / molar \ mass
$$

$$
mass = moles x molar \ mass
$$

$$
number \ of \ particles = moles x N_A
$$

## 55. Percentage Composition

Mass percent of an element in a compound is:

$$
mass \ percent \ of \ element = (mass \ of \ element \ in \ one \ mole \ compound / molar \ mass \ of \ compound) x 100
$$

Example for water:

Molar mass of `H2O` is:

$$
2(1.008) + 16.00 = 18.016 \ g \ mol^{-1}
$$

Hydrogen percent:

$$
(2.016 / 18.016) x 100 = 11.19 \ percent
$$

Oxygen percent:

$$
(16.00 / 18.016) x 100 = 88.81 \ percent
$$

## 56. Empirical Formula

Empirical formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.

Example:

`CH2O` may be an empirical formula.

It tells ratio `C:H:O = 1:2:1`.

## 57. Molecular Formula

Molecular formula gives the actual number of atoms in one molecule.

Example:

Glucose has molecular formula `C6H12O6`.

Its empirical formula is `CH2O`.

## 58. Empirical Formula from Percent Composition

Steps:

1. Assume `100 g` of compound.
2. Convert each percent into grams.
3. Convert grams of each element into moles.
4. Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value.
5. Convert ratios to whole numbers.
6. Write empirical formula.

## 59. Molecular Formula from Empirical Formula

Steps:

1. Find empirical formula mass.
2. Divide molar mass by empirical formula mass.
3. Multiply empirical formula subscripts by this number.

Formula:

$$
n = molar \ mass / empirical \ formula \ mass
$$

Then:

$$
molecular \ formula = n x empirical \ formula
$$

## 60. Worked Example: Empirical and Molecular Formula

A compound contains `4.07% H`, `24.27% C`, and `71.65% Cl`. Its molar mass is `98.96 g mol^-1`.

Assume `100 g` sample:

| Element | Mass in 100 g | Atomic mass | Moles |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| H | 4.07 g | 1.008 | 4.04 |
| C | 24.27 g | 12.01 | 2.021 |
| Cl | 71.65 g | 35.45 | 2.021 |

Divide by smallest value, `2.021`:

```text
H : C : Cl = 2 : 1 : 1
```

Empirical formula:

```text
CH2Cl
```

Empirical formula mass:

$$
12.01 + 2(1.008) + 35.45 = 49.48
$$

Value of `n`:

$$
n = 98.96 / 49.48 = 2
$$

Molecular formula:

```text
C2H4Cl2
```

## 61. Chemical Equations

A chemical equation represents a chemical reaction using formulas.

Example:

```text
CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)
```

Reactants are on the left.

Products are on the right.

Coefficients show mole ratios.

## 62. Balanced Chemical Equation

A balanced equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides.

This follows the law of conservation of mass.

Example:

```text
4Fe(s) + 3O2(g) -> 2Fe2O3(s)
```

## 63. Steps to Balance an Equation

1. Write correct formulas of reactants and products.
2. Balance atoms of elements other than hydrogen and oxygen first.
3. Balance hydrogen.
4. Balance oxygen.
5. Check every element.
6. Do not change subscripts in formulas.

Example:

```text
C3H8(g) + 5O2(g) -> 3CO2(g) + 4H2O(l)
```

## 64. Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry deals with calculations of reactants and products using a balanced chemical equation.

The balanced equation gives ratios in:

1. Molecules.
2. Moles.
3. Masses.
4. Gas volumes, when gases are at the same temperature and pressure.

## 65. Stoichiometric Meaning of Methane Combustion

For:

```text
CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)
```

It means:

1. `1 mol CH4` reacts with `2 mol O2`.
2. `1 mol CH4` forms `1 mol CO2`.
3. `1 mol CH4` forms `2 mol H2O`.
4. `16 g CH4` reacts with `64 g O2`.
5. `16 g CH4` forms `44 g CO2`.
6. `16 g CH4` forms `36 g H2O`.

## 66. General Stoichiometry Method

Use this sequence:

```text
given mass
-> moles of given substance
-> mole ratio from balanced equation
-> moles of required substance
-> required mass or particles
```

## 67. Worked Example: Water from Methane

Question: Calculate mass of water produced by combustion of `16 g CH4`.

Balanced equation:

```text
CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O
```

`16 g CH4 = 1 mol CH4`.

From equation:

```text
1 mol CH4 -> 2 mol H2O
```

Mass of `2 mol H2O`:

$$
2 x 18 = 36 \ g
$$

Answer:

```text
36 g H2O
```

## 68. Limiting Reagent

The limiting reagent is the reactant that is consumed first.

It limits the amount of product formed.

The other reactant is present in excess.

## 69. How to Identify Limiting Reagent

Method:

1. Write balanced equation.
2. Convert given masses into moles.
3. Use mole ratio to calculate required amount.
4. Compare required and available amount.
5. The reactant that is insufficient is limiting reagent.

## 70. Worked Example: Ammonia Formation

Reaction:

```text
N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g)
```

Given:

```text
50.0 kg N2 and 10.0 kg H2
```

Moles of `N2`:

$$
(50.0 x 1000) / 28.0 = 1.786 x 10^3 \ mol
$$

Moles of `H2`:

$$
(10.0 x 1000) / 2.016 = 4.96 x 10^3 \ mol
$$

Required `H2` for `1.786 x 10^3 mol N2`:

$$
3 x 1.786 x 10^3 = 5.36 x 10^3 \ mol
$$

Available `H2` is only `4.96 x 10^3 mol`, so `H2` is limiting.

Moles of `NH3` formed:

$$
4.96 x 10^3 x (2/3) = 3.31 x 10^3 \ mol
$$

Mass of `NH3`:

$$
3.31 x 10^3 x 17.0 = 5.63 x 10^4 \ g = 56.3 \ kg
$$

## 71. Reactions in Solutions

Many laboratory reactions occur in solutions.

The amount of solute in a solution may be expressed as:

1. Mass percent.
2. Mole fraction.
3. Molarity.
4. Molality.

## 72. Mass Percent

Mass percent is:

$$
mass \ percent = (mass \ of \ solute / mass \ of \ solution) x 100
$$

Example:

`2 g` solute in `18 g` water gives total solution mass `20 g`.

$$
mass \ percent = (2/20) x 100 = 10 \ percent
$$

## 73. Mole Fraction

Mole fraction of a component is:

$$
X_A = n_A / (n_A + n_B)
$$

For component `B`:

$$
X_B = n_B / (n_A + n_B)
$$

For a solution:

$$
X_A + X_B = 1
$$

## 74. Molarity

Molarity is moles of solute per litre of solution.

$$
M = moles \ of \ solute / volume \ of \ solution \ in \ litres
$$

Unit:

$$
mol \ L^{-1}
$$

Molarity depends on temperature because volume changes with temperature.

## 75. Dilution Formula

During dilution, moles of solute remain constant.

$$
M_1V_1 = M_2V_2
$$

Use same volume units on both sides.

Example:

To make `1 L` of `0.2 M NaOH` from `1.0 M NaOH`:

$$
V_1 = (0.2 x 1000) / 1.0 = 200 \ mL
$$

So take `200 mL` stock solution and dilute to `1 L`.

## 76. Molality

Molality is moles of solute per kilogram of solvent.

$$
m = moles \ of \ solute / mass \ of \ solvent \ in \ kg
$$

Molality does not change with temperature because mass is not temperature-dependent.

## 77. Molarity and Molality Compared

| Basis | Molarity | Molality |
|---|---|---|
| Formula | Moles solute per litre solution | Moles solute per kg solvent |
| Symbol | `M` | `m` |
| Depends on volume | Yes | No |
| Changes with temperature | Yes | No |
| Common use | Laboratory solutions | Colligative properties and temperature-variable cases |

## 106. Common Student Confusions

|---|---|
| Mass and weight are same | Mass is amount of matter; weight is gravitational force |

| Accuracy and precision are same | Accuracy is closeness to true value; precision is closeness among measurements |

## 107. Exam Answer: Importance of Chemistry

Chemistry is important because it studies matter and its transformations. It helps in agriculture through fertilisers and pesticides, healthcare through medicines, industry through acids, alkalis, dyes, polymers and metals, and technology through materials such as optical fibres and conducting polymers. It also helps solve environmental problems by designing safer substances and understanding processes such as ozone depletion and greenhouse gases.

## 108. Exam Answer: Matter and Its Classification

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists mainly as solid, liquid, and gas. At the bulk level, matter is classified into pure substances and mixtures. Pure substances are elements or compounds. Elements contain one type of atom, while compounds contain two or more elements combined in fixed ratio. Mixtures contain two or more substances physically combined and may be homogeneous or heterogeneous.

## 109. Exam Answer: Significant Figures

Significant figures are meaningful digits in a measured quantity. They include all certain digits and one uncertain digit. All non-zero digits are significant, zeros between non-zero digits are significant, leading zeros are not significant, and trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant. Significant figures show the uncertainty and reliability of a measurement.

## 110. Exam Answer: Laws of Chemical Combination

The laws of chemical combination are:

1. Law of conservation of mass: mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
2. Law of definite proportions: a compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed proportion by mass.
3. Law of multiple proportions: when two elements form more than one compound, masses of one element combining with fixed mass of the other are in small whole-number ratios.
4. Gay Lussac's law: gaseous reactants and products combine in simple whole-number volume ratios at the same temperature and pressure.
5. Avogadro's law: equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

## 111. Exam Answer: Mole Concept

The mole is the SI unit of amount of substance. One mole contains exactly `6.02214076 x 10^23` elementary entities. This number is called Avogadro constant. The mass of one mole of a substance is called its molar mass. Mole concept connects microscopic particles with measurable mass and is essential for chemical calculations.

## 112. Exam Answer: Limiting Reagent

In a chemical reaction, the limiting reagent is the reactant that is consumed first. It determines the maximum amount of product formed. To identify it, convert given quantities into moles, compare them using the balanced equation, and find which reactant is insufficient. The other reactant is present in excess.

## 120. End Summary

Chemistry studies matter and its changes. Matter exists as solids, liquids, and gases, and can be classified into elements, compounds, and mixtures. Chemical study requires accurate measurement using SI units, scientific notation, significant figures, and dimensional analysis. Chemical combination follows laws such as conservation of mass, definite proportions, multiple proportions, gaseous volumes, and Avogadro's law. These laws supported Dalton's atomic theory. Atomic mass, molecular mass, formula mass, mole, molar mass, and Avogadro constant connect invisible particles to measurable quantities. Percentage composition helps determine empirical and molecular formulas. Balanced chemical equations support stoichiometric calculations, while limiting reagent determines maximum product. Solution concentration can be expressed as mass percent, mole fraction, molarity, and molality.
