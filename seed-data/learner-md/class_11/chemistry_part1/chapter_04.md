# Chapter 4: Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Chemical bonding explains why atoms combine, why only certain combinations are stable, why molecules have definite shapes, and why compounds show different physical and chemical properties.

The central idea is:

```text
Atoms combine because the bonded arrangement is usually lower in energy than separated atoms.
```

This chapter studies bonding through five major models:

1. Kossel-Lewis approach.
2. VSEPR theory.
3. Valence bond theory.
4. Hybridisation.
5. Molecular orbital theory.

## 5. Why Atoms Combine

Atoms combine because the resulting arrangement is usually more stable than isolated atoms.

Stability often means:

1. Lower potential energy.
2. More favourable electronic arrangement.
3. Strong attractive forces between particles.
4. Attainment of noble gas type configuration in many simple cases.

Except noble gases, most elements do not commonly exist as isolated atoms under ordinary conditions. They combine to form molecules, ions, crystals, and networks.

## 6. Chemical Bond

A chemical bond is the attractive force that holds atoms, ions, or groups of atoms together in a chemical species.

Chemical bonds explain:

1. Why sodium and chlorine form sodium chloride.
2. Why hydrogen exists as `H2`.
3. Why water is bent.
4. Why carbon dioxide is linear.
5. Why oxygen is paramagnetic.
6. Why ice has an open structure.

## 7. Major Bonding Models

| Model | Main use |
|---|---|
| Kossel-Lewis approach | Electron transfer, sharing, octet rule, Lewis structures |
| VSEPR theory | Shape prediction from electron pair repulsions |
| Valence bond theory | Bond formation by overlap of atomic orbitals |
| Hybridisation | Explains equivalent bonds and molecular geometry |
| Molecular orbital theory | Explains stability, bond order, and magnetism using MOs |

Each model is useful, but no single model explains every detail perfectly.

## 8. Lewis Symbols

Lewis symbols show valence electrons as dots around the element symbol.

Only valence electrons are shown because inner electrons usually do not participate in ordinary bonding.

Examples:

| Element | Valence electrons | Lewis idea |
|---|---:|---|
| H | 1 | H with one dot |
| C | 4 | C with four dots |
| N | 5 | N with five dots |
| O | 6 | O with six dots |
| F | 7 | F with seven dots |
| Ne | 8 | Ne with eight dots |

The number of dots helps predict common valence.

## 9. Kossel-Lewis Approach

Kossel and Lewis explained bonding using valence electrons and noble gas stability.

Core ideas:

1. Noble gases are stable because their valence shells are complete.
2. Most atoms tend to attain a stable noble gas arrangement.
3. Atoms may do this by losing, gaining, or sharing electrons.
4. Electron transfer gives ionic bonding.
5. Electron sharing gives covalent bonding.

Helium has a duplet, meaning two electrons in its only shell.

Other noble gases generally have an octet:

```text
ns2 np6
```

## 10. Octet Rule

Octet rule states:

```text
Atoms combine by losing, gaining, or sharing electrons to attain eight electrons in the valence shell.
```

Hydrogen follows a duplet rule:

```text
Hydrogen becomes stable with 2 electrons.
```

Why the octet rule is useful:

1. It explains many simple ionic compounds.
2. It explains many covalent molecules.
3. It helps draw Lewis structures.
4. It gives a simple way to predict valence.

## 11. Ionic Bond Formation

An ionic bond forms when one atom transfers electron(s) to another atom, producing oppositely charged ions.

Example: sodium chloride.

Sodium loses one electron:

$$
Na \rightarrow Na^+ + e^-
$$

Chlorine gains one electron:

$$
Cl + e^- \rightarrow Cl^-
$$

Ions attract:

$$
Na^+ + Cl^- \rightarrow NaCl
$$

The bond is due to electrostatic attraction between `Na+` and `Cl-`.

## 12. Electrovalence

Electrovalence is the number of unit charges on an ion.

Examples:

| Ion | Electrovalence |
|---|---:|
| Na+ | +1 |
| Ca2+ | +2 |
| Cl- | -1 |
| O2- | -2 |

Ionic compounds are electrically neutral overall.

## 13. Formation of Calcium Fluoride

Calcium loses two electrons:

$$
Ca \rightarrow Ca^{2+} + 2e^-
$$

Each fluorine gains one electron:

$$
F + e^- \rightarrow F^-
$$

Overall:

$$
Ca^{2+} + 2F^- \rightarrow CaF_2
$$

Calcium fluoride contains `Ca2+` and `F-` ions in a crystal lattice.

## 14. Conditions Favoring Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonding is favoured when:

1. Metal has low ionization enthalpy.
2. Non-metal has highly negative electron gain enthalpy.
3. Resulting ions have stable electronic configurations.
4. Lattice formation releases large energy.
5. Cation and anion have suitable sizes and charges for a stable crystal.

## 15. Lattice Enthalpy

Lattice enthalpy is the energy required to completely separate one mole of solid ionic compound into gaseous ions.

For sodium chloride:

$$
NaCl(s) \rightarrow Na^+(g) + Cl^-(g)
$$

Lattice enthalpy of NaCl is about:

```text
788 kJ mol^-1
```

High lattice enthalpy means strong ionic crystal.

## 16. Why Lattice Enthalpy Matters

Formation of gaseous ions may require energy overall.

For NaCl:

1. Ionization of sodium needs energy.
2. Electron gain by chlorine releases energy.
3. Formation of crystal lattice releases large energy.

The large energy released during lattice formation stabilizes the ionic compound.

So ionic compound stability depends not only on octet formation but also on lattice energy.

## 17. Covalent Bond

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share one or more electron pairs.

Example: chlorine molecule.

Each chlorine atom has 7 valence electrons and needs one more electron for octet.

Two chlorine atoms share one pair:

```text
Cl-Cl
```

Each chlorine effectively has an octet.

## 18. Single, Double, and Triple Bonds

| Bond type | Shared electron pairs | Example |
|---|---:|---|
| Single bond | 1 | H-H, Cl-Cl, C-H |
| Double bond | 2 | O=O, C=O, C=C |
| Triple bond | 3 | N#N, C#C |

In plain text, `#` is used here to show a triple bond.

General relation:

```text
More shared pairs usually mean higher bond order, shorter bond length, and stronger bond.
```

## 19. Lewis Structures

Lewis structures show:

1. Atoms.
2. Bond pairs.
3. Lone pairs.
4. Formal charges when needed.

They are useful for understanding:

1. Connectivity.
2. Electron distribution.
3. Octet completion.
4. Resonance possibilities.
5. Approximate bonding pattern.

They do not always show actual molecular shape or complete bonding reality.

## 20. Steps for Drawing Lewis Structures

Use this method:

1. Count total valence electrons.
2. Add electrons for negative charge.
3. Subtract electrons for positive charge.
4. Choose a skeletal structure.
5. Usually put least electronegative atom at the centre, except hydrogen.
6. Connect atoms with single bonds.
7. Complete octets of terminal atoms.
8. Put remaining electrons on central atom.
9. If central atom lacks octet, form multiple bonds.
10. Check formal charges.

## 21. Lewis Structure of CO

Valence electrons:

```text
C = 4
O = 6
Total = 10
```

To satisfy octets, carbon and oxygen form a triple bond with one lone pair on each atom.

Plain structure:

```text
:C#O:
```

Formal charges exist in a careful Lewis treatment, but the main learner point is that a triple bond is required to complete octets.

## 22. Lewis Structure of Nitrite Ion

Nitrite ion:

```text
NO2-
```

Valence electrons:

```text
N = 5
O = 6 x 2 = 12
charge = +1 electron
Total = 18
```

Skeleton:

```text
O-N-O
```

One N-O bond becomes double to complete nitrogen octet.

There are two equivalent resonance forms:

```text
O=N-O-   and   -O-N=O
```

The actual ion is a resonance hybrid.

## 23. Formal Charge

Formal charge is a bookkeeping charge assigned to atoms in a Lewis structure.

Formula:

$$
\text{Formal charge} = V - L - \frac{B}{2}
$$

Where:

```text
V = valence electrons of free atom
L = nonbonding electrons assigned to atom
B = bonding electrons around atom
```

Use formal charge to compare possible Lewis structures.

Generally, better Lewis structures have:

1. Smaller formal charges.
2. Negative formal charge on more electronegative atom.
3. Less charge separation.

## 24. Formal Charge Example: Ozone

One resonance form of ozone is:

```text
O=O-O
```

The central oxygen has a positive formal charge and the singly bonded terminal oxygen has a negative formal charge.

The two resonance forms are equivalent:

```text
O=O-O-   and   -O-O=O
```

The real ozone molecule has two equal O-O bonds with bond length between single and double bond values.

## 25. Limitations of Octet Rule

Octet rule is useful but not universal.

Main limitations:

1. Incomplete octet species.
2. Odd-electron species.
3. Expanded octet species.
4. Does not explain shapes.
5. Does not explain bond energies.
6. Does not explain relative stability.
7. Does not explain bonding in many transition metal compounds.
8. Does not explain noble gas compounds.

## 26. Incomplete Octet

Some stable molecules have central atoms with fewer than 8 electrons.

Examples:

```text
BeCl2
BF3
AlCl3
```

In `BF3`, boron has only 6 electrons around it in the simple Lewis structure.

## 27. Odd-Electron Molecules

Some molecules have an odd number of electrons, so all atoms cannot have complete octets.

Examples:

```text
NO
NO2
ClO2
```

Such species are often reactive because they contain unpaired electrons.

## 28. Expanded Octet

Some atoms in period 3 and beyond can have more than 8 electrons in common Lewis descriptions.

Examples:

```text
PCl5
SF6
H2SO4
```

The octet rule does not handle these simple structures well.

## 29. Bond Length

Bond length is the equilibrium distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms.

It is measured in picometres, pm.

General trends:

1. Triple bond is shorter than double bond.
2. Double bond is shorter than single bond.
3. Larger atoms form longer bonds.
4. Higher bond order usually means shorter bond.

Examples:

```text
C-C > C=C > C#C
```

## 30. Covalent Radius and van der Waals Radius

Covalent radius:

```text
Half the distance between nuclei of two identical atoms joined by a covalent bond.
```

van der Waals radius:

```text
Half the distance between nuclei of two nonbonded identical atoms in neighbouring molecules.
```

van der Waals radius is larger than covalent radius because nonbonded atoms are not pulled together by a covalent bond.

## 31. Bond Angle

Bond angle is the angle between two bonds around a central atom.

Examples:

| Molecule | Shape | Approximate bond angle |
|---|---|---:|
| BeCl2 | Linear | 180 deg |
| BF3 | Trigonal planar | 120 deg |
| CH4 | Tetrahedral | 109.5 deg |
| NH3 | Trigonal pyramidal | 107 deg |
| H2O | Bent | 104.5 deg |

Bond angle gives information about molecular shape.

## 32. Bond Enthalpy

Bond enthalpy is the energy required to break one mole of a particular type of bond in gaseous state.

Example:

$$
H_2(g) \rightarrow H(g) + H(g)
$$

For hydrogen:

```text
Bond enthalpy = 435.8 kJ mol^-1
```

Higher bond enthalpy usually means stronger bond.

## 33. Mean Bond Enthalpy

In polyatomic molecules, breaking identical-looking bonds may require different energies because the chemical environment changes after each bond is broken.

Example for water:

```text
First O-H bond = 502 kJ mol^-1
Second O-H bond = 427 kJ mol^-1
```

Mean bond enthalpy:

$$
\frac{502 + 427}{2} = 464.5 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}
$$

## 34. Bond Order in Lewis Description

In Lewis structures, bond order is the number of bonds between two atoms.

| Species | Bond order |
|---|---:|
| H-H | 1 |
| O=O | 2 |
| N#N | 3 |
| C#O | 3 |

Higher bond order generally means:

1. Higher bond enthalpy.
2. Shorter bond length.
3. Greater stability, if other factors are comparable.

## 35. Resonance

Sometimes one Lewis structure cannot describe a molecule or ion accurately.

In such cases, multiple contributing structures are written.

The real structure is called a resonance hybrid.

Important points:

1. Resonance forms are not separate molecules.
2. The molecule does not keep switching between forms.
3. Resonance forms have the same arrangement of nuclei.
4. Only electron arrangement differs.
5. Resonance hybrid is more stable than any single contributing form.
6. Resonance averages bond characteristics.

## 36. Resonance in Carbonate Ion

Carbonate ion:

```text
CO3^2-
```

Any one Lewis form has one C=O bond and two C-O bonds.

Experimentally all three C-O bonds are equivalent.

Therefore carbonate is represented by three resonance forms in which the double bond can be placed with any one oxygen.

Real carbonate ion:

```text
all C-O bonds equivalent
bond order of each C-O bond = 4/3
```

## 37. Resonance in Ozone

Ozone:

```text
O3
```

Two main contributing forms:

```text
O=O-O   and   O-O=O
```

The real molecule has two equal O-O bonds.

Each O-O bond is intermediate between single and double bond.

## 38. Resonance in CO2

Carbon dioxide is usually represented as:

```text
O=C=O
```

Some advanced resonance descriptions may include charge-separated contributing forms.

For Class 11 learning, remember:

1. CO2 is linear.
2. It has two C=O bonds in the main Lewis structure.
3. The molecule has zero net dipole moment because bond dipoles cancel.

## 39. Polarity of Bonds

If two bonded atoms have equal electronegativity, the bond is nonpolar covalent.

Examples:

```text
H-H
Cl-Cl
N#N
```

If electronegativity differs, shared electrons are pulled more toward the more electronegative atom.

Example:

```text
H-F
```

Fluorine attracts the shared pair more strongly, so the bond is polar.

## 40. Dipole Moment

Dipole moment measures separation of positive and negative charge.

Formula:

$$
\mu = Q \times r
$$

Where:

```text
mu = dipole moment
Q = magnitude of charge
r = distance between charge centres
```

Unit:

```text
Debye, D
```

Conversion:

$$
1D = 3.33564 \times 10^{-30} \text{ C m}
$$

Dipole moment is a vector quantity.

## 41. Molecular Dipole Moment

Molecular dipole moment depends on:

1. Polarity of individual bonds.
2. Shape of molecule.
3. Direction of bond dipoles.
4. Lone pair contribution.

A molecule may have polar bonds but zero dipole moment if bond dipoles cancel.

Examples:

| Molecule | Bond polarity | Shape | Net dipole |
|---|---|---|---|
| CO2 | Polar C=O bonds | Linear | Zero |
| BeF2 | Polar Be-F bonds | Linear | Zero |
| BF3 | Polar B-F bonds | Trigonal planar | Zero |
| H2O | Polar O-H bonds | Bent | Nonzero |
| NH3 | Polar N-H bonds | Pyramidal | Nonzero |

## 42. NH3 and NF3 Dipole Moment

`NH3` has higher dipole moment than `NF3`.

Reason:

In `NH3`:

1. N-H bond dipoles point toward nitrogen.
2. Lone pair dipole is in the same general direction.
3. Effects add.

In `NF3`:

1. N-F bond dipoles point toward fluorine atoms.
2. Lone pair dipole is opposite to the resultant of N-F bond dipoles.
3. Effects partly cancel.

Therefore:

```text
NH3 has higher dipole moment than NF3.
```

## 43. Fajan's Rules

Ionic bonds may have partial covalent character.

Fajan's rules predict covalent character in ionic bonds.

Covalent character increases when:

1. Cation is small.
2. Cation has high charge.
3. Anion is large.
4. Anion has high charge.
5. Cation has poor shielding and strong polarising power.

In simple terms:

```text
More polarization of anion -> more covalent character.
```

## 44. Increasing Ionic Character

Ionic character increases with electronegativity difference and with ease of ion formation.

For the raw exercise list:

```text
N2, SO2, ClF3, LiF, K2O
```

A useful increasing ionic character order is:

```text
N2 < SO2 < ClF3 < K2O < LiF
```

Reason:

1. `N2` is nonpolar covalent.
2. `SO2` and `ClF3` are covalent molecules with polar bonds.
3. `K2O` is ionic because it is formed from a highly electropositive metal and oxygen.
4. `LiF` has the largest electronegativity difference in this list, so it is placed last in the standard textbook order.

## 45. VSEPR Theory

VSEPR stands for Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion.

Main idea:

```text
Electron pairs around a central atom repel one another and arrange as far apart as possible.
```

This helps predict molecular shape.

Electron pairs include:

1. Bond pairs.
2. Lone pairs.
3. Multiple bonds treated as one electron domain for shape.

## 46. Repulsion Order

Repulsion strength:

```text
lone pair - lone pair > lone pair - bond pair > bond pair - bond pair
```

Reason:

Lone pairs are localized on the central atom and occupy more space than bond pairs.

This is why lone pairs reduce bond angles.

Examples:

| Molecule | Type | Shape |

## 50. Why H2O Angle Is Less Than NH3

Both `NH3` and `H2O` have tetrahedral electron pair arrangement.

`NH3`:

```text
3 bond pairs + 1 lone pair
bond angle about 107 deg
```

`H2O`:

```text
2 bond pairs + 2 lone pairs
bond angle about 104.5 deg
```

Water has two lone pairs. Lone pair-lone pair and lone pair-bond pair repulsions are stronger than bond pair-bond pair repulsions.

So bond angle in water is compressed more.

## 51. CO2 and H2O Shapes by Dipole Moment

`CO2`:

```text
O=C=O
linear
bond dipoles equal and opposite
net dipole moment = 0
```

`H2O`:

```text
bent
O-H bond dipoles do not cancel
net dipole moment is nonzero
```

Thus, both are triatomic, but their molecular shapes differ because electron pair arrangement around central atom differs.

## 52. Why BeH2 Has Zero Dipole Moment

`BeH2` is linear:

```text
H-Be-H
```

The two Be-H bond dipoles are equal and opposite.

They cancel each other.

Therefore:

```text
BeH2 has zero dipole moment even though Be-H bonds are polar.
```

## 53. Why CH4 Is Not Square Planar

Carbon in methane uses four equivalent `sp3` hybrid orbitals.

These orbitals point toward the corners of a tetrahedron.

Tetrahedral geometry gives maximum separation among four electron pairs.

Square planar geometry would force 90 deg interactions and would not match the equivalent `sp3` orbital arrangement.

Therefore:

```text
CH4 is tetrahedral, not square planar.
```

## 54. Valence Bond Theory

Valence bond theory explains covalent bond formation using overlap of atomic orbitals.

Main idea:

```text
A covalent bond forms when half-filled atomic orbitals of two atoms overlap and electrons pair with opposite spins.
```

As two atoms approach:

1. Attraction increases between each nucleus and the other atom's electron.
2. Repulsion occurs between nuclei and between electrons.
3. At a certain distance, attraction balances repulsion.
4. Potential energy is minimum.
5. A stable bond is formed.

## 55. H2 Formation by VB Theory

Two hydrogen atoms approach.

Each has:

```text
1s1
```

The two 1s orbitals overlap.

Electrons pair with opposite spins.

A stable H-H bond forms.

Bond length:

```text
about 74 pm
```

Bond enthalpy:

```text
about 435.8 kJ mol^-1
```

## 56. Orbital Overlap

Orbital overlap means partial interpenetration of atomic orbitals.

Greater overlap usually gives:

1. Stronger bond.
2. Greater bond enthalpy.
3. Shorter bond length.
4. More electron density between nuclei.

Effective overlap requires:

1. Suitable orientation.
2. Comparable energy.
3. Proper phase of orbital wave functions.

## 57. Positive, Negative, and Zero Overlap

Positive overlap:

```text
same phase lobes overlap
bonding possible
```

Negative overlap:

```text
opposite phase lobes overlap
bonding not favoured
```

Zero overlap:

```text
orbitals do not overlap effectively due to symmetry
```

Only effective positive overlap leads to bond formation.

## 58. Sigma Bond

A sigma bond is formed by end-to-end overlap along the internuclear axis.

Common sigma overlaps:

1. s-s overlap.
2. s-p overlap.
3. p-p axial overlap.
4. hybrid-hybrid overlap.
5. hybrid-s overlap.

Sigma bonds have electron density along the line joining the nuclei.

Every single bond is a sigma bond.

## 59. Pi Bond

A pi bond is formed by sidewise overlap of parallel p orbitals.

Features:

1. Electron density lies above and below the internuclear axis.
2. It is weaker than a sigma bond.
3. It forms only after a sigma bond has formed between the same two atoms.
4. It occurs in double and triple bonds.

## 60. Sigma and Pi in Multiple Bonds

| Bond | Composition |
|---|---|
| Single bond | 1 sigma |
| Double bond | 1 sigma + 1 pi |
| Triple bond | 1 sigma + 2 pi |

Examples:

```text
C=C contains 1 sigma and 1 pi.
C#C contains 1 sigma and 2 pi.
```

## 61. Why Sigma Bond Is Stronger Than Pi Bond

Sigma bond is stronger because:

1. Overlap is direct and axial.
2. Electron density is concentrated between nuclei.
3. Orbital overlap is greater.

Pi bond is weaker because:

1. Overlap is sideways.
2. Electron density is above and below the internuclear axis.
3. Overlap is less effective.

## 62. Hybridisation

Hybridisation is the intermixing of atomic orbitals of slightly different energies to form a new set of equivalent hybrid orbitals.

Key features:

1. Number of hybrid orbitals equals number of atomic orbitals mixed.
2. Hybrid orbitals are equivalent in energy and shape.
3. Hybrid orbitals are more directional than pure atomic orbitals.
4. Hybrid orbitals form stronger bonds by better overlap.
5. Type of hybridisation helps predict molecular geometry.

## 63. Conditions for Hybridisation

Important points:

1. Orbitals of the same atom hybridise.
2. Orbitals should have comparable energy.
3. Orbitals usually belong to valence shell.
4. Promotion of electron is not always necessary.
5. Filled, half-filled, and empty orbitals may participate if they are suitable.

## 64. Common Hybridisation Types

| Hybridisation | Orbitals mixed | Number of hybrids | Geometry | Angle |
|---|---|---:|---|---:|
| sp | 1s + 1p | 2 | Linear | 180 deg |
| sp2 | 1s + 2p | 3 | Trigonal planar | 120 deg |
| sp3 | 1s + 3p | 4 | Tetrahedral | 109.5 deg |
| sp3d | 1s + 3p + 1d | 5 | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90 deg, 120 deg |
| sp3d2 | 1s + 3p + 2d | 6 | Octahedral | 90 deg |
| dsp2 | 1d + 1s + 2p | 4 | Square planar | 90 deg |

## 65. sp Hybridisation

sp hybridisation mixes:

```text
one s orbital + one p orbital
```

It gives:

```text
two sp hybrid orbitals
```

Properties:

1. 50 percent s character.
2. 50 percent p character.
3. Linear geometry.
4. Bond angle 180 deg.

Examples:

```text
BeCl2
C2H2 carbon atoms
```

## 66. sp2 Hybridisation

sp2 hybridisation mixes:

```text
one s orbital + two p orbitals
```

It gives:

```text
three sp2 hybrid orbitals
```

Properties:

1. 33.3 percent s character.
2. 66.7 percent p character.
3. Trigonal planar geometry.
4. Bond angle 120 deg.

Examples:

```text
BCl3
BF3
C2H4 carbon atoms
```

## 67. sp3 Hybridisation

sp3 hybridisation mixes:

```text
one s orbital + three p orbitals
```

It gives:

```text
four sp3 hybrid orbitals
```

Properties:

1. 25 percent s character.
2. 75 percent p character.
3. Tetrahedral electron pair geometry.
4. Ideal angle 109.5 deg.

```text
C2H6 carbon atoms
```

Each `sp3` hybrid orbital overlaps with hydrogen `1s`.

Result:


## 72. sp3d Hybridisation in PCl5

Phosphorus in `PCl5` uses `sp3d` hybridisation in the textbook model.

Geometry:

```text
trigonal bipyramidal
```

Bonds:

1. Three equatorial P-Cl bonds.
2. Two axial P-Cl bonds.

Angles:

```text

axial-equatorial = 90 deg
axial-axial = 180 deg
```

## 73. Why Axial Bonds in PCl5 Are Longer

Axial P-Cl bonds experience more repulsion.

Each axial bond pair has three 90 deg interactions with equatorial bond pairs.

Equatorial bond pairs have fewer strong 90 deg interactions.

Therefore:

```text
axial P-Cl bonds are longer and weaker than equatorial P-Cl bonds
```

This contributes to higher reactivity of `PCl5`.

Sulfur in `SF6` uses `sp3d2` hybridisation in the textbook model.

Geometry:

```text
octahedral
```

Features:

1. Six equivalent S-F sigma bonds.
2. Bond angles 90 deg and 180 deg.
3. Highly symmetrical structure.

In `AlCl3`:


In `AlCl4-`:



## 79. Sigma Bond Formation with x-Axis as Internuclear Axis

If x-axis is the internuclear axis:

1. `1s and 1s` can form sigma bond.
2. `1s and 2px` can form sigma bond because `2px` lies along x-axis.
3. `2py and 2py` do not form sigma bond along x-axis; sidewise overlap gives pi bond.
4. `1s and 2s` can form sigma bond.

Answer to "which will not form a sigma bond":

```text
2py and 2py
```

Reason:

Their axes are perpendicular to the internuclear x-axis, so their sidewise overlap forms pi interaction, not sigma.

## 80. Bond Pair and Lone Pair

Bond pair:

```text
Shared pair of electrons between two atoms.
```

Example:

In `H2`, the shared pair between two hydrogen atoms is a bond pair.

Lone pair:

```text
Pair of valence electrons not involved in bonding.
```

Example:

In `NH3`, nitrogen has one lone pair.

In `H2O`, oxygen has two lone pairs.

## 81. Molecular Orbital Theory

Molecular orbital theory describes electrons as occupying orbitals that belong to the molecule as a whole.

Main ideas:

1. Atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals.
2. Number of molecular orbitals formed equals number of atomic orbitals combined.
3. Bonding MO has lower energy.
4. Antibonding MO has higher energy.
5. Electrons fill MOs using Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule.
6. MO theory explains bond order, stability, and magnetic nature.

## 82. LCAO

LCAO means Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals.

When two atomic orbitals combine:

1. Addition gives bonding molecular orbital.
2. Subtraction gives antibonding molecular orbital.

For hydrogen 1s orbitals:

$$
\psi_{MO} = \psi_A + \psi_B
$$

Bonding MO:

$$
\sigma_{1s} = \psi_A + \psi_B
$$

Antibonding MO:

$$
\sigma^*_{1s} = \psi_A - \psi_B
$$

## 83. Bonding and Antibonding MOs

Bonding MO:

1. Formed by constructive combination.
2. Electron density increases between nuclei.
3. Energy is lower than parent atomic orbitals.
4. Stabilizes molecule.

Antibonding MO:

1. Formed by destructive combination.
2. Has a node between nuclei.
3. Energy is higher than parent atomic orbitals.
4. Destabilizes molecule.

Antibonding orbitals are marked with `*`.

## 84. Conditions for Atomic Orbital Combination

Atomic orbitals combine effectively only when:

1. They have same or nearly same energy.
2. They have proper symmetry about the molecular axis.
3. They overlap significantly.

Example:

`1s` combines well with another `1s`.

If z-axis is molecular axis, `2pz` combines with `2pz` to form sigma MOs.

`2px` and `2py` combine sidewise to form pi MOs.

## 85. Types of Molecular Orbitals

Sigma molecular orbitals:

```text
symmetrical around internuclear axis
```

Pi molecular orbitals:

```text
electron density above and below internuclear axis
not cylindrically symmetrical
```

Antibonding molecular orbitals:

```text
sigma*, pi*
```

## 86. MO Energy Order for O2 and F2

For `O2` and `F2`, use:

```text
sigma1s < sigma*1s < sigma2s < sigma*2s < sigma2pz < pi2px = pi2py < pi*2px = pi*2py < sigma*2pz
```

This order is commonly used for oxygen and fluorine.

## 87. MO Energy Order for B2, C2, and N2

For `B2`, `C2`, and `N2`, use:

```text
sigma1s < sigma*1s < sigma2s < sigma*2s < pi2px = pi2py < sigma2pz < pi*2px = pi*2py < sigma*2pz
```

Important difference:

```text
pi2p orbitals are lower than sigma2pz for B2, C2, and N2.
```

## 88. MO Bond Order

Bond order in MO theory:

$$
\text{Bond order} = \frac{N_b - N_a}{2}
$$

Where:

```text
Nb = number of electrons in bonding molecular orbitals
Na = number of electrons in antibonding molecular orbitals
```

Interpretation:

| Bond order | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| Positive | Species may be stable |
| Zero | Species is unstable or does not exist as normal molecule |
| Higher value | Stronger and shorter bond |

## 89. Magnetic Nature

Diamagnetic:

```text
all electrons paired
repelled weakly by magnetic field
```

Paramagnetic:

```text
one or more unpaired electrons
attracted by magnetic field
```

MO theory explains why `O2` is paramagnetic.

Lewis structures alone do not explain this properly.

## 90. H2 by MO Theory

Hydrogen molecule has 2 electrons.

Configuration:

```text
H2: (sigma1s)^2
```

Bonding electrons:

```text
Nb = 2
```

Antibonding electrons:

```text
Na = 0
```

Bond order:

$$
\frac{2 - 0}{2} = 1
$$

Magnetic nature:

```text
diamagnetic
```

## 91. He2 by MO Theory

Helium molecule would have 4 electrons.

Configuration:

```text
He2: (sigma1s)^2 (sigma*1s)^2
```

Bond order:

$$
\frac{2 - 2}{2} = 0
$$

Therefore:

```text
He2 is not stable as an ordinary molecule.
```

## 92. Li2 by MO Theory

Lithium has configuration:

```text
Li: 1s2 2s1
```

`Li2` has 6 electrons.

Configuration:

```text
Li2: (sigma1s)^2 (sigma*1s)^2 (sigma2s)^2
```

Bond order:

$$
\frac{4 - 2}{2} = 1
$$

Magnetic nature:

```text
diamagnetic
```

`Li2` exists in vapour phase.

## 93. C2 by MO Theory

Carbon has 6 electrons, so `C2` has 12 electrons.

Using B2-C2-N2 order:

```text
C2: KK (sigma2s)^2 (sigma*2s)^2 (pi2px)^2 (pi2py)^2
```

Here `KK` represents filled inner 1s bonding and antibonding orbitals.

Bond order:

```text
bonding electrons = 8
antibonding electrons = 4
```

$$
\frac{8 - 4}{2} = 2
$$

Magnetic nature:

```text
diamagnetic
```

Important:

In `C2`, the bond order 2 comes mainly from two pi bonds in the simple MO description.

## 94. N2 by MO Theory

Nitrogen has 7 electrons, so `N2` has 14 electrons.

Bond order:

```text
3
```

Magnetic nature:

```text
diamagnetic
```

Reason:

All electrons are paired, and the bond order is high.

This explains why `N2` has a very strong triple bond.

## 95. O2 by MO Theory

Oxygen has 8 electrons, so `O2` has 16 electrons.

Using O2-F2 order, valence part includes two electrons in antibonding pi orbitals.

Bond order:

```text
2
```

Magnetic nature:

```text
paramagnetic
```

Reason:

`O2` has two unpaired electrons in pi antibonding molecular orbitals.

This is a major success of MO theory.

## 96. O2+, O2-, and O2^2-

Use bond order of `O2` as 2.

Removing one electron from antibonding orbital gives `O2+`:

```text
bond order = 2.5
```

Adding one electron to antibonding orbital gives `O2-`:

```text
bond order = 1.5
```

Adding two electrons to antibonding orbitals gives `O2^2-`:

```text
bond order = 1
```

Stability order:

```text
O2+ > O2 > O2- > O2^2-
```

Bond length order is reverse:

```text
O2+ < O2 < O2- < O2^2-
```

Magnetic nature:

| Species | Bond order | Magnetic nature |
|---|---:|---|
| O2+ | 2.5 | Paramagnetic |
| O2 | 2 | Paramagnetic |
| O2- | 1.5 | Paramagnetic |
| O2^2- | 1 | Diamagnetic |

## 97. Hydrogen Bonding

Hydrogen bonding occurs when hydrogen is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom and is attracted to another electronegative atom.

The most common atoms involved are:

```text
F, O, N
```

General representation:

```text
X-H ... Y
```

Where:

1. `X-H` is a covalent bond.
2. `...` represents hydrogen bond.
3. `X` and `Y` are usually F, O, or N.

Hydrogen bond is weaker than covalent bond but stronger than ordinary van der Waals forces.

## 98. Cause of Hydrogen Bonding

When hydrogen is bonded to F, O, or N:

1. The bond becomes highly polar.
2. Hydrogen gets partial positive character.
3. The electronegative atom gets partial negative character.
4. The partially positive H is attracted to lone pair of another electronegative atom.

Example:

```text
H-F ... H-F ... H-F
```

## 99. Types of Hydrogen Bonding

Intermolecular hydrogen bonding:

```text
between different molecules
```

Examples:

1. Water.
2. Hydrogen fluoride.
3. Alcohols.

Intramolecular hydrogen bonding:

```text
within the same molecule
```

Example:

```text
o-nitrophenol
```

## 100. Effects of Hydrogen Bonding

Hydrogen bonding affects:

1. Boiling point.
2. Melting point.
3. Solubility.
4. Viscosity.
5. Structure of ice.
6. Structure of biological molecules.

Water has unusually high boiling point for its molar mass because of hydrogen bonding.

## 103. Resonance in H3PO3

Two structures can be resonance forms only if:

1. Same atomic positions.
2. Same number of electrons.
3. Same overall charge.
4. Only electron positions change.

For `H3PO3`, structures that differ by movement of a hydrogen atom or different atom connectivity are not resonance forms.

Actual phosphorous acid is commonly represented as:

```text
H-P(=O)(OH)2
```

It has one P-H bond and two O-H bonds.

If two proposed structures have different positions of H atoms, they are not canonical resonance forms.

## 116. Exam Answer: Octet Rule

Octet rule states that atoms tend to combine by losing, gaining, or sharing electrons so that they acquire eight electrons in their valence shell. It explains many ionic and covalent compounds and helps write Lewis structures. Its limitations are that it cannot explain incomplete octets, odd-electron molecules, expanded octets, molecular shapes, bond energies, and many noble gas or transition metal compounds.

## 117. Exam Answer: Ionic Bond

An ionic bond is formed by transfer of electron(s) from an electropositive atom to an electronegative atom, followed by electrostatic attraction between the resulting cation and anion. For example, sodium transfers one electron to chlorine to form `Na+` and `Cl-`; these ions attract to form sodium chloride.

## 118. Exam Answer: Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share one or more electron pairs. Each atom contributes at least one electron to the shared pair. Sharing helps atoms attain stable electronic configurations. A single bond has one shared pair, a double bond has two shared pairs, and a triple bond has three shared pairs.

## 119. Exam Answer: Resonance

Resonance is used when a single Lewis structure cannot describe a molecule or ion accurately. Two or more contributing structures with the same atomic positions but different electron arrangements are written. The real species is a resonance hybrid, which is more stable than any single contributing structure. Resonance averages bond lengths and bond character.

## 120. Exam Answer: VSEPR Theory

VSEPR theory states that electron pairs in the valence shell of a central atom repel one another and arrange themselves as far apart as possible. Lone pairs repel more strongly than bond pairs, so they distort bond angles. The repulsion order is lone pair-lone pair greater than lone pair-bond pair greater than bond pair-bond pair.

## 121. Exam Answer: Hybridisation

Hybridisation is the intermixing of atomic orbitals of slightly different energies to form equivalent hybrid orbitals of the same shape and energy. The number of hybrid orbitals formed equals the number of atomic orbitals mixed. Hybridisation explains molecular shapes such as linear `sp`, trigonal planar `sp2`, tetrahedral `sp3`, trigonal bipyramidal `sp3d`, and octahedral `sp3d2`.

## 122. Exam Answer: Molecular Orbital Theory

Molecular orbital theory states that atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals spread over the molecule. The number of molecular orbitals formed equals the number of atomic orbitals combined. Bonding molecular orbitals are lower in energy and stabilize the molecule, while antibonding orbitals are higher in energy and destabilize it. Electrons fill molecular orbitals according to Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule.

## 130. End Summary

Chemical bonding is the attractive force that holds atoms or ions together and lowers the energy of a system. The Kossel-Lewis approach explains ionic and covalent bonds using electron transfer, electron sharing, Lewis symbols, and octet rule. Lewis structures are useful but limited, so formal charge and resonance are used for better electron accounting. Bond properties such as length, angle, enthalpy, order, and dipole moment connect structure with stability and polarity. VSEPR theory predicts shapes using electron pair repulsions. Valence bond theory explains bonds through orbital overlap and distinguishes sigma and pi bonds. Hybridisation explains equivalent bonds and common molecular geometries. Molecular orbital theory explains bond order, stability, and magnetic behaviour, especially the paramagnetism of oxygen. Hydrogen bonding explains special attractions involving hydrogen bonded to F, O, or N and strongly affects molecular properties.
