The central idea is:

```text
Chemical behaviour is mainly controlled by the arrangement and energy of electrons in atoms.
```

## 5. Why Atomic Structure Matters

Atomic structure explains:

1. Why atoms have different sizes.
2. Why elements form ions.
3. Why atoms combine to form molecules.
4. Why some elements are reactive and others are inert.
5. Why atoms emit line spectra.
6. Why electrons are arranged in shells and subshells.
7. Why periodic properties repeat.
8. Why chemical bonding occurs.

## 6. Early View of Atom

Ancient Indian and Greek thinkers proposed that matter is made of tiny indivisible particles.

The word atom comes from a Greek idea meaning indivisible or uncuttable.

Dalton later gave the first scientific atomic theory, treating atom as the ultimate particle of matter.

However, later experiments showed that atoms are divisible and contain:

1. Electrons.
2. Protons.
3. Neutrons.

## 7. Charged Particles Rule

A basic rule is:

```text
Like charges repel.
Unlike charges attract.
```

This rule helps explain cathode rays, canal rays, electron-nucleus attraction, and Rutherford scattering.

## 8. Discovery of Electron

Electron was discovered through cathode ray discharge tube experiments.

In a cathode ray tube:

1. Gas is kept at very low pressure.
2. High voltage is applied between electrodes.
3. Rays move from cathode to anode.
4. These rays are called cathode rays.

## 9. Properties of Cathode Rays

Cathode rays:

1. Start from the cathode.
2. Move towards the anode.
3. Travel in straight lines in absence of electric or magnetic field.
4. Produce fluorescence on suitable materials.
5. Are deflected by electric and magnetic fields.
6. Behave like negatively charged particles.
7. Do not depend on the gas or electrode material.

Conclusion:

Cathode rays are streams of electrons, and electrons are constituents of all atoms.

## 10. Charge-to-Mass Ratio of Electron

J. J. Thomson measured the charge-to-mass ratio of electron.

$$
e/m_e = 1.758820 x 10^{11} \ C \ kg^{-1}
$$

This showed that electron has very small mass and negative charge.

## 11. Charge and Mass of Electron

Millikan's oil drop experiment measured the charge of electron.

Charge of electron:

$$
e = -1.602176 x 10^{-19} \ C
$$

Mass of electron:

$$
m_e = 9.1094 x 10^{-31} \ kg
$$

## 12. Millikan Oil Drop Experiment: Core Idea

Millikan observed charged oil drops between electrically charged plates.

He found that charge on oil drops was always an integral multiple of the elementary charge.

Formula:

$$
q = n e
$$

where `n = 1, 2, 3, ...`.

This confirmed quantisation of electric charge.

## 13. Discovery of Protons

Modified cathode ray tube experiments led to canal rays, or positively charged rays.

Properties:

1. Positively charged particles moved opposite to cathode rays.
2. Their charge-to-mass ratio depended on gas used.
3. The lightest positive ion came from hydrogen.
4. This lightest positive particle was called proton.

## 14. Discovery of Neutrons

Chadwick discovered neutrons in 1932.

He bombarded beryllium with alpha particles and observed neutral particles with mass slightly greater than protons.

Neutrons:

1. Have no charge.
2. Are present in nucleus.
3. Have mass close to proton mass.

## 15. Fundamental Particles

| Particle | Symbol | Relative charge | Approximate mass in u | Actual mass in kg |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Electron | e | -1 | 0.00054 | `9.1094 x 10^-31` |
| Proton | p | +1 | 1.00727 | `1.6726 x 10^-27` |
| Neutron | n | 0 | 1.00867 | `1.6749 x 10^-27` |

## 16. Why Atomic Models Were Needed

After discovery of sub-atomic particles, scientists needed to explain:

1. How positive and negative particles are arranged in atom.
2. Why atoms are electrically neutral.
3. Why atoms are stable.
4. Why different atoms show different properties.
5. Why atoms emit or absorb electromagnetic radiation.
6. How atoms combine to form molecules.

## 17. Thomson Model of Atom

Thomson proposed that:

1. Atom is a positively charged sphere.
2. Electrons are embedded in this positive sphere.
3. Total positive charge balances total negative charge.
4. Mass is uniformly distributed.

This model is called:

1. Plum pudding model.
2. Raisin pudding model.
3. Watermelon model.

## 18. Success and Failure of Thomson Model

Success:

1. Explained electrical neutrality of atom.

Failure:

1. Could not explain Rutherford's alpha-particle scattering results.
2. Incorrectly assumed positive charge and mass were spread uniformly.

## 19. X-Rays and Radioactivity Context

Around the same period, scientists discovered:

1. X-rays.
2. Radioactivity.
3. Alpha rays.
4. Beta rays.
5. Gamma rays.

Alpha particles are positively charged helium nuclei.

Beta rays are streams of negatively charged particles.

Gamma rays are high-energy electromagnetic radiation.

These discoveries helped develop atomic structure.

## 20. Rutherford's Alpha-Particle Scattering Experiment

Rutherford and his students Geiger and Marsden bombarded a thin gold foil with alpha particles.

Expected according to Thomson model:

1. Alpha particles should pass through with small deflections.

Actual observations:

1. Most alpha particles passed through undeflected.
2. Some were deflected by small angles.
3. Very few bounced back, nearly 180 degrees.

## 21. Rutherford's Conclusions

Rutherford concluded:

1. Most space in atom is empty.
2. Positive charge is concentrated in a very small region.
3. Most mass is concentrated in that small region.
4. That small region is the nucleus.
5. Electrons move around the nucleus.

Size comparison:

```text
atomic radius about 10^-10 m
nuclear radius about 10^-15 m
```

## 22. Rutherford Nuclear Model

According to Rutherford:

1. Atom has a tiny positively charged nucleus.
2. Most mass is in the nucleus.
3. Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular paths called orbits.
4. Electrons and nucleus are held by electrostatic attraction.
5. Atom is mostly empty space.

## 23. Atomic Number

Atomic number `Z` is the number of protons in the nucleus.

For a neutral atom:

$$
Z = number \ of \ protons = number \ of \ electrons
$$

Examples:

1. Hydrogen has `Z = 1`.
2. Sodium has `Z = 11`.

## 24. Mass Number

Mass number `A` is the total number of nucleons.

Nucleons are protons and neutrons.

$$
A = Z + n
$$

where `n` is number of neutrons.

Therefore:

$$
n = A - Z
$$

## 25. Atomic Symbol

An atom is represented as:

$$
{}^A_ZX
$$

where:

1. `X` is element symbol.
2. `A` is mass number.
3. `Z` is atomic number.

## 26. Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with:

1. Same atomic number.
2. Different mass number.
3. Different number of neutrons.

Examples:

1. Protium, deuterium, and tritium are isotopes of hydrogen.
2. Carbon-12, carbon-13, and carbon-14 are isotopes of carbon.
3. Chlorine-35 and chlorine-37 are isotopes of chlorine.

Isotopes have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of electrons.

## 27. Isobars

Isobars are atoms with:

1. Same mass number.
2. Different atomic number.

Example:

```text
carbon-14 and nitrogen-14
```

## 28. Isotopes and Isobars Compared

| Basis | Isotopes | Isobars |
|---|---|---|
| Atomic number | Same | Different |
| Mass number | Different | Same |
| Element | Same element | Different elements |
| Chemical properties | Similar | Different |
| Reason | Same electrons | Different protons and electrons |

## 29. Worked Example: Protons, Neutrons, Electrons

For bromine atom represented as `Br-80` with `Z = 35`:

```text
protons = 35
electrons = 35 for neutral atom
neutrons = 80 - 35 = 45
```

## 30. Worked Example: Ion Symbol

Given:

```text
electrons = 18
protons = 16
neutrons = 16
```

Atomic number is `16`, so element is sulphur, `S`.

Mass number:

```text
A = 16 + 16 = 32
```

Since electrons exceed protons by 2, charge is `2-`.

Symbol:

```text
S^2- with mass number 32 and atomic number 16
```

## 31. Drawbacks of Rutherford Model

Rutherford model failed because:

1. It could not explain atom stability.
2. An accelerating charged electron should emit radiation.
3. If electron loses energy continuously, it should spiral into nucleus.
4. Calculations showed collapse should happen in about `10^-8 s`.
5. Real atoms are stable.
6. It did not explain electron distribution.
7. It did not explain atomic spectra.

## 32. Electromagnetic Radiation

Electromagnetic radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

Properties:

1. It travels as waves.
2. Electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular to each other.
3. Both are perpendicular to direction of propagation.
4. It does not need a medium.
5. It travels in vacuum at speed of light.

Speed of light:

$$
c = 3.0 x 10^8 \ m \ s^{-1}
$$

## 33. Wavelength, Frequency, and Wavenumber

Wavelength, `lambda`, is distance between two successive crests or troughs.

Frequency, `nu`, is number of waves passing a point per second.

Wavenumber is number of wavelengths per unit length.

Relations:

$$
c = \nu \lambda
$$

$$
\bar{\nu} = 1/\lambda
$$

Common units:

1. Frequency: `Hz` or `s^-1`.
2. Wavelength: `m`, `nm`, `pm`, `Angstrom`.
3. Wavenumber: `m^-1` or `cm^-1`.

## 34. Electromagnetic Spectrum

Electromagnetic spectrum includes, in increasing frequency:

1. Radio waves.
2. Microwaves.
3. Infrared.
4. Visible light.
5. Ultraviolet.
6. X-rays.
7. Gamma rays.

Visible light is only a small part of the full spectrum.

## 35. Worked Example: Sodium Yellow Light

Yellow sodium light has wavelength `580 nm`.

Convert:

```text
580 nm = 580 x 10^-9 m = 5.80 x 10^-7 m
```

Frequency:

$$
\nu = c/\lambda = (3.0 x 10^8)/(5.80 x 10^{-7})
$$

$$
\nu = 5.17 x 10^{14} \ s^{-1}
$$

Wavenumber:

$$
\bar{\nu} = 1/(5.80 x 10^{-7}) = 1.72 x 10^6 \ m^{-1}
$$

or:

$$
1.72 x 10^4 \ cm^{-1}
$$

## 36. Black Body Radiation

A black body is an ideal body that absorbs and emits all frequencies of radiation.

Observations:

1. Hot objects emit radiation.
2. Intensity depends on temperature.
3. As temperature increases, maximum emission shifts to shorter wavelength.
4. Classical wave theory could not fully explain this distribution.

Planck solved the problem by proposing quantised energy.

## 37. Planck's Quantum Theory

Planck proposed that energy is emitted or absorbed in discrete packets called quanta.

Energy of one quantum:

$$
E = h\nu
$$

where:

1. `h` is Planck constant.
2. `nu` is frequency.

Planck constant:

$$
h = 6.626 x 10^{-34} \ J \ s
$$

Using wavelength:

$$
E = hc/\lambda
$$

## 38. Quantisation

Quantisation means a property can have only certain allowed values, not all continuous values.

Energy values can be:

$$
E = 0, h\nu, 2h\nu, 3h\nu, ... , nh\nu
$$

This is like standing on steps of a staircase: you can stand on a step, but not between steps.

## 39. Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric effect is the ejection of electrons from a metal surface when light of suitable frequency falls on it.

Main observations:

1. Electrons are ejected immediately.
2. Number of electrons ejected depends on light intensity.
3. Each metal has threshold frequency.
4. Below threshold frequency, no electrons are ejected.
5. Kinetic energy of ejected electrons depends on frequency, not intensity.

## 40. Einstein Explanation of Photoelectric Effect

Einstein explained that light consists of photons.

Energy of photon:

$$
E = h\nu
$$

Minimum energy needed to remove electron:

$$
W_0 = h\nu_0
$$

Kinetic energy of emitted electron:

$$
KE = h\nu - h\nu_0
$$

Also:

$$
KE = (1/2)m_ev^2
$$

## 41. Work Function and Threshold Frequency

Work function is the minimum energy required to eject an electron from a metal.

$$
W_0 = h\nu_0
$$

If `nu < nu_0`, no photoelectron is emitted.

If `nu > nu_0`, extra energy becomes kinetic energy.

## 42. Dual Behaviour of Electromagnetic Radiation

Light has dual behaviour:

1. Wave nature explains interference and diffraction.
2. Particle nature explains black body radiation and photoelectric effect.

When radiation propagates, wave nature is observed.

When radiation interacts with matter, particle nature is observed.

## 43. Worked Example: Energy of One Mole of Photons

Given:

```text
nu = 5.0 x 10^14 s^-1
```

Energy of one photon:

$$
E = h\nu = 6.626 x 10^{-34} x 5.0 x 10^{14}
$$

$$
E = 3.313 x 10^{-19} \ J
$$

Energy of one mole of photons:

$$
3.313 x 10^{-19} x 6.022 x 10^{23}
$$

$$
= 1.995 x 10^5 \ J \ mol^{-1} = 199.5 \ kJ \ mol^{-1}
$$

## 44. Atomic Spectra

When atoms absorb energy, electrons move to higher energy states.

When electrons return to lower energy states, radiation is emitted.

The emitted radiation forms a spectrum.

Types:

1. Continuous spectrum.
2. Line spectrum.

## 45. Continuous Spectrum

A continuous spectrum contains all wavelengths in a region.

Example:

White light passing through a prism gives a continuous visible spectrum from violet to red.

## 46. Line Spectrum

Line spectrum contains only specific wavelengths.

Atomic gases give line spectra.

Each element has a unique line spectrum, like a fingerprint.

This helps identify elements.

## 47. Emission and Absorption Spectra

Emission spectrum:

1. Formed when excited atoms emit radiation.
2. Shows bright lines on dark background.

Absorption spectrum:

1. Formed when atoms absorb selected wavelengths from continuous radiation.
2. Shows dark lines in a bright spectrum.

Absorption spectrum is like the negative of emission spectrum.

## 48. Hydrogen Spectrum

Hydrogen has the simplest atomic spectrum.

When excited hydrogen atoms emit radiation, spectral lines appear in different series.

Important series:

| Series | Final level `n1` | Region |
|---|---:|---|
| Lyman | 1 | Ultraviolet |
| Balmer | 2 | Visible |
| Paschen | 3 | Infrared |
| Brackett | 4 | Infrared |
| Pfund | 5 | Infrared |

## 49. Rydberg Formula

For hydrogen spectrum:

$$
\bar{\nu} = R_H(1/n_1^2 - 1/n_2^2)
$$

where:

1. `n2 > n1`.
2. `R_H = 109677 cm^-1`.

For Balmer series:

```text
n1 = 2 and n2 = 3, 4, 5, ...
```

## 50. Bohr Model of Hydrogen Atom

Bohr improved Rutherford's model using quantisation.

Main postulates:

1. Electron moves in certain allowed circular orbits.
2. Electron in allowed orbit does not radiate energy.
3. Each orbit has fixed energy.
4. Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when electron jumps between orbits.
5. Angular momentum of electron is quantised.

## 51. Bohr Angular Momentum Condition

Bohr proposed:

$$
mvr = nh/(2\pi)
$$

where:

1. `m` is electron mass.
2. `v` is velocity.
3. `r` is orbit radius.
4. `n` is principal quantum number.

## 52. Bohr Energy Formula

For hydrogen-like species:

$$
E_n = -2.18 x 10^{-18}(Z^2/n^2) \ J \ atom^{-1}
$$

For hydrogen, `Z = 1`:

$$
E_n = -2.18 x 10^{-18}/n^2 \ J \ atom^{-1}
$$

Negative energy means electron is bound to nucleus.

## 53. Bohr Radius Formula

For hydrogen-like species:

$$
r_n = 0.0529(n^2/Z) \ nm
$$

For hydrogen first orbit:

$$
r_1 = 0.0529 \ nm
$$

## 54. Energy Change in Bohr Model

When electron moves from `ni` to `nf`:

$$
\Delta E = E_f - E_i
$$

If electron falls to lower level, energy is emitted.

Frequency of radiation:

$$
\nu = |\Delta E|/h
$$

Wavelength:

$$
\lambda = c/\nu
$$

## 55. Meaning of Negative Energy

Energy of electron in hydrogen atom is negative because electron is attracted to nucleus and is bound.

When `n = infinity`, energy is zero and electron is free.

Ionisation means removing electron completely, taking it to `n = infinity`.

## 56. Limitations of Bohr Model

Bohr model failed because:

1. It could explain hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions, but not multi-electron atoms.
2. It could not explain fine structure of spectral lines.
3. It could not explain Zeeman effect.
4. It could not explain Stark effect.
5. It could not explain chemical bonding.
6. It ignored wave nature of electron.
7. It contradicted Heisenberg uncertainty principle by using definite orbits.

## 57. de Broglie Dual Behaviour of Matter

de Broglie proposed that matter also has dual behaviour.

Particles such as electrons have both:

1. Particle nature.
2. Wave nature.

de Broglie wavelength:

$$
\lambda = h/(mv)
$$

or:

$$
\lambda = h/p
$$

where `p = mv` is momentum.

## 58. Why Electron Waves Matter

Wave nature is important for microscopic particles such as electrons because their mass is very small.

For large objects like balls, wavelength is too small to detect.

Electron diffraction confirmed wave nature of electrons.

Electron microscope works because electrons show wave behaviour.

## 59. Worked Example: de Broglie Wavelength of Electron

Given:

```text
m = 9.1 x 10^-31 kg
v = 1.6 x 10^6 m s^-1
```

$$
\lambda = h/(mv)
$$

$$
\lambda = (6.626 x 10^{-34})/(9.1 x 10^{-31} x 1.6 x 10^6)
$$

$$
\lambda = 4.55 x 10^{-10} \ m
$$

## 60. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Heisenberg stated that exact position and exact momentum of a microscopic particle cannot be determined simultaneously.

$$
\Delta x \Delta p \ge h/(4\pi)
$$

Since `p = mv`:

$$
\Delta x \ m \Delta v \ge h/(4\pi)
$$

## 61. Meaning of Uncertainty Principle

If position is measured very accurately, momentum becomes more uncertain.

If momentum is measured very accurately, position becomes more uncertain.

For electrons, this uncertainty is significant.

For macroscopic objects, it is negligible.

## 62. Why Bohr Orbits Fail

Bohr orbit is a definite path.

To define a path, position and velocity must both be known exactly.

Heisenberg principle says this is impossible for electrons.

Therefore, electrons cannot be described as moving in fixed circular orbits.

## 63. Quantum Mechanical Model

Quantum mechanics considers:

1. Wave nature of matter.
2. Particle nature of matter.
3. Uncertainty principle.

Schrodinger equation gives allowed energy levels and wave functions for electrons.

The wave function is represented by `psi`.

`psi` itself has no direct physical meaning.

`psi^2` gives probability density.

## 64. Atomic Orbital

An atomic orbital is a one-electron wave function for an electron in an atom.

In simple learner language:

```text
An orbital is a region around the nucleus where the probability of finding an electron is high.
```

Orbit is not the same as orbital.

## 65. Orbit and Orbital Compared

| Basis | Orbit | Orbital |
|---|---|---|
| Model | Bohr model | Quantum mechanical model |
| Meaning | Fixed circular path | Probability region |
| Electron path | Definite | Not definite |
| Basis | Classical picture | Wave function |
| Validity | Limited | Modern atomic model |

## 66. Main Features of Quantum Mechanical Model

1. Electron energy is quantised.
2. Energy levels arise from wave nature of electrons.
3. Exact path of electron cannot be known.
4. Only probability of finding electron can be described.
5. Orbital is described by quantum numbers.
6. Each orbital can hold maximum two electrons.
7. `psi^2` gives probability density.
8. In multi-electron atoms, orbital energy depends on `n` and `l`.

## 67. Quantum Numbers

An electron in an atom is described by four quantum numbers:

1. Principal quantum number, `n`.
2. Azimuthal quantum number, `l`.
3. Magnetic quantum number, `m_l`.
4. Spin quantum number, `m_s`.

## 68. Principal Quantum Number

Principal quantum number `n`:

1. Has values `1, 2, 3, ...`.
2. Identifies the shell.
3. Determines size of orbital.
4. Determines energy in hydrogen-like atoms.
5. Number of orbitals in a shell is `n^2`.
6. Maximum electrons in shell is `2n^2`.

Shell names:

| n | Shell |
|---:|---|
| 1 | K |
| 2 | L |
| 3 | M |
| 4 | N |

## 69. Azimuthal Quantum Number

Azimuthal quantum number `l`:

1. Defines subshell.
2. Determines shape of orbital.
3. Has values from `0` to `n - 1`.

Subshell symbols:

| l | Subshell |
|---:|---|
| 0 | s |
| 1 | p |
| 2 | d |
| 3 | f |
| 4 | g |

## 70. Magnetic Quantum Number

Magnetic quantum number `m_l` gives orientation of orbital.

For a given `l`, possible values are:

$$
m_l = -l, ..., 0, ..., +l
$$

Number of orbitals in a subshell:

$$
2l + 1
$$

Examples:

| Subshell | l | Possible `m_l` values | Number of orbitals |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| s | 0 | 0 | 1 |
| p | 1 | -1, 0, +1 | 3 |
| d | 2 | -2, -1, 0, +1, +2 | 5 |
| f | 3 | -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3 | 7 |

## 71. Spin Quantum Number

Spin quantum number `m_s` describes electron spin orientation.

Possible values:

$$
m_s = +1/2 \ or \ -1/2
$$

Two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins.

## 72. Quantum Number Rules

For any electron:

1. `n = 1, 2, 3, ...`.
2. `l = 0` to `n - 1`.
3. `m_l = -l` to `+l`.
4. `m_s = +1/2` or `-1/2`.

Invalid examples:

1. `n = 0` is impossible.
2. `l = n` is impossible.
3. `m_l` cannot be outside `-l` to `+l`.
4. `m_s` cannot be anything other than `+1/2` or `-1/2`.

## 73. Shapes of Orbitals

Orbital shape is described by probability density.

Boundary surface diagrams show regions where electron is likely to be found.

No finite boundary contains 100 percent probability, so diagrams usually show high probability regions, such as about 90 percent.

## 74. Shape of s Orbitals

s orbitals:

1. Are spherical.
2. Have `l = 0`.
3. Have one orientation.
4. Increase in size as `n` increases.

Size order:

```text
4s > 3s > 2s > 1s
```

## 75. Shape of p Orbitals

p orbitals:

1. Have `l = 1`.
2. Have three orientations.
3. Are dumbbell-shaped.
4. Are designated `p_x`, `p_y`, and `p_z`.
5. Have one angular node.

## 76. Shape of d Orbitals

d orbitals:

1. Have `l = 2`.
2. Have five orientations.
3. Include `d_xy`, `d_yz`, `d_xz`, `d_x2-y2`, and `d_z2`.
4. Generally have clover-like shapes, except `d_z2`.

## 77. Nodes

Nodes are regions where probability density is zero.

Total nodes:

$$
total \ nodes = n - 1
$$

Angular nodes:

$$
angular \ nodes = l
$$

Radial nodes:

$$
radial \ nodes = n - l - 1
$$

Examples:

1. `2s`: total nodes = 1, angular nodes = 0, radial nodes = 1.
2. `2p`: total nodes = 1, angular nodes = 1, radial nodes = 0.
3. `3d`: total nodes = 2, angular nodes = 2, radial nodes = 0.

## 78. Energies of Orbitals in Hydrogen

For hydrogen and hydrogen-like species, orbital energy depends only on `n`.

Energy order:

```text
1s < 2s = 2p < 3s = 3p = 3d < 4s = 4p = 4d = 4f
```

Orbitals with same energy are called degenerate orbitals.

## 79. Energies of Orbitals in Multi-Electron Atoms

In multi-electron atoms, energy depends on:

1. Principal quantum number `n`.
2. Azimuthal quantum number `l`.

For same shell:

```text
s < p < d < f
```

This happens because of electron-electron repulsion, shielding, and effective nuclear charge.

## 80. Shielding and Effective Nuclear Charge

Inner electrons reduce the attraction between nucleus and outer electrons.

This is called shielding.

The net positive charge experienced by an outer electron is effective nuclear charge.

Higher effective nuclear charge means electron is held more strongly.

## 81. The n Plus l Rule

The `n + l` rule predicts relative orbital energy.

Rules:

1. Lower `n + l` means lower energy.
2. If two orbitals have same `n + l`, lower `n` has lower energy.

Examples:

| Orbital | n | l | n + l |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 4s | 4 | 0 | 4 |
| 3d | 3 | 2 | 5 |

So `4s` fills before `3d`.

## 82. Aufbau Principle

Aufbau means building up.

The principle states:

```text
In ground state, electrons fill orbitals in increasing order of energy.
```

Common filling order:

```text
1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s
```

## 83. Pauli Exclusion Principle

Pauli exclusion principle states:

```text
No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
```

Simpler form:

```text
One orbital can hold maximum two electrons, and they must have opposite spins.
```

## 84. Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity

Hund's rule states:

```text
Pairing of electrons in degenerate orbitals does not occur until each orbital has one electron.
```

For p orbitals:

1. First electron enters one p orbital.
2. Second enters another p orbital.
3. Third enters the third p orbital.
4. Pairing starts with the fourth electron.

Shell capacity:

$$

$$

Examples:

2. L shell, `n = 2`: `8` electrons.

## 87. Noble Gas Notation

Noble gas notation uses previous noble gas as core.

Examples:

1. Sodium: `[Ne] 3s^1`.

## 88. Core and Valence Electrons

Core electrons are inner-shell electrons.

Valence electrons are electrons in outermost shell or involved in chemical behaviour.

Example:

For sodium `[Ne] 3s^1`:

1. `[Ne]` represents core electrons.
2. `3s^1` is valence electron.

## 92. Calculation Pattern: Photon Energy

To calculate photon energy:

1. If frequency is given, use `E = h nu`.
2. If wavelength is given, use `E = hc/lambda`.
3. Convert wavelength to metre.
4. For one mole of photons, multiply by Avogadro constant.

## 93. Calculation Pattern: Photoelectric Effect

Use:

$$
KE = h\nu - W_0
$$

If work function is in eV, convert photon energy to eV or convert work function to joules.

Conversion:

$$
1 \ eV = 1.602 x 10^{-19} \ J
$$

Velocity:

$$

## 94. Calculation Pattern: Bohr Transition

For hydrogen-like species:

1. Use `E_n = -2.18 x 10^-18 (Z^2/n^2)`.
2. Calculate `E_i` and `E_f`.
3. Find `Delta E = E_f - E_i`.
4. Use magnitude for photon energy.
5. Use `nu = E/h`.
6. Use `lambda = c/nu`.

## 95. Calculation Pattern: Quantum Numbers

For a given `n`:

1. Possible `l` values: `0` to `n - 1`.
2. For each `l`, possible `m_l` values: `-l` to `+l`.
3. For each orbital, possible `m_s`: `+1/2` or `-1/2`.

Example for `n = 3`:

```text

3s has 1 orbital
3p has 3 orbitals
3d has 5 orbitals
total orbitals = 9 = n^2
```

## 109. Exam Answer: Discovery of Electron

Electron was discovered through cathode ray discharge tube experiments. At low pressure and high voltage, rays moved from cathode to anode. These rays travelled in straight lines, produced fluorescence, and were deflected by electric and magnetic fields as negatively charged particles. Their properties did not depend on gas or electrode material, proving that electrons are fundamental constituents of all atoms.

## 110. Exam Answer: Rutherford Model

Rutherford bombarded thin gold foil with alpha particles. Most particles passed undeflected, some deflected slightly, and very few bounced back. He concluded that most of the atom is empty space, positive charge and most mass are concentrated in a tiny nucleus, and electrons revolve around the nucleus. This led to the nuclear model of atom.

## 111. Exam Answer: Failure of Rutherford Model

According to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating charged electron should emit energy. An electron moving around the nucleus should therefore lose energy and spiral into the nucleus, making atom unstable. Real atoms are stable. Rutherford's model also did not explain distribution and energies of electrons or atomic spectra.

## 112. Exam Answer: Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric effect is emission of electrons from a metal surface when radiation of suitable frequency falls on it. Electrons are emitted instantly, their number depends on intensity, and their kinetic energy depends on frequency. A minimum threshold frequency is required. Einstein explained it by treating light as photons, each with energy `h nu`. Energy beyond work function becomes kinetic energy of the photoelectron.

## 113. Exam Answer: Bohr Model

Bohr proposed that electrons move only in certain permitted circular orbits around nucleus. An electron in an allowed orbit does not radiate energy. Each orbit has definite energy. Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when electron jumps between orbits. The angular momentum of electron is quantised as `mvr = nh/(2pi)`. Bohr model explained hydrogen spectrum but failed for multi-electron atoms.

## 114. Exam Answer: de Broglie and Heisenberg

de Broglie proposed that moving particles have wave nature and wavelength `lambda = h/mv`. This was confirmed by electron diffraction. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle states that exact position and exact momentum of an electron cannot be known simultaneously. These ideas showed that fixed Bohr orbits are not valid and led to the quantum mechanical model of atom.

## 115. Exam Answer: Quantum Numbers

Four quantum numbers describe an electron in an atom. Principal quantum number `n` gives shell, size, and energy. Azimuthal quantum number `l` gives subshell and shape. Magnetic quantum number `m_l` gives orbital orientation. Spin quantum number `m_s` gives spin orientation and has values `+1/2` or `-1/2`. Together they describe the state of an electron.

## 116. Exam Answer: Aufbau, Pauli, and Hund Rules

Aufbau principle states that electrons fill orbitals in increasing order of energy. Pauli exclusion principle states that no two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers; one orbital can contain maximum two electrons with opposite spins. Hund's rule states that degenerate orbitals are singly occupied before pairing begins.

## 124. End Summary

Atoms are made of electrons, protons, and neutrons. Cathode ray experiments revealed electrons, canal ray experiments led to protons, and Chadwick discovered neutrons. Thomson proposed a positive sphere with embedded electrons, but Rutherford's gold foil experiment showed that atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus and mostly empty space. Rutherford's model could not explain stability or spectra. Electromagnetic radiation has wave properties described by wavelength and frequency and particle properties described by photons. Planck's quantum theory and Einstein's photoelectric explanation introduced quantised energy. Hydrogen line spectrum led Bohr to propose quantised orbits, but Bohr's model failed for complex atoms. de Broglie's matter waves and Heisenberg's uncertainty principle led to the quantum mechanical model, where electrons occupy orbitals described by quantum numbers. Electronic configurations follow Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule, with extra stability for half-filled and fully-filled subshells.
