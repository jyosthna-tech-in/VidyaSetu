## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains why India has such varied physical features.

India contains young folded mountains, old stable plateaus, deep alluvial plains, deserts, coastal plains, and island groups. These features did not appear suddenly. They are the result of geological structure, plate movements, endogenic forces, exogenic processes, erosion, deposition, uplift, faulting, and long periods of landform development.

The central idea is:

> India's physiography is the visible result of its geological structure, geomorphic processes, and stage of landform development.

# 1. Geological Background

## 1.1 Earth Has A Long History

The earth and its landforms have developed over a very long time.

Modern geological understanding places the age of the earth at about 4,600 million years, or 4.6 billion years.

During this long history, the surface and subsurface features of the earth have changed again and again.

## 1.2 Forces That Shape Landforms

Two broad types of forces shape landforms:

1. endogenic forces,
2. exogenic forces.

## 1.3 Endogenic Forces

Endogenic forces originate inside the earth.

They produce:

- folding,
- faulting,
- uplift,
- subsidence,
- volcanism,
- earthquakes,
- plate movement.

Endogenic forces are responsible for large-scale relief features such as mountains, plateaus, rift valleys, and faults.

## 1.4 Exogenic Forces

Exogenic forces operate on the earth's surface.

They include:

- weathering,
- erosion,
- transportation,
- deposition,
- river action,
- wind action,
- glacial action,
- marine action.

Exogenic forces wear down highlands and fill lowlands with sediments.

## 1.5 Indian Plate Movement

Millions of years ago, the Indian Plate was located south of the equator.

It was part of a larger Indo-Australian Plate.

Over time:

- the Australian Plate moved toward the southeast,
- the Indian Plate moved northward.

This northward movement is still continuing.

## 1.6 Consequences Of Indian Plate Movement

The northward movement of the Indian Plate had major consequences:

- collision with the Eurasian Plate,
- formation and uplift of the Himalayas,
- folding and faulting in northern India,
- creation of the Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra depression,
- frequent seismic activity in some regions,
- development of complex relief in the Indian subcontinent.

# 2. India's Three Geological Divisions

India can be divided into three broad geological divisions:

1. the Peninsular Block,
2. the Himalayas and other mountains,
3. the Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain.

These geological divisions broadly correspond with India's physical features.

---

# 3. The Peninsular Block

## 3.1 Location And Boundary

The northern boundary of the Peninsular Block may be taken as an irregular line:

- from Kachchh,
- along the western flank of the Aravali Range near Delhi,
- roughly parallel to the Yamuna and Ganga,
- up to the Rajmahal Hills and the Ganga delta.

Extensions of this block are also found in:

- Karbi Anglong,
- Meghalaya Plateau,
- Rajasthan.

The northeastern extension is separated from the Chotanagpur Plateau by the Malda fault in West Bengal.

## 3.2 Ancient Rocks

The Peninsular Block is made mainly of very ancient rocks such as:

- gneisses,
- granites.

It is one of the oldest parts of India.

## 3.3 Stable And Rigid Character

Since the Cambrian period, the Peninsular Block has behaved like a rigid block.

It is much more stable than the Himalayas.

However, stability does not mean there has been no change.

Some parts have experienced:

- vertical movements,
- block faulting,
- uplift,
- submergence,
- fractures.

## 3.4 Rift Valleys And Block Mountains

Examples of tectonic activity in the Peninsular Block include:

- Narmada rift valley,
- Tapi rift valley,
- Mahanadi rift-related valley,
- Satpura block mountains.

## 3.5 Relict And Residual Mountains

The Peninsular Block contains old mountains reduced by erosion.

Examples include:

- Aravali Hills,
- Nallamala Hills,
- Javadi Hills,
- Veliconda Hills,
- Palkonda Range,
- Mahendragiri Hills.

These are not young fold mountains like the Himalayas. They are older, worn down, and more stable.

## 3.6 River Valleys

River valleys in the Peninsular Block are generally:

- shallow,
- low-gradient,
- mature compared with Himalayan rivers.

Most east-flowing Peninsular rivers form deltas before entering the Bay of Bengal.

Important deltas are formed by:

- Mahanadi,
- Godavari,
- Krishna,
- Kaveri.

---

# 4. Himalayas And Other Mountains

## 4.1 Geological Character

The Himalayas are young, weak, and flexible in geological structure.

They are different from the old, rigid Peninsular Block.

## 4.2 Tectonic Origin

The Himalayas are tectonic mountains.

They were formed because of plate movement and collision.

They are still affected by:

- folding,
- faulting,
- thrusting,
- earthquakes,
- landslides,
- river erosion.

## 4.3 Youthful Landforms

Himalayan rivers are fast-flowing and often in a youthful stage.

Common landforms include:

- gorges,
- V-shaped valleys,
- rapids,
- waterfalls,
- steep slopes.

These features show active erosion and youthful topography.

---

# 5. Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain

## 5.1 Formation

The Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain was originally a large geosynclinal depression.

It developed strongly during the later phases of Himalayan mountain formation.

Over time, the depression was filled by sediments brought by:

- Himalayan rivers,
- Peninsular rivers.

## 5.2 Alluvial Depth

The average depth of alluvial deposits in these plains ranges from:

```text
1,000 m to 2,000 m
```

This makes it one of the world's great alluvial plains.

## 5.3 Importance

The plain is important because it has:

- fertile alluvial soils,
- large river systems,
- dense population,
- intensive agriculture,
- major settlements,
- transport corridors.

---

# 6. Meaning Of Physiography

Physiography means the physical form and relief of an area.

It is the outcome of:

1. structure,
2. process,
3. stage of development.

## 6.1 Structure

Structure refers to geology:

- rocks,
- folds,
- faults,
- plates,
- formations.

## 6.2 Process

Process refers to forces acting on the land:

- erosion,
- deposition,
- weathering,
- tectonic movement,
- river action,
- wind action,
- marine action.

## 6.3 Stage

Stage refers to the level of development of a landform.

A youthful river valley looks different from a mature river valley.

A young fold mountain looks different from an old eroded plateau.

---

# 7. Physiographic Divisions Of India

India can be divided into six major physiographic divisions:

1. Northern and Northeastern Mountains
2. Northern Plain
3. Peninsular Plateau
4. Indian Desert
5. Coastal Plains
6. Islands

## 7.1 Quick Comparison

| Division | Main Character |
| --- | --- |
| Northern and Northeastern Mountains | Young folded mountains and eastern hill systems. |
| Northern Plain | Alluvial plain formed by Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra systems. |
| Peninsular Plateau | Old stable plateau with residual hills, rift valleys, and mineral resources. |
| Indian Desert | Arid region with dunes, ephemeral streams, and inland drainage. |
| Coastal Plains | Western submerged coast and eastern emergent coast. |
| Islands | Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea island groups. |

---

# 8. Northern And Northeastern Mountains

## 8.1 General Features

This division includes:

- Himalayas,
- Northeastern hills.

The Himalayas consist of parallel mountain ranges.

Important ranges include:

- Greater Himalayas,
- Lesser Himalayas,
- Shiwaliks.

## 8.2 Orientation Of Ranges

The orientation of Himalayan ranges changes regionally:

| Region | General Direction |
| --- | --- |
| Northwestern India | Northwest to southeast |
| Darjiling and Sikkim | East-west |
| Arunachal Pradesh | Southwest to northeast |
| Nagaland, Manipur, Mizoram | North-south |

## 8.3 Size Of Great Himalayas

The Great Himalayan range, also called the central axial range, is about:

```text
2,500 km long from east to west
```

Its width varies roughly between:

```text
160 km and 400 km from north to south
```

## 8.4 Himalayas As A Divide

The Himalayas are not only a physical barrier.

They are also:

- a climatic divide,
- a drainage divide,
- a cultural divide.

They influence monsoon winds, river systems, settlement, biodiversity, and historical interaction.

---

# 9. Regional Divisions Of The Himalayas

The Himalayas can be divided into five regional sections:

1. Kashmir or Northwestern Himalayas
2. Himachal and Uttarakhand Himalayas
3. Darjiling and Sikkim Himalayas
4. Arunachal Himalayas
5. Eastern Hills and Mountains

---

# 10. Kashmir Or Northwestern Himalayas

## 10.1 Main Ranges

This region includes:

- Karakoram,
- Ladakh,
- Zaskar,
- Pir Panjal.

## 10.2 Cold Desert

The northeastern part of the Kashmir Himalayas is a cold desert.

It lies between:

- Greater Himalayas,
- Karakoram Range.

## 10.3 Kashmir Valley

Between the Great Himalayas and Pir Panjal lies the famous Kashmir Valley.

Dal Lake is located in this region.

Srinagar lies on the banks of the Jhelum River.

## 10.4 Glaciers

Important glaciers of South Asia are found here:

- Baltoro,
- Siachen.

## 10.5 Karewa Formations

Karewas are thick deposits of glacial clay and other material embedded with moraines.

They are important for cultivation of saffron, locally called Zafran.

## 10.6 Passes

Important passes include:

| Pass | Range |
| --- | --- |
| Zoji La | Great Himalayas |
| Banihal | Pir Panjal |
| Photu La | Zaskar |
| Khardung La | Ladakh |

## 10.7 Lakes

Freshwater lakes include:

- Dal,
- Wular.

Salt water lakes include:

- Pangong Tso,
- Tso Moriri.

## 10.8 Drainage

This region is drained by:

- Indus,
- Jhelum,
- Chenab.

## 10.9 Duns

The southernmost part contains longitudinal valleys called duns.

Examples:

- Jammu Dun,
- Pathankot Dun.

# 11. Himachal And Uttarakhand Himalayas

## 11.1 Location

This section lies approximately between:

- Ravi River in the west,
- Kali River in the east.

The Kali is a tributary of the Ghaghara.

## 11.2 Drainage

This region is drained by two major river systems:

- Indus system,
- Ganga system.

Indus tributaries include:

- Ravi,
- Beas,
- Satluj.

Ganga tributaries include:

- Yamuna,
- Ghaghara.

## 11.3 Mountain Ranges

All three Himalayan ranges are prominent:

- Great Himalayas,
- Lesser Himalayas,
- Shiwaliks.

The Lesser Himalayas are locally known as:

- Dhauladhar in Himachal Pradesh,
- Nag Tibba in Uttarakhand.

## 11.4 Hill Stations

The altitude belt of about 1,000 m to 2,000 m attracted the British colonial administration.

Important hill stations and cantonment towns include:

- Dharamshala,
- Mussoorie,
- Shimla,
- Kausani,
- Kasauli,
- Almora,
- Lansdowne,
- Ranikhet.

## 11.5 Duns

Important duns in this region include:

- Chandigarh-Kalka Dun,
- Nalagarh Dun,
- Dehra Dun,
- Harike Dun,
- Kota Dun.

Dehra Dun is the largest, about:

```text
35-45 km long and 22-25 km wide
```

## 11.6 People And Pastoral Movement

In the Great Himalayan range, valleys are inhabited by groups such as the Bhotias.

They traditionally migrate to high summer grasslands called Bugyals during summer and return to valleys in winter.

## 11.7 Pilgrimage And Tourism

Important places include:

- Valley of Flowers,
- Gangotri,
- Yamunotri,
- Kedarnath,
- Badrinath,
- Hemkund Sahib.

The region is also known for important river confluences called Prayags.

---

# 12. Darjiling And Sikkim Himalayas

## 12.1 Location

This section is flanked by:

- Nepal Himalayas in the west,
- Bhutan Himalayas in the east.

It is small in area but very significant.

## 12.2 Physical Features

Important features include:

- fast-flowing Tista River,
- high peaks such as Kanchenjunga,
- deep valleys,
- scenic landscapes.

## 12.3 People

The higher reaches are inhabited by Lepcha communities.

The southern part, especially Darjiling Himalayas, has a mixed population including:

- Nepalis,
- Bengalis,
- tribal groups from Central India.

## 12.4 Tea Plantations

The British introduced tea plantations here because of:

- moderate slopes,
- thick soil cover,
- high organic content,
- well-distributed rainfall,
- mild winters.

## 12.5 Absence Of Shiwaliks

Unlike many other Himalayan sections, Shiwalik formations are absent here.

Instead, duar formations are important.

These duars have also supported tea gardens.

## 12.6 Biodiversity

Sikkim and Darjiling Himalayas are known for:

- scenic beauty,
- rich flora,
- rich fauna,
- orchids.

---

# 13. Arunachal Himalayas

## 13.1 Location

The Arunachal Himalayas extend from east of the Bhutan Himalayas to the Diphu Pass.

Their general direction is:

```text
southwest to northeast
```

## 13.2 Peaks

Important peaks include:

- Kangtu,
- Namcha Barwa.

## 13.3 Rivers And Gorges

The ranges are cut by fast-flowing rivers from north to south.

Important rivers include:

- Kameng,
- Subansiri,
- Dihang,
- Dibang,
- Lohit.

The Brahmaputra flows through a deep gorge after crossing Namcha Barwa.

## 13.4 Hydroelectric Potential

These rivers are:

- perennial,
- steep,
- fast-flowing.

They have high hydroelectric power potential.

## 13.5 People And Cultivation

Important tribal communities include:

- Monpa,
- Abor,
- Mishmi,
- Nyishi,
- Naga groups.

Many communities practise Jhum cultivation, also called shifting or slash-and-burn cultivation.

## 13.6 Biodiversity And Transport

The region is rich in biodiversity.

Rugged topography makes inter-valley transportation difficult.

Many interactions occur through the duar region along the Arunachal-Assam border.

# 14. Eastern Hills And Mountains

## 14.1 Alignment

These hills are part of the Himalayan mountain system.

Their general alignment is:

```text
north to south
```

## 14.2 Local Names

They are known by different names:

- Patkai Bum,
- Naga Hills,
- Manipur Hills,
- Mizo or Lushai Hills.

## 14.3 People And Land Use

These are low hills inhabited by many tribal communities.

Jhum cultivation is common.

## 14.4 Rivers

The Barak is an important river in Manipur and Mizoram.

Many rivers in Nagaland are tributaries of the Brahmaputra.

Some rivers of Mizoram and Manipur join the Barak, which later becomes part of the Meghna system.

Rivers in eastern Manipur join the Chindwin, a tributary of the Irrawaddy of Myanmar.

## 14.5 Loktak Lake

Manipur has a unique physiography because of Loktak Lake.

It lies near the centre of Manipur and is surrounded by mountains.

## 14.6 Mizoram As Molassis Basin

Mizoram is known as a molassis basin.

It is made of soft, unconsolidated deposits.

# 15. Northern Plains

## 15.1 Formation

The Northern Plains are formed by alluvial deposits brought by:

- Indus,
- Ganga,
- Brahmaputra.

## 15.2 Size

They extend approximately:

```text
3,200 km from east to west
```

Average width:

```text
150-300 km
```

Maximum depth of alluvium:

```text
1,000-2,000 m
```

## 15.3 North-South Zones

From north to south, the plains can be divided into:

1. Bhabar,
2. Tarai,
3. Alluvial plains.

The alluvial plains are further divided into:

1. Bhangar,
2. Khadar.

## 15.4 Bhabar

Bhabar is a narrow belt:

```text
8-10 km wide
```

It lies parallel to the Shiwalik foothills.

Here, rivers descending from mountains deposit heavy materials such as rocks and boulders.

Streams often disappear in this porous belt.

## 15.5 Tarai

South of Bhabar lies the Tarai belt.

Its width is about:

```text
10-20 km
```

Here, streams re-emerge and create marshy and swampy conditions.

The Tarai supports:

- dense natural vegetation,
- varied wildlife,
- moist conditions.

## 15.6 Bhangar And Khadar

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Bhangar | Older alluvial deposits. |
| Khadar | Newer alluvial deposits. |

Khadar is generally renewed by floods and is often very fertile.

## 15.7 Fluvial Features

The Northern Plains show mature river landforms such as:

- sand bars,
- meanders,
- ox-bow lakes,
- braided channels,
- riverine islands,
- deltas.

## 15.8 Brahmaputra Plains

The Brahmaputra plains are known for:

- riverine islands,
- sand bars,
- periodic floods,
- shifting river courses,
- braided streams.

## 15.9 Sunderbans Delta

The mouths of large rivers form huge deltas.

The Sunderbans delta is one of the largest delta regions in the world.

## 15.10 Elevation And Agriculture

The Northern Plains are generally:

```text
50-150 m above mean sea level
```

They have fertile alluvial soils and support crops such as:

- wheat,
- rice,
- sugarcane,
- jute.

This is why they support a very large population.

# 16. Peninsular Plateau

## 16.1 General Character

The Peninsular Plateau rises from about:

```text
150 m above the river plains
```

to elevations of about:

```text
600-900 m
```

It forms an irregular triangular region.

## 16.2 Boundaries

Outer extent includes:

- Delhi Ridge in the northwest,
- Rajmahal Hills in the east,
- Gir Range in the west,
- Cardamom Hills in the south.

The plateau also extends northeast as:

- Shillong Plateau,
- Karbi Anglong Plateau.

## 16.3 Patland Plateaus

The Peninsular Plateau includes many patland plateaus such as:

- Hazaribagh Plateau,
- Palamu Plateau,
- Ranchi Plateau,
- Malwa Plateau,
- Coimbatore Plateau,
- Karnataka Plateau.

## 16.4 Old Stable Landmass

This is one of India's oldest and most stable landmasses.

Its general slope is from west to east, as shown by many east-flowing rivers.

## 16.5 Important Features

Important physiographic features include:

- tors,
- block mountains,
- rift valleys,
- spurs,
- bare rocky structures,
- hummocky hills,
- quartzite dykes.

Some quartzite dykes provide natural sites for water storage.

## 16.6 Black Soil

The western and northwestern parts of the plateau have a strong presence of black soil.

This is important for agriculture, especially cotton in many regions.

## 16.7 Faulting And Ravines

The Peninsular Plateau has undergone uplift, submergence, faulting, and fractures.

The Bhima fault is important because of recurrent seismic activity.

The northwestern part has ravines and gorges.

Examples:

- Chambal ravines,
- Bhind ravines,
- Morena ravines.

## 16.8 Divisions

The Peninsular Plateau is divided into:

1. Deccan Plateau,
2. Central Highlands,
3. Northeastern Plateau.

---

# 17. Deccan Plateau

## 17.1 Boundaries

The Deccan Plateau is bordered by:

- Western Ghats in the west,
- Eastern Ghats in the east,
- Satpura, Maikal Range, and Mahadeo Hills in the north.

## 17.2 Western Ghats

Western Ghats are known by different local names:

- Sahyadri in Maharashtra,
- Nilgiri Hills in Karnataka and Tamil Nadu,
- Anaimalai Hills and Cardamom Hills in Kerala.

They are:

- higher than Eastern Ghats,
- more continuous,
- about 1,500 m average elevation.

Their height generally increases from north to south.

## 17.3 Important Peaks

Important peaks include:

- Anaimudi, 2,695 m, highest peak of the Peninsular Plateau,
- Dodabetta, 2,637 m, in the Nilgiri Hills.

## 17.4 Eastern Ghats

Eastern Ghats are:

- lower,
- discontinuous,
- highly eroded by rivers.

Important rivers cutting across them include:

- Mahanadi,
- Godavari,
- Krishna,
- Kaveri.

Important ranges include:

- Javadi Hills,
- Palconda Range,
- Nallamala Hills,
- Mahendragiri Hills.

## 17.5 Meeting Point

Eastern and Western Ghats meet at the Nilgiri Hills.

---

# 18. Central Highlands

## 18.1 Location

The Central Highlands are bounded by the Aravali Range in the west.

The Satpura Range forms a scarped plateau system to the south and marks the northern boundary of the Deccan Plateau.

## 18.2 Elevation

The general elevation ranges between:

```text
700 m and 1,000 m above mean sea level
```

The region slopes toward the north and northeast.

## 18.3 Relict Mountains

The region contains relict mountains that are highly denuded and discontinuous.

## 18.4 Western Extension

The extension of the Peninsular Plateau reaches as far west as Jaisalmer.

There, it is covered by:

- longitudinal sand ridges,
- crescent-shaped dunes called barchans.

## 18.5 Rocks And Rivers

The region has metamorphic rocks such as:

- marble,
- slate,
- gneiss.

Most tributaries of the Yamuna originate in the Vindhyan and Kaimur ranges.

Banas is an important tributary of Chambal and originates from the Aravalli in the west.

## 18.6 Chotanagpur

The eastern extension includes the Rajmahal Hills.

South of Rajmahal lies the Chotanagpur Plateau, a region rich in mineral resources.

---

# 19. Northeastern Plateau

## 19.1 Extension Of Peninsular Plateau

The Northeastern Plateau is actually an extension of the main Peninsular Plateau.

It became separated because of faulting between the Rajmahal Hills and the Meghalaya Plateau.

The depression between them was later filled by river deposits.

## 19.2 Present Components

Today, the detached plateau includes:

- Meghalaya Plateau,
- Karbi Anglong Plateau.

## 19.3 Meghalaya Plateau Divisions

The Meghalaya Plateau is divided into:

1. Garo Hills,
2. Khasi Hills,
3. Jaintia Hills.

These names are linked with tribal groups living in the region.

## 19.4 Minerals

The Meghalaya Plateau is rich in minerals such as:

- coal,
- iron ore,
- sillimanite,
- limestone,
- uranium.

## 19.5 Rainfall And Erosion

The area receives very heavy rainfall from the southwest monsoon.

As a result, the plateau surface is highly eroded.

Cherrapunji shows bare rocky surfaces with little permanent vegetation cover.

# 20. Indian Desert

## 20.1 Location

The Great Indian Desert lies northwest of the Aravali Hills.

It is also known as:

```text
Marusthali
```

## 20.2 Climate

The region receives low rainfall:

```text
below 150 mm per year
```

It has:

- arid climate,
- low vegetation cover,
- high evaporation,
- water deficit conditions.

## 20.3 Landforms

Common desert landforms include:

- longitudinal dunes,
- barchans,
- mushroom rocks,
- shifting dunes,
- oasis areas.

## 20.4 Geological Evidence

It is believed that during the Mesozoic era, parts of this region were under the sea.

Evidence includes:

- wood fossils at Aakal,
- marine deposits near Brahmsar, close to Jaisalmer.

The wood fossils are estimated to be about 180 million years old.

## 20.5 Drainage

Most rivers are ephemeral, meaning they flow only temporarily.

The Luni River is important in the southern part of the desert.

Some streams disappear after flowing for some distance and show inland drainage.

Playas and lakes often contain brackish water, used for obtaining salt.

## 20.6 Desert Slope

The desert can be divided by orientation:

- northern part slopes toward Sindh,
- southern part slopes toward the Rann of Kachchh.

# 22. Western Coastal Plains

## 22.1 Submerged Coast

The western coastal plain is an example of a submerged coastal plain.

Because of submergence, it is a narrow belt.

This also provides natural conditions for ports and harbours.

## 22.2 Important Ports

Important west coast ports include:

- Kandla,
- Mazagaon,
- Jawaharlal Nehru Port or Nhava Sheva,
- Marmagao,
- Mangalore,
- Cochin.

## 22.3 Divisions

From north to south, the western coast includes:

- Kachchh and Kathiawar coast in Gujarat,
- Konkan coast in Maharashtra,
- Goan coast,
- Malabar coast in Karnataka and Kerala.

## 22.4 Width

The western coastal plains are narrow in the middle and broader toward the north and south.

## 22.5 No Deltas

Rivers flowing through the western coastal plain do not form large deltas.

Reasons:

- the coastal plain is narrow,
- rivers are short and swift,
- slope toward the sea is steep,
- the coast is submerged.

## 22.6 Kayals

The Malabar coast has backwaters called Kayals.

They are used for:

- fishing,
- inland navigation,
- tourism.

The Nehru Trophy Vallamkali boat race is held in Punnamada Kayal in Kerala.

# 23. Eastern Coastal Plains

## 23.1 Emergent Coast

The eastern coastal plain is broader than the western coastal plain.

- Godavari,
- Krishna,

## 23.3 Ports And Harbours

The eastern coast has fewer natural ports and harbours compared with the western coast.

Examples of important eastern ports include:

- Kolkata-Haldia,
- Paradip,
- Visakhapatnam,
- Chennai,
- Tuticorin.

# 34. Strong Short Answers

## Question: What is physiography?

Physiography is the physical form and relief of an area. It is the outcome of geological structure, geomorphic processes, and the stage of landform development.

## Question: What are the three geological divisions of India?

India's three geological divisions are the Peninsular Block, the Himalayas and other mountains, and the Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain.

## Question: Why is the Peninsular Block stable?

The Peninsular Block is made of very ancient rocks such as gneisses and granites. It has behaved like a rigid block since the Cambrian period, though some areas have undergone faulting, uplift, and submergence.

## Question: Why are the Himalayas called young mountains?

The Himalayas are young tectonic mountains formed by plate movement. They are still affected by endogenic and exogenic forces and show youthful landforms such as gorges, V-shaped valleys, rapids, and waterfalls.

## Question: What are Bhabar and Tarai?

Bhabar is a narrow pebbly belt along the Shiwalik foothills where streams often disappear. Tarai lies south of Bhabar and is a marshy belt where streams re-emerge.

## Question: What is the difference between Bhangar and Khadar?

Bhangar is older alluvium of the northern plains. Khadar is newer alluvium, often renewed by flood deposits.

## Question: Why does the western coastal plain not have large deltas?

The western coastal plain is narrow and submerged. Its rivers are short and swift, descending rapidly from the Western Ghats, so they do not deposit enough sediment to form large deltas.

## Question: Compare the two island groups of India.

The Andaman and Nicobar Islands lie in the Bay of Bengal and are mainly elevated submarine mountains, with some volcanic origin. Lakshadweep lies in the Arabian Sea off Kerala and is built of coral deposits.

# 35. Long Answer: Structure And Physiography Of India

India's physiography is closely connected with its geological structure and geomorphic history. The landforms of India are shaped by endogenic forces, exogenic forces, and the movement of the Indian Plate.

Geologically, India has three major divisions. The Peninsular Block is an old, stable, rigid landmass made mainly of ancient gneisses and granites. It includes rift valleys, block mountains, residual hills, and old plateaus. The Himalayas and other mountains are young, weak, folded, and tectonic in origin. They are affected by faulting, folding, thrusting, earthquakes, and fast river erosion. The Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain is a deep alluvial plain formed by sediments deposited in a former depression.

Physiographically, India is divided into six major regions: Northern and Northeastern Mountains, Northern Plain, Peninsular Plateau, Indian Desert, Coastal Plains, and Islands.

The Northern and Northeastern Mountains include the Himalayas and eastern hill ranges. The Northern Plain is formed by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra systems. The Peninsular Plateau is an old stable plateau rich in minerals and varied relief. The Indian Desert is an arid region with dunes and inland drainage. The Coastal Plains include the western submerged coast and eastern emergent coast. The Islands include the Andaman-Nicobar and Lakshadweep groups.

Thus, India's physical diversity is the result of long geological history and active geomorphic processes.

---

# 36. Long Answer: Northern Plains

The Northern Plains are one of India's most important physiographic divisions. They are formed by alluvial deposits brought by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems.

These plains extend about 3,200 km from east to west. Their width varies from about 150 km to 300 km. The depth of alluvial deposits ranges from about 1,000 m to 2,000 m.

From north to south, the plains are divided into Bhabar, Tarai, and alluvial plains. Bhabar is a narrow pebbly belt along the Shiwalik foothills where streams often disappear. South of Bhabar lies the Tarai, a marshy belt where streams re-emerge. South of Tarai are the alluvial plains, divided into Bhangar and Khadar. Bhangar is older alluvium, while Khadar is newer alluvium.

The plains show features such as sand bars, meanders, ox-bow lakes, braided channels, riverine islands, and deltas. The Brahmaputra plains are especially known for floods, shifting channels, and riverine islands.

Because of fertile alluvial soil, the plains support crops such as wheat, rice, sugarcane, and jute. They also support a very large population.

---

# 37. Long Answer: Peninsular Plateau

The Peninsular Plateau is one of the oldest and most stable landmasses of India. It rises from about 150 m above the river plains to about 600-900 m.

It is broadly triangular in shape. Its outer limits include the Delhi Ridge, Rajmahal Hills, Gir Range, and Cardamom Hills. It also extends northeastward as the Shillong and Karbi Anglong plateaus.

The plateau contains many patland plateaus such as Hazaribagh, Palamu, Ranchi, Malwa, Coimbatore, and Karnataka plateaus. It has features such as tors, block mountains, rift valleys, bare rocks, hummocky hills, quartzite dykes, ravines, and gorges.

The plateau is divided into the Deccan Plateau, Central Highlands, and Northeastern Plateau. The Deccan Plateau is bordered by Western Ghats, Eastern Ghats, and northern hill ranges. The Western Ghats are higher and more continuous than the Eastern Ghats. Anaimudi is the highest peak of the Peninsular Plateau. The Central Highlands include old denuded ranges and mineral-rich extensions. The Northeastern Plateau includes Meghalaya and Karbi Anglong and is rich in minerals.

Thus, the Peninsular Plateau is old, stable, mineral-rich, and highly varied in relief.

# 39. Final Chapter Summary

India's physical features are the result of geological structure, plate movement, endogenic forces, and exogenic processes. The northward movement of the Indian Plate played a major role in the formation of the Himalayas and the Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain.

Geologically, India has three major divisions: the old and stable Peninsular Block, the young and tectonic Himalayas and other mountains, and the alluvial Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain.

Physiographically, India is divided into the Northern and Northeastern Mountains, Northern Plain, Peninsular Plateau, Indian Desert, Coastal Plains, and Islands.

The Himalayas show strong regional variation: Kashmir Himalayas, Himachal-Uttarakhand Himalayas, Darjiling-Sikkim Himalayas, Arunachal Himalayas, and Eastern Hills. The Northern Plains are formed by deep alluvium and include Bhabar, Tarai, Bhangar, and Khadar. The Peninsular Plateau is old, stable, and mineral-rich. The Indian Desert is arid and shaped by wind action. The western coast is submerged and narrow, while the eastern coast is emergent and deltaic. The Andaman-Nicobar Islands and Lakshadweep differ strongly in origin and physical character.

Understanding this chapter means connecting names and facts to processes: plate movement, erosion, deposition, faulting, river action, wind action, and marine action.
