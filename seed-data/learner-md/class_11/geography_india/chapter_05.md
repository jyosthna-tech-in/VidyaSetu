## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains India's natural vegetation, forests, wetlands, wildlife, and conservation efforts.

Natural vegetation is not the same as planted vegetation. A mango tree in an orchard may be planted and managed by people, but a similar tree growing naturally in a forest is part of natural vegetation.

The central idea is:

> Natural vegetation develops when plant communities adjust to local climate, soil, relief, and moisture conditions over a long period, and India's great environmental diversity creates a great diversity of forests, wetlands, wildlife, and conservation needs.

# 1. Meaning Of Natural Vegetation

## 1.1 Definition

Natural vegetation refers to a plant community that has remained undisturbed for a long time.

Over time, its species adjust to:

- climate,
- soil,
- relief,
- moisture,
- local ecological conditions.

## 1.2 Natural Vegetation vs Planted Vegetation

The same species can be natural in one place and planted in another.

Example:

- a mango tree growing in a forest may be part of natural vegetation;
- a mango tree in an orchard is planted vegetation.

## 1.3 Main Difference

| Natural Vegetation | Planted Vegetation |
| --- | --- |
| Grows under natural conditions | Grown under human supervision |
| Species adjust over time | Species selected by people |
| Found in forests, grasslands, wetlands | Found in orchards, plantations, gardens |
| Ecologically complex | Often managed for production or beauty |

## 1.4 Why Natural Vegetation Matters

Natural vegetation:

- protects soil,
- stores carbon,
- supports wildlife,
- regulates water flow,
- reduces floods and droughts,
- supports tribal and rural livelihoods,
- maintains biodiversity,
- influences local climate.

---

# 2. Why India Has Great Vegetation Diversity

India has a wide variety of natural vegetation because the country has wide variation in:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- altitude,
- soil,
- drainage,
- relief,
- humidity,
- human interference.

## 2.1 Regional Examples

| Region | Vegetation Feature |
| --- | --- |
| Himalayan heights | Temperate and alpine vegetation |
| Western Ghats | Tropical rain forests and shola forests in high areas |
| Andaman and Nicobar Islands | Tropical rain forests and mangroves |
| Deltaic regions | Mangroves and swamp forests |
| Rajasthan desert and semi-desert | Cacti, thorny bushes, scrub vegetation |
| Northeastern hills | Evergreen and semi-evergreen forests |

## 2.2 Main Lesson

Vegetation changes from region to region because climate and soil change from region to region.

Where rainfall is high, vegetation is dense.

Where rainfall is low, vegetation becomes thorny, open, or scrub-like.

Where altitude increases, temperature falls and vegetation changes in belts.

---

# 3. Major Types Of Forests In India

Indian forests can be divided into five major groups:

1. tropical evergreen and semi-evergreen forests,
2. tropical deciduous forests,
3. tropical thorn forests,
4. montane forests,
5. littoral and swamp forests.

## 3.1 Classification Logic

The classification is based on:

- predominant vegetation type,
- rainfall,
- temperature,
- humidity,
- altitude,
- soil,
- coastal or inland location.

## 3.2 Quick Comparison

| Forest Type | Rainfall Or Location | Major Feature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tropical evergreen | Above 200 cm; warm humid areas | Dense, layered, green all year |
| Semi-evergreen | Less rainy parts of evergreen regions | Evergreen and moist deciduous mix |
| Moist deciduous | 100-200 cm rainfall | Monsoon forests with valuable timber |
| Dry deciduous | 70-100 cm rainfall | Trees shed leaves in dry season |
| Thorn | Below 50 cm rainfall | Scrub, thorny, drought resistant |
| Montane | Mountain areas | Vegetation changes with altitude |
| Littoral and swamp | Coasts, deltas, wetlands | Mangroves and water-tolerant species |

# 4. Tropical Evergreen Forests

## 4.1 Distribution

Tropical evergreen forests are found in:

- western slopes of the Western Ghats,
- hills of northeastern India,
- Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

## 4.2 Climatic Conditions

They develop in warm and humid areas with:

- annual rainfall above 200 cm,
- mean annual temperature above 22 degrees C,
- high humidity.

## 4.3 Structure

These forests are well stratified.

That means they have several layers:

- shrubs and creepers near the ground,
- shorter trees above them,
- taller trees forming the upper canopy.

## 4.4 Height

Trees may reach heights of 60 metres or more.

This is possible because rainfall, heat, and humidity support rapid growth.

## 4.5 Evergreen Character

There is no fixed season when all trees shed their leaves.

Different species shed leaves at different times.

So the forest appears green throughout the year.

## 4.6 Important Species

Important species include:

- rosewood,
- mahogany,
- aini,
- ebony.

## 4.7 Learning Tip

If a question asks for evergreen forests, include:

- high rainfall,
- warm humid climate,
- layered structure,
- no common leaf-shedding season,
- Western Ghats, northeast, Andaman and Nicobar examples.

---

# 5. Tropical Semi-Evergreen Forests

## 5.1 Location

Semi-evergreen forests occur in the less rainy parts of tropical evergreen regions.

## 5.2 Composition

They contain a mixture of:

- evergreen trees,
- moist deciduous trees.

## 5.3 Evergreen Appearance

Climbers and undergrowth give these forests an evergreen appearance.

They are not as dense as true evergreen forests, but they do not look completely dry either.

## 5.4 Important Species

Important species include:

- white cedar,
- hollock,
- kail.

## 5.5 Comparison With Evergreen Forest

| Tropical Evergreen | Tropical Semi-Evergreen |
| --- | --- |
| Higher rainfall | Slightly less rainfall |
| Denser and more layered | Mixed evergreen and deciduous character |
| Always green | Mostly evergreen appearance |
| Taller closed canopy | Somewhat more open |

# 6. Colonial Exploitation Of Forests

## 6.1 Economic Value Of Forests

The British recognised the economic value of Indian forests.

Forests were used for:

- timber,
- railway sleepers,
- construction,
- plantations,
- commercial extraction.

## 6.2 Change In Forest Structure

The structure of forests was altered.

Example:

- oak forests in Garhwal and Kumaon were replaced by pine because pine was useful for railway lines.

## 6.3 Plantation Expansion

Forests were cleared to introduce plantations such as:

- tea,
- rubber,
- coffee.

## 6.4 Shift In Forest Use

Earlier, forests had protective, ecological, and livelihood functions.

Under colonial commercial use, forests became sources of profit and raw material.

This damaged ecological balance in many regions.

# 7. Tropical Deciduous Forests

## 7.1 Basic Meaning

Tropical deciduous forests are the most widespread forests in India.

They are also called monsoon forests.

## 7.2 Rainfall Range

They occur in regions receiving about 70 cm to 200 cm of rainfall.

## 7.3 Why They Shed Leaves

During the dry season, water becomes limited.

Trees shed leaves to reduce water loss.

This is why deciduous forests change appearance between rainy and dry seasons.

## 7.4 Subtypes

Tropical deciduous forests are divided into:

1. moist deciduous forests,
2. dry deciduous forests.

The difference depends mainly on water availability.

## 8.1 Rainfall

Moist deciduous forests occur in areas receiving about 100 cm to 200 cm rainfall.

## 8.2 Distribution

They are found in:

- northeastern states,
- foothills of the Himalayas,
- eastern slopes of the Western Ghats,
- Odisha.

## 8.3 Important Species

Important species include:

- teak,
- sal,
- shisham,
- hurra,
- mahua,
- amla,

## 8.4 Importance

These forests are economically important because many species provide timber, fuelwood, fruits, flowers, leaves, and other forest products.

# 9. Dry Deciduous Forests

## 9.1 Rainfall

Dry deciduous forests occur in areas receiving about 70 cm to 100 cm rainfall.

## 9.2 Distribution

They cover large parts of India, especially:

- rainier parts of the Peninsular plateau,
- plains of Uttar Pradesh,
- plains of Bihar.

## 9.3 Transition Zones

Dry deciduous forests form a transition:

- toward moist deciduous forests on wetter margins,
- toward thorn forests on drier margins.

## 9.4 Parkland Landscape

In some regions, these forests appear like parklands.

They have:

- open stretches,
- patches of grass,
- scattered trees such as teak.

## 9.5 Dry Season Appearance

During the dry season, trees shed leaves completely.

The forest may look like a grassland with leafless trees.

## 9.6 Important Species

Important species include:

- tendu,
- palas,
- amaltas,
- bel,
- khair,
- axlewood.

## 9.7 Rajasthan Example

In western and southern Rajasthan, vegetation cover is very scanty because of:

- low rainfall,
- overgrazing.

# 10. Tropical Thorn Forests

## 10.1 Rainfall

Tropical thorn forests occur in areas receiving less than 50 cm rainfall.

## 10.2 Distribution

They occur in semi-arid areas of:

- southwest Punjab,
- Haryana,
- Rajasthan,
- Gujarat,
- Madhya Pradesh,
- Uttar Pradesh.

## 10.3 Vegetation Character

These forests consist of:

- grasses,
- shrubs,
- thorny bushes,
- drought-resistant trees.

Plants may remain leafless for much of the year.

This gives the vegetation a scrub-like appearance.

## 10.4 Adaptations

Plants survive dryness through:

- deep roots,
- small leaves,
- thorns,
- thick bark,
- reduced leaf surface,
- ability to remain dormant during severe drought.

## 10.5 Important Species

Important species include:

- babool,
- ber,
- wild date palm,
- khair,
- neem,
- khejri,
- palas.

## 10.6 Tussocky Grass

Tussocky grass may grow up to about 2 metres as undergrowth.

## 10.7 Exam Use

If rainfall is below 50 cm and vegetation is scrubby, thorny, and drought-resistant, identify it as tropical thorn forest.

# 11. Montane Forests

## 11.1 Meaning

Montane forests are mountain forests.

They change with altitude because temperature decreases as height increases.

## 11.2 Two Broad Types

Mountain forests are divided into:

1. northern mountain forests,
2. southern mountain forests.

## 11.3 Core Principle

As altitude increases:

- temperature decreases,
- rainfall pattern changes,
- growing season changes,
- vegetation changes from tropical to temperate to alpine to tundra.

---

# 12. Northern Mountain Forests

## 12.1 Himalayan Succession

The Himalayan ranges show a succession of vegetation from tropical to tundra.

This succession depends on altitude.

## 12.2 Foothill Zone

Deciduous forests occur in the foothills of the Himalayas.

## 12.3 Wet Temperate Zone

Between about 1,000 m and 2,000 m, wet temperate forests occur.

In higher hill ranges of northeastern India, West Bengal, and Uttarakhand, evergreen broadleaf trees are important.

Examples:

- oak,
- chestnut.

## 12.4 Pine Zone

Between about 1,500 m and 1,750 m, pine forests are well developed.

Chir pine is an important commercial tree.

## 12.5 Western Himalayan Species

Deodar grows mainly in the western Himalayas.

It is a durable wood used in construction.

Other important species of this zone include:

- chinar,
- walnut.

These support famous Kashmir handicrafts.

## 12.6 Higher Temperate Zone

Blue pine and spruce occur around 2,225 m to 3,048 m.

Temperate grasslands also occur in places.

## 12.7 Alpine Forests And Pastures

At higher altitudes, there is a transition to alpine forests and pastures.

Between about 3,000 m and 4,000 m, important species include:

- silver fir,
- juniper,
- pine,
- birch,
- rhododendron.

## 12.8 Tundra Vegetation

At still higher altitudes, mosses and lichens form part of tundra vegetation.

## 12.9 Transhumance

Alpine pastures are used by pastoral communities for seasonal migration.

Examples include:

- Gujjars,
- Bakarwals,
- Bhotiyas,
- Gaddis.

This seasonal movement between summer and winter pastures is called transhumance.

## 12.10 Slope Difference

Southern slopes of the Himalayas carry thicker vegetation because they receive more precipitation.

Northern slopes are drier and have thinner vegetation.

# 13. Southern Mountain Forests

## 13.1 Distribution

Southern mountain forests occur mainly in:

- Western Ghats,
- Vindhyas,
- Nilgiris.

## 13.2 Altitude And Latitude

These mountains are closer to the tropics than the Himalayas.

Because of this:

- lower slopes may have subtropical vegetation;
- higher areas may have temperate vegetation.

## 13.3 Shola Forests

Temperate forests in the Nilgiris, Anaimalai, and Palani hills are called sholas.

Sholas are important because they:

- occur in high-altitude valleys,
- support biodiversity,
- regulate water,
- are linked with grassland ecosystems.

## 13.4 Important Species

Economically important trees include:

- magnolia,
- laurel,
- cinchona,
- wattle.

## 13.5 Other Occurrence

Similar forests are also found in:

- Satpura range,
- Maikal range.

## 14.1 Meaning

Littoral and swamp forests are associated with:

- coasts,
- tidal areas,
- marshes,
- estuaries,
- deltas,
- lagoons,
- waterlogged areas.

## 14.2 Wetlands In India

India has a rich variety of wetland habitats.

The chapter states that:

- about 70 percent of wetlands comprise areas under paddy cultivation;

## 14.3 Ramsar Sites Mentioned

Two important wetland habitats mentioned are:

- Chilika Lake in Odisha,

They are protected as waterfowl habitats under the Ramsar Convention.

## 14.4 Meaning Of International Convention

An international convention is an agreement among member states of the United Nations.

The Ramsar Convention focuses on wetlands of international importance.

# 16. Mangrove Forests

## 16.1 Location

Mangroves grow along coasts in:

- salt marshes,
- tidal creeks,
- mud flats,
- estuaries,
- deltas.

## 16.2 Adaptation

Mangrove species are salt-tolerant.

They survive in conditions where ordinary plants may not grow well.

They are adapted to:

- salinity,
- tidal flow,
- waterlogging,
- muddy soil,
- low oxygen in soil.

## 16.3 Ecological Role

Mangroves:

- protect coasts,
- reduce wave impact,
- support fish and shrimp breeding,
- provide habitat for birds,
- trap sediments,
- support biodiversity,
- reduce cyclone damage.

## 16.4 Distribution In India

Mangrove forests in India cover about 6,740 sq km according to the chapter.

This is about 7 percent of the world's mangrove forests.

Important areas include:

- Andaman and Nicobar Islands,
- Sunderbans of West Bengal,
- Mahanadi delta,
- Godavari delta,
- Krishna delta.

## 16.5 Conservation Need

Mangroves are being encroached upon.

They need conservation because they protect coasts and support fragile ecosystems.

# 17. Forest Area And Forest Cover

## 17.1 Forest Area

Forest area means land officially recorded as forest land.

It is based on government records.

Trees may or may not actually exist on all of this land.

## 17.2 Forest Cover

Forest cover means the actual area occupied by forest canopy.

It is measured using:

- aerial photographs,
- satellite imagery.

## 17.3 Key Difference

| Forest Area | Forest Cover |
| --- | --- |
| Legal or recorded category | Actual tree canopy on land |
| Based on revenue records | Based on remote sensing |
| May include land without trees | Shows actual forested surface |
| Official land status | Ecological reality |

## 17.4 Why The Difference Matters

A state may have recorded forest land but low actual tree cover.

For conservation, actual forest cover is more important than only legal notification.

# 18. Forest Cover In India

## 18.1 Textbook Data

According to the chapter:

- recorded forest area covers 23.28 percent of India's total land area;
- actual forest cover is 21.05 percent according to India State of Forest Report 2011;
- dense forest cover is 12.29 percent;
- open forest cover is 8.75 percent.

These figures are textbook figures and may differ from newer reports.

## 18.2 State Variation

Forest area and forest cover vary greatly from state to state.

Examples:

- Lakshadweep has zero percent recorded forest area;
- Andaman and Nicobar Islands have very high recorded forest area;
- many north and northwestern states have less than 10 percent forest area.

## 18.3 Low Forest Area States

States with less than 10 percent forest area include:

- Rajasthan,
- Gujarat,
- Punjab,
- Haryana,
- Delhi.

Punjab and Haryana have low forest area because much forest land was cleared for cultivation.

## 18.4 Medium Forest Area

Tamil Nadu and West Bengal are placed in the 10-20 percent forest area group in the chapter.

## 18.5 Peninsular India

Much of Peninsular India, excluding some areas such as Tamil Nadu, Dadra and Nagar Haveli, and Goa, has 20-30 percent forest cover.

## 18.6 Northeastern India

Northeastern states generally have more than 30 percent land under forest.

Reasons:

- hilly topography,
- heavy rainfall,
- lower density of intensive agriculture in many hilly areas.

## 18.7 Actual Forest Cover Range

The chapter notes strong variation in actual forest cover, ranging from low cover in Jammu and Kashmir to very high cover in Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

## 18.8 Forest Cover Regions

States can be grouped by forest cover percentage:

| Region | Forest Cover Percentage |
| --- | --- |
| High concentration | More than 40 percent |
| Medium concentration | 20-40 percent |
| Low concentration | 10-20 percent |

# 19. Forest Conservation

## 19.1 Why Conservation Is Necessary

Forests are closely linked with life and environment.

They provide direct and indirect benefits to:

- economy,
- society,
- climate,
- soil,
- water,
- wildlife,
- rural livelihoods.

Forest conservation is essential for human survival and prosperity.

## 19.2 Forest Policy

The Government of India adopted a forest policy in 1952.

It was later modified in 1988.

The 1988 policy emphasised sustainable forest management.

It aimed to conserve and expand forests while also meeting the needs of local people.

## 19.3 Major Goals Of Forest Policy

The forest policy aimed at:

1. bringing 33 percent of geographical area under forest cover;
2. maintaining environmental stability;
3. restoring forests where ecological balance was disturbed;
4. conserving biological diversity and genetic resources;
5. checking soil erosion;
6. checking extension of desert lands;
7. reducing floods and droughts;
8. increasing forest cover through social forestry;
9. afforesting degraded land;
10. increasing forest productivity;
11. making timber, fuel, fodder, and food available to rural people;
12. encouraging substitutes for wood;
13. creating a people's movement involving women;
14. reducing pressure on existing forests.

## 19.4 Sustainable Forest Management

Sustainable forest management means forests should be used without destroying their long-term ecological capacity.

It balances:

- conservation,
- local needs,
- ecological stability,
- future generations.

# 20. Forests And Life

For many tribal communities, forests are:

- home,
- source of food,
- source of livelihood,
- cultural space,
- economic support.

## 20.2 Forest Products Used By Tribal Communities

Forests provide:

- fruits,
- edible leaves,
- honey,
- roots,
- wild game,
- house-building material,
- raw material for arts and crafts.

## 20.3 Tribal Districts And Forest Cover

The chapter notes that 188 districts were identified as tribal districts.

These tribal districts account for a large share of India's forest cover.

This shows that tribal areas and forests are closely related.

## 20.4 Tribal Knowledge

Tribal communities often possess age-old knowledge of forests.

This knowledge can help:

- conservation,
- sustainable harvesting,
- minor forest produce management,
- ecological restoration.

## 20.5 Participation-Based View

Instead of treating tribal communities only as collectors of minor forest produce, they should be encouraged as:

- growers,
- managers,
- protectors,
- participants in conservation.

This creates a more just and effective conservation model.

# 21. Social Forestry

## 21.1 Meaning

Social forestry means management and protection of forests and afforestation on barren land for:

- environmental development,
- social development,
- rural development.

## 21.2 Classification

The National Commission on Agriculture in 1976 classified social forestry into three categories:

1. urban forestry,
2. rural forestry,
3. farm forestry.

## 21.3 Purpose

Social forestry aims to:

- reduce pressure on natural forests,
- provide fuelwood,
- provide fodder,
- provide small timber,
- improve local environment,
- support rural livelihoods,
- involve communities.

## 22.2 Areas Included

It includes trees on:

- public land,
- private land,
- green belts,
- parks,
- roadside avenues,
- industrial green belts,
- commercial green belts.

## 22.3 Importance

Urban forestry:

- reduces pollution,
- moderates temperature,
- improves urban beauty,

# 23. Rural Forestry

Rural forestry includes:

1. agro-forestry,
2. community forestry.

## 23.1 Agro-Forestry

Agro-forestry means raising trees and agricultural crops on the same land.

It may include waste patches within farms.

It combines forestry with agriculture and can produce:

- food,
- fodder,
- fuel,
- timber,
- fruit.

## 23.2 Community Forestry

Community forestry means raising trees on public or community land.

Examples:

- village pastures,
- temple land,
- roadsides,
- canal banks,
- railway strips,
- school land.

## 23.3 Importance Of Community Forestry

Community forestry benefits the community as a whole.

It also allows landless people to participate in tree raising and receive benefits that may otherwise be limited to landowners.

# 24. Farm Forestry

## 24.1 Meaning

# 40. Common Misunderstandings

## 40.1 Forest Area And Forest Cover Are The Same

Incorrect.

Forest area is recorded legal forest land.

Forest cover is actual tree canopy.

## 40.2 Evergreen Forests Never Shed Leaves

Incorrect.

Trees do shed leaves, but not all at one fixed time.

That is why the forest remains green throughout the year.

## 40.3 All Mountain Forests Are Same

Incorrect.

Mountain vegetation changes with altitude and differs between Himalayas and southern mountains.

## 40.4 Mangroves Are Ordinary Forests

Incorrect.

Mangroves are salt-tolerant coastal forests adapted to tides, mud, salinity, and waterlogging.

## 40.5 Conservation Means No Human Use

Incomplete.

Modern conservation often includes sustainable use, community participation, and local livelihoods.

## 40.6 Social Forestry Means Only Planting Trees In Villages

Incomplete.

Social forestry includes urban forestry, rural forestry, agro-forestry, community forestry, and farm forestry.

## 40.7 Wildlife Conservation Means Only Saving Animals

Incomplete.

Wildlife conservation must also protect habitats, food chains, corridors, vegetation, and ecosystems.

# 45. Final Chapter Summary

Natural vegetation is vegetation that develops under natural conditions over a long period. India has great vegetation diversity because of major differences in climate, rainfall, soil, relief, altitude, and humidity.

The main forest types are tropical evergreen and semi-evergreen forests, tropical deciduous forests, tropical thorn forests, montane forests, and littoral and swamp forests. Tropical evergreen forests occur in warm humid high-rainfall regions; deciduous forests are the most widespread and shed leaves during the dry season; thorn forests occur in dry areas; montane forests change with altitude; mangroves occur in coastal wetlands, deltas, and tidal areas.

Forest area and forest cover are different. Forest area is recorded forest land, while forest cover is actual canopy cover. This distinction is important for understanding the real condition of forests.

Forest conservation is essential because forests support soil, water, biodiversity, climate stability, rural livelihoods, tribal communities, and the economy. India's forest policy aims at sustainable management, ecological restoration, increased forest cover, social forestry, and people's participation.

Wildlife is part of India's natural heritage, but habitat loss, hunting, poaching, grazing, forest fires, and development have reduced many species. Conservation efforts include the Wildlife Act, national parks, sanctuaries, Project Tiger, Project Elephant, and biosphere reserves.

Biosphere reserves such as Nilgiri, Nanda Devi, Sunderbans, and Gulf of Mannar show that conservation must protect ecosystems, species, local communities, and sustainable use together.

To master this chapter, connect vegetation with rainfall, altitude, soil, human use, and conservation. Good answers should always include examples, data, and map locations.
