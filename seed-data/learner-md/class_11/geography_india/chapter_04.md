# Chapter 4: Climate

### NCERT Class 11 - Geography India

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains the climate of India with special focus on the Indian monsoon.

India does not have one uniform climate everywhere. The same country contains:

- hot deserts,
- humid coasts,
- snowy mountains,
- wet hills,
- dry plateaus,
- flood-prone deltas,
- drought-prone interiors.

Yet India has a broad climatic unity because most regions are influenced by the seasonal rhythm of the monsoon.

The central idea is:

> India's climate is monsoonal in character, but this monsoonal climate shows strong regional variations in temperature, rainfall, winds, seasons, and economic impact.

# 1. Weather And Climate

## 1.1 Weather

Weather means the momentary state of the atmosphere.

It answers questions such as:

- Is it hot today?
- Is it raining now?
- Is the wind strong this evening?
- Is the sky cloudy this morning?

Weather changes quickly.

It may change:

- within a few hours,
- within one day,
- within one week.

## 1.2 Climate

Climate means the average condition of weather over a long period.

It answers questions such as:

- Is this region generally hot?
- Does this area receive rainfall every year?
- Are winters severe here?
- Is the climate humid or dry?

Climate changes slowly. It is usually observed over many decades.

## 1.3 Main Elements Of Weather And Climate

The chapter focuses on these elements:

- temperature,
- air pressure,
- wind direction,
- wind velocity,
- humidity,
- precipitation.

These elements create seasonal changes.

For example:

- in summer, temperature rises and light clothes are used;
- in winter, temperature falls and woollen clothes are used in north India;
- in the rainy season, humidity and precipitation increase.

## 1.4 Weather vs Climate

| Basis | Weather | Climate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Time scale | Short term | Long term |
| Change | Changes quickly | Changes slowly |
| Example | It is raining today | Kerala has a humid monsoon climate |
| Study focus | Daily atmospheric condition | Average atmospheric pattern |
| Useful for | Daily planning | Agriculture, settlement, long-term planning |

## 1.5 Simple Memory Line

Weather is what you experience today.

Climate is what a place usually experiences over many years.

# 2. Meaning Of Monsoon

## 2.1 Basic Meaning

Monsoon refers to a climate associated with seasonal reversal in wind direction.

This means winds do not blow in the same direction throughout the year.

In India:

- summer winds mainly bring moisture from the ocean toward land;
- winter winds generally blow from land toward sea.

## 2.2 India As A Monsoon Climate Region

India has a hot monsoonal climate.

This type of climate is also found widely in:

- South Asia,
- Southeast Asia.

The monsoon controls:

- rainfall,
- agriculture,
- river flow,
- droughts,
- floods,
- crop calendar,
- daily life,
- seasonal rhythm.

## 2.3 Why Monsoon Is More Than Rain

Many learners think monsoon only means rainfall. That is incomplete.

Monsoon includes:

- seasonal wind reversal,
- pressure changes,
- rainfall distribution,
- wet and dry seasons,
- onset and withdrawal,
- breaks in rainfall,
- regional variation.

The rain is the most visible part, but the complete monsoon is a larger atmospheric system.

---

# 3. Unity And Diversity In India's Monsoon Climate

## 3.1 Broad Unity

India's climate has unity because most regions are influenced by the monsoon.

This unity is seen in:

- seasonal rhythm,
- summer heat before monsoon,
- rainy season in June-September for most of India,
- retreat of monsoon in autumn,
- connection of agriculture with rainfall.

## 3.2 Strong Diversity

The unity does not mean uniformity.

India shows large regional differences in:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- type of precipitation,
- timing of rainfall,
- wind direction,
- dryness and wetness,
- seasonal contrast.

These differences create sub-types within the larger monsoon climate.

## 3.3 Temperature Diversity

| Region or Place | Climatic Example | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Western Rajasthan | Summer temperature may reach around 55 degrees C | Extreme heat |
| Leh | Winter temperature may fall around minus 45 degrees C | Extreme cold |
| Churu | June day may reach 50 degrees C or more | Hot continental interior |
| Tawang | Same June day may be around 19 degrees C | Altitude reduces temperature |
| Drass | December night may fall around minus 45 degrees C | Severe Himalayan winter |
| Thiruvananthapuram or Chennai | Same winter night may be around 20-22 degrees C | Coastal and southern moderation |

## 3.4 Daily Temperature Contrast

The daily temperature difference is also uneven.

In Kerala and the Andaman Islands:

- the difference between day and night temperature may be only 7-8 degrees C.

In the Thar Desert:

- day temperature may be around 50 degrees C,
- night temperature may fall by 15-20 degrees C.

This happens because dry desert air loses heat quickly at night, while humid coastal air retains heat more evenly.

## 3.5 Precipitation Diversity

| Region | Precipitation Feature |
| --- | --- |
| Himalayas | Snowfall occurs in winter and high-altitude regions |
| Most of India | Rainfall is the main form of precipitation |
| Cherrapunji and Mawsynram | Very high annual rainfall |
| Jaisalmer | Very low annual rainfall |
| Meghalaya | Some areas receive more than 400 cm rainfall |
| Western deserts | Some areas receive less than 10 cm precipitation |
| Tamil Nadu coast | Major rain comes in early winter, not peak southwest monsoon |

## 3.6 The Main Conclusion

India's climate is:

- monsoonal in rhythm,
- diverse in local expression.

A strong answer should include both ideas.

# 4. Factors Determining India's Climate

India's climate is controlled by two broad groups of factors:

1. factors related to location and relief,
2. factors related to air pressure and winds.

## 4.1 Factors Related To Location And Relief

These include:

- latitude,
- Himalayan mountains,
- distribution of land and water,
- distance from the sea,
- altitude,
- relief.

## 4.2 Factors Related To Air Pressure And Winds

These include:

- surface pressure and winds,
- upper air circulation,
- air masses,
- jet streams,
- western disturbances,
- tropical depressions.

The chapter explains these mechanisms separately for winter and summer.

---

# 5. Latitude

## 5.1 Tropic Of Cancer

The Tropic of Cancer passes through the central part of India in an east-west direction.

Because of this:

- northern India lies mainly in the subtropical and temperate zone;
- southern India lies mainly in the tropical zone.

## 5.2 Tropical South

The southern part is closer to the equator.

Therefore it has:

- high temperature throughout the year,
- small daily range of temperature,
- small annual range of temperature,
- less severe winter.

## 5.3 Subtropical And Temperate North

The northern part is farther from the equator.

Therefore it has:

- higher seasonal contrast,
- colder winters,
- hotter summers in interiors,
- larger daily and annual temperature range.

## 5.4 Exam Use

If asked why north India has more extreme climate than south India, mention:

- distance from equator,
- continental location,
- distance from sea,
- Himalayan influence,
- seasonal pressure changes.

Do not give latitude alone as the full answer.

---

# 6. The Himalayan Mountains

## 6.1 Climatic Divide

The Himalayas act as an effective climatic divide.

They separate the Indian subcontinent from the cold air masses of Central and East Asia.

## 6.2 Protection From Cold Winds

Cold winds from areas near the Arctic Circle and Central Asia are blocked by the Himalayas.

Without the Himalayas, north India would experience much colder winters.

## 6.3 Trapping The Monsoon

The Himalayas also trap moisture-bearing monsoon winds.

When these winds reach the mountains:

- they are forced to rise,
- they cool,
- condensation occurs,
- rainfall increases.

## 6.4 Importance

The Himalayas influence:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- snow,
- river flow,
- agriculture,
- settlement,
- winter weather.

They are not just physical barriers; they are major climatic controls.

---

# 7. Distribution Of Land And Water

## 7.1 India Between Ocean And Mountains

India is surrounded by the Indian Ocean on three sides.

It also has the Himalayas as a high northern barrier.

This land-water arrangement strongly affects climate.

## 7.2 Different Heating Of Land And Water

Land heats and cools faster than water.

Water heats and cools slowly.

Because of this:

- land becomes very hot in summer;
- land becomes cooler in winter;
- sea temperature changes more slowly;
- seasonal pressure differences develop.

## 7.3 Link With Monsoon

The difference in heating creates pressure differences.

Pressure differences cause wind movement.

Seasonal changes in pressure help reverse wind direction.

This reversal is the foundation of the monsoon.

---

# 8. Distance From The Sea

## 8.1 Coastal Areas

Coastal areas have an equable climate.

This means:

- summers are less extreme,
- winters are less severe,
- temperature range is small,
- humidity is generally higher.

Examples:

- Mumbai,
- Konkan coast,
- Chennai,
- Thiruvananthapuram.

## 8.2 Interior Areas

Interior areas are far from the moderating influence of the sea.

They experience:

- hotter summers,
- colder winters,
- higher daily and annual range,
- stronger seasonal contrast.

Examples:

- Delhi,
- Kanpur,
- Amritsar.

## 8.3 Simple Comparison

| Coastal Climate | Interior Climate |
| --- | --- |
| Moderated by sea | Less sea influence |
| Small temperature range | Large temperature range |
| Humid | Often drier |
| Less extreme | More extreme |

---

# 9. Altitude

## 9.1 Temperature Decreases With Height

Temperature decreases as altitude increases.

High places are cooler because air becomes thinner and holds less heat.

## 9.2 Example

Agra and Darjiling are located near the same latitude.

But:

- Agra has a January temperature of about 16 degrees C;
- Darjiling has a January temperature of about 4 degrees C.

The difference is mainly due to altitude.

## 9.3 Exam Point

When two places have similar latitude but different temperatures, check altitude.

Altitude often explains why hill stations are cooler than plains.

---

# 10. Relief

## 10.1 Meaning

Relief means the shape and height of landforms.

It includes:

- mountains,
- plateaus,
- plains,
- valleys,
- slopes.

## 10.2 Effect On Climate

Relief affects:

- temperature,
- pressure,
- wind direction,
- wind speed,
- rainfall amount,
- rainfall distribution.

## 10.3 Windward And Leeward Effects

The windward side of a mountain receives more rainfall.

The leeward side receives less rainfall and may become a rain shadow area.

## 10.4 Indian Example

During June-September:

- the windward side of the Western Ghats receives heavy rainfall;
- the southern plateau east of the Western Ghats remains relatively dry.

This is because the plateau lies on the leeward side.

---

# 11. Air Pressure And Wind Controls

To understand local differences in India's climate, three atmospheric controls are important.

## 11.1 First Control: Surface Pressure And Winds

Air moves from high pressure to low pressure.

Seasonal pressure patterns decide:

- wind direction,
- wind speed,
- moisture movement,
- dry or wet weather.

## 11.2 Second Control: Upper Air Circulation

Weather is not controlled only at the ground surface.

Upper air circulation includes:

- jet streams,
- air masses,
- winds in the troposphere,
- global pressure belts.

These upper air systems influence surface weather.

## 11.3 Third Control: Cyclones And Depressions

Two systems are especially important:

- western disturbances in winter,
- tropical depressions during the southwest monsoon.

They create conditions favourable for rainfall.

## 11.4 Why This Matters

Indian climate cannot be explained by only land and sea heating.

A complete explanation must include:

- surface pressure,
- upper air circulation,
- jet streams,
- ITCZ,
- disturbances,
- depressions.

# 12. Mechanism Of Weather In Winter

## 12.1 General Pattern

During winter, India is influenced by pressure conditions over Central and Western Asia.

A high-pressure centre develops north of the Himalayas.

This high-pressure centre sends dry continental air toward the Indian subcontinent.

## 12.2 Surface Pressure And Winds

In winter:

- high pressure develops north of the Himalayas;
- winds move from high pressure toward lower pressure areas;
- dry continental air enters northwestern India;
- these winds interact with trade winds.

The contact zone is not fixed. Sometimes it shifts toward the middle Ganga valley.

When this happens, much of northwestern and northern India comes under dry northwestern winds.

## 12.3 Nature Of Winter Winds

Winter winds are generally:

- dry,
- continental,
- cool or cold,
- low in humidity.

That is why most parts of India do not receive much winter rainfall from these winds.

---

# 13. Jet Streams In Winter

## 13.1 What Are Jet Streams?

Jet streams are fast-moving upper-air winds.

They blow at high altitude, usually in the upper troposphere.

## 13.2 Westerly Jet Stream

In winter, Western and Central Asia are influenced by westerly winds at about 9-13 km altitude.

These winds move from west to east.

They blow north of the Himalayas, roughly parallel to the Tibetan highlands.

## 13.3 Bifurcation By Tibetan Highlands

The Tibetan highlands act as a barrier.

The jet stream divides into two branches:

- one branch flows north of the Tibetan highlands;
- the other branch flows south of the Himalayas.

The southern branch influences winter weather in India.

## 13.4 Why The Southern Branch Matters

The southern branch helps bring western disturbances into India.

These disturbances are important because they bring winter rain and snowfall to northwestern and northern India.

---

# 14. Western Disturbances And Tropical Cyclones In Winter

## 14.1 Western Disturbances

Western disturbances are cyclonic systems that enter India from the west and northwest during winter.

They originate around the Mediterranean region.

They travel eastward across:

- West Asia,
- Iran,
- Afghanistan,
- Pakistan,
- northwestern India.

## 14.2 Moisture Sources

On their way, their moisture may be increased by:

- the Caspian Sea,
- the Persian Gulf.

## 14.3 Weather Effects

Western disturbances bring:

- winter rainfall in northwestern India,
- snowfall in the Himalayas,
- slight rise in night temperature before arrival,
- useful moisture for rabi crops.

## 14.4 Rainfall Areas

They affect:

- Punjab,
- Haryana,
- Delhi,
- western Uttar Pradesh,
- Himalayan regions.

Winter rainfall decreases from west to east in the plains.

It also decreases from north to south in the mountains.

## 14.5 Tropical Cyclones

Tropical cyclones also occur over:

- the Bay of Bengal,
- the Indian Ocean.

They may affect:

- Tamil Nadu coast,
- Andhra Pradesh coast,
- Odisha coast.

They bring:

- high wind speed,
- heavy rainfall,
- destructive storm conditions.

# 15. Cold Weather Season

## 15.1 Timing

The cold weather season usually begins by mid-November in northern India.

December and January are the coldest months in the northern plain.

## 15.2 Temperature

In much of northern India:

- mean daily temperature remains below 21 degrees C;
- night temperature may fall very low;
- in Punjab and Rajasthan, night temperature may sometimes fall below freezing.

## 15.3 Why North India Becomes Very Cold

There are three major reasons:

1. Punjab, Haryana, and Rajasthan are far from the moderating influence of the sea.
2. Snowfall in nearby Himalayan ranges creates cold-wave conditions.
3. Cold winds from the Caspian Sea and Turkmenistan region may bring frost and fog to northwestern India.

## 15.4 Peninsular India In Winter

Peninsular India does not have a sharply defined cold season.

Reasons:

- it is closer to the equator;
- it is surrounded by seas;
- coastal areas are moderated by oceanic influence.

For example:

- Thiruvananthapuram remains warm even in January;
- hills of the Western Ghats are cooler due to altitude.

## 15.5 Pressure And Winds

By late December, the sun is over the Tropic of Capricorn in the southern hemisphere.

In India:

- high pressure develops over the northern plain;
- pressure is slightly lower over south India and the Indian Ocean;
- light winds blow outward from northwestern India.

These winds are:

- weak,
- dry,
- often northwesterly or northeasterly depending on topography.

## 15.6 Winter Rainfall

Winter monsoon winds generally do not bring rainfall because:

- they blow from land to sea,
- they have little moisture,
- anti-cyclonic conditions reduce rainfall.

Important exceptions:

- northwestern India receives rain from western disturbances;
- lower Himalayas receive snowfall;
- central India and northern parts of the southern peninsula may receive occasional winter rain;
- Arunachal Pradesh and Assam receive some winter rain;
- Tamil Nadu and nearby regions receive rain from northeast monsoon winds after they cross the Bay of Bengal.

## 15.7 Why Winter Rain Is Important

Winter rain in northwestern India is small in quantity but important for rabi crops.

Snowfall in the Himalayas also helps sustain Himalayan rivers during summer.

# 16. Hot Weather Season

## 16.1 Timing

The hot weather season is mainly March to June in north India.

It begins as the sun appears to move northward toward the Tropic of Cancer.

## 16.2 Temperature Pattern

Temperatures rise gradually from March.

Important pattern:

- March: high day temperature appears over the Deccan Plateau;
- April: high temperatures spread over Gujarat and Madhya Pradesh;
- May: the heat belt moves farther north;
- northwest India may record very high temperatures.

## 16.3 North India vs South India

North India has a more intense hot season.

South India has a milder hot season because:

- it is peninsular,
- it is surrounded by seas,
- the ocean moderates temperature,
- coastal isotherms show strong maritime influence.

## 16.4 Pressure And Winds

In summer, northern India becomes intensely heated.

This leads to:

- falling air pressure,
- northward movement of ITCZ,
- development of a long low-pressure trough,
- attraction of moist winds from the ocean.

The monsoon trough extends roughly from the Thar Desert toward the eastern parts of India.

## 16.5 Loo

The loo is a hot, dry, oppressive wind.

It blows mainly in the northern plains from Punjab to Bihar.

It is especially intense between Delhi and Patna.

It usually blows in the afternoon and may continue into the night.

## 16.6 Dust Storms

Dust storms are common in May in:

- Punjab,
- Haryana,
- eastern Rajasthan,
- Uttar Pradesh.

They bring temporary relief from intense heat.

Sometimes they bring light rain and a cool breeze.

## 16.7 Local Storms

When dry and moist air masses meet suddenly, violent local storms may form.

These storms may include:

- strong winds,
- torrential rain,
- hailstorms,
- thunder.

# 17. Famous Local Storms Of The Hot Weather Season

## 17.1 Mango Showers

Mango showers are pre-monsoon showers in Kerala and coastal Karnataka.

They help in the early ripening of mangoes.

## 17.2 Blossom Showers

Blossom showers help coffee flowers bloom.

They occur in Kerala and nearby coffee-growing regions.

## 17.3 Nor Westers

Nor Westers are evening thunderstorms in Bengal and Assam.

In Bengal, they are locally known as Kalbaisakhi.

They can be destructive, but they are also useful for:

- tea,
- jute,
- rice cultivation.

In Assam, such storms are known by a local name mentioned in the textbook.

## 17.4 Loo

The loo is not a rainfall storm.

It is a hot, dry wind of the northern plains.

It causes heat stress and marks the severity of the pre-monsoon season.

# 18. Mechanism Of Weather In Summer

## 18.1 Complete Reversal

As summer sets in and the sun shifts northward, wind circulation over India changes strongly.

The reversal happens:

- near the surface,
- in the upper atmosphere.

## 18.2 Northward Shift Of ITCZ

By mid-July, the low-pressure belt near the surface shifts northward.

It lies roughly parallel to the Himalayas between about 20 degrees N and 25 degrees N.

This belt is called the ITCZ.

In India during summer, it is also called the monsoon trough.

## 18.3 Withdrawal Of Westerly Jet

By this time, the westerly jet stream withdraws from the Indian region.

The northward movement of ITCZ and withdrawal of the westerly jet are related.

This relationship is important in the development of the monsoon.

## 18.4 Arrival Of Moist Air

The ITCZ is a low-pressure zone.

It attracts winds from different directions.

Moist maritime tropical air from the southern hemisphere crosses the equator and moves toward India.

Because of the Coriolis force, it moves in a southwesterly direction.

This moist air current is known as the southwest monsoon.

---

# 19. Inter Tropical Convergence Zone, Or ITCZ

## 19.1 Definition

The ITCZ is a low-pressure zone where trade winds converge and air rises.

It is normally located near the equator, but it shifts north and south with the apparent movement of the sun.

## 19.2 ITCZ In July

In July, the ITCZ shifts northward over the Gangetic plain.

Its position around 20 degrees N to 25 degrees N is called the monsoon trough.

## 19.3 Role In Monsoon

The monsoon trough:

- encourages the thermal low over north and northwest India;
- attracts moist winds;
- helps form tropical depressions;
- influences rainfall distribution.

## 19.4 Trade Winds And Coriolis Force

Trade winds from the southern hemisphere cross the equator between about 40 degrees E and 60 degrees E.

After crossing the equator, they are deflected.

They begin to blow from southwest to northeast.

This becomes the southwest monsoon.

## 19.5 Winter Position

In winter, the ITCZ moves southward.

This helps reverse winds from northeast toward south and southwest.

These are called northeast monsoon winds.

---

# 20. Easterly Jet Stream And Tropical Depressions

## 20.1 Easterly Jet In Summer

An easterly jet stream flows over the southern part of the peninsula in June.

Its speed may be very high.

It is generally strongest during the early monsoon period.

## 20.2 Position

The easterly jet stream:

- appears over southern India in June,
- is around 15 degrees N in August,
- shifts toward about 22 degrees N in September,
- usually does not extend north of 30 degrees N.

## 20.3 Role In Rainfall

The easterly jet stream steers tropical depressions into India.

These depressions are important because:

- they bring heavy rain,
- their tracks become zones of high rainfall,
- their frequency affects monsoon strength,
- their direction affects regional distribution of rain.

## 20.4 Exam Use

If asked why rainfall varies during the southwest monsoon, mention:

- position of monsoon trough,
- movement of tropical depressions,
- frequency of depressions,
- relief,
- distance from sea,
- breaks in monsoon.

# 21. Nature Of The Indian Monsoon

## 21.1 Difficult To Explain Fully

The monsoon has been observed for centuries.

Still, no single theory explains it completely.

It is a complex system involving:

- land-sea heating,
- pressure differences,
- ITCZ,
- jet streams,
- ocean currents,
- tropical depressions,
- global circulation.

## 21.2 Important Aspects

The chapter highlights three important aspects:

1. onset of the monsoon,
2. rain-bearing systems and rainfall distribution,
3. breaks in the monsoon.

## 21.3 Modern Understanding

A better understanding came when scientists studied the monsoon at a global level, not only as a regional land-sea breeze.

This is why systems like El Nino, Southern Oscillation, jet streams, and ocean temperatures matter.

# 22. Onset Of The Monsoon

## 22.1 Land-Sea Heating

During April and May, the sun shines almost vertically over the Tropic of Cancer.

The landmass north of the Indian Ocean heats intensely.

This creates a strong low-pressure area over northwestern India and adjoining regions.

## 22.2 Oceanic High Pressure

The Indian Ocean to the south heats more slowly.

Therefore pressure remains relatively higher over the ocean.

The low pressure over land attracts winds from the ocean.

## 22.3 Crossing The Equator

Southeast trade winds from the southern hemisphere cross the equator.

After crossing, they are deflected and become southwest monsoon winds.

They cross the equator mainly between about 40 degrees E and 60 degrees E.

## 22.4 Relation With Jet Streams

The onset is also linked with:

- northward shift of ITCZ,
- withdrawal of the westerly jet stream,
- establishment of the easterly jet stream.

The easterly jet stream is linked with the burst of monsoon.

## 22.5 Entry Into India

The southwest monsoon normally:

- reaches the Kerala coast around 1 June;
- reaches Mumbai and Kolkata around 10-13 June;
- covers most of the subcontinent by mid-July.

These dates are normal averages, not fixed dates every year.

# 23. Burst Of The Monsoon

## 23.1 Meaning

The burst of monsoon means the sudden onset of moisture-laden winds with rain, thunder, and lightning.

It is called a burst because rainfall begins abruptly after the hot dry season.

## 23.2 Weather Change

The burst brings:

- sudden rain,
- thunder,
- lightning,
- strong winds,
- fall in temperature,
- relief from pre-monsoon heat.

## 23.3 Regional Timing

The burst may occur:

- in the first week of June over Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, and Maharashtra coasts;
- later in interior parts of the country, often toward early July.

## 23.4 Temperature Effect

Day temperature may fall noticeably after the monsoon arrives.

This is because:

- cloud cover increases,
- rain cools the surface,
- moist air replaces hot dry air.

# 24. Rain-Bearing Systems

India has two main rain-bearing systems during the southwest monsoon.

## 24.1 Bay Of Bengal System

The Bay of Bengal branch causes rainfall over large parts of northern and northeastern India.

It is especially important for:

- West Bengal,
- Bangladesh region,
- Assam,
- Meghalaya,
- Brahmaputra valley,
- Ganga plain.

## 24.2 Arabian Sea System

The Arabian Sea branch brings rain to the west coast of India.

It is strongly influenced by the Western Ghats.

Moist winds rise along the Ghats and produce orographic rainfall.

## 24.3 West Coast Rainfall

Rainfall over the west coast depends on:

- offshore meteorological conditions,
- position of the equatorial jet stream near eastern Africa,
- strength of moisture-laden winds,
- relief of Western Ghats.

# 25. El Nino And The Indian Monsoon

## 25.1 Meaning

El Nino is a complex ocean-atmosphere system.

It appears once every few years and can disturb weather in many parts of the world.

## 25.2 Location

It is associated with warm ocean currents off the coast of Peru in the eastern Pacific Ocean.

It temporarily weakens or replaces the usual cold Peruvian, or Humboldt, current.

## 25.3 Effects Near Peru

The warm current may:

- increase water temperature near the Peruvian coast,
- disturb equatorial atmospheric circulation,
- change evaporation patterns,
- reduce plankton,
- reduce fish availability.

## 25.4 Meaning Of The Name

El Nino means Child Christ because the warm current often appears around Christmas.

December is summer in Peru because Peru lies in the southern hemisphere.

## 25.5 Link With India

El Nino is used as one indicator in long-range monsoon forecasting.

It may be associated with:

- delayed monsoon onset,
- drought conditions,
- flood conditions in some regions,
- irregular rainfall,
- extreme weather.

It does not automatically mean drought every time, but it is an important warning signal.

## 25.6 Example

The textbook mentions the 1990-91 El Nino event, when the southwest monsoon onset was delayed over many parts of India by several days.

# 26. Breaks In The Monsoon

## 26.1 Meaning

A break in the monsoon occurs when rainfall stops for one or more weeks after a wet spell during the southwest monsoon season.

It is a dry spell within the rainy season.

## 26.2 Why Breaks Occur In North India

In northern India, rainfall may fail when rain-bearing storms are not frequent along the monsoon trough or ITCZ.

If depressions do not move along the trough, rainfall weakens.

## 26.3 Why Breaks Occur On The West Coast

On the west coast, dry spells may occur when winds blow parallel to the coast.

When winds do not strike the Western Ghats effectively, orographic uplift weakens and rainfall reduces.

## 26.4 Importance

Breaks affect:

- sowing,
- crop growth,
- soil moisture,
- reservoir storage,
- drought risk,
- flood timing.

## 27.2 Why Seasons Matter

- crop calendar,
- festivals,
- water storage,
- travel,

# 28. Southwest Monsoon Season

## 28.1 General Mechanism

By early June, low pressure over northwest India becomes strong.

It attracts trade winds from the southern hemisphere.

These winds:

- cross the equator,
- enter the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal,
- collect moisture,
- turn southwesterly,
- reach India as the southwest monsoon.

## 28.2 Moisture Supply

The winds pass over warm equatorial waters.

This allows them to collect abundant moisture.

When they reach land and rise, they release rainfall.

## 28.3 Two Main Branches

The southwest monsoon approaches India in two branches:

1. Arabian Sea branch,
2. Bay of Bengal branch.

Both branches are essential for India's rainfall.

---

# 29. Arabian Sea Branch

The Arabian Sea branch splits into three major sub-branches.

## 29.1 Western Ghats Branch

One branch strikes the Western Ghats.

The moist winds climb the slopes of the Ghats.

As they rise:

- they cool,
- condensation occurs,
- heavy rainfall falls on the windward side.

The windward side of the Sahyadris and the Western Coastal Plain receive very heavy rainfall, often between about 250 cm and 400 cm.

After crossing the Ghats, winds descend and become warmer.

Their humidity decreases.

So the eastern side receives much less rain.

This creates the rain shadow region.

## 29.2 Narmada-Tapi Branch

Another branch strikes the coast north of Mumbai.

It moves along the Narmada and Tapi valleys.

It causes rainfall in parts of central India.

It later enters the Ganga plain and merges with the Bay of Bengal branch.

## 29.3 Saurashtra-Kachchh-Rajasthan Branch

A third branch strikes Saurashtra and Kachchh.

It then moves over west Rajasthan and along the Aravalis.

Because the Aravalis are not ideally placed to block these winds, rainfall is scanty.

This branch later joins the Bay of Bengal branch over Punjab and Haryana.

Together, the branches bring rain to parts of the western Himalayas.

---

# 30. Bay Of Bengal Branch

## 30.1 Entry Direction

The Bay of Bengal branch first moves toward Myanmar and southeast Bangladesh.

The Arakan Hills deflect a large part of it toward India.

As a result, it enters West Bengal and Bangladesh from the south and southeast.

## 30.2 Two Sub-Branches

After entering the region, it splits into two parts:

1. one branch moves westward along the Ganga plain;
2. another branch moves up the Brahmaputra valley toward the northeast.

## 30.3 Ganga Plain Branch

The westward branch moves along the Ganga plain.

It reaches as far as the Punjab plains.

Rainfall generally decreases as the branch moves farther inland.

## 30.4 Northeastern Branch

The northeastern branch moves along the Brahmaputra valley.

It causes widespread rainfall in northeastern India.

One sub-branch strikes the Garo and Khasi hills of Meghalaya.

Mawsynram, on the crest of the Khasi Hills, receives the highest average annual rainfall in the world.

## 30.5 Why Tamil Nadu Is Dry During Southwest Monsoon

Tamil Nadu remains relatively dry during the southwest monsoon because:

1. the Tamil Nadu coast lies almost parallel to the Bay of Bengal branch;
2. it lies in the rain shadow of the Arabian Sea branch after the winds cross the Western Ghats.

This is a very common exam question.

---

# 31. Characteristics Of Monsoonal Rainfall

## 31.1 Seasonal Character

Most monsoonal rainfall occurs between June and September.

This is the southwest monsoon season.

## 31.2 Relief Control

Relief strongly controls rainfall.

Examples:

- windward Western Ghats receive heavy rainfall;
- northeastern hills receive heavy rainfall;
- rain shadow areas receive less rainfall.

## 31.3 Distance From Sea

Rainfall decreases with increasing distance from the sea.

Examples from the southwest monsoon period:

| Place | Rainfall Pattern |
| --- | --- |
| Kolkata | Higher rainfall |
| Patna | Less than Kolkata |
| Allahabad | Less than Patna |
| Delhi | Less than Allahabad |

The broad principle is inland decline.

## 31.4 Wet Spells And Breaks

Monsoon rain does not fall evenly every day.

It comes in wet spells.

These wet spells are interrupted by breaks.

## 31.5 Heavy Downpour

Monsoon rain often falls as heavy downpour.

This may cause:

- runoff,
- soil erosion,
- floods,
- waterlogging.

## 31.6 Agricultural Importance

More than three-fourths of India's annual rainfall is received during the southwest monsoon season.

This makes it crucial for:

- kharif crops,
- soil moisture,
- groundwater recharge,
- rivers,
- reservoirs,
- rural economy.

## 31.7 Uncertainty

The monsoon may:

- begin late,
- withdraw early,
- have long breaks,
- have uneven distribution.

Such irregularity can damage crops and affect food production.

---

# 32. Retreating Monsoon Season

## 32.1 Timing

October and November are the main months of retreating monsoon.

## 32.2 Why It Retreats

By the end of September, the southwest monsoon weakens.

The low-pressure trough over the Ganga plain moves southward as the sun moves south.

Winds start withdrawing from northern and northwestern India.

## 32.3 Withdrawal Pattern

The monsoon usually:

- retreats from western Rajasthan by the first week of September;
- withdraws from Rajasthan, Gujarat, the western Ganga plain, and central highlands by the end of September;
- shifts low pressure toward the Bay of Bengal and southern peninsula in October-November;
- withdraws completely from the peninsula by around mid-December.

## 32.4 October Heat

The retreating monsoon season has:

- clear skies,
- high temperature,
- moist land,
- high humidity.

These conditions create oppressive weather called October heat.

## 32.5 Rainfall In Eastern Peninsula

North India is mostly dry during this season.

But the eastern peninsula receives rain.

For Tamil Nadu and the Coromandel coast, October and November are very important rainy months.

## 32.6 Cyclonic Depressions

Many cyclonic depressions originate over the Andaman Sea.

They cross the eastern coast of the southern peninsula.

They may be destructive, especially in the deltas of:

- Godavari,
- Krishna,
- Kaveri.

Some cyclones also affect:

- West Bengal,
- Bangladesh,
- Myanmar.

Cyclones are less frequent in the Arabian Sea than in the Bay of Bengal during this period.

# 34. Distribution Of Rainfall In India

## 34.1 Average Rainfall

India's average annual rainfall is about 125 cm.

But the distribution is highly uneven.

Some areas receive extremely high rainfall, while others receive very little.

## 34.2 Areas Of High Rainfall

Rainfall above 200 cm occurs in:

- west coast,
- Western Ghats,
- sub-Himalayan areas of the northeast,
- hills of Meghalaya.

In parts of Khasi and Jaintia hills, rainfall may exceed 1,000 cm.

The Brahmaputra valley and adjoining hills receive less than the wettest Meghalaya hill locations.

## 34.3 Areas Of Medium Rainfall

Rainfall between 100 cm and 200 cm occurs in:

- southern Gujarat,
- eastern Tamil Nadu,
- Odisha,
- Jharkhand,
- Bihar,
- eastern Madhya Pradesh,
- northern Ganga plain along the sub-Himalayas,
- Cachar Valley,
- Manipur.

## 34.4 Areas Of Low Rainfall

Rainfall between 50 cm and 100 cm occurs in:

- western Uttar Pradesh,
- Delhi,
- Haryana,
- Punjab,
- Jammu and Kashmir,
- eastern Rajasthan,
- Gujarat,
- Deccan Plateau.

## 34.5 Areas Of Inadequate Rainfall

Rainfall below 50 cm occurs in:

- parts of Andhra Pradesh,
- parts of Karnataka,
- parts of Maharashtra,
- Ladakh,
- most of western Rajasthan.

## 34.6 Snowfall

Snowfall is largely restricted to the Himalayan region.

In most other parts of India, precipitation occurs as rain.

# 35. Variability Of Rainfall

## 35.1 Meaning

Rainfall variability means the degree to which actual rainfall differs from average rainfall.

In India, rainfall may vary strongly from year to year.

## 35.2 Formula

The coefficient of variation is calculated as:

```text
C.V. = (Standard deviation / Mean rainfall) x 100
```

Where:

- C.V. means coefficient of variation;
- standard deviation shows spread from the average;
- mean rainfall is average rainfall.

## 35.3 Interpretation

Higher coefficient of variation means rainfall is less dependable.

Lower coefficient of variation means rainfall is more dependable.

## 35.4 Areas With Less Than 25 Percent Variability

These areas generally have annual rainfall above 100 cm.

They include:

- western coasts,
- Western Ghats,
- northeastern Peninsula,
- eastern plains of the Ganga,
- northeastern India,
- Uttarakhand,
- Himachal Pradesh,
- southwestern Jammu and Kashmir.

## 35.5 Areas With More Than 50 Percent Variability

These areas generally have annual rainfall below 50 cm.

They include:

- western Rajasthan,
- northern Jammu and Kashmir,
- interior parts of the Deccan Plateau.

## 35.6 Areas With 25-50 Percent Variability

Most remaining areas fall in this category.

They receive annual rainfall between about 50 cm and 100 cm.

## 35.7 Exam Insight

Low rainfall areas often have high variability.

This means drought risk is greater where rainfall is already low.

# 36. Climatic Regions Of India

## 36.1 Meaning Of Climatic Region

A climatic region is an area with broadly homogeneous climatic conditions.

It is identified by the combination of climatic elements such as:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- seasonal rhythm,
- dryness,
- humidity.

## 36.2 Why Classification Is Needed

India has a monsoon climate overall.

But the country contains many regional variations.

Climatic classification helps organise these variations.

## 36.3 Basis Of Koeppen's Classification

Koeppen's classification uses monthly values of:

- temperature,
- precipitation.

These two elements are considered decisive.

---

# 37. Koeppen's Major Climatic Types

## 37.1 A - Tropical Climates

Mean monthly temperature remains above 18 degrees C throughout the year.

These climates are warm year-round.

## 37.2 B - Dry Climates

Precipitation is low in comparison to temperature.

Subtypes:

- S means semi-arid;
- W means arid.

## 37.3 C - Warm Temperate Climates

The mean temperature of the coldest month lies between 18 degrees C and minus 3 degrees C.

## 37.4 D - Cool Temperate Climates

The warmest month has mean temperature above 10 degrees C.

The coldest month has mean temperature below minus 3 degrees C.

## 37.5 E - Ice Climates

The warmest month has mean temperature below 10 degrees C.

These are very cold climates.

# 40. Monsoon And Economic Life In India

## 40.1 Agriculture

The monsoon is the axis of India's agricultural cycle.

A large share of India's population depends on agriculture, and agriculture depends heavily on the southwest monsoon.

## 40.2 Crop Calendar

The timing of rain affects:

- sowing,
- transplanting,
- crop growth,
- harvesting,
- irrigation demand.

Delayed rain can delay sowing.

Early withdrawal can damage standing crops.

## 40.3 Temperature Advantage

Except in the Himalayas, much of India has temperatures above the threshold needed for crop growth during most of the year.

This allows a wide variety of crops.

## 40.4 Regional Crop Diversity

Regional variations in rainfall and temperature allow different crops to grow in different regions.

Examples:

- rice in wet areas,
- millets in drier regions,
- tea in humid northeastern hills,
- coffee in parts of the Western Ghats,
- wheat where winter moisture helps rabi cultivation.

## 40.5 Droughts And Floods

Rainfall variability brings droughts and floods almost every year in some part of India.

The same monsoon may cause:

- drought in one region,
- flood in another region.

## 40.6 Soil Erosion

Sudden heavy rainfall during the monsoon may cause:

- runoff,
- sheet erosion,
- gully erosion,
- loss of fertile topsoil.

## 40.7 Rabi Crops

Winter rainfall from western disturbances is highly beneficial for rabi crops in northwestern India.

Even small amounts of winter rain can be agriculturally valuable.

## 40.8 Daily Life

Regional climatic variation is reflected in:

- food habits,
- clothing,
- house types,
- festivals,
- work patterns,
- water storage practices.

# 41. Global Warming

## 41.1 Meaning

Global warming means rise in the average temperature of the earth's surface.

Climate has changed naturally in the past, but current warming is strongly linked with human activities.

## 41.2 Human Causes

Major human causes include:

- large-scale industrialisation,
- burning of fossil fuels,
- increasing carbon dioxide concentration,
- release of methane,
- release of chlorofluorocarbons,
- release of nitrous oxide,
- deforestation and land-use change.

## 41.3 Greenhouse Effect

Greenhouse gases absorb long-wave radiation.

This traps heat in the atmosphere.

Important greenhouse gases include:

- carbon dioxide,
- methane,
- chlorofluorocarbons,
- nitrous oxide.

## 41.4 Why Carbon Dioxide Matters

Carbon dioxide is a major concern because it is released in large quantities by burning fossil fuels.

Its concentration has increased with industrial activity.

## 41.5 Possible Effects

Global warming may cause:

- melting of polar ice caps,
- melting of mountain glaciers,
- rise in sea level,
- more frequent flooding in coastal areas,
- spread of insect-borne diseases such as malaria,
- shifting climatic boundaries,
- wetter conditions in some regions,
- drier conditions in others,
- changes in agriculture,
- stress on ecosystems,
- impact on human population.

## 41.6 Relevance For India

India has a long coastline and many densely populated deltas.

Sea-level rise can affect:

- coastal settlements,
- ports,
- agriculture in deltas,
- groundwater salinity,
- cyclone damage,
- mangrove ecosystems.

The chapter asks learners to think about what would happen to Indian coasts if sea level rises significantly.

# 45. Common Misunderstandings

## 45.1 Monsoon Means Only Rain

Incorrect.

Monsoon means seasonal reversal of winds.

Rainfall is a major result, but not the full meaning.

## 45.2 All India Gets Rain In June-September

Incorrect.

Most of India receives major rainfall in June-September, but Tamil Nadu and parts of the Coromandel coast receive important rainfall in October-November.

## 45.3 Coastal Areas Are Always Cooler

Partly correct but incomplete.

Coastal areas are moderated, so they have smaller temperature range.

They may still be warm and humid.

## 45.4 Low Rainfall Means Low Variability

Incorrect.

Low rainfall areas often have high variability.

This makes them more drought-prone.

## 45.5 Western Disturbances Are Part Of Southwest Monsoon

Incorrect.

Western disturbances are mainly winter systems.

They come from the west and bring rain to northwest India and snow to the Himalayas.

## 45.6 Tamil Nadu Is Wet During Peak Southwest Monsoon

Incorrect.

Tamil Nadu is relatively dry during southwest monsoon because of branch direction and rain shadow conditions.

It receives much of its rain during the retreating monsoon season.

## 47.2 Short Answer: Three Factors Influencing Indian Weather

The three important factors are:

1. distribution of surface pressure and winds,

3. inflow of western disturbances in winter and tropical depressions during the southwest monsoon.

## 47.3 Short Answer: ITCZ

The ITCZ is a low-pressure zone where trade winds from both hemispheres converge and air rises.

In July it shifts northward over the Gangetic plain, where it is called the monsoon trough.

This trough helps attract moist monsoon winds toward India.

## 47.4 Short Answer: Burst Of Monsoon

Burst of monsoon means the sudden onset of moisture-laden southwest monsoon winds with thunder, lightning, and heavy rain.

It causes a noticeable fall in temperature after the hot weather season.

Mawsynram in the Khasi Hills of Meghalaya receives the highest average annual rainfall in the world.

## 47.5 Short Answer: Climatic Region And Koeppen's Basis

A climatic region is an area with broadly similar climatic conditions.

Koeppen classified climates mainly on the basis of monthly temperature and precipitation.

## 47.6 Short Answer: Winter Cyclones In Northwestern India

Northwestern India receives winter rainfall from western cyclonic disturbances.

They originate near the Mediterranean region and travel eastward with the westerly jet stream.

They bring rain to Punjab, Haryana, Delhi, and western Uttar Pradesh, and snow to the Himalayas.

## 48.3 Mechanism Of Southwest Monsoon

Use this structure:

1. Land heats intensely in April-May.
2. Low pressure forms over northwest India.
3. Indian Ocean remains relatively high pressure.
4. Southeast trades cross the equator.
5. Coriolis force deflects them into southwest winds.
6. ITCZ shifts northward and becomes monsoon trough.
7. Westerly jet withdraws and easterly jet develops.
8. Moisture-laden winds enter India in two branches.
9. Relief and depressions distribute rainfall.

## 50.1 One-Line Definitions

- Monsoon: seasonal reversal of wind direction.
- ITCZ: low-pressure convergence zone of trade winds.
- Monsoon trough: northward summer position of ITCZ over India.
- Burst of monsoon: sudden arrival of monsoon rain.
- Break in monsoon: dry spell during rainy season.
- Rain shadow: dry leeward region behind a mountain barrier.
- Western disturbance: winter cyclonic system from the west.
- El Nino: periodic warming of eastern Pacific waters affecting global weather.

- Mawsynram: highest average annual rainfall.
- Jaisalmer: very low rainfall.
- Western Ghats: orographic rainfall.
- Tamil Nadu coast: retreating monsoon rain.

- Thar Desert: hot weather and low rainfall.

## 50.3 Must-Remember Processes

- Land-water heating creates pressure difference.
- Pressure difference creates wind movement.
- ITCZ shifts north in summer.
- Southeast trades cross equator.

- Relief redistributes rainfall.
- Monsoon trough and depressions create wet spells.
- Breaks interrupt rainfall.
- Monsoon withdraws from northwest to southeast.

# 51. Final Chapter Summary

India's climate is controlled by location, relief, pressure systems, winds, jet streams, and ocean-atmosphere processes.

The monsoon gives India a broad climatic unity because most regions follow a seasonal rhythm of heat, rain, withdrawal, and winter.

At the same time, India has strong climatic diversity. Rajasthan may be extremely hot, Ladakh may be extremely cold, Meghalaya may be extremely wet, and western Rajasthan may be extremely dry.

The southwest monsoon forms because of intense heating over land, low pressure over northwest India, crossing of southeast trade winds, Coriolis deflection, northward ITCZ shift, and changes in jet streams.

The monsoon enters India in two branches: Arabian Sea branch and Bay of Bengal branch. Relief, distance from sea, and tropical depressions decide where rainfall becomes heavy or weak.

India's rainfall is highly uneven and variable. This makes the monsoon both a resource and a risk.

The monsoon is central to India's agriculture, economy, water availability, and daily life.

Global warming adds a new concern because it may affect sea level, coasts, diseases, rainfall patterns, agriculture, ecosystems, and human settlements.

To master this chapter, learn the processes, not just the facts. Every good answer should connect wind, pressure, relief, rainfall, season, and human impact.
