# Chapter 3: Drainage System

### NCERT Class 11 - Geography India

---

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how India's rivers are organised into drainage systems.

Rivers are not isolated lines on a map. Each river drains a definite area, receives tributaries, follows the slope and structure of the land, carries sediments, creates landforms, supports agriculture and settlements, and also produces floods, disputes, pollution, and management challenges.

The central idea is:

> A drainage system is the network of river channels through which water is collected, transported, and finally discharged into a sea, lake, depression, or another river.

# 1. Meaning Of Drainage

## 1.1 Drainage

The flow of water through well-defined channels is called drainage.

During the rainy season, water flows through:

- rivers,
- streams,
- nalas,
- channels.

If channels are blocked or poorly defined, flooding becomes common.

## 1.2 Drainage System

A drainage system is the network of channels through which water flows in an area.

It includes:

- main river,
- tributaries,
- smaller streams,
- distributaries,
- channels.

## 1.3 Why Drainage Matters

Drainage affects:

- flooding,
- agriculture,
- settlement,
- transport,
- soil erosion,
- groundwater recharge,
- water supply,
- river pollution,
- planning and development.

---

# 2. Drainage Pattern

## 2.1 Meaning

A drainage pattern is the arrangement of river channels in an area.

It depends on:

- geological age,
- rock structure,
- slope,
- topography,
- amount of water,
- seasonal flow,
- tectonic history.

## 2.2 Dendritic Pattern

A dendritic pattern looks like the branches of a tree.

It develops where rocks are fairly uniform and streams follow the general slope.

Example:

- rivers of the Northern Plain.

## 2.3 Radial Pattern

A radial pattern develops when rivers originate from a hill or highland and flow outward in all directions.

Example:

- rivers originating from the Amarkantak region.

## 2.4 Trellis Pattern

A trellis pattern develops when primary tributaries flow parallel to each other and secondary tributaries join them at near right angles.

It is common in regions with alternating hard and soft rocks or folded structures.

## 2.5 Centripetal Pattern

A centripetal pattern develops when rivers flow inward from all directions into a lake, basin, or depression.

This is the opposite of radial drainage.

---

# 3. Catchment Area, Basin, And Watershed

## 3.1 Catchment Area

The area from which a river collects water is called its catchment area.

Rainfall, snowmelt, springs, and tributaries within this area contribute water to the river.

## 3.2 Drainage Basin

An area drained by a river and its tributaries is called a drainage basin.

Example:

The Ganga basin includes the area drained by the Ganga and its tributaries.

## 3.3 River Basin

The catchment of a large river is often called a river basin.

Examples:

- Ganga Basin,
- Brahmaputra Basin,
- Indus Basin,
- Godavari Basin.

## 3.4 Watershed

The boundary line separating one drainage basin from another is called a watershed or water divide.

The term watershed is also used for smaller drainage units.

## 3.5 Basin Versus Watershed

| Basis | River Basin | Watershed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Size | Larger area | Smaller area or boundary unit |
| Example | Ganga Basin | Local catchment divided by a ridge |
| Planning level | Macro or meso level | Micro level |
| Meaning | Area drained by a river and tributaries | Divide or smaller drainage unit |

## 3.6 Unity Of Basin

River basins and watersheds are integrated units.

What happens in one part affects other parts.

Examples:

- deforestation upstream increases flood risk downstream,
- pollution in one tributary affects the main river,
- dams alter flow and sediment downstream,
- soil erosion increases silt load.

This is why basins and watersheds are useful planning regions.

# 4. Classification Of Indian Drainage

Indian drainage can be classified in three major ways:

1. by direction of discharge,
2. by size of basin,
3. by origin and characteristics.

---

# 5. Classification By Direction Of Discharge

On the basis of sea outlet, Indian drainage is divided into:

1. Arabian Sea drainage,
2. Bay of Bengal drainage.

## 5.1 Water Divide

The two systems are separated by water divides such as:

- Delhi Ridge,
- Aravalis,
- Sahyadris or Western Ghats.

## 5.2 Bay Of Bengal Drainage

About 77 percent of India's drainage area is oriented toward the Bay of Bengal.

Examples:

- Ganga,
- Brahmaputra,
- Mahanadi,
- Godavari,
- Krishna,
- Kaveri.

## 5.3 Arabian Sea Drainage

About 23 percent of the drainage area is oriented toward the Arabian Sea.

Examples:

- Indus,
- Narmada,
- Tapi,
- Mahi,
- Periyar.

---

# 6. Classification By Basin Size

## 6.1 Major River Basins

Major river basins have catchment areas of more than:

```text
20,000 sq km
```

Examples include:

- Ganga,
- Brahmaputra,
- Krishna,
- Tapi,
- Narmada,
- Mahi,
- Pennar,
- Sabarmati,
- Barak.

## 6.2 Medium River Basins

Medium river basins have catchment areas between:

```text
2,000 sq km and 20,000 sq km
```

Examples include:

- Kalindi,
- Periyar,
- Meghna.

## 6.3 Minor River Basins

Minor river basins have catchment areas of less than:

```text
2,000 sq km
```

Many such rivers are found in areas of low rainfall or along short coastal slopes.

---

# 7. Classification By Origin

Indian rivers are commonly classified into:

1. Himalayan drainage,
2. Peninsular drainage.

This classification is based on:

- origin,
- age,
- flow character,
- valley form,
- drainage pattern,
- seasonal behaviour.

There are some complications because rivers such as Chambal, Betwa, and Son originate in the Peninsular region but join the Ganga system. Still, Himalayan versus Peninsular drainage remains the most accepted broad classification.

---

# 8. Himalayan Drainage

## 8.1 Main Systems

The Himalayan drainage includes three major systems:

1. Indus,
2. Ganga,
3. Brahmaputra.

## 8.2 Perennial Character

Himalayan rivers are perennial because they are fed by:

- melting snow and glaciers,
- monsoon rainfall.

They carry water throughout the year.

## 8.3 Landforms In Mountain Course

In the mountains, Himalayan rivers form:

- deep gorges,
- V-shaped valleys,
- rapids,
- waterfalls.

These are signs of active erosion and youthful river behaviour.

## 8.4 Landforms In Plains

In the plains, Himalayan rivers form:

- flat valleys,
- flood plains,
- ox-bow lakes,
- braided channels,
- meanders,
- deltas.

## 8.5 Shifting Courses

In the plains, these rivers often meander and shift courses.

The Kosi River is known as the "sorrow of Bihar" because of frequent floods and channel shifts.

It brings large quantities of sediment from the Himalayas. When sediment blocks the channel, the river may change its course.

---

# 9. Evolution Of Himalayan Drainage

## 9.1 Indo-Brahma Or Shiwalik River

Some geologists believe that a large river called the Shiwalik or Indo-Brahma once flowed along the length of the Himalayas from Assam to Punjab and onward to Sind.

It is believed to have discharged into the Gulf of Sind during the Miocene period, about 5 to 24 million years ago.

## 9.2 Evidence

Evidence used for this view includes:

- continuity of Shiwalik deposits,
- lacustrine origin of some deposits,
- sands,
- silts,
- clay,
- boulders,
- conglomerates.

## 9.3 Dismemberment

Over time, the Indo-Brahma system was divided into three main drainage systems:

1. Indus and its five tributaries in the west,
2. Ganga and its Himalayan tributaries in the central part,
3. Brahmaputra and its Himalayan tributaries in the east.

## 9.4 Causes

Important causes include:

- Pleistocene upheaval in western Himalayas,
- uplift of the Potwar Plateau or Delhi Ridge,
- creation of water divide between Indus and Ganga systems,
- down-thrusting of the Malda gap between Rajmahal Hills and Meghalaya Plateau,
- diversion of Ganga and Brahmaputra toward the Bay of Bengal.

# 10. Indus River System

## 10.1 General Facts

The Indus, also called Sindhu, is the westernmost Himalayan river in India.

It is one of the world's large river basins.

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Total basin area | About 11,65,000 sq km |
| Basin area in India | About 3,21,289 sq km |
| Total length | About 2,880 km |
| Length in India | About 1,114 km |
| Outlet | Arabian Sea east of Karachi |

## 10.2 Source

The Indus originates near Bokhar Chu in the Tibetan region at an altitude of about:

```text
4,164 m
```

It rises near the Kailash Mountain range.

In Tibet, it is known as:

```text
Singi Khamban, or Lion's Mouth
```

## 10.3 Course

The Indus flows northwest between the Ladakh and Zaskar ranges.

It passes through:

- Ladakh,
- Baltistan.

It cuts across the Ladakh Range and forms a deep gorge near Gilgit.

It enters Pakistan near Chilas in the Dardistan region.

## 10.4 Tributaries

Important Himalayan tributaries include:

- Shyok,
- Gilgit,
- Zaskar,
- Hunza,
- Nubra,
- Shigar,
- Gasting,
- Dras.

Near Attock, it receives the Kabul River.

Other right bank tributaries include:

- Khurram,
- Tochi,
- Gomal,
- Viboa,
- Sangar.

These originate in the Sulaiman ranges.

## 10.5 Panjnad

Panjnad refers to the five rivers of Punjab:

1. Satluj,
2. Beas,
3. Ravi,
4. Chenab,
5. Jhelum.

The Indus receives Panjnad above Mithankot.

## 10.6 Indus In India

The Indus flows in India through the Jammu and Kashmir region shown in the NCERT account.

# 11. Major Tributaries Of The Indus

## 11.1 Jhelum

The Jhelum rises from a spring at Verinag at the foot of the Pir Panjal range.

It flows through:

- Srinagar,
- Wular Lake.

It enters Pakistan through a deep narrow gorge and joins the Chenab near Jhang.

## 11.2 Chenab

The Chenab is the largest tributary of the Indus.

It is formed by the meeting of:

- Chandra,
- Bhaga.

They meet at Tandi near Keylong in Himachal Pradesh.

The river is also called Chandrabhaga.

It flows about 1,180 km before entering Pakistan.

## 11.3 Ravi

The Ravi rises west of Rohtang Pass in the Kullu hills of Himachal Pradesh.

It flows through Chamba Valley and joins the Chenab near Sarai Sidhu in Pakistan.

## 11.4 Beas

The Beas originates from Beas Kund near Rohtang Pass at about 4,000 m.

It flows through Kullu Valley and forms gorges at Kati and Largi in the Dhauladhar Range.

It enters the Punjab plains and meets the Satluj near Harike.

## 11.5 Satluj

The Satluj originates in Rakas Tal near Mansarovar in Tibet at about 4,555 m.

In Tibet, it is known as Langchen Khambab.

It flows almost parallel to the Indus for about 400 km before entering India.

It passes through Shipki La and enters the Punjab plains.

It is an antecedent river and feeds the canal system of the Bhakra Nangal project.

# 12. Ganga River System

## 12.1 Importance

The Ganga is India's most important river in terms of:

- basin size,
- cultural significance,
- population support,
- agriculture,
- settlement.

## 12.2 Source

The Ganga rises in the Gangotri glacier near Gaumukh at about 3,900 m in Uttarkashi district of Uttarakhand.

Here, it is called:

```text
Bhagirathi
```

## 12.3 Formation Of Ganga

At Devprayag, the Bhagirathi meets the Alaknanda.

After this confluence, the river is known as the Ganga.

## 12.4 Alaknanda System

The Alaknanda originates from the Satopanth glacier above Badrinath.

Important confluences:

| Confluence | Rivers |
| --- | --- |
| Vishnuprayag | Dhauli Ganga and Vishnu Ganga meet Alaknanda system |
| Karnaprayag | Pindar joins Alaknanda |
| Rudraprayag | Mandakini joins Alaknanda |
| Devprayag | Bhagirathi and Alaknanda form Ganga |

## 12.5 Course And Length

The Ganga enters the plains at Haridwar.

It flows first south, then southeast and east.

It finally enters the Bay of Bengal near Sagar Island.

Its total length is about:

```text
2,525 km
```

Approximate state-wise course:

| State | Length |
| --- | ---: |
| Uttarakhand | 110 km |
| Uttar Pradesh | 1,450 km |
| Bihar | 445 km |
| West Bengal | 520 km |

The Ganga basin covers about:

```text
8.6 lakh sq km in India
```

## 12.6 Tributaries

Major right bank tributary:

- Son.

Important left bank tributaries:

- Ramganga,
- Gomati,
- Ghaghara,
- Gandak,
- Kosi,
- Mahananda.

# 13. Yamuna And Other Ganga Tributaries

## 13.1 Yamuna

The Yamuna is the westernmost and longest tributary of the Ganga.

It originates from the Yamunotri glacier on the western slopes of the Banderpunch range.

It joins the Ganga at Prayag, now Prayagraj.

Right bank tributaries include:

- Chambal,
- Sind,
- Betwa,
- Ken.

Left bank tributaries include:

- Hindan,
- Rind,
- Sengar,
- Varuna.

The Yamuna also feeds irrigation canals such as the Western Yamuna, Eastern Yamuna, and Agra canals.

## 13.2 Chambal

The Chambal rises near Mhow in the Malwa Plateau of Madhya Pradesh.

It flows northward through a gorge near Kota, where the Gandhisagar Dam has been constructed.

It is famous for badland topography called Chambal ravines.

## 13.3 Gandak

The Gandak consists of two streams:

- Kaligandak,
- Trishulganga.

It rises in the Nepal Himalayas between Dhaulagiri and Mount Everest.

It enters the Ganga plain in Champaran district of Bihar and joins the Ganga at Sonpur near Patna.

## 13.4 Ghaghara

The Ghaghara originates in glaciers of Mapchachungo.

It receives tributaries such as:

- Tila,
- Seti,
- Beri.

It cuts a deep gorge at Shishapani.

The Sarda or Kali joins it in the plains before it meets the Ganga at Chhapra.

## 13.5 Kosi

The Kosi is an antecedent river.

Its source lies north of Mount Everest in Tibet, where its main stream Arun rises.

It is joined by:

- Son Kosi from the west,
- Tamur Kosi from the east.

After uniting with the Arun, it forms the Sapt Kosi.

The Kosi is known for floods and shifting channels.

## 13.6 Ramganga

The Ramganga rises in the Garhwal hills near Gairsain.

It enters the plains of Uttar Pradesh near Najibabad and joins the Ganga near Kannauj.

## 13.7 Damodar

The Damodar flows along the eastern margins of the Chotanagpur Plateau through a rift valley and joins the Hugli.

Its main tributary is the Barakar.

It was once known as the "sorrow of Bengal" but has been controlled through the Damodar Valley Corporation.

## 13.8 Sarda Or Saryu

The Sarda or Saryu rises in the Milam glacier in the Nepal Himalayas, where it is known as Goriganga.

Along the Indo-Nepal border, it is called Kali or Chauk.

It joins the Ghaghara.

## 13.9 Mahananda

The Mahananda rises in the Darjiling hills.

It joins the Ganga as its last left bank tributary in West Bengal.

## 13.10 Son

The Son originates in the Amarkantak Plateau.

It forms waterfalls at the edge of the plateau and joins the Ganga near Arrah, west of Patna.

# 14. Brahmaputra River System

## 14.1 Source

The Brahmaputra originates in the Chemayungdung glacier of the Kailash range near Mansarovar Lake.

## 14.2 Tibetan Course

In Tibet, it flows eastward for nearly:

```text
1,200 km
```

There it is known as:

```text
Tsangpo
```

meaning purifier.

The Rango Tsangpo is a major right bank tributary in Tibet.

## 14.3 Himalayan Gorge

Near Namcha Barwa, the river cuts a deep gorge through the Central Himalayas.

It then emerges from the foothills as:

- Siang,
- Dihang.

## 14.4 Entry Into India

It enters India west of Sadiya town in Arunachal Pradesh.

Flowing southwest, it receives:

- Dibang or Sikang,
- Lohit.

After this, it is known as the Brahmaputra.

## 14.5 Tributaries In Assam

During its journey through Assam, the Brahmaputra receives many tributaries.

Left bank tributaries:

- Burhi Dihing,
- Dhansari South.

Right bank tributaries:

- Subansiri,
- Kameng,
- Manas,
- Sankosh.

The Subansiri is an antecedent river.

## 14.6 Course In Bangladesh

The Brahmaputra enters Bangladesh near Dhubri and flows southward.

In Bangladesh:

- the Tista joins it on the right bank,
- it is called the Jamuna,
- it merges with the Padma,
- the combined system drains into the Bay of Bengal.

## 14.7 Floods And Erosion

The Brahmaputra is known for:

- floods,
- channel shifting,
- bank erosion,
- large sediment load.

Reasons include:

- heavy rainfall in the catchment,
- large tributaries,
- steep slopes in upper regions,
- high sediment supply.

# 15. Peninsular Drainage System

## 15.1 General Character

The Peninsular drainage system is older than the Himalayan drainage system.

Evidence:

- broad valleys,
- shallow valleys,
- graded profiles,
- mature rivers.

## 15.2 Water Divide

The Western Ghats run close to the western coast and act as an important water divide.

Most major Peninsular rivers flow eastward to the Bay of Bengal.

Small west-flowing rivers drain into the Arabian Sea.

## 15.3 Exceptions

The Narmada and Tapi are major exceptions.

They flow westward through rift valleys and drain into the Arabian Sea.

## 15.4 Characteristics

Peninsular rivers generally have:

- fixed courses,
- absence or limited development of meanders,
- non-perennial flow,
- dependence on rainfall,
- smaller basins than Himalayan rivers,
- mature valleys.

## 15.5 Peninsular Rivers Joining Ganga System

Some rivers originate in the northern part of the Peninsular Plateau but join the Ganga system.

Examples:

- Chambal,
- Sind,
- Betwa,
- Ken,
- Son.

# 16. Evolution Of Peninsular Drainage

Three major geological events shaped Peninsular drainage.

## 16.1 Western Flank Subsidence

The western flank of the Peninsula subsided and submerged below the sea during the early Tertiary period.

This disturbed the original symmetrical drainage plan.

## 16.2 Himalayan Uplift And Trough Faulting

During Himalayan uplift, the northern flank of the Peninsular Block underwent subsidence and trough faulting.

The Narmada and Tapi flow through trough faults.

They filled cracks with detrital material.

This is why these rivers lack large alluvial and deltaic deposits.

## 16.3 Tilting Toward Southeast

The Peninsular Block tilted slightly from northwest to southeast.

This gave many Peninsular rivers an eastward orientation toward the Bay of Bengal.

The Tapi is another important west-flowing river.

Key facts:

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Length | About 724 km |
| Catchment area | About 65,145 sq km |

## 17.7 Luni

The Luni is the largest river system of Rajasthan west of the Aravali.

It originates near Pushkar in two branches:

- Saraswati,
- Sabarmati.

They join at Govindgarh.

From there, the river is called Luni.

It flows westward till Telwara and then southwest toward the Rann of Kachchh.

The entire river system is ephemeral.

# 21. River Regime

## 21.1 Meaning

The pattern of flow of water in a river channel over a year is called its regime.

River flow is not the same throughout the year.

It varies by:

- season,
- rainfall,
- snowmelt,
- basin size,
- terrain,
- groundwater contribution,
- dams and diversions.

## 21.2 Discharge

Discharge is the volume of water flowing in a river measured over time.

It is measured in:

- cusecs, or cubic feet per second,
- cumecs, or cubic metres per second.

## 21.3 Himalayan River Regime

North Indian rivers from the Himalayas are perennial.

They receive:

- water from glaciers and snowmelt,
- rainfall during the monsoon.

The Ganga has low flow from January to June and maximum flow in August or September.

It also maintains a sizeable flow in early summer due to snowmelt before monsoon rains begin.

At Farakka, the mean maximum discharge is about 55,000 cusecs, while the mean minimum is about 1,300 cusecs.

## 21.4 Peninsular River Regime

South Indian rivers do not originate from glaciers.

Their flow depends mainly on rainfall.

The Narmada has very low discharge from January to July and suddenly rises in August.

At Garudeshwar, the Narmada has a maximum flow of about 2,300 cusecs and a minimum of about 15 cusecs.

The Godavari has minimum discharge in May and maximum discharge in July-August.

At Polavaram, the mean maximum discharge is about 3,200 cusecs, while the mean minimum is about 50 cusecs.

# 22. Usability Of River Water

## 22.1 Uneven Distribution

India's rivers carry huge volumes of water, but it is unevenly distributed:

- in time,
- in space.

Some rivers carry water throughout the year.

Others have little water in the dry season.

During the rainy season, much water flows down to the sea as floods.

At the same time, another region may face drought.

## 22.2 Water Management Problem

The issue is not only availability.

It is also management.

Problems include:

- floods,
- droughts,
- seasonal unevenness,
- pollution,
- silt load,
- interstate disputes,
- shrinking channels due to settlements,
- ecological damage.

## 22.3 Inter-Basin Transfer

Inter-basin transfer means transferring water from a surplus basin to a deficit basin.

## 22.4 Difficulties In River Linking

Major difficulties include:

- uneven terrain,
- lifting water from plains to plateaus,
- high cost,
- ecological impact,
- displacement,
- sedimentation,
- interstate disputes,
- uncertainty about surplus water,

## 24.2 River Basin And Watershed

River basin is the area drained by a river and its tributaries.

Watershed is the boundary separating two basins or a smaller drainage unit.

Basins are usually larger. Watersheds are smaller and useful for micro-level planning.

## 24.3 Dendritic And Trellis Patterns

Dendritic drainage resembles tree branches and develops on uniform rocks.

Trellis drainage has parallel main tributaries joined by smaller tributaries at near right angles.

## 24.4 Radial And Centripetal Patterns

Radial drainage flows outward from a central highland.

Centripetal drainage flows inward toward a lake, basin, or depression.

## 24.5 Delta And Estuary

A delta is a depositional feature at a river mouth where sediments accumulate and the river splits into distributaries.

An estuary is a funnel-shaped river mouth where the river meets the sea, often with tidal influence and without a large delta.

## 24.6 Socio-Economic Advantages Of Inter-Linking Rivers

- flood moderation in surplus basins,

- inland navigation,
- hydropower potential,
- drinking water supply,
- regional development.

# 28. Strong Short Answers

## Question: What is drainage?

Drainage is the flow of water through well-defined channels. The network of such channels in an area is called a drainage system.

## Question: What is a drainage basin?

A drainage basin is the area drained by a river and its tributaries. Water from the entire basin eventually flows into the main river system.

## Question: What is a watershed?

A watershed is the boundary line separating one drainage basin from another. The term is also used for smaller drainage units useful in local planning.

## Question: What is river regime?

River regime is the pattern of flow of water in a river channel over a year. It shows seasonal variation in river discharge.

## Question: Why are Himalayan rivers perennial?

Himalayan rivers are perennial because they are fed by both melting snow and monsoon rainfall. Therefore, they carry water throughout the year.

## Question: Why is the Kosi called the sorrow of Bihar?

The Kosi brings huge quantities of sediment from the Himalayas and frequently shifts its course in the plains. This causes severe floods, so it is called the sorrow of Bihar.

## Question: Why do Narmada and Tapi not form large deltas?

Narmada and Tapi flow westward through rift valleys and enter the Arabian Sea as estuaries. Their trough-fault origin and westward flow limit large alluvial and deltaic deposition.

## Question: What is the difference between delta and estuary?

A delta is a sedimentary depositional feature where a river divides into distributaries before entering the sea. An estuary is a funnel-shaped river mouth where the river meets the sea, usually without a large delta.

# 29. Long Answer: Himalayan Drainage

The Himalayan drainage system includes the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems. These rivers have evolved through a long geological history.

They are perennial because they receive water from both snowmelt and rainfall. In the Himalayan region, they cut deep gorges and form V-shaped valleys, rapids, and waterfalls. These features show youthful erosion.

When these rivers enter the plains, their behaviour changes. They form floodplains, ox-bow lakes, braided channels, meanders, and deltas. Their plains courses are often unstable and may shift over time.

The Indus system drains the western Himalayas and flows into the Arabian Sea. The Ganga system drains a large part of northern India and enters the Bay of Bengal. The Brahmaputra originates near Mansarovar, flows as Tsangpo in Tibet, enters India as Siang or Dihang, and becomes the Brahmaputra in Assam.

Himalayan rivers are important for irrigation, agriculture, settlements, hydropower, navigation in some stretches, and cultural life. They also create floods, erosion, and sedimentation problems.

---

# 30. Long Answer: Peninsular Drainage

The Peninsular drainage system is older than the Himalayan drainage system. Its rivers have broad, shallow, well-graded valleys and more mature profiles.

The Western Ghats act as an important water divide. Most major Peninsular rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal. These include the Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri. The Narmada and Tapi are important exceptions because they flow westward through rift valleys into the Arabian Sea.

Peninsular rivers are mostly rainfall-dependent and seasonal. They generally have fixed courses, smaller basins, and less meandering than Himalayan rivers.

The Godavari is the largest Peninsular river and is called Dakshin Ganga. The Krishna is the second largest east-flowing Peninsular river. The Kaveri has comparatively less seasonal fluctuation because its basin receives both southwest and northeast monsoon rainfall. The Narmada forms marble gorges and Dhuandhar Falls near Jabalpur and enters the Arabian Sea near Bharuch.

Thus, Peninsular drainage reflects the old stable plateau, monsoon climate, and geological tilting of Peninsular India.

# 31. Long Answer: River Water Use And Problems

India's rivers carry large volumes of water, but the water is unevenly distributed in time and space. Some rivers are perennial, while others have little water in the dry season. During the monsoon, some regions face floods while others may face drought.

This creates problems of water management. Floods can damage crops, houses, roads, and lives. Droughts affect agriculture, drinking water, and livelihoods. River water also carries silt, which can block channels and reduce storage capacity.

Pollution is another major problem. Rivers receive sewage, industrial effluents, solid waste, religious offerings, and agricultural runoff. Bathing, washing, and settlement expansion along river channels also affect river health.

Inter-basin transfer has been suggested to move water from surplus basins to deficit basins. Examples include the Periyar Diversion Scheme, Indira Gandhi Canal Project, Kurnool-Cuddapah Canal, Beas-Satluj Link Canal, and Ganga-Kaveri Link Canal. However, such projects face technical, ecological, social, financial, and political challenges.

Better river management requires pollution control, floodplain protection, watershed management, efficient irrigation, sewage treatment, interstate cooperation, and careful planning.

# 33. Final Chapter Summary

Drainage is the flow of water through well-defined channels, while a drainage system is the network of such channels. A river drains a catchment area; a river and its tributaries drain a basin; and watersheds divide basins.

India's drainage may be classified by sea outlet, basin size, and origin. About 77 percent of the drainage area flows toward the Bay of Bengal, while about 23 percent flows toward the Arabian Sea.

The Himalayan drainage includes the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra systems. These rivers are perennial, have large basins, create deep gorges in mountains, and form meanders, floodplains, braided channels, and deltas in plains.

The Peninsular drainage is older and more mature. Most major Peninsular rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal, while Narmada and Tapi flow westward through rift valleys into the Arabian Sea.

River regime is the seasonal pattern of flow. Himalayan rivers have sustained flow due to snowmelt and rainfall, while Peninsular rivers depend mainly on monsoon rainfall and show stronger seasonal fluctuations.

India's river water use faces problems of uneven distribution, floods, droughts, pollution, silt load, interstate disputes, and seasonal variation. River management requires basin-level planning, pollution control, and careful use of water resources.
