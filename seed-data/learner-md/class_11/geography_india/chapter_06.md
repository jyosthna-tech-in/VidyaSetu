## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains soil as a living and valuable resource.

Soil supports crops, grasses, trees, forests, animals, and human life. Most food and much clothing come from crops that grow in soil. Soil forms slowly over thousands of years, but it can be degraded quickly through erosion, misuse, over-irrigation, deforestation, and faulty farming.

The central idea is:

> Soil is a thin, living layer formed from weathered rock and organic matter; its type, fertility, and conservation depend on parent material, relief, climate, organisms, time, and human use.

# 1. Soil As A Resource

## 1.1 Why Soil Is Important

Soil supports:

- trees,
- grasses,
- crops,
- animals,
- microorganisms,
- human food systems.

Without soil, land-based crops cannot grow.

The bulk of our food and much of our clothing come from crops grown in soil.

## 1.2 Soil Takes A Long Time To Form

Soil evolves over thousands of years.

It forms when:

- rocks weather,
- minerals break down,
- organic matter accumulates,
- water and air enter the material,
- organisms act on the material.

Because soil forms slowly, it must be conserved carefully.

## 1.3 Soil Can Be Lost Quickly

Even though soil takes thousands of years to form, it can be damaged quickly by:

- erosion,
- deforestation,
- overgrazing,
- shifting cultivation,
- over-irrigation,
- excessive chemical use,
- faulty farming on slopes.

---

# 2. Meaning And Components Of Soil

## 2.1 Definition

Soil is the mixture of rock debris and organic materials that develops on the earth's surface.

It is not just powdered rock.

It is a living system containing minerals, organic matter, water, air, and organisms.

## 2.2 Main Components

Soil contains:

- mineral particles,
- humus,
- water,
- air.

The amount of each component varies from one soil type to another.

## 2.3 Why Components Matter

| Component | Importance |
| --- | --- |
| Mineral particles | Provide texture and some nutrients |
| Humus | Improves fertility and water-holding capacity |
| Water | Dissolves nutrients and supports plant growth |
| Air | Supports roots and soil organisms |

## 2.4 Soil As A Living System

Soils develop, change, degrade, and recover when treated properly.

They respond to:

- climate,
- vegetation,
- farming,
- irrigation,
- erosion,
- conservation.

This is why the chapter describes soils as living systems.

---

# 3. Factors Of Soil Formation

The main factors affecting soil formation are:

1. relief,
2. parent material,
3. climate,
4. vegetation and other life forms,
5. time,
6. human activities.

## 3.1 Relief

Relief affects:

- slope,
- drainage,
- erosion,
- deposition,
- soil depth.

Steep slopes often have thin soils because materials are removed by erosion.

Plains and valleys often have deeper soils because sediments accumulate.

## 3.2 Parent Material

Parent material is the rock material from which soil forms.

It influences:

- colour,
- texture,
- mineral content,
- fertility,
- chemical properties.

For example, black soils are linked with basaltic parent material of the Deccan region.

## 3.3 Climate

Climate affects soil through:

- temperature,
- rainfall,
- evaporation,
- leaching,
- vegetation growth,
- decomposition of organic matter.

High rainfall may cause intense leaching.

Dry climate may cause salt accumulation.

## 3.4 Vegetation And Life Forms

Plants and organisms add organic matter to soil.

Roots break rocks and bind soil.

Microorganisms decompose leaves and other materials into humus.

## 3.5 Time

Soil formation is slow.

Older soils generally have more developed profiles.

Young soils may have weak horizons.

## 3.6 Human Activities

Humans influence soil through:

- farming,
- irrigation,
- deforestation,
- fertiliser use,
- mining,
- construction,
- conservation.

Human activity can improve soil or degrade it.

# 4. Soil Profile

## 4.1 Meaning

A soil profile is the vertical arrangement of soil layers from top to bottom.

These layers are called horizons.

## 4.2 Horizon A

Horizon A is the topmost zone.

It contains:

- organic matter,
- mineral matter,
- nutrients,
- water.

It is the most important layer for plant growth.

It is also the layer most easily lost through erosion.

## 4.3 Horizon B

Horizon B is a transition zone between Horizon A and Horizon C.

It contains material derived from:

- the layer above,
- the layer below.

It has some organic matter, but mineral matter is more weathered and noticeable.

## 4.4 Horizon C

Horizon C is made of loose parent material.

It is the first stage in soil formation.

Over time, it helps form the upper horizons.

## 4.5 Bedrock

Below the horizons lies bedrock, also called parent rock.

It is the solid rock from which soil material may gradually develop.

## 4.6 Simple Diagram In Words

```text
Surface
Horizon A - topsoil, humus, nutrients, roots
Horizon B - transition layer, weathered minerals
Horizon C - loose parent material
Bedrock   - solid parent rock
```

# 5. Classification Of Soils

## 5.1 Why Classify Soils?

Soils are complex and varied.

Classification helps us:

- understand soil properties,
- compare soils,
- plan agriculture,
- manage irrigation,
- conserve degraded land,
- study fertility,
- use land correctly.

## 5.2 Ancient Classification

In ancient times, Indian soils were classified into:

- Urvara: fertile soil,
- Usara: sterile or infertile soil.

This was a simple fertility-based classification.

## 5.3 Sixteenth Century Classification

In the sixteenth century, soils were classified by:

- texture,
- colour,
- slope,
- moisture content.

Based on texture, soils were identified as:

- sandy,
- clayey,
- silty,
- loam.

Based on colour, soils were described as:

- red,
- yellow,
- black.

## 5.4 Modern Scientific Surveys

After Independence, scientific soil surveys were conducted by agencies such as:

- Soil Survey of India,
- National Bureau of Soil Survey and Land Use Planning,
- Indian Council of Agricultural Research.

Soil Survey of India was established in 1956.

It conducted important studies in selected areas such as the Damodar Valley.

## 5.5 ICAR And USDA Soil Taxonomy

ICAR classified Indian soils according to their nature and character using USDA Soil Taxonomy.

This made Indian soil classification more scientific and comparable at an international level.

3. red and yellow soils,
4. laterite soils,
5. arid soils,

# 8. Alluvial Soils

## 8.1 Distribution

Alluvial soils are widespread in:

- northern plains,
- river valleys,
- deltas of the east coast,
- some river valleys of Peninsular India.

They also extend into the plains of Gujarat through a narrow corridor in Rajasthan.

## 8.2 Area

Alluvial soils cover about 40 percent of India's total area.

This makes them one of the most widespread and productive soil categories.

## 8.3 Formation

Alluvial soils are depositional soils.

They are transported and deposited by:

- rivers,
- streams,
- floods.

## 8.4 Texture

Their texture varies from:

- sandy loam,
- loam,
- clay.

Texture depends on:

- distance from river,
- flood history,
- sediment size,
- age of deposition.

## 8.5 Chemical Properties

Alluvial soils are generally:

- rich in potash,
- poor in phosphorus.

## 8.6 Colour

Their colour varies from light grey to ash grey.

Colour depends on:

- depth of deposition,
- texture of material,
- stage of maturity.

## 8.7 Agriculture

Alluvial soils are intensively cultivated because they are fertile and occur in plains with water availability.

They support many crops, including:

- rice,
- wheat,
- sugarcane,
- pulses,
- oilseeds,
- vegetables.

# 9. Khadar And Bhangar

## 9.1 Khadar

Khadar is new alluvium.

It is deposited by annual floods.

It is enriched by fine silt every year.

It is usually more fertile than Bhangar.

## 9.2 Bhangar

Bhangar is older alluvium.

It is deposited away from active floodplains.

It is older, more mature, and less frequently renewed by flood deposits.

## 9.3 Kankar

Both Khadar and Bhangar may contain calcareous concretions called kankar.

## 9.4 Comparison

| Khadar | Bhangar |
| --- | --- |
| New alluvium | Old alluvium |
| Deposited by annual floods | Deposited away from floodplains |
| More frequently renewed | Less frequently renewed |
| Usually more fertile | Comparatively less fertile |
| Fine silt added regularly | May contain more kankar |

# 10. Black Soils

## 10.1 Distribution

Black soils cover much of the Deccan Plateau.

They occur in parts of:

- Maharashtra,
- Madhya Pradesh,
- Gujarat,
- Andhra Pradesh,
- Tamil Nadu.

They are very deep in:

- upper reaches of the Godavari,
- upper reaches of the Krishna,
- northwestern part of the Deccan Plateau.

## 10.2 Other Names

Black soils are also known as:

- Regur soils,
- black cotton soils.

## 10.3 Texture And Structure

Black soils are generally:

- clayey,
- deep,
- impermeable.

## 10.4 Behaviour When Wet And Dry

When wet, black soils:

- swell,
- become sticky.

When dry, black soils:

- shrink,
- develop wide cracks.

## 10.5 Self-Ploughing

The cracking of black soil during the dry season creates a kind of self-ploughing.

The cracks help aerate and loosen the soil naturally.

## 10.6 Moisture Retention

Black soil absorbs water slowly and loses moisture slowly.

This helps crops survive during dry periods, especially rain-fed crops.

## 10.7 Chemical Composition

Black soils are rich in:

- lime,
- iron,
- magnesia,
- alumina,
- potash.

They are poor in:

- phosphorus,
- nitrogen,
- organic matter.

## 10.8 Colour

The colour ranges from deep black to grey.

## 10.9 Agricultural Importance

Black soil is important for cotton cultivation.

It also supports crops such as:

- jowar,
- wheat,
- gram,
- oilseeds,
- pulses,
- sugarcane in irrigated areas.

# 11. Red And Yellow Soils

## 11.1 Formation

Red soil develops on crystalline igneous rocks in areas of low rainfall.

It is common in parts of the eastern and southern Deccan Plateau.

## 11.2 Distribution

Red and yellow soils occur in:

- eastern Deccan Plateau,
- southern Deccan Plateau,
- piedmont zone of Western Ghats,
- parts of Odisha,
- parts of Chhattisgarh,
- southern parts of the middle Ganga plain.

## 11.3 Why Red?

The red colour is due to wide diffusion of iron in crystalline and metamorphic rocks.

## 11.4 Why Yellow?

The soil appears yellow when iron occurs in hydrated form.

## 11.5 Fertility

Fine-grained red and yellow soils are normally fertile.

Coarse-grained soils in dry upland areas are poor in fertility.

## 11.6 Nutrient Status

These soils are generally poor in:

- nitrogen,
- phosphorus,
- humus.

## 11.7 Learning Tip

Remember the colour logic:

- red: iron diffusion,
- yellow: hydrated iron.

# 12. Laterite Soils

## 12.1 Meaning

The word laterite comes from the Latin word later, meaning brick.

Laterite soils are often cut and used as bricks in house construction.

## 12.2 Formation Conditions

Laterite soils develop in areas with:

- high temperature,
- high rainfall.

## 12.3 Leaching

These soils form through intense leaching under tropical rainfall.

During leaching:

- lime is removed,
- silica is removed,
- soluble nutrients are washed down,
- iron oxide and aluminium compounds remain.

## 12.4 Humus Loss

High temperature supports rapid bacterial activity.

As a result, humus is removed quickly.

## 12.5 Nutrient Status

Laterite soils are poor in:

- organic matter,
- nitrogen,
- phosphate,
- calcium.

They are rich in:

- iron oxide,
- potash.

## 12.6 Suitability For Cultivation

Laterite soils are generally not very suitable for cultivation in their natural condition.

They need:

- manures,
- fertilisers,
- careful management.

## 12.7 Tree Crops

Red laterite soils in Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and Kerala are more suitable for tree crops such as cashew nut.

## 12.8 Distribution

Laterite soils are found mainly in higher areas of the Peninsular plateau, including:

- Karnataka,
- Kerala,
- Tamil Nadu,
- Madhya Pradesh,
- hilly areas of Odisha,
- hilly areas of Assam.

# 13. Arid Soils

## 13.1 Distribution

Arid soils are characteristically developed in western Rajasthan.

They are associated with arid topography.

## 13.2 Colour And Texture

Arid soils range from red to brown.

They are generally:

- sandy,
- dry,
- loose in structure.

## 13.3 Salinity

They are often saline.

In some areas, salt content is so high that common salt can be obtained by evaporating saline water.

## 13.4 Moisture And Humus

Due to dry climate, high temperature, and rapid evaporation, arid soils lack:

- moisture,
- humus.

## 13.5 Nutrient Status

Nitrogen is insufficient.

Phosphate content is generally normal.

## 13.6 Kankar Layer

Lower horizons may contain kankar layers because calcium content increases downward.

This kankar layer restricts water infiltration.

When irrigation is available, soil moisture may remain accessible for plant growth because water is held above the kankar layer.

## 13.7 Main Limitation

Arid soils are poor and contain little organic matter.

They need careful irrigation and management to become productive.

# 14. Saline Soils

## 14.1 Other Name

Saline soils are also known as Usara soils.

## 14.2 Composition

They contain high proportions of:

- sodium,
- potassium,
- magnesium.

Because of excess salts, they are infertile and do not support good vegetation.

## 14.3 Causes

Saline soils develop due to:

- dry climate,
- poor drainage,
- high evaporation,
- waterlogging,
- seawater intrusion,
- excessive irrigation in dry regions.

## 14.4 Texture

Their structure ranges from sandy to loamy.

## 14.5 Nutrient Deficiency

They lack:

- nitrogen,
- calcium.

## 14.6 Distribution

Saline soils are found in:

- western Gujarat,
- eastern coast deltas,
- Sunderban areas of West Bengal,
- arid and semi-arid regions,
- waterlogged and swampy areas.

## 14.7 Rann Of Kachchh

In the Rann of Kachchh, the southwest monsoon brings salt particles and deposits them as a crust.

## 14.8 Coastal Salinity

In deltas, seawater intrusion promotes saline soils.

## 14.9 Irrigation-Induced Salinity

In intensively cultivated irrigated areas, fertile alluvial soils may become saline.

This is common where:

- irrigation is excessive,
- climate is dry,
- evaporation is high,
- salts rise through capillary action.

The salt then accumulates on the top layer.

## 14.10 Control

In areas such as Punjab and Haryana, farmers are advised to add gypsum to reduce salinity problems.

Better drainage and controlled irrigation are also important.

# 15. Peaty Soils

## 15.1 Formation Conditions

Peaty soils form in areas of:

- heavy rainfall,
- high humidity,
- good vegetation growth.

## 15.2 Organic Matter

Large quantities of dead organic matter accumulate.

This gives the soil high humus and organic content.

Organic matter may be as high as 40-50 percent.

## 15.3 Appearance

Peaty soils are usually:

- heavy,
- black in colour.

At many places, they are alkaline.

## 15.4 Distribution

They occur in:

- northern Bihar,
- southern Uttarakhand,
- coastal West Bengal,
- coastal Odisha,
- coastal Tamil Nadu.

## 15.5 Use

Peaty soils may be fertile because of organic matter, but drainage and acidity or alkalinity conditions must be managed.

# 16. Forest Soils

## 16.1 Formation

Forest soils form in forest areas where sufficient rainfall is available.

They vary depending on the mountain environment.

## 16.2 Texture

Their texture varies with slope:

- loamy and silty on valley sides,
- coarse-grained on upper slopes.

## 16.3 Himalayan Snow-Bound Areas

In snow-bound Himalayan areas, soils experience denudation.

They are:

- acidic,
- low in humus.

## 16.4 Lower Valleys

Soils in lower valleys are generally fertile.

## 16.5 Main Idea

Forest soils are not uniform.

They change with:

- altitude,
- slope,
- rainfall,
- vegetation,
- erosion.

# 17. Soil Fertility

## 17.1 Meaning

Soil fertility is the ability of soil to support plant growth.

Fertility depends on:

- nutrients,
- humus,
- texture,
- moisture,
- depth,
- drainage,
- soil organisms.

## 17.2 Naturally Determined Fertility

Naturally determined fertility comes from natural soil properties.

It depends on:

- parent rock,
- mineral content,
- climate,
- natural vegetation,
- natural humus.

Example:

- alluvial soils are naturally fertile because rivers deposit fresh sediments.

## 17.3 Culturally Induced Fertility

Culturally induced fertility is created or improved by human action.

It includes:

- irrigation,
- manuring,
- fertiliser application,
- crop rotation,
- drainage improvement,
- soil conservation,
- scientific farming.

## 17.4 Exam Use

If asked how to know whether a soil is fertile, examine:

- crop productivity,
- humus content,
- nutrient content,
- texture,
- moisture retention,
- drainage,
- response to fertilisers,
- erosion status.

# 18. Soil Degradation

## 18.1 Definition

Soil degradation is the decline in soil fertility.

It happens when:

- nutritional status declines,
- depth of soil decreases,
- soil structure worsens,
- erosion removes topsoil,
- misuse damages soil.

## 18.2 Importance

Soil degradation is a major factor depleting India's soil resource base.

It affects:

- agriculture,
- water quality,
- flood risk,
- rural livelihoods,
- national productivity.

## 18.3 Variation

The degree of soil degradation varies from place to place.

It depends on:

- topography,
- wind velocity,
- rainfall amount,
- land use,
- vegetation cover,
- farming methods.

# 19. Soil Erosion

## 19.1 Definition

The destruction or removal of soil cover is called soil erosion.

## 19.2 Natural Balance

Soil formation and soil erosion occur simultaneously.

Normally, there is a balance:

- soil particles are added through weathering,
- soil particles are removed by erosion.

When removal becomes faster than formation, erosion becomes a problem.

## 19.3 Human Disturbance

Human activities disturb this balance by:

- removing forests,
- expanding cultivation,
- overgrazing,
- building settlements,
- cutting slopes,
- practicing shifting cultivation,
- using faulty irrigation.

## 19.4 Main Agents

The main agents of soil erosion are:

- running water,
- wind.

Wind erosion is important in arid and semi-arid regions.

Water erosion is more serious and widespread in India.

---

# 20. Water Erosion

## 20.1 Where It Is Important

Water erosion is important in areas with:

- heavy rainfall,
- steep slopes,
- poor vegetation cover,
- loose soil.

## 20.2 Main Forms

Water erosion occurs mainly as:

1. sheet erosion,
2. gully erosion.

---

# 21. Sheet Erosion

## 21.1 Meaning

Sheet erosion is the removal of a thin layer of soil from a broad surface.

It usually occurs on level land after heavy rainfall.

## 21.2 Why It Is Dangerous

Sheet erosion is not easily noticed.

But it removes the finer and more fertile topsoil.

This reduces soil fertility gradually.

## 21.3 Example Situation

After a heavy shower, water may flow as a thin sheet over a field.

It carries away the topsoil without forming obvious channels.

---

# 22. Gully Erosion

## 22.1 Meaning

Gully erosion occurs when running water cuts deep channels into land.

It is common on steep slopes.

## 22.2 Process

Rainwater collects in small channels.

The channels deepen with repeated rainfall.

Over time, they cut agricultural land into small fragments.

The land may become unfit for cultivation.

## 22.3 Badland Topography

A region with many deep gullies or ravines is called badland topography.

## 22.4 Ravines

Ravines are widespread in the Chambal basin.

They are also found in:

- Tamil Nadu,
- West Bengal.

The chapter states that India loses about 8,000 hectares of land to ravines every year.

## 22.5 Land Prone To Gully Erosion

Gully erosion is common on:

- steep slopes,
- bare land,
- soft sediments,
- areas with heavy runoff,
- deforested slopes,
- overgrazed land.

---

# 23. Wind Erosion

## 23.1 Where It Occurs

Wind erosion is significant in:

- arid regions,
- semi-arid regions,
- dry sandy areas,
- areas with little vegetation.

## 23.2 Process

Strong winds lift and transport fine soil particles.

This leaves behind coarse material and reduces fertility.

## 23.3 Indian Example

Western Rajasthan is vulnerable to wind erosion because of:

- dry climate,
- sandy soil,
- sparse vegetation,
- strong winds.

---

# 24. Causes Of Soil Erosion In India

## 24.1 Deforestation

Deforestation is a major cause of soil erosion.

Plants bind soil with their roots.

Leaves and twigs add humus.

When forests are removed:

- roots no longer hold soil,
- raindrops hit soil directly,
- runoff increases,
- erosion becomes faster.

## 24.2 Population Pressure

As population increases, demand for land increases.

Natural vegetation is removed for:

- settlement,
- cultivation,
- grazing,
- roads,
- mining,
- other needs.

## 24.3 Overgrazing

Overgrazing removes grass cover.

Without protective cover, soil becomes exposed to wind and water.

## 24.4 Shifting Cultivation

Shifting cultivation can damage natural cover when practiced without enough recovery time.

This increases erosion.

## 24.5 Farming On Slopes

Cultivation on steep slopes without terraces increases runoff and erosion.

## 24.6 Over-Irrigation

Over-irrigation can make soils saline.

Salinity reduces fertility and degrades land.

## 24.7 Chemical Fertilisers Without Organic Manure

Chemical fertilisers without enough organic manure can harden soil and reduce long-term fertility.

Humus is necessary for healthy soil structure.

---

# 25. Effects Of Soil Erosion And Degradation

## 25.1 Loss Of Fertile Topsoil

Erosion removes the nutrient-rich top layer.

This directly reduces crop productivity.

## 25.2 Flooding

Eroded material is carried into rivers.

It reduces the carrying capacity of rivers.

This can increase floods.

## 25.3 Damage To Agricultural Land

Gullies and ravines cut agricultural land into fragments.

Such land may become unfit for cultivation.

## 25.4 Salinity In Irrigated Areas

In irrigated dry areas, salts may rise to the surface through capillary action.

This destroys fertility.

## 25.5 National Productivity

The chapter notes that about half of India's total land is under some degree of degradation.

India loses millions of tonnes of soil and nutrients every year.

This affects national productivity.

---

# 26. Soil Conservation

## 26.1 Definition

Soil conservation is a set of methods used to:

- maintain soil fertility,
- prevent soil erosion,
- prevent soil exhaustion,
- improve degraded soil.

## 26.2 Basic Principle

If soil erosion is caused by faulty human practices, it can also be reduced by improved human practices.

Soil conservation means using land according to its capability.

## 26.3 Avoid Cultivation On Steep Slopes

Open cultivable land on slopes should be protected from farming where erosion risk is high.

Lands with slope gradient of about 15-25 percent should generally not be used for ordinary cultivation.

If cultivation is necessary, terraces should be made carefully.

## 26.4 Control Overgrazing And Shifting Cultivation

Overgrazing and shifting cultivation should be regulated.

Villagers should be educated about their consequences.

## 26.5 Main Conservation Measures

Important measures include:

- contour bunding,
- contour terracing,
- regulated forestry,
- controlled grazing,
- cover cropping,
- mixed farming,
- crop rotation.

---

# 27. Contour Bunding And Terracing

## 27.1 Contour Bunding

Contour bunding means building embankments along contour lines.

It slows runoff and reduces soil loss.

## 27.2 Contour Terracing

Contour terracing means cutting slopes into step-like fields.

It reduces the speed of water flow and allows cultivation on slopes.

## 27.3 Terrace Farming

Terrace farming is important in hilly areas.

It helps:

- conserve water,
- reduce runoff,
- prevent soil erosion,
- make slopes cultivable.

---

# 28. Gully Control

## 28.1 Prevent Gully Formation

Efforts should be made to stop gullies from forming and expanding.

## 28.2 Finger Gullies

Small finger gullies can be removed or controlled by terracing.

## 28.3 Check Dams

In bigger gullies, check dams can reduce the erosive velocity of water.

They slow down runoff and encourage deposition.

## 28.4 Headward Extension

Special attention should be given to controlling headward extension of gullies.

This can be done through:

- gully plugging,
- terracing,
- planting cover vegetation.

---

# 29. Conservation In Arid And Semi-Arid Areas

## 29.1 Problem

In arid and semi-arid areas, cultivable land may be threatened by:

- wind erosion,
- sand dunes,
- low vegetation cover.

## 29.2 Shelter Belts

Shelter belts of trees should be developed to reduce wind speed.

They protect fields from sand movement.

## 29.3 Agro-Forestry

Agro-forestry combines trees with crops or grazing.

It helps stabilise soil and improve land use.

## 29.4 Pastures

Land not suitable for cultivation should be converted into pastures for grazing.

This reduces pressure on fragile cultivated land.

## 29.5 Sand Dune Stabilisation

Experiments have been made to stabilise sand dunes in western Rajasthan by the Central Arid Zone Research Institute, or CAZRI.

# 30. Planning For Soil Conservation

## 30.1 Central Soil Conservation Board

The Central Soil Conservation Board was set up by the Government of India.

It prepared plans for soil conservation in different parts of the country.

## 30.2 Basis Of Plans

Plans are based on:

- climate,
- land configuration,
- social behaviour of people.

## 30.3 Need For Integrated Land Use Planning

Fragmented plans are not enough.

Integrated land use planning is a better technique.

It requires:

- classifying land according to capability,
- preparing land use maps,
- using land for the right purpose,
- involving local people.

## 30.4 Final Responsibility

The final responsibility for land conservation rests with the people who use land and receive its benefits.

Soil conservation must become a people's practice, not only a government plan.

# 34. Common Misunderstandings

## 34.1 Soil Is Just Dirt

Incorrect.

Soil is a living system containing minerals, humus, water, air, organisms, and nutrients.

## 34.2 All Black Soils Are Fertile In Every Nutrient

Incorrect.

Black soils are rich in lime, iron, magnesia, alumina, and potash, but poor in phosphorus, nitrogen, and organic matter.

## 34.3 Alluvial Soil Is Found Only In North India

Incorrect.

It is widespread in northern plains, but also occurs in east coast deltas and river valleys of Peninsular India.

## 34.4 Laterite Soil Is Naturally Very Fertile

Incorrect.

Laterite soils are heavily leached and often need manures and fertilisers for cultivation.

## 34.5 Saline Soil Occurs Only Near The Sea

Incorrect.

It occurs in coastal areas, but also in arid, semi-arid, waterlogged, and over-irrigated regions.

## 34.6 Sheet Erosion Is Harmless Because It Is Not Visible

Incorrect.

Sheet erosion is dangerous because it quietly removes fertile topsoil.

## 34.7 Soil Conservation Is Only Tree Planting

Incomplete.

Soil conservation includes tree planting, terracing, bunding, controlled grazing, crop rotation, check dams, drainage, and proper land use planning.

## 36.2 Short Answer: What Is Soil?

Soil is the mixture of rock debris and organic materials that develops on the earth's surface.

It contains mineral particles, humus, water, and air.

It supports plants, crops, organisms, and human life.

## 36.3 Short Answer: Factors Of Soil Formation

The main factors responsible for soil formation are:

- relief,
- parent material,
- climate,
- vegetation and other life forms,
- time,
- human activities.

## 36.4 Short Answer: Soil Profile Horizons

The three main horizons are:

- Horizon A: topsoil with organic matter, minerals, nutrients, and water;
- Horizon B: transition zone with material from above and below;
- Horizon C: loose parent material.

Below these lies parent rock or bedrock.

## 36.5 Short Answer: Soil Degradation

Soil degradation is the decline in soil fertility and quality.

It occurs when nutrient status falls, soil depth decreases, topsoil is eroded, or soil is misused.

It reduces agricultural productivity.

## 36.6 Short Answer: Khadar And Bhangar

Khadar is new alluvium deposited by annual floods and is usually more fertile.

Bhangar is older alluvium deposited away from floodplains and is not renewed every year.

Both may contain kankar.

# 37. Long Answer Guide

## 37.1 Black Soils: Formation And Characteristics

Use this structure:

1. Location:
   Black soils cover much of the Deccan Plateau, including parts of Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu.

2. Other names:
   They are called Regur soils or black cotton soils.

3. Formation:
   They are associated with the Deccan region and are linked with basaltic parent material.

4. Texture:
   They are clayey, deep, and impermeable.

5. Wet-dry behaviour:
   They swell and become sticky when wet, shrink and crack when dry.

6. Self-ploughing:
   Cracking creates natural loosening during the dry season.

7. Moisture:
   They retain moisture for a long time, helping rain-fed crops.

8. Chemistry:
   Rich in lime, iron, magnesia, alumina, and potash; poor in phosphorus, nitrogen, and organic matter.

9. Agriculture:
   Important for cotton and other dryland crops.

## 37.2 Soil Conservation Measures

Use this structure:

1. Define soil conservation:
   It is the method of maintaining soil fertility, preventing erosion and exhaustion, and improving degraded soil.

2. Avoid risky cultivation:
   Slopes of 15-25 percent should not be cultivated without terraces.

3. Use terracing and contour bunding:
   These reduce runoff on slopes.

4. Control grazing and shifting cultivation:
   These protect vegetation cover.

5. Use cover cropping, mixed farming, and crop rotation:
   These protect and improve soil.

6. Control gullies:
   Use check dams, gully plugging, terracing, and cover vegetation.

7. Protect arid areas:
   Use shelter belts and agro-forestry to reduce wind erosion and sand dune movement.

8. Plan land use:
   Classify land by capability and use it properly.

## 37.3 Fertility: Natural And Culturally Induced

Use this structure:

1. Fertility means the capacity of soil to support plant growth.
2. Naturally determined fertility comes from parent material, minerals, climate, vegetation, and natural humus.
3. Culturally induced fertility comes from human actions such as irrigation, manuring, fertilisers, drainage, crop rotation, and conservation.
4. A soil can be naturally fertile but degraded by misuse.
5. A soil can be naturally poor but improved by scientific management.

# 40. Final Chapter Summary

Soil is a thin, living, and valuable layer of the earth's crust. It is formed from weathered rock and organic matter over thousands of years. It contains mineral particles, humus, water, and air, and supports most land-based life and agriculture.

Soil formation depends on relief, parent material, climate, vegetation, organisms, time, and human activity. A soil profile has Horizon A, Horizon B, Horizon C, and bedrock.

Indian soils are classified into alluvial, black, red and yellow, laterite, arid, saline, peaty, and forest soils. Alluvial soils are widespread and productive; black soils retain moisture and support cotton; red and yellow soils get their colour from iron; laterite soils form through leaching; arid soils are dry and sandy; saline soils contain excess salts; peaty soils are rich in organic matter; forest soils vary with mountain environments.

Soil degradation means decline in soil fertility, nutrients, and depth. Soil erosion means removal of soil cover by water, wind, or misuse. Sheet erosion removes fertile topsoil quietly, while gully erosion cuts land into ravines and badlands.

Soil conservation is essential for agriculture and national productivity. Measures include terracing, contour bunding, controlled grazing, regulated forestry, cover cropping, crop rotation, check dams, shelter belts, agro-forestry, and integrated land use planning.

To master this chapter, connect soil type with formation, location, fertility, degradation risk, and conservation method.
