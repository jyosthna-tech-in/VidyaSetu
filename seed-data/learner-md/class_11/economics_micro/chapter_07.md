This chapter explains how a consumer's demand for a commodity is determined.

> Demand is not just desire. Demand means willingness and ability to buy at given prices, given income, related prices, and preferences.

# 1. Meaning Of Demand

## 1.1 Demand Is Not Mere Desire

In economics, demand has a precise meaning.

A person may want a commodity, but that alone is not demand.

For demand to exist, three conditions must be present:

1. The consumer must want the commodity.
2. The consumer must be able to afford it.
3. The consumer must be willing to buy it at the given price.

So demand is both willingness and ability to buy.

## 1.2 Definition

Demand for a commodity is the quantity that a consumer is willing and able to buy, given:

- price of the commodity,
- prices of other goods,
- income of the consumer,
- tastes and preferences of the consumer.

## 1.3 Why Demand Depends On Many Factors

The amount of a commodity chosen by a consumer depends on:

| Factor | How It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Own price of the commodity | Usually, a higher price reduces demand and a lower price increases demand. |
| Prices of related goods | Demand changes if substitutes or complements become costlier or cheaper. |
| Consumer income | Demand changes differently for normal and inferior goods. |
| Tastes and preferences | Favourable preferences increase demand; unfavourable preferences reduce demand. |

## 1.4 Example

Suppose a student buys notebooks.

Notebook demand may change because:

- the price of notebooks changes,
- the student's pocket money changes,
- the price of tablets or loose sheets changes,
- exam season increases the need for notebooks,
- the student develops a preference for digital notes.

Demand is therefore not determined by price alone in real life.

However, to study demand clearly, economists often change one factor at a time.

# 2. Demand Function

## 2.1 Meaning Of A Function

A function is a relation between two variables where each value of one variable gives a unique value of another variable.

If:

```text
y = f(x)
```

then:

- `x` is the independent variable,
- `y` is the dependent variable,
- the value of `y` depends on the value of `x`.

## 2.2 Example Of A Function

Suppose:

| x | y |
| ---: | ---: |
| 0 | 10 |
| 1 | 15 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 20 |

Here each value of `x` gives one value of `y`.

So `y` is a function of `x`.

## 2.3 Increasing And Decreasing Functions

An increasing function means `y` does not fall when `x` rises.

Example:

```text
y = 5 + x
```

As `x` increases, `y` increases.

A decreasing function means `y` does not rise when `x` rises.

Example:

```text
y = 50 - x
```

As `x` increases, `y` falls.

## 2.4 Demand Function

When other things remain constant, a consumer's demand for a good can be written as:

```text
X = f(P)
```

Here:

- `X` means quantity demanded,
- `P` means price of the good.

This says that quantity demanded depends on price.

## 2.5 Full Demand Function In Words

More completely, demand depends on:

```text
Demand = f(own price, income, prices of related goods, preferences)
```

But when drawing a demand curve, we keep income, related prices, and preferences constant. Then we focus only on the relation between own price and quantity demanded.

# 3. Demand Curve

## 3.1 Meaning

A demand curve is the graphical representation of the demand function.

It shows how much of a commodity a consumer demands at different prices, other things remaining unchanged.

## 3.2 Axes In Economics

In many mathematics graphs:

- independent variable is on the horizontal axis,
- dependent variable is on the vertical axis.

But in economics demand diagrams, the convention is different:

- price is shown on the vertical axis,
- quantity demanded is shown on the horizontal axis.

This is important for drawing diagrams correctly.

## 3.3 Why The Demand Curve Usually Slopes Downward

The demand curve usually slopes downward because price and quantity demanded are generally inversely related.

When price falls, the commodity becomes cheaper, so the consumer tends to buy more.

When price rises, the commodity becomes costlier, so the consumer tends to buy less.

## 3.4 Demand Curve Reading

If the demand curve shows:

- price `P1` and quantity `Q1`,
- lower price `P2` and higher quantity `Q2`,

then the curve shows the law of demand.

The graph does not simply show points. It shows a behavioural relation between price and consumer choice.

# 4. Law Of Demand

## 4.1 Statement

The law of demand states:

> Other things remaining the same, when the price of a commodity rises, demand for it falls; when the price falls, demand for it rises.

In short:

```text
Price rises -> Quantity demanded falls
Price falls -> Quantity demanded rises
```

## 4.2 Meaning Of Other Things Remaining The Same

The law of demand assumes that the following do not change:

- consumer income,
- prices of other goods,
- tastes and preferences,
- expectations,
- other relevant conditions.

This is called the ceteris paribus condition.

## 4.3 Why This Condition Matters

Suppose the price of ice cream rises, but summer begins at the same time and people want more ice cream.

Demand may not fall in the observed market because preferences changed.

To isolate the effect of price, economists keep other factors constant.

That is why the law of demand is a ceteris paribus law.

## 4.4 Simple Demand Schedule

| Price | Quantity Demanded |
| ---: | ---: |
| 10 | 2 |
| 8 | 4 |
| 6 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |
| 2 | 10 |

This schedule follows the law of demand because quantity demanded rises as price falls.

# 5. Deriving Demand Curve From Consumer Equilibrium

## 5.1 Link With Indifference Curves And Budget Lines

The chapter connects demand with consumer equilibrium.

A consumer chooses the best affordable bundle using:

- preferences,
- income,
- prices,
- budget set,
- indifference curves.

When the price of one good changes, the budget line changes. The consumer's optimum bundle also changes. By recording the quantity chosen at each price, we derive the demand curve.

## 5.2 Banana And Mango Example

Assume a consumer consumes:

- bananas, called good `X1`,
- mangoes, called good `X2`.

The consumer has:

- income `M`,
- price of bananas `P1`,
- price of mangoes `P2`.

At the first price of bananas, the consumer chooses a bundle with a certain quantity of bananas.

Now suppose the price of bananas falls while income and price of mangoes remain unchanged.

The budget set expands because bananas are cheaper.

The consumer moves to a new optimum bundle and buys more bananas.

If the price of bananas falls again, the consumer may buy still more bananas.

## 5.3 How The Demand Curve Is Built

For each price of bananas:

1. Find the consumer's optimum bundle.
2. Record the quantity of bananas chosen.
3. Plot price of bananas on the vertical axis.
4. Plot quantity of bananas on the horizontal axis.
5. Join the points.

The result is the demand curve for bananas.

## 5.4 Main Conclusion

When the price of bananas falls, the consumer chooses more bananas.

Therefore, the demand curve for bananas is negatively sloped.

# 6. Substitution Effect And Income Effect

A fall in price affects demand through two channels:

1. substitution effect,
2. income effect.

## 6.1 Substitution Effect

When the price of a commodity falls, it becomes cheaper relative to other goods.

The consumer tends to substitute the cheaper good for relatively costlier goods.

Example:

If bananas become cheaper compared with mangoes, the consumer may buy more bananas and fewer mangoes.

This increases demand for bananas.

## 6.2 Income Effect

When the price of a commodity falls, the consumer's purchasing power rises.

The consumer can buy the same bundle as before and still have income left, or can buy a better bundle.

This change in purchasing power is called the income effect.

For a normal good, the income effect increases demand when price falls.

## 6.3 Combined Effect For Normal Goods

For normal goods:

- substitution effect of a price fall increases quantity demanded,
- income effect of a price fall also increases quantity demanded.

So the demand curve slopes downward clearly.

## 6.4 When The Effects Work Oppositely

For inferior goods, the income effect may work opposite to the substitution effect.

When price falls:

- substitution effect increases demand,
- income effect may reduce demand because the consumer feels richer and buys less of the inferior good.

Usually, the substitution effect is stronger, so demand still rises when price falls.

But if the income effect is stronger, demand may move in the same direction as price. Such a case is called a Giffen good.

# 7. Linear Demand

## 7.1 Meaning

A linear demand curve is a straight-line demand curve.

It can be written as:

```text
d(p) = a - bp
```

where:

- `d(p)` is quantity demanded at price `p`,
- `a` is demand when price is zero,
- `b` shows how much demand falls when price rises by one unit,
- `a / b` is the price at which demand becomes zero.

## 7.2 Complete Linear Demand Function

The chapter expresses linear demand as:

```text
d(p) = a - bp, for 0 <= p <= a / b
d(p) = 0, for p > a / b
```

This means quantity demanded cannot become negative.

Once price becomes too high, demand becomes zero.

## 7.3 Example

Suppose:

```text
d(p) = 20 - 2p
```

At `p = 0`:

```text
d(0) = 20 - 2(0)
d(0) = 20
```

At `p = 5`:

```text
d(5) = 20 - 2(5)
d(5) = 10
```

At `p = 10`:

```text
d(10) = 20 - 2(10)
d(10) = 0
```

At prices above 10, demand is zero.

## 7.4 Slope Interpretation

In:

```text
d(p) = a - bp
```

`b` tells us the fall in quantity demanded when price rises by one unit.

The negative sign shows inverse relation between price and demand.

# 8. Normal Goods And Inferior Goods

## 8.1 Income And Demand

Demand changes when consumer income changes.

But the direction of change depends on the type of good.

The two main categories are:

1. normal goods,
2. inferior goods.

## 8.2 Normal Goods

A normal good is a good whose demand rises when consumer income rises.

It also means demand falls when income falls.

The relation is:

```text
Income rises -> Demand rises
Income falls -> Demand falls
```

Examples may include:

- better quality food,
- branded clothing,
- education services,
- restaurant meals,
- electronics.

The exact classification can depend on the consumer's income level and preferences.

## 8.3 Inferior Goods

An inferior good is a good whose demand falls when consumer income rises.

It also means demand rises when income falls.

The relation is:

```text
Income rises -> Demand falls
Income falls -> Demand rises
```

Examples may include:

- coarse cereals for some consumers,
- very low-quality food items,
- cheaper substitutes used mainly when income is low.

## 8.4 Same Good Can Change Category

A good may be normal at one income level and inferior at another.

For a very poor consumer, demand for coarse cereal may rise when income rises slightly because the consumer can afford more food.

After income rises beyond a point, the consumer may shift toward better quality cereals and reduce demand for coarse cereal.

So the same good can behave differently at different income levels.

## 8.5 Giffen Good

A Giffen good is a special inferior good for which demand rises when price rises.

This can happen when:

- the good is inferior,
- it takes a large share of the consumer's budget,
- the income effect is stronger than the substitution effect.

For most goods, demand falls when price rises.

Giffen goods are exceptional.

# 9. Substitutes And Complements

Demand for one good can change when the price of another related good changes.

Related goods are mainly of two types:

1. substitutes,
2. complements.

## 9.1 Substitute Goods

Substitute goods are goods that can be used in place of each other.

Examples:

- tea and coffee,
- pen and pencil,
- bus travel and metro travel,
- two brands of soap,
- two brands of biscuits.

## 9.2 Demand Relation For Substitutes

If the price of a substitute rises, demand for the good rises.

Example:

If coffee becomes costlier, some consumers may shift to tea. Demand for tea rises.

So:

```text
Price of substitute rises -> Demand for the good rises
Price of substitute falls -> Demand for the good falls
```

Demand for a good usually moves in the same direction as the price of its substitute.

## 9.3 Complementary Goods

Complementary goods are goods consumed together.

Examples:

- tea and sugar,
- shoes and socks,
- pen and ink,
- car and petrol,
- printer and ink cartridge.

## 9.4 Demand Relation For Complements

If the price of a complement rises, demand for the good falls.

Example:

If sugar becomes costlier, some consumers may consume less tea. Demand for tea may fall.

So:

```text
Price of complement rises -> Demand for the good falls
Price of complement falls -> Demand for the good rises
```

Demand for a good usually moves in the opposite direction from the price of its complement.

## 9.5 Substitutes Versus Complements

| Feature | Substitutes | Complements |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Relationship | Used instead of each other | Used together |
| Example | Tea and coffee | Tea and sugar |
| If related good's price rises | Demand for this good rises | Demand for this good falls |
| Direction | Same direction | Opposite direction |

# 10. Shifts In The Demand Curve

## 10.1 Meaning Of Shift

A shift in the demand curve means the whole demand curve moves.

At the same price, the consumer now demands a different quantity.

A rightward shift means demand has increased.

A leftward shift means demand has decreased.

## 10.2 Causes Of Shift

The demand curve shifts because of changes in factors other than the commodity's own price.

Main causes:

1. change in income,
2. change in price of related goods,
3. change in tastes and preferences.

## 10.3 Income Change

For a normal good:

```text
Income rises -> Demand curve shifts right
Income falls -> Demand curve shifts left
```

For an inferior good:

```text
Income rises -> Demand curve shifts left
Income falls -> Demand curve shifts right
```

## 10.4 Price Of Substitute Changes

If the price of a substitute rises:

```text
Demand for this good rises
Demand curve shifts right
```

If the price of a substitute falls:

```text
Demand for this good falls
Demand curve shifts left
```

Example:

If coffee becomes costlier, demand for tea may rise. The demand curve for tea shifts right.

## 10.5 Price Of Complement Changes

If the price of a complement rises:

```text
Demand for this good falls
Demand curve shifts left
```

If the price of a complement falls:

```text
Demand for this good rises
Demand curve shifts right
```

Example:

If sugar becomes costlier, demand for tea may fall. The demand curve for tea shifts left.

## 10.6 Preferences Change

If preferences become favourable toward a good:

```text
Demand curve shifts right
```

If preferences become unfavourable:

```text
Demand curve shifts left
```

Examples:

- Demand for ice cream may rise in summer because preference for it increases.
- Demand for cold drinks may fall if people believe they are harmful to health.

## 10.7 Shift Summary

| Cause | Direction Of Demand Curve |
| --- | --- |
| Income rises for normal good | Right |
| Income falls for normal good | Left |
| Income rises for inferior good | Left |
| Income falls for inferior good | Right |
| Price of substitute rises | Right |
| Price of substitute falls | Left |
| Price of complement rises | Left |
| Price of complement falls | Right |
| Preference becomes favourable | Right |
| Preference becomes unfavourable | Left |

# 11. Movement Along Demand Curve And Shift Of Demand Curve

This is one of the most important distinctions in demand analysis.

## 11.1 Movement Along Demand Curve

Movement along the same demand curve happens when the commodity's own price changes.

Other factors remain constant.

Examples:

- price rises from Rs 10 to Rs 12,
- quantity demanded falls along the same curve.

This is called contraction of demand.

If price falls and quantity demanded rises along the same curve, it is called extension of demand.

## 11.2 Shift Of Demand Curve

Shift of the demand curve happens when any factor other than the commodity's own price changes.

Examples:

- income changes,
- price of substitute changes,
- price of complement changes,
- preferences change.

The whole curve moves right or left.

## 11.3 Movement Versus Shift

| Basis | Movement Along Demand Curve | Shift Of Demand Curve |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cause | Change in own price | Change in other determinants |
| Curve | Same curve | New curve |
| Diagram | Point moves on same curve | Entire curve moves |
| Example | Price of tea falls | Income rises or coffee price rises |
| Terms | Extension or contraction | Increase or decrease in demand |

## 11.4 Exam Warning

Do not write "demand increases" for every case.

Use precise language:

- If own price falls, quantity demanded increases. This is movement along the curve.
- If income rises for a normal good, demand increases. This is a rightward shift.

# 12. Market Demand

## 12.1 Individual Demand

Individual demand is the demand of one consumer for a commodity at different prices.

Each consumer may have a different demand curve because consumers differ in income, preferences, needs, and habits.

## 12.2 Market Demand

Market demand is the total demand of all consumers in the market at a given price.

At each price:

```text
Market demand = Sum of all individual demands
```

If there are two consumers:

```text
Market demand = Demand of consumer 1 + Demand of consumer 2
```

If there are many consumers:

```text
Market demand = q1 + q2 + q3 + ... + qn
```

## 12.3 Horizontal Summation

Market demand is found by adding individual demand curves horizontally.

This means:

- take one price,
- find quantity demanded by each consumer at that price,
- add the quantities,
- repeat for all prices.

We add quantities, not prices.

That is why it is called horizontal summation.

## 12.4 Example With Two Consumers

Suppose at price `p'`:

- consumer 1 demands `q1'`,
- consumer 2 demands `q2'`.

Then market demand is:

```text
q1' + q2'
```

At another price:

- consumer 1 demands `q1`,
- consumer 2 demands `q2`.

Market demand is:

```text
q1 + q2
```

This method can be extended to any number of consumers.

# 13. Adding Two Linear Demand Curves

The chapter gives an example of two consumers.

Consumer 1:

```text
d1(p) = 10 - p
```

Consumer 2:

```text
d2(p) = 15 - p
```

Consumer 1 demands zero when price is greater than 10.

Consumer 2 demands zero when price is greater than 15.

## 13.1 Market Demand For Price Up To 10

When:

```text
p <= 10
```

both consumers demand positive quantities.

So:

```text
Market demand = d1(p) + d2(p)
Market demand = (10 - p) + (15 - p)
Market demand = 25 - 2p
```

## 13.2 Market Demand Between 10 And 15

When:

```text
10 < p <= 15
```

consumer 1 demands zero because price is above 10.

Consumer 2 still demands:

```text
15 - p
```

So:

```text
Market demand = 15 - p
```

## 13.3 Market Demand Above 15

When:

```text
p > 15
```

both consumers demand zero.

So:

```text
Market demand = 0
```

## 13.4 Complete Market Demand Function

```text
Market demand = 25 - 2p, for p <= 10
Market demand = 15 - p, for 10 < p <= 15
Market demand = 0, for p > 15
```

## 13.5 Sample Table

| Price | Consumer 1 Demand `10 - p` | Consumer 2 Demand `15 - p` | Market Demand |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 5 | 5 | 10 | 15 |
| 10 | 0 | 5 | 5 |
| 12 | 0 | 3 | 3 |
| 15 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| 16 | 0 | 0 | 0 |

This table shows why the market demand curve is obtained by adding quantities at each price.

# 14. How To Write Demand Answers

## 14.1 Definition Question

If asked "What is demand?", write:

```text
Demand for a commodity is the quantity that a consumer is willing and able to buy at given prices, given income, prices of related goods, and tastes and preferences.
```

Then add:

```text
Demand is different from mere desire because it requires purchasing power and willingness to buy.
```

## 14.2 Law Of Demand Question

Use this structure:

```text
The law of demand states that, other things remaining the same, when the price of a commodity rises, its quantity demanded falls, and when price falls, quantity demanded rises.
This happens because the commodity becomes relatively cheaper when its price falls, and the consumer's purchasing power also rises.
Therefore, the demand curve generally slopes downward.
```

## 14.3 Demand Curve Question

Use this structure:

```text
A demand curve is the graphical representation of the demand function.
It shows the quantity demanded at different prices, keeping income, related prices, and preferences constant.
In economics, price is measured on the vertical axis and quantity demanded on the horizontal axis.
The demand curve usually slopes downward because price and quantity demanded are inversely related.
```

## 14.4 Movement Versus Shift Question

Use this structure:

```text
Movement along a demand curve occurs due to a change in the commodity's own price.
The consumer moves from one point to another on the same demand curve.
Shift of the demand curve occurs due to a change in factors other than own price, such as income, prices of related goods, or preferences.
In a shift, the entire demand curve moves rightward or leftward.
```

## 14.5 Market Demand Question

Use this structure:

```text
Market demand is the total demand of all consumers in the market at a given price.
It is obtained by adding individual demands at each price.
Graphically, the market demand curve is obtained by horizontal summation of individual demand curves.
```

# 15. Common Mistakes And Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Demand means desire. | Demand means willingness and ability to buy at given conditions. |
| Demand depends only on price. | Demand depends on own price, income, related prices, and preferences. |
| Price is put on horizontal axis in demand diagrams. | In economics, price is usually on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis. |
| Demand curve always shifts when price changes. | Own price change causes movement along the same demand curve. |
| Demand increases when price falls. | More precise: quantity demanded increases when own price falls. |
| Income rise always increases demand. | It increases demand for normal goods but reduces demand for inferior goods. |
| Substitute price rise reduces demand. | Substitute price rise increases demand for this good. |
| Complement price rise increases demand. | Complement price rise reduces demand for this good. |
| Market demand is found by adding prices. | Market demand is found by adding quantities at each price. |
| All inferior goods are Giffen goods. | Giffen goods are rare special inferior goods where income effect dominates substitution effect. |

# 17. Solved Mini Examples

## Example 1: Normal Good

Suppose milk is a normal good for a family.

If family income rises, demand for milk increases.

The demand curve shifts right.

## Example 2: Inferior Good

Suppose coarse cereal is an inferior good for a consumer.

If income rises, the consumer shifts to better-quality cereals.

Demand for coarse cereal falls.

The demand curve shifts left.

## Example 3: Substitutes

Tea and coffee are substitutes.

If the price of coffee rises, consumers may buy more tea.

Demand curve for tea shifts right.

## Example 4: Complements

Tea and sugar are complements.

If the price of sugar rises, tea consumption may fall.

Demand curve for tea shifts left.

## Example 5: Movement Along Demand Curve

If the price of apples falls and all other factors remain constant, quantity demanded of apples rises.

This is movement along the same demand curve.

## Example 6: Shift Of Demand Curve

If doctors recommend eating more apples for health, preference for apples improves.

Demand for apples rises at every price.

The demand curve shifts right.

# 18. Strong Short Answers

## Question: What is a demand function?

A demand function shows the relation between quantity demanded and its determinants. When income, prices of related goods, and preferences are kept constant, the demand function can be written as `X = f(P)`, where `X` is quantity demanded and `P` is the price of the good.

## Question: State the law of demand.

The law of demand states that, other things remaining the same, there is an inverse relation between price and quantity demanded. When price rises, quantity demanded falls. When price falls, quantity demanded rises.

## Question: What is a normal good?

A normal good is a good whose demand increases when consumer income increases and decreases when consumer income decreases. Demand for a normal good moves in the same direction as income.

## Question: What is an inferior good?

An inferior good is a good whose demand decreases when consumer income increases and increases when consumer income decreases. Demand for an inferior good moves in the opposite direction of income.

## Question: What are substitutes?

Substitutes are goods that can be used in place of each other. If the price of one substitute rises, demand for the other usually rises. Tea and coffee are common examples.

## Question: What are complements?

Complements are goods that are consumed together. If the price of one complement rises, demand for the other usually falls. Tea and sugar are common examples.

## Question: What is market demand?

Market demand is the total quantity demanded by all consumers in a market at a given price. It is obtained by adding individual demands at each price.

# 19. Long Answer: Law Of Demand

The law of demand states that, other things remaining the same, price and quantity demanded are inversely related. When the price of a commodity rises, the consumer demands less of it. When the price falls, the consumer demands more of it.

This law assumes that consumer income, prices of related goods, tastes, and preferences remain unchanged. This assumption is necessary because these other factors can also affect demand.

The law can be explained through substitution effect and income effect. When the price of a commodity falls, it becomes cheaper relative to other goods. The consumer substitutes this cheaper good for relatively costlier goods. This is the substitution effect.

Also, a fall in price increases the consumer's purchasing power. The consumer can now buy more with the same income. This is the income effect.

For normal goods, both effects usually increase quantity demanded when price falls. Therefore, the demand curve slopes downward.

---

# 20. Long Answer: Movement And Shift

Movement along a demand curve occurs when the commodity's own price changes, while all other determinants remain constant. If price falls, quantity demanded rises and the consumer moves downward along the same demand curve. If price rises, quantity demanded falls and the consumer moves upward along the same curve.

A shift in the demand curve occurs when a factor other than own price changes. These factors include income, prices of related goods, and tastes and preferences. If demand increases at every price, the curve shifts rightward. If demand decreases at every price, the curve shifts leftward.

For example, if the price of tea falls, there is movement along the demand curve for tea. But if income rises and tea is a normal good, the entire demand curve for tea shifts rightward.

Thus, own price causes movement along the curve, while other determinants cause shifts of the curve.

---

# 21. Long Answer: Individual Demand And Market Demand

Individual demand is the quantity demanded by a single consumer at different prices. Market demand is the total demand of all consumers in the market at each price.

Market demand is obtained by adding individual demands horizontally. This means that at each price, we add the quantities demanded by all consumers. We do not add prices.

For example, if at price Rs 10, consumer 1 demands 3 units and consumer 2 demands 5 units, market demand is 8 units. At another price, the quantities are again added to get market demand at that price.

If there are two consumers with demand functions `d1(p) = 10 - p` and `d2(p) = 15 - p`, then for prices up to 10, market demand is:

```text
(10 - p) + (15 - p) = 25 - 2p
```

For prices above 10 and up to 15, consumer 1 demands zero, so market demand is:

```text
15 - p
```

For prices above 15, both consumers demand zero, so market demand is zero.

This process is called horizontal summation of demand curves.

# 23. Final Chapter Summary

Demand is the quantity of a commodity that a consumer is willing and able to buy at given prices, income, related prices, and preferences.

The demand function shows how quantity demanded depends on its determinants. When other factors are constant, demand can be written as a function of price: `X = f(P)`.

The demand curve is the graphical representation of this function. In economics, price is placed on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis. The demand curve usually slopes downward because price and quantity demanded are inversely related.

The law of demand says that, other things remaining equal, a rise in price reduces quantity demanded and a fall in price increases quantity demanded. This is explained through substitution effect and income effect.

Normal goods have demand that moves in the same direction as income. Inferior goods have demand that moves in the opposite direction of income. Giffen goods are rare inferior goods where demand may rise when price rises.

Substitute goods are used in place of each other; demand for a good moves in the same direction as the price of its substitute. Complementary goods are used together; demand for a good moves in the opposite direction of the price of its complement.

A change in own price causes movement along the demand curve. A change in income, related prices, or preferences shifts the demand curve.

Market demand is found by adding individual demands at each price. This process is called horizontal summation.
