## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how an individual consumer makes choices.

A consumer has limited income. Goods have prices. The consumer has preferences. The basic question is:

**How does a consumer choose the best affordable bundle of goods?**

The chapter develops this question in two ways:

1. **Cardinal utility analysis**, where utility is treated as measurable in numbers.
2. **Ordinal utility analysis**, where the consumer only ranks bundles as better, worse, or equally preferred.

The chapter then uses consumer choice to explain:

- budget set
- budget line
- indifference curve
- marginal rate of substitution
- consumer equilibrium
- demand curve
- law of demand
- normal and inferior goods
- substitutes and complements
- market demand
- price elasticity of demand
- relation between elasticity and expenditure

The central idea is:

**Consumer demand is shaped by preferences, income, prices, and the trade-off between satisfaction and affordability.**

# 1. The Consumer's Problem

## 1.1 What the Consumer Has to Decide

A consumer has income.

Many goods are available.

Each good has a price.

The consumer wants maximum satisfaction.

Therefore, the consumer must decide:

**How should income be spent among different goods?**

This is a problem of choice.

## 1.2 What Determines the Best Bundle?

The best bundle depends on two things:

| Factor | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Preferences | What the consumer likes more or less. |
| Affordability | What the consumer can buy with income at given prices. |

Preferences answer:

What does the consumer want?

Budget answers:

What can the consumer afford?

Choice happens where these two meet.

## 1.3 Two-Good Simplification

To make diagrams easy, the chapter assumes the consumer chooses between two goods:

- bananas
- mangoes

Let:

`x1 = quantity of bananas`

`x2 = quantity of mangoes`

A bundle is written as:

`(x1, x2)`

Examples:

| Bundle | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `(5, 10)` | 5 bananas and 10 mangoes |
| `(10, 5)` | 10 bananas and 5 mangoes |
| `(0, 6)` | 0 bananas and 6 mangoes |
| `(4, 0)` | 4 bananas and 0 mangoes |

## 1.4 Why Bundles Matter

Consumers do not usually choose one good in isolation.

They choose combinations.

Economics studies how the consumer ranks these combinations and chooses the best one that can be afforded.

# 2. Utility

## 2.1 Meaning of Utility

Utility is the want-satisfying capacity of a commodity.

If a good satisfies a stronger want, it gives higher utility.

Example:

A bottle of water gives very high utility to a thirsty person.

The same bottle may give less utility to someone who is not thirsty.

## 2.2 Utility Is Subjective

Utility differs from person to person.

It also changes with place and time.

Examples:

| Situation | Utility Difference |
| --- | --- |
| Chocolate lover vs non-lover | Same chocolate gives different utility. |
| Room heater in Ladakh vs Chennai | Utility is higher in a cold place. |
| Room heater in winter vs summer | Utility is higher in winter. |

Important:

Utility does not mean moral usefulness.

A commodity has utility if it satisfies a want, even if the want itself is not socially desirable.

# 3. Cardinal Utility Analysis

## 3.1 Meaning

Cardinal utility analysis assumes utility can be measured in numbers.

Example:

A consumer may say:

```text
This shirt gives me 50 units of utility.
```

This is a simplifying assumption.

It helps build concepts such as total utility and marginal utility.

## 3.2 Total Utility

Total utility is the total satisfaction obtained from consuming a given quantity of a commodity.

Notation:

`TUn`

Meaning:

Total utility from consuming `n` units.

Example:

If 4 bananas give 28 units of utility:

`TU4 = 28`

## 3.3 Marginal Utility

Marginal utility is the change in total utility due to consumption of one additional unit.

Formula:

```text
MUn = TUn - TU(n-1)
```

Example:

If:

`TU4 = 28`

`TU5 = 30`

Then:

```text
MU5 = TU5 - TU4
MU5 = 30 - 28
MU5 = 2
```

The fifth banana adds 2 units of utility.

## 3.4 Relation Between TU and MU

Total utility is the sum of marginal utilities.

Formula:

```text
TUn = MU1 + MU2 + ... + MU(n-1) + MUn
```

Example:

If marginal utilities of first five units are:

`12, 6, 4, 2, 0`

Then:

`TU5 = 12 + 6 + 4 + 2 + 0 = 24`

## 3.5 Utility Table

| Units Consumed | Total Utility | Marginal Utility |
| ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 1 | 12 | 12 |
| 2 | 18 | 6 |
| 3 | 22 | 4 |
| 4 | 24 | 2 |
| 5 | 24 | 0 |
| 6 | 22 | -2 |

## 3.6 Reading the Utility Table

From unit 1 to unit 4:

- TU rises
- MU is positive
- but MU falls

At unit 5:

- TU is constant
- MU is zero

At unit 6:

- TU falls
- MU is negative

## 3.7 Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

The law states:

**As consumption of a commodity increases, marginal utility from each additional unit tends to decline, keeping consumption of other commodities constant.**

Reason:

As the consumer gets more of a good, the intensity of desire for additional units becomes weaker.

Example:

The first glass of water when thirsty gives high utility.

The second gives less additional utility.

After many glasses, another glass may give zero or negative utility.

## 3.8 TU and MU Relationship

| MU Behaviour | TU Behaviour |
| --- | --- |
| MU positive | TU increases |
| MU falling but positive | TU increases at decreasing rate |
| MU zero | TU maximum/constant |
| MU negative | TU falls |

## 3.9 Demand Curve from Diminishing MU

The law of diminishing marginal utility helps explain why demand curves slope downward.

Reasoning:

1. Each additional unit gives less marginal utility.
2. Consumer is willing to pay less for each additional unit.
3. More quantity is demanded only at a lower price.
4. Therefore, price and quantity demanded are inversely related.

Example:

If a consumer buys 5 units at Rs 40, the 6th unit gives lower marginal utility than the 5th.

So the consumer will buy the 6th unit only if the price falls below Rs 40.

# 4. Ordinal Utility Analysis

## 4.1 Why Ordinal Utility Is Needed

Cardinal utility is easy to understand but unrealistic.

In real life, consumers usually do not say:

```text
This bundle gives me 47 units of utility.
```

Instead, they say:

- I prefer bundle A to bundle B.
- I am indifferent between bundle C and bundle D.
- Bundle X is better than bundle Y.

Ordinal utility analysis uses ranking, not numerical measurement.

## 4.2 Meaning of Indifference

If a consumer receives the same satisfaction from two bundles, the consumer is indifferent between them.

Example:

The consumer may be equally satisfied with:

- 1 banana and 15 mangoes
- 2 bananas and 12 mangoes
- 3 bananas and 10 mangoes
- 4 bananas and 9 mangoes

These bundles lie on the same indifference curve.

## 4.3 Indifference Curve

An indifference curve joins all bundles that give the consumer equal satisfaction.

All points on one indifference curve are equally preferred.

Important:

The consumer is not saying the bundles are identical.

The consumer is saying they give the same level of satisfaction.

## 4.4 Marginal Rate of Substitution

Marginal rate of substitution, MRS, is the amount of one good the consumer is willing to give up for one more unit of another good while remaining on the same indifference curve.

In the banana-mango example:

MRS of bananas for mangoes means:

How many mangoes the consumer gives up to get one more banana while satisfaction remains unchanged.

Formula:

```text
MRS = |change in mangoes / change in bananas|
```

## 4.5 MRS Table

| Combination | Bananas | Mangoes | MRS |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| A | 1 | 15 | - |
| B | 2 | 12 | 3:1 |
| C | 3 | 10 | 2:1 |
| D | 4 | 9 | 1:1 |

Reading:

From A to B, consumer gives up 3 mangoes for 1 more banana.

From B to C, consumer gives up 2 mangoes for 1 more banana.

From C to D, consumer gives up 1 mango for 1 more banana.

## 4.6 Law of Diminishing Marginal Rate of Substitution

The law states:

**As the consumer gets more of one good, she is willing to sacrifice smaller and smaller amounts of the other good for each additional unit.**

Reason:

As bananas increase, marginal utility of bananas falls.

As mangoes decrease, marginal utility of mangoes rises.

So the consumer becomes less willing to give up mangoes for more bananas.

## 4.7 Shape of Indifference Curve

A normal indifference curve is convex to the origin.

Reason:

MRS diminishes as the consumer moves down along the curve.

## 4.8 Perfect Substitutes

Perfect substitutes are goods that can be used in place of each other and provide exactly the same utility.

Example:

Five-rupee notes and five-rupee coins.

If the consumer treats them exactly alike, she may always exchange one five-rupee note for one five-rupee coin.

In this case:

- MRS remains constant
- indifference curve is a straight line

## 4.9 Indifference Map

An indifference map is a family of indifference curves.

Each curve represents a different satisfaction level.

Higher indifference curves represent higher satisfaction if preferences are monotonic.

## 4.10 Monotonic Preferences

Preferences are monotonic if:

Between two bundles, the consumer prefers the bundle that has more of at least one good and no less of the other good.

Example:

Compare:

`(10, 8)` and `(8, 6)`

The first bundle has more of both goods.

With monotonic preferences, the consumer prefers `(10, 8)`.

## 4.11 Features of Indifference Curves

### Feature 1: Indifference Curves Slope Downward

If the consumer gets more bananas and remains equally satisfied, she must give up some mangoes.

If she gets more bananas without giving up mangoes, she moves to a higher satisfaction level.

Therefore, an indifference curve slopes downward.

### Feature 2: Higher Indifference Curve Gives Higher Utility

If one curve lies above another, it represents bundles with more of at least one good and no less of the other.

With monotonic preferences, higher curve gives higher utility.

### Feature 3: Two Indifference Curves Never Intersect

If two indifference curves intersect, the same bundle would lie on both curves.

That would imply two different satisfaction levels for the same bundle, which is impossible.

It would also violate consistent preference ranking.

### Feature 4: Indifference Curves Are Usually Convex to the Origin

This follows from diminishing MRS.

As the consumer has more bananas and fewer mangoes, she is willing to give up fewer mangoes for another banana.

# 5. The Consumer's Budget

## 5.1 Why Budget Matters

Preferences tell what the consumer wants.

Budget tells what the consumer can afford.

A consumer may prefer a very large bundle, but cannot choose it if income is not enough.

## 5.2 Budget Set

The budget set is the collection of all bundles that the consumer can buy with given income and prices.

Let:

`p1 = price of good 1`

`p2 = price of good 2`

`x1 = quantity of good 1`

`x2 = quantity of good 2`

`M = consumer income`

Then a bundle is affordable if:

```text
p1x1 + p2x2 <= M
```

## 5.3 Budget Line

The budget line shows all bundles that cost exactly the consumer's income.

Equation:

```text
p1x1 + p2x2 = M
```

It is the boundary of the budget set.

## 5.4 Example

Suppose:

`p1 = Rs 4`

`p2 = Rs 5`

`M = Rs 20`

Budget line:

```text
4x1 + 5x2 = 20
```

If all income is spent on good 1:

```text
x1 = 20/4 = 5
```

If all income is spent on good 2:

```text
x2 = 20/5 = 4
```

So intercepts are:

- 5 units of good 1
- 4 units of good 2

## 5.5 Slope of Budget Line

From:

```text
p1x1 + p2x2 = M
```

Solve for `x2`:

```text
x2 = M/p2 - (p1/p2)x1
```

Slope:

```text
-p1/p2
```

Meaning:

The slope shows how many units of good 2 must be given up to get one more unit of good 1 while spending the same income.

## 5.6 Why Budget Line Slopes Downward

Income is fixed.

If the consumer buys more of good 1, less income remains for good 2.

Therefore, good 2 must decrease when good 1 increases.

This is why the budget line slopes downward.

## 5.7 Changes in Budget Set

The budget set changes when:

- income changes
- price of good 1 changes
- price of good 2 changes

### Income Increase

If income increases and prices remain unchanged:

- budget line shifts outward/rightward
- slope remains same
- consumer can afford more of both goods

Example:

Income rises from Rs 20 to Rs 40.

Prices remain Rs 4 and Rs 5.

New budget line:

```text
4x1 + 5x2 = 40
```

Intercepts:

- good 1: `40/4 = 10`
- good 2: `40/5 = 8`

### Income Decrease

If income decreases and prices remain unchanged:

- budget line shifts inward/leftward
- slope remains same

### Price of Good 1 Falls

If price of good 1 falls:

- consumer can buy more of good 1 if all income is spent on it
- x-axis intercept increases
- budget line rotates outward on good 1 axis

### Price of Good 2 Falls

If price of good 2 falls:

- consumer can buy more of good 2 if all income is spent on it
- y-axis intercept increases
- budget line rotates outward on good 2 axis

### Both Prices and Income Double

If both prices and income double:

Original:

```text
p1x1 + p2x2 <= M
```

New:

```text
2p1x1 + 2p2x2 <= 2M
```

Divide by 2:

```text
p1x1 + p2x2 <= M
```

So the budget set remains unchanged.

# 6. Optimal Choice of the Consumer

## 6.1 What Is Consumer Optimum?

Consumer optimum is the bundle chosen by a rational consumer from the budget set.

A rational consumer chooses the most preferred affordable bundle.

## 6.2 Graphical Idea

The consumer wants the highest possible indifference curve.

But the consumer cannot go beyond the budget set.

Therefore, the optimum is usually where:

**the budget line is tangent to the highest attainable indifference curve.**

## 6.3 Tangency Condition

At the optimum:

```text
MRS = p1/p2
```

Meaning:

The rate at which the consumer is willing to substitute one good for another equals the rate at which the market allows substitution through prices.

## 6.4 Why Tangency Makes Sense

MRS shows the consumer's personal trade-off.

Price ratio shows the market trade-off.

At optimum:

- consumer's preference trade-off equals market trade-off
- there is no reason to shift spending from one good to another

## 6.5 If MRS Is Not Equal to Price Ratio

If:

`MRS > p1/p2`

The consumer values good 1 relatively more than the market cost. She may buy more good 1.

If:

`MRS < p1/p2`

The consumer values good 1 relatively less than the market cost. She may buy less good 1.

The adjustment continues until the best affordable bundle is reached.

# 7. Demand

## 7.1 Meaning of Demand

Demand is the quantity of a commodity that a consumer is willing and able to buy at given:

- price of the commodity
- prices of related goods
- income of the consumer
- tastes and preferences

Both willingness and ability matter.

Want without purchasing power is not demand.

Purchasing power without willingness is also not demand.

## 7.2 Demand Function

The consumer's demand for a good depends on many variables.

Main factors:

```text
Demand for good X = f(price of X, prices of related goods, income, tastes/preferences)
```

When other things remain unchanged and only price changes:

```text
X = f(P)
```

where:

`X = quantity demanded`

`P = price`

## 7.3 Demand Curve

A demand curve is the graphical representation of the demand function.

It shows quantity demanded at different prices, keeping other factors constant.

In economics:

- price is usually measured on vertical axis
- quantity demanded is measured on horizontal axis

## 7.4 Law of Demand

The law of demand states:

**Other things being equal, there is a negative relationship between price of a commodity and its quantity demanded.**

If price rises, demand falls.

If price falls, demand rises.

## 7.5 Why Demand Curve Slopes Downward

Two explanations:

1. Diminishing marginal utility.
2. Substitution effect and income effect.

### Diminishing MU Explanation

Additional units give lower marginal utility.

So the consumer is willing to buy additional units only at lower prices.

### Substitution Effect

When a good becomes cheaper relative to another good, the consumer substitutes the cheaper good for the relatively expensive good.

Example:

If bananas become cheaper relative to mangoes, the consumer may buy more bananas.

### Income Effect

When the price of a good falls, the consumer's purchasing power rises.

The consumer can buy more with the same income.

For normal goods, this increases demand further.

## 7.6 Linear Demand

A linear demand curve may be written as:

```text
d(p) = a - bp
```

where:

`a` = demand when price is zero

`b` = amount by which demand falls when price rises by one unit

At price:

```text
p = a/b
```

demand becomes zero.

## 7.7 Normal Goods

A normal good is a good whose demand increases when consumer income increases.

Demand moves in the same direction as income.

Examples:

- better quality food
- clothing
- transport services
- many consumer goods

## 7.8 Inferior Goods

An inferior good is a good whose demand decreases when consumer income increases.

Demand moves in the opposite direction of income.

Example:

Low quality cereals may be inferior for some consumers. As income rises, they may shift to better quality cereals.

Important:

A good may be normal at one income level and inferior at another.

## 7.9 Giffen Good

A Giffen good is a special inferior good for which demand may rise when price rises.

This can happen when the income effect is stronger than the substitution effect and works in the opposite direction.

Important:

Giffen goods are rare and are treated as exceptions to the usual law of demand.

# 8. Substitutes and Complements

## 8.1 Substitute Goods

Substitutes are goods that can be used in place of each other.

Examples:

- tea and coffee
- Coke and Pepsi
- bus travel and metro travel in some cases

If price of coffee rises, demand for tea may rise.

General rule:

**Demand for a good moves in the same direction as the price of its substitute.**

## 8.2 Complementary Goods

Complements are goods consumed together.

Examples:

- tea and sugar
- shoes and socks
- pen and ink
- car and petrol

If price of sugar rises, demand for tea may fall.

General rule:

**Demand for a good moves in the opposite direction of the price of its complement.**

## 8.3 Comparison

| Basis | Substitutes | Complements |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Relationship | Used in place of each other | Used together |
| Example | tea and coffee | tea and sugar |
| If price of related good rises | Demand for this good rises | Demand for this good falls |
| Direction | Same direction | Opposite direction |

# 9. Shifts and Movements in Demand

## 9.1 Movement Along Demand Curve

A movement along the demand curve happens when the price of the commodity itself changes.

Other factors remain constant.

Examples:

- price falls -> movement down along demand curve -> quantity demanded rises
- price rises -> movement up along demand curve -> quantity demanded falls

## 9.2 Shift in Demand Curve

A shift in demand curve happens when a factor other than the commodity's own price changes.

Factors:

- income
- price of related goods
- tastes and preferences
- expectations
- number of consumers

## 9.3 Rightward Shift

Rightward shift means demand increases at every price.

Causes:

- income rises for a normal good
- price of substitute rises
- price of complement falls
- preferences become favourable
- number of consumers increases

Example:

Demand for ice cream may shift rightward in summer.

## 9.4 Leftward Shift

Leftward shift means demand decreases at every price.

Causes:

- income falls for a normal good
- income rises for an inferior good
- price of substitute falls
- price of complement rises
- preferences become unfavourable

Example:

If people learn that cold drinks may be harmful to health, demand for cold drinks may shift leftward.

## 9.5 Quick Rule

| Cause | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Own price changes | Movement along demand curve |
| Income or other factors change | Shift of demand curve |

# 10. Market Demand

## 10.1 Meaning

Market demand is the total demand of all consumers in a market at a particular price.

It is found by adding individual demands at that price.

## 10.2 Example with Two Consumers

Suppose at price `p`:

Consumer 1 demands `q1`.

Consumer 2 demands `q2`.

Market demand:

```text
Q = q1 + q2
```

## 10.3 Horizontal Summation

The market demand curve is obtained by horizontal summation of individual demand curves.

This means:

At each price, add the quantities demanded by all consumers.

Do not add prices.

Add quantities.

## 10.4 Example: Linear Demand Curves

Suppose:

```text
d1(p) = 10 - p
d2(p) = 15 - p
```

At price `p <= 10`:

```text
Market demand = (10 - p) + (15 - p)
Market demand = 25 - 2p
```

At price greater than 10 but less than or equal to 15:

Consumer 1 demands zero.

Consumer 2 still demands:

```text
15 - p
```

At price greater than 15:

Both demand zero.

## 10.5 Why Market Demand Slopes Downward

Market demand usually slopes downward because:

- individual demand curves generally slope downward
- lower price attracts more quantity from existing consumers
- lower price may also attract more buyers

# 11. Price Elasticity of Demand

## 11.1 Meaning

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price.

Formula:

```text
Price elasticity of demand =
percentage change in quantity demanded / percentage change in price
```

Symbol:

`eD`

Because price and demand usually move in opposite directions, elasticity is often negative.

For simplicity, we often use the absolute value.

## 11.2 Formula Using Change

```text
eD = (Delta Q / Q) / (Delta P / P)
```

or:

```text
eD = (Delta Q / Delta P) x (P / Q)
```

where:

`Delta Q = change in quantity`

`Delta P = change in price`

## 11.3 Banana Example

Old price:

`P1 = Rs 5`

Old quantity:

`Q1 = 15`

New price:

`P2 = Rs 7`

New quantity:

`Q2 = 12`

Change in quantity:

```text
Delta Q = 12 - 15 = -3
```

Percentage change in quantity:

```text
(-3/15) x 100 = -20%
```

Change in price:

```text
Delta P = 7 - 5 = 2
```

Percentage change in price:

```text
(2/5) x 100 = 40%
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = -20/40 = -0.5
```

Absolute elasticity:

```text
|eD| = 0.5
```

Demand is inelastic because elasticity is less than 1.

## 11.4 Types of Elasticity

| Elasticity Value | Name | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `|eD| > 1` | Elastic demand | Quantity changes by larger percentage than price. |
| `|eD| = 1` | Unitary elastic demand | Quantity and price change by equal percentages. |
| `|eD| < 1` | Inelastic demand | Quantity changes by smaller percentage than price. |
| `|eD| = 0` | Perfectly inelastic demand | Quantity does not change when price changes. |
| `|eD| = infinity` | Perfectly elastic demand | Any price change causes extremely large response. |

## 11.5 Elasticity Along a Linear Demand Curve

For a linear demand curve:

```text
q = a - bp
```

Change in quantity per unit change in price:

```text
Delta q / Delta p = -b
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = -b x (p/q)
```

Since `p/q` changes at different points, elasticity changes along a straight-line demand curve.

## 11.6 Elasticity Positions on a Straight-Line Demand Curve

| Point on Demand Curve | Elasticity |
| --- | --- |
| Vertical-axis intercept | Infinite |
| Above midpoint | Greater than 1 |
| Midpoint | Equal to 1 |
| Below midpoint | Less than 1 |
| Horizontal-axis intercept | Zero |

## 11.7 Geometric Measure

At any point on a straight-line demand curve:

```text
Elasticity = lower segment / upper segment
```

Meaning:

The segment below the point is divided by the segment above the point.

This gives a quick geometric way to judge elasticity.

## 11.8 Constant Elasticity Demand Curves

| Curve Shape | Elasticity |
| --- | --- |
| Vertical demand curve | Perfectly inelastic, `eD = 0` |
| Horizontal demand curve | Perfectly elastic, `eD = infinity` |
| Rectangular hyperbola | Unitary elastic at every point |

## 11.9 Rectangular Hyperbola

A rectangular hyperbola has equation:

```text
xy = c
```

For demand:

```text
pq = constant
```

This means total expenditure remains the same at all points.

Therefore, elasticity is unitary at every point.

# 12. Determinants of Price Elasticity

## 12.1 Nature of the Good

Necessities usually have inelastic demand.

Examples:

- basic food
- medicines
- essential utilities

Luxuries usually have elastic demand.

Examples:

- expensive vacations
- luxury cars
- jewellery

## 12.2 Availability of Close Substitutes

If close substitutes are easily available, demand is more elastic.

Example:

If one variety of pulses becomes expensive, consumers may switch to another variety.

If no close substitute is available, demand is less elastic.

## 12.3 Specific vs Broad Goods

Demand for a broad category like food is less elastic.

Demand for a specific food item may be more elastic.

Reason:

Specific items have closer substitutes.

## 12.4 Summary

| Factor | Elasticity Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Necessity | More inelastic |
| Luxury | More elastic |
| Many close substitutes | More elastic |
| Few/no substitutes | More inelastic |
| Broad category | More inelastic |
| Narrow specific good | More elastic |

# 13. Elasticity and Expenditure

## 13.1 Total Expenditure

Total expenditure on a good is:

```text
Total Expenditure = Price x Quantity
```

or:

```text
TE = P x Q
```

## 13.2 Why Elasticity Matters for Expenditure

Price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions.

When price rises:

- price effect increases expenditure
- quantity effect decreases expenditure

Which effect is stronger depends on elasticity.

## 13.3 If Demand Is Elastic

Elastic demand means quantity changes by a larger percentage than price.

If price rises:

- quantity falls strongly
- total expenditure falls

If price falls:

- quantity rises strongly
- total expenditure rises

Therefore, price and expenditure move in opposite directions.

## 13.4 If Demand Is Inelastic

Inelastic demand means quantity changes by a smaller percentage than price.

If price rises:

- quantity falls only slightly
- total expenditure rises

If price falls:

- quantity rises only slightly
- total expenditure falls

Therefore, price and expenditure move in the same direction.

## 13.5 If Demand Is Unitary Elastic

Unitary elastic demand means percentage change in quantity equals percentage change in price.

Total expenditure remains unchanged.

## 13.6 Expenditure Table

| Price Change | Quantity Change | Elasticity | Expenditure Effect |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Price rises | Quantity falls by larger percentage | Elastic | Expenditure falls |
| Price rises | Quantity falls by smaller percentage | Inelastic | Expenditure rises |
| Price rises | Quantity falls by same percentage | Unitary | No change |
| Price falls | Quantity rises by larger percentage | Elastic | Expenditure rises |
| Price falls | Quantity rises by smaller percentage | Inelastic | Expenditure falls |
| Price falls | Quantity rises by same percentage | Unitary | No change |

## 13.7 Quick Rule

| Elasticity | Price and Expenditure Relation |
| --- | --- |
| Elastic | Opposite direction |
| Inelastic | Same direction |
| Unitary elastic | Expenditure unchanged |

Question:

4x1 + 5x2 = 20

x1 = 20/4 = 5

## 14.2 Income Doubles

Income rises to Rs 40.

New equation:

```text
4x1 + 5x2 = 40
```

The budget line shifts outward parallel to the old line.

## 14.3 Price of Good 2 Falls by Rs 1

Old price of good 2:

`Rs 5`

New price:

`Rs 4`

New budget line:

```text
4x1 + 4x2 = 20
```

Good 1 intercept remains:

`20/4 = 5`

Good 2 intercept becomes:

`20/4 = 5`

The budget line rotates outward on the good 2 axis.

## 14.4 Both Prices and Income Double

If:

`p1`, `p2`, and `M` all double,

the budget set does not change.

Reason:

Purchasing power remains the same.

## 14.5 Consumer Income from Affordable Bundle

If a consumer can afford 6 units of good 1 and 8 units of good 2, and prices are Rs 6 and Rs 8:

```text
Income = 6 x 6 + 8 x 8
Income = 36 + 64
Income = Rs 100
```

## 14.6 Integer Bundle Question

If:

`p1 = Rs 10`

`p2 = Rs 10`

`M = Rs 40`

Budget condition:

```text
10x1 + 10x2 <= 40
```

or:

```text
x1 + x2 <= 4
```

All affordable integer bundles are combinations where total units are 4 or less.

Bundles costing exactly Rs 40 satisfy:

```text
x1 + x2 = 4
```

Examples:

`(0,4), (1,3), (2,2), (3,1), (4,0)`

## 14.7 Monotonic Preferences Question

Can a consumer with monotonic preferences be indifferent between `(10,8)` and `(8,6)`?

No.

Reason:

`(10,8)` has more of both goods than `(8,6)`.

With monotonic preferences, more of both goods is preferred.

## 14.8 Market Demand Function

If:

```text
d1(p) = 20 - p, for p <= 20
d1(p) = 0, for p > 20
```

and:

```text
d2(p) = 30 - 2p, for p <= 15
d2(p) = 0, for p > 15
```

Then:

For `p <= 15`:

```text
D(p) = (20 - p) + (30 - 2p)
D(p) = 50 - 3p
```

For `15 < p <= 20`:

```text
D(p) = 20 - p
```

For `p > 20`:

```text
D(p) = 0
```

## 14.9 Elasticity Calculation

Question:

At price Rs 4, demand is 25 units. Price rises to Rs 5, demand falls to 20 units.

Percentage change in quantity:

```text
Delta Q = 20 - 25 = -5
(-5/25) x 100 = -20%
```

Percentage change in price:

```text
Delta P = 5 - 4 = 1
(1/4) x 100 = 25%
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = -20/25 = -0.8
```

Absolute value:

```text
|eD| = 0.8
```

Demand is inelastic.

## 14.10 Elasticity from Demand Curve

If:

```text
D(p) = 10 - 3p
```

Then:

```text
Delta Q / Delta P = -3
```

At price:

```text
p = 5/3
```

Quantity:

```text
q = 10 - 3(5/3)
q = 10 - 5
q = 5
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = (-3) x (p/q)
eD = (-3) x ((5/3)/5)
eD = -1
```

Demand is unitary elastic at this point.

# 15. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Treating utility as same for all people | Utility is subjective and differs across persons, place, and time. |
| Saying TU always rises | TU rises while MU is positive; TU falls when MU is negative. |
| Confusing TU and MU | TU is total satisfaction; MU is extra satisfaction from one more unit. |
| Thinking ordinal utility measures utility in numbers | Ordinal utility ranks bundles; it does not measure exact units. |
| Saying all indifference curves are straight lines | Normal indifference curves are convex; perfect substitutes have straight-line indifference curves. |
| Forgetting budget line slope | Slope is `-p1/p2`. |
| Adding prices to get market demand | Market demand is horizontal summation: add quantities at each price. |
| Confusing movement and shift | Own price change causes movement; other factors cause shift. |
| Saying all inferior goods are Giffen goods | Giffen goods are rare special inferior goods. |
| Ignoring negative sign in elasticity | Demand elasticity is usually negative; often absolute value is used. |

# 16. Answer-Writing Frames

## 16.1 Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

Write:

The law of diminishing marginal utility states that as a consumer consumes more units of a commodity, the marginal utility from each additional unit declines, other things remaining constant. This happens because the intensity of desire for the commodity falls as more of it is consumed. When marginal utility is positive, total utility rises; when marginal utility is zero, total utility is maximum or constant; when marginal utility is negative, total utility falls.

## 16.2 Indifference Curve

Write:

An indifference curve is a curve joining all bundles of two goods that give the consumer the same level of satisfaction. The consumer is indifferent among all points on the same curve. An indifference curve slopes downward because, to get more of one good while remaining equally satisfied, the consumer must give up some of the other good. It is usually convex to the origin because marginal rate of substitution diminishes.

## 16.3 Budget Line

Write:

A budget line represents all bundles of two goods that cost exactly the consumer's income at given prices. If prices are `p1` and `p2`, quantities are `x1` and `x2`, and income is `M`, the budget line is `p1x1 + p2x2 = M`. Its slope is `-p1/p2`, showing the rate at which the market allows the consumer to substitute one good for another.

## 16.4 Consumer Equilibrium

Write:

A rational consumer chooses the most preferred bundle from the budget set. Graphically, the consumer optimum is usually the point where the budget line is tangent to the highest attainable indifference curve. At this point, the marginal rate of substitution equals the price ratio, or `MRS = p1/p2`. This means the consumer's willingness to substitute equals the market rate of substitution.

## 16.5 Law of Demand

Write:

The law of demand states that, other things remaining constant, there is an inverse relation between the price of a commodity and its quantity demanded. When price rises, quantity demanded falls; when price falls, quantity demanded rises. This is explained by diminishing marginal utility, substitution effect, and income effect.

## 16.6 Price Elasticity of Demand

Write:

Price elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. It is defined as percentage change in quantity demanded divided by percentage change in price. If the absolute value is greater than one, demand is elastic; if it is less than one, demand is inelastic; and if it is equal to one, demand is unitary elastic.

# 17. Quick Revision

## 17.1 Utility

- Utility means want-satisfying capacity.
- Cardinal utility assumes numerical measurement.
- Ordinal utility assumes ranking.
- TU is total satisfaction.
- MU is extra satisfaction.
- `MUn = TUn - TU(n-1)`.
- `TUn = MU1 + MU2 + ... + MUn`.
- MU usually diminishes as consumption increases.

## 17.2 Indifference Curves

- Same curve = same satisfaction.
- Higher curve = higher satisfaction.
- Curves slope downward.
- Curves do not intersect.
- Normal curves are convex due to diminishing MRS.
- Perfect substitutes have straight-line indifference curves.

## 17.3 Budget

- Budget set: all affordable bundles.
- Budget line: bundles costing exactly income.
- Equation: `p1x1 + p2x2 = M`.
- Slope: `-p1/p2`.
- Income rise shifts line outward.
- Price change rotates line.
- If income and prices change proportionately, budget set may remain unchanged.

## 17.4 Demand

- Demand depends on own price, related prices, income, and preferences.
- Law of demand: price rises -> demand falls, other things equal.
- Normal good: income rises -> demand rises.
- Inferior good: income rises -> demand falls.
- Substitute price rises -> demand for this good rises.
- Complement price rises -> demand for this good falls.
- Own price change -> movement along curve.
- Other factor change -> curve shifts.

## 17.5 Elasticity

- `eD = percentage change in Q / percentage change in P`.
- Elastic: `|eD| > 1`.
- Inelastic: `|eD| < 1`.
- Unitary: `|eD| = 1`.
- Straight-line demand has different elasticity at different points.
- Elastic demand: price and expenditure move opposite.
- Inelastic demand: price and expenditure move same.
- Unitary demand: expenditure unchanged.

# 18. Final One-Page Summary

Consumer behaviour studies how a consumer chooses the best affordable bundle of goods. The consumer has preferences, but also faces a budget constraint because income is limited and goods have prices.

Utility is the want-satisfying capacity of a commodity. Cardinal utility analysis assumes utility can be measured numerically and uses total utility and marginal utility. Marginal utility is the change in total utility from consuming one extra unit. The law of diminishing marginal utility states that marginal utility declines as consumption increases.

Ordinal utility analysis assumes that consumers rank bundles. An indifference curve joins bundles giving equal satisfaction. It slopes downward because more of one good requires less of the other to keep satisfaction constant. It is usually convex because marginal rate of substitution diminishes. Higher indifference curves represent higher satisfaction, and two indifference curves cannot intersect.

The budget set contains all affordable bundles. The budget line contains bundles that cost exactly the consumer's income and is written as `p1x1 + p2x2 = M`. Its slope is `-p1/p2`. The consumer optimum is the most preferred affordable bundle, usually where the budget line is tangent to the highest attainable indifference curve.

Demand is the quantity a consumer is willing and able to buy. The law of demand says price and quantity demanded are inversely related, other things constant. Demand changes with income, related goods' prices, and tastes. Market demand is the horizontal summation of individual demands. Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to price change. Elasticity also determines whether total expenditure rises, falls, or remains unchanged after a price change.
