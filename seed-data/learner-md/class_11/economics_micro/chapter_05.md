## Chapter Purpose

This chapter brings demand and supply together.

Earlier chapters studied consumers and firms separately:

- consumers decide how much to buy at different prices
- firms decide how much to sell at different prices

This chapter asks:

**At what price and quantity do buyers' plans and sellers' plans match?**

That matching point is called market equilibrium.

The chapter also explains what happens when:

- price is above equilibrium
- price is below equilibrium
- demand shifts
- supply shifts
- firms freely enter or exit
- government imposes price ceiling or price floor

The central idea is:

**Market equilibrium occurs where market demand equals market supply.**

# 1. Meaning of Market Equilibrium

## 1.1 What Is Equilibrium?

Equilibrium is a situation where the plans of buyers and sellers match.

In market equilibrium:

```text
Market demand = Market supply
```

At this point:

- consumers are able to buy the quantity they want at that price
- firms are able to sell the quantity they want at that price
- there is no pressure for price to change

## 1.2 Equilibrium Price and Quantity

The price at which demand equals supply is called equilibrium price.

The quantity bought and sold at that price is called equilibrium quantity.

If:

`p* = equilibrium price`

`q* = equilibrium quantity`

then:

```text
qD(p*) = qS(p*)
```

where:

`qD(p*) = quantity demanded at p*`

`qS(p*) = quantity supplied at p*`

## 1.3 Zero Excess Demand and Zero Excess Supply

Equilibrium can also be described as:

- zero excess demand
- zero excess supply

At equilibrium:

```text
qD - qS = 0
```

and:

```text
qS - qD = 0
```

# 2. Excess Demand and Excess Supply

## 2.1 Excess Demand

Excess demand occurs when quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied.

Formula:

```text
Excess demand = qD - qS
```

It happens when market price is below equilibrium price.

At a low price:

- consumers want to buy more
- firms want to sell less
- shortage occurs

Price tends to rise.

## 2.2 Excess Supply

Excess supply occurs when quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded.

Formula:

```text
Excess supply = qS - qD
```

It happens when market price is above equilibrium price.

At a high price:

- firms want to sell more
- consumers want to buy less
- unsold stock appears

Price tends to fall.

## 2.3 Invisible Hand

The "invisible hand" refers to the price adjustment process in a competitive market.

When there is excess demand, price rises.

When there is excess supply, price falls.

This adjustment continues until demand equals supply.

## 2.4 Adjustment Table

| Market Situation | Meaning | Price Tendency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `qD > qS` | Excess demand/shortage | Price rises |
| `qS > qD` | Excess supply/surplus | Price falls |
| `qD = qS` | Equilibrium | No tendency to change |

# 3. Market Equilibrium with Fixed Number of Firms

## 3.1 Graphical Meaning

With a fixed number of firms, market equilibrium occurs where the demand curve intersects the supply curve.

At the intersection:

```text
Demand = Supply
```

The corresponding price is equilibrium price.

The corresponding quantity is equilibrium quantity.

## 3.2 If Price Is Below Equilibrium

At a price below equilibrium:

- demand is high
- supply is low
- excess demand exists

Some consumers are willing to pay more.

Price rises.

As price rises:

- quantity demanded falls
- quantity supplied rises

The market moves toward equilibrium.

## 3.3 If Price Is Above Equilibrium

At a price above equilibrium:

- supply is high
- demand is low
- excess supply exists

Some firms cannot sell desired quantity.

They reduce price.

As price falls:

- quantity demanded rises
- quantity supplied falls

The market moves toward equilibrium.

## 3.4 Numerical Example

Demand:

```text
qD = 200 - p
```

Supply:

```text
qS = 120 + p
```

Equilibrium:

```text
qD = qS
200 - p = 120 + p
80 = 2p
p = 40
```

Equilibrium price:

```text
p* = Rs 40
```

Equilibrium quantity:

```text
q* = 200 - 40 = 160
```

or:

```text
q* = 120 + 40 = 160
```

## 3.5 Excess Demand Example

At price `p = 25`:

```text
qD = 200 - 25 = 175
qS = 120 + 25 = 145
```

Since:

```text
qD > qS
```

there is excess demand.

Amount of excess demand:

```text
175 - 145 = 30
```

## 3.6 Excess Supply Example

At price `p = 45`:

```text
qD = 200 - 45 = 155
qS = 120 + 45 = 165
```

Since:

```text
qS > qD
```

there is excess supply.

Amount of excess supply:

```text
165 - 155 = 10
```

## 3.7 Excess Demand and Excess Supply Functions

Using the same example:

Excess demand:

```text
ED = qD - qS
ED = (200 - p) - (120 + p)
ED = 80 - 2p
```

Excess supply:

```text
ES = qS - qD
ES = (120 + p) - (200 - p)
ES = 2p - 80
```

At `p = 40`, both are zero.

# 4. Shifts in Demand and Supply with Fixed Number of Firms

## 4.1 Demand Shift

Demand curve shifts when demand changes due to a factor other than own price.

Examples:

- income changes
- preferences change
- price of substitute changes
- price of complement changes
- number of consumers changes

## 4.2 Rightward Demand Shift

A rightward demand shift means demand increases at every price.

With supply unchanged:

- equilibrium price rises
- equilibrium quantity rises

Reason:

At the old price, excess demand appears.

Price rises until the market clears again.

## 4.3 Leftward Demand Shift

A leftward demand shift means demand decreases at every price.

With supply unchanged:

- equilibrium price falls
- equilibrium quantity falls

Reason:

At the old price, excess supply appears.

Price falls until the market clears again.

## 4.4 Supply Shift

Supply curve shifts when supply changes due to a factor other than own price.

Examples:

- input prices change
- technology changes
- tax changes
- number of firms changes
- weather affects production

## 4.5 Rightward Supply Shift

A rightward supply shift means supply increases at every price.

With demand unchanged:

- equilibrium price falls
- equilibrium quantity rises

Reason:

At the old price, excess supply appears.

Price falls and quantity traded increases.

## 4.6 Leftward Supply Shift

A leftward supply shift means supply decreases at every price.

With demand unchanged:

- equilibrium price rises
- equilibrium quantity falls

Reason:

At the old price, excess demand appears.

Price rises and quantity traded falls.

## 4.7 Single Shift Summary

| Change | Equilibrium Price | Equilibrium Quantity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demand increases | rises | rises |
| Demand decreases | falls | falls |
| Supply increases | falls | rises |
| Supply decreases | rises | falls |

## 4.8 Simultaneous Shifts

When both demand and supply shift, one effect may be certain while the other depends on magnitude.

| Demand Shift | Supply Shift | Price Effect | Quantity Effect |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Right | Right | uncertain | rises |
| Left | Left | uncertain | falls |
| Right | Left | rises | uncertain |
| Left | Right | falls | uncertain |

## 4.9 Memory Rule

When demand and supply shift in the same direction:

- quantity effect is clear
- price effect is uncertain

When demand and supply shift in opposite directions:

- price effect is clear
- quantity effect is uncertain

## 4.10 Income Change Example

For a normal good:

Income rise shifts demand right.

Equilibrium price rises.

Equilibrium quantity rises.

Income fall shifts demand left.

Equilibrium price falls.

Equilibrium quantity falls.

For an inferior good, the direction is opposite.

## 4.11 Price of Related Goods

### Substitute Example: Coffee and Tea

If price of coffee rises, consumers may shift to tea.

Demand for tea shifts right.

Equilibrium price of tea rises.

Equilibrium quantity of tea rises.

### Complement Example: Shoes and Socks

If price of shoes rises, demand for shoes falls.

Because socks are used with shoes, demand for socks may fall.

Demand curve for socks shifts left.

Equilibrium price of socks falls.

Equilibrium quantity of socks falls.

## 4.12 Input Price Change

If price of an input rises:

- cost of production rises
- supply shifts left
- equilibrium price rises
- equilibrium quantity falls

If input price falls:

- cost falls
- supply shifts right
- equilibrium price falls
- equilibrium quantity rises

# 5. Labour Market Equilibrium

## 5.1 Goods Market vs Labour Market

In a goods market:

- households demand goods
- firms supply goods

In a labour market:

- households supply labour
- firms demand labour

This is the main difference.

## 5.2 Labour Means Hours of Work

In this chapter, labour means hours of work supplied by labourers, not simply the number of labourers.

## 5.3 Demand for Labour

A firm hires labour up to the point where the extra cost of hiring labour equals the extra benefit from labour.

Extra cost:

```text
wage rate = w
```

Extra benefit:

```text
Marginal Revenue Product of Labour = MRPL
```

Condition:

```text
w = MRPL
```

## 5.4 Marginal Revenue Product of Labour

Marginal revenue product of labour is:

```text
MRPL = MR x MPL
```

where:

`MR = marginal revenue`

`MPL = marginal product of labour`

For a perfectly competitive firm:

```text
MR = price
```

So:

```text
MRPL = price x MPL
```

This is also called value of marginal product of labour:

```text
VMPL = p x MPL
```

## 5.5 Why Labour Demand Slopes Downward

Because of diminishing marginal product of labour.

At higher wage:

The firm hires less labour so that `VMPL` rises enough to equal the higher wage.

Therefore, demand for labour is downward sloping.

## 5.6 Labour Supply

Households decide how much labour to supply by choosing between:

- income from work
- leisure

When wage rises, two effects occur:

1. Leisure becomes more costly, so people may work more.
2. Income rises, so people may want more leisure and work less.

For an individual, the labour supply curve may bend backward at high wages.

But market labour supply is generally upward sloping because higher wages attract more people to work.

## 5.7 Wage Determination

The wage rate is determined where labour demand equals labour supply.

At equilibrium wage:

```text
Labour demanded = Labour supplied
```

# 6. Market Equilibrium with Free Entry and Exit

## 6.1 Meaning of Free Entry and Exit

Free entry and exit means firms can enter the market when profit is attractive and leave the market when production is not worthwhile.

For simplicity, the chapter assumes identical firms.

## 6.2 Key Result

With free entry and exit:

```text
Equilibrium price = minimum average cost
```

or:

```text
p = min AC
```

## 6.3 Why Price Cannot Stay Above Minimum AC

If price is above minimum average cost:

- firms earn supernormal profit
- new firms enter
- market supply shifts right
- price falls
- supernormal profit disappears

Entry continues until firms earn only normal profit.

## 6.4 Why Price Cannot Stay Below Minimum AC

If price is below minimum average cost:

- firms earn less than normal profit or incur loss
- some firms exit
- market supply shifts left
- price rises
- losses disappear

Exit continues until remaining firms earn normal profit.

## 6.5 Equilibrium Quantity

When price equals minimum average cost, market quantity is determined by demand at that price.

If:

`p0 = min AC`

then:

```text
q0 = market demand at p0
```

## 6.6 Equilibrium Number of Firms

If each identical firm supplies `qf` at the equilibrium price, and total market quantity is `q0`, then:

```text
n = q0 / qf
```

where:

`n = number of firms`

## 6.7 Numerical Example

Market demand:

```text
qD = 200 - p
```

Individual firm supply:

```text
qf = 10 + p, for p >= 20
```

With free entry and exit:

```text
p0 = min AC = 20
```

Market quantity:

```text
q0 = 200 - 20 = 180
```

Each firm supply:

```text
qf = 10 + 20 = 30
```

Number of firms:

```text
n = 180 / 30 = 6
```

Equilibrium:

- price = Rs 20
- market quantity = 180
- number of firms = 6

## 6.8 Demand Shifts with Free Entry and Exit

With free entry and exit, demand shifts do not change long-run equilibrium price if firms are identical and cost conditions remain unchanged.

Reason:

Price returns to minimum average cost through entry or exit.

### Demand Increases

- at old price, excess demand appears
- price tends to rise
- supernormal profit attracts new firms
- supply expands
- price returns to min AC
- quantity increases
- number of firms increases

### Demand Decreases

- at old price, excess supply appears
- price tends to fall
- firms exit
- supply contracts
- price returns to min AC
- quantity decreases
- number of firms decreases

## 6.9 Fixed Firms vs Free Entry

| Demand Shift | Fixed Number of Firms | Free Entry and Exit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Demand increases | price rises, quantity rises | price unchanged in long run, quantity rises more |
| Demand decreases | price falls, quantity falls | price unchanged in long run, quantity falls more |

Important:

With free entry and exit, quantity effect is larger and price effect disappears in long-run equilibrium.

# 7. Applications of Demand-Supply Analysis

Government sometimes intervenes when market price is considered too high or too low.

Two important interventions:

1. Price ceiling.
2. Price floor.

---

# 8. Price Ceiling

## 8.1 Meaning

A price ceiling is a government-imposed maximum price.

It is an upper limit on price.

Price cannot legally rise above the ceiling.

## 8.2 When Price Ceiling Is Used

Price ceiling is usually imposed on essential goods when market price is considered too high for some consumers.

Examples:

- wheat
- rice
- kerosene
- sugar
- rent control in housing

## 8.3 Effective Price Ceiling

A price ceiling affects the market only if it is set below equilibrium price.

If ceiling is above equilibrium price, it does not bind.

## 8.4 Effect of Price Ceiling Below Equilibrium

At a price below equilibrium:

- quantity demanded rises
- quantity supplied falls
- excess demand occurs
- shortage is created

So price ceiling creates:

```text
Excess demand = quantity demanded - quantity supplied
```

## 8.5 Rationing

Because quantity supplied is less than quantity demanded, the available good must be distributed somehow.

One method is rationing.

Under rationing:

- ration coupons may be issued
- each consumer can buy only a limited amount
- goods may be sold through fair price shops

## 8.6 Problems of Price Ceiling

Price ceiling may create:

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Shortage | Demand exceeds supply. |
| Long queues | Consumers wait to buy rationed goods. |
| Black market | Some buyers pay illegally higher prices. |
| Reduced quality | Sellers may reduce quality if price is controlled. |
| Allocation problem | Not everyone gets desired quantity. |

## 8.7 Rent Control Example

If government fixes rent below market equilibrium rent:

- more people want apartments
- fewer owners want to rent out apartments
- shortage of rental housing appears
- informal payments or black-market rents may arise

---

# 9. Price Floor

## 9.1 Meaning

A price floor is a government-imposed minimum price.

It is a lower limit on price.

Price cannot legally fall below the floor.

## 9.2 When Price Floor Is Used

Examples:

- agricultural price support programmes
- minimum wage laws

In these cases, government may want to protect producers or workers from very low prices/wages.

## 9.3 Effective Price Floor

A price floor affects the market only if it is set above equilibrium price.

If floor is below equilibrium price, it does not bind.

## 9.4 Effect of Price Floor Above Equilibrium

At a price above equilibrium:

- quantity supplied rises
- quantity demanded falls
- excess supply occurs
- surplus is created

So:

```text
Excess supply = quantity supplied - quantity demanded
```

## 9.5 Agricultural Price Support

If government sets a support price above equilibrium:

- farmers supply more
- consumers demand less
- surplus appears

To maintain the floor, government may need to buy the surplus.

## 9.6 Minimum Wage

If minimum wage is set above equilibrium wage:

- more workers are willing to work
- firms demand fewer workers
- excess supply of labour occurs

This may create unemployment among workers willing to work at that wage.

## 9.7 Price Ceiling vs Price Floor

| Basis | Price Ceiling | Price Floor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Type of control | Maximum price | Minimum price |
| Effective when | Set below equilibrium | Set above equilibrium |
| Immediate result | Excess demand/shortage | Excess supply/surplus |
| Common use | Essential consumer goods, rent control | Agricultural support, minimum wage |
| Possible problem | queues, rationing, black market | unsold surplus, unemployment, government purchase |

# 10. Numerical Exercise Methods

## 10.1 Finding Equilibrium

Given:

```text
qD = 700 - p
qS = 500 + 3p
```

Set:

```text
qD = qS
```

Then:

```text
700 - p = 500 + 3p
200 = 4p
p = 50
```

Quantity:

```text
q = 700 - 50 = 650
```

or:

```text
q = 500 + 3(50) = 650
```

## 10.2 Supply Zero Below a Price

If a supply function says:

```text
qS = 500 + 3p, for p >= 15
qS = 0, for 0 <= p < 15
```

The reason is usually shutdown logic.

Below Rs 15, price is too low for firms to cover relevant cost, so firms supply zero.

## 10.3 Free Entry Example

Given:

```text
qD = 700 - p
qf = 8 + 3p, for p >= 20
```

Significance of `p = 20`:

It is the minimum average cost/long-run break-even price. Below this, firms do not produce.

With free entry and exit:

```text
p = 20
```

Market quantity:

```text
q = 700 - 20 = 680
```

Each firm supply:

```text
qf = 8 + 3(20) = 68
```

Number of firms:

```text
n = 680 / 68 = 10
```

## 10.4 Salt Market Example

Demand:

```text
qD = 1000 - p
```

Supply:

```text
qS = 700 + 2p
```

Equilibrium:

```text
1000 - p = 700 + 2p
300 = 3p
p = 100
```

Quantity:

```text
q = 1000 - 100 = 900
```

### Input Price Rise

New supply:

```text
qS = 400 + 2p
```

Equilibrium:

```text
1000 - p = 400 + 2p
600 = 3p
p = 200
```

Quantity:

```text
q = 1000 - 200 = 800
```

As expected:

- supply decreases
- equilibrium price rises
- equilibrium quantity falls

### Unit Tax Method

If a tax of Rs 3 per unit is imposed on sellers, the supply curve shifts left/up.

One way to handle it:

Use producer price:

```text
producer price = consumer price - tax
```

If original supply is:

```text
qS = 700 + 2p
```

with tax:

```text
qS = 700 + 2(p - 3)
qS = 700 + 2p - 6
qS = 694 + 2p
```

Set equal to demand:

```text
1000 - p = 694 + 2p
306 = 3p
p = 102
```

Quantity:

```text
q = 1000 - 102 = 898
```

So consumer price rises and quantity falls.

# 11. Answer-Writing Frames

## 11.1 Market Equilibrium

Write:

Market equilibrium is a situation where the plans of buyers and sellers match. At equilibrium, market demand equals market supply. The price at which this equality holds is called equilibrium price, and the quantity bought and sold at this price is called equilibrium quantity.

## 11.2 Excess Demand

Write:

Excess demand occurs when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied at a given price. It usually occurs when price is below equilibrium. Some consumers are unable to obtain the good and are willing to pay more, so price tends to rise. As price rises, quantity demanded falls and quantity supplied rises until equilibrium is restored.

## 11.3 Excess Supply

Write:

Excess supply occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded at a given price. It usually occurs when price is above equilibrium. Some firms cannot sell the quantity they wish to sell and therefore reduce price. As price falls, quantity demanded rises and quantity supplied falls until equilibrium is restored.

## 11.4 Fixed Number of Firms

Write:

When the number of firms is fixed, equilibrium is determined at the intersection of the market demand and market supply curves. A rightward demand shift raises both equilibrium price and quantity. A rightward supply shift lowers equilibrium price and raises equilibrium quantity.

## 11.5 Free Entry and Exit

Write:

With free entry and exit of identical firms, the long-run equilibrium price equals the minimum average cost of firms. If price is above minimum average cost, firms earn supernormal profit and new firms enter, shifting supply right and reducing price. If price is below minimum average cost, firms exit, shifting supply left and raising price. Thus price returns to minimum average cost.

## 11.6 Price Ceiling

Write:

A price ceiling is a government-imposed maximum price. If it is fixed below the equilibrium price, it creates excess demand because consumers demand more while firms supply less. This causes shortage. The government may use rationing, but price ceiling may also lead to queues and black marketing.

## 11.7 Price Floor

Write:

A price floor is a government-imposed minimum price. If it is fixed above the equilibrium price, it creates excess supply because firms supply more while consumers demand less. Agricultural support prices and minimum wages are examples. In agricultural markets, the government may need to buy surplus output to maintain the floor.

# 12. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Saying equilibrium means demand is high | Equilibrium means demand equals supply. |
| Confusing excess demand with demand increase | Excess demand is a gap at a given price; demand increase is a curve shift. |
| Confusing excess supply with supply increase | Excess supply is a gap at a given price; supply increase is a curve shift. |
| Saying price ceiling causes surplus | Effective price ceiling causes shortage/excess demand. |
| Saying price floor causes shortage | Effective price floor causes surplus/excess supply. |
| Forgetting free entry keeps price at min AC | With identical firms and free entry/exit, long-run price equals minimum AC. |
| Adding firm output incorrectly | Number of firms = market quantity divided by output per firm. |
| Treating labour market like goods market | In labour market, households supply and firms demand. |
| Assuming every demand shift changes long-run price with free entry | With free entry/exit and identical firms, price returns to minimum AC. |

# 14. Final One-Page Summary

Market equilibrium occurs where market demand equals market supply. The equilibrium price is the price at which the market clears, and the equilibrium quantity is the quantity bought and sold at that price. If price is below equilibrium, excess demand occurs and price tends to rise. If price is above equilibrium, excess supply occurs and price tends to fall.

With a fixed number of firms, equilibrium is found at the intersection of demand and supply curves. A rightward demand shift raises both price and quantity; a leftward demand shift lowers both. A rightward supply shift lowers price and raises quantity; a leftward supply shift raises price and lowers quantity. When demand and supply shift together, either price or quantity may become uncertain depending on the directions and magnitudes of shifts.

In the labour market, households supply labour and firms demand labour. A firm hires labour until wage equals marginal revenue product of labour. Under perfect competition, this is also the value of marginal product of labour. The wage rate is determined by the intersection of labour demand and labour supply.

With free entry and exit of identical firms, the long-run equilibrium price equals minimum average cost. Demand shifts change equilibrium quantity and number of firms but do not change the long-run equilibrium price.

Government price controls create important market effects. A price ceiling below equilibrium creates shortage or excess demand and may lead to rationing, queues, and black markets. A price floor above equilibrium creates surplus or excess supply and may require government purchase of surplus, as in agricultural support programmes.
