## Chapter Purpose

This chapter studies markets where the assumptions of perfect competition do not hold.

In perfect competition, each firm is a price taker. It sells a homogeneous product, faces many other firms, and cannot influence the market price by changing its own output.

Non-competitive markets are different. A firm may be able to influence price, entry may be restricted, products may be differentiated, or a few large firms may dominate the market. Because of this, output, price, revenue, profit, and consumer welfare behave differently from the perfect competition case studied earlier.

The chapter mainly studies three market structures:

1. **Monopoly**
2. **Monopolistic competition**
3. **Oligopoly**

The most detailed part of the chapter is monopoly, because it shows clearly how market power changes price and output decisions.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Perfect competition is useful as a benchmark, but many real markets do not look perfectly competitive.

For example:

- A patented medicine may be sold by one firm.
- A branded biscuit firm may face many competitors, but its product is not exactly the same as others.
- Two or three telecom firms may dominate a market and watch each other's pricing decisions closely.
- A railway service, electricity distribution network, or water supply system may have only one main supplier in a region.

In such cases, firms are not simple price takers. They must think about how much to produce, what price to charge, how consumers will respond, and how rivals may react.

That is why non-competitive markets are central to understanding real-world pricing and market power.

# 1. From Perfect Competition To Non-Competitive Markets

## 1.1 Perfect Competition As The Starting Point

The chapter begins by recalling perfect competition.

In perfect competition:

1. There are many firms and many consumers.
2. Each firm sells only a tiny share of total market output.
3. Each consumer buys only a tiny share of total market quantity.
4. Firms can freely enter or exit the industry.
5. The product sold by all firms is homogeneous.
6. No other industry's output is a close substitute for this product.
7. Firms and consumers have perfect knowledge of products, inputs, and prices.

Because of these conditions, no single firm can influence the market price.

The firm simply accepts the market price and chooses how much to produce.

## 1.2 What Changes In Non-Competitive Markets?

A non-competitive market arises when one or more assumptions of perfect competition fail.

The most important failures are:

| Perfect Competition Assumption | If This Assumption Fails | Possible Market Structure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Free entry and exit | New firms cannot easily enter | Monopoly or oligopoly |
| Very large number of firms | Market may have one or a few large firms | Monopoly or oligopoly |
| Homogeneous product | Products are similar but not identical | Monopolistic competition |
| No market power | Firms can influence price | Monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly |

## 1.3 Three Main Non-Competitive Structures

### Monopoly

A monopoly exists when there is one seller and many buyers.

It requires:

- one producer of the commodity,
- no close substitute for the commodity,
- restrictions on the entry of other firms.

### Oligopoly

An oligopoly exists when a market has only a few large sellers.

Each firm is large enough to affect market supply, market price, and the profits of other firms.

### Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition exists when:

- many firms are present,
- entry and exit are free,
- products are differentiated,
- products are close substitutes but not perfect substitutes.

# 2. Competitive Structure And Competitive Behaviour

## 2.1 The Important Distinction

The chapter makes an important point:

**Competitive market structure** and **competitive behaviour** are not the same thing.

In a perfectly competitive market, the market structure is highly competitive because there are many firms and no firm has price power. But an individual firm does not need to fight other firms aggressively. It can sell any quantity at the given market price.

In contrast, firms in less competitive structures may behave more aggressively. They may advertise, cut prices, change packaging, improve quality, or try to capture market share.

## 2.2 Example

An individual farmer in a perfectly competitive agricultural market may not fight another farmer for buyers. The market price is given.

But two soft drink companies may compete through:

- price discounts,
- advertising,
- product variants,
- distribution networks,
- brand loyalty,
- sponsorships,
- packaging.

The second case may involve more visible competitive behaviour even though the market structure is less perfectly competitive.

## 2.3 Core Rule

As a broad idea:

| Market Structure | Firm Behaviour |
| --- | --- |
| More competitive structure | Less need for individual rivalry |
| Less competitive structure | More visible rivalry among firms |
| Monopoly | No direct rival firm in the same commodity market |

This does not mean monopoly is socially better. It only means there is no competing seller of the same commodity inside the monopoly market.

# 3. Monopoly In The Commodity Market

## 3.1 Meaning Of Monopoly

A monopoly is a market structure in which there is a single seller.

But this short definition is not enough. For a proper monopoly, three conditions are required:

1. There is only one producer of the commodity.
2. There is no close substitute for the commodity.
3. Other firms are prevented from entering the market.

If any of these conditions fails, the firm may not be a pure monopolist.

## 3.2 Why Entry Restrictions Matter

If a monopoly firm earns high profits and entry is free, new firms will enter the market.

Once new firms enter:

- the monopolist is no longer the only seller,
- market supply increases,
- price may fall,
- the original firm's market power weakens.

Therefore, entry barriers are essential for monopoly to continue over time.

## 3.3 Other Markets Are Assumed Competitive

To study monopoly clearly, the chapter assumes that only the commodity market is monopolised.

Other markets are treated as competitive:

1. Consumers are price takers.
2. Input markets are perfectly competitive.
3. The monopolist buys inputs at given prices.

This helps isolate the effect of monopoly in one commodity market.

# 4. Demand Curve Faced By A Monopolist

## 4.1 Perfect Competition Case

In perfect competition, an individual firm can sell any amount at the given market price.

Therefore, the demand curve faced by the firm is horizontal.

The firm cannot raise price above the market price because buyers will shift to other identical sellers.

The firm does not need to reduce price to sell more because its own output is tiny compared with the market.

## 4.2 Monopoly Case

A monopolist is the only seller in the market.

Therefore, the demand curve faced by the monopolist is the entire market demand curve.

The market demand curve slopes downward.

This means:

- if the monopolist wants to sell more output, it must lower price,
- if it sells less output, it can charge a higher price,
- price depends on the quantity supplied by the monopolist.

## 4.3 The Monopolist Chooses A Point On Demand Curve

A monopolist can choose:

- a price and then sell the quantity demanded at that price, or
- a quantity and then charge the price consumers are willing to pay for that quantity.

But the monopolist cannot independently choose both any price and any quantity.

It must stay on the demand curve.

For example:

- If it chooses a high price, consumers buy a smaller quantity.
- If it chooses a large quantity, the price must be lower.

## 4.4 Demand Curve As A Constraint

The demand curve is not just a graph. It is a constraint on the monopolist.

It tells the monopolist:

> "For each quantity you want to sell, this is the maximum price consumers will pay."

This is why the monopolist must consider consumer demand before deciding output and price.

# 5. Demand, Price, Total Revenue, Average Revenue, And Marginal Revenue

The chapter uses a simple numerical demand function:

```text
q = 20 - 2p
```

Here:

- `q` is quantity sold,
- `p` is price in rupees.

This can be rewritten as:

```text
p = 10 - 0.5q
```

This second form is useful because it gives the price corresponding to each quantity.

## 5.1 Price Schedule From The Demand Function

If the monopolist chooses different quantities, the price is:

| Quantity `q` | Price `p = 10 - 0.5q` |
| ---: | ---: |
| 0 | 10.0 |
| 1 | 9.5 |
| 2 | 9.0 |
| 3 | 8.5 |
| 4 | 8.0 |
| 5 | 7.5 |
| 6 | 7.0 |
| 7 | 6.5 |
| 8 | 6.0 |
| 9 | 5.5 |
| 10 | 5.0 |
| 11 | 4.5 |
| 12 | 4.0 |
| 13 | 3.5 |

As quantity increases, price falls.

This is the basic sign of a downward-sloping demand curve.

---

## 5.2 Total Revenue

Total revenue is:

```text
TR = P x Q
```

Using the demand equation:

```text
TR = p x q
TR = (10 - 0.5q) x q
TR = 10q - 0.5q^2
```

This is not a straight line.

It is a quadratic expression with a negative squared term. Therefore, its graph has the shape of an inverted vertical parabola.

## 5.3 Revenue Table

| Quantity `q` | Price `p` | Total Revenue `TR` | Average Revenue `AR` | Marginal Revenue `MR` |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 0 | 10.0 | 0.0 | - | - |
| 1 | 9.5 | 9.5 | 9.5 | 9.5 |
| 2 | 9.0 | 18.0 | 9.0 | 8.5 |
| 3 | 8.5 | 25.5 | 8.5 | 7.5 |
| 4 | 8.0 | 32.0 | 8.0 | 6.5 |
| 5 | 7.5 | 37.5 | 7.5 | 5.5 |
| 6 | 7.0 | 42.0 | 7.0 | 4.5 |
| 7 | 6.5 | 45.5 | 6.5 | 3.5 |
| 8 | 6.0 | 48.0 | 6.0 | 2.5 |
| 9 | 5.5 | 49.5 | 5.5 | 1.5 |
| 10 | 5.0 | 50.0 | 5.0 | 0.5 |
| 11 | 4.5 | 49.5 | 4.5 | -0.5 |
| 12 | 4.0 | 48.0 | 4.0 | -1.5 |
| 13 | 3.5 | 45.5 | 3.5 | -2.5 |

## 5.4 How To Read The Table

The table shows five important ideas.

First, price falls as output increases.

Second, total revenue initially rises.

Third, total revenue reaches a maximum around 10 units.

Fourth, after 10 units, total revenue falls.

Fifth, marginal revenue falls continuously and becomes negative after a point.

# 6. Average Revenue

## 6.1 Meaning

Average revenue is revenue per unit sold.

```text
AR = TR / Q
```

Since:

```text
TR = P x Q
```

we get:

```text
AR = (P x Q) / Q
AR = P
```

Therefore:

```text
Average Revenue = Price
```

## 6.2 Why Market Demand Curve Is AR Curve For Monopoly

For a monopolist, the price comes from the market demand curve.

Since average revenue equals price, the monopolist's AR curve is the same as its demand curve.

So:

```text
Demand curve = AR curve
```

This is one of the central results of the chapter.

## 6.3 Graphical Meaning Of AR From TR Curve

Average revenue can also be read from the total revenue curve.

For any output level:

1. Mark the corresponding point on the TR curve.
2. Join that point to the origin.
3. The slope of this line from the origin gives AR.

Example:

At `q = 6`, total revenue is `42`.

```text
AR = TR / Q
AR = 42 / 6
AR = 7
```

This is also the slope of the line from the origin to the TR point for `q = 6`.

---

# 7. Marginal Revenue

## 7.1 Meaning

Marginal revenue is the change in total revenue caused by selling one more unit of output.

```text
MR = Change in TR / Change in Q
```

When output changes by one unit:

```text
MR = TR at current quantity - TR at previous quantity
```

## 7.2 Example Calculations

When quantity rises from 0 to 1:

```text
MR = 9.5 - 0
MR = 9.5
```

When quantity rises from 4 to 5:

```text
MR = 37.5 - 32
MR = 5.5
```

When quantity rises from 11 to 12:

```text
MR = 48 - 49.5
MR = -1.5
```

The last result is negative because total revenue falls when output increases from 11 to 12.

## 7.3 Why MR Falls

A monopolist must lower price to sell more units.

This has two effects:

1. It earns revenue from the extra unit sold.
2. It receives a lower price on the units it was already selling.

Because of the second effect, the extra revenue from selling one more unit is less than the price of that unit.

Therefore:

```text
MR < AR
```

when the demand curve slopes downward.

## 7.4 MR And TR

The relation between MR and TR is:

| Situation | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| MR is positive | TR is rising |
| MR is zero | TR is at its maximum |
| MR is negative | TR is falling |

This relation is extremely important for monopoly equilibrium.

## 7.5 Graphical Meaning Of MR

Marginal revenue can be understood from the total revenue curve.

At any point on a smooth TR curve:

- the slope of the tangent gives MR,
- a positive slope means positive MR,
- a horizontal tangent means zero MR,
- a negative slope means negative MR.

The NCERT table uses discrete units, so MR at exactly 10 units appears as `0.5`. But if output is treated as continuous and we move very close to 10 units, the MR approaches zero at the point where TR is maximum.

---

# 8. Why MR Lies Below AR

## 8.1 The Core Logic

For a downward-sloping demand curve:

- to sell more, price must fall,
- the lower price applies to all units sold,
- total revenue rises by less than the price of the extra unit,
- therefore marginal revenue is less than average revenue.

So the MR curve lies below the AR curve.

## 8.2 Simple Example

Suppose the monopolist sells 4 units at Rs 8 each.

```text
TR = 4 x 8 = 32
```

To sell 5 units, it must reduce price to Rs 7.5.

```text
TR = 5 x 7.5 = 37.5
```

The fifth unit sells at Rs 7.5, but total revenue rises only by:

```text
37.5 - 32 = 5.5
```

So:

```text
MR = 5.5
AR = Price = 7.5
MR < AR
```

This happens because the price reduction affects the earlier 4 units also.

## 8.3 Steep And Flat Demand Curves

The distance between AR and MR depends on the steepness of the demand curve.

| Type Of Demand Curve | Relation Between AR And MR |
| --- | --- |
| Flatter demand curve | MR is closer to AR |
| Steeper demand curve | MR is farther below AR |

Reason:

When demand is steep, selling extra units requires a larger fall in price. The loss on previous units is larger, so MR falls much below AR.

---

# 9. Marginal Revenue And Price Elasticity Of Demand

## 9.1 The Relation

The chapter gives an important relation between MR and elasticity.

| MR Value | Elasticity Of Demand | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| MR > 0 | Elastic demand, `e > 1` | Lowering price increases total revenue. |
| MR = 0 | Unitary elastic demand, `e = 1` | Total revenue is maximum. |
| MR < 0 | Inelastic demand, `e < 1` | Lowering price reduces total revenue. |

## 9.2 Why This Relation Makes Sense

When demand is elastic, consumers respond strongly to a price fall. Quantity rises enough to increase total revenue. Therefore, MR is positive.

When demand is unit elastic, the gain from selling more exactly balances the loss from charging a lower price. Total revenue is at its maximum. Therefore, MR is zero.

When demand is inelastic, consumers do not respond strongly to a price fall. Quantity rises too little to compensate for the lower price. Total revenue falls. Therefore, MR is negative.

## 9.3 Table-Based Interpretation

Using the chapter's example:

- before the TR maximum, MR is positive and demand is elastic,
- near the TR maximum, demand is unit elastic,
- after the TR maximum, MR is negative and demand is inelastic.

## 9.4 Exam Rule

If a question asks:

**What is the value of MR when the demand curve is elastic?**

Write:

When demand is elastic, price elasticity is greater than one and marginal revenue is positive.

# 10. Short-Run Equilibrium Of A Monopoly Firm

## 10.1 Profit Maximisation

Like other firms, a monopoly firm is assumed to maximise profit.

```text
Profit = TR - TC
```

The monopolist chooses the output level at which profit is maximum.

After choosing output, the price is obtained from the demand curve.

This is different from perfect competition, where the firm takes price as given.

---

# 11. Simple Case: Monopoly With Zero Cost

## 11.1 The Village Well Example

The chapter uses a simple example:

- A village is far from other villages.
- There is only one well.
- Everyone needs water from this well.
- The well is owned by one person.
- The owner can prevent others from drawing water except by buying it.
- Buyers draw the water themselves, so the owner has zero cost.

The well owner is a monopolist with zero production cost.

## 11.2 Profit When Cost Is Zero

If total cost is zero:

```text
Profit = TR - TC
Profit = TR - 0
Profit = TR
```

Therefore, the monopolist maximises profit by maximising total revenue.

In the chapter's numerical example, total revenue is maximum at about 10 units.

At 10 units:

```text
Price = Rs 5
Quantity = 10 units
TR = 5 x 10
TR = Rs 50
Profit = Rs 50
```

This is the monopoly equilibrium in the zero-cost case.

## 11.3 MR Condition In Zero-Cost Case

If cost is zero, marginal cost is also zero.

The general monopoly condition is:

```text
MR = MC
```

Since `MC = 0`, equilibrium occurs where:

```text
MR = 0
```

This is the same point where total revenue is maximum.

---

# 12. Zero-Cost Monopoly Compared With Perfect Competition

## 12.1 Perfect Competition With Many Wells

Now imagine that there are many wells instead of one.

If one well-owner charges Rs 5 per bucket, another owner can attract buyers by charging slightly less.

Then another seller may charge even less.

This process continues because water is available from many sellers and the product is homogeneous.

## 12.2 Price Tends Toward Zero

If production cost is zero, no seller needs a positive price to cover cost.

Under perfect competition, competition among many sellers pushes price down toward zero.

The competitive output is where price, or AR, becomes zero.

In the chapter's demand example:

```text
p = 10 - 0.5q
```

Set price equal to zero:

```text
0 = 10 - 0.5q
0.5q = 10
q = 20
```

So in the zero-cost case:

| Market Structure | Output | Price |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Monopoly | 10 | Rs 5 |
| Perfect competition | 20 | Rs 0 |

## 12.3 Important Conclusion

Compared with perfect competition, monopoly gives:

- lower output,
- higher price,
- higher profit for the firm,
- lower quantity available to consumers.

This is one reason monopoly is often criticised.

# 13. Monopoly Equilibrium With Positive Costs

Most firms have costs, so we now include total cost, average cost, and marginal cost.

## 13.1 Total Curve Method

Using total curves:

```text
Profit = TR - TC
```

The monopolist chooses the output where the vertical distance between TR and TC is maximum, provided TR is above TC.

This output gives maximum profit.

If TR is below TC, the firm is making a loss.

## 13.2 What The Diagram Shows

In a total curve diagram:

- the TR curve shows revenue at each output,
- the TC curve shows cost at each output,
- the vertical distance between them shows profit or loss.

Profit is maximum where the gap between TR and TC is greatest.

If the chapter labels this output as `q0`, then `q0` is the monopoly equilibrium output.

## 13.3 Marginal Curve Method

The same equilibrium can be found using marginal curves.

The monopolist compares:

- marginal revenue, the extra revenue from one more unit,
- marginal cost, the extra cost of one more unit.

If:

```text
MR > MC
```

then producing one more unit adds more to revenue than to cost. Profit rises. The firm should increase output.

If:

```text
MR < MC
```

then producing one more unit adds more to cost than to revenue. Profit falls. The firm should reduce output.

Therefore, profit is maximised where:

```text
MR = MC
```

with MC rising.

## 13.4 Why MC Must Be Rising

The equality `MR = MC` alone is not enough.

For maximum profit, the firm must be at a point where moving away would reduce profit.

The usual NCERT condition is:

```text
MR = MC and MC is rising
```

This gives the stable profit-maximising output.

## 13.5 Price Is Not Found From MC

This is a common mistake.

The equality `MR = MC` gives the output.

The price is then found from the demand curve, or AR curve.

Steps:

1. Find output where `MR = MC`.
2. Go vertically up to the demand curve.
3. Read the price from the demand curve.

The monopolist charges the price consumers are willing to pay for that output.

# 14. Profit Area In Monopoly Diagram

At the equilibrium output `q0`:

- price is read from the demand curve,
- average cost is read from the AC curve,
- total revenue is price multiplied by quantity,
- total cost is average cost multiplied by quantity,
- profit is the difference between total revenue and total cost.

If:

```text
Price > AC
```

then the firm earns profit.

If:

```text
Price = AC
```

then the firm earns normal profit.

If:

```text
Price < AC
```

then the firm incurs loss.

## 14.1 Diagram Reading Method

In the standard monopoly diagram:

1. The MR and MC curves intersect at equilibrium output.
2. From this output, move up to the demand curve to find price.
3. From the same output, move up to the AC curve to find average cost.
4. The rectangle between price and average cost, multiplied by quantity, shows profit.

## 14.2 Profit Formula From Diagram

```text
Profit = (Price - AC) x Quantity
```

This is the same as:

```text
Profit = TR - TC
```

because:

```text
TR = Price x Quantity
TC = AC x Quantity
```

# 15. Monopoly Compared With Perfect Competition

## 15.1 Different Equilibrium Conditions

| Market Structure | Equilibrium Condition | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Perfect competition | `P = MC` | Firm is a price taker and `P = MR = AR`. |
| Monopoly | `MR = MC` | Firm faces downward-sloping demand and `MR < AR`. |

## 15.2 Why Perfect Competition Produces More

In perfect competition, the firm expands output until:

```text
P = MC
```

Since price equals AR, the competitive output is found where the demand or AR curve intersects MC.

In monopoly, output is found where:

```text
MR = MC
```

Since MR lies below AR, the MR curve intersects MC at a lower output than the demand curve would.

Therefore, monopoly output is lower than competitive output.

## 15.3 Why Monopoly Price Is Higher

After choosing the lower monopoly output, the monopolist reads price from the demand curve.

Because the demand curve slopes downward, a lower output corresponds to a higher price.

Therefore:

```text
Monopoly price > Competitive price
```

## 15.4 Final Comparison

| Feature | Monopoly | Perfect Competition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Output | Lower | Higher |
| Price | Higher | Lower |
| Equilibrium condition | `MR = MC` | `P = MC` |
| Demand curve faced by firm | Downward sloping | Horizontal |
| Long-run profit | Can remain positive | Becomes zero under free entry and exit |

# 16. Monopoly In The Long Run

## 16.1 Perfect Competition Long Run

In perfect competition, positive profit attracts new firms.

As new firms enter:

- market supply rises,
- price falls,
- profits of existing firms decline.

If firms are making losses, some firms exit.

As firms exit:

- market supply falls,
- price rises,
- losses reduce.

In the long run, firms earn zero supernormal profit.

## 16.2 Monopoly Long Run

In monopoly, entry is prevented.

Therefore, if a monopolist earns positive profit, new firms cannot freely enter to remove that profit.

So positive monopoly profit can continue in the long run.

This is a key difference between monopoly and perfect competition.

# 17. Critical Views On Monopoly

The chapter presents more than one view about monopoly.

## 17.1 Monopoly As Exploitative

Monopoly is often criticised because it usually charges a higher price and produces a lower output than perfect competition.

Consumers pay more and receive less quantity.

This can be seen as exploitative, especially when the commodity is essential.

## 17.2 Pure Monopoly May Be Rare

One view says that pure monopoly is difficult to find in the real world.

Reason:

- many goods are substitutes in a broad sense,
- consumers have limited income,
- firms producing different goods compete for consumer spending,
- new technologies can create alternative products.

For example, a firm may dominate one technology, but another technology may later become a substitute.

## 17.3 Threat Of Competition

Even if a firm is a monopolist today, it may fear future competition.

New products, new production methods, imports, policy changes, or innovation can reduce monopoly power.

Therefore, a monopolist may not always behave as freely as a simple model suggests.

## 17.4 Possible Benefits Of Monopoly

Another view says that monopoly can sometimes support research and development.

Because monopoly firms may earn large profits, they may have funds to:

- invest in new technology,
- improve quality,
- reduce production costs,
- develop new products.

If such innovation lowers costs or improves goods, consumers may benefit.

## 17.5 Balanced Conclusion

Monopoly can harm consumers through high prices and low output.

But in some cases, monopoly profits may finance innovation.

A good answer should mention both views and then conclude based on the context.

# 18. Monopolistic Competition

## 18.1 Meaning

Monopolistic competition is a market structure with:

1. many firms,
2. free entry and exit,
3. differentiated products,
4. close substitutes between products.

It lies between perfect competition and monopoly.

It has many firms like perfect competition, but each firm has some control over price because its product is differentiated.

## 18.2 Example: Biscuit Brands

Many firms produce biscuits.

But their biscuits differ by:

- brand name,
- packaging,
- taste,
- size,
- quality,
- advertising,
- consumer habit.

A consumer may prefer one brand and may not immediately shift to another.

But if the price difference becomes large, the consumer may switch.

Therefore, products are close substitutes, but not perfect substitutes.

## 18.3 Demand Curve Under Monopolistic Competition

A firm under monopolistic competition faces a downward-sloping demand curve.

Reason:

- if it reduces price, some customers shift toward it,
- if it raises price, some customers leave,
- but not all customers shift immediately because of product preference.

The demand curve is not horizontal as in perfect competition.

It is also not the entire market demand curve as in monopoly.

It is the demand curve for that firm's differentiated product.

## 18.4 AR And MR Under Monopolistic Competition

Since the firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve:

```text
AR curve slopes downward
MR curve lies below AR
```

The firm maximises profit where:

```text
MR = MC
```

This is the same profit-maximising rule as monopoly.

## 18.5 Comparison With Perfect Competition

For a firm in perfect competition:

```text
P = AR = MR
```

For a firm in monopolistic competition:

```text
P = AR > MR
```

Because MR lies below AR, the firm under monopolistic competition produces less than a perfectly competitive firm in a similar cost situation.

Given the lower output, price is higher.

So compared with perfect competition:

| Feature | Monopolistic Competition |
| --- | --- |
| Output | Lower |
| Price | Higher |
| Product | Differentiated |
| Demand curve | Downward sloping |

## 18.6 Short-Run Profit

In the short run, a firm under monopolistic competition may earn supernormal profit.

It may also earn normal profit or face losses.

The exact result depends on the position of demand and cost curves.

## 18.7 Long-Run Adjustment

Because entry and exit are free, long-run profit changes.

If firms earn supernormal profit:

- new firms enter,
- buyers are divided among more products,
- demand for existing firms shifts left,
- price and profit fall.

If firms incur losses:

- some firms exit,
- remaining firms get more customers,
- demand for existing firms shifts right,
- losses reduce.

The process stops when supernormal profit becomes zero.

## 18.8 Long-Run Result

In the long run under monopolistic competition:

```text
Supernormal profit = 0
```

Firms earn normal profit.

However, price remains higher and output lower than under perfect competition because products are differentiated and the firm still faces a downward-sloping demand curve.

# 19. Oligopoly

## 19.1 Meaning

An oligopoly is a market structure in which there are only a few sellers.

A duopoly is a special case with exactly two sellers.

In this chapter, the product is assumed to be homogeneous and without an outside substitute.

## 19.2 Why Oligopoly Is Different

In oligopoly, each firm is large relative to the market.

Therefore, each firm's decision affects:

- total market supply,
- market price,
- rivals' profits,
- rivals' future decisions.

This creates strategic interdependence.

## 19.3 Strategic Interdependence

Strategic interdependence means that each firm must think about how other firms will react before making its own decision.

For example, if one firm reduces price:

- its sales may increase,
- rivals may lose customers,
- rivals may cut their prices too,
- a price war may begin,
- profits of all firms may fall.

Therefore, an oligopoly firm cannot think only about demand and cost. It must also think about rival reactions.

## 19.4 Collusion And Cartel

At one extreme, oligopoly firms may collude.

Collusion means firms cooperate instead of competing.

If firms form a cartel, they may act like a single monopolist.

They may jointly decide:

- total industry output,
- price,
- market shares,
- production limits.

If successful, the cartel outcome may resemble monopoly:

- lower output,
- higher price,
- higher collective profit.

## 19.5 Fierce Competition

At the other extreme, oligopoly firms may compete aggressively.

One firm may reduce price slightly to attract customers.

Rivals may respond by reducing their prices too.

This price-cutting process may continue until price moves close to marginal cost.

That resembles perfect competition.

## 19.6 Realistic Oligopoly Outcome

In real markets, oligopoly usually lies between these extremes.

Pure collusion is often difficult because:

- firms may secretly cheat,
- demand may change,
- cost conditions may differ,
- legal restrictions may exist,
- trust among firms may be weak.

Continuous price cutting is also harmful because it reduces profits.

So firms may avoid extreme competition and settle into an outcome between monopoly and perfect competition.

## 19.7 Price Rigidity

Price rigidity means prices do not change easily.

Oligopoly can lead to rigid prices because firms fear rival reactions.

If one firm raises price:

- rivals may not raise price,
- the firm may lose customers.

If one firm lowers price:

- rivals may also lower price,
- the firm may not gain many customers,
- industry profits may fall.

Because both raising and lowering price can be risky, firms may keep prices stable.

# 21. Worked Numerical Understanding

## 21.1 Calculating TR, AR, And MR

Suppose:

```text
p = 10 - 0.5q
```

For `q = 4`:

```text
p = 10 - 0.5(4)
p = 10 - 2
p = 8
```

```text
TR = P x Q
TR = 8 x 4
TR = 32
```

```text
AR = TR / Q
AR = 32 / 4
AR = 8
```

If TR at `q = 3` is `25.5`, then:

```text
MR at q = 4 = TR at q = 4 - TR at q = 3
MR = 32 - 25.5
MR = 6.5
```

## 21.2 Interpreting MR

At `q = 4`, price is Rs 8.

But marginal revenue is Rs 6.5.

This is because selling the fourth unit requires reducing price on all units, not only the fourth unit.

## 21.3 Finding Zero-Cost Monopoly Equilibrium

If total cost is zero:

```text
Profit = TR
```

So choose the output where TR is maximum.

From the table:

```text
Maximum TR = Rs 50 at q = 10
Price at q = 10 is Rs 5
```

Therefore:

```text
Equilibrium quantity = 10
Equilibrium price = Rs 5
Profit = Rs 50
```

## 21.4 Finding Perfect Competition Output With Zero Cost

In perfect competition:

```text
P = MC
```

If cost is zero:

```text
MC = 0
```

So:

```text
P = 0
```

Using:

```text
p = 10 - 0.5q
```

Set `p = 0`:

```text
0 = 10 - 0.5q
q = 20
```

Therefore, perfect competition gives higher output than monopoly.

Use this structure:

```text
A monopolist is the only seller in the market, so it faces the entire market demand curve.
The market demand curve slopes downward because consumers demand more at lower prices and less at higher prices.
```

## 22.3 Explain Why AR Curve Is Demand Curve

Use this structure:

```text
Average revenue is revenue per unit sold.
AR = TR / Q.
Since TR = P x Q, AR = (P x Q) / Q = P.
Therefore average revenue equals price.
For a monopolist, price is shown by the market demand curve.
Hence the market demand curve is also the AR curve of the monopolist.
```

## 22.4 Explain Why MR Lies Below AR

Use this structure:

```text
Under monopoly, the demand curve slopes downward.
To sell an additional unit, the monopolist must reduce price.
This lower price applies not only to the extra unit but also to earlier units.
Therefore, the addition to total revenue is less than the price.
Hence MR is less than AR and the MR curve lies below the AR curve.
```

## 22.5 Explain Monopoly Equilibrium

Use this structure:

```text
A monopolist maximises profit where MR = MC and MC is rising.
If MR is greater than MC, producing more increases profit.
If MC is greater than MR, producing less increases profit.
Therefore, equilibrium output is determined at MR = MC.
The price is then found from the demand curve corresponding to that output.
```

# 23. Common Mistakes And Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Saying monopoly means a large firm only. | Monopoly means one seller, no close substitute, and restricted entry. A firm can be large without being a monopolist. |
| Saying a monopolist can charge any price. | A monopolist is limited by the demand curve. A higher price means lower quantity demanded. |
| Finding monopoly price from MC. | `MR = MC` gives output. Price is read from the demand curve. |
| Saying AR and MR are the same in monopoly. | In monopoly, AR slopes downward and MR lies below AR. |
| Forgetting that AR equals price. | Since `AR = TR / Q` and `TR = P x Q`, AR equals price. |
| Saying monopoly always has no competition in any sense. | Pure monopoly has no direct seller of the same commodity, but substitutes and new technology may create indirect competition. |
| Saying monopolistic competition is the same as monopoly. | Monopolistic competition has many firms and free entry; monopoly has one seller and restricted entry. |
| Saying oligopoly has many sellers. | Oligopoly has a few sellers. |
| Saying collusion and competition are the same. | Collusion means firms cooperate; competition means they act against each other to gain customers. |
| Saying positive profit disappears in monopoly long run. | Monopoly profit can continue if entry remains blocked. |

# 24. Diagram Guide

## 24.1 Monopoly Demand, AR, And MR

In a monopoly diagram:

- vertical axis shows price, revenue, or cost,
- horizontal axis shows output,
- demand curve slopes downward,
- AR curve is the same as demand curve,
- MR curve lies below AR.

The MR curve lies below AR because the monopolist must reduce price to sell extra output.

## 24.2 TR Curve

For a straight-line downward-sloping demand curve:

- TR first rises,
- TR reaches a maximum,
- TR then falls.

The TR curve is an inverted vertical parabola.

## 24.3 Monopoly Equilibrium Diagram

To draw monopoly equilibrium:

1. Draw downward-sloping AR or demand curve.
2. Draw MR below AR.
3. Draw MC curve.
4. Find the intersection of MR and MC.
5. Drop down to the quantity axis to get equilibrium quantity.
6. Move up from that quantity to the AR curve to get price.
7. Use AC to show profit if required.

## 24.4 Profit Rectangle

If price is above average cost:

```text
Profit per unit = Price - AC
Total profit = (Price - AC) x Quantity
```

This is shown by a rectangle between price and AC at equilibrium output.

# 25. Exercise Support

## 25.1 Shape Of Demand Curve When TR Is A Positively Sloped Straight Line Through Origin

If TR is a positively sloped straight line through the origin, total revenue rises in the same proportion as quantity.

This means price is constant.

Therefore, the demand curve is horizontal.

This is the perfect competition type of demand curve for an individual firm.

## 25.2 Shape Of Demand Curve When TR Is Horizontal

If TR is horizontal, total revenue remains constant as quantity changes.

This means price must fall in the same proportion as quantity rises.

The demand curve is a rectangular hyperbola with unit elasticity at all points.

## 25.3 MR When Demand Is Elastic

When demand is elastic:

```text
Elasticity > 1
MR > 0
```

So marginal revenue is positive.

## 25.4 MR Curve When TR Is Positively Sloped Straight Line

If TR is a positively sloped straight line, its slope is constant and positive.

Since MR is the slope of TR, MR is a horizontal line above the output axis.

## 25.5 MR Curve When TR Is Horizontal

If TR is horizontal, its slope is zero.

Since MR is the slope of TR, MR is zero at all output levels.

The MR curve coincides with the output axis.

## 25.6 Loss In The Short Run

A monopolist may continue producing in the short run even if it suffers a loss, provided price covers average variable cost.

If the firm can cover variable cost and part of fixed cost, producing may reduce losses compared with shutting down.

If price is below average variable cost, the firm should shut down in the short run.

This principle follows from producer theory and applies to monopoly as well.

## 25.7 Why Monopolistic Competition Has Zero Profit In Long Run

Because entry and exit are free.

If existing firms earn supernormal profit, new firms enter and take away some demand.

If existing firms face losses, some firms exit and remaining firms gain demand.

This process continues until supernormal profit is zero.

## 25.8 Three Ways Oligopoly Firms May Behave

Oligopoly firms may:

1. collude and act like a monopoly,
2. compete fiercely by cutting prices,
3. avoid frequent price changes and keep prices rigid because of rival reactions.

# 26. Strong Short Answers

## Question: What is monopoly?

Monopoly is a market structure in which there is only one seller of a commodity. The commodity has no close substitute and entry of new firms is prevented. Because the monopolist is the only seller, it faces the market demand curve and can influence price by changing the quantity supplied.

## Question: Why is the demand curve faced by a monopolist downward sloping?

A monopolist is the only seller, so it faces the entire market demand curve. Since market demand is downward sloping, consumers buy more only at lower prices. Therefore, if the monopolist wants to sell more output, it must reduce price. Hence its demand curve slopes downward.

## Question: Why is the demand curve the AR curve for a monopolist?

Average revenue is total revenue divided by quantity. Since total revenue equals price multiplied by quantity, average revenue equals price. The demand curve shows the price consumers are willing to pay for each quantity. Therefore, the demand curve is also the AR curve of the monopolist.

## Question: Why is MR below AR in monopoly?

To sell an extra unit, a monopolist must lower price. The lower price applies to all units sold, not only the extra unit. Therefore, the addition to total revenue is less than the price. Since price equals AR, MR is less than AR.

## Question: What is the condition for monopoly equilibrium?

The condition for monopoly equilibrium is `MR = MC`, with MC rising. This gives the profit-maximising output. The corresponding price is found from the demand curve at that output.

## Question: How does monopoly differ from perfect competition?

In perfect competition, the firm is a price taker and equilibrium occurs where price equals marginal cost. In monopoly, the firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve and equilibrium occurs where marginal revenue equals marginal cost. Monopoly usually leads to lower output and higher price than perfect competition.

## Question: What is monopolistic competition?

Monopolistic competition is a market structure with many firms, free entry and exit, and differentiated products. The products are close substitutes but not identical. Each firm has some price power and faces a downward-sloping demand curve. In the long run, free entry and exit remove supernormal profit.

## Question: What is oligopoly?

Oligopoly is a market structure with a few large firms. Each firm is large enough to affect market price, output, and rivals' profits. Therefore, firms are strategically interdependent and may collude, compete, or keep prices rigid.

# 27. Long Answer: Monopoly Equilibrium

A monopoly firm maximises profit like any other firm. Profit is the difference between total revenue and total cost.

```text
Profit = TR - TC
```

Using total curves, the monopolist chooses the output where the vertical distance between TR and TC is maximum and TR is above TC.

Using marginal curves, the firm compares marginal revenue with marginal cost. If MR is greater than MC, producing an extra unit increases profit because the addition to revenue is greater than the addition to cost. Therefore, the firm expands output.

If MC is greater than MR, producing the extra unit reduces profit because the addition to cost is greater than the addition to revenue. Therefore, the firm reduces output.

The firm reaches equilibrium where:

```text
MR = MC
```

and MC is rising.

This equality determines the equilibrium output. The monopolist then uses the demand curve to find the price that consumers are willing to pay for that output.

Thus, monopoly equilibrium has two steps:

1. output is determined by `MR = MC`,
2. price is determined from the demand curve.

This differs from perfect competition, where price is already given and the firm produces where `P = MC`.

---

# 28. Long Answer: Monopoly Versus Perfect Competition

Monopoly and perfect competition differ mainly because of market power.

In perfect competition, there are many firms selling a homogeneous product. Each firm is too small to influence market price. Therefore, the firm is a price taker and faces a horizontal demand curve. For such a firm:

```text
P = AR = MR
```

The equilibrium condition is:

```text
P = MC
```

In monopoly, there is only one seller. The monopolist faces the market demand curve, which slopes downward. If it wants to sell more output, it must reduce price. Since this price reduction applies to earlier units also, marginal revenue lies below average revenue.

Therefore, monopoly equilibrium is:

```text
MR = MC
```

Since MR lies below AR, the monopoly output is lower than the competitive output. The lower output is sold at a higher price because the demand curve slopes downward.

Thus, compared with perfect competition, monopoly generally produces less, charges a higher price, and may earn positive profit even in the long run if entry remains blocked.

---

# 29. Long Answer: Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition is a market structure in which many firms sell differentiated products. Entry and exit are free, but products are not homogeneous.

Examples include branded biscuits, toothpaste, restaurants, clothing brands, and many consumer goods. These products are close substitutes, but consumers may prefer one brand because of taste, quality, packaging, advertising, or habit.

Because products are differentiated, each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. If it reduces price, some consumers shift toward it. If it raises price, some consumers leave, but not all immediately. Therefore, the firm has limited price power.

The firm's average revenue curve slopes downward, and its marginal revenue curve lies below average revenue. It maximises profit where:

```text
MR = MC
```

In the short run, firms may earn supernormal profit. But because entry is free, new firms enter the market. This shifts demand away from existing firms and reduces their profit.

If firms make losses, some firms exit. Demand for remaining firms rises and losses reduce.

In the long run, entry and exit continue until supernormal profit becomes zero. Thus, firms earn normal profit in the long run, but output is lower and price is higher than in perfect competition.

---

# 30. Long Answer: Oligopoly Behaviour

Oligopoly is a market structure with a small number of large firms. Each firm is large relative to the market and can affect total supply, market price, and the profits of rival firms.

The key feature of oligopoly is strategic interdependence. Each firm must consider how rivals will react before changing price or output.

At one extreme, oligopoly firms may collude. If they cooperate and form a cartel, they may act like a single monopolist. In this case, they restrict output and charge a higher price to maximise collective profit.

At the other extreme, firms may compete fiercely. One firm may reduce price to attract customers, and rivals may respond with their own price cuts. If this continues, price may fall toward marginal cost, resembling perfect competition.

In practice, oligopoly often lies between these two extremes. Firms may avoid continuous price cutting because it damages profits. They may also avoid raising prices because rivals may not follow. This can produce price rigidity.

Thus, oligopoly behaviour may take three forms:

1. collusion,
2. price competition,
3. price rigidity due to rival reactions.

# 33. Final Chapter Summary

Non-competitive markets arise when the assumptions of perfect competition fail.

A monopoly has one seller, no close substitute, and blocked entry. The monopolist faces the market demand curve, which is also its AR curve. Since the demand curve slopes downward, the monopolist must reduce price to sell more output. This makes MR lie below AR.

Total revenue first rises, reaches a maximum, and then falls when demand is a straight-line downward-sloping curve. MR is positive when TR rises, zero when TR is maximum, and negative when TR falls. MR is also connected to elasticity: positive MR means elastic demand, zero MR means unit elastic demand, and negative MR means inelastic demand.

A monopoly firm maximises profit where `MR = MC` and MC is rising. This gives the equilibrium output. The price is found from the demand curve at that output. Compared with perfect competition, monopoly generally produces less and charges a higher price.

In the long run, monopoly profits may continue because entry is prevented. However, monopoly is debated: it may exploit consumers through high prices, but monopoly profits may also support research and development.

Monopolistic competition has many firms, free entry and exit, and differentiated products. Each firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve and maximises profit where `MR = MC`. In the long run, free entry and exit remove supernormal profit.

Oligopoly has a few large firms. Each firm's decision affects rival firms, so firms are strategically interdependent. They may collude like a monopoly, compete fiercely like perfect competition, or keep prices rigid because of expected rival reactions.
