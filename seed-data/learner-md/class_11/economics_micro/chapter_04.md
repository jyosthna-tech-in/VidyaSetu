## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how a firm decides its output under perfect competition.

The previous chapter studied production and cost. This chapter uses those cost ideas to answer a new question:

**How much should a firm produce when it wants to maximise profit?**

The chapter assumes that the firm is a profit maximiser.

Profit is:

```text
Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost
```

Under perfect competition, a firm is a price taker. It cannot control the market price. It chooses only how much to produce.

The central idea is:

**A perfectly competitive firm chooses output where price equals marginal cost, provided production is worthwhile.**

# 1. Perfect Competition

## 1.1 Why Market Environment Matters

A firm's output decision depends on the market in which it operates.

This chapter studies a perfectly competitive market.

The firm is assumed to be one small seller among many sellers.

It cannot influence price.

It chooses output.

## 1.2 Defining Features

A perfectly competitive market has four defining features:

1. Large number of buyers and sellers.
2. Homogeneous product.
3. Free entry and exit.
4. Perfect information.

## 1.3 Large Number of Buyers and Sellers

Each buyer and seller is very small compared with the whole market.

No single buyer or seller can influence market price by individual action.

Therefore, each firm treats market price as given.

## 1.4 Homogeneous Product

The product of one firm is identical to the product of another firm.

Buyers do not care which firm produces the product if the product and price are the same.

If one firm charges a higher price, buyers can shift to other firms.

## 1.5 Free Entry and Exit

Firms can enter the market when they find it profitable.

Firms can leave the market when continuing is not worthwhile.

This feature is important for long-run adjustment.

## 1.6 Perfect Information

Buyers and sellers know:

- market price
- product quality
- relevant market conditions

Because buyers know the market price, a firm cannot secretly charge more for an identical product.

## 1.7 Price-Taking Behaviour

The main result of perfect competition is price-taking behaviour.

A price-taking firm believes:

- if it charges more than the market price, it sells nothing
- if it charges the market price, it can sell as much as it wants
- it has no reason to charge less than market price

Therefore, the firm sells at the market price.

## 1.8 Buyer as Price Taker

A buyer is also a price taker.

If the buyer offers less than the market price, firms may not sell to her.

If she is willing to pay the market price, she can buy the quantity she wants.

## 1.9 Why Price Taking Is Reasonable Here

Price taking is reasonable because:

- many firms exist
- each firm is small
- products are identical
- buyers know the market price
- buyers can switch firms easily

# 2. Revenue Under Perfect Competition

## 2.1 Total Revenue

Total revenue is the money a firm earns by selling output.

If:

`p = market price`

`q = quantity sold`

Then:

```text
TR = p x q
```

## 2.2 Total Revenue Example

Suppose market price of a box of candles is Rs 10.

| Boxes Sold | Total Revenue |
| ---: | ---: |
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 20 |
| 3 | 30 |
| 4 | 40 |
| 5 | 50 |

The total revenue curve:

- starts from origin
- is upward sloping
- is a straight line
- has slope equal to price

## 2.3 Why TR Curve Passes Through Origin

If output sold is zero:

```text
TR = p x 0 = 0
```

So total revenue is zero.

Therefore, the TR curve passes through origin.

## 2.4 Why TR Curve Is a Straight Line

Under perfect competition, price is constant for the firm.

If each additional unit is sold at the same price, total revenue rises by the same amount for every extra unit.

Therefore, TR is a straight line.

## 2.5 Average Revenue

Average revenue is total revenue per unit of output.

Formula:

```text
AR = TR / q
```

Since:

```text
TR = p x q
```

Then:

```text
AR = (p x q) / q = p
```

So:

```text
AR = p
```

For a price-taking firm, average revenue equals market price.

## 2.6 Marginal Revenue

Marginal revenue is the extra revenue earned from selling one more unit.

Formula:

```text
MR = Delta TR / Delta q
```

If output changes from `q1` to `q2`:

```text
MR = (p q2 - p q1) / (q2 - q1)
MR = p(q2 - q1) / (q2 - q1)
MR = p
```

So:

```text
MR = p
```

For a price-taking firm:

```text
MR = AR = p
```

## 2.7 Price Line

The price line is a horizontal line at the market price.

It represents:

- market price
- average revenue curve
- marginal revenue curve
- demand curve facing the firm

The firm can sell any quantity at price `p`.

Therefore, the demand curve facing a perfectly competitive firm is perfectly elastic.

## 2.8 Revenue Summary

| Concept | Formula | Perfect Competition Result |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Total revenue | `TR = p x q` | Straight line through origin |
| Average revenue | `AR = TR/q` | `AR = p` |
| Marginal revenue | `MR = Delta TR/Delta q` | `MR = p` |
| Price line | horizontal at `p` | `AR = MR = demand curve` |

# 3. Profit Maximisation

## 3.1 Profit

Profit is total revenue minus total cost.

Formula:

```text
pi = TR - TC
```

The firm wants to choose the output level where profit is maximum.

## 3.2 Basic Logic

If producing one more unit adds more to revenue than to cost, profit rises.

If producing one more unit adds more to cost than to revenue, profit falls.

Therefore:

- if `MR > MC`, increase output
- if `MR < MC`, decrease output
- profit maximum requires `MR = MC`

Under perfect competition:

```text
MR = p
```

So the condition becomes:

```text
p = MC
```

## 3.3 Three Conditions for Positive Profit-Maximising Output

For positive output to be profit maximising:

1. Price must equal marginal cost.
2. Marginal cost must be non-decreasing at that output.
3. Production must be worthwhile:
   - in short run: `p >= AVC`
   - in long run: `p >= AC` or `p >= LRAC`

## 3.4 Condition 1: Price Equals MC

For a competitive firm:

```text
p = MC
```

Reason:

If `p > MC`, the extra unit adds more revenue than cost. Profit rises by producing more.

If `p < MC`, the extra unit adds more cost than revenue. Profit rises by producing less.

Only where `p = MC` can profit be maximised.

## 3.5 Condition 2: MC Must Be Non-Decreasing

The equality `p = MC` may occur more than once.

The profit-maximising point must be on the non-decreasing, or rising, part of MC.

Why?

If MC is falling at the point where `p = MC`, then nearby lower output may give higher profit.

The firm should not choose an output where MC is falling.

So the firm chooses the `p = MC` point on the rising part of the marginal cost curve.

## 3.6 Condition 3 in Short Run: Price Covers AVC

In the short run, fixed cost must be paid even if output is zero.

The firm compares:

- loss if it produces
- loss if it shuts down

If the firm shuts down:

```text
TR = 0
TVC = 0
Loss = TFC
Profit = -TFC
```

If it produces when `p < AVC`:

Revenue does not even cover variable cost.

Then loss is greater than fixed cost.

Therefore, the firm should shut down.

Short-run production condition:

```text
p >= AVC
```

More precisely, positive output is possible only if price is at least the minimum AVC.

## 3.7 Condition 3 in Long Run: Price Covers AC

In the long run, all costs are variable.

If price is less than average cost:

```text
TR < TC
```

The firm makes a loss.

In the long run, it can exit and earn zero profit instead of loss.

Therefore, the firm produces in the long run only if:

```text
p >= LRAC
```

Positive output requires price at least equal to minimum LRAC.

## 3.8 Profit Rectangle

At output `q0`, suppose:

- price = `p`
- average cost = `AC`
- output = `q0`

Total revenue:

```text
TR = p x q0
```

Total cost:

```text
TC = AC x q0
```

Profit:

```text
Profit = (p - AC) x q0
```

If `p > AC`, the firm earns profit.

If `p = AC`, the firm breaks even.

If `p < AC`, the firm incurs loss.

# 4. Supply Curve of a Firm

## 4.1 Meaning of Firm Supply

A firm's supply is the quantity it chooses to sell at a given price, given:

- technology
- input prices
- cost conditions

A supply schedule is a table showing quantity supplied at different prices.

A supply curve is the graphical representation of the supply schedule.

## 4.2 Short-Run Supply Curve

In the short run, the firm supplies positive output only if price is at least minimum AVC.

If price is below minimum AVC:

```text
q = 0
```

If price is at or above minimum AVC:

The firm chooses output where:

```text
p = SMC
```

on the rising part of SMC.

Therefore:

**The short-run supply curve is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above minimum AVC, together with zero output for prices below minimum AVC.**

## 4.3 Long-Run Supply Curve

In the long run, the firm supplies positive output only if price is at least minimum LRAC.

If price is below minimum LRAC:

```text
q = 0
```

If price is at or above minimum LRAC:

The firm chooses output where:

```text
p = LRMC
```

on the rising part of LRMC.

Therefore:

**The long-run supply curve is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above minimum LRAC, together with zero output for prices below minimum LRAC.**

## 4.4 Shutdown Point

The short-run shutdown point is the minimum point of AVC.

At this point:

```text
p = minimum AVC
```

Below this price, the firm stops production in the short run.

In the long run, the shutdown/exit point is minimum LRAC.

## 4.5 Break-Even Point

The break-even point is the point where the firm earns normal profit.

At break-even:

```text
Price = Average Cost
```

In the long run:

```text
p = minimum LRAC
```

In the short run, break-even occurs where the supply curve cuts the SAC curve.

## 4.6 Normal Profit

Normal profit is the minimum profit needed to keep the firm in the current business.

It is treated as part of total cost.

Reason:

The entrepreneur could use resources elsewhere. The return sacrificed is an opportunity cost.

## 4.7 Supernormal Profit

Supernormal profit is profit above normal profit.

If:

```text
p > AC
```

the firm earns supernormal profit.

If:

```text
p = AC
```

the firm earns normal profit only.

# 5. Determinants of a Firm's Supply Curve

A firm's supply curve is based on its marginal cost curve.

Therefore, anything that shifts marginal cost can shift supply.

Main determinants:

1. Technological progress.
2. Input prices.
3. Unit tax.

## 5.1 Technological Progress

Technological progress means the firm can produce more output from the same inputs or the same output from fewer inputs.

Effect:

- marginal cost falls
- supply curve shifts right
- at the same price, firm supplies more output

Answer sentence:

Technological progress shifts the supply curve to the right because it lowers marginal cost at each output level.

## 5.2 Increase in Input Prices

If input prices rise, cost of production rises.

Example:

Wage rate increases.

Effect:

- average cost rises
- marginal cost rises
- supply curve shifts left
- at the same price, firm supplies less output

If input prices fall, supply shifts right.

## 5.3 Unit Tax

A unit tax is a tax per unit of output sold.

Example:

If unit tax is Rs 2 and firm sells 10 units:

```text
Tax payment = 2 x 10 = Rs 20
```

Effect of unit tax:

- cost per unit rises by tax amount
- MC rises
- AC rises
- supply curve shifts left/upward
- at the same market price, firm supplies less

## 5.4 Opportunity Cost Connection

Normal profit is connected to opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost is the gain from the next best alternative forgone.

Example:

If Rs 1,000 could earn 10 percent interest in a bank but is invested in a family business, the forgone bank interest is the opportunity cost of using that money in the business.

This opportunity cost must be covered for the entrepreneur to continue.

# 6. Market Supply Curve

## 6.1 Meaning

The market supply curve shows total quantity supplied by all firms at each market price.

It is obtained by adding the quantities supplied by individual firms at each price.

## 6.2 Horizontal Summation

At a given price:

```text
Market supply = supply of firm 1 + supply of firm 2 + ... + supply of firm n
```

This is called horizontal summation.

Important:

Add quantities, not prices.

## 6.3 Two-Firm Example

Suppose:

Firm 1 supplies only when price is at least `p1`.

Firm 2 supplies only when price is at least `p2`.

Assume:

`p2 > p1`

Then:

| Price Range | Market Supply |
| --- | --- |
| `p < p1` | both supply zero |
| `p1 <= p < p2` | only firm 1 supplies |
| `p >= p2` | both firms supply; add their quantities |

## 6.4 Numerical Example

Firm 1:

```text
S1(p) = 0, if p < 10
S1(p) = p - 10, if p >= 10
```

Firm 2:

```text
S2(p) = 0, if p < 15
S2(p) = p - 15, if p >= 15
```

Market supply:

For `p < 10`:

```text
Sm(p) = 0
```

For `10 <= p < 15`:

```text
Sm(p) = p - 10
```

For `p >= 15`:

```text
Sm(p) = (p - 10) + (p - 15)
Sm(p) = 2p - 25
```

## 6.5 Effect of Number of Firms

If number of firms increases:

- market supply shifts right
- more output is supplied at each price

If number of firms decreases:

- market supply shifts left
- less output is supplied at each price

# 7. Price Elasticity of Supply

## 7.1 Meaning

Price elasticity of supply measures how responsive quantity supplied is to a change in price.

Formula:

```text
eS = percentage change in quantity supplied / percentage change in price
```

Using changes:

```text
eS = (Delta Q / Q) / (Delta P / P)
```

or:

```text
eS = (Delta Q / Delta P) x (P / Q)
```

## 7.2 Sign of Supply Elasticity

Supply curve is usually upward sloping.

So when price rises, quantity supplied rises.

Therefore, elasticity of supply is usually positive.

If supply is vertical, quantity supplied does not respond to price and elasticity is zero.

## 7.3 Cricket Ball Example

Old price:

`P1 = Rs 10`

Old quantity:

`Q1 = 200`

New price:

`P2 = Rs 30`

New quantity:

`Q2 = 1000`

Change in quantity:

```text
Delta Q = 1000 - 200 = 800
```

Percentage change in quantity:

```text
(800/200) x 100 = 400%
```

Change in price:

```text
Delta P = 30 - 10 = 20
```

Percentage change in price:

```text
(20/10) x 100 = 200%
```

Elasticity:

```text
eS = 400/200 = 2
```

Supply is elastic.

## 7.4 Geometric Method for Straight-Line Supply Curves

For a straight-line supply curve, elasticity depends on where the extended supply curve cuts the quantity axis.

| Supply Curve Position | Elasticity |
| --- | --- |
| Cuts price axis, extension cuts negative quantity axis | `eS > 1` |
| Passes through origin | `eS = 1` |
| Cuts positive quantity axis | `eS < 1` |
| Vertical supply curve | `eS = 0` |

Simple memory:

- through origin: unit elastic
- cuts quantity axis to the right of origin: inelastic
- cuts price axis/negative quantity extension: elastic

# 8. Calculation Frames

## 8.1 Total Revenue, AR, and MR

If market price is Rs 10:

| Quantity | TR | AR | MR |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 0 | 0 | - | - |
| 1 | 10 | 10 | 10 |
| 2 | 20 | 10 | 10 |
| 3 | 30 | 10 | 10 |
| 4 | 40 | 10 | 10 |

Rules:

```text
TR = p x q
AR = TR/q
MR = change in TR/change in q
```

For a perfectly competitive firm:

```text
AR = MR = p
```

## 8.2 Profit Schedule

Given TR and TC:

```text
Profit = TR - TC
```

To find profit-maximising output:

1. Calculate profit for each output.
2. Choose output with highest profit.
3. If using marginal method, check `P = MC`, rising MC, and shutdown condition.

## 8.3 Market Supply Schedule

Given supply schedules of firms:

At each price:

```text
Market supply = SS1 + SS2 + SS3 + ...
```

Example:

If at price Rs 5:

Firm 1 supplies 3 units.

Firm 2 supplies 1 unit.

Market supply:

```text
3 + 1 = 4 units
```

## 8.4 Identical Firms

If there are 3 identical firms and one firm supplies `q` units at a price:

```text
Market supply = 3q
```

At every price, multiply the individual supply by the number of firms.

## 8.5 Elasticity of Supply from Revenue Data

If a firm's revenue and price are given, first find quantity:

```text
Quantity = Revenue / Price
```

Example:

Revenue Rs 50 at price Rs 10:

```text
Q1 = 50/10 = 5
```

Revenue Rs 150 at price Rs 15:

```text
Q2 = 150/15 = 10
```

Then calculate elasticity:

```text
eS = (Delta Q / Q1) / (Delta P / P1)
```

Here:

```text
Delta Q = 10 - 5 = 5
Delta P = 15 - 10 = 5
Percentage change Q = 5/5 = 1 = 100%
Percentage change P = 5/10 = 0.5 = 50%
eS = 100/50 = 2
```

## 8.6 Finding New Quantity from Elasticity

Formula:

```text
eS = percentage change Q / percentage change P
```

So:

```text
percentage change Q = eS x percentage change P
```

Example:

If price rises from Rs 10 to Rs 30:

```text
percentage change P = (20/10) x 100 = 200%
```

If `eS = 1.25`:

```text
percentage change Q = 1.25 x 200% = 250%
```

If old quantity is 4:

```text
increase = 250% of 4 = 10
new quantity = 4 + 10 = 14
```

# 9. Answer-Writing Frames

## 9.1 Characteristics of Perfect Competition

Write:

A perfectly competitive market has a large number of buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, free entry and exit of firms, and perfect information. Because each firm is very small relative to the market and sells an identical product, it cannot influence market price. Therefore, a perfectly competitive firm is a price taker.

## 9.2 Price Line

Write:

The price line is the horizontal line showing the market price faced by a perfectly competitive firm. Since the firm can sell any quantity at the given market price, the price line is also the demand curve facing the firm. It is also the firm's AR and MR curve because `AR = MR = p`.

## 9.3 Why TR Curve Is an Upward-Sloping Straight Line

Write:

For a price-taking firm, market price is constant. Total revenue is `TR = p x q`. When output is zero, total revenue is zero, so the TR curve passes through the origin. Since price is constant, total revenue increases by the same amount for every additional unit sold, making the TR curve an upward-sloping straight line with slope equal to price.

## 9.4 Profit-Maximising Conditions

Write:

For a competitive firm producing positive output, profit maximisation requires three conditions. First, price must equal marginal cost because `MR = p` and profit maximisation requires `MR = MC`. Second, marginal cost must be non-decreasing at that output, so the firm chooses the rising part of MC. Third, production must cover the relevant average cost: in the short run, `p >= AVC`; in the long run, `p >= LRAC`.

## 9.5 Short-Run Supply Curve

Write:

The short-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above the minimum point of AVC, together with zero output for all prices below minimum AVC. If price is below minimum AVC, the firm cannot cover variable cost and shuts down in the short run.

## 9.6 Long-Run Supply Curve

Write:

The long-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above the minimum point of LRAC, together with zero output for all prices below minimum LRAC. In the long run all costs are variable, so a firm exits if price is below average cost.

## 9.7 Technological Progress and Supply

Write:

Technological progress allows the firm to produce the same output with fewer inputs or more output with the same inputs. This lowers marginal cost. Since a firm's supply curve is based on its marginal cost curve, technological progress shifts the supply curve to the right.

## 9.8 Input Price Increase and Supply

Write:

An increase in input prices raises the cost of production. It usually raises marginal cost at each output level. Since the supply curve is based on marginal cost, the firm's supply curve shifts leftward. At the same market price, the firm supplies fewer units.

## 9.9 Unit Tax and Supply

Write:

A unit tax is a tax per unit of output sold. It raises marginal cost and average cost by the tax amount per unit. Therefore, the firm's supply curve shifts leftward/upward, and at any given market price the firm supplies less output.

## 9.10 Market Supply

Write:

The market supply curve is obtained by horizontally summing the supply curves of individual firms. At each price, the quantities supplied by all firms are added. If the number of firms increases, market supply shifts right; if the number of firms decreases, market supply shifts left.

## 9.11 Price Elasticity of Supply

Write:

Price elasticity of supply measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price. It is calculated as percentage change in quantity supplied divided by percentage change in price. If supply is very responsive, elasticity is high. If quantity supplied does not respond to price, elasticity is zero.

# 10. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Saying a competitive firm can choose any price | It is a price taker; it chooses output, not market price. |
| Confusing market demand with firm demand | Firm demand under perfect competition is horizontal at market price. |
| Forgetting `MR = AR = P` | For a price-taking firm, all three are equal. |
| Saying `P = MC` alone is enough | Must also be on rising MC and satisfy shutdown/exit condition. |
| Confusing shutdown and break-even | Shutdown is minimum AVC in short run; break-even is where price equals average cost. |
| Saying firm exits in short run whenever it makes loss | It may continue if price covers AVC. |
| Saying firm produces in long run with loss | In long run it exits if price is below LRAC. |
| Adding prices to get market supply | Add quantities supplied at each price. |
| Saying tax shifts supply right | A unit tax raises cost and shifts supply left. |
| Treating supply elasticity as negative | Supply elasticity is usually positive because price and quantity supplied move together. |

## 11.5 Supply Shifts

| Change | Supply Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Technological progress | shifts right |
| Input price increase | shifts left |
| Input price decrease | shifts right |
| Unit tax | shifts left/upward |
| Number of firms increases | market supply shifts right |
| Number of firms decreases | market supply shifts left |

# 12. Final One-Page Summary

Perfect competition is a market structure with many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, free entry and exit, and perfect information. These features make each firm a price taker. A competitive firm accepts the market price and chooses only how much output to produce.

Total revenue is `TR = p x q`. Since price is constant for a competitive firm, total revenue is an upward-sloping straight line through the origin. Average revenue and marginal revenue both equal price: `AR = MR = p`. The price line is horizontal and is also the demand curve facing the firm.

Profit is `pi = TR - TC`. A profit-maximising competitive firm producing positive output must satisfy three conditions. First, price equals marginal cost. Second, marginal cost must be non-decreasing at that output. Third, production must be worthwhile: in the short run price must cover average variable cost, and in the long run price must cover average cost.

The short-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above the minimum AVC, along with zero output below that price. The long-run supply curve is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above the minimum LRAC, along with zero output below that price. The short-run shutdown point is minimum AVC. The break-even point is where price equals average cost and the firm earns normal profit.

Technological progress lowers cost and shifts supply right. Higher input prices and unit taxes raise cost and shift supply left. Market supply is obtained by horizontally summing individual firm supply curves. Price elasticity of supply measures responsiveness of quantity supplied to price and is calculated as percentage change in quantity supplied divided by percentage change in price.
