## Chapter Purpose

This chapter introduces the basic way economists think.

Economics begins with a simple fact:

**Human wants are many, but resources are limited.**

Because resources are scarce, individuals and societies cannot have everything they want at the same time. They must choose. Every choice involves giving up something else. This gives rise to the idea of **opportunity cost**.

The chapter explains:

1. What an economy is.
2. Why scarcity creates the problem of choice.
3. What central problems every economy faces.
4. What a production possibility frontier means.
5. How economic activities may be organised by planning or by markets.
6. What positive and normative economics mean.
7. How microeconomics differs from macroeconomics.

The central idea is:

**Economics studies how individuals and societies use scarce resources to produce, exchange, consume, and distribute goods and services.**

# 1. A Simple Economy

## 1.1 What a Simple Economy Shows

The chapter begins by asking us to imagine a society.

People in a society need many goods and services:

- food
- clothing
- shelter
- roads
- railways
- postal services
- education
- medical care
- transport

No person has all these things alone.

Each person or decision-making unit has only some resources.

Examples:

| Decision-Making Unit | Resources It May Have | What It Can Produce or Provide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Family farm | land, grains, tools, bullocks, family labour | crops such as corn |
| Weaver | yarn, cotton, weaving instruments, skill | cloth |
| Teacher | knowledge and teaching skill | education service |
| Labourer | labour power | work/service for wage |

## 1.2 Why Exchange Is Needed

Each person can produce only some goods or services.

But each person needs many goods and services.

Therefore, people exchange.

Example:

A family farm may produce corn.

It can:

1. consume part of the corn
2. exchange the remaining corn for clothes, housing, tools, or services

A teacher may provide teaching services and receive money. The teacher then uses money to buy food, clothes, and other goods.

## 1.3 Decision-Making Units

The word "individual" in economics does not always mean one person.

It may mean any unit that makes decisions.

Examples:

- a person
- a household
- a firm
- a school
- a farm
- an organisation

Economics studies how these decision-making units use resources and make choices.

## 1.4 Goods and Services

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Goods | Physical things that satisfy wants | food, clothes, houses, machines |
| Services | Activities that satisfy wants | teaching, treatment by doctors, transport, banking |

Important difference:

Goods are tangible.

Services are intangible.

## 1.5 Resources

Resources are goods and services used to produce other goods and services.

Examples:

- land
- labour
- tools
- machines
- buildings
- raw materials
- skills
- technology

The key feature of resources:

**They are limited.**

# 2. Scarcity and Choice

## 2.1 Meaning of Scarcity

Scarcity means resources are limited in comparison with wants.

It does not mean that there is nothing.

It means:

**There is not enough to satisfy every want fully at the same time.**

Example:

A family farm has limited land, labour, seeds, and tools.

It cannot produce unlimited corn.

It also cannot buy unlimited clothes, housing, or services in exchange for corn.

## 2.2 Why Scarcity Forces Choice

Because resources are scarce, people must choose.

If a family wants a bigger house, it may have to give up buying more land.

If it wants better education for children, it may have to give up some luxuries.

This is not only true for families.

It is true for:

- individuals
- firms
- villages
- states
- nations
- the whole world

## 2.3 The Basic Economic Logic

The chain of reasoning is:

```text
Unlimited wants + limited resources -> scarcity
scarcity -> choice
choice -> sacrifice
sacrifice -> opportunity cost
```

This chain is one of the foundations of economics.

## 2.4 Compatibility Between Production and Consumption

Society must make sure that what is produced is connected with what people want.

Example:

If farming units produce more corn than people want to consume, some resources used in corn production could have been used to produce another good or service in higher demand.

If people want more corn than society produces, resources may need to be shifted from other goods to corn production.

So society must coordinate:

- what people want
- what producers produce
- how resources are allocated
- how final goods are distributed

## 2.5 Two Basic Economic Problems in a Society

Every society faces two broad problems:

| Problem | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Allocation of resources | How scarce resources are assigned to different goods and services. |
| Distribution of final goods and services | How produced goods and services are divided among people. |

Allocation answers:

Which resources should be used where?

Distribution answers:

Who gets the final output?

# 3. Central Problems of an Economy

Every economy faces three central problems:

1. What is produced and in what quantities?
2. How are these goods produced?
3. For whom are these goods produced?

These problems arise because resources are scarce and have alternative uses.

---

## 3.1 What Is Produced and in What Quantities?

Society must decide what goods and services to produce and how much of each.

Examples of choices:

| Choice | Economic Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| More food or more luxury goods? | Basic needs vs comfort/luxury. |
| More agriculture or more industry? | Allocation between sectors. |
| More education/health or more military services? | Social welfare vs defence. |
| More basic education or more higher education? | Type of public investment. |
| More consumer goods or more machines? | Present consumption vs future production. |

This problem exists because resources used for one purpose cannot be used for another at the same time.

## 3.2 How Are Goods Produced?

Society must decide which resources and technologies to use.

Examples:

- more labour or more machines
- traditional technology or modern technology
- hand production or automated production
- energy-intensive method or labour-intensive method

This is about the method of production.

Important:

Different methods have different costs and effects on employment, output, environment, and income distribution.

## 3.3 For Whom Are Goods Produced?

Society must decide how produced goods and services are distributed.

Questions:

- Who gets more?
- Who gets less?
- Should everyone receive minimum consumption?
- Should basic health care be available to everyone?
- Should elementary education be free for all?

This problem concerns equity and distribution.

## 3.4 Summary of Central Problems

| Central Problem | Main Question | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What to produce? | Which goods and how much? | food or luxury cars |
| How to produce? | Which technique and resources? | labour or machines |
| For whom to produce? | Who receives output? | rich only or wider population |

# 4. Production Possibility Set

## 4.1 Meaning

The production possibility set is the collection of all combinations of goods and services that an economy can produce with:

- given resources
- given technology
- available knowledge

It shows what is feasible.

## 4.2 Why It Matters

The production possibility set helps us understand:

- scarcity
- choice
- opportunity cost
- efficient use of resources
- trade-off between goods

## 4.3 Corn and Cotton Example

The chapter gives an example of an economy that can produce corn or cotton.

Production possibilities:

| Possibility | Corn | Cotton |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| A | 0 | 10 |
| B | 1 | 9 |
| C | 2 | 7 |
| D | 3 | 4 |
| E | 4 | 0 |

If all resources are used for cotton, the economy can produce 10 units of cotton and 0 corn.

If all resources are used for corn, the economy can produce 4 units of corn and 0 cotton.

Other combinations are also possible.

## 4.4 Reading the Table

Moving from A to E:

| Movement | Corn Gained | Cotton Given Up |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| A to B | +1 | -1 |
| B to C | +1 | -2 |
| C to D | +1 | -3 |
| D to E | +1 | -4 |

The table shows that as the economy produces more corn, it must give up more cotton.

This is opportunity cost.

## 4.5 Why Opportunity Cost May Increase

In the example:

- first extra unit of corn costs 1 unit of cotton
- next extra unit costs 2 units of cotton
- next extra unit costs 3 units of cotton
- next extra unit costs 4 units of cotton

This can happen because resources are not equally efficient in all uses.

Some resources are better suited to cotton.

When these are shifted to corn, more cotton must be sacrificed for each additional unit of corn.

# 5. Production Possibility Frontier

## 5.1 Meaning

The production possibility frontier, or PPF, is the curve showing the maximum possible combinations of two goods that can be produced when resources are fully and efficiently used.

In the chapter example, the PPF shows combinations of corn and cotton.

## 5.2 Points on the PPF

A point on the PPF means:

- resources are fully used
- production is efficient
- to produce more of one good, some amount of another good must be sacrificed

Example:

Point C:

`2 corn and 7 cotton`

If the economy wants more than 2 corn, it must move toward D or E and give up cotton.

## 5.3 Points Inside the PPF

A point below or inside the PPF means:

- resources are underemployed
- or resources are used wastefully
- the economy can produce more of at least one good without reducing the other

Example:

If the economy is capable of producing 2 corn and 7 cotton but produces only 1 corn and 5 cotton, it is inside the PPF.

## 5.4 Points Outside the PPF

A point outside the PPF is not attainable with current resources and technology.

It may become possible only if:

- resources increase
- technology improves
- productivity rises

## 5.5 Shape of the PPF

The PPF is usually downward sloping.

Reason:

With limited resources, producing more of one good requires producing less of another.

The PPF is often concave to the origin.

Reason:

Opportunity cost may increase as more resources are shifted from one use to another.

## 5.6 PPF and Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost of more corn is the cotton sacrificed.

From the table:

| Movement | Opportunity Cost of 1 More Corn |
| --- | --- |
| A to B | 1 cotton |
| B to C | 2 cotton |
| C to D | 3 cotton |
| D to E | 4 cotton |

## 5.7 Exam Answer Frame: Production Possibility Frontier

Write:

A production possibility frontier is the curve that shows all combinations of two goods that an economy can produce when its resources are fully and efficiently used, given the available technology. Points on the frontier show efficient production. Points inside the frontier show underutilisation or waste of resources. Points outside the frontier are unattainable with present resources and technology.

# 6. Opportunity Cost

## 6.1 Meaning

Opportunity cost is the cost of choosing one option in terms of the next best alternative given up.

It is also called economic cost.

## 6.2 Simple Examples

| Choice | Opportunity Cost |
| --- | --- |
| Studying for two hours | leisure or work you gave up |
| Producing more corn | cotton production sacrificed |
| Building more hospitals | other projects not built with the same resources |
| Buying a machine | alternative use of that money |

## 6.3 Opportunity Cost in PPF

If the economy moves from B to C:

- corn rises from 1 to 2
- cotton falls from 9 to 7

Opportunity cost of 1 extra corn:

`2 cotton`

## 6.4 Why Opportunity Cost Is Central in Economics

Opportunity cost is central because every decision uses scarce resources.

Whenever resources are used for one purpose, the society gives up another possible use.

This is true for:

- households
- producers
- governments
- nations

## 6.5 Common Mistake

Mistake:

Thinking opportunity cost means money paid.

Correction:

Opportunity cost means the value of the next best alternative sacrificed. It may involve money, time, resources, or any other forgone option.

# 7. Organisation of Economic Activities

The basic economic problems may be solved in different ways.

Two broad systems are:

1. Centrally planned economy.
2. Market economy.

In reality, most economies are mixed economies.

---

## 7.1 Centrally Planned Economy

In a centrally planned economy, the government or central authority makes major economic decisions.

It decides:

- what to produce
- how much to produce
- how to produce
- how to distribute output
- which sectors should receive resources

## 7.2 Why a Government May Plan

A central authority may intervene when:

- essential services are not produced enough by private individuals
- education or health services need expansion
- some people receive too little to survive
- equitable distribution is needed
- long-term social goals are important

Example:

If education is under-produced by individuals, the government may produce it directly or encourage others to produce it.

## 7.3 Strengths of Central Planning

| Strength | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Social priorities | Government can focus on health, education, and basic needs. |
| Equity | It can try to reduce extreme inequality. |
| Long-term goals | It can direct resources toward national development. |
| Public goods | It can provide goods that markets may under-provide. |

## 7.4 Limitations of Central Planning

| Limitation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Information problem | Central authority may not know all local needs. |
| Inefficiency | Resources may be misallocated. |
| Low incentives | Producers may lack motivation to innovate. |
| Slow response | Decisions may take time. |

---

## 7.5 Market Economy

In a market economy, economic activities are organised through markets.

A market is not only a physical place.

In economics, a market means:

**an arrangement that allows buyers and sellers to exchange goods and services freely.**

Examples:

- village market
- city supermarket
- phone-based transaction
- internet purchase
- commodity exchange

## 7.6 Role of Prices in a Market Economy

Prices coordinate decisions.

If demand for a good rises, its price may rise.

This price rise signals producers:

People want more of this good.

Producers may increase production.

Thus, prices carry information.

They help solve the problem of:

- what to produce
- how much to produce
- where resources should move

## 7.7 Price Signal Example

Suppose demand for milk increases.

Possible sequence:

```text
Demand for milk rises
-> price of milk rises
-> dairy producers earn more
-> more resources move into milk production
-> milk supply increases
```

This is how market coordination works.

## 7.8 Strengths of Market Economy

| Strength | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Decentralised decisions | Many individuals make decisions based on their own information. |
| Price signals | Prices guide producers and consumers. |
| Incentives | Profit encourages production and innovation. |
| Flexibility | Markets may respond quickly to changing demand. |

## 7.9 Limitations of Market Economy

| Limitation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Inequality | Markets may distribute goods according to purchasing power, not need. |
| Public goods | Some goods may not be provided adequately by private markets. |
| Externalities | Pollution and social costs may be ignored. |
| Basic services | Health and education may remain unaffordable for some. |

---

## 7.10 Mixed Economy

In reality, most economies are mixed economies.

A mixed economy uses:

- market mechanism for many goods and services
- government planning or intervention for important sectors and social goals

India is an example where the government played a major planning role after Independence, while markets also functioned. Over time, the role of markets has increased in many sectors.

## 7.11 Comparison: Planning and Market

| Basis | Centrally Planned Economy | Market Economy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main decision-maker | Government/central authority | Buyers and sellers |
| Coordination tool | Plan/directive | Price mechanism |
| Production decision | Based on social plan | Based on demand, cost, and profit |
| Distribution | Can be shaped by policy | Based largely on income and purchasing power |
| Strength | Can focus on equity and public welfare | Can use price signals and incentives |
| Limitation | May suffer from information and efficiency problems | May create inequality and under-provide public goods |

# 8. Positive and Normative Economics

## 8.1 Why the Distinction Matters

Economics does two kinds of work:

1. It explains how economic mechanisms function.
2. It evaluates whether outcomes are desirable.

The first is positive analysis.

The second is normative analysis.

## 8.2 Positive Economic Analysis

Positive economics studies how things work.

It asks:

- What is happening?
- Why is it happening?
- What will happen if a variable changes?
- How does a mechanism function?

Examples:

| Positive Statement | Why Positive? |
| --- | --- |
| If price rises, quantity demanded usually falls. | It describes a relationship. |
| A rise in income may increase demand for normal goods. | It explains likely behaviour. |
| A tax on petrol may reduce petrol consumption. | It predicts an effect. |
| In a market, price signals coordinate buyers and sellers. | It explains a mechanism. |

Positive statements can be tested using logic, evidence, and data.

## 8.3 Normative Economic Analysis

Normative economics evaluates what should be done.

It asks:

- Is this outcome desirable?
- Should the government intervene?
- Is the distribution fair?
- Which policy is better for society?

Examples:

| Normative Statement | Why Normative? |
| --- | --- |
| The government should provide free basic education. | It recommends a policy. |
| Income inequality should be reduced. | It contains a value judgement. |
| Health services ought to be available to everyone. | It expresses what should happen. |
| More resources should be spent on public transport. | It evaluates priorities. |

Normative statements involve values and judgement.

## 8.4 Positive vs Normative

| Basis | Positive Economics | Normative Economics |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Focus | What is, how it works | What ought to be |
| Nature | Descriptive/explanatory | Evaluative/prescriptive |
| Testability | Can often be tested | Depends on values and goals |
| Example question | What happens if price rises? | Should prices be controlled? |

## 8.5 Important Note

The distinction is useful but not always perfectly sharp.

Positive and normative issues are closely related.

To decide whether a policy is desirable, we must first understand how it works.

To study how a policy works, we often care because of some social goal.

# 9. Microeconomics and Macroeconomics

## 9.1 Microeconomics

Microeconomics studies individual economic agents and specific markets.

It studies:

- individual consumers
- individual producers
- firms
- markets for particular goods
- price determination in a single market
- quantity determination in a single market

Examples of microeconomic questions:

- How does a consumer choose between two goods?
- How does a firm decide how much to produce?
- How is the price of wheat determined?
- Why does demand fall when price rises?
- How do costs affect supply?

## 9.2 Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole.

It focuses on aggregate measures such as:

- total output
- total employment
- aggregate price level
- national income
- inflation
- unemployment
- economic growth

Examples of macroeconomic questions:

- What is the level of total output in the economy?
- How is total output determined?
- Why does unemployment occur?
- Why do prices rise?
- How does the economy grow over time?
- Are resources fully employed?

## 9.3 Comparison

| Basis | Microeconomics | Macroeconomics |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unit of study | Individual agent or market | Economy as a whole |
| Main variables | Price of one good, quantity of one good, consumer choice, firm output | National income, total output, employment, inflation |
| Example | Price of rice | General price level |
| Example | Output of one firm | Total output of the economy |
| Example | Demand for one commodity | Aggregate demand |

# 10. How to Write Answers from This Chapter

## 10.1 Central Problems of an Economy

Strong answer structure:

1. Start with scarcity.
2. State that scarce resources have alternative uses.
3. List the three problems.
4. Explain each briefly with examples.
5. Conclude that every economy must allocate resources and distribute output.

Model answer:

Every economy faces central problems because resources are scarce and have alternative uses. The first problem is what to produce and in what quantities, such as whether to produce more food or more luxury goods. The second problem is how to produce, meaning whether to use labour-intensive or machine-intensive techniques. The third problem is for whom to produce, meaning how the produced goods and services are distributed among people. These problems are central because every society must allocate limited resources and distribute the final output.

## 10.2 Production Possibility Frontier

Strong answer structure:

1. Define PPF.
2. Mention given resources and technology.
3. Explain points on, inside, and outside.
4. Link to scarcity and opportunity cost.

Model answer:

A production possibility frontier shows the maximum possible combinations of two goods that an economy can produce with given resources and technology when resources are fully and efficiently used. Points on the PPF are efficient. Points inside the PPF show underutilisation or waste of resources. Points outside the PPF are unattainable with current resources and technology. The PPF is downward sloping because producing more of one good requires sacrificing some amount of another good, showing opportunity cost.

## 10.3 Positive and Normative Economics

Strong answer structure:

1. Define positive economics.
2. Define normative economics.
3. Give one example of each.
4. State the difference clearly.

Model answer:

Positive economics studies how economic mechanisms function. It deals with statements that can be tested, such as "a rise in price usually reduces quantity demanded." Normative economics evaluates whether outcomes are desirable and suggests what ought to be done, such as "the government should provide free basic education." Positive economics is descriptive, while normative economics is evaluative.

## 10.4 Microeconomics and Macroeconomics

Strong answer structure:

1. Define microeconomics.
2. Define macroeconomics.
3. Compare the unit of study.
4. Give examples.

Model answer:

Microeconomics studies the behaviour of individual economic agents such as consumers, firms, and particular markets. It explains how the price and quantity of a specific commodity are determined. Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole using aggregate variables such as total output, employment, national income, and general price level. The price of wheat is a microeconomic issue, while inflation is a macroeconomic issue.

# 11. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| Saying scarcity means poverty | Scarcity means wants exceed resources; even rich societies face scarcity. |
| Treating goods and services as the same | Goods are tangible; services are intangible. |
| Thinking opportunity cost means only money cost | Opportunity cost means next best alternative forgone. |
| Saying points inside PPF are impossible | Points inside PPF are possible but inefficient. |
| Saying points outside PPF are inefficient | Points outside PPF are unattainable with current resources and technology. |
| Confusing market with physical marketplace | In economics, market means exchange arrangement, physical or non-physical. |
| Calling every government action macroeconomics | Micro/macro depends on level of analysis, not who acts. |
| Treating positive and normative as completely separate | They are related, but the distinction helps clarify analysis and value judgement. |

# 14. Final One-Page Summary

Economics begins with scarcity. People have many wants, but resources such as land, labour, tools, machines, and skills are limited. Since resources have alternative uses, individuals and societies must choose. Every choice involves opportunity cost, which is the next best alternative forgone.

Every economy faces three central problems: what to produce and in what quantities, how to produce, and for whom to produce. These problems are about allocation of scarce resources and distribution of final goods and services.

The production possibility set shows all combinations of goods and services that an economy can produce with given resources and technology. The production possibility frontier shows the maximum possible combinations when resources are fully and efficiently used. Points on the frontier are efficient, points inside show underutilisation, and points outside are unattainable.

Economic activities may be organised through central planning, market mechanism, or a mixture of both. In a centrally planned economy, the government makes major decisions. In a market economy, buyers and sellers interact freely and prices coordinate decisions. Most real economies are mixed economies.

Positive economics explains how economic mechanisms work. Normative economics evaluates whether outcomes are desirable and what should be done. Microeconomics studies individual consumers, producers, and markets. Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole through aggregate variables such as output, employment, and price level.
