## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how strongly quantity demanded or quantity supplied responds when price changes.

The law of demand tells us the direction of change:

- price rises, quantity demanded usually falls,
- price falls, quantity demanded usually rises.

But the law of demand does not tell us the size of the change.

Price elasticity answers that question.

It asks:

> When price changes by a certain percentage, by what percentage does quantity change?

This chapter studies:

1. price elasticity of demand,
2. elasticity along a linear demand curve,
3. constant elasticity demand curves,
4. factors affecting demand elasticity,
5. relation between elasticity and expenditure,
6. price elasticity of supply.

# 1. Meaning Of Price Elasticity

## 1.1 Why Elasticity Is Needed

The law of demand says that price and quantity demanded usually move in opposite directions.

But this is incomplete.

It does not tell us whether demand changes a lot or a little.

For example:

- If the price of salt rises, demand may fall only slightly.
- If the price of a luxury phone rises, demand may fall strongly.

Both follow the law of demand, but the degree of response is different.

Elasticity measures this degree of response.

## 1.2 Basic Meaning

Price elasticity measures how responsive quantity is to a change in price.

It uses percentage changes because percentage changes allow comparison across goods measured in different units.

For example, we can compare elasticity of bananas, petrol, medicine, and clothes because elasticity is unit-free.

## 1.3 Elasticity Is A Pure Number

Elasticity has no unit.

Reason:

```text
Elasticity = percentage change / percentage change
```

Both numerator and denominator are percentages, so units cancel out.

That is why elasticity can compare responsiveness across goods.

# 2. Price Elasticity Of Demand

## 2.1 Definition

Price elasticity of demand is the percentage change in demand for a good divided by the percentage change in its price.

Formula:

```text
eD = percentage change in quantity demanded / percentage change in price
```

More formally:

```text
eD = (delta Q / Q) / (delta P / P)
```

or:

```text
eD = (delta Q / delta P) x (P / Q)
```

Here:

- `Q` is original quantity demanded,
- `P` is original price,
- `delta Q` is change in quantity demanded,
- `delta P` is change in price.

## 2.2 Why Demand Elasticity Is Usually Negative

For most goods, price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions.

If price rises, quantity demanded falls.

If price falls, quantity demanded rises.

Therefore, `delta Q` and `delta P` usually have opposite signs.

So price elasticity of demand is usually negative.

## 2.3 Why We Often Use Absolute Value

For simplicity, elasticity of demand is often discussed using absolute value.

Example:

If:

```text
eD = -0.5
```

we say:

```text
|eD| = 0.5
```

and call demand inelastic.

The minus sign shows direction. The absolute value shows responsiveness.

# 3. Worked Example: Bananas

## 3.1 Given Data

A consumer buys 15 bananas when price is Rs 5 per banana.

When price rises to Rs 7 per banana, demand falls to 12 bananas.

| Item | Old Value | New Value |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Price `P` | Rs 5 | Rs 7 |
| Quantity demanded `Q` | 15 bananas | 12 bananas |

## 3.2 Percentage Change In Quantity Demanded

```text
Percentage change in Q = [(new Q - old Q) / old Q] x 100
```

Substitute values:

```text
Percentage change in Q = [(12 - 15) / 15] x 100
Percentage change in Q = (-3 / 15) x 100
Percentage change in Q = -20 percent
```

Quantity demanded falls by 20 percent.

## 3.3 Percentage Change In Price

```text
Percentage change in P = [(new P - old P) / old P] x 100
```

Substitute values:

```text
Percentage change in P = [(7 - 5) / 5] x 100
Percentage change in P = (2 / 5) x 100
Percentage change in P = 40 percent
```

Price rises by 40 percent.

## 3.4 Elasticity Calculation

```text
eD = percentage change in Q / percentage change in P
eD = -20 / 40
eD = -0.5
```

Using absolute value:

```text
|eD| = 0.5
```

## 3.5 Interpretation

Demand for bananas in this example is inelastic because:

```text
|eD| < 1
```

The percentage fall in quantity demanded is smaller than the percentage rise in price.

# 4. Types Of Price Elasticity Of Demand

## 4.1 Elastic Demand

Demand is elastic when:

```text
|eD| > 1
```

This means quantity demanded changes by a larger percentage than price.

Example:

If price rises by 10 percent and quantity demanded falls by 20 percent, demand is elastic.

## 4.2 Inelastic Demand

Demand is inelastic when:

```text
|eD| < 1
```

This means quantity demanded changes by a smaller percentage than price.

Example:

If price rises by 10 percent and quantity demanded falls by 5 percent, demand is inelastic.

## 4.3 Unit Elastic Demand

Demand is unit elastic when:

```text
|eD| = 1
```

This means quantity demanded changes by the same percentage as price.

Example:

If price rises by 10 percent and quantity demanded falls by 10 percent, demand is unit elastic.

## 4.4 Summary Table

| Elasticity Value | Name | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `|eD| > 1` | Elastic demand | Quantity response is stronger than price change. |
| `|eD| < 1` | Inelastic demand | Quantity response is weaker than price change. |
| `|eD| = 1` | Unit elastic demand | Quantity response equals price change. |
| `|eD| = 0` | Perfectly inelastic demand | Quantity does not change at all. |
| `|eD| = infinity` | Perfectly elastic demand | Any price change can reduce demand to zero. |

# 5. Elasticity Along A Linear Demand Curve

## 5.1 Linear Demand Function

Consider a linear demand curve:

```text
q = a - bp
```

Here:

- `q` is quantity demanded,
- `p` is price,
- `a` is quantity demanded when price is zero,
- `b` shows how much quantity falls when price rises by one unit.

For this demand curve:

```text
delta q / delta p = -b
```

## 5.2 Elasticity Formula For Linear Demand

Start with:

```text
eD = (delta q / delta p) x (p / q)
```

Since:

```text
delta q / delta p = -b
```

we get:

```text
eD = -b x (p / q)
```

Using `q = a - bp`:

```text
eD = -bp / (a - bp)
```

This shows that elasticity changes as `p` and `q` change.

## 5.3 Important Result

Elasticity is not constant along a straight-line downward-sloping demand curve.

It varies from point to point.

## 5.4 Elasticity At Important Points

For the linear demand curve:

```text
q = a - bp
```

| Point On Demand Curve | Elasticity |
| --- | --- |
| Where price is zero | 0 |
| Midpoint of the demand curve | 1 |
| Where quantity is zero | Infinity |
| Above midpoint, toward price axis | Greater than 1 |
| Below midpoint, toward quantity axis | Less than 1 |

## 5.5 Why Elasticity Is High Near The Price Axis

Near the price axis:

- price is high,
- quantity is low,
- the ratio `p / q` is large.

Since elasticity includes `p / q`, elasticity is high.

## 5.6 Why Elasticity Is Low Near The Quantity Axis

Near the quantity axis:

- price is low,
- quantity is high,
- the ratio `p / q` is small.

So elasticity is low.

# 6. Geometric Measure Of Elasticity On A Straight-Line Demand Curve

## 6.1 Rule

At any point on a straight-line demand curve:

```text
Elasticity = lower segment of demand curve / upper segment of demand curve
```

This is a geometric shortcut.

## 6.2 Meaning Of Segments

Imagine a straight-line demand curve cutting:

- the price axis at the top,
- the quantity axis at the bottom.

Choose a point on the demand curve.

The part below the point is the lower segment.

The part above the point is the upper segment.

## 6.3 Results

| Location On Straight-Line Demand Curve | Segment Relation | Elasticity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| At price-axis intercept | Lower segment is entire line, upper segment is zero | Infinity |
| Above midpoint | Lower segment > upper segment | Greater than 1 |
| At midpoint | Lower segment = upper segment | 1 |
| Below midpoint | Lower segment < upper segment | Less than 1 |
| At quantity-axis intercept | Lower segment is zero | 0 |

## 6.4 Exam Use

If asked to measure elasticity geometrically, write:

```text
Elasticity at a point on a straight-line demand curve is measured as the ratio of the lower segment to the upper segment of the demand curve at that point.
```

Then mention:

- midpoint gives unit elasticity,
- above midpoint gives elastic demand,
- below midpoint gives inelastic demand.

# 7. Constant Elasticity Demand Curves

Usually, elasticity changes along a demand curve.

But some demand curves have constant elasticity at all points.

The chapter discusses three important cases.

## 7.1 Perfectly Inelastic Demand

A vertical demand curve is perfectly inelastic.

Quantity demanded remains the same whatever the price.

Elasticity is:

```text
|eD| = 0
```

Example idea:

If demand does not change at all when price changes, demand is perfectly inelastic.

## 7.2 Perfectly Elastic Demand

A horizontal demand curve is perfectly elastic.

At the given price, any quantity may be demanded.

At any other price, demand may fall to zero.

Elasticity is:

```text
|eD| = infinity
```

## 7.3 Unit Elastic Demand

A rectangular hyperbola demand curve has unit elasticity at every point.

This means:

```text
|eD| = 1
```

at all points.

## 7.4 Rectangular Hyperbola

A rectangular hyperbola has the form:

```text
x y = constant
```

For demand:

```text
P x Q = constant
```

This means total expenditure remains the same at every point.

If price rises, quantity falls in exactly the same percentage.

If price falls, quantity rises in exactly the same percentage.

So elasticity is unit elastic everywhere.

# 8. Factors Determining Price Elasticity Of Demand

## 8.1 Nature Of The Good

Necessities usually have inelastic demand.

Reason:

Consumers cannot easily reduce their consumption of essential goods when price rises.

Examples:

- basic food,
- medicines,
- essential fuel,
- basic transport.

Luxuries usually have elastic demand.

Reason:

Consumers can postpone, reduce, or avoid luxury purchases when price rises.

Examples:

- expensive watches,
- foreign holidays,
- luxury cars,
- premium electronics.

## 8.2 Availability Of Close Substitutes

Demand is more elastic when close substitutes are easily available.

If the price of one good rises, consumers can shift to another similar good.

Example:

If the price of one variety of pulses rises, consumers may shift to another variety.

Demand is less elastic when close substitutes are not easily available.

## 8.3 Broad Category Versus Specific Item

Demand for food as a broad category may be inelastic because food is necessary.

But demand for a specific food item may be more elastic because substitutes exist within food.

Example:

Demand for "food" may be inelastic.

Demand for one particular type of pulse may be elastic.

## 8.4 Summary

| Factor | Elastic Demand More Likely When | Inelastic Demand More Likely When |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nature of good | Good is a luxury | Good is a necessity |
| Substitutes | Many close substitutes exist | Few close substitutes exist |
| Category | Narrowly defined good | Broadly defined essential category |

# 9. Elasticity And Expenditure

## 9.1 Meaning Of Expenditure

Total expenditure on a good is:

```text
Expenditure = Price x Quantity demanded
```

From the seller's view, this is total revenue.

So:

```text
Consumer expenditure = seller's total revenue
```

## 9.2 Why Elasticity Matters For Expenditure

When price changes, quantity demanded moves in the opposite direction.

The final effect on expenditure depends on which percentage change is stronger.

If price rises:

- price effect tends to increase expenditure,
- quantity effect tends to reduce expenditure.

If price falls:

- price effect tends to reduce expenditure,
- quantity effect tends to increase expenditure.

Elasticity tells us which effect dominates.

---

# 10. Price Rise And Expenditure

## 10.1 Inelastic Demand

If price rises and demand is inelastic:

- price rises by a larger percentage,
- quantity demanded falls by a smaller percentage,
- expenditure rises.

Example:

```text
Price rises by 10 percent
Quantity falls by 8 percent
Expenditure rises
```

## 10.2 Elastic Demand

If price rises and demand is elastic:

- price rises by a smaller percentage,
- quantity demanded falls by a larger percentage,
- expenditure falls.

Example:

```text
Price rises by 10 percent
Quantity falls by 12 percent
Expenditure falls
```

## 10.3 Unit Elastic Demand

If price rises and demand is unit elastic:

- price rises by the same percentage as quantity falls,
- expenditure remains unchanged.

Example:

```text
Price rises by 10 percent
Quantity falls by 10 percent
Expenditure unchanged
```

---

# 11. Price Fall And Expenditure

## 11.1 Elastic Demand

If price falls and demand is elastic:

- price falls by a smaller percentage,
- quantity demanded rises by a larger percentage,
- expenditure rises.

Example:

```text
Price falls by 10 percent
Quantity rises by 15 percent
Expenditure rises
```

## 11.2 Inelastic Demand

If price falls and demand is inelastic:

- price falls by a larger percentage,
- quantity demanded rises by a smaller percentage,
- expenditure falls.

Example:

```text
Price falls by 10 percent
Quantity rises by 7 percent
Expenditure falls
```

## 11.3 Unit Elastic Demand

If price falls and demand is unit elastic:

- price falls by the same percentage as quantity rises,
- expenditure remains unchanged.

Example:

```text
Price falls by 10 percent
Quantity rises by 10 percent
Expenditure unchanged
```

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Price rises | Inelastic | Quantity falls less than price rises | Expenditure rises |
| Price rises | Elastic | Quantity falls more than price rises | Expenditure falls |

| Price falls | Elastic | Quantity rises more than price falls | Expenditure rises |

Use this rule:

| Demand Type | Price And Expenditure Move |
| --- | --- |
| Elastic demand | Price and expenditure move in opposite directions. |
| Inelastic demand | Price and expenditure move in same direction. |
| Unit elastic demand | Expenditure does not change. |

# 13. Mathematical Link Between Elasticity And Expenditure

This section is for deeper understanding.

Suppose original price and quantity are:

```text
P and Q
```

New price and quantity are:

```text
P + delta P and Q + delta Q
```

Original expenditure:

```text
E = P x Q
```

New expenditure:

```text
E2 = (P + delta P)(Q + delta Q)
```

Change in expenditure:

```text
delta E = (P + delta P)(Q + delta Q) - PQ
```

Expanding:

```text
delta E = Q delta P + P delta Q + delta P delta Q
```

For very small changes, the term `delta P delta Q` is very small and can be ignored.

So approximately:

```text
delta E = Q delta P + P delta Q
```

This is why the direction of expenditure depends on whether the price effect or quantity effect is stronger.

## 13.1 Signed Elasticity Interpretation

Demand elasticity is negative because demand and price move oppositely.

Using signed elasticity:

- if `eD < -1`, demand is elastic and expenditure moves opposite to price,
- if `eD > -1`, demand is inelastic and expenditure moves with price,
- if `eD = -1`, expenditure does not change.

Using absolute value:

- if `|eD| > 1`, expenditure moves opposite to price,
- if `|eD| < 1`, expenditure moves with price,
- if `|eD| = 1`, expenditure remains unchanged.

# 14. Price Elasticity Of Supply

## 14.1 Meaning

Price elasticity of supply measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.

Formula:

```text
eS = percentage change in quantity supplied / percentage change in price
```

More formally:

```text
eS = (delta Q / Q) / (delta P / P)
```

or:

```text
eS = (delta Q / delta P) x (P / Q)
```

Here:

- `Q` is original quantity supplied,
- `P` is original price,
- `delta Q` is change in quantity supplied,
- `delta P` is change in price.

## 14.2 Sign Of Supply Elasticity

For a normal upward-sloping supply curve, price and quantity supplied move in the same direction.

If price rises, quantity supplied rises.

If price falls, quantity supplied falls.

Therefore, price elasticity of supply is usually positive.

## 14.3 Unit-Free Measure

Like demand elasticity, supply elasticity is independent of units.

It compares percentage change with percentage change.

So it can compare responsiveness across goods even if they are measured in different units.

# 15. Worked Example: Cricket Balls

## 15.1 Given Data

Suppose the market for cricket balls is perfectly competitive.

At price Rs 10, firms produce and sell 200 cricket balls.

At price Rs 30, firms produce and sell 1,000 cricket balls.

| Item | Old Value | New Value |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Price `P` | Rs 10 | Rs 30 |
| Quantity supplied `Q` | 200 | 1,000 |

## 15.2 Percentage Change In Quantity Supplied

```text
Percentage change in Q = [(new Q - old Q) / old Q] x 100
```

Substitute values:

```text
Percentage change in Q = [(1000 - 200) / 200] x 100
Percentage change in Q = (800 / 200) x 100
Percentage change in Q = 400 percent
```

## 15.3 Percentage Change In Price

```text
Percentage change in P = [(new P - old P) / old P] x 100
```

Substitute values:

```text
Percentage change in P = [(30 - 10) / 10] x 100
Percentage change in P = (20 / 10) x 100
Percentage change in P = 200 percent
```

## 15.4 Supply Elasticity Calculation

```text
eS = percentage change in Q / percentage change in P
eS = 400 / 200
eS = 2
```

## 15.5 Interpretation

Supply is elastic because:

```text
eS > 1
```

Quantity supplied responds more than proportionately to price.

# 16. Types Of Supply Elasticity

## 16.1 Elastic Supply

Supply is elastic when:

```text
eS > 1
```

Quantity supplied changes by a larger percentage than price.

## 16.2 Inelastic Supply

Supply is inelastic when:

```text
eS < 1
```

Quantity supplied changes by a smaller percentage than price.

## 16.3 Unit Elastic Supply

Supply is unit elastic when:

```text
eS = 1
```

Quantity supplied changes by the same percentage as price.

## 16.4 Perfectly Inelastic Supply

A vertical supply curve has:

```text
eS = 0
```

Quantity supplied does not respond to price at all.

## 16.5 Positively Sloped Supply Curve

For an upward-sloping supply curve, supply elasticity is positive because price and quantity supplied move in the same direction.

# 17. Geometric Method For Supply Elasticity

The chapter explains supply elasticity using straight-line supply curves.

The key ratio is:

```text
eS = Mq0 / Oq0
```

where:

- `O` is the origin,
- `q0` is the quantity at the point being studied,
- `M` is where the extended supply curve cuts the quantity axis,
- `Mq0` and `Oq0` are line segments along the quantity axis.

## 17.1 Supply Curve Cutting Price Axis

If a straight-line supply curve cuts the price axis and, when extended, cuts the quantity axis on the negative side:

```text
Mq0 > Oq0
```

Therefore:

```text
eS > 1
```

Supply is elastic.

## 17.2 Supply Curve Passing Through Origin

If a straight-line supply curve passes through the origin:

```text
Mq0 = Oq0
```

Therefore:

```text
eS = 1
```

Supply is unit elastic at all points.

## 17.3 Supply Curve Cutting Quantity Axis

If a straight-line supply curve cuts the quantity axis on the positive side:

```text
Mq0 < Oq0
```

Therefore:

```text
eS < 1
```

Supply is inelastic.

## 17.4 Supply Geometry Summary

| Straight-Line Supply Curve | Elasticity |
| --- | --- |
| Cuts price axis, extension meets negative quantity axis | `eS > 1` |
| Passes through origin | `eS = 1` |
| Cuts quantity axis on positive side | `eS < 1` |
| Vertical supply curve | `eS = 0` |

---

# 18. Demand Elasticity Versus Supply Elasticity

| Basis | Price Elasticity Of Demand | Price Elasticity Of Supply |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Measures | Responsiveness of quantity demanded | Responsiveness of quantity supplied |
| Formula | `% change in demand / % change in price` | `% change in supply / % change in price` |
| Usual sign | Negative | Positive |
| Absolute value used? | Yes, often | Usually not necessary |
| Linked with | Consumer response | Producer response |
| Expenditure relation | Very important | Not the main focus here |
| Perfectly inelastic curve | Vertical | Vertical |
| Perfectly elastic curve | Horizontal | Horizontal case possible, but not central here |

# 19. Numerical Practice Models

## 19.1 Demand Elasticity Model

Question:

At price Rs 4, demand is 25 units. Price rises to Rs 5 and demand falls to 20 units. Calculate price elasticity of demand.

Solution:

```text
Old price = 4
New price = 5
Old quantity = 25
New quantity = 20
```

Percentage change in quantity:

```text
[(20 - 25) / 25] x 100 = -20 percent
```

Percentage change in price:

```text
[(5 - 4) / 4] x 100 = 25 percent
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = -20 / 25
eD = -0.8
```

Absolute value:

```text
|eD| = 0.8
```

Interpretation:

Demand is inelastic.

## 19.2 Demand Function Model

Question:

Find elasticity for:

```text
D(p) = 10 - 3p
```

at:

```text
p = 5 / 3
```

Solution:

First find quantity:

```text
q = 10 - 3(5/3)
q = 10 - 5
q = 5
```

For this demand curve:

```text
delta q / delta p = -3
```

Elasticity:

```text
eD = (delta q / delta p) x (p / q)
eD = -3 x [(5/3) / 5]
eD = -3 x (1/3)
eD = -1
```

Absolute value:

```text
|eD| = 1
```

Interpretation:

Demand is unit elastic at this price.

## 19.3 Demand Change From Known Elasticity

Question:

Price elasticity of demand is `-0.2`. If price increases by 5 percent, by what percentage will demand fall?

Use:

```text
eD = percentage change in Q / percentage change in P
```

Substitute:

```text
-0.2 = percentage change in Q / 5
```

So:

```text
percentage change in Q = -0.2 x 5
percentage change in Q = -1 percent
```

Demand falls by 1 percent.

## 19.4 Expenditure From Known Elasticity

Question:

Price elasticity of demand is `-0.2`. What happens to expenditure if price rises by 10 percent?

Since:

```text
|eD| = 0.2 < 1
```

demand is inelastic.

For inelastic demand, price and expenditure move in the same direction.

Therefore, expenditure rises.

## 19.5 Supply Elasticity From Revenue Data

Question:

A firm earns revenue of Rs 50 when price is Rs 10. Price rises to Rs 15 and revenue becomes Rs 150. Find supply elasticity.

Step 1: Find quantities supplied.

```text
TR = P x Q
Q = TR / P
```

Old quantity:

```text
Q1 = 50 / 10 = 5
```

New quantity:

```text
Q2 = 150 / 15 = 10
```

Step 2: Percentage change in quantity:

```text
[(10 - 5) / 5] x 100 = 100 percent
```

Step 3: Percentage change in price:

```text
[(15 - 10) / 10] x 100 = 50 percent
```

Step 4: Elasticity:

```text
eS = 100 / 50 = 2
```

Supply is elastic.

## 19.6 Supply Quantity From Elasticity

Question:

At price Rs 10, a firm supplies 4 units. Price rises to Rs 30. Supply elasticity is 1.25. What is the new quantity?

Percentage change in price:

```text
[(30 - 10) / 10] x 100 = 200 percent
```

Use:

```text
eS = percentage change in Q / percentage change in P
```

Substitute:

```text
1.25 = percentage change in Q / 200
```

So:

```text
percentage change in Q = 1.25 x 200
percentage change in Q = 250 percent
```

Initial quantity is 4.

Increase in quantity:

```text
250 percent of 4 = 10
```

New quantity:

```text
4 + 10 = 14 units
```

# 24. Long Answer: Price Elasticity Of Demand

Price elasticity of demand measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. It is defined as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price.

```text
eD = percentage change in quantity demanded / percentage change in price
```

Since price and quantity demanded usually move in opposite directions, elasticity of demand is generally negative. However, for interpretation, economists often use its absolute value.

If the absolute value of elasticity is greater than one, demand is elastic. This means quantity demanded changes more than proportionately compared with price. If the absolute value is less than one, demand is inelastic. This means quantity demanded changes less than proportionately. If the absolute value is equal to one, demand is unit elastic.

Elasticity helps us compare responsiveness across goods and predict how total expenditure changes when price changes.

---

# 25. Long Answer: Elasticity Along A Linear Demand Curve

For a linear demand curve:

```text
q = a - bp
```

the change in quantity per unit change in price is:

```text
delta q / delta p = -b
```

Price elasticity of demand is:

```text
eD = (delta q / delta p) x (p / q)
```

Substituting:

```text
eD = -b x (p / q)
```

This shows that elasticity depends on the ratio of price to quantity. Since price and quantity differ at different points on a straight-line demand curve, elasticity also differs at different points.

At the price-axis intercept, quantity is zero and elasticity is infinity. At the midpoint, elasticity is one. At the quantity-axis intercept, price is zero and elasticity is zero. Above the midpoint demand is elastic, while below the midpoint demand is inelastic.

Therefore, a straight-line demand curve does not have constant elasticity.

---

# 26. Long Answer: Elasticity And Expenditure

Total expenditure on a good is price multiplied by quantity demanded.

```text
Expenditure = P x Q
```

When price changes, quantity demanded changes in the opposite direction. The effect on expenditure depends on which change is proportionately larger.

If demand is elastic, quantity demanded changes more than proportionately compared with price. Therefore, price and expenditure move in opposite directions. A rise in price reduces expenditure, while a fall in price increases expenditure.

If demand is inelastic, quantity demanded changes less than proportionately compared with price. Therefore, price and expenditure move in the same direction. A rise in price increases expenditure, while a fall in price reduces expenditure.

If demand is unit elastic, quantity demanded changes by the same percentage as price. Therefore, total expenditure remains unchanged.

Thus, elasticity is essential for predicting the effect of price changes on consumer expenditure and seller revenue.

---

# 27. Long Answer: Price Elasticity Of Supply

Price elasticity of supply measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.

```text
eS = percentage change in quantity supplied / percentage change in price
```

For a normal upward-sloping supply curve, supply elasticity is positive because price and quantity supplied move in the same direction.

For example, if price rises from Rs 10 to Rs 30 and quantity supplied rises from 200 units to 1,000 units, the percentage change in quantity supplied is 400 percent and the percentage change in price is 200 percent.

```text
eS = 400 / 200 = 2
```

This means supply is elastic because quantity supplied changes more than proportionately compared with price.

Supply elasticity can also be studied geometrically. A vertical supply curve has zero elasticity. A straight-line supply curve passing through the origin has unit elasticity. If the supply curve cuts the price axis, elasticity is greater than one. If it cuts the quantity axis, elasticity is less than one.

# 29. Final Chapter Summary

Price elasticity measures responsiveness of quantity to price. Price elasticity of demand is the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. It is usually negative because price and demand move in opposite directions, but absolute value is often used for interpretation.

Demand is elastic when the absolute value of elasticity is greater than one, inelastic when it is less than one, and unit elastic when it equals one.

Along a straight-line demand curve, elasticity differs at different points. It is infinity at the price-axis intercept, one at the midpoint, and zero at the quantity-axis intercept.

Some curves have constant elasticity. A vertical demand curve is perfectly inelastic, a horizontal demand curve is perfectly elastic, and a rectangular hyperbola has unit elasticity everywhere.

Price elasticity of demand depends on the nature of the good and the availability of close substitutes. Necessities usually have inelastic demand, while luxuries and goods with close substitutes usually have elastic demand.

Elasticity is closely related to total expenditure. With elastic demand, price and expenditure move in opposite directions. With inelastic demand, they move in the same direction. With unit elastic demand, expenditure does not change.

Price elasticity of supply measures responsiveness of quantity supplied to price. It is calculated as the percentage change in quantity supplied divided by the percentage change in price. For a normal upward-sloping supply curve, it is positive.
