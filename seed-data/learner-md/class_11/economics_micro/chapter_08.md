## Chapter Purpose

This chapter explains how a firm decides the quantity it is willing to sell at different market prices.

In demand analysis, the focus was on the consumer. In supply analysis, the focus shifts to the producer or firm.

A firm does not supply output randomly. Its supply decision depends on:

- the market price of the good,
- the technology available,
- prices of factors of production,
- cost curves,
- whether the firm is in the short run or long run.

The central idea of this chapter is:

> A firm's supply curve is closely connected to its marginal cost curve, but the firm supplies positive output only when price covers the relevant average cost condition.

# 1. Meaning Of Supply

## 1.1 Definition

A firm's supply is the quantity of a good that the firm chooses to sell at a given price, given technology and given prices of factors of production.

This definition has four important parts:

1. It is about a firm.
2. It is about quantity sold.
3. It is linked to a given market price.
4. It assumes technology and input prices are given.

## 1.2 Supply Is Not Production Alone

A firm may produce output, but supply refers to the quantity it chooses to offer for sale.

In the simple theory here, we usually assume output produced is output sold.

But conceptually:

- production is creation of output,
- supply is offering output for sale at a price.

## 1.3 Determinants Included In The Definition

Supply depends on:

| Determinant | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Market price | Higher price can make production more profitable. |
| Technology | Better technology lowers cost and raises supply. |
| Factor prices | Higher input prices raise cost and reduce supply. |
| Cost curves | Supply is derived from marginal and average cost curves. |
| Time period | Short-run and long-run supply conditions differ. |

---

# 2. Supply Schedule And Supply Curve

## 2.1 Supply Schedule

A supply schedule is a table that shows how much a firm supplies at different prices, keeping technology and factor prices unchanged.

Example:

| Price | Quantity Supplied |
| ---: | ---: |
| 5 | 0 |
| 10 | 2 |
| 15 | 5 |
| 20 | 9 |

This table shows that the firm supplies more at higher prices.

## 2.2 Supply Curve

A supply curve is the graphical representation of the supply schedule.

In the supply diagram:

- output is measured on the horizontal axis,
- price is measured on the vertical axis.

## 2.3 Why Supply Curve Usually Slopes Upward

The supply curve usually slopes upward because higher output is often associated with higher marginal cost.

A profit-maximising firm expands output when price covers the additional cost of production.

As price rises, more units become profitable to produce.

## 2.4 Short Run And Long Run

The chapter distinguishes between:

1. short-run supply curve,
2. long-run supply curve.

This distinction matters because:

- in the short run, some costs are fixed,
- in the long run, all costs are variable,
- the shutdown condition is different in each period.

---

# 3. Short-Run Supply Curve Of A Firm

## 3.1 Starting Point

In perfect competition, a firm is a price taker.

The market price is given.

The firm chooses output to maximise profit.

The profit-maximising output is found using marginal cost, but the firm will produce only if price covers the relevant variable cost condition.

## 3.2 Conditions For Positive Output In The Short Run

For a competitive firm to produce positive output in the short run:

1. price must equal short-run marginal cost on the rising part of SMC,
2. short-run marginal cost must be rising,
3. price must be greater than or equal to average variable cost.

The third condition is the shutdown condition.

## 3.3 Case 1: Price Is At Least Minimum AVC

Suppose the market price is above the minimum point of AVC.

The firm finds the output where:

```text
Price = SMC
```

on the rising part of the SMC curve.

If the price also covers AVC at that output, the firm produces.

Why?

Because revenue from each unit is enough to cover variable cost, and the firm can also contribute something toward fixed cost.

## 3.4 Case 2: Price Is Below Minimum AVC

Suppose the market price is below the minimum point of AVC.

Then at every positive output level:

```text
AVC > Price
```

This means the firm cannot even cover its variable cost.

If it produces, it loses more than it would lose by shutting down.

Therefore, the firm produces zero output.

## 3.5 Short-Run Supply Curve Result

The firm's short-run supply curve is:

```text
The rising part of the SMC curve from and above minimum AVC,
plus zero output for all prices below minimum AVC.
```

This is one of the most important statements in the chapter.

## 3.6 Why The Supply Curve Does Not Include All Of SMC

The whole SMC curve is not the supply curve.

Only the part of SMC above minimum AVC is included.

Reason:

Below minimum AVC, the firm shuts down and supplies zero output.

So the lower part of SMC is not economically relevant as a supply curve.

---

# 4. Short-Run Shutdown Point

## 4.1 Meaning

The short-run shutdown point is the point at which the firm is just willing to continue producing in the short run.

It occurs at the minimum point of AVC.

At this point:

```text
Price = minimum AVC
```

## 4.2 Why Minimum AVC Matters

In the short run, fixed costs must be paid even if output is zero.

Therefore, the firm compares price with variable cost.

If price covers average variable cost, production can reduce losses.

If price does not cover average variable cost, production increases losses.

## 4.3 Shutdown Rule

| Price Condition | Firm's Short-Run Decision |
| --- | --- |
| `Price > minimum AVC` | Produce positive output. |
| `Price = minimum AVC` | Just willing to produce. |
| `Price < minimum AVC` | Shut down; produce zero output. |

## 4.4 Important Clarification

Shutdown does not mean the firm has no fixed cost.

It means the firm stops producing output in the short run because continuing production cannot cover variable cost.

The fixed cost remains, but it is unavoidable in the short run.

---

# 5. Long-Run Supply Curve Of A Firm

## 5.1 Long-Run Difference

In the long run, all costs are variable.

The firm does not have to remain in the industry if it cannot cover total cost.

Therefore, the relevant cost condition is not AVC. It is LRAC.

## 5.2 Case 1: Price Is At Least Minimum LRAC

If market price is greater than or equal to minimum LRAC, the firm can cover long-run average cost.

The firm finds output where:

```text
Price = LRMC
```

on the rising part of the LRMC curve.

If price covers LRAC at that output, the firm supplies positive output in the long run.

## 5.3 Case 2: Price Is Below Minimum LRAC

If market price is below the minimum point of LRAC, then the firm cannot cover long-run average cost at any positive output.

Since all costs are variable in the long run, the firm exits or produces zero output.

## 5.4 Long-Run Supply Curve Result

The firm's long-run supply curve is:

```text
The rising part of the LRMC curve from and above minimum LRAC,
plus zero output for all prices below minimum LRAC.
```

## 5.5 Long-Run Shutdown Point

The long-run shutdown point is the minimum point of LRAC.

At this point:

```text
Price = minimum LRAC
```

Below this price, the firm will not produce in the long run.

# 6. Short Run Versus Long Run Supply

| Basis | Short Run | Long Run |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Some costs fixed? | Yes | No |
| Relevant average cost for shutdown | AVC | LRAC |
| Supply curve based on | Rising part of SMC | Rising part of LRMC |
| Positive output condition | Price >= minimum AVC | Price >= minimum LRAC |
| Shutdown point | Minimum AVC | Minimum LRAC |
| Exit possible? | Not fully, because fixed factors remain | Yes, the firm can leave the industry |

## 6.1 Why The Difference Exists

In the short run, fixed cost is unavoidable. The firm may continue producing even if it cannot cover total cost, as long as it covers variable cost.

In the long run, all costs must be covered. If price is below LRAC, staying in business is not worthwhile.

# 7. Normal Profit, Supernormal Profit, And Break-Even Point

## 7.1 Normal Profit

Normal profit is the minimum level of profit required to keep a firm in its present business.

It is treated as part of total cost.

This may sound unusual, but it is important in economics.

The owner or entrepreneur could use time, effort, money, and ability elsewhere. Normal profit is the return needed to keep these resources in the current business.

## 7.2 Normal Profit As Opportunity Cost

Normal profit is linked with opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative that is given up.

If an entrepreneur can earn a certain return by using resources elsewhere, the current business must at least match that return.

Otherwise, the entrepreneur has a reason to leave.

## 7.3 Opportunity Cost Example

Suppose you have Rs 1,000.

You can:

- keep it at home and earn zero return,
- deposit it in bank 1 and earn 10 percent interest,
- deposit it in bank 2 and earn 5 percent interest,
- invest it in a family business.

The best alternative to investing in the family business is bank 1, which gives 10 percent.

Therefore, the opportunity cost of investing Rs 1,000 in the family business is the interest you give up from bank 1.

## 7.4 Supernormal Profit

Supernormal profit is profit over and above normal profit.

If a firm earns only normal profit, it is just covering all economic costs, including the opportunity cost of entrepreneurship.

If it earns more than normal profit, it earns supernormal profit.

## 7.5 Break-Even Point

The break-even point is the point on the supply curve where the firm earns only normal profit.

At this point:

```text
Price = Average Cost
```

In the long run, this occurs at the minimum point of LRAC where the supply curve begins.

In the short run, the break-even point is connected with SAC.

## 7.6 Shutdown Point Versus Break-Even Point

| Concept | Short Run | Long Run | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Shutdown point | Minimum AVC | Minimum LRAC | Below this, firm supplies zero. |
| Break-even point | Minimum SAC | Minimum LRAC | Firm earns only normal profit. |

In the long run, shutdown and break-even both relate to LRAC because all costs must be covered.

In the short run, they differ because fixed cost exists.

# 8. Determinants Of A Firm's Supply Curve

The firm's supply curve is based on its marginal cost curve.

Therefore, any factor that affects marginal cost can shift the supply curve.

The chapter focuses mainly on:

1. technological progress,
2. input prices,
3. unit tax,
4. number of firms for market supply.

---

# 9. Technological Progress

## 9.1 Meaning

Technological progress means an improvement in the way production is organised or carried out.

The firm can produce more output with the same inputs, or the same output with fewer inputs.

## 9.2 Effect On Cost

If technology improves:

- fewer inputs are needed for each output level,
- cost of producing output falls,
- marginal cost falls,
- average cost may also fall.

## 9.3 Effect On Supply Curve

Since the supply curve is based on MC, a fall in MC shifts the supply curve to the right.

At every given price, the firm is now willing to supply more.

So:

```text
Technological progress -> MC falls -> supply shifts right
```

## 9.4 Example

If a factory uses a better machine that produces more units with the same labour and capital, production cost per unit may fall.

At the old market price, the firm can profitably sell more output.

This is a rightward shift of supply.

---

# 10. Input Prices

## 10.1 Meaning

Inputs are factors of production such as:

- labour,
- capital,
- raw materials,
- land,
- electricity,
- fuel.

Input prices are payments made for these inputs.

Examples include wages, rent, interest, and raw material prices.

## 10.2 Rise In Input Prices

If an input price rises, production becomes costlier.

For example, if wages rise:

- total cost rises,
- average cost usually rises,
- marginal cost usually rises.

## 10.3 Effect On Supply

When marginal cost rises, the supply curve shifts left.

At each market price, the firm supplies fewer units.

So:

```text
Input price rises -> MC rises -> supply shifts left
```

## 10.4 Fall In Input Prices

If input prices fall, production becomes cheaper.

Marginal cost falls and supply shifts right.

So:

```text
Input price falls -> MC falls -> supply shifts right
```

# 11. Unit Tax And Supply

## 11.1 Meaning Of Unit Tax

A unit tax is a tax imposed per unit of output sold.

Example:

If the government imposes a tax of Rs 2 per unit and the firm sells 10 units:

```text
Tax payment = 2 x 10
Tax payment = Rs 20
```

## 11.2 Effect On Cost Curves

A unit tax increases cost for every unit produced and sold.

If the tax is Rs `t` per unit, then at each output level:

- marginal cost rises by Rs `t`,
- average cost also rises by Rs `t` per unit in the long-run model used here.

So LRMC and LRAC shift upward.

## 11.3 Effect On Supply Curve

Since the long-run supply curve is based on LRMC from and above minimum LRAC, the supply curve shifts left after a unit tax.

At any given market price, the firm supplies fewer units.

So:

```text
Unit tax -> cost per unit rises -> supply shifts left
```

## 11.4 Diagram Interpretation

In the diagram:

- original cost curves are LRMC0 and LRAC0,
- after tax, cost curves become LRMC1 and LRAC1,
- the vertical difference is the unit tax `t`,
- original supply curve is S0,
- new supply curve is S1,
- S1 lies to the left of S0.

# 12. Market Supply Curve

## 12.1 Meaning

The market supply curve shows the total quantity supplied by all firms in the market at different prices.

It is an aggregate curve.

It is not the supply of one firm.

## 12.2 How Market Supply Is Derived

At a given market price:

```text
Market supply = supply of firm 1 + supply of firm 2 + ... + supply of firm n
```

If there are `n` firms, we add the output supplied by all `n` firms at that price.

## 12.3 Horizontal Summation

Market supply is obtained by horizontal summation of individual firm supply curves.

This means:

1. take a price,
2. find each firm's quantity supplied at that price,
3. add the quantities,
4. repeat for other prices.

We add quantities, not prices.

## 12.4 Two-Firm Graphical Logic

Suppose a market has two firms.

Firm 1 begins producing at price `p1`.

Firm 2 begins producing at a higher price `p2`.

If the market price is below `p1`:

- firm 1 supplies zero,
- firm 2 supplies zero,
- market supply is zero.

If price is between `p1` and `p2`:

- firm 1 supplies positive output,
- firm 2 supplies zero,
- market supply equals firm 1's supply.

If price is at or above `p2`:

- both firms supply positive output,
- market supply is the sum of both supplies.

# 13. Numerical Example: Adding Two Firm Supply Curves

The chapter gives two firm supply functions.

Firm 1:

```text
S1(p) = 0, if p < 10
S1(p) = p - 10, if p >= 10
```

Firm 2:

```text
S2(p) = 0, if p < 15
S2(p) = p - 15, if p >= 15
```

## 13.1 Market Supply When Price Is Below 10

If:

```text
p < 10
```

then:

```text
S1(p) = 0
S2(p) = 0
```

So:

```text
Sm(p) = 0
```

## 13.2 Market Supply When Price Is From 10 To Below 15

If:

```text
10 <= p < 15
```

then firm 1 supplies:

```text
p - 10
```

but firm 2 supplies zero.

So:

```text
Sm(p) = p - 10
```

## 13.3 Market Supply When Price Is At Least 15

If:

```text
p >= 15
```

both firms supply positive output.

So:

```text
Sm(p) = S1(p) + S2(p)
Sm(p) = (p - 10) + (p - 15)
Sm(p) = 2p - 25
```

## 13.4 Complete Market Supply Function

```text
Sm(p) = 0, if p < 10
Sm(p) = p - 10, if 10 <= p < 15
Sm(p) = 2p - 25, if p >= 15
```

## 13.5 Sample Table

| Price `p` | Firm 1 Supply | Firm 2 Supply | Market Supply |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 8 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| 10 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| 12 | 2 | 0 | 2 |
| 15 | 5 | 0 | 5 |
| 20 | 10 | 5 | 15 |
| 25 | 15 | 10 | 25 |

Note: At `p = 15`, firm 2's supply is `15 - 15 = 0`. It begins positive supply only when price rises above 15.

# 14. Number Of Firms And Market Supply

The market supply curve is derived for a fixed number of firms.

If the number of firms changes, market supply changes.

## 14.1 More Firms

If more firms enter the market:

- total supply at each price rises,
- market supply curve shifts right.

## 14.2 Fewer Firms

If firms leave the market:

- total supply at each price falls,
- market supply curve shifts left.

## 14.3 Important Difference

A movement along the market supply curve happens when the market price changes.

A shift of the market supply curve happens when:

- number of firms changes,
- technology changes,
- input prices change,
- taxes change,
- other cost conditions change.

---

# 15. Supply Curve Shifts

## 15.1 Rightward Shift

A rightward shift means more output is supplied at every price.

Causes include:

- technological progress,
- fall in input prices,
- fall in unit tax,
- increase in number of firms.

## 15.2 Leftward Shift

A leftward shift means less output is supplied at every price.

Causes include:

- rise in input prices,
- unit tax,
- technological setback,
- decrease in number of firms.

## 15.3 Shift Summary

| Event | Effect On Cost | Effect On Supply |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Better technology | Cost falls | Supply shifts right |
| Input price rises | Cost rises | Supply shifts left |
| Input price falls | Cost falls | Supply shifts right |
| Unit tax imposed | Cost rises | Supply shifts left |
| Number of firms rises | More producers | Market supply shifts right |
| Number of firms falls | Fewer producers | Market supply shifts left |

---

# 16. Movement Along Supply Curve Versus Shift

## 16.1 Movement Along Supply Curve

Movement along a supply curve occurs when the good's own market price changes, while technology, input prices, and other conditions remain constant.

If price rises, the firm moves upward along the supply curve and supplies more.

If price falls, the firm moves downward along the supply curve and supplies less, or zero below shutdown price.

## 16.2 Shift Of Supply Curve

A shift of the supply curve occurs when a factor other than own price changes.

Examples:

- technology improves,
- wages rise,
- tax is imposed,
- more firms enter the market.

## 16.3 Movement Versus Shift Table

| Basis | Movement Along Supply Curve | Shift Of Supply Curve |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cause | Change in own price | Change in cost or number of firms |
| Curve | Same curve | New curve |
| Example | Market price rises | Wage rate rises |
| Language | Quantity supplied changes | Supply changes |

# 17. Diagram Guide

## 17.1 Short-Run Supply Curve

To draw the short-run supply curve:

1. Draw AVC as a U-shaped curve.
2. Draw SMC cutting AVC at the minimum point of AVC.
3. Take the rising part of SMC from the minimum AVC point upward.
4. This part is the short-run supply curve.
5. For prices below minimum AVC, supply is zero.

## 17.2 Long-Run Supply Curve

To draw the long-run supply curve:

1. Draw LRAC as a U-shaped curve.
2. Draw LRMC cutting LRAC at the minimum point of LRAC.
3. Take the rising part of LRMC from the minimum LRAC point upward.
4. This part is the long-run supply curve.
5. For prices below minimum LRAC, supply is zero.

## 17.3 Market Supply Curve

To draw market supply from two firms:

1. Draw firm 1's supply curve.
2. Draw firm 2's supply curve.
3. For each price, add the two quantities horizontally.
4. Plot the total quantity at that price.
5. Join the points to get market supply.

# 18. Answer-Writing Templates

## 18.1 Define Supply

Use this structure:

```text
Supply of a firm is the quantity of a good that the firm chooses to sell at a given price, given technology and prices of factors of production.
A supply schedule shows this relation in tabular form, while a supply curve shows it graphically.
```

## 18.2 Short-Run Supply Curve

Use this structure:

```text
In the short run, a competitive firm's supply curve is the rising part of its SMC curve from and above the minimum AVC.
If price is greater than or equal to minimum AVC, the firm produces the output where price equals SMC on the rising part.
If price is below minimum AVC, the firm shuts down and supplies zero output.
Therefore, the short-run supply curve includes zero output below minimum AVC and the rising SMC above minimum AVC.
```

## 18.3 Long-Run Supply Curve

Use this structure:

```text
In the long run, all costs are variable.
The firm produces only if price is at least equal to minimum LRAC.
If price is greater than or equal to minimum LRAC, output is determined where price equals LRMC on the rising part of LRMC.
If price is below minimum LRAC, the firm produces zero output.
Thus, the long-run supply curve is the rising part of LRMC from and above minimum LRAC, together with zero output below it.
```

## 18.4 Shutdown Point

Use this structure:

```text
The shutdown point is the last price-output point at which the firm is willing to produce.
In the short run, it occurs at the minimum point of AVC.
If price falls below minimum AVC, the firm cannot cover variable cost and supplies zero output.
In the long run, the shutdown point is the minimum point of LRAC because all costs must be covered.
```

## 18.5 Market Supply

Use this structure:

```text
Market supply is the total quantity supplied by all firms in the market at a given price.
It is obtained by adding the quantities supplied by individual firms at that price.
Graphically, the market supply curve is obtained by horizontal summation of individual firm supply curves.
```

## 18.6 Effect Of Technology

Use this structure:

```text
Technological progress allows a firm to produce more output with the same inputs or the same output with fewer inputs.
This lowers marginal cost.
Since the supply curve is based on the marginal cost curve, a fall in marginal cost shifts the supply curve to the right.
At any given price, the firm supplies more output.
```

## 18.7 Effect Of Input Prices

Use this structure:

```text
An increase in input prices raises the cost of production.
Average cost and marginal cost usually rise.
Since the supply curve is based on the marginal cost curve, the supply curve shifts left.
At any given price, the firm supplies fewer units of output.
```

## 18.8 Effect Of Unit Tax

Use this structure:

```text
A unit tax is imposed per unit of output sold.
It raises the cost of producing and selling each unit.
As a result, marginal cost and average cost rise by the amount of the tax.
The firm's supply curve shifts left, so at any given price the firm supplies less output.
```

# 19. Common Mistakes And Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Supply means total production. | Supply means quantity offered for sale at a given price. |
| The whole SMC curve is the short-run supply curve. | Only the rising part of SMC from and above minimum AVC is the short-run supply curve. |
| A firm produces whenever price equals MC. | Price must equal MC on the rising part and must also cover the relevant average cost condition. |
| Shutdown point is minimum SAC in the short run. | Short-run shutdown point is minimum AVC. |
| Break-even point and shutdown point are always the same. | They differ in the short run; shutdown uses AVC, break-even uses average cost. |
| Normal profit is extra profit. | Normal profit is the minimum profit needed to stay in business and is included in cost. |
| Market supply is found by adding prices. | Market supply is found by adding quantities at each price. |
| A unit tax shifts supply right. | A unit tax raises cost and shifts supply left. |
| Technological progress shifts supply left. | Technological progress lowers cost and shifts supply right. |
| Increase in number of firms causes movement along supply curve. | It shifts the market supply curve right. |

# 20. Strong Short Answers

## Question: What is supply?

Supply is the quantity of a good that a firm chooses to sell at a given price, given technology and prices of factors of production.

## Question: What is a supply schedule?

A supply schedule is a table showing the quantities a firm supplies at different prices, while technology and input prices remain unchanged.

## Question: What is a supply curve?

A supply curve is the graphical representation of a supply schedule. It shows the quantity supplied at different prices, with output on the horizontal axis and price on the vertical axis.

## Question: What is the short-run supply curve of a firm?

The short-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above minimum AVC, along with zero output for all prices below minimum AVC.

## Question: What is the long-run supply curve of a firm?

The long-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above minimum LRAC, along with zero output for all prices below minimum LRAC.

## Question: What is the shutdown point?

The shutdown point is the price-output point below which a firm stops producing. In the short run it is the minimum point of AVC; in the long run it is the minimum point of LRAC.

## Question: What is normal profit?

Normal profit is the minimum profit required to keep a firm in its present business. It is treated as part of the firm's total cost because it represents the opportunity cost of entrepreneurship.

## Question: What is the break-even point?

The break-even point is the point where a firm earns only normal profit. At this point, price equals average cost.

## Question: What is market supply?

Market supply is the total quantity supplied by all firms in the market at a given price. It is obtained by adding individual firm supplies at that price.

# 21. Long Answer: Short-Run Supply Curve

In the short run, a perfectly competitive firm is a price taker. It chooses the output that maximises profit at the given market price.

For positive output, the firm chooses the output where price equals short-run marginal cost on the rising part of the SMC curve. However, this condition alone is not sufficient. The firm must also be able to cover average variable cost.

If price is greater than or equal to minimum AVC, the firm produces positive output. It may or may not cover total cost, but it is at least covering variable cost and contributing toward fixed cost.

If price is below minimum AVC, the firm cannot cover variable cost at any positive output. Producing would increase its losses. Therefore, it shuts down and supplies zero output.

Thus, the short-run supply curve of a firm is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above minimum AVC, together with zero output for prices below minimum AVC.

---

# 22. Long Answer: Long-Run Supply Curve

In the long run, all costs are variable. The firm can leave the industry if it cannot cover total cost.

Therefore, the relevant cost condition is long-run average cost. If the market price is greater than or equal to minimum LRAC, the firm can cover long-run average cost and produce positive output.

The profit-maximising output is found where price equals LRMC on the rising part of the LRMC curve.

If price is below minimum LRAC, the firm cannot cover long-run average cost at any positive output. Since all costs are variable in the long run, the firm produces zero output.

Thus, the long-run supply curve is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above minimum LRAC, together with zero output for prices below minimum LRAC.

---

# 23. Long Answer: Determinants Of Supply

A firm's supply curve is derived from its marginal cost curve. Therefore, any factor that changes marginal cost can shift the supply curve.

Technological progress lowers the amount of input needed to produce output. This reduces marginal cost and shifts the supply curve right. At each price, the firm supplies more output.

An increase in input prices raises the cost of production. Marginal cost rises and the supply curve shifts left. At each price, the firm supplies fewer units.

A unit tax also raises cost because the firm must pay tax on each unit sold. If the tax is Rs `t` per unit, cost rises by Rs `t` per unit. The supply curve shifts left.

In the market, the number of firms also matters. If more firms enter, market supply shifts right. If firms exit, market supply shifts left.

---

# 24. Long Answer: Market Supply

Market supply is the total quantity supplied by all firms in a market at a given price.

It is derived by adding the quantities supplied by individual firms at each price. This is called horizontal summation.

For example, if firm 1 supplies 10 units and firm 2 supplies 5 units at price Rs 20, market supply at Rs 20 is 15 units.

If firm 1's supply function is:

```text
S1(p) = 0, if p < 10
S1(p) = p - 10, if p >= 10
```

and firm 2's supply function is:

```text
S2(p) = 0, if p < 15
S2(p) = p - 15, if p >= 15
```

then market supply is:

```text
Sm(p) = 0, if p < 10
Sm(p) = p - 10, if 10 <= p < 15
Sm(p) = 2p - 25, if p >= 15
```

This shows that market supply is found by adding quantities supplied by firms at each price, not by adding prices.

# 26. Final Chapter Summary

Supply is the quantity a firm chooses to sell at a given price, given technology and input prices. A supply schedule presents this relation in a table, while a supply curve presents it graphically.

In the short run, the firm's supply curve is the rising part of the SMC curve from and above minimum AVC, together with zero output below minimum AVC. Minimum AVC is the short-run shutdown point.

In the long run, the firm's supply curve is the rising part of the LRMC curve from and above minimum LRAC, together with zero output below minimum LRAC. Minimum LRAC is the long-run shutdown point.

Normal profit is the minimum profit required to keep the firm in business and is treated as part of cost. Profit above normal profit is supernormal profit. The break-even point is where the firm earns only normal profit.

Technological progress lowers marginal cost and shifts supply right. Higher input prices raise marginal cost and shift supply left. A unit tax raises cost per unit and shifts supply left.

Market supply is the sum of individual firm supplies at each price. It is derived by horizontal summation. If the number of firms rises, market supply shifts right; if the number of firms falls, market supply shifts left.
