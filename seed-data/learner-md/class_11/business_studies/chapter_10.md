# Chapter 10: Internal Trade

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Trade means buying and selling goods and services with the objective of earning profit. When buying and selling take place within the boundaries of one country, it is called internal trade. This chapter explains internal trade, wholesale trade, retail trade, types of retailers, large-scale retail formats, Goods and Services Tax in the textbook context, and the role of commerce and industry associations in promoting internal trade.

The central idea is:

```text
Internal trade moves goods from producers to consumers within a country through wholesalers, retailers, markets, stores, and trade-support institutions.
```

## 5. Meaning of Trade

Trade means buying and selling goods and services for profit.

Trade exists because:

1. No individual can produce everything needed.
2. No country can produce all goods and services efficiently.
3. Producers specialise in what they can produce best.
4. Surplus production is exchanged with others.
5. Consumers need access to goods made by many producers.
6. Markets connect buyers and sellers.

Trade is therefore an essential part of business.

## 6. Trade Based on Geography

On the basis of geographical location of buyers and sellers, trade is divided into:

1. Internal trade.
2. External trade.

| Basis | Internal trade | External trade |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Buying and selling within one country | Buying and selling between countries |
| Boundaries | Within national boundaries | Across national boundaries |
| Currency | Usually domestic legal tender | May involve foreign currency |
| Custom duty | Not involved as import duty | May involve import or export duties |
| Example | Goods sold from a factory in Delhi to a retailer in Jaipur | Goods exported from India to another country |

## 7. Meaning of Internal Trade

Internal trade means buying and selling goods and services within the boundaries of a nation.

Examples:

1. Buying groceries from a nearby store.
2. Buying clothes from a market in the same country.
3. Buying goods from a departmental store.
4. Buying vegetables from a street vendor.
5. Buying products from a mall.
6. Selling goods from a manufacturer to a wholesaler within India.
7. Selling goods from a wholesaler to a retailer within India.

## 8. Features of Internal Trade

Important features:

1. It takes place within one country.
2. Goods are meant for domestic consumption or domestic use.
3. Import duty and custom duty are not levied.
4. Payment is generally made in domestic legal tender.
5. It includes both wholesale and retail trade.
6. It helps distribute goods throughout the country.
7. It connects producers with final consumers.
8. It may take place in local markets, central markets, malls, door-to-door selling, exhibitions, shops, and online domestic selling.

## 9. Objective of Internal Trade

The main objective of internal trade is to ensure that goods are distributed within a nation:

1. Equitably.
2. Speedily.
3. Conveniently.
4. At reasonable cost.
5. In the required quantity.
6. At the required place.
7. At the required time.

## 10. Why Markets Are Needed

Markets are needed because producers and consumers are often separated by:

1. Distance.
2. Time.
3. Quantity needs.
4. Information gaps.
5. Storage needs.
6. Transport problems.
7. Payment arrangements.

Example:

A soap factory may produce millions of soaps in one region. Consumers are spread across the whole country. Wholesalers, retailers, transporters, warehouses, and markets help the soaps reach consumers in small quantities.

## 11. Classification of Internal Trade

Internal trade is classified into:

1. Wholesale trade.
2. Retail trade.

| Basis | Wholesale trade | Retail trade |
|---|---|---|
| Quantity | Large quantities | Small quantities |
| Buyer | Retailers, institutions, industrial users | Final consumers |
| Purpose | Resale or intermediate use | Personal or non-business use |
| Link | Between producer and retailer | Between business and final consumer |
| Example | Wholesaler buying 5,000 cartons from manufacturer | Consumer buying one packet from a shop |

## 12. Meaning of Wholesale Trade

Wholesale trade means buying and selling goods and services in large quantities for:

1. Resale.
2. Intermediate use.
3. Industrial use.
4. Institutional use.
5. Commercial use.

Wholesalers usually buy from producers and sell to retailers, merchants, industrial users, or institutional buyers.

They generally do not sell in significant amounts to final consumers.

## 13. Who Is a Wholesaler?

A wholesaler is a marketing intermediary who:

1. Buys goods in bulk.
2. Takes title to goods.
3. Stores goods in warehouses.
4. Sells in smaller lots to retailers or users.
5. Bears risks related to goods.
6. Provides market information.
7. Helps distribute goods over a large area.

Wholesalers are an important link between manufacturers and retailers.

## 14. Why Wholesalers Are Needed

Wholesalers are needed because manufacturers may find it difficult to sell directly to many small retailers across wide areas.

Wholesalers help by:

1. Collecting orders from retailers.
2. Buying in large quantities from producers.
3. Storing goods.
4. Breaking bulk into smaller lots.
5. Transporting goods.
6. Providing credit to retailers.
7. Giving market information to producers.
8. Reducing distribution burden.

## 15. Functions Performed by Wholesalers

Common wholesaler functions:

1. Bulk buying.
2. Bulk selling.
3. Warehousing.
4. Transportation.
5. Grading.
6. Packing into smaller lots.
7. Risk bearing.
8. Financing.
9. Market information.
10. Promotion support.
11. Order collection.
12. Supply distribution.

These functions cannot be eliminated. If wholesalers are removed, manufacturers or retailers would have to perform many of the same tasks.

## 16. Time Utility and Place Utility

Wholesalers create:

1. Time utility.
2. Place utility.

Time utility means goods are available when needed.

Place utility means goods are available where needed.

Example:

If a manufacturer produces fans in one city but retailers need fans across the country before summer, wholesalers store and distribute the fans at the right time and place.

## 17. Services of Wholesalers to Manufacturers

Wholesalers provide these major services to manufacturers:

1. Facilitating large-scale production.
2. Bearing risk.
3. Financial assistance.
4. Expert advice.
5. Help in marketing.
6. Continuity of production.
7. Storage.

## 18. Manufacturer Service 1: Facilitating Large-Scale Production

Wholesalers collect many small orders from retailers and place large orders with manufacturers.

This helps manufacturers:

1. Produce on a large scale.
2. Reduce per unit cost.
3. Use machinery efficiently.
4. Plan production better.
5. Enjoy economies of scale.

Without wholesalers, manufacturers would have to handle many small orders individually.

## 19. Manufacturer Service 2: Bearing Risk

Wholesalers buy goods in their own name and keep them in warehouses.

They bear risks such as:

1. Price fall.
2. Theft.
3. Pilferage.
4. Spoilage.
5. Fire.
6. Damage.
7. Demand fluctuation.

This reduces the risk burden of manufacturers.

## 20. Manufacturer Service 3: Financial Assistance

Wholesalers often pay manufacturers in cash or make advance payments for bulk orders.

This helps manufacturers because:

1. Funds are not blocked in stock.
2. Working capital improves.
3. Production can continue.
4. Manufacturers receive quicker cash flow.
5. Credit collection burden is reduced.

## 21. Manufacturer Service 4: Expert Advice

Wholesalers remain in contact with retailers and markets.

They can advise manufacturers about:

1. Customer tastes.
2. Customer preferences.
3. Market conditions.
4. Competing products.
5. Price trends.
6. Product features preferred by buyers.
7. Seasonal demand.

This information helps manufacturers make better marketing and production decisions.

## 22. Manufacturer Service 5: Help in Marketing

Wholesalers distribute goods to many retailers.

This helps manufacturers by:

1. Reducing the need to contact individual retailers.
2. Expanding market reach.
3. Saving selling effort.
4. Allowing manufacturers to focus on production.
5. Increasing availability of goods in different regions.

## 23. Manufacturer Service 6: Continuity of Production

Wholesalers purchase goods when manufacturers produce them and store goods until retailers demand them.

This helps manufacturers:

1. Continue production throughout the year.
2. Avoid production stoppage due to seasonal demand.
3. Maintain regular output.
4. Use plant capacity better.
5. Avoid excessive finished stock at factory premises.

## 24. Manufacturer Service 7: Storage

Wholesalers take delivery of goods and store them in warehouses or godowns.

This reduces the need for manufacturers to maintain large storage facilities.

Storage also protects goods until they are demanded by retailers.

## 25. Services of Wholesalers to Retailers

Wholesalers provide these services to retailers:

1. Availability of goods.
2. Marketing support.
3. Credit.
4. Specialised knowledge.
5. Risk sharing.

## 26. Retailer Service 1: Availability of Goods

Retailers need to maintain stock of different goods for customers.

Wholesalers help by:

1. Supplying goods from many manufacturers.
2. Providing variety.
3. Reducing the need for retailers to contact each producer.
4. Allowing retailers to buy in smaller lots.
5. Keeping regular supply available.

## 27. Retailer Service 2: Marketing Support

Wholesalers may support retailers through:

1. Advertising.
2. Sales promotion.
3. Product display material.
4. Information about new products.
5. Schemes and offers.

This helps retailers increase demand and sales.

## 28. Retailer Service 3: Credit

Wholesalers often give credit to regular retailers.

This helps retailers:

1. Operate with less working capital.
2. Maintain adequate stock.
3. Sell goods before making full payment.
4. Manage cash flow.
5. Expand business gradually.

## 29. Retailer Service 4: Specialised Knowledge

Wholesalers usually specialise in a line of goods.

They can inform retailers about:

1. New products.
2. Quality differences.
3. Price changes.
4. Customer demand.
5. Shelf arrangement.
6. Demonstration methods.
7. Suitable stock mix.

## 30. Retailer Service 5: Risk Sharing

Retailers can buy goods in smaller quantities from wholesalers.

This reduces risks such as:

1. Unsold large stock.
2. Storage cost.
3. Pilferage.
4. Obsolescence.
5. Price fall.
6. Demand fluctuation.

## 31. Meaning of Retail Trade

Retail trade means buying and selling goods or services directly to final consumers, usually in small quantities.

Retail trade is the final stage in distribution.

The retailer may sell:

1. In a shop.
2. At a roadside stall.
3. Door to door.
4. Through vending machines.
5. By telephone order.
6. Through mail.
7. Through a supermarket.
8. Through domestic online channels.

The key condition is that the buyer is the ultimate consumer.

## 32. Who Is a Retailer?

A retailer is a business enterprise that sells goods and services directly to final consumers for personal or non-business use.

Retailers usually:

1. Buy goods from wholesalers or manufacturers.
2. Store goods.
3. Sell in small quantities.
4. Provide information to customers.
5. Display goods.
6. Provide credit to some customers.
7. Handle complaints.
8. Collect market information.

## 33. Why Retailers Are Needed

Retailers are needed because final consumers usually:

1. Buy in small quantities.
2. Need goods near their homes.
3. Want variety.
4. Need product information.
5. Want convenience.
6. Need after-sales support.
7. May want credit.
8. Prefer personal contact.

Retailers connect mass production with individual consumption.

## 34. Services of Retailers to Manufacturers and Wholesalers

Retailers provide these services to manufacturers and wholesalers:

1. Help in distribution.
2. Personal selling.
3. Enabling large-scale operations.
4. Collecting market information.
5. Help in promotion.

## 35. Service 1: Help in Distribution

Retailers make goods available to final consumers scattered over a large area.

This gives manufacturers and wholesalers access to many consumers without dealing with each consumer directly.

Retailers create place utility by keeping goods near consumers.

## 36. Service 2: Personal Selling

Many consumer goods require personal selling.

Retailers help by:

1. Explaining product features.
2. Answering customer questions.
3. Demonstrating products.
4. Suggesting suitable choices.
5. Persuading customers to buy.

This reduces the selling burden of manufacturers.

## 37. Service 3: Enabling Large-Scale Operations

Retailers handle small individual sales.

This allows manufacturers and wholesalers to operate on a larger scale because they do not need to sell one unit at a time to individual consumers.

## 38. Service 4: Collecting Market Information

Retailers are in direct contact with consumers.

They can collect information about:

1. Customer tastes.
2. Preferences.
3. Complaints.
4. Demand changes.
5. Price sensitivity.
6. New product response.
7. Competitor products.

This information helps manufacturers improve products and marketing strategies.

## 39. Service 5: Help in Promotion

Retailers support promotion by:

1. Displaying goods.
2. Explaining offers.
3. Participating in schemes.
4. Giving coupons.
5. Displaying posters.
6. Offering free gifts.
7. Running in-store promotions.

## 40. Services of Retailers to Consumers

Retailers provide these major services to consumers:

1. Regular availability of products.
2. Information about new products.
3. Convenience in buying.
4. Wide selection.
5. After-sales services.
6. Credit facilities.

## 41. Consumer Service 1: Regular Availability

Retailers maintain stock of various products from different manufacturers.

This helps consumers buy goods when needed instead of waiting for producers or wholesalers.

## 42. Consumer Service 2: Information About New Products

Retailers inform consumers about:

1. New arrivals.
2. Special features.
3. Price differences.
4. Uses of products.
5. Offers and schemes.
6. Quality differences.

This helps consumers make buying decisions.

## 43. Consumer Service 3: Convenience in Buying

Retailers provide convenience because they:

1. Are located near residential areas.
2. Remain open for long hours.
3. Sell in small quantities.
4. Provide immediate delivery.
5. Offer familiar buying surroundings.
6. Save consumer time.

## 44. Consumer Service 4: Wide Selection

Retailers may keep goods from different manufacturers.

This allows consumers to compare:

1. Price.
2. Quality.
3. Size.
4. Colour.
5. Design.
6. Brand.

## 45. Consumer Service 5: After-Sales Services

Retailers may provide:

1. Home delivery.
2. Installation.
3. Repair support.
4. Spare parts.
5. Exchange support.
6. Complaint handling.

After-sales service increases repeat purchases and customer trust.

## 46. Consumer Service 6: Credit Facilities

Some retailers provide credit to regular customers.

This helps customers buy goods even when immediate cash is limited.

It also helps retailers build customer loyalty.

## 47. Terms of Trade

The chapter includes some common trade terms:

1. Cash on delivery, or COD.
2. Free on Board, or FoB.
3. Free on Rail, or FOR.
4. Cost, Insurance and Freight, commonly called CIF.
5. Errors and Omissions Excepted, or E&OE.

## 48. Cash on Delivery

Cash on delivery means payment is made at the time of delivery.

If the buyer cannot pay when goods are delivered, the goods may be returned to the seller.

COD reduces the seller's risk of non-payment.

## 49. Free on Board and Free on Rail

FoB or FOR refers to a contract where expenses up to the point of delivery to a carrier are borne by the seller.

The carrier may be:

1. Ship.
2. Rail.
3. Lorry.
4. Other transport carrier.

After that point, responsibility may shift according to the contract terms.

## 50. Cost, Insurance and Freight

Cost, Insurance and Freight means the price includes:

1. Cost of goods.
2. Insurance charges.
3. Freight charges up to the destination port.

It helps the buyer know that major transport and insurance costs up to the specified point are included in the quoted price.

## 51. Errors and Omissions Excepted

E&OE is a term used in trade documents to indicate that mistakes and omissions may be corrected.

It protects the seller from being bound by accidental clerical mistakes in price lists, invoices, or quotations.

## 52. Types of Retailing Trade

Retailers may be classified on several bases.

Common bases:

1. Size of business.
2. Ownership.
3. Merchandise handled.
4. Whether the retailer has a fixed place of business.

On the basis of fixed place of business, retailers are classified into:

1. Itinerant retailers.
2. Fixed shop retailers.

## 53. Itinerant Retailers

Itinerant retailers are retailers who do not have a fixed place of business.

They move from place to place with their goods in search of customers.

Examples:

1. Hawkers.
2. Peddlers.
3. Market traders.
4. Street traders.
5. Cheap jacks.

## 54. Characteristics of Itinerant Retailers

Important characteristics:

1. They are small traders.
2. They operate with limited resources.
3. They do not have a permanent shop.
4. They move to customers or crowded places.
5. They usually sell daily-use consumer goods.
6. They provide doorstep convenience.
7. They keep limited inventory.
8. They may store goods at home or another small place.

## 55. Peddlers and Hawkers

Peddlers and hawkers are small traders who carry goods on:

1. Their heads.
2. Bicycles.
3. Hand carts.
4. Cycle-rickshaws.
5. Small vehicles.

They move from place to place and sell goods at the customer's doorstep or in busy areas.

Common goods:

1. Vegetables.
2. Fruits.
3. Toys.
4. Snacks.
5. Fabrics.
6. Ice creams.
7. Small household items.

Main advantage:

They provide convenience to consumers.

Main caution:

Quality and price may not always be reliable.

## 56. Market Traders

Market traders are small retailers who open shops at different places on fixed days or dates.

Examples:

1. Weekly market trader.
2. Trader who sells every Saturday.
3. Trader who visits villages on market days.

They may sell:

1. Fabrics.
2. Ready-made garments.
3. Toys.
4. Crockery.
5. Low-priced daily-use goods.

They mainly serve lower-income groups and customers who prefer low-priced goods.

## 57. Street Traders or Pavement Vendors

Street traders are small retailers found near places with large floating population.

Common locations:

1. Railway stations.
2. Bus stands.
3. Markets.
4. Street corners.
5. Office areas.
6. School or college areas.

Goods sold:

1. Stationery.
2. Eatables.
3. Ready-made garments.
4. Newspapers.
5. Magazines.
6. Low-value daily-use items.

Street traders usually do not change their place as frequently as hawkers.

## 58. Cheap Jacks

Cheap jacks are petty retailers with independent temporary shops in business localities.

Features:

1. They have a temporary shop.
2. They may shift from one locality to another.
3. They do not move as frequently as hawkers.
4. They deal in low-priced consumer goods or services.

Examples:

1. Watch repair.
2. Shoe repair.
3. Bucket repair.
4. Small household goods.
5. Temporary local retail stalls.

## 59. Itinerant Retailers Compared

| Type | Place of operation | Movement | Goods or services |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peddlers and hawkers | Doorsteps, streets, busy places | Very frequent | Vegetables, toys, snacks, small goods |
| Market traders | Weekly or periodic markets | Move by fixed market schedule | Garments, toys, crockery, daily-use goods |
| Street traders | Railway stations, bus stands, pavements | Less frequent | Stationery, eatables, newspapers, garments |
| Cheap jacks | Temporary shops in localities | Occasional shifting | Low-priced goods and repair services |

## 60. Fixed Shop Retailers

Fixed shop retailers have a permanent place of business.

They sell goods from a fixed establishment and do not move from place to place.

## 61. Characteristics of Fixed Shop Retailers

Important characteristics:

1. They have permanent shops.
2. They generally have more resources than itinerant retailers.
3. They may operate on small, medium, or large scale.
4. They may sell durable and non-durable goods.
5. They have greater credibility among customers.
6. They can provide services such as home delivery, guarantee, repair, credit, and spare parts.
7. They maintain regular inventory.
8. Customers know where to find them again.

## 62. Types of Fixed Shop Retailers

Fixed shop retailers are divided into:

1. Small shopkeepers.
2. Large retailers.

Fixed shop small retailers include:

1. General stores.
2. Speciality shops.
3. Street stall holders.
4. Second-hand goods shops.
5. Single-line stores.

Fixed shop large retailers include:

1. Departmental stores.
2. Chain stores or multiple shops.
3. Mail order houses.
4. Consumer cooperative stores.
5. Supermarkets.
6. Vending machines.

## 63. General Stores

General stores carry a variety of products needed for day-to-day use.

Common goods:

1. Groceries.
2. Soft drinks.
3. Toiletries.
4. Stationery.
5. Confectionery.
6. Household items.

Features:

1. Located in residential areas or local markets.
2. Open for long hours.
3. Provide convenience.
4. May give credit to regular customers.
5. Depend on goodwill and personal relation with local customers.

## 64. Speciality Shops

Speciality shops specialise in one specific line of products.

Examples:

1. Children's garments.
2. Men's wear.
3. Ladies' shoes.
4. Toys and gifts.
5. School uniforms.
6. College books.
7. Consumer electronics.

Features:

1. Narrow product line.
2. Deep variety within that product line.
3. Usually located in central markets.
4. Attract customers looking for a specific type of product.
5. Provide better product choice within the specialised category.

## 65. Street Stall Holders

Street stall holders are small vendors found at street crossings or crowded points.

They usually sell cheap goods such as:

1. Hosiery products.
2. Toys.
3. Cigarettes.
4. Soft drinks.
5. Snacks.
6. Small accessories.

Features:

1. Limited space.
2. Small scale.
3. Convenient location.
4. Depend on passing customers.
5. Buy from local suppliers or wholesalers.

## 66. Second-Hand Goods Shops

Second-hand goods shops sell used goods.

Common goods:

1. Books.
2. Clothes.
3. Furniture.
4. Automobiles.
5. Household articles.
6. Scooters and motorcycles.
7. Rare and antique items.

These shops are useful for customers with modest means because goods are available at lower prices.

Some second-hand goods may also be expensive if they are rare or antique.

## 67. Single-Line Stores

Single-line stores deal in one line of products but keep many varieties within that line.

Examples:

1. Ready-made garments store.
2. Watch store.
3. Shoe store.
4. Book store.
5. Mobile phone store.

Single-line stores are different from speciality shops because they may deal in one broad product line, while speciality shops may focus on a narrower segment.

## 68. Single-Line Stores and Speciality Shops

| Basis | Single-line store | Speciality shop |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | One broad line of goods | One specific product category or segment |
| Variety | Many items within one line | Deep choice in a specialised segment |
| Example | Shoe store | Sports shoe store |
| Customer need | General need within one line | Specific need within a narrow area |

## 69. Departmental Stores

A departmental store is a large retail establishment offering a wide variety of products through well-defined departments under one roof.

Departments may include:

1. Toiletries.
2. Medicines.
3. Furniture.
4. Groceries.
5. Electronics.
6. Clothing.
7. Dress material.
8. Household goods.

The idea is to satisfy many customer needs at one place.

## 70. Features of Departmental Stores

Important features:

1. Large establishment.
2. Many departments.
3. Wide variety of goods.
4. Central location.
5. Maximum customer service.
6. Usually organised as a joint stock company.
7. Professional management.
8. Centralised purchasing.
9. Decentralised selling through departments.
10. May provide restaurants, restrooms, telephone booths, information counters, and similar facilities.

## 71. Advantages of Departmental Stores

Advantages:

1. Attract large number of customers.
2. Provide convenience under one roof.
3. Offer many services.
4. Enjoy economies of large-scale operation.
5. Can spend more on advertising and promotion.
6. Provide wide variety.
7. Save customer time.

## 72. Limitation 1: Lack of Personal Attention

Because departmental stores operate on a large scale, customers may not receive the same level of personal attention as in small local shops.

## 73. Limitation 2: High Operating Cost

Departmental stores spend heavily on:

1. Large buildings.
2. Staff.
3. Lighting.
4. Decoration.
5. Customer facilities.
6. Advertising.
7. Warehousing.

High operating cost may lead to higher prices.

## 74. Limitation 3: High Possibility of Loss

Because of high operating cost and large inventory, departmental stores may suffer losses if:

1. Fashion changes.
2. Demand falls.
3. Stock becomes outdated.
4. Clearance sales become necessary.
5. Expenses rise faster than sales.

## 75. Limitation 4: Inconvenient Location

Departmental stores are often located in central city areas.

This may be inconvenient for customers who need goods quickly near home.

## 76. Chain Stores or Multiple Shops

Chain stores, also called multiple shops, are networks of retail shops owned and operated by one organisation.

They usually:

1. Have similar appearance.
2. Sell standardised and branded products.
3. Operate in different localities.
4. Follow common policies.
5. Use centralised procurement.
6. Sell at fixed prices.
7. Deal in goods with high and regular turnover.

Examples in the textbook context include Bata, DCM, Nirula's, and Raymonds.

## 77. Features of Chain Stores

Important features:

1. Shops are located in populous localities.
2. Goods are centrally procured by the head office.
3. Goods are distributed to branches according to need.
4. Each shop is managed by a branch manager.
5. Branches send reports to head office.
6. Policies are decided centrally.
7. Prices are fixed.
8. Sales are generally made on cash basis.
9. Cash is deposited daily in bank accounts.
10. Inspectors may supervise quality and policy compliance.

## 78. Advantages of Chain Stores

Advantages:

1. Economies of scale due to centralised buying.
2. Elimination of unnecessary middlemen.
3. No bad debts because sales are usually cash-based.
4. Transfer of goods from low-demand branch to high-demand branch.
5. Diffusion of risk across many branches.
6. Lower cost due to centralised purchasing and promotion.
7. Flexibility to close or shift an unprofitable branch.
8. Uniform quality and service policies.

## 101. Goods and Services Tax in the Textbook Context

Goods and Services Tax, or GST, was implemented in India from July 1, 2017 in the textbook context.

The chapter presents GST as a major tax reform based on the idea:

```text
One nation and one tax
```

GST is described as a destination-based single tax on the supply of goods and services from manufacturer to consumer.

For current tax filing, rates, exemptions, and compliance, always verify the latest official rules. This learner note explains the textbook concepts.

## 102. Why GST Was Introduced

The textbook explains that GST was introduced to:

1. Create a unified national market.
2. Smoothen movement of goods across the country.
3. Replace multiple indirect taxes.
4. Reduce tax-on-tax effect.
5. Improve ease of doing business.
6. Improve tax administration.
7. Reduce tax evasion.
8. Broaden the organised economy.
9. Support tax revenue.

## 103. Destination-Based Consumption Tax

GST is described as a destination-based consumption tax.

This means tax revenue is linked to the place where goods or services are consumed, not merely where they are produced.

Example:

If a product is manufactured in one state and consumed in another, GST treatment follows the destination principle in the textbook explanation.

## 104. Cascading Effect and Input Tax Credit

Cascading effect means tax on tax.

GST tries to reduce this through input tax credit.

Simplified chain:

```text
Supplier pays GST on inputs
-> adds value
-> charges GST on output
-> claims credit for GST already paid on inputs
-> final burden reaches consumer
```

Input tax credit helps avoid repeated taxation on the same value.

## 105. CGST, SGST, and IGST

The chapter uses the following terms:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| CGST | Central Goods and Services Tax |
| SGST | State Goods and Services Tax |
| IGST | Integrated Goods and Services Tax |

Textbook explanation:

1. CGST and SGST apply to intra-state supply.
2. IGST applies to inter-state supply.
3. Tax liability arises when a taxable person crosses the exemption limit in the applicable framework.

## 106. GST Slabs in the Textbook Context

The raw chapter lists these GST slabs:

## 107. Key Features of GST in the Textbook Context

Key features:

1. Territorial spread covers the whole country.

3. It follows destination-based consumption taxation.

## 125. Exam Answer: Meaning of Internal Trade

Internal trade means buying and selling goods and services within the boundaries of a nation. Goods are part of domestic production and are meant for domestic consumption, so import duty or custom duty is not levied. Internal trade includes wholesale trade and retail trade. Its objective is to distribute goods speedily and at reasonable cost within the country.

## 126. Exam Answer: Wholesale Trade

Wholesale trade means purchase and sale of goods and services in large quantities for resale or intermediate use. Wholesalers buy goods in bulk from manufacturers and sell in smaller lots to retailers, industrial users, or institutional buyers. They help in storage, transport, grading, risk-bearing, finance, market information, and distribution.

## 127. Exam Answer: Services of Wholesalers to Manufacturers

Wholesalers provide the following services to manufacturers:

1. They collect small orders from retailers and place large orders, helping large-scale production.
2. They bear risks such as price fall, theft, spoilage, fire, and demand fluctuation.
3. They provide financial assistance through cash or advance payment.
4. They give expert advice about market trends and customer preferences.
5. They help in marketing by distributing goods to many retailers.
6. They facilitate continuity of production by buying and storing goods.
7. They provide storage facilities and reduce the manufacturer's warehousing burden.

## 128. Exam Answer: Services of Wholesalers to Retailers

Wholesalers provide the following services to retailers:

1. They make goods from different manufacturers available at one place.
2. They provide marketing support through advertising and promotion.
3. They grant credit to regular retailers.
4. They provide specialised knowledge about products, prices, quality, and demand.
5. They share risk by allowing retailers to buy in smaller quantities.

## 129. Exam Answer: Retail Trade

Retail trade means sale of goods and services directly to final consumers, usually in small quantities. A retailer is the final link in the distribution chain. Retailers buy from wholesalers or manufacturers, maintain stock, sell in small quantities, provide product information, offer convenience, and often provide after-sales service and credit.

## 130. Exam Answer: Services of Retailers

Retailers provide services to manufacturers, wholesalers, and consumers.

To manufacturers and wholesalers, they help in distribution, personal selling, large-scale operations, market information, and promotion.

To consumers, they provide regular availability of goods, information about new products, convenience in buying, wide selection, after-sales services, and credit facilities.

## 131. Exam Answer: Characteristics of Fixed Shop Retailers

Fixed shop retailers have a permanent place of business. They generally have more resources than itinerant retailers and may operate on small, medium, or large scale. They sell durable and non-durable goods, have greater credibility among customers, maintain regular inventory, and can provide services such as home delivery, guarantee, repair, spare parts, and credit.

## 132. Exam Answer: Street Traders and Street Shops

Street traders are small retailers commonly found near places where many people pass, such as bus stands, railway stations, pavements, and markets. They generally sell low-priced goods such as eatables, stationery, garments, newspapers, and magazines. Street shops or street stall holders have a more fixed stall-like arrangement and may operate from a small space in a busy street. Street traders are more informal, while street shops may have a more identifiable selling spot.

## 133. Exam Answer: Departmental Store

A departmental store is a large retail establishment offering a wide variety of goods through different departments under one roof. Each department deals with a particular category of goods. Departmental stores are usually located in central places and provide many services such as restrooms, restaurants, telephone facilities, credit, home delivery, and product variety. They provide convenience but have high operating costs and limited personal attention.

## 134. Exam Answer: Chain Stores

Chain stores or multiple shops are networks of retail shops owned and controlled by one organisation. They sell standardised and branded goods through branches located in different areas. Procurement is centralised, branches follow common policies, prices are fixed, and sales are generally on cash basis. They enjoy economies of scale, reduce middlemen, avoid bad debts, and spread risk across branches.

## 135. Exam Answer: Departmental Stores vs Chain Stores

Departmental stores are large stores located centrally and sell a wide variety of goods under one roof through different departments. They provide many services and may give credit. Chain stores are multiple branches located in different localities under one organisation. They sell standardised goods, follow fixed prices, usually sell for cash, and offer fewer services. Departmental stores focus on variety and service; chain stores focus on standardisation and reach.

## 136. Exam Answer: Consumer Cooperative Stores

Consumer cooperative stores are owned, managed, and controlled by consumers themselves. Their objective is to reduce middlemen and provide goods to members at reasonable prices. They are formed by voluntary association, registered under cooperative law, and managed democratically on the principle of one member, one vote. They offer lower prices, limited liability, democratic management, and convenient location, but may suffer from lack of funds, lack of initiative, and weak business training.

## 137. Exam Answer: Mail Order Houses

Mail order houses sell goods through mail without direct personal contact with buyers. Customers are approached through advertisements, catalogues, circulars, samples, and price lists. Goods suitable for mail order selling are standardised, graded, easy to transport, available in large quantity, and easy to describe. Mail order houses require limited capital, eliminate middlemen, avoid bad debts, and have wide reach, but suffer from lack of personal contact, high promotion cost, delayed delivery, no credit, and no after-sales service.

## 138. Exam Answer: Usefulness of Vending Machines

Vending machines are useful for selling pre-packed, standardised, low-priced, high-turnover goods such as beverages, chocolates, tickets, newspapers, and snacks. They provide quick service without a salesperson and can operate for long hours. However, they require high installation and maintenance cost, need reliable operation, and are not suitable for goods that customers must inspect before buying.

## 139. Exam Answer: GST

Goods and Services Tax, or GST, is presented in the textbook as a destination-based tax on supply of goods and services. It was implemented in India from July 1, 2017 to create a unified market, replace multiple indirect taxes, reduce tax-on-tax effect, improve ease of doing business, and simplify tax administration. CGST and SGST apply to intra-state supply, while IGST applies to inter-state supply in the textbook framework.

## 140. Exam Answer: Role of Commerce and Industry Associations

Commerce and industry associations such as ASSOCHAM, CII, and FICCI promote and protect the common interests of business and industry. They interact with government to reduce trade barriers, improve interstate movement of goods, simplify taxes, promote infrastructure, support marketing of agro products, improve weights and measures laws, prevent duplicate brands, represent business concerns on duties, and help create a better environment for internal trade.

## 153. End Summary

Internal trade means buying and selling goods and services within a country. It includes wholesale trade and retail trade. Wholesalers buy goods in bulk and sell them to retailers or other users, providing services such as storage, risk-bearing, finance, market information, and distribution. Retailers sell goods directly to final consumers and provide convenience, variety, information, after-sales support, and credit. Retailers may be itinerant, such as hawkers, market traders, street traders, and cheap jacks, or fixed shop retailers, such as general stores, speciality shops, departmental stores, chain stores, mail order houses, cooperative stores, supermarkets, and vending machines. GST is explained in the textbook as a destination-based tax intended to create a unified market and reduce tax-on-tax. Commerce and industry associations promote internal trade by interacting with government on transport, taxes, infrastructure, fair trade rules, brand protection, agro marketing, and labour matters.
