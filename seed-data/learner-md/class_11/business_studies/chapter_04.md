# Chapter 4: Business Services

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Business cannot run only with production and sale. It needs support services such as banking, insurance, communication, transport, and warehousing. These services help businesses get funds, reduce risk, contact customers and suppliers, move goods, and store goods safely.

This chapter explains:

1. Meaning and nature of services.
2. Difference between goods and services.
3. Types of services.
4. Banking and e-banking.
5. Insurance and its principles.
6. Communication services.
7. Transportation.
8. Warehousing.

## 3. Big Idea

A business is supported by many invisible services.

Example: A petrol pump needs more than petrol.

It needs:

1. Transport to bring petrol and diesel.
2. Warehousing or depots to store fuel.
3. Communication to contact oil companies and customers.
4. Banking to arrange finance and payments.
5. Insurance to protect against fire, accident, theft, and other risks.

So, business services are the support system that keeps business moving.

## 4. Meaning of Services

Services are separately identifiable, essentially intangible economic activities that provide satisfaction of wants and involve interaction between service provider and consumer.

In simple words:

```text
Service = Useful work performed for someone, usually without transfer of ownership of a physical thing
```

Examples:

1. Medical treatment.
2. Banking.
3. Insurance.
4. Transport.
5. Warehousing.
6. Communication.
7. Education.
8. Tourism.
9. Restaurant service.

## 5. Meaning of Goods

Goods are physical products that can be seen, touched, stored, delivered, and owned.

Examples:

1. Books.
2. Medicines.
3. Clothes.
4. Mobile phones.
5. Vehicles.
6. Furniture.
7. Food items.

## 6. Services and Goods: Basic Difference

Goods are produced, owned, and stored. Services are performed, experienced, and usually consumed at the time of delivery.

Example:

Buying ice cream from a store is purchase of a good. Eating ice cream in a restaurant also includes restaurant service.

Watching a movie in a cinema hall is a service experience. Buying a movie recording is purchase of a good or digital product depending on the form.

## 7. The Five Features of Services

The five major features of services are called the five Is:

1. Intangibility.
2. Inconsistency.
3. Inseparability.
4. Inventory-less nature.
5. Involvement.

## 8. Intangibility

Services cannot be touched, seen as a physical object, or stored like goods. They are experienced.

Examples:

1. Doctor's advice.
2. Bank account service.
3. Insurance protection.
4. Movie experience.
5. Legal consultation.

### 8.1 Business Implication

Because services cannot be inspected physically before purchase, the customer often judges quality through:

1. Reputation.
2. Trust.
3. Behaviour of staff.
4. Speed.
5. Cleanliness.
6. Reliability.
7. Past experience.
8. Reviews or recommendations.

## 9. Inconsistency

Services may differ from one customer to another because they are performed separately each time.

Examples:

1. A haircut depends on customer preference and barber skill.
2. A hotel stay depends on room, staff, timing, and customer expectation.
3. Mobile service experience may vary by network area.

### 9.1 Business Implication

Service providers must train staff, standardize processes, and still allow customization.

## 10. Inseparability

In many services, production and consumption happen at the same time.

Examples:

1. A doctor treats while the patient receives treatment.
2. A teacher teaches while students learn.
3. A passenger travels while transport service is delivered.

Goods can be produced today and sold later, but many services must be consumed when produced.

## 11. Inventory-Less Nature

Services cannot usually be stored for future sale. They are perishable.

Examples:

1. An empty seat on a flight cannot be sold after the flight departs.
2. A doctor's unused appointment time cannot be stored.
3. A hotel room vacant for one night cannot be stocked for tomorrow.

### 11.1 Business Implication

Service businesses must manage demand and capacity carefully.

## 12. Involvement

Customer participation is often necessary in service delivery.

Examples:

1. A bank customer uses ATM or internet banking.
2. A patient explains symptoms to a doctor.
3. A student participates in learning.
4. A restaurant customer chooses food and service preferences.

Services can often be customized because customers are involved.

## 13. Difference Between Services and Goods

| Basis | Services | Goods |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Action, process, or experience | Physical object |
| Tangibility | Intangible | Tangible |
| Ownership | Ownership usually not transferred | Ownership transferred |
| Production and consumption | Often simultaneous | Usually separate |
| Storage | Cannot be stored | Can be stored |
| Standardisation | More variable | More standardized |
| Customer involvement | Often high | Usually lower during production |
| Example | Medical treatment | Medicine |
| Quality judgement | Often judged after experience | Can often be inspected before purchase |

## 14. Types of Services

Services are broadly classified into:

1. Business services.
2. Social services.
3. Personal services.

## 15. Business Services

Business services are services used by business enterprises for carrying out their operations.

Examples:

1. Banking.
2. Insurance.
3. Transport.
4. Warehousing.
5. Communication.
6. Postal services.
7. Telecom services.

### 15.1 Why Business Services Are Needed

Business services help firms:

1. Arrange funds.
2. Protect against risk.
3. Move raw materials.
4. Move finished goods.
5. Store goods.
6. Contact suppliers and customers.
7. Make payments.
8. Handle information.

## 16. Social Services

Social services are services provided voluntarily or with a welfare motive to achieve social goals.

Examples:

1. Education services for weaker sections.
2. Healthcare camps.
3. Hygiene support in slum areas.
4. Services provided by government agencies.
5. Services provided by non-government organisations.

These services may charge some amount to cover cost, but the main motive is social welfare.

## 17. Personal Services

Personal services are services experienced differently by different customers because they depend on individual preferences and service provider skill.

Examples:

1. Tourism.
2. Restaurant service.
3. Recreation.
4. Personal grooming.
5. Tailoring.

## 18. Banking

Banking is an important business service because it mobilises savings and provides funds to individuals, firms, and industries.

A bank accepts deposits from the public and lends money for investment or business use.

### 18.1 Simple Definition

A bank is an institution that accepts deposits and lends money.

### 18.2 Business Definition

Banking means accepting deposits of money from the public for the purpose of lending or investment, repayable on demand or otherwise, and withdrawable by cheque, draft, order, or other permitted methods.

### 18.3 Role of Banks in Business

Banks help business by:

1. Providing loans.
2. Collecting deposits.
3. Allowing payments by cheque and digital transfer.
4. Transferring funds.
5. Discounting bills.
6. Providing locker facility.
7. Collecting dividends and payments.
8. Supporting trade and industry.

## 19. Types of Banks

Banks are broadly classified into:

1. Commercial banks.
2. Cooperative banks.
3. Specialised banks.
4. Central bank.

## 20. Commercial Banks

Commercial banks deal in money by accepting deposits and lending funds. They are governed by banking law and operate to serve individuals, businesses, and the economy.

### 20.1 Public Sector Banks

Public sector banks are banks in which the government has a major stake. They often combine commercial banking with social objectives.

Examples include major government-owned banks.

### 20.2 Private Sector Banks

Private sector banks are owned, managed, and controlled by private promoters or shareholders.

Examples include private banking companies operating under banking regulation.

## 21. Cooperative Banks

Cooperative banks are formed on cooperative principles and are often meant to provide cheap credit to members.

They are especially important in rural credit and agricultural finance.

## 22. Specialised Banks

Specialised banks serve specific financial needs.

Examples:

1. Foreign exchange banks.
2. Industrial banks.
3. Development banks.
4. Export-import banks.

They support industries, foreign trade, long-term investment, and specialised projects.

## 23. Central Bank

The central bank supervises, controls, and regulates commercial banks. It also controls currency and credit policy and acts as banker to the government.

In India, the Reserve Bank of India is the central bank.

## 24. Functions of Commercial Banks

The main functions of commercial banks are:

1. Acceptance of deposits.
2. Lending of funds.
3. Cheque facility.
4. Remittance of funds.
5. Allied services.

## 25. Acceptance of Deposits

Deposits are the basis of lending operations. Banks borrow money from depositors and lend it to borrowers.

### 25.1 Current Account

Current accounts are suitable for business persons and firms. Money can be deposited and withdrawn frequently.

Features:

1. High liquidity.
2. Usually no or low interest.
3. Suitable for frequent transactions.
4. Cheque facility.

### 25.2 Savings Account

Savings accounts encourage saving by individuals.

Features:

1. Interest is paid.
2. Withdrawal may be subject to rules.
3. Suitable for households and individuals.
4. Used for regular banking and digital payments.

### 25.3 Fixed Deposit Account

Fixed deposits are time deposits for a fixed period.

Features:

1. Higher interest than savings accounts.
2. Money is deposited for a fixed period.
3. Premature withdrawal may reduce interest.
4. Suitable for savings not needed immediately.

## 26. Lending of Funds

Banks lend funds from deposits received.

Common lending forms:

1. Overdraft.
2. Cash credit.
3. Discounting of trade bills.
4. Term loans.
5. Consumer credit.
6. Other advances.

### 26.1 Overdraft

Overdraft allows an account holder to withdraw more than the available balance up to an approved limit.

### 26.2 Cash Credit

Cash credit is a short-term loan facility usually given to businesses against security such as stock or receivables.

### 26.3 Discounting of Trade Bills

Banks may pay the value of a bill before its due date after deducting discount. This gives immediate funds to the seller.

### 26.4 Term Loans

Term loans are given for a fixed period, usually for business expansion, machinery, or long-term needs.

## 27. Cheque Facility

A cheque is an instruction to the bank to pay a specified amount from the customer's account.

### 27.1 Bearer Cheque

A bearer cheque can be encashed by the person who presents it at the bank counter.

### 27.2 Crossed Cheque

A crossed cheque cannot be encashed directly at the counter. It must be deposited into a bank account.

## 28. Remittance of Funds

Banks transfer money from one place to another through instruments and systems such as:

1. Bank draft.
2. Pay order.
3. Mail transfer in traditional banking.
4. Electronic transfer systems.

This helps businesses make payments across regions.

## 29. Allied Services

Banks also provide services such as:

1. Bill payment.
2. Locker facility.
3. Underwriting.
4. Buying and selling securities on instructions.
5. Insurance premium payment.
6. Collection of dividends.
7. Collection of cheques.

## 30. E-Banking

E-banking means banking through electronic media such as internet, mobile, ATM, cards, and electronic transfer systems.

It allows customers to perform banking transactions without visiting a branch for every service.

### 30.1 Examples of E-Banking Services

1. ATM.
2. Point of sale transactions.
3. Credit cards.
4. Debit cards.
5. Electronic fund transfer.
6. Internet banking.
7. Mobile banking.
8. Digital cash or wallet-based payments where permitted.

### 30.2 NEFT

NEFT stands for National Electronic Funds Transfer. It is used to transfer money electronically from one bank account to another.

### 30.3 RTGS

RTGS stands for Real Time Gross Settlement. It is used for high-value real-time fund transfer according to banking rules.

## 33. Insurance

Life and business are full of uncertainty. A person may face death, illness, accident, fire, theft, marine loss, crop failure, or other losses. Insurance helps reduce the financial burden of such uncertain events.

Insurance is a contract in which the insurer agrees, in return for premium, to compensate the insured for loss, damage, injury, or specified event covered by the policy.

## 34. Important Insurance Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Insured | Person or business whose risk is covered |
| Insurer | Insurance company that accepts the risk |
| Premium | Amount paid by insured to insurer |
| Policy | Written insurance contract |
| Claim | Demand for payment after insured event occurs |
| Sum assured | Amount promised in life insurance |
| Sum insured | Maximum cover amount in general insurance |
| Risk | Possibility of loss |

## 35. Fundamental Principle of Insurance

The basic principle of insurance is risk pooling.

Many persons exposed to similar risk pay small premiums into a common fund. When a covered loss occurs to one insured person, compensation is paid from that fund.

```text
Small certain premium
instead of
Large uncertain loss
```

Insurance does not stop risk from happening. It reduces the financial impact of risk.

## 36. Functions of Insurance

### 36.1 Providing Certainty

Insurance gives certainty of compensation for covered loss. The insured pays premium and receives financial protection.

### 36.2 Protection

Insurance protects against the financial effect of uncertain events. It cannot prevent the event, but it can compensate the loss.

### 36.3 Risk Sharing

Loss of one person is shared by many insured persons through premium pooling.

### 36.4 Assist in Capital Formation

Insurance companies collect premiums and invest accumulated funds in income-generating schemes. This supports capital formation in the economy.

## 37. Principles of Insurance

The main principles are:

1. Utmost good faith.
2. Insurable interest.
3. Indemnity.
4. Proximate cause.
5. Subrogation.
6. Contribution.
7. Mitigation.

## 38. Utmost Good Faith

Insurance is a contract of utmost good faith. Both insurer and insured must disclose all material facts honestly.

### 38.1 Example

In life insurance, the proposer must disclose age, health condition, smoking habits, and medical history. If material facts are hidden, the insurer may reject the claim or treat the contract as voidable according to policy and law.

## 39. Insurable Interest

The insured must have a financial interest in the subject matter of insurance. The insured should suffer financial loss if the insured event occurs.

### 39.1 Examples

1. A person has insurable interest in their own life.
2. A shopkeeper has insurable interest in shop stock.
3. A lender has insurable interest in the life of a borrower to the extent of debt.
4. A mortgagee has insurable interest in mortgaged property.

## 40. Indemnity

Indemnity means the insurer restores the insured to the same financial position as before the loss, as far as money can do.

This principle applies mainly to fire and marine insurance.

It does not apply to life insurance because human life cannot be measured in money.

## 41. Proximate Cause

Proximate cause means the nearest, dominant, and most effective cause of loss.

If loss results from many causes, the insurer pays only if the proximate cause is covered by the policy.

### 41.1 Example

If goods are damaged mainly because of a peril covered by the policy, the claim may be payable. If the dominant cause is excluded, the claim may be rejected.

## 42. Subrogation

After paying the claim, the insurer gets the right to stand in place of the insured and recover from third parties or damaged property.

### 42.1 Example

If insured goods are stolen and the insurer pays the claim, then later the stolen goods are recovered, the insurer may have rights over those recovered goods to prevent double benefit to the insured.

## 43. Contribution

Contribution applies when the same property is insured with more than one insurer. If one insurer pays the claim, it can ask other insurers to contribute proportionately.

The insured cannot recover more than the actual loss.

## 44. Mitigation

Mitigation means the insured must take reasonable steps to reduce loss.

### 44.1 Example

If goods in a warehouse catch fire, the owner must try to save goods and reduce damage instead of doing nothing just because insurance exists.

## 45. Types of Insurance

The main types studied are:

1. Life insurance.
2. Fire insurance.
3. Marine insurance.

The chapter also mentions other forms such as health insurance, motor vehicle insurance, burglary insurance, cattle insurance, crop insurance, and selected social security schemes.

## 46. Life Insurance

Life insurance is a contract in which the insurer, in consideration of premium, agrees to pay a specified amount on the death of the assured or on expiry of a certain period.

### 46.1 Purpose of Life Insurance

Life insurance provides:

1. Protection to family in case of premature death.
2. Financial support in old age or maturity.
3. Saving habit through regular premium.
4. Security to dependents.
5. Investment element in many plans.

### 46.2 Main Elements

1. Valid contract essentials must exist.
2. Utmost good faith is required.
3. Insurable interest must exist at the time of taking policy.
4. It is not a contract of indemnity.
5. The amount payable is fixed in advance.

## 47. Types of Life Insurance Policies

### 47.1 Whole Life Policy

The amount is payable after death of the assured. Premium may be paid for a fixed period or throughout life, depending on policy terms.

### 47.2 Endowment Life Assurance Policy

The insurer pays the assured sum on death or on reaching a specified age or period, whichever occurs earlier.

### 47.3 Joint Life Policy

This policy is taken by two or more persons. The amount is payable to the survivor or survivors on death of one assured person. It is often used by spouses or business partners.

### 47.4 Annuity Policy

The assured receives periodic payments after reaching a certain age. It is useful for retirement income.

### 47.5 Children's Endowment Policy

This policy is taken to meet future needs of children, such as education or marriage. The amount is payable when the child reaches a specified age.

## 48. Fire Insurance

Fire insurance is a contract in which the insurer agrees, in return for premium, to compensate loss or damage caused by fire during a specified period, up to the policy amount.

### 48.1 Conditions for Fire Claim

1. There must be actual loss.
2. Fire must be accidental and non-intentional.
3. Fire must be the proximate cause of loss.

### 48.2 Main Elements

1. Insurable interest must exist at the time of policy and at the time of loss.
2. Utmost good faith is required.
3. It is a contract of strict indemnity.
4. Compensation is limited to actual loss, subject to policy amount.

## 49. Marine Insurance

Marine insurance provides protection against loss caused by marine perils during sea transport.

Marine perils may include:

1. Collision of ship.
2. Fire at sea.
3. Capture by pirates.
4. Perils caused by sea conditions.
5. Damage or loss of cargo.
6. Non-payment of freight due to loss.

### 49.1 Types of Marine Insurance

1. Hull insurance.
2. Cargo insurance.
3. Freight insurance.

### 49.2 Hull Insurance

Hull insurance covers loss or damage to the ship.

### 49.3 Cargo Insurance

Cargo insurance covers goods being transported by ship.

### 49.4 Freight Insurance

Freight insurance covers loss of freight income if cargo is lost or damaged and freight is not payable.

### 49.5 Main Elements

1. It is a contract of indemnity.
2. Utmost good faith is required.
3. Insurable interest must exist at the time of loss.
4. Proximate cause applies.

## 50. Life, Fire, and Marine Insurance: Comparison

| Basis | Life insurance | Fire insurance | Marine insurance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject matter | Human life | Physical property or assets | Ship, cargo, or freight |
| Main element | Protection and investment | Protection | Protection |
| Insurable interest | Needed at policy start | Needed at policy start and at loss | Needed at time of loss |
| Duration | Usually long-term | Usually one year | Usually voyage-based or period-based |
| Indemnity | Not based on indemnity | Based on indemnity | Based on indemnity |
| Loss measurement | Life cannot be measured in money | Loss is measurable | Loss is measurable |
| Policy amount | Can be any agreed amount within rules | Cannot exceed property value | May be based on market value or agreed value |
| Event | Death or maturity is certain to occur eventually | Fire may or may not occur | Marine loss may or may not occur |

## 51. Other Insurance Forms

### 51.1 Health Insurance

Health insurance covers medical expenses for illness or injury according to policy terms.

### 51.2 Motor Vehicle Insurance

Motor insurance covers vehicle-related risks. It may include third-party liability and damage to the vehicle depending on policy type.

### 51.3 Burglary Insurance

### 51.6 Sports Insurance

Sports insurance covers selected risks connected with amateur sports, equipment, personal accident, and legal liability according to policy terms.

## 55. Transportation

Transportation means movement of goods and passengers from one place to another through rail, road, air, sea, pipelines, and other modes.

For business, transportation removes the hindrance of place.

```text
Production place != Consumption place
Transport connects them
```

### 55.1 Importance of Transportation

1. Moves raw materials to factories.
2. Moves finished goods to markets.
3. Connects producers and consumers.
4. Supports large-scale production.
5. Reduces regional shortages.
6. Helps agriculture and food distribution.
7. Supports domestic and international trade.
8. Saves time when speed is important.

### 55.2 Need for Transport Infrastructure

Business needs:

1. Good roads.
2. Efficient railways.
3. Modern ports.
4. Efficient airports.
5. Logistics systems.
6. Cold chain where needed.
7. Reliable freight handling.

Poor transport causes delay, damage, higher cost, and wastage.

## 56. Warehousing

Warehousing means scientific and systematic storage of goods so that their quality, value, and usefulness are maintained until they are needed.

Modern warehouses are not just storage places. They are logistics service centers that help make goods available in the right quantity, at the right place, at the right time, in the right condition, and at the right cost.

## 57. Users of Warehouses

Warehouses are used by:

1. Manufacturers.
2. Importers.
3. Exporters.
4. Wholesalers.
5. Retail chains.
6. Transport businesses.
7. Customs authorities.
8. Agricultural marketing agencies.

## 58. Types of Warehouses

### 58.1 Private Warehouses

Private warehouses are owned, operated, or leased by companies for storing their own goods.

Benefits:

1. Better control.
2. Flexibility.
3. Better dealer relations.
4. Efficient product movement.
5. Suitable for large firms.

### 58.2 Public Warehouses

Public warehouses provide storage facilities to traders, manufacturers, or the public for a fee.

Benefits:

1. Useful for small manufacturers.
2. No need to build own warehouse.
3. Flexible location choice.
4. No fixed warehouse ownership cost.
5. May provide packaging and labelling.

### 58.3 Bonded Warehouses

Bonded warehouses are licensed by government to store imported goods before customs duty is paid.

Benefits:

1. Importer need not pay full duty immediately.
2. Goods can be removed in parts.
3. Duty can be paid in instalments as goods are removed.
4. Goods can be inspected, graded, branded, packaged, or blended.
5. Supports entrepot trade.

### 58.4 Government Warehouses

Government warehouses are owned and managed by government or public sector organisations.

Examples include warehouses managed by public agencies for food grains, trading, or central warehousing functions.

### 58.5 Cooperative Warehouses

Cooperative warehouses are set up by cooperative societies for their members, especially in agriculture and marketing.

## 59. Functions of Warehousing

### 59.1 Consolidation

Warehouse receives goods from different plants and combines them into one shipment for a customer.

### 59.2 Break Bulk

Warehouse receives goods in large quantities and divides them into smaller lots according to customer requirement.

### 59.3 Stock Piling

Warehouse stores seasonal goods or raw materials until needed.

Examples:

1. Agricultural produce harvested once but consumed throughout the year.
2. Raw materials bought in bulk but used gradually.

### 59.4 Value Added Services

Warehouses may provide:

1. Packaging.
2. Labelling.
3. Grading.
4. Sorting.
5. Mixing.
6. Repacking.
7. Inspection support.

### 59.5 Price Stabilisation

Warehousing helps adjust supply according to demand. When supply is high and demand is low, goods can be stored. When demand rises, goods can be released.

This helps reduce sharp price fluctuations.

### 59.6 Financing

Warehouse owners or financial institutions may advance money against goods stored in warehouses. Goods can serve as security.

## 60. Business Services as a System

Business services do not work separately. They support one another.

Example:

```text
Factory produces goods
-> Bank finances production
-> Insurance covers factory and stock
-> Transport moves goods
-> Warehouse stores goods
-> Communication connects buyer and seller
-> Bank settles payment
```

This is why business services are called auxiliaries to trade in commerce.

## 62. Long Answer Practice

### 62.1 What are services? Explain their characteristics.

Services are intangible economic activities that provide satisfaction of wants and usually involve interaction between provider and consumer.

Their five characteristics are:

1. Intangibility: Services cannot be touched; they are experienced.
2. Inconsistency: Services may vary by customer, provider, and situation.
3. Inseparability: Production and consumption often occur together.
4. Inventory-less nature: Services cannot usually be stored.
5. Involvement: Customer participation is often required.

### 62.2 Explain functions of commercial banks.

Commercial banks perform the following functions:

1. Accept deposits through current, savings, and fixed deposit accounts.
2. Lend funds through overdraft, cash credit, bill discounting, term loans, and consumer credit.
3. Provide cheque facility through bearer and crossed cheques.
4. Remit funds using drafts, pay orders, and electronic transfers.
5. Provide allied services such as locker facility, bill payment, underwriting, securities purchase and sale, insurance premium payment, and dividend collection.

### 62.3 Write a note on postal facilities.

Postal services support business communication and financial transactions. They provide financial facilities such as savings schemes, recurring deposits, time deposits, PPF, certificates, and money transfer services. Mail facilities include parcel, registered post, insured articles, and speed delivery. Other facilities include greeting cards, postal advertising, direct mail, international money transfer, passport application support, and bill collection.

### 62.4 Describe types of insurance and risks covered.

Life insurance covers risk connected with human life, such as premature death or old-age financial need. It provides protection and often an investment element.

Fire insurance covers loss or damage to property caused by accidental fire, subject to policy conditions.

Marine insurance covers loss to ship, cargo, or freight due to marine perils such as sea risks, collision, fire at sea, or loss in transit.

Other insurance forms include health insurance, motor vehicle insurance, burglary insurance, cattle insurance, crop insurance, and sports insurance.

### 62.5 Explain warehousing services in detail.

Warehousing means storage of goods in a scientific and systematic manner. Modern warehouses also provide logistics services. Types include private, public, bonded, government, and cooperative warehouses. Their functions include consolidation, break bulk, stock piling, packaging, labelling, grading, sorting, price stabilisation, and financing against stored goods.

## 65. Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct understanding |
|---|---|
| Services can be stored like goods | Services are usually perishable and cannot be stocked |
| E-banking means only ATM | E-banking includes ATM, internet banking, mobile banking, cards, and fund transfer |
| Insurance prevents loss | Insurance compensates financial loss; it does not prevent the event |
| Life insurance is indemnity | Life insurance is not indemnity because life cannot be measured in money |
| Insurable interest means ownership only | A person can have insurable interest without ownership, such as a mortgagee |
| Public warehouse is government warehouse | Public warehouse is open to public for fee; government warehouse is owned by government |
| Transport removes time hindrance | Transport removes place hindrance; warehousing removes time hindrance |
| Premium is claim amount | Premium is paid by insured; claim is demanded after loss |

## 68. End Summary

Business services are essential support services that allow business to function smoothly. Services are intangible, variable, inseparable from delivery, difficult to store, and often require customer involvement. Banking provides deposits, loans, payments, remittance, and allied services. E-banking uses electronic media for faster and more convenient banking. Insurance spreads risk and protects against financial loss through principles such as utmost good faith, insurable interest, indemnity, proximate cause, subrogation, contribution, and mitigation. Communication services connect business with the outside world. Transportation moves goods and removes the hindrance of place. Warehousing stores goods, supports logistics, stabilises supply, and removes the hindrance of time.
