# Chapter 2: Forms of Business Organisation

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

This chapter explains the main forms in which a business can be owned, controlled, financed, managed, and legally organized. A business idea can be carried out by one person, a family, a group of partners, a cooperative group, or a company with many shareholders. Each form has its own benefits, limits, legal position, risk pattern, capital capacity, and management style.

The central question of the chapter is simple:

```text
Which form of business organisation is suitable for a particular business situation?
```

There is no single best form for every business. A small personal service shop may be best as a sole proprietorship, while a large manufacturing unit may need a company. A farming group may choose a cooperative society, and professional service providers may prefer partnership.

## 2. Learning Targets

By the end of this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. Identify the major forms of business organisation.
2. Explain sole proprietorship, joint Hindu family business, partnership, cooperative society, and joint stock company.
3. Describe the features, merits, and limitations of each form.
4. Compare the different forms on capital, control, liability, continuity, and legal formalities.
5. Explain types of partners and types of partnerships.
6. Explain partnership deed and registration of partnership firms.
7. Explain types of cooperative societies.
8. Distinguish between private and public companies.
9. Choose a suitable form of organisation for a given business case.

## 3. Meaning of Form of Business Organisation

A form of business organisation means the legal and ownership structure in which a business is started and run.

It answers questions such as:

1. Who owns the business?
2. Who manages the business?
3. Who provides capital?
4. Who receives profit?
5. Who bears loss?
6. Who is liable for business debts?
7. Does the business have a separate legal identity?
8. Will the business continue after death or retirement of the owner?
9. How much legal work is needed to form and close it?

## 4. Major Forms of Business Organisation

The major forms studied in this chapter are:

1. Sole proprietorship.

4. Cooperative society.

## 5. Why Choice of Form Matters

Choosing the form of organisation is one of the earliest and most important business decisions. A poor choice can create problems in finance, control, risk, taxation, legal compliance, continuity, and growth.

### 5.1 Example

A person selling decorated pottery from home may begin as a sole proprietor because:

1. Capital need is small.
2. Legal formalities are few.
3. Personal skill matters.
4. Direct control is useful.
5. The scale is small.

But if demand increases, the same person may need:

1. A partner for more capital and skill.
2. A cooperative model if many artisans work together.
3. A company if the business expands nationwide and needs large funds.

The form must match the business need.

## 6. Sole Proprietorship

Sole proprietorship is a form of business owned, managed, controlled, and financed by one individual who receives all profits and bears all risks.

The word "sole" means only, and "proprietor" means owner. Therefore, a sole proprietor is the only owner of the business.

### 6.1 Simple Definition

Sole proprietorship is a one-owner business.

### 6.2 Complete Definition

Sole proprietorship is a form of business organisation in which one individual owns, manages, controls, finances, and carries on the business, receives all profits, and bears all losses and risks.

### 6.3 Common Examples

1. Small grocery shop.
2. Stationery store.
3. Tailoring shop.
4. Beauty parlour.
5. Local bakery.
6. Small repair shop.
7. Street food stall.
8. Freelance craft business.
9. Local coaching service run by one person.

## 7. Features of Sole Proprietorship

### 7.1 Easy Formation and Closure

There is no separate special law that creates a sole proprietorship. It can be started with minimum legal formalities, though licenses or registrations may be required for some businesses.

Closure is also relatively easy because the owner alone takes the decision.

### 7.2 Unlimited Liability

Unlimited liability means the owner is personally responsible for business debts. If business assets are not enough to repay creditors, the owner's personal assets may be used.

Example:

If a sole proprietorship has debts of Rs. 80,000 and business assets of Rs. 50,000, the owner must arrange the remaining Rs. 30,000 personally.

### 7.3 Sole Risk Bearer and Profit Recipient

The sole proprietor bears all losses alone. If the business succeeds, the proprietor receives all profit.

This creates direct incentive as well as heavy personal risk.

### 7.4 Full Control

All decisions are made by the owner. The proprietor can decide quickly because there is no need to take approval from partners, directors, or members.

### 7.5 No Separate Legal Entity

The owner and the business are treated as one in the eyes of law. The business does not have a legal identity separate from the proprietor.

### 7.6 Lack of Business Continuity

Death, illness, insolvency, imprisonment, or incapacity of the proprietor can directly affect the business and may even lead to closure.

## 8. Merits of Sole Proprietorship

### 8.1 Quick Decision-Making

The owner can make decisions immediately. This helps the business take advantage of market opportunities.

Example:

If customers suddenly demand a new product, the sole proprietor can start stocking it without waiting for group approval.

### 8.2 Confidentiality

The proprietor can keep business information private. There is usually no requirement to publish accounts publicly.

### 8.3 Direct Incentive

Since the owner receives all profit, there is a strong motivation to work hard and control costs.

### 8.4 Sense of Accomplishment

Running one's own business gives personal satisfaction, confidence, and pride.

### 8.5 Easy Formation and Closure

It is easy and inexpensive to start and close compared with more formal organisations.

### 8.6 Personal Contact with Customers

Small sole proprietorship businesses often build strong customer relationships because the owner directly interacts with customers.

## 9. Limitations of Sole Proprietorship

### 9.1 Limited Resources

Capital is usually limited to the owner's savings and borrowing capacity. Banks may hesitate to provide large long-term loans.

### 9.2 Limited Life

The life of the business depends heavily on the life, health, and financial condition of the owner.

### 9.3 Unlimited Liability

The owner may lose personal property if business debts cannot be paid from business assets.

### 9.4 Limited Managerial Ability

One person may not be expert in production, finance, marketing, purchase, selling, human resources, and law. This can weaken decision-making.

### 9.5 Limited Growth

Because capital and managerial resources are limited, the business may remain small.

## 10. Suitability of Sole Proprietorship

Sole proprietorship is suitable when:

1. Business is small.
2. Capital need is low.
3. Personal attention is important.
4. Risk is limited.
5. Legal formalities should be minimum.
6. Quick decisions are important.
7. Customers expect direct service.

Examples:

1. Small retail shops.
2. Local service providers.
3. Small craft units.
4. Personal skill-based businesses.

## 11. Joint Hindu Family Business

Joint Hindu family business is a form of business owned and carried on by members of a Hindu Undivided Family. It is governed by Hindu law and is one of the oldest forms of business organisation in India.

### 11.1 Basic Idea

Membership in this form arises by birth in the family. The business is usually based on ancestral property. The head of the family manages the business and is called the karta.

### 11.2 Important Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Hindu Undivided Family | A family unit recognised under Hindu law |
| Karta | Head of the family business who manages it |
| Coparceners | Members who have a right in ancestral property |
| Ancestral property | Property inherited from earlier generations |

### 11.3 Gender Equality Note

After the Hindu Succession Amendment Act, 2005, daughters also became coparceners by birth. At the time of partition, coparcenary property is divided without gender discrimination. The eldest member can become karta according to applicable law and interpretation.

## 12. Features of Joint Hindu Family Business

### 12.1 Formation

There should be at least two members in the family and ancestral property to be inherited. No formal agreement is required because membership arises by birth.

### 12.2 Liability

The liability of all members except the karta is limited to their share in the family business property. The karta has unlimited liability.

### 12.3 Control

The karta controls and manages the business. The karta's decisions are generally binding on other members.

### 12.4 Continuity

The business can continue even after the death of the karta because the next eldest eligible member can become karta.

### 12.5 Minor Members

Minors can be members because membership arises by birth, not by contract.

## 13. Merits of Joint Hindu Family Business

### 13.1 Effective Control

The karta has clear authority, so decisions can be quick and conflicts in decision-making may be reduced.

### 13.2 Continued Existence

Death of the karta does not necessarily close the business. The next eligible member can take over.

### 13.3 Limited Liability of Members

Except for the karta, members have liability limited to their share in the family business property.

### 13.4 Loyalty and Cooperation

Family members may show strong loyalty because the growth of the business is connected with family prestige and welfare.

## 14. Limitations of Joint Hindu Family Business

### 14.1 Limited Resources

Capital mainly comes from ancestral property, so expansion may be limited.

### 14.2 Unlimited Liability of Karta

The karta bears unlimited liability, which is a heavy burden.

### 14.3 Dominance of Karta

Other members may feel ignored if the karta makes decisions alone. This can create family conflict.

### 14.4 Limited Managerial Skill

The karta may not be expert in all areas of management. Poor decisions can harm the business.

### 14.5 Declining Relevance

With the decline of traditional joint families, this form has become less common than before.

## 15. Partnership

Partnership is a form of business organisation in which two or more persons agree to carry on a lawful business and share its profits.

Partnership became important because sole proprietorship often faces problems of limited capital, limited skill, and heavy individual risk.

### 15.1 Definition

Partnership is the relation between persons who have agreed to share the profits of a business carried on by all or any one of them acting for all.

### 15.2 Key Meaning

Partnership involves:

1. Agreement.
2. Lawful business.
3. Profit motive.
4. Sharing of profits.
5. Mutual agency.

## 16. Features of Partnership

### 16.1 Formation by Agreement

Partnership arises from an agreement among partners. The agreement may be oral or written, but a written agreement is preferred.

The business must be lawful and must be carried on for profit. A group formed for charity is not a partnership.

### 16.2 Number of Partners

There must be at least two partners. The maximum number is subject to law. In general, the Companies Act restricts the number of partners in a firm to 50 unless another law provides otherwise.

### 16.3 Unlimited Liability

Partners usually have unlimited liability. Their personal assets may be used to pay firm debts if business assets are insufficient.

### 16.4 Joint and Several Liability

Partners are jointly and individually liable for firm debts.

This means:

1. Creditors can claim from all partners together.
2. Creditors can also claim the full amount from any one partner.
3. The partner who pays more than their share may recover from other partners according to the agreement.

### 16.5 Risk Sharing

Partners share losses and risks according to the agreed ratio.

### 16.6 Decision-Making and Control

Partners usually share management and decision-making. Decisions are often taken by mutual consent.

### 16.7 Continuity

Partnership may be affected by death, insolvency, retirement, or incapacity of a partner unless the agreement provides otherwise.

### 16.8 Mutual Agency

Mutual agency means every partner is both an agent and a principal.

As an agent, a partner can bind the firm and other partners by actions done in the ordinary course of business.

As a principal, a partner is bound by actions of other partners done for the firm.

Mutual agency is the true test of partnership.

## 17. Merits of Partnership

### 17.1 Easy Formation and Closure

Partnership can be formed with fewer legal formalities compared with a company. Closure is also simpler than winding up a company.

### 17.2 Larger Resources

Capital can be contributed by several partners, so the firm may have more funds than a sole proprietorship.

### 17.3 Balanced Decision-Making

Partners may bring different skills and experience. Combined judgment can improve decisions.

### 17.4 Sharing of Risk

Losses are shared among partners, reducing the burden on one person.

### 17.5 Secrecy

Partnership firms are not required to publish accounts publicly in the way companies may be required to disclose information.

### 17.6 Flexibility

Partners can change business operations by mutual agreement. This gives flexibility.

## 18. Limitations of Partnership

### 18.1 Unlimited Liability

Partners may lose personal assets if firm assets are not enough to repay debts.

### 18.2 Limited Resources

Although resources are greater than sole proprietorship, they are still limited compared with a company.

### 18.3 Possibility of Conflict

Differences of opinion among partners may delay decisions or damage the business.

### 18.4 Lack of Continuity

Death, insolvency, retirement, or incapacity of a partner may affect continuity unless the deed provides for continuation.

### 18.5 Lack of Public Confidence

Since accounts are not usually published, outsiders may have less confidence in the firm's financial position.

### 18.6 Risk of Wrong Action by One Partner

Because of mutual agency, one partner's action in the ordinary course of business can bind all partners.

## 19. Types of Partners

### 19.1 Active Partner

An active partner contributes capital, participates in management, shares profit and loss, and has unlimited liability.

### 19.2 Sleeping or Dormant Partner

A sleeping partner contributes capital and shares profit and loss but does not take active part in management. Liability is still usually unlimited.

### 19.3 Secret Partner

A secret partner participates in the business but does not let outsiders know about the partnership status. Liability is unlimited.

### 19.4 Nominal Partner

A nominal partner allows the firm to use their name or reputation but may not contribute capital or share profit. Such a person can still be liable to outsiders who relied on that representation.

### 19.5 Partner by Estoppel

A partner by estoppel is a person who is not actually a partner but behaves or represents in such a way that others believe the person is a partner. If outsiders give credit to the firm on that belief, the person may be held liable.

### 19.6 Partner by Holding Out

A partner by holding out is a person who knowingly allows others to represent them as a partner and does not deny it. If creditors rely on this representation, the person becomes liable to them.

### 19.7 Minor Admitted to Benefits

A minor cannot become a full partner because partnership is based on contract and a minor cannot enter into a valid contract.

However, a minor may be admitted to the benefits of partnership with the consent of all partners.

Rules:

1. The minor can share profits.
2. The minor is not personally liable for losses.
3. Liability is limited to the minor's share in the firm.
4. The minor cannot actively manage the firm.
5. The minor can inspect firm accounts.
6. After attaining majority, the minor must decide whether to become a partner.

## 20. Partner Type Summary

| Type of partner | Capital contribution | Management role | Profit or loss | Liability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Active partner | Yes | Active | Shares profit and loss | Unlimited |
| Sleeping partner | Yes | Not active | Shares profit and loss | Unlimited |
| Secret partner | Yes | May be active secretly | Shares profit and loss | Unlimited |
| Nominal partner | Usually no | No active role | Usually no share | Liable to outsiders in some cases |
| Partner by estoppel | No | No | No | Liable if representation is relied on |
| Partner by holding out | No | No | No | Liable if representation is not denied |
| Minor admitted to benefits | May contribute | No active role | Shares profit only | Limited to share in firm |

## 21. Types of Partnership

Partnerships can be classified on two bases:

1. Duration.
2. Liability.

## 22. Classification by Duration

### 22.1 Partnership at Will

Partnership at will continues as long as partners wish. It can be ended when any partner gives notice of withdrawal.

### 22.2 Particular Partnership

A particular partnership is formed for a specific venture or for a specific period. It ends when the purpose is completed or the period expires.

Example:

Two contractors form a partnership to construct one building. After completion, the partnership ends.

## 23. Classification by Liability

### 23.1 General Partnership

In a general partnership:

1. Liability of partners is unlimited.
2. Partners have the right to participate in management.
3. Acts of partners bind the firm and other partners.
4. Registration is optional.
5. Firm existence may be affected by death, insolvency, retirement, or incapacity of partners.

### 23.2 Limited Partnership

In a limited partnership:

1. At least one partner has unlimited liability.
2. Other partners may have limited liability.
3. Limited partners usually do not participate in management.
4. Acts of limited partners do not bind the firm in the same way as active general partners.
5. Registration is compulsory.
6. Death, insolvency, or incapacity of limited partners does not necessarily end the firm.

## 24. Partnership Deed

A partnership deed is a written agreement that contains the terms and conditions governing the partnership.

Although an oral agreement can create a partnership, a written deed is better because it reduces misunderstanding and acts as evidence.

### 24.1 Contents of Partnership Deed

A partnership deed generally includes:

1. Name of the firm.
2. Nature of business.
3. Location of business.
4. Duration of partnership.
5. Capital contributed by each partner.
6. Profit and loss sharing ratio.
7. Duties of partners.
8. Rights of partners.
9. Salaries or commission to partners.
10. Drawings allowed to partners.
11. Interest on capital.
12. Interest on drawings.
13. Admission of a new partner.
14. Retirement of a partner.
15. Expulsion of a partner.
16. Death or insolvency of a partner.
17. Maintenance of accounts.
18. Audit of accounts.
19. Settlement of disputes.
20. Procedure for dissolution.

### 24.2 Why Partnership Deed Is Important

The deed is important because it:

1. Defines rights and duties clearly.
2. Reduces conflict.
3. Provides evidence of agreed terms.
4. Helps settle disputes.
5. Guides profit distribution.
6. Explains how partners may enter or leave.

## 25. Registration of Partnership Firm

Registration means entering the firm's name and other details in the Register of Firms kept by the Registrar of Firms.

Registration gives legal evidence of the firm's existence.

### 25.1 Is Registration Compulsory?

For a general partnership, registration is optional. However, it is strongly advisable because an unregistered firm suffers serious legal disadvantages.

### 25.2 Consequences of Non-Registration

If a firm is not registered:

1. A partner cannot sue the firm or other partners to enforce rights under the partnership agreement.
2. The firm cannot sue third parties to enforce contractual rights.
3. The firm cannot sue partners to enforce rights under the agreement.

Because of these disadvantages, firms usually prefer registration.

### 25.3 Registration Procedure

The usual procedure is:

1. Submit application to the Registrar of Firms.
2. Provide firm name, location, other business places, partner names, partner addresses, joining dates, and duration.
3. Get the application signed by all partners.
4. Deposit required fees.
5. Registrar examines and approves the application.
6. Registrar enters the firm in the register.
7. Certificate of registration is issued.

## 26. Cooperative Society

A cooperative society is a voluntary association of persons who join together to protect their common economic interests through mutual help and democratic control.

The main motive is service to members, not profit maximisation.

### 26.1 Basic Idea

People with limited means may face exploitation by middlemen, moneylenders, big traders, landlords, or large businesses. By forming a cooperative society, they pool resources and improve bargaining power.

### 26.2 Registration

A cooperative society must be registered under the relevant cooperative law. After registration, it gets separate legal identity.

In India:

1. Societies operating within one state are governed by the state's cooperative law.
2. Societies operating in more than one state are governed by multi-state cooperative law.

## 27. Features of Cooperative Society

### 27.1 Voluntary Membership

Membership is voluntary. A person may join or leave according to the rules of the society.

Membership is open without discrimination on religion, caste, or gender.

### 27.2 Separate Legal Status

After registration, the society becomes a separate legal entity. It can own property, enter contracts, sue, and be sued in its own name.

### 27.3 Limited Liability

Members' liability is limited to the amount contributed by them as capital.

### 27.4 Democratic Control

Members elect a managing committee. The principle is generally one member, one vote, regardless of capital contribution.

### 27.5 Service Motive

The main motive is welfare of members. If surplus arises, it may be distributed according to the by-laws.

## 28. Merits of Cooperative Society

### 28.1 Equality in Voting

Each member has one vote. This supports democratic control.

### 28.2 Limited Liability

Personal assets of members are safe beyond their capital contribution.

### 28.3 Stable Existence

Death, insolvency, or exit of a member does not end the society.

### 28.4 Economy in Operations

Members often provide service voluntarily, and middlemen may be eliminated. This reduces cost.

### 28.5 Government Support

Cooperatives may receive support such as subsidies, low-interest loans, tax concessions, or training assistance.

### 28.6 Ease of Formation

Compared with a company, forming a cooperative society is simpler, though registration is compulsory.

## 29. Limitations of Cooperative Society

### 29.1 Limited Resources

Members may have limited means, and low dividend may make it difficult to attract large capital.

### 29.2 Inefficient Management

Cooperatives may not be able to hire highly paid expert managers. Members may lack professional management skill.

### 29.3 Lack of Secrecy

Open member meetings and legal reporting requirements can reduce secrecy.

### 29.4 Government Control

Because cooperatives receive state support and are regulated, they may face government interference.

### 29.5 Differences of Opinion

Member disagreements may delay decisions. Personal interest may sometimes dominate group welfare.

## 30. Types of Cooperative Societies

### 30.1 Consumers' Cooperative Society

Formed to protect consumers by providing good quality goods at reasonable prices.

Functions:

1. Buys goods in bulk.
2. Eliminates middlemen.
3. Sells to members.
4. Distributes surplus according to rules.

### 30.2 Producers' Cooperative Society

Formed to protect small producers.

Functions:

1. Provides raw materials.
2. Supplies tools and equipment.
3. Improves bargaining power.
4. Buys output from members for sale.

### 30.3 Marketing Cooperative Society

Formed to help small producers sell their products at better prices.

Functions:

1. Pools output of members.
2. Performs grading, packing, transport, and storage.
3. Sells in larger markets.
4. Distributes surplus based on contribution.

### 30.4 Farmers' Cooperative Society

Formed to help farmers obtain better inputs and benefits of large-scale farming.

Functions:

1. Provides seeds.
2. Provides fertilisers.
3. Provides machinery.
4. Supports modern farming methods.
5. Helps fragmented landholders work more efficiently.

### 30.5 Credit Cooperative Society

Formed to provide loans to members at reasonable interest rates.

Purpose:

1. Protect members from exploitative lenders.
2. Provide easy credit.
3. Encourage savings.
4. Use member deposits and capital for lending.

### 30.6 Cooperative Housing Society

Formed to help members obtain houses or plots at reasonable cost.

Functions:

1. Acquires land.
2. Constructs flats.
3. Provides plots.
4. Allows payment in instalments.
5. Reduces housing cost for members.

## 31. Cooperative Society Type Summary

| Type | Main members | Main purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Consumers' cooperative | Consumers | Goods at reasonable prices |
| Producers' cooperative | Small producers | Inputs and bargaining power |
| Marketing cooperative | Producers | Better selling price |
| Farmers' cooperative | Farmers | Better inputs and large-scale farming benefits |
| Credit cooperative | Borrowers and savers | Reasonable credit |
| Housing cooperative | People needing housing | Affordable homes or plots |

## 32. Joint Stock Company

A joint stock company is an association of persons formed for business, incorporated under company law, with a separate legal entity, limited liability, and capital divided into shares.

### 32.1 Simple Meaning

A company is an artificial person created by law.

It is not a human being, but law gives it a legal identity. It can own property, enter contracts, borrow money, sue, and be sued.

### 32.2 Owners and Managers

Shareholders are the owners of the company. They contribute capital by buying shares.

The Board of Directors manages and controls the company. Directors are elected by shareholders.

### 32.3 Share Capital

The capital of a company is divided into smaller units called shares.

Example:

If a company has capital of Rs. 10,00,000 divided into shares of Rs. 10 each, it has 1,00,000 shares.

## 33. Features of Joint Stock Company

### 33.1 Artificial Person

A company is created by law and has legal rights and duties. It can own property, borrow money, enter contracts, and file legal claims.

However, it is not a natural person. It cannot breathe, eat, or act physically. Human agents act on its behalf.

### 33.2 Separate Legal Entity

A company is separate from its members. Its assets and liabilities are separate from shareholder assets and liabilities.

Example:

If a company owns a building, shareholders do not personally own that building.

### 33.3 Formation

Company formation is more complex than sole proprietorship or partnership. It requires legal documents, registration, and compliance with company law.

### 33.4 Perpetual Succession

A company continues even if shareholders die, retire, become insolvent, or transfer shares. It ends only through legal winding-up or closure procedure.

### 33.5 Control through Board of Directors

Shareholders elect directors. Directors appoint managers and control company affairs. Shareholders do not usually manage day-to-day operations.

### 33.6 Limited Liability

Shareholders' liability is limited to unpaid amount on shares held by them.

Example:

If a shareholder holds 2,000 shares of Rs. 10 each and has paid Rs. 7 per share, the unpaid amount is Rs. 3 per share. The maximum liability is:

```text
2,000 shares x Rs. 3 = Rs. 6,000
```

Beyond this, the shareholder is not personally liable for company debts.

### 33.7 Transferability of Shares

Shares of a public company can generally be transferred freely. In a private company, transfer is restricted.

### 33.8 Common Seal

A common seal traditionally represented the official signature of a company. Under modern company law, its use may be optional, but the idea remains that company acts must be authenticated through legally recognised methods.

## 34. Merits of Joint Stock Company

### 34.1 Limited Liability

Shareholders risk only the amount unpaid on their shares. Personal property is usually safe from company creditors.

### 34.2 Transfer of Interest

Shares of a public company can be sold, giving investors liquidity.

### 34.3 Perpetual Existence

The company continues despite changes in membership.

### 34.4 Scope for Expansion

A company can raise large capital by issuing shares, debentures, and borrowing from institutions.

### 34.5 Professional Management

Companies can hire specialists in finance, marketing, production, law, technology, and human resources.

### 34.6 Risk Spread

Risk is spread among many shareholders instead of being concentrated in one or few persons.

## 35. Limitations of Joint Stock Company

### 35.1 Complexity in Formation

Formation requires documents, registration, legal compliance, and time.

### 35.2 Lack of Secrecy

Companies, especially public companies, must disclose many details to regulators and shareholders.

### 35.3 Impersonal Work Environment

Large size and separation of ownership from management can reduce personal contact with employees, customers, and owners.

### 35.4 Numerous Regulations

Companies must follow rules about audit, reports, meetings, voting, filings, securities, and governance.

### 35.5 Delay in Decision-Making

Decisions may move through several levels: board, top management, middle management, and lower management.

### 35.6 Oligarchic Management

Although shareholders are owners, actual control may lie with a small group of directors or top managers because many shareholders do not attend meetings or participate actively.

### 35.7 Conflict of Interest

Different stakeholders may want different things:

1. Shareholders want higher returns.
2. Employees want better pay.
3. Customers want lower prices and better quality.
4. Managers want authority and resources.
5. Government wants legal compliance and tax.

Balancing these interests is difficult.

## 36. Types of Companies

The chapter mainly compares:

1. Private company.
2. Public company.

## 37. Private Company

A private company is a company that restricts transfer of shares, has a limited number of members, and does not invite the public to subscribe to its securities.

### 37.1 Features of Private Company

1. Minimum members: 2.
2. Maximum members: 200, excluding present and past employees in certain cases.
3. Restricts transfer of shares.
4. Cannot invite the public to subscribe to securities.
5. Must use the words "Private Limited" after its name.

### 37.2 Privileges of Private Company

Compared with a public company, a private company enjoys some privileges:

1. It can be formed with only two members.
2. It need not issue a prospectus because public subscription is not invited.
3. It can allot shares without receiving minimum public subscription.
4. It can start business after incorporation.
5. It needs at least two directors, while a public company needs at least three.
6. It is not required to maintain an index of members in the same way as a public company.

## 38. Public Company

A public company is a company that is not a private company and can invite the public to subscribe to its securities.

### 38.1 Features of Public Company

1. Minimum members: 7.
2. Maximum members: No limit.
3. Minimum directors: 3.
4. Shares are generally freely transferable.
5. It can invite the public to subscribe to shares or debentures.
6. It must follow more detailed legal requirements.

### 38.2 Subsidiary Rule

A private company that is a subsidiary of a public company may be treated as a public company for certain legal purposes.

## 39. Private Company and Public Company: Comparison

| Basis | Private company | Public company |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum members | 2 | 7 |
| Maximum members | 200 | No limit |
| Minimum directors | 2 | 3 |
| Transfer of shares | Restricted | Generally free |
| Public invitation | Cannot invite public to subscribe | Can invite public to subscribe |
| Index of members | Not compulsory in the same way | Compulsory |
| Name ending | Private Limited | Limited |
| Suitability | Closely held businesses | Large capital and wider ownership |

| Continuity | Weak | Relatively stable | May be affected by partner changes | Stable | Very stable |

## 41. Choice of Form of Organisation

Choosing the correct form depends on several factors. The factors are interrelated. A decision should be made after considering all relevant factors together.

## 42. Factors Affecting Choice

### 42.1 Cost and Ease of Formation

Sole proprietorship is the easiest and least expensive to start. Partnership is also relatively easy. Cooperative society and company require registration. Company formation is the most complex and expensive.

Best for low formation cost:

```text
Sole proprietorship
```

Least convenient for low formation cost:

```text
Company
```

### 42.2 Liability

If owners want limited liability, cooperative society or company is better. Sole proprietorship and partnership involve unlimited liability.

From investor safety point of view:

```text
Company is usually more suitable than sole proprietorship.
```

### 42.3 Continuity

If the business needs long-term permanent existence, company is highly suitable because it has perpetual succession.

Sole proprietorship has weak continuity because owner and business are not separate.

### 42.4 Management Ability

If business operations are simple, sole proprietorship may work. If specialized management is needed, partnership or company may be better.

Company is most suitable for complex operations requiring professional management.

### 42.5 Capital Requirement

If capital requirement is small, sole proprietorship or partnership may be enough.

If capital requirement is large, company is more suitable because it can raise funds from many investors.

### 42.6 Degree of Control

If the owner wants complete control, sole proprietorship is best.

If the owner is willing to share control, partnership or company may be chosen.

In a company, ownership and management are separated.

### 42.7 Nature of Business

The form should match the nature of business.

| Nature of business | Suitable form |
|---|---|
| Small personal service | Sole proprietorship |
| Professional service by experts | Partnership |
| Family-based inherited business | Joint Hindu family business |
| Member welfare | Cooperative society |
| Large manufacturing or national expansion | Company |

### 42.8 Flexibility

Sole proprietorship is most flexible because one person decides. Company is least flexible because of legal rules and organizational layers.

### 42.9 Transfer of Ownership

Transfer is easiest in a public company because shares can be transferred. It is difficult in partnership because a new partner usually needs consent of other partners.

## 45. Case Analysis: Creditors and Unlimited Liability

Suppose business assets are Rs. 50,000 and debts are Rs. 80,000. The unpaid amount after using business assets is:

```text
Rs. 80,000 - Rs. 50,000 = Rs. 30,000
```

### 45.1 If It Is Sole Proprietorship

The sole proprietor has unlimited liability. Creditors can recover Rs. 50,000 from business assets and Rs. 30,000 from the proprietor's personal assets.

### 45.2 If It Is Partnership

Partners have unlimited, joint, and several liability. Creditors can approach both partners together or any one partner individually for repayment. If one partner pays more than their share, that partner can later recover the excess from the other partner according to the agreement.

## 48. Short Answer Practice

### 48.1 Compare the status of a minor in joint Hindu family business and partnership.

In a joint Hindu family business, a minor can become a member by birth because membership is based on family status. In partnership, a minor cannot become a full partner because partnership is based on contract. A minor may only be admitted to the benefits of partnership with consent of all partners.

### 48.2 If partnership registration is optional, why do firms register?

Firms register because non-registration creates legal disadvantages. An unregistered firm cannot sue third parties to enforce contractual rights, and a partner cannot sue the firm or other partners to enforce rights under the partnership agreement. Registration gives stronger legal protection.

### 48.3 State important privileges of a private company.

A private company can be formed with only two members, need not invite public subscription, need not issue a prospectus, can allot shares without minimum public subscription, can start business after incorporation, and requires only two directors as minimum.

### 48.4 How does a cooperative society show democracy and secularism?

A cooperative society shows democracy because it generally follows one member, one vote. It shows secularism because membership is open to all without discrimination based on religion, caste, or gender.

### 48.5 What is partner by estoppel?

A partner by estoppel is a person who is not actually a partner but behaves in a way that makes others believe that they are a partner. If outsiders give credit to the firm on that belief, the person may be held liable.

### 48.6 Explain perpetual succession.

Perpetual succession means continuous existence. A company continues even if its members die, retire, become insolvent, or transfer shares.

### 48.7 Explain common seal.

Common seal traditionally meant the official signature or stamp of the company. It authenticated company documents where used. Modern legal practice may allow other forms of authorization.

### 48.8 Explain karta.

Karta is the head of a joint Hindu family business who manages and controls the business.

### 48.9 Explain artificial person.

A company is called an artificial person because it is created by law and has legal rights and duties, but it is not a natural human being.

## 49. Long Answer Practice

### 49.1 Explain sole proprietorship with merits and limitations.

Sole proprietorship is a form of business owned, managed, and controlled by one individual. The owner receives all profits and bears all losses and risks.

Merits include quick decision-making, confidentiality, direct incentive, personal satisfaction, ease of formation and closure, and close customer contact.

Limitations include limited resources, limited life, unlimited liability, limited managerial ability, and limited growth.

It is suitable for small businesses, local shops, personal service businesses, and businesses requiring direct owner-customer contact.

### 49.2 Explain merits and limitations of partnership.

Partnership is an agreement between two or more persons to carry on a lawful business and share profits.

Merits:

1. Easy formation and closure.
2. Larger resources than sole proprietorship.
3. Balanced decision-making through combined skill.
4. Sharing of risk.
5. Secrecy.
6. Flexibility.

Limitations:

1. Unlimited liability.
2. Limited resources compared with company.
3. Possibility of conflict.
4. Lack of continuity.
5. Lack of public confidence.
6. Risk of one partner binding all partners.

Partnership may be unpopular in some cases because unlimited liability and mutual agency create high personal risk.

### 49.3 Why is it important to choose an appropriate form of organisation?

Choosing the correct form is important because it affects capital, liability, control, continuity, secrecy, legal compliance, management quality, expansion, and risk. A form suitable for a small business may not suit a large business. A business needing large capital and professional management may require a company, while a small personal service business may work better as a sole proprietorship.

The main factors are cost and ease of formation, liability, continuity, management ability, capital requirement, degree of control, nature of business, flexibility, and transfer of ownership.

### 49.4 Explain cooperative society with characteristics, merits, limits, and types.

A cooperative society is a voluntary association of persons who join together to protect common economic interests through mutual help and democratic control.

Characteristics:

1. Voluntary membership.
2. Separate legal status.
3. Limited liability.
4. Democratic control.
5. Service motive.
6. Compulsory registration.

Merits:

1. Equality in voting.
2. Limited liability.
3. Stable existence.
4. Economy in operations.
5. Government support.
6. Ease of formation.

Limitations:

1. Limited resources.
2. Inefficient management.
3. Lack of secrecy.
4. Government control.
5. Differences of opinion.

Types include consumers' cooperative, producers' cooperative, marketing cooperative, farmers' cooperative, credit cooperative, and cooperative housing society.

### 49.5 Distinguish between joint Hindu family business and partnership.

| Basis | Joint Hindu family business | Partnership |
|---|---|---|
| Formation | By birth in family | By agreement |
| Governing law | Hindu law | Partnership law |
| Membership | Members of Hindu Undivided Family | Any persons competent to contract |
| Minor status | Minor can be member by birth | Minor cannot be full partner |
| Control | Karta controls | Partners usually share control |
| Liability | Karta unlimited, others limited | Partners usually unlimited, joint, and several |
| Registration | Not required in the same way | Optional for general partnership but advisable |
| Continuity | More stable after death of karta | May be affected by death or insolvency of partner |

### 49.6 Why do many people prefer sole proprietorship despite limits?

Many people prefer sole proprietorship because it is simple, inexpensive, flexible, easy to start, easy to close, and gives full control to the owner. It allows quick decisions, direct customer contact, confidentiality, and direct enjoyment of profit. For small businesses where capital need and risk are limited, these benefits may outweigh the limitations.

## 54. End Summary

Forms of business organisation differ in ownership, control, liability, capital capacity, continuity, legal status, and management. Sole proprietorship is simple and flexible but has limited resources and unlimited liability. Joint Hindu family business is family-based and controlled by karta, but it depends on ancestral property and may suffer from limited managerial skill. Partnership brings more capital and skill than sole proprietorship but involves unlimited liability and possible conflict. Cooperative society is democratic and service-oriented, but may face limited resources and weaker management. Joint stock company has separate legal identity, limited liability, perpetual succession, and large capital capacity, but it is complex and highly regulated. The best form depends on cost, liability, continuity, management, capital, control, nature of business, flexibility, and expansion plans.
