# Chapter 5: Emerging Modes of Business

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Business methods keep changing. Earlier, business was mainly done through physical shops, paper records, face-to-face meetings, and manual processes. Modern business increasingly uses computer networks, digital payments, websites, online marketplaces, outsourcing, call centres, and global service delivery.

This chapter studies two major emerging modes:

1. E-business.
2. Business process outsourcing.

The chapter also explains online transactions, e-business risks, digital security, and the resources needed to start e-business.

## 3. Meaning of Emerging Modes of Business

Mode of business means the way business is carried out.

Emerging modes of business are new ways of conducting business that are developing because of technology, competition, customer expectations, and globalisation.

The major emerging modes are:

1. E-business: conducting business through computer networks.
2. Outsourcing: contracting selected business processes to outside specialists.

## 4. Three Major Trends Shaping Business

The chapter identifies three strong trends:

1. Digitisation.
2. Outsourcing.
3. Internationalisation and globalisation.

### 4.1 Digitisation

Digitisation means conversion of text, sound, image, video, and other information into digital form that can be stored and transmitted electronically.

Digitisation makes possible:

1. Online shopping.
2. Digital payments.
3. Electronic records.
4. E-mails.
5. Video meetings.
6. Online customer service.
7. E-learning.
8. E-procurement.

### 4.2 Outsourcing

Outsourcing means contracting out selected business tasks to outside agencies that can perform them efficiently.

Examples:

1. Call centre services.
2. Payroll processing.
3. Customer support.
4. Data entry.
5. Accounting support.
6. IT services.
7. Recruitment support.

### 4.3 Internationalisation and Globalisation

Businesses increasingly operate across national boundaries. Digital networks and outsourcing make it easier for firms to buy, sell, serve, and collaborate across countries.

## 5. Meaning of E-Business

E-business means conducting industry, trade, commerce, and other business functions through computer networks.

In simple words:

```text
E-business = Doing business electronically
```

It includes:

1. Online buying and selling.
2. Online payments.
3. Digital customer service.
4. Online procurement.
5. Online marketing.
6. Digital inventory control.
7. Electronic accounting.
8. Online recruitment and training.
9. Internal communication through intranet.

## 6. E-Business and E-Commerce

E-commerce and e-business are related but not identical.

E-commerce mainly means buying and selling through electronic networks.

E-business is wider. It includes e-commerce plus all other business functions conducted electronically.

```text
E-business > E-commerce
```

## 7. Difference Between E-Business and E-Commerce

| Basis | E-commerce | E-business |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Electronic buying and selling | Electronic conduct of business functions |
| Scope | Narrower | Wider |
| Main focus | Transactions with customers and suppliers | Complete business processes |
| Includes | Online shopping, online selling, online payment | Production, inventory, finance, HR, accounting, procurement, customer service, internal processes |
| Example | Customer buys a phone online | Firm manages purchase, stock, payment, delivery, HR, and customer support digitally |

The scope of e-business is very wide. It covers:

1. Production.
2. Inventory management.
3. Product development.
4. Finance.
5. Accounting.
6. Human resource management.
7. Marketing.

From the viewpoint of parties involved, e-business includes:

1. B2B commerce.
2. B2C commerce.
3. Intra-B commerce.

## 9. B2B Commerce

B2B means business-to-business commerce.

It involves electronic transactions between two business firms.

### 9.1 Examples

1. Manufacturer places online order with component supplier.
2. Retailer orders goods from wholesaler through a portal.
3. Automobile company monitors supply of parts through vendor network.
4. Company pays suppliers electronically.
5. Business uses electronic data interchange for invoices and purchase orders.

### 9.2 Importance

B2B commerce helps firms:

1. Place orders faster.
2. Monitor stock.
3. Track delivery.
4. Reduce paperwork.
5. Coordinate with suppliers.
6. Reduce dependence on one supplier.
7. Make faster payments.
8. Control stock-in-transit.

### 9.3 Electronic Data Interchange

Electronic Data Interchange, or EDI, means electronic exchange of business documents such as purchase orders, invoices, and delivery notes between businesses in a standardized format.

## 10. B2C Commerce

B2C means business-to-consumer commerce.

It involves electronic interaction between a business and its customers.

### 10.1 Examples

1. Customer buys clothes from an online store.
2. Customer books movie tickets online.
3. Customer withdraws money through ATM.
4. Customer downloads music, software, e-books, or videos.
5. Customer gives online feedback.

### 10.2 B2C Is Wider Than Online Sale

B2C includes the entire marketing process:

1. Identifying customer needs.
2. Online promotion.
3. Product display.
4. Customer reviews.
5. Online ordering.
6. Payment.
7. Delivery.
8. After-sale service.
9. Complaint handling.
10. Customer surveys.

### 10.3 Benefit to Customers

B2C allows customers to:

1. Shop anytime.
2. Compare prices.
3. Read reviews.
4. Get customized products.
5. Make digital payments.
6. Track orders.
7. Contact customer service.

## 11. Intra-B Commerce

Intra-B commerce means electronic transactions and communication within the same business firm.

It uses internal networks, intranet, enterprise software, and digital systems.

### 11.1 Examples

1. Marketing department sends customer orders to production department.
2. HR department conducts online recruitment or training.
3. Employees send field reports by e-mail.
4. Inventory data is shared with purchase department.
5. Managers hold online meetings.
6. Employees work remotely using virtual private network.

### 11.2 Importance

Intra-B commerce helps:

1. Faster internal communication.
2. Better coordination.
3. Flexible manufacturing.
4. Mass customization.
5. Efficient inventory management.
6. Faster decision-making.
7. Remote working.

## 12. C2C Commerce

C2C means consumer-to-consumer commerce.

It involves electronic transactions between consumers.

### 12.1 Examples

1. A person sells used books online to another person.
2. A person sells second-hand clothes through an online platform.
3. A person sells furniture through a consumer marketplace.
4. Consumers exchange or sell products through auction platforms.

### 12.2 Why C2C Is Useful

C2C is useful where there is no established market mechanism for certain goods.

It helps consumers:

1. Find buyers globally.
2. Compare offers.
3. Use seller ratings.
4. Use payment intermediaries.
5. Create consumer forums.
6. Warn others about poor products or sellers.

## 13. C2B Commerce

C2B means consumer-to-business commerce.

In this form, consumers influence businesses or offer value to businesses.

Examples:

1. Customer posts a requirement and businesses quote prices.
2. Customers give feedback that affects product design.
3. Freelancers offer services to companies through digital platforms.
4. Consumer groups influence company decisions through online pressure.

1. Online share trading.

## 20. Benefits of E-Business

The main benefits are:

1. Easy formation and lower investment.
2. Convenience.
3. Speed.
4. Global reach.
5. Movement toward paperless transactions.
6. Lower operating cost.

## 21. Easy Formation and Lower Investment

E-business can be started more easily than many physical businesses because it may not require large physical premises, heavy display area, or large sales infrastructure.

A firm can use websites, online marketplaces, digital catalogues, and delivery partners to reach customers.

## 22. Convenience

E-business works on the idea of:

```text
Anything, anywhere, anytime
```

Customers can shop 24 hours a day. Employees can work from different locations. Managers can monitor operations digitally.

## 23. Speed

E-business speeds up:

1. Information exchange.
2. Order placement.
3. Payment.
4. Customer support.
5. Document transfer.
6. Fund transfer.

For digital products such as software, music, videos, and e-books, delivery can also be immediate.

## 24. Global Reach

Internet has no physical boundary in the same way as a local store. Sellers can reach national and international customers, and buyers can choose from sellers across regions.

## 25. Paperless Society

E-business benefits consumers and society through:

## 27. Traditional Business and E-Business: Comparison

| Basis | Traditional business | E-business |
|---|---|---|
| Ease of formation | More difficult | Easier |
| Physical presence | Required | Not always required |
| Location | Important; near raw materials or market | Less important |
| Setup cost | High | Lower |
| Operating cost | High due to physical facilities | Lower due to digital networks |
| Contact with suppliers and customers | Often through intermediaries | Direct |
| Internal communication | Hierarchical | Direct, vertical, horizontal, and diagonal |
| Response time | Longer | Faster |
| Organisation shape | Tall and vertical | Flatter and more networked |
| Process cycle | Sequential | Simultaneous |
| Personal touch | More | Less |
| Physical pre-sampling | More possible | Less for physical goods; high for digital goods |
| Going global | More difficult | Easier |
| Human capital | Semi-skilled and unskilled may be enough in many roles | Technically skilled people needed |
| Transaction risk | Lower because of face-to-face contact | Higher because of distance and anonymity |

## 28. Limitations of E-Business

E-business has important limitations:

1. Low personal touch.
2. Gap between order speed and delivery speed.
3. Need for technology capability.
4. Increased risk due to anonymity.
5. People resistance.
6. Ethical concerns.
7. Digital divide.

## 29. Low Personal Touch

E-business lacks warmth of face-to-face interaction. It may be less suitable for products where customers want personal advice, physical trial, or emotional assurance.

Examples:

1. Garments.
2. Jewellery.
3. High-value customized goods.
4. Personal care items.

## 30. Gap Between Order and Delivery Speed

Information moves instantly, but physical goods still need packaging, shipping, and delivery time. Customers may become impatient if delivery is slow.

## 31. Need for Technology Capability

Both sellers and buyers need some digital literacy. This creates a digital divide between those familiar with technology and those not familiar with it.

## 32. Increased Risk Due to Anonymity

Online parties may not know each other's real identity or location. This creates risk of:

1. Impersonation.
2. Fraud.
3. Fake sellers.
4. Fake buyers.
5. Wrong delivery.
6. Payment failure.
7. Misuse of card details.
8. Leakage of confidential information.

## 33. People Resistance

Employees and customers may resist digital systems because of fear, stress, lack of training, or insecurity about job changes.

## 34. Ethical Concerns

Digital tools can be misused for excessive surveillance, privacy invasion, unauthorized e-mail monitoring, and misuse of personal data.

## 35. Online Transactions

Online transactions can be understood in three stages:

1. Pre-purchase or pre-sale stage.
2. Purchase or sale stage.
3. Delivery stage.

## 36. Pre-Purchase or Pre-Sale Stage

This stage includes:

1. Advertising.
2. Search for information.
3. Product comparison.
4. Reading reviews.
5. Checking availability.
6. Asking questions.

## 37. Purchase or Sale Stage

This stage includes:

1. Price negotiation where available.
2. Adding items to shopping cart.
3. Confirming order.
4. Selecting delivery address.
5. Choosing payment method.
6. Completing payment or payment arrangement.

## 38. Delivery Stage

Delivery may be:

1. Physical delivery for goods.
2. Digital delivery for software, music, e-books, tickets, or media.
3. Service delivery through online consultation or platform access.

## 39. Steps in Online Trading

### 39.1 Registration

The buyer registers with the online seller by creating an account. The account is protected by password.

Registration may require:

1. Name.
2. Address.
3. Contact number.
4. E-mail.
5. Password.
6. Payment preference.

### 39.2 Placing an Order

The buyer selects products and adds them to a shopping cart. After reviewing items, quantity, price, and delivery details, the buyer checks out.

### 39.3 Payment

The buyer chooses a payment method and completes payment or agrees to pay at delivery.

### 39.4 Confirmation and Delivery

The seller confirms the order and arranges delivery or digital access.

## 40. Payment Mechanisms in Online Transactions

Main payment methods include:

1. Cash on delivery.
2. Cheque collection.
3. Net banking transfer.
4. Credit card.
5. Debit card.
6. Digital cash.
7. Electronic fund transfer.
8. Mobile wallet or UPI-style systems where available.

## 41. Cash on Delivery

Cash on delivery means payment is made when goods are physically delivered.

It is useful where customers do not want to pay before receiving goods.

## 42. Cheque

The seller may arrange cheque pickup. Goods may be delivered after cheque realization.

## 43. Net Banking Transfer

The buyer transfers money from their bank account to the seller's account through electronic banking systems such as IMPS, NEFT, or RTGS.

## 44. Credit Card

Credit card allows purchase on credit. The card issuing bank pays the seller, and the buyer pays the bank later according to card terms.

## 45. Debit Card

Debit card allows purchase up to the available balance in the customer's bank account. The amount is deducted electronically.

## 46. Digital Cash

Digital cash is electronic money used in digital transactions. It exists in electronic form and can be used for online purchases according to system rules.

## 47. E-Business Risks

Online transactions have risks because parties may be distant, anonymous, and dependent on technology.

Main risk groups:

1. Transaction risks.
2. Data storage and transmission risks.
3. Threat to intellectual property and privacy.

## 48. Transaction Risks

Transaction risks include:

1. Seller denies receiving order.
2. Buyer denies placing order.
3. Goods are not delivered.
4. Goods are delivered to wrong address.
5. Wrong goods are delivered.
6. Seller does not receive payment.
7. Buyer claims payment was made.

### 48.1 Risk Reduction

These risks can be reduced through:

1. Identity verification.
2. Address verification.
3. Order confirmation.
4. Payment authorization.
5. Seller ratings.
6. Reputed platforms.
7. Tracking systems.
8. Secure payment gateways.

## 49. Data Storage and Transmission Risks

Data stored in computers or transmitted through networks may be stolen, changed, intercepted, or destroyed.

Risks include:

1. Hacking.
2. Virus attack.
3. Data theft.
4. Data modification.
5. System failure.
6. Unauthorized access.

### 49.1 Virus

A virus is a program that can replicate itself and harm computer systems or data.

Virus effects may include:

1. Annoying screen messages.
2. Slow system.
3. Disruption of work.
4. Damage to data files.
5. Complete system destruction in severe cases.

### 49.2 Protection

Protection methods include:

1. Anti-virus software.
2. Regular updates.
3. Scanning files.
4. Strong passwords.
5. Firewalls.
6. Secure backups.
7. Access control.

## 50. Intellectual Property and Privacy Risks

Internet is open. Once information is placed online, it can be copied, shared, or misused.

Risks include:

1. Copying of digital content.
2. Misuse of customer data.
3. Spam messages.
4. Unauthorized use of brand or design.
5. Leakage of private information.

## 51. Security Tools in E-Business

### 51.1 Passwords

Passwords protect accounts and restrict access.

Good password practice:

1. Use strong passwords.
2. Avoid sharing passwords.
3. Change passwords periodically.
4. Use multi-factor authentication where available.

### 51.2 Cookies

Cookies are small data files stored by websites to remember user information, preferences, and prior visits. They may help verification and personalization, but they also raise privacy concerns.

### 51.3 Encryption

Encryption means converting readable information into unreadable form so unauthorized persons cannot understand it.

Readable text is called plaintext. Encrypted text is called ciphertext. Authorized users decrypt it using a key.

### 51.4 Secure Sockets Layer

Secure Sockets Layer, or SSL, is a security technology used to protect information transmitted between browser and website. Modern websites use related secure protocols to protect payment and login data.

### 51.5 Digital Signature

Digital signature is used to authenticate electronic records and verify that a document or message came from the stated sender and has not been altered.

Website should be:

## 54. Business Process Outsourcing

Business process outsourcing, or BPO, means contracting out selected business processes to outside specialists.

In simple words:

```text
BPO = Getting selected business work done by an outside expert agency
```

Examples:

1. Customer care handled by call centre.
2. Payroll processed by outside firm.
3. Data entry done by specialist agency.
4. IT support handled by technology service provider.
5. Accounting back office handled by outside firm.
6. Recruitment screening handled by HR service provider.

## 55. Meaning of Business Process

A business process is a sequence of tasks that produces a business result.

Examples:

1. Taking customer orders.
2. Processing salaries.
3. Handling complaints.
4. Managing invoices.
5. Maintaining employee records.
6. Providing technical support.

## 56. Need for Outsourcing

Businesses outsource because:

1. They want to focus on core activities.
2. Outside specialists may perform better.
3. Costs may reduce.
4. Services may be available 24 hours.
5. Technology investment can be shared.
6. Firms can scale operations faster.
7. Managers can spend more time on strategy.
8. Quality may improve when experts handle routine work.

## 57. Core and Non-Core Activities

Core activities are activities central to the firm's competitive strength.

Examples:

1. Product design for a design-led company.
2. Manufacturing technology for a manufacturing firm.
3. Brand management for a consumer goods firm.

Non-core activities support the business but may not be the firm's main strength.

Examples:

1. Payroll.
2. Routine data entry.
3. Customer helpline.
4. Housekeeping.
5. Basic IT maintenance.

Firms often outsource non-core activities first.

## 58. Benefits of Outsourcing

### 58.1 Focus on Core Competence

Outsourcing routine or support work allows the firm to focus on its main strengths.

### 58.2 Cost Reduction

Specialized providers may perform work at lower cost because of scale, technology, and expertise.

### 58.3 Better Quality

Expert agencies may provide better service quality in their specialized area.

### 58.4 Speed and Flexibility

Outsourcing can help firms expand or reduce support operations quickly.

### 58.5 Access to Specialized Skills

Firms can access skills that may not be available internally.

### 58.6 Economic Development

Outsourcing creates employment and export opportunities in countries that provide these services.

## 59. Limitations of Outsourcing

### 59.1 Confidentiality Risk

Outside agencies may access sensitive business or customer data.

### 59.2 Quality Risk

If the outsourcing partner performs poorly, customers may blame the original firm.

### 59.3 Loss of Control

The firm may lose direct control over outsourced processes.

### 59.4 Dependence on Vendor

Too much dependence on one vendor may create operational risk.

### 59.5 Ethical Concerns

Outsourcing may be criticized if it leads to unfair labour practices, poor working conditions, or job loss concerns.

### 59.6 Hidden Costs

Savings may reduce if there are high coordination, monitoring, training, or contract management costs.

## 60. BPO, KPO, and Call Centres

### 60.1 BPO

BPO covers routine or semi-routine business processes such as customer care, data entry, payroll, and transaction processing.

### 60.2 KPO

Knowledge process outsourcing involves knowledge-intensive work such as research, analytics, legal process support, market research, and financial analysis.

### 60.3 Call Centres

Call centres handle customer calls, queries, complaints, technical support, sales support, and service requests.

## 61. Captive BPO and Third-Party BPO

### 61.1 Captive BPO

A captive BPO unit is owned by the same company whose processes it handles.

Example:

A multinational company sets up its own back-office unit in another country.

### 61.2 Third-Party BPO

A third-party BPO is an independent service provider that handles work for client companies.

## 62. Horizontals and Verticals in BPO

## 63. E-Business and Outsourcing Together

E-business and outsourcing are connected. Digital networks make it easier to outsource business processes across cities and countries.

Example:

```text
Customer places complaint online
```

## 65. Long Answer Practice

### 65.1 Why are e-business and outsourcing called emerging modes of business?

They are called emerging modes because they represent new ways of doing business that are growing due to digitisation, competition, customer demand, technology, and globalisation. E-business allows firms to conduct operations electronically and overcome time and location barriers. Outsourcing allows firms to contract selected processes to outside specialists, reduce cost, improve quality, and focus on core competence. Both are reshaping how business is performed.

### 65.2 Explain the steps in online trading.

Online trading usually involves registration, placing an order, payment, and delivery. First, the customer creates an account with password protection. Then the customer selects products and adds them to the shopping cart. After checking details, the customer selects a payment method such as cash on delivery, cheque, net banking, card, or digital cash. Finally, the seller confirms the order and arranges delivery physically or digitally.

### 65.3 Evaluate the need for outsourcing and its limitations.

Outsourcing is needed because it helps firms focus on core activities, reduce cost, access expert services, improve speed, scale operations, and improve service quality. It can also create employment and export opportunities.

However, it has limitations such as confidentiality risk, loss of control, dependence on vendors, quality risk, hidden costs, and ethical concerns related to labour practices or job loss.

### 65.4 Explain B2C commerce.

B2C commerce is electronic interaction between a business and its customers. It includes online advertising, product display, customer search, online ordering, digital payment, physical or digital delivery, customer feedback, online surveys, complaint handling, and after-sale service. B2C gives customers convenience, wider choice, round-the-clock access, and customized service.

### 65.5 Explain limitations of e-business and whether they restrict its scope.

E-business has limitations such as low personal touch, delivery delays for physical goods, need for technology knowledge, security risk, anonymity of parties, people resistance, ethical issues, and digital divide.

These limitations are serious but not enough to stop e-business growth. Technology is improving through secure payment systems, encryption, better logistics, customer support, interactive websites, digital literacy, and stronger regulation. Therefore, e-business continues to grow despite its limitations.

## 71. End Summary

Emerging modes of business are new ways of conducting business through digital technology and external specialization. E-business means carrying out business through computer networks and is wider than e-commerce. Its scope includes B2B, B2C, intra-B, C2C, e-procurement, e-bidding, e-delivery, e-promotion, and e-trading. E-business provides convenience, speed, lower cost, global reach, and paperless operation, but it also has limitations such as low personal touch, technology requirement, delivery delays, security risks, privacy concerns, and digital divide. Online transactions involve registration, order placement, payment, and delivery. Business process outsourcing means contracting selected processes to outside specialists. It helps firms focus on core work and reduce cost, but it requires careful control over quality, confidentiality, vendor dependence, and ethical issues.
