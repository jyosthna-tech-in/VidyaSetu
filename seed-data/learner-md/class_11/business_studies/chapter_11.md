## 6. Meaning of Domestic Business

Domestic business means business transactions that take place within the geographical boundaries of one nation.

It is also called:

1. National business.
2. Internal business.
3. Home trade.

Example:

An Indian manufacturer selling goods to an Indian wholesaler within India is doing domestic business.

## 7. Meaning of International Business

International business means business activities that take place across national frontiers.

It includes movement of:

1. Goods.
2. Services.
3. Capital.
4. Personnel.
5. Technology.
6. Intellectual property such as patents, trademarks, know-how, and copyrights.

International business includes both trade and production across borders.

## 8. International Business and International Trade

International trade is narrower than international business.

International trade mainly includes:

1. Export of goods.
2. Import of goods.
3. Export of services.
4. Import of services.

International business includes international trade plus:

1. Foreign investment.
2. Overseas production.
3. Licensing.
4. Franchising.
5. Contract manufacturing.
6. Joint ventures.
7. Wholly owned subsidiaries.
8. Movement of technology and intellectual property.

## 9. Difference Between International Trade and International Business

| Basis | International trade | International business |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Export and import of goods and services | All business activities across national borders |
| Scope | Narrow | Broad |
| Main focus | Buying and selling | Trade, investment, production, technology, services, and ownership |
| Example | Exporting garments from India to Australia | Exporting garments, setting up a foreign unit, licensing designs, and investing abroad |
| Includes foreign production | Not necessarily | Yes, it can include foreign production |

## 10. Why International Business Happens

The major reason is that countries cannot produce everything equally well or cheaply.

Countries differ in:

1. Natural resources.
2. Climate.
3. Labour availability.
4. Capital availability.
5. Technology.
6. Productivity.
7. Skills.
8. Infrastructure.
9. Cost of production.
10. Political and economic conditions.

Because of these differences, countries specialise in goods and services they can produce efficiently and obtain the rest through trade.

## 11. Geographical Specialisation

Geographical specialisation means different regions or countries specialise in producing goods and services for which they are better suited.

Examples from the textbook idea:

1. West Bengal specialises in jute products.
2. Mumbai and nearby areas in Maharashtra are known for cotton textiles.
3. Labour-abundant developing countries may specialise in garments.
4. Capital-rich developed countries may specialise in advanced machinery.

The same principle applies internationally.

## 12. International Business for Firms

Firms engage in international business to:

1. Import goods available at lower prices abroad.
2. Export goods where they can earn better prices.
3. Use surplus production capacity.
4. Enter larger markets.
5. Reduce dependence on domestic demand.
6. Gain access to technology.
7. Build international brand presence.
8. Diversify business risk.

## 13. Globalisation Context

The chapter explains that international business grew because of:

1. Better communication.
2. Better transport.
3. Technology development.
4. Infrastructure development.
5. Economic reforms.
6. Lower barriers to cross-border movement.
7. Growth of institutions such as WTO.
8. Countries becoming more integrated with the world economy.

This is why the world is often described as a global village.

## 14. India's Globalisation Path in the Textbook Context

The chapter refers to India's economic reforms after the 1991 balance of payments crisis.

Key textbook points:

1. India faced a severe foreign exchange and balance of payments crisis.
2. India approached the International Monetary Fund for assistance.
3. The assistance came with expectations of structural economic changes.
4. India liberalised many economic policies.
5. Foreign companies entered Indian markets.
6. Indian companies also started expanding abroad.

This context helps explain why international business became increasingly important for Indian firms.

## 15. Domestic and International Business: Main Difference

International business is more complex than domestic business because it operates across different:

1. Countries.
2. Legal systems.
3. Currencies.
4. Cultures.
5. Languages.
6. Political environments.
7. Consumer preferences.
8. Business practices.

## 16. Difference 1: Nationality of Buyers and Sellers

In domestic business, buyers and sellers belong to the same country.

This makes communication and understanding easier.

In international business, buyers and sellers belong to different countries.

This creates differences in:

1. Language.
2. Customs.
3. Business goals.
4. Attitudes.
5. Negotiation styles.
6. Legal expectations.

## 17. Difference 2: Nationality of Other Stakeholders

Stakeholders include:

1. Employees.
2. Suppliers.
3. Shareholders.
4. Partners.
5. Middlemen.
6. General public.

In domestic business, most stakeholders are from the same country.

In international business, stakeholders may belong to different nations. This makes decision-making more complex.

## 18. Difference 3: Mobility of Factors of Production

Factors of production include:

1. Land.
2. Labour.
3. Capital.
4. Technology.
5. Enterprise.

Within a country, labour and capital may move more easily.

Across countries, movement is restricted by:

1. Immigration rules.
2. Investment regulations.
3. Cultural differences.
4. Climate differences.
5. Economic conditions.
6. Political conditions.

## 19. Difference 4: Customer Heterogeneity

International markets have more customer diversity.

Customers differ in:

1. Language.
2. Tastes.
3. Customs.
4. Fashion.
5. Beliefs.
6. Product preferences.
7. Purchase behaviour.
8. Income levels.

Therefore, firms may need to adapt product design, packaging, pricing, promotion, and distribution.

## 20. Difference 5: Business Systems and Practices

Countries differ in:

1. Economic development.
2. Infrastructure.
3. Market support services.
4. Business customs.
5. Commercial practices.
6. Contract practices.
7. Distribution systems.

This affects production, finance, marketing, and human resource decisions.

## 21. Difference 6: Political Systems and Risks

Political systems differ across countries.

International firms face risks such as:

1. Change in government policy.
2. Import restrictions.
3. Foreign ownership restrictions.
4. National preference for local goods.
5. Political instability.
6. Expropriation risk.
7. Change in taxation.

Firms must monitor political changes continuously.

## 22. Difference 7: Business Regulations and Policies

Each country has its own:

1. Business laws.
2. Taxation rules.
3. Tariffs.
4. Quotas.
5. Import controls.
6. Export controls.
7. Subsidies.
8. Labour laws.
9. Investment rules.

International firms must comply with multiple countries' rules.

## 23. Difference 8: Currency

Domestic business uses one country's currency.

International business may use more than one currency.

This creates foreign exchange risk because exchange rates fluctuate.

Example:

An Indian exporter may quote in US dollars. If the rupee-dollar rate changes before payment, the exporter's rupee receipt may change.

## 24. Domestic and International Business Compared

| Basis | Domestic business | International business |
|---|---|---|
| Buyers and sellers | From same country | From different countries |
| Stakeholders | Mostly same nationality | Different nationalities |
| Factor mobility | Relatively higher | Relatively lower |
| Customers | More homogeneous | More heterogeneous |
| Business practices | Similar within country | Vary across countries |
| Political environment | One country | Multiple countries |
| Regulations | One legal system | Multiple legal systems |
| Currency | Domestic currency | Multiple currencies |
| Risk level | Lower | Higher |
| Complexity | Lower | Higher |

## 25. Scope of International Business

International business includes:

1. Merchandise exports and imports.
2. Service exports and imports.
3. Licensing and franchising.
4. Foreign investment.
5. Contract manufacturing.
6. Joint ventures.
7. Wholly owned subsidiaries.
8. International movement of technology and intellectual property.

## 26. Merchandise Exports and Imports

Merchandise means tangible goods that can be seen and touched.

Merchandise export means sending tangible goods to another country.

Merchandise import means bringing tangible goods from another country.

Examples:

1. Exporting garments.
2. Importing textile machinery.
3. Exporting tea.
4. Importing crude oil.
5. Exporting automobile components.

## 27. Service Exports and Imports

Services are intangible. Trade in services is also called invisible trade.

Examples of internationally traded services:

1. Tourism.
2. Travel.
3. Transportation.
4. Hotel services.
5. Entertainment.
6. Banking.
7. Insurance.
8. Communication.
9. Consultancy.
10. Training.
11. Research.
12. Engineering.
13. Construction.
14. Advertising.
15. Warehousing.

## 28. Business Services in International Trade

When a firm in one country provides services to clients in another country and earns foreign exchange, it is part of international business.

Examples:

1. Engineering services.
2. Management services.
3. Banking services.
4. Insurance services.
5. Construction work in a foreign country.
6. Overseas management contracts.

## 29. Licensing and Franchising as Scope

International business also includes permission-based business models.

Licensing allows a foreign firm to use technology, patents, trademarks, or know-how for royalty.

Franchising allows a foreign service provider to use a service system, name, brand, or business method under agreed rules.

Examples:

1. Bottling and selling a beverage brand abroad under licence.
2. Running a fast-food restaurant chain abroad through franchisees.

## 30. Foreign Investment

Foreign investment means investing funds abroad for financial return or business control.

It has two main forms:

1. Foreign direct investment.
2. Portfolio investment.

## 31. Foreign Direct Investment

Foreign direct investment, or FDI, occurs when a company directly invests in assets such as plant, machinery, or business operations in a foreign country.

FDI usually gives control or significant influence over business operations.

FDI may be through:

1. Joint venture.
2. Wholly owned subsidiary.
3. Foreign production unit.
4. Acquisition of an existing foreign firm.

## 32. Portfolio Investment

Portfolio investment means investment in financial assets abroad without direct control over production or marketing.

Examples:

1. Buying shares of a foreign company.
2. Buying foreign bonds.
3. Giving loans to a foreign business.

The investor earns dividends or interest but does not normally manage the business.

## 33. Benefits of International Business to Countries

International business benefits countries by:

1. Earning foreign exchange.
2. Using resources efficiently.
3. Improving growth prospects.
4. Increasing employment potential.
5. Raising standard of living.

## 34. Country Benefit 1: Earning Foreign Exchange

Exports help a country earn foreign exchange.

Foreign exchange can be used to import:

1. Capital goods.
2. Technology.
3. Petroleum products.
4. Fertilisers.
5. Pharmaceutical products.
6. Consumer products not available domestically.

## 35. Country Benefit 2: Efficient Use of Resources

International business encourages countries to produce what they can produce more efficiently.

This leads to:

1. Specialisation.
2. Larger total output.
3. Better use of natural resources.
4. Lower production cost.
5. Better access to goods from other countries.

## 36. Country Benefit 3: Growth and Employment

If a country produces only for its domestic market, growth may be limited by domestic demand.

Exports allow firms to produce for larger world markets.

This can:

1. Increase production.
2. Create jobs.
3. Improve capacity utilisation.
4. Encourage industrial growth.
5. Support national income.

## 37. Country Benefit 4: Higher Standard of Living

International business allows people to consume goods and services produced in other countries.

Consumers get:

1. More variety.
2. Better quality.
3. New technology.
4. Competitive prices.
5. Goods not available domestically.

## 38. Benefits of International Business to Firms

International business benefits firms by:

1. Higher profit prospects.
2. Increased capacity utilisation.
3. Growth prospects.
4. Way out of intense domestic competition.
5. Improved business vision.

## 39. Firm Benefit 1: Higher Profits

Firms may earn higher profits by selling in countries where:

1. Prices are higher.
2. Demand is stronger.
3. Competition is lower.
4. Customers value the product more.

## 40. Firm Benefit 2: Capacity Utilisation

Some firms have production capacity greater than domestic demand.

Foreign orders help them:

1. Use spare capacity.
2. Produce on a larger scale.
3. Reduce per unit cost.
4. Improve profitability.

## 41. Firm Benefit 3: Growth Prospects

Domestic markets may become saturated.

International markets give firms new growth opportunities by:

1. Entering countries with growing demand.
2. Reaching larger customer bases.
3. Extending product life cycles.
4. Diversifying markets.

## 42. Firm Benefit 4: Escape from Domestic Competition

If domestic competition is intense, firms may seek foreign markets.

International business can become a growth path when domestic growth is difficult.

## 43. Firm Benefit 5: Improved Business Vision

Going international may improve a firm's strategic vision because it forces the firm to:

1. Become more competitive.
2. Improve quality.
3. Learn global practices.
4. Diversify operations.
5. Build long-term capabilities.

## 44. Modes of Entry into International Business

Mode of entry means the way a firm enters a foreign market.

Main modes:

1. Exporting and importing.
2. Contract manufacturing.
3. Licensing and franchising.
4. Joint ventures.
5. Wholly owned subsidiaries.

## 45. Exporting and Importing

Exporting means sending goods or services from the home country to a foreign country.

Importing means buying foreign goods or services and bringing them into the home country.

This is often the first mode used by firms entering international business.

## 46. Direct and Indirect Exporting or Importing

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direct exporting or importing | Firm itself contacts foreign buyers or suppliers and handles formalities |
| Indirect exporting or importing | Middlemen such as export houses, buying offices, or importers handle most formalities |

Direct mode gives more control but requires more knowledge and effort.

Indirect mode is easier but gives less direct market contact.

## 47. Advantages of Exporting and Importing

Advantages:

1. Easiest mode of entry.
2. Less complex than setting up foreign production.
3. Requires less investment abroad.
4. Lower foreign investment risk.
5. Useful for initial international exposure.
6. Allows firms to test foreign markets.

## 48. Limitations of Exporting and Importing

Limitations:

1. High transport cost.
2. Additional packaging cost.
3. Insurance cost.
4. Custom duties and other charges.
5. Import restrictions in foreign countries.
6. Less direct contact with foreign customers.
7. Difficulty understanding local market needs.
8. Less control over foreign distribution if intermediaries are used.

## 49. Contract Manufacturing

Contract manufacturing means an international firm enters into a contract with local manufacturers in a foreign country to produce goods or components according to the international firm's specifications.

It is also called outsourcing in the chapter context.

## 50. Forms of Contract Manufacturing

Contract manufacturing may involve:

1. Production of components.
2. Assembly of components into final products.
3. Complete manufacture of products.

Examples:

1. Producing shoe uppers for shoe companies.
2. Assembling computer parts into finished computers.
3. Manufacturing garments for an international brand.

## 51. Advantages of Contract Manufacturing

Advantages for the international firm:

1. No need to set up its own production facility abroad.
2. Lower investment.
3. Lower investment risk.
4. Lower cost if foreign local producers have cheaper labour or materials.
5. Faster entry into foreign production.

Advantages for local producers:

1. Better use of idle production capacity.
2. Ready market for output.
3. Exposure to international business.
4. Possible access to export incentives in the textbook context.
5. Learning from technology and management guidance.

## 52. Limitations of Contract Manufacturing

Limitations:

1. Local producer may not follow design and quality standards.
2. International firm may face quality problems.
3. Local producer loses control over manufacturing decisions.
4. Local producer may not freely sell contracted output.
5. Predetermined prices may reduce local producer's profit if market prices rise.

## 53. Licensing

Licensing is a contractual arrangement in which one firm gives another firm in a foreign country the right to use its:

1. Patents.
2. Trade secrets.
3. Technology.
4. Trademark.
5. Know-how.
6. Copyrights.

The fee paid is called royalty.

The firm giving rights is the licensor.

The firm receiving rights is the licensee.

## 54. Cross-Licensing

Cross-licensing means two firms exchange technology, knowledge, patents, or rights with each other.

It is common when both firms own valuable knowledge.

## 55. Franchising

Franchising is similar to licensing but is used mainly for services.

The parent firm is called the franchiser.

The foreign party is called the franchisee.

The franchiser gives rights to use:

1. Brand name.
2. Service system.
3. Business format.
4. Technology.
5. Trademark.
6. Operating method.

Franchising is usually more strict than licensing because the franchiser sets detailed operating rules.

## 56. Licensing and Franchising Compared

| Basis | Licensing | Franchising |
|---|---|---|
| Main use | Goods and technology | Services and business format |
| Control | Less strict | More strict |
| Payment | Royalty or fee | Royalty, fee, or share as per agreement |
| Parties | Licensor and licensee | Franchiser and franchisee |
| Example | Technology or brand used by foreign producer | Restaurant chain run by foreign franchisee |

## 57. Advantages of Licensing and Franchising

Advantages:

1. Less expensive mode of entry.
2. Little or no investment abroad.
3. Lower risk of foreign losses.
4. Royalty or fee income.
5. Local licensee or franchisee has market knowledge.
6. Lower risk of government takeover compared with direct ownership.
7. Legal protection of trademark, patent, and brand rights under agreement.

## 58. Limitations of Licensing and Franchising

Limitations:

1. Licensee or franchisee may become a future competitor.
2. Trade secrets may be leaked.
3. Quality may not be maintained.
4. Conflicts may arise over royalty or accounts.
5. Disputes may arise over rules and standards.
6. Legal cases may be costly.

## 59. Joint Ventures

A joint venture is an enterprise jointly owned by two or more independent firms.

It is a common way to enter foreign markets when firms want to share:

1. Capital.
2. Risk.
3. Technology.
4. Local knowledge.
5. Market access.

## 60. Ways to Form a Joint Venture

A joint venture may be formed in three ways:

1. Foreign investor buys an interest in a local company.
2. Local firm acquires an interest in an existing foreign firm.
3. Foreign and local firms jointly form a new enterprise.

## 61. Advantages of Joint Ventures

Advantages:

1. Financial burden is shared.
2. Large projects become possible.
3. Local partner provides knowledge of host country.
4. Local partner understands language, culture, political system, and business practices.
5. Costs and risks are shared.
6. Entry into difficult markets becomes easier.

## 62. Limitations of Joint Ventures

Limitations:

1. Technology and trade secrets may be shared with local partner.
2. Risk of leakage of confidential information.
3. Dual ownership may create conflict.
4. Disputes may arise over control.
5. Differences in business culture may affect operations.

## 63. Wholly Owned Subsidiaries

A wholly owned subsidiary is a foreign business unit fully owned by the parent company.

The parent company makes 100 percent investment in equity capital and exercises full control over foreign operations.

## 64. Ways to Establish a Wholly Owned Subsidiary

Two methods:

1. Setting up a new firm in the foreign country, called a greenfield venture in the textbook context.
2. Acquiring an existing foreign firm and using it for production or marketing.

## 65. Advantages of Wholly Owned Subsidiaries

Advantages:

1. Full control over foreign operations.
2. Full control over production and marketing.
3. No need to share technology with partners.
4. Better protection of trade secrets.
5. Stronger brand and quality control.

## 66. Limitations of Wholly Owned Subsidiaries

Limitations:

1. Requires very high investment.
2. Not suitable for many small and medium firms.
3. Parent company bears all losses.
4. Higher political risk.
5. Some countries restrict full foreign ownership.

## 67. Entry Modes Compared

| Mode | Investment | Control | Risk | Suitable when |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Exporting | Low | Low to moderate | Low to moderate | Firm is starting international operations |
| Contract manufacturing | Low | Moderate | Moderate | Foreign production is needed without own factory |
| Licensing | Low | Low | Low to moderate | Firm owns technology or brand rights |
| Franchising | Low | Moderate | Low to moderate | Service format can be replicated |
| Joint venture | Moderate | Shared | Shared | Local knowledge and shared risk are needed |
| Wholly owned subsidiary | Very high | Very high | High | Full control is essential |

## 68. Export-Import Procedures: Why They Are Complex

Export and import procedures are more complex than domestic buying and selling because they involve:

1. Movement across national borders.
2. Customs rules.
3. Foreign exchange.
4. International transport.
5. Insurance.
6. Quality inspection.
7. Banking documents.
8. Legal compliance in more than one country.

## 69. Export Procedure Overview

Typical export procedure:

1. Receipt of enquiry and sending quotation.
2. Receipt of order or indent.
3. Assessing importer creditworthiness and securing payment guarantee.
4. Obtaining export licence.
5. Obtaining pre-shipment finance.
6. Production or procurement of goods.
7. Pre-shipment inspection.
8. Excise clearance in the textbook context.
9. Obtaining certificate of origin.
10. Reservation of shipping space.
11. Packing and forwarding.
12. Insurance of goods.
13. Customs clearance.
14. Obtaining mate's receipt.
15. Payment of freight and issue of bill of lading.
16. Preparation of invoice.
17. Securing payment.

## 70. Export Step 1: Enquiry and Quotation

A prospective foreign buyer sends an enquiry asking for:

1. Price.
2. Quality.
3. Grade.
4. Size.
5. Weight.
6. Delivery terms.
7. Packing details.
8. Payment terms.

The exporter replies with a quotation called a proforma invoice.

## 71. Export Step 2: Order or Indent

If the buyer accepts the quotation, the buyer places an order.

The order is also called indent.

It contains:

1. Description of goods.
2. Price.
3. Quantity.
4. Delivery terms.
5. Packing instructions.
6. Marking details.
7. Delivery instructions.

## 72. Export Step 3: Creditworthiness and Payment Guarantee

The exporter checks the importer's creditworthiness to reduce risk of non-payment.

The exporter may demand a letter of credit from the importer.

A letter of credit is a guarantee by the importer's bank that it will pay the export bills up to a specified amount.

## 73. Export Step 4: Export Licence

In the textbook context, export compliance may require:

1. Bank account with an authorised bank.
2. Import Export Code, or IEC.
3. Registration with an export promotion council.
4. Registration with Export Credit and Guarantee Corporation, or ECGC.

IEC is required on many export-import documents.

Registration with ECGC helps protect against commercial and political non-payment risks.

## 74. Export Step 5: Pre-Shipment Finance

Pre-shipment finance is finance needed before shipment.

It helps the exporter pay for:

1. Raw material.
2. Components.
3. Processing.
4. Packing.
5. Transport to port.
6. Other pre-shipment expenses.

## 75. Export Step 6: Production or Procurement

The exporter prepares goods according to the importer's specifications.

The exporter may:

1. Produce goods in its own factory.
2. Procure goods from the market.
3. Arrange packing according to order terms.

## 76. Export Step 7: Pre-Shipment Inspection

Pre-shipment inspection ensures that goods meet required quality standards.

For certain goods, inspection by a designated agency may be compulsory in the textbook context.

The exporter obtains an inspection certificate when needed.

This certificate may be submitted with other export documents.

## 77. Export Step 8: Excise Clearance

In the textbook context, goods manufactured for export may require excise clearance or may be eligible for exemption or refund.

Duty drawback means refund of duty paid on inputs used in export goods.

The purpose is to make exports more competitive.

## 78. Export Step 9: Certificate of Origin

Certificate of origin proves the country where goods were produced.

It may help the importer claim:

1. Tariff concessions.
2. Quota benefits.
3. Exemptions.
4. Permission where goods from some countries are restricted.

## 79. Export Step 10: Reservation of Shipping Space

The exporter applies to a shipping company for space.

The application mentions:

1. Type of goods.
2. Probable date of shipment.
3. Port of destination.

If accepted, the shipping company issues a shipping order.

## 80. Export Step 11: Packing and Forwarding

Goods are packed and marked with details such as:

1. Importer's name and address.
2. Gross weight.
3. Net weight.
4. Port of shipment.
5. Port of destination.
6. Country of origin.
7. Package marks.

Goods are then transported to the port.

## 81. Export Step 12: Insurance

The exporter gets goods insured against risks of loss or damage during transit.

Marine insurance protects against sea transit risks.

Air shipment may require relevant transport insurance.

## 82. Export Step 13: Customs Clearance

Goods must be cleared by customs before loading.

The main document is the shipping bill.

Documents commonly submitted include:

1. Export contract or export order.
2. Letter of credit.
3. Commercial invoice.
4. Certificate of origin.
5. Certificate of inspection, where needed.
6. Marine insurance policy.

After document submission, a carting order permits cargo to enter the dock area.

## 83. Export Step 14: Mate's Receipt

After cargo is loaded on the ship, the mate or captain issues mate's receipt.

It records:

1. Name of vessel.
2. Berth.
3. Date of shipment.
4. Description of packages.
5. Marks and numbers.
6. Condition of cargo when loaded.

The shipping company usually issues the bill of lading after receiving mate's receipt.

## 84. Export Step 15: Freight and Bill of Lading

The C&F agent gives mate's receipt to the shipping company for freight calculation.

After freight is paid, the shipping company issues bill of lading.

Bill of lading is evidence that the shipping company accepted goods for carriage to the destination.

If goods are sent by air, the corresponding document is airway bill.

## 85. Export Step 16: Invoice

After sending goods, the exporter prepares invoice.

Invoice states:

1. Quantity of goods sent.
2. Amount payable by importer.
3. Other sale details.

The C&F agent gets it attested by customs where needed in the textbook context.

## 86. Export Step 17: Securing Payment

The exporter informs the importer about shipment and sends documents through the bank.

Documents may include:

1. Certified copy of invoice.
2. Bill of lading.
3. Packing list.
4. Insurance policy.
5. Certificate of origin.
6. Letter of credit.
7. Bill of exchange.

Submission of documents to the bank to obtain payment is called negotiation of documents.

## 87. Sight Draft and Usance Draft

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Sight draft | Documents are released to importer only against payment |
| Usance draft | Documents are released against acceptance of bill of exchange, with payment later |

In a sight draft, payment happens immediately.

In a usance draft, the importer accepts the bill and pays at the end of a specified period.

## 88. Bank Certificate of Payment

After receiving export payment, the exporter obtains a bank certificate of payment.

It confirms that:

The importer arranges funds in advance to pay the exporter when goods arrive.

## 99. Import Step 7: Shipment Advice

5. Certificate of origin.

## 101. Import Step 9: Arrival of Goods

6. Get customs assessment.
7. Pay import duty, if any.

9. Get goods examined.

## 131. Exam Answer: International Business

International business refers to business activities that take place across national frontiers. It includes export and import of goods and services, foreign investment, overseas production, licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint ventures, wholly owned subsidiaries, and movement of technology, capital, personnel, and intellectual property. It is broader than international trade.

## 132. Exam Answer: International Trade and International Business

International trade means export and import of goods and services between countries. International business is a broader term. It includes international trade as well as foreign investment, production and marketing in foreign countries, licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint ventures, wholly owned subsidiaries, and movement of technology and intellectual property across borders.

## 133. Exam Answer: Major Reason for Trade Between Nations

The major reason for trade between nations is that countries cannot produce all goods and services equally well or cheaply. Natural resources, labour, capital, technology, productivity, climate, and production costs differ among countries. Therefore, each country specialises in producing what it can produce efficiently and obtains other goods through trade with countries that can produce them better or at lower cost.

## 134. Exam Answer: Benefits to Countries

International business benefits countries in the following ways:

1. It helps earn foreign exchange.
2. It permits more efficient use of resources through specialisation.
3. It improves growth prospects by expanding markets.
4. It creates employment through larger production.
5. It raises standard of living by providing access to foreign goods and services.

## 135. Exam Answer: Benefits to Firms

Firms derive the following benefits from international business:

1. Higher profits from markets where prices are better.
2. Better capacity utilisation through foreign orders.
3. Growth when domestic markets are saturated.
4. Escape from intense domestic competition.
5. Improved business vision through global exposure, quality improvement, and strategic diversification.

## 136. Exam Answer: Exporting as an Entry Mode

Exporting is often better for initial entry into international markets because it is simple, requires less investment, involves lower foreign investment risk, and allows a firm to test foreign demand without setting up production facilities abroad. However, it may involve transport cost, packaging cost, insurance, customs duties, import restrictions, and limited contact with foreign customers.

## 137. Exam Answer: Contract Manufacturing vs Wholly Owned Subsidiary

Contract manufacturing involves getting goods or components produced by a local manufacturer in a foreign country according to the international firm's specifications. It requires little investment and has lower risk, but quality control may be difficult and production depends on the local manufacturer. A wholly owned subsidiary is a foreign company fully owned by the parent company. It gives full control and better protection of technology, but requires 100 percent investment and the parent bears all losses.

## 138. Exam Answer: Licensing and Franchising

Licensing is a contractual arrangement where a licensor permits a foreign licensee to use patents, technology, trademarks, trade secrets, or know-how for royalty. Franchising is similar but is generally used for services and involves stricter rules about how the business should operate. Licensing is common for goods and technology, while franchising is common for restaurants, hotels, travel agencies, banks, and other service businesses.

## 139. Exam Answer: Joint Venture

A joint venture is an enterprise jointly owned by two or more independent firms. It may be formed when a foreign investor buys into a local company, a local company buys into a foreign firm, or both jointly form a new enterprise. Joint ventures reduce financial burden, share risk, provide local market knowledge, and help execute large projects, but may create conflicts over control and risk leakage of technology or trade secrets.

## 140. Exam Answer: Pre-Shipment Inspection

Pre-shipment inspection is necessary to ensure that export goods meet required quality standards. Some goods must be inspected by a designated agency before export in the textbook context. The inspection certificate assures the foreign buyer and authorities that the consignment has been inspected and is export-worthy. It helps maintain the reputation of the exporting country and reduces rejection risk.

## 141. Exam Answer: Bill of Lading and Bill of Entry

Bill of lading is a document issued by a shipping company acknowledging receipt of goods on board the ship and undertaking to carry them to the destination. It is also a document of title to goods. Bill of entry is a customs document filled by the importer when imported goods arrive. It gives details such as importer, exporter, ship, packages, goods, value, and customs duty. Bill of lading is connected with shipment, while bill of entry is connected with import clearance.

## 142. Exam Answer: Letter of Credit

A letter of credit is a guarantee issued by the importer's bank that it will pay the exporter up to a specified amount if required documents are presented. The exporter needs it because the importer is located in another country and there is risk of non-payment. A letter of credit makes international payment safer and is one of the most secure methods used in export-import transactions.

## 143. Exam Answer: Securing Export Payment

After shipment, the exporter sends documents such as invoice, bill of lading, packing list, insurance policy, certificate of origin, and bill of exchange through the bank. The bank presents documents to the importer. If it is a sight draft, documents are released only against payment. If it is a usance draft, documents are released against acceptance of the bill for later payment. The exporter's bank receives payment through the importer's bank and credits the exporter's account. The exporter then obtains a bank certificate of payment.

## 158. End Summary

International business includes all business activities across national borders. It is broader than international trade because it includes not only export and import but also services, investment, production abroad, licensing, franchising, contract manufacturing, joint ventures, and wholly owned subsidiaries. Countries engage in international business because resources, productivity, technology, and costs differ. International business is more complex than domestic business because it involves different countries, currencies, cultures, laws, political systems, and customer preferences. Firms enter foreign markets through exporting, contract manufacturing, licensing, franchising, joint ventures, and wholly owned subsidiaries. Export and import transactions require careful procedures, documents, finance, insurance, customs clearance, and payment security. Institutions and schemes support exporters, while IMF and WTO provide broader global monetary and trade frameworks in the textbook context.
