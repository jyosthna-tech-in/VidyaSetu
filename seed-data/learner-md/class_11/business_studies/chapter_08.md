# Chapter 8: Business Finance and Funding Options

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Business needs money at every stage. Funds are required to start a business, buy fixed assets, run daily operations, modernise technology, expand operations, and meet unexpected needs. This chapter explains the meaning of business finance, the types of financial needs, the classification of funds, major funding options, international financing, and factors affecting the choice of finance.

The key idea is:

```text
No single funding option is best for every business.
The best choice depends on purpose, period, cost, risk, control, and financial position.
```

## 2. Learning Targets

By the end of this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. Define business finance.
2. Explain why business needs funds.
3. Distinguish fixed capital and working capital.
4. Classify funds by period, ownership, and generation.
5. Explain owner funds and borrowed funds.
6. Explain internal and external funding.
7. Explain retained earnings, trade credit, factoring, lease financing, public deposits, commercial paper, shares, debentures, bank loans, financial institutions, and international finance.
8. Compare equity shares and preference shares.
9. Compare shares and debentures.
10. Explain GDR, ADR, IDR, and FCCB.
11. Choose suitable funding options for different business situations.

## 3. Meaning of Business Finance

Business finance means funds required by a business to carry out its activities.

It includes money needed for:

1. Starting business.
2. Purchasing land and building.
3. Buying plant and machinery.
4. Purchasing raw material.
5. Paying wages and salaries.
6. Paying rent, taxes, and electricity.
7. Holding inventory.
8. Giving credit to customers.
9. Expanding business.
10. Modernising technology.

Finance is called the lifeblood of business because no business can operate without adequate funds.

## 4. Why Business Needs Funds

Business needs funds for three broad reasons:

1. Fixed capital requirements.
2. Working capital requirements.
3. Growth, expansion, and modernisation.

## 5. Fixed Capital Requirements

Fixed capital means funds invested in fixed assets that remain in business for a long period.

Examples:

1. Land.
2. Building.
3. Plant.
4. Machinery.
5. Furniture.
6. Vehicles.
7. Equipment.

### 5.1 Factors Affecting Fixed Capital Need

Fixed capital depends on:

1. Nature of business.
2. Scale of operation.
3. Technology used.
4. Manufacturing or trading activity.
5. Growth plans.

A manufacturing concern usually needs more fixed capital than a trading concern.

## 6. Working Capital Requirements

Working capital means funds needed for day-to-day operations.

It is used for current assets and current expenses.

Examples:

1. Raw material.
2. Stock.
3. Bills receivable.
4. Wages.
5. Salaries.
6. Rent.
7. Electricity.
8. Taxes.
9. Transport.
10. Repairs.

### 6.1 Factors Affecting Working Capital Need

Working capital depends on:

1. Credit sales.
2. Sales turnover.
3. Inventory level.
4. Production cycle.
5. Seasonal demand.
6. Size of business.
7. Speed of debt collection.

A business selling goods on credit needs more working capital than a business selling mostly in cash.

## 7. Classification of Funds

Funds can be classified on three bases:

1. Period basis.
2. Ownership basis.
3. Generation basis.

```text
Funds
-> Period basis: long-term, medium-term, short-term
-> Ownership basis: owner funds, borrowed funds
-> Generation basis: internal, external
```

## 8. Period Basis

On the basis of period, funds are:

1. Long-term funds.
2. Medium-term funds.
3. Short-term funds.

## 9. Long-Term Funds

Long-term funds are required for a period exceeding five years.

They are generally used for:

1. Fixed assets.
2. Expansion.
3. Modernisation.
4. Large projects.

Examples:

1. Equity shares.
2. Preference shares.
3. Debentures.
4. Long-term loans.
5. Loans from financial institutions.

## 10. Medium-Term Funds

Medium-term funds are required for more than one year but not more than five years.

Examples:

1. Commercial bank loans.
2. Public deposits.
3. Lease financing.
4. Loans from financial institutions.

## 11. Short-Term Funds

Short-term funds are required for a period not exceeding one year.

They are generally used for:

1. Current assets.
2. Inventory.
3. Receivables.
4. Seasonal working capital.
5. Temporary cash shortage.

Examples:

1. Trade credit.
2. Commercial bank credit.
3. Commercial paper.
4. Factoring.

## 12. Ownership Basis

On ownership basis, funds are:

1. Owner funds.
2. Borrowed funds.

## 13. Owner Funds

Owner funds are provided by the owners of the business.

In a sole proprietorship, they come from the proprietor. In a partnership, they come from partners. In a company, they come from shareholders and retained profits.

Examples:

1. Equity share capital.
2. Preference share capital.
3. Retained earnings.

### 13.1 Features of Owner Funds

1. Remain invested for longer period.
2. Not required to be refunded during normal life of business.
3. Provide control rights to owners.
4. Carry higher risk for investors.
5. Dividend is not compulsory in the same way as interest.

## 14. Borrowed Funds

Borrowed funds are raised through loans or borrowings.

Examples:

1. Debentures.
2. Bank loans.
3. Public deposits.
4. Trade credit.
5. Loans from financial institutions.

### 14.1 Features of Borrowed Funds

1. Repaid after a fixed period.
2. Interest is paid at a fixed or agreed rate.
3. May require security.
4. Do not usually give voting control.
5. Create financial burden because interest must be paid even in low-profit periods.

## 15. Generation Basis

On generation basis, funds are:

1. Internal funds.
2. External funds.

## 16. Internal Funds

Internal funds are generated from within the business.

Examples:

1. Retained earnings.
2. Faster collection of receivables.
3. Sale of surplus inventory.
4. Better working capital management.

Internal funds are usually limited but cheaper and more flexible.

## 17. External Funds

External funds are raised from outside the business.

Examples:

1. Suppliers.
2. Lenders.
3. Investors.
4. Banks.
5. Financial institutions.
6. Public deposits.
7. Debenture holders.
8. International capital markets.

External funds can raise large amounts but may be costly and may require security or compliance.

## 18. Main Funding Options

Major options are:

1. Retained earnings.
2. Trade credit.
3. Factoring.
4. Lease financing.
5. Public deposits.
6. Commercial paper.
7. Issue of shares.
8. Debentures.
9. Commercial banks.
10. Financial institutions.
11. International financing.

## 19. Retained Earnings

Retained earnings are the part of net profit not distributed as dividend and kept in the business for future use.

It is also called:

1. Self-financing.
2. Ploughing back of profits.
3. Internal financing.

### 19.1 Merits of Retained Earnings

1. Permanent funds available to business.
2. No explicit cost such as interest or flotation cost.
3. More operational freedom.
4. Helps absorb unexpected losses.
5. May increase market price of equity shares.
6. No dilution of control.

### 19.2 Limitations of Retained Earnings

1. Excessive retention may dissatisfy shareholders because dividends fall.
2. Uncertain because profits fluctuate.
3. Opportunity cost may be ignored.
4. May lead to inefficient use of funds if management is careless.

## 20. Trade Credit

Trade credit is credit extended by one trader to another for purchase of goods and services.

The buyer receives goods now and pays later.

In buyer records, it appears as:

1. Sundry creditors.
2. Accounts payable.

### 20.1 Factors Affecting Trade Credit

1. Buyer's reputation.
2. Buyer's financial position.
3. Seller's financial position.
4. Volume of purchases.
5. Past payment record.
6. Degree of competition.
7. Industry practice.

### 20.2 Merits of Trade Credit

1. Convenient and continuous.
2. Readily available to creditworthy customers.
3. Helps promote sales.
4. Useful for increasing inventory before expected sales rise.
5. Does not create charge on assets.

### 20.3 Limitations of Trade Credit

1. May lead to overtrading.
2. Provides only limited funds.
3. Can be costly compared with some other funding methods.
4. Depends on supplier confidence.

## 21. Factoring

Factoring is a financial service in which a factor buys or manages the receivables of a business and helps collect debts from customers.

The factor may provide:

1. Discounting of bills.
2. Collection of debts.
3. Credit control.
4. Protection against bad debts in non-recourse factoring.
5. Credit information.
6. Consultancy in finance or marketing.

## 22. Types of Factoring

### 22.1 Recourse Factoring

In recourse factoring, the client is not protected against bad debt. If customer fails to pay, the risk remains with the client.

### 22.2 Non-Recourse Factoring

In non-recourse factoring, the factor assumes credit risk. If debt becomes bad, the factor bears the loss according to the agreement.

## 23. Merits of Factoring

1. May be cheaper than some bank credit options.
2. Accelerates cash flow.
3. Helps meet liabilities on time.
4. Gives predictable cash inflow from credit sales.
5. Does not create charge on assets.
6. Allows business to focus on production, marketing, and other functions.

## 24. Limitations of Factoring

1. Expensive when invoices are numerous and small.
2. Advance finance may carry higher interest cost.
3. Customers may feel uncomfortable dealing with a third-party factor.
4. May not suit all industries.

## 25. Lease Financing

Lease financing is an arrangement in which the owner of an asset gives another party the right to use the asset for a fixed period in return for periodic payment.

Parties:

1. Lessor: Owner of the asset.
2. Lessee: User of the asset.
3. Lease rental: Periodic payment made by lessee.

At the end of lease period, the asset usually returns to the lessor unless agreement provides otherwise.

## 26. Merits of Lease Financing

1. Asset can be used with lower initial investment.
2. Documentation is simpler.
3. Lease rentals may be deductible for tax computation according to rules.
4. Ownership and control of business are not diluted.
5. Debt raising capacity may not be affected in the same way as borrowing.
6. Risk of obsolescence may be borne by lessor.
7. Useful for assets that become outdated quickly, such as computers.

## 27. Limitations of Lease Financing

1. Restrictions may be placed on use of asset.
2. Lessee cannot freely alter the asset.
3. Non-renewal may affect operations.
4. Premature termination may be costly.
5. Lessee does not become owner.
6. Lessee loses residual value of asset.

## 28. Public Deposits

Public deposits are deposits raised by companies directly from the public.

The company issues a deposit receipt acknowledging the debt.

Public deposits may serve short-term and medium-term needs.

### 28.1 Merits of Public Deposits

1. Simple procedure.
2. Fewer restrictive conditions than loans.
3. Lower cost than some bank or institutional borrowings.
4. Usually no charge on assets.
5. No dilution of control because depositors have no voting rights.

### 28.2 Limitations of Public Deposits

1. New companies may find it difficult to raise funds.
2. Public response may be uncertain.
3. Not reliable for large fund needs.
4. Collection may be difficult.
5. Regulated by authorities.

## 29. Commercial Paper

Commercial paper is an unsecured money market instrument issued in the form of a promissory note.

It is used by highly rated companies to raise short-term funds.

### 29.1 Features

1. Unsecured.
2. Short-term.
3. Issued by financially sound firms.
4. Freely transferable.
5. Regulated by financial market rules.
6. Can be used by investors to park surplus funds.

### 29.2 Merits of Commercial Paper

1. No security or restrictive condition.
2. High liquidity.
3. Cost may be lower than bank loan.
4. Continuous funding possible by issuing new paper when old paper matures.
5. Useful investment for companies with surplus funds.

### 29.3 Limitations of Commercial Paper

1. Only highly rated firms can issue it.
2. Amount depends on market liquidity.
3. Maturity cannot be easily extended.
4. Impersonal method of financing.
5. Not suitable for new or weak firms.

## 30. Issue of Shares

Share capital is capital raised by issue of shares.

A company's capital is divided into small units called shares.

Example:

A company may issue 1,00,000 shares of Rs. 10 each, making share capital of Rs. 10,00,000.

Types:

1. Equity shares.
2. Preference shares.

## 31. Equity Shares

Equity shares represent ownership capital of a company. Equity shareholders are owners and risk bearers.

They receive dividend after other claims are met and may participate in management through voting rights.

They are called residual owners because they get what remains after other claims on income and assets are settled.

### 31.1 Merits of Equity Shares

1. Suitable for investors willing to take risk for higher return.
2. Dividend is not compulsory.
3. Permanent capital.
4. Provides cushion to creditors.
5. Improves creditworthiness.
6. No charge on assets.
7. Democratic control through voting rights.

### 31.2 Limitations of Equity Shares

1. Not suitable for investors wanting steady income.
2. Cost of equity may be high.
3. Additional issue dilutes control and earnings of existing shareholders.
4. More legal formalities and procedural delay.
5. Dividend fluctuates with profits.

## 32. Preference Shares

Preference shares carry preferential rights over equity shares in two matters:

1. Fixed dividend before equity dividend.
2. Repayment of capital before equity shareholders at liquidation, after creditor claims.

Preference shares have features of both equity and debentures.

### 32.1 Merits of Preference Shares

1. Steady income through fixed dividend rate.
2. Lower risk than equity for investors.
3. No dilution of control because voting rights are limited.
4. Can help equity shareholders receive higher dividend in good years.
5. Preference in repayment over equity shareholders.
6. No charge on assets.

### 32.2 Limitations of Preference Shares

1. Not suitable for high-risk, high-return investors.
2. Dilutes claim of equity shareholders over assets.
3. Dividend rate may be higher than interest on debentures.
4. Dividend is paid only when profit is available and board declares it.
5. Preference dividend is not tax deductible like interest on loans.

## 33. Types of Preference Shares

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Cumulative | Unpaid dividend accumulates for future years |
| Non-cumulative | Unpaid dividend does not accumulate |
| Participating | May participate in surplus profit after fixed dividend |
| Non-participating | Does not participate in surplus profit |
| Convertible | Can be converted into equity shares |
| Non-convertible | Cannot be converted into equity shares |

## 34. Debentures

Debentures are instruments used by a company to raise long-term debt capital.

Debenture holders are creditors of the company.

They receive fixed interest at specified intervals and get repayment at a future date.

### 34.1 Merits of Debentures

1. Preferred by investors wanting fixed income with lower risk than equity.
2. Debenture holders do not share profits.
3. Suitable when sales and earnings are stable.
4. No dilution of management control because debentures usually have no voting rights.
5. Interest on debentures is tax deductible.
6. Cost may be lower than equity or preference capital.

### 34.2 Limitations of Debentures

1. Fixed interest burden even when profits are low.
2. Redeemable debentures require repayment on specified date.
3. Borrowing capacity reduces after debenture issue.
4. Higher financial risk if earnings fluctuate.

## 35. Types of Debentures

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Secured | Charge is created on company assets |
| Unsecured | No charge on assets |
| Registered | Holder name recorded in company register |
| Bearer | Transferable by delivery |
| Convertible | Can be converted into equity shares |
| Non-convertible | Cannot be converted into equity shares |
| First | Repaid before other debentures |
| Second | Repaid after first debentures |
| Zero interest | No explicit interest; return comes through discount or redemption difference |

## 36. Commercial Banks

Commercial banks provide short-term and medium-term funds to businesses.

Forms of bank credit include:

1. Cash credit.
2. Overdraft.
3. Term loan.
4. Bill discounting.
5. Letter of credit.

### 36.1 Merits of Bank Credit

1. Timely assistance.
2. Confidentiality.
3. No prospectus or underwriting required.
4. Flexible loan amount.
5. Advance repayment may be possible.
6. Suitable for short and medium needs.

### 36.2 Limitations of Bank Credit

1. Generally available for short periods.
2. Renewal may be uncertain.
3. Detailed investigation is done.
4. Security or personal surety may be required.
5. Restrictive terms may affect normal business operations.

## 37. Financial Institutions

Financial institutions are established by central and state governments to provide industrial finance.

They are also called development banks.

They provide:

1. Long-term finance.
2. Medium-term finance.
3. Owned capital.
4. Loan capital.
5. Technical advice.
6. Managerial services.
7. Market surveys.

They are suitable for expansion, reorganisation, and modernisation.

### 37.1 Merits of Financial Institutions

1. Provide long-term finance.
2. Provide technical, managerial, and financial advice.
3. Improve goodwill of borrowing company.
4. Easy instalment repayment.
5. Funds may be available even during depression.

### 37.2 Limitations of Financial Institutions

1. Rigid loan criteria.
2. Many formalities.
3. Time-consuming process.
4. Restrictions may be imposed, such as dividend restrictions.
5. Nominee directors may be appointed, reducing management freedom.

## 38. International Financing

Business can also raise funds internationally, especially when operations become global.

Major international financing options are:

1. Commercial banks.
2. International agencies and development banks.
3. International capital markets.

## 39. International Commercial Banks

Commercial banks across the world provide foreign currency loans for business purposes.

They are important for non-trade international operations and foreign currency funding.

## 40. International Agencies and Development Banks

International agencies and development banks provide medium-term and long-term loans and grants for trade, development, and projects in less-developed regions.

Examples include:

1. International Finance Corporation.
2. EXIM Bank.
3. Asian Development Bank.

## 41. International Capital Markets

Modern firms raise funds internationally through instruments such as:

1. GDR.
2. ADR.
3. IDR.
4. FCCB.

## 42. Global Depository Receipt

GDR stands for Global Depository Receipt.

In the Indian context, a GDR is issued abroad by an Indian company to raise funds in foreign currency. It is listed and traded on a foreign stock exchange.

Features:

1. Denominated in foreign currency, often US dollars.
2. Negotiable instrument.
3. Can be traded like other securities.
4. Represents underlying shares.
5. Holder may convert it into shares it represents.
6. Holder gets benefits such as dividend and capital appreciation.

## 43. American Depository Receipt

ADR stands for American Depository Receipt.

It is a depository receipt issued by a company in the USA. It is bought and sold in American markets like regular stocks.

### 43.1 GDR and ADR Difference

| Basis | GDR | ADR |
|---|---|---|
| Market | Foreign markets generally | USA market |
| Investor base | International investors | American investors |
| Listing | Foreign stock exchange | US stock exchange |
| Similarity | Represents shares of issuing company | Represents shares of issuing company |

## 44. Indian Depository Receipt

IDR stands for Indian Depository Receipt.

It is denominated in Indian rupees and issued by an Indian depository to allow a foreign company to raise funds from the Indian securities market.

Features:

1. Foreign company deposits shares with Indian depository.
2. Indian depository issues receipts to Indian investors.
3. Indian residents can invest according to rules.
4. Benefits of underlying shares accrue to IDR holders.

## 45. Foreign Currency Convertible Bonds

FCCBs are foreign currency convertible bonds.

They are equity-linked debt securities issued in foreign currency. They carry fixed interest and can be converted into equity shares or depository receipts after a specified period.

Features:

1. Issued in foreign currency.
2. Carry fixed interest.
3. Convertible into equity or receipts.
4. Traded on foreign exchanges.
5. Similar to convertible debentures.

## 46. Inter Corporate Deposits

Inter Corporate Deposits, or ICDs, are unsecured short-term deposits made by one company with another company.

Types include:

1. Three-month deposits.
2. Six-month deposits.
3. Call deposits.

They are used for short-term cash management and usually carry interest higher than banks.

## 47. Factors Affecting Choice of Funding Option

No funding option is free from limitations. Business should use a suitable mix.

Factors affecting choice are:

1. Cost.
2. Financial strength and stability.
3. Form of organisation and legal status.
4. Purpose and time period.
5. Risk profile.
6. Control.
7. Effect on creditworthiness.
8. Flexibility and ease.
9. Tax benefits.

## 48. Cost

Cost includes:

1. Cost of obtaining funds.
2. Cost of using funds.

A firm should consider interest, dividend expectations, flotation cost, documentation cost, and compliance cost.

## 49. Financial Strength and Stability

If earnings are stable, the firm may use fixed charge funds such as debentures or loans.

If earnings are uncertain, too much debt may be risky because interest must be paid even during loss.

## 50. Form of Organisation and Legal Status

Not every firm can use every funding method.

Example:

A partnership cannot issue equity shares. Only a company can issue shares.

## 51. Purpose and Time Period

Funds should match the purpose and period.

Examples:

1. Long-term expansion should use long-term funds.
2. Temporary inventory need may use trade credit or bank credit.
3. Machinery can be acquired through long-term loan, equity, debenture, or lease.

## 52. Risk Profile

Equity has lower repayment risk for the company because dividend is not compulsory and capital is repaid only at winding up.

Debt has higher financial risk because interest and principal repayment are compulsory.

## 53. Control

Equity may dilute control because shareholders have voting rights.

Debentures and loans usually do not dilute control, but lenders may impose conditions.

## 54. Effect on Creditworthiness

Excessive debt may reduce creditworthiness. Secured debentures may reduce protection available to unsecured creditors.

## 55. Flexibility and Ease

Some methods are easy and flexible, such as trade credit. Others require detailed procedures, investigations, and documents, such as institutional loans or public issue of shares.

## 56. Tax Benefits

Interest on debentures and loans is generally deductible as expense for tax purposes. Dividend on shares is not treated the same way for company tax computation.

## 57. Quick Matching Guide

| Business need | Suitable options |
|---|---|
| Day-to-day cash | Trade credit, bank credit, factoring |
| Seasonal inventory | Trade credit, bank credit, commercial paper |
| Large expansion | Equity, debentures, financial institutions, retained earnings |
| Modern machinery | Lease financing, term loan, debentures |
| No control dilution | Debentures, loans, public deposits, retained earnings |
| No fixed interest burden | Equity, retained earnings |
| Investor wants steady income | Preference shares, debentures |
| International funding | GDR, ADR, FCCB, foreign currency loans |

## 58. Short Answer Practice

### 58.1 What is business finance?

Business finance means funds required by a business for starting, running, expanding, and modernising its operations.

### 58.2 Why do businesses need funds?

Businesses need funds for fixed assets, working capital, expansion, modernisation, technology upgradation, inventory, debt payment, and daily expenses.

### 58.3 List long-term and short-term funding options.

Long-term options include equity shares, preference shares, debentures, long-term loans, retained earnings, and financial institution loans. Short-term options include trade credit, bank credit, factoring, commercial paper, and inter corporate deposits.

### 58.4 Difference between internal and external funds.

Internal funds are generated within the business, such as retained earnings and collection of receivables. External funds are raised from outside parties such as banks, investors, suppliers, financial institutions, and public deposits.

### 58.5 What preferential rights do preference shareholders enjoy?

Preference shareholders enjoy preferential right to receive fixed dividend before equity shareholders and preferential right to repayment of capital before equity shareholders at liquidation after creditor claims are settled.

### 58.6 Difference between GDR and ADR.

GDR is issued in foreign markets generally and may be listed on foreign stock exchanges. ADR is issued in the USA and traded in American markets. Both represent underlying shares of the issuing company.

## 59. Long Answer Practice

### 59.1 Explain trade credit and bank credit as short-term finance.

Trade credit is credit given by one trader to another for purchase of goods or services. It is convenient, continuous, promotes sales, and does not create charge on assets, but it gives limited funds, can be costly, and may lead to overtrading.

Bank credit includes cash credit, overdraft, term loan, bill discounting, and letters of credit. It provides timely finance, maintains secrecy, avoids prospectus formalities, and is flexible, but it may require security, detailed investigation, restrictive terms, and renewal uncertainty.

### 59.2 What can a large industrial enterprise use for modernisation and expansion?

A large industrial enterprise can raise funds through equity shares, preference shares, debentures, retained earnings, financial institutions, commercial banks, public deposits, lease financing, GDRs, ADRs, FCCBs, and international loans. The final choice depends on cost, period, risk, control, tax benefits, and financial strength.

### 59.3 Advantages of debentures over equity shares.

Debentures provide fixed income to investors, do not dilute control, may be less costly than equity, are suitable when earnings are stable, and interest on debentures is tax deductible. Equity shares dilute voting control and may have a higher expected cost because shareholders bear higher risk.

### 59.4 Merits and demerits of public deposits and retained earnings.

Public deposits have simple procedure, lower cost than many loans, no charge on assets, and no control dilution. Their limits are difficulty for new companies, uncertain public response, and unsuitability for very large amounts.

Retained earnings are permanent, internally generated, cost no explicit interest, give freedom, and help absorb losses. Their limits are uncertain profits, shareholder dissatisfaction due to lower dividends, and possible inefficient use.

## 60. Common Mistakes and Corrections

|---|---|
| Working capital means only cash | It includes current assets and funds for day-to-day operations |
| Equity shareholders get fixed dividend | Equity dividend depends on profit and board decision |
| Debenture holders are owners | They are creditors |
| Preference shareholders always have voting rights | Their voting rights are generally limited |
| Retained earnings are free in every sense | They have opportunity cost |
| Trade credit has no cost | It may be costly through higher price or lost discount |
| Commercial paper can be issued by any firm | Usually only highly rated firms can issue it |
| Lease means ownership transfer | Lease gives right to use asset, not ownership |
| Public deposits dilute control | Depositors do not get voting rights |

## 64. End Summary

Business finance means funds required for starting, operating, expanding, and modernising business. Funds are needed for fixed capital, working capital, and growth. They may be classified by period into long-term, medium-term, and short-term funds; by ownership into owner funds and borrowed funds; and by generation into internal and external funds. Major funding options include retained earnings, trade credit, factoring, lease financing, public deposits, commercial paper, shares, debentures, banks, financial institutions, and international finance. Equity shares provide ownership capital, preference shares provide preferential rights, and debentures provide borrowed capital. The choice of finance depends on cost, financial strength, legal form, purpose, time period, risk, control, creditworthiness, flexibility, ease, and tax benefits.
