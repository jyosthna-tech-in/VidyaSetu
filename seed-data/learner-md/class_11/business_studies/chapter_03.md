# Chapter 3: Private, Public and Global Enterprises

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

This chapter explains how enterprises are classified by ownership and area of operation. Some businesses are owned by private individuals or groups. Some are owned by the government. Some operate across national boundaries as global enterprises. Some are formed through joint ventures or public-private partnerships.

The chapter is built around five linked ideas:

1. Private sector and public sector.
2. Forms of public sector enterprises.
3. Changing role of the public sector.
4. Global enterprises.
5. Joint ventures and public-private partnership.

## 2. Learning Targets

By the end of this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning of private sector and public sector.
2. Explain why India is called a mixed economy.
3. Identify forms of public sector enterprises.
4. Compare departmental undertakings, statutory corporations, and government companies.
5. Explain the role of the public sector before and after 1991.
6. Explain the meaning and features of global enterprises.
7. Explain joint ventures and their types.
8. List benefits of joint ventures.
9. Explain public-private partnership.
10. Answer case-based and policy-based questions on enterprise ownership.

## 3. Core Idea

An economy contains many kinds of enterprises. They differ in ownership, control, purpose, scale, legal form, market reach, and accountability.

```text
Enterprises by ownership and operation
-> Private sector enterprises
-> Public sector enterprises
-> Global enterprises
-> Joint ventures
-> Public-private partnerships
```

The main question is:

```text
Who owns the enterprise, who controls it, and for what purpose is it run?
```

## 4. Private Sector

The private sector consists of enterprises owned, managed, and controlled by private individuals or groups.

### 4.1 Forms in the Private Sector

Private sector enterprises may take forms such as:

1. Sole proprietorship.
2. Partnership.
3. Joint Hindu family business.
4. Cooperative society.
5. Private company.
6. Public company owned by private shareholders.

### 4.2 Main Features of Private Sector

1. Owned by private persons or groups.
2. Operated mainly for profit.
3. Managed by owners, partners, directors, or professional managers.
4. Capital comes from private savings, partners, shareholders, loans, or capital market.
5. Risk is borne by private owners or investors.
6. Decisions are usually faster than government-controlled enterprises.

### 4.3 Examples

1. A local grocery shop.
2. A partnership firm of lawyers.
3. A private hospital.
4. A manufacturing company owned by private shareholders.
5. A private courier company.

## 5. Public Sector

The public sector consists of enterprises owned and managed wholly or partly by the government.

The owner may be:

1. Central government.
2. State government.
3. Central and state government together.
4. A government department.
5. A statutory corporation.
6. A government company.

### 5.1 Main Features of Public Sector

1. Owned by government wholly or partly.
2. Uses public funds.
3. Accountable to the public through government systems.
4. May pursue social, strategic, and economic objectives.
5. May be controlled by a ministry, an Act, or company law.
6. Often operates in infrastructure, defence, transport, energy, finance, and basic industries.

### 5.2 Examples

Examples may include:

1. Indian Railways as a departmental undertaking.
2. Public sector banks.
3. State electricity boards or corporations.
4. Government companies in energy, engineering, mining, or infrastructure.
5. Public transport undertakings.

## 6. Mixed Economy

A mixed economy is an economy where both private sector and public sector enterprises operate.

India is called a mixed economy because private enterprises and government enterprises coexist and contribute to economic development.

### 6.1 Why Mixed Economy Was Adopted

At independence, India needed:

1. Rapid industrialisation.
2. Infrastructure.
3. Heavy industries.
4. Balanced regional development.
5. Employment generation.
6. Control over strategic sectors.
7. Private enterprise and entrepreneurship.

The government allowed private enterprise but also created public enterprises to handle sectors where private investment was insufficient or national interest was involved.

## 7. Private Sector and Public Sector: Comparison

| Basis | Private sector | Public sector |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership | Private individuals or groups | Government wholly or partly |
| Main motive | Profit and growth | Public welfare, strategic needs, economic development, and sometimes profit |
| Capital | Private funds, loans, shares | Public funds, government capital, public borrowings, revenue |
| Control | Owners, partners, directors, managers | Ministry, government, board, statute, or government-appointed management |
| Accountability | Owners, shareholders, customers, regulators | Government, Parliament or legislature, public, auditors |
| Flexibility | Usually higher | Often lower because of rules |
| Risk | Private owners or investors | Government and public funds, depending on form |
| Examples | Shops, private companies, professional firms | Railways, statutory corporations, government companies |

## 8. Public Sector Enterprises

Public sector enterprises are enterprises owned and controlled by the government to participate in economic and business operations.

They are generally characterized by:

1. Public ownership.
2. Use of public funds.
3. Public accountability.
4. Economic and social objectives.
5. Government control or supervision.

## 9. Forms of Public Sector Enterprises

Public sector enterprises may be organised in three main forms:

1. Departmental undertakings.
2. Statutory corporations.
3. Government companies.

```text
Public sector enterprise
-> Departmental undertaking
-> Statutory corporation
-> Government company
```

Each form gives a different balance between government control and operational autonomy.

## 10. Departmental Undertakings

A departmental undertaking is the oldest and most traditional form of public enterprise. It is established as a department or part of a government ministry.

It is not separate from the government. It acts through government officers and employees.

### 10.1 Examples

1. Railways in the traditional textbook example.
2. Postal department in the traditional textbook example.
3. Government defence production departments in some contexts.

## 11. Features of Departmental Undertakings

### 11.1 Finance from Government Treasury

Funds come directly from the government budget. Revenue earned is deposited into the government treasury.

### 11.2 Government Accounting and Audit

They follow accounting and audit controls applicable to government departments.

### 11.3 Employees Are Government Servants

Employees are government employees. Their recruitment, service conditions, transfer rules, and discipline are governed by government rules.

### 11.4 Direct Ministry Control

The undertaking is under direct control of the concerned ministry.

### 11.5 Accountability to Ministry and Legislature

The undertaking is accountable to the ministry, and indirectly to the public through Parliament or the state legislature.

## 12. Merits of Departmental Undertakings

### 12.1 Strong Parliamentary Control

The legislature can exercise effective control because the undertaking is part of government.

### 12.2 High Public Accountability

Use of public money is subject to government procedures, audit, and legislative oversight.

### 12.3 Revenue Goes to Treasury

Revenue earned becomes income of the government.

### 12.4 Suitable for National Security

This form is suitable where secrecy, defence, strategic control, or national security is important.

## 13. Limitations of Departmental Undertakings

### 13.1 Lack of Flexibility

Business needs quick and flexible decisions, but departmental undertakings are bound by government procedures.

### 13.2 Delay in Decision-Making

Important decisions may require ministry approval, creating delays.

### 13.3 Limited Business Initiative

Officials may avoid risk because of strict rules and fear of criticism.

### 13.4 Red Tapism

Files may move through many levels before action is taken.

### 13.5 Political Interference

Ministry control can lead to political influence in business decisions.

### 13.6 Weak Consumer Orientation

Because competition and profit pressure may be low, customer service may suffer.

## 14. Statutory Corporations

A statutory corporation is a public enterprise created by a special Act of Parliament or state legislature.

The Act defines:

1. Its objectives.
2. Its powers.
3. Its functions.
4. Its relationship with government.
5. Its rules for employees.
6. Its financial powers.

### 14.1 Core Idea

A statutory corporation combines:

```text
Government ownership + Legal autonomy + Operational flexibility
```

It is a separate legal body and can act in its own name.

## 15. Features of Statutory Corporations

### 15.1 Created by Special Act

The corporation comes into existence through a specific law.

### 15.2 Wholly Owned by State

It is generally owned by the government. The government has ultimate financial responsibility.

### 15.3 Separate Corporate Body

It can enter contracts, acquire property, sue, and be sued in its own name.

### 15.4 Financial Independence

It can obtain funds through government borrowings, public borrowings, and revenue from sale of goods or services.

### 15.5 Separate Accounting and Audit Rules

It is not subject to exactly the same budget, accounting, and audit rules as ordinary government departments.

### 15.6 Employee Rules Defined by Act

Employees are generally not ordinary civil servants. Their service conditions are governed by the Act and rules of the corporation.

## 16. Merits of Statutory Corporations

### 16.1 Operational Autonomy

They enjoy more freedom than departmental undertakings.

### 16.2 Financial Flexibility

They can use revenue and manage finances with greater independence.

### 16.3 Own Policies and Procedures

They can frame policies within the powers given by the Act.

### 16.4 Useful for Economic Development

They combine government authority with business-like initiative.

## 17. Limitations of Statutory Corporations

### 17.1 Autonomy May Be Limited in Practice

Although they are autonomous in theory, rules and approvals may still restrict them.

### 17.2 Government and Political Interference

Major decisions may be influenced by government or political considerations.

### 17.3 Corruption Risk

Where public dealings are large and controls are weak, corruption may arise.

### 17.4 Delay Due to Government Reference

If disagreement arises between board and government, matters may be referred back to government, delaying decisions.

## 18. Government Company

A government company is a company in which at least 51 percent of paid-up share capital is held by the central government, state government, or jointly by central and state governments. It also includes a subsidiary of a government company.

It is registered under the Companies Act, 2013.

### 18.1 Core Idea

A government company has company form but government control.

```text
Company law structure + Government majority ownership
```

### 18.2 Ownership

Shares may be held in the name of the President of India or the Governor of a state, depending on ownership.

## 19. Features of Government Company

### 19.1 Created Under Companies Act

No special Act is needed. It is incorporated like other companies under company law.

### 19.2 Separate Legal Entity

It can sue and be sued, enter contracts, own property, and borrow money in its own name.

### 19.3 Management Under Company Law

Management is regulated by company law, memorandum, and articles of association.

### 19.4 Own Employee Rules

Employees are appointed according to rules of the company, not necessarily ordinary civil service rules.

### 19.5 Audit and Reporting

An auditor may be appointed by the central government. Annual reports are placed before Parliament or the state legislature, as applicable.

### 19.6 Funds

Funds come from government shareholding, private shareholders, borrowings, and capital market routes where permitted.

## 20. Merits of Government Company

### 20.1 Easy Formation Compared with Statutory Corporation

A separate Act of Parliament is not needed. It can be formed by following company law.

### 20.2 Separate Legal Entity

It is separate from the government and can function in its own name.

### 20.3 Managerial Autonomy

It can take business decisions with more autonomy than a departmental undertaking.

### 20.4 Market Regulation Role

It can provide goods and services at reasonable prices and help reduce unhealthy business practices.

### 20.5 Commercial Orientation

It can compete with private sector companies while still serving public goals.

## 21. Limitations of Government Company

### 21.1 Company Form May Become Formal Only

If government owns almost all shares, the company form may not bring real independence.

### 21.2 Weak Direct Parliamentary Control

It may not be directly answerable to Parliament in the same way as a department.

### 21.3 Government Dominance

Since government is majority shareholder, management may remain under government influence.

### 21.4 Public Accountability Concern

A company funded by government money should remain accountable, but company structure may sometimes reduce direct constitutional responsibility.

## 22. Public Sector Forms: Comparison

| Basis | Departmental undertaking | Statutory corporation | Government company |
|---|---|---|---|
| Formation | By government department | By special Act | By Companies Act |
| Legal entity | No separate legal entity | Separate legal entity | Separate legal entity |
| Ownership | Government | Government | At least 51 percent government shareholding |
| Control | Direct ministry control | Control as per Act | Board and government as majority owner |
| Finance | Government budget | Own revenue, borrowings, government funds | Share capital, borrowings, market funds |
| Employees | Government servants | Governed by Act | Governed by company rules |
| Flexibility | Low | Moderate to high in theory | Higher than department |
| Accountability | Direct to ministry and legislature | As provided by Act | Through company reporting and government ownership |
| Suitability | Defence, strategic, essential government services | Public utilities and development bodies | Commercial public sector operations |

## 23. Changing Role of Public Sector

The role of the public sector has changed over time. At independence, public sector was expected to build the foundation of industrial development. After 1991, the emphasis shifted toward competition, efficiency, restructuring, and reducing government ownership in non-strategic areas.

## 24. Role of Public Sector Before 1991

### 24.1 Development of Infrastructure

Industrialisation requires roads, railways, ports, power, communication, fuel, and basic industries. Private sector often lacked the capital or willingness to invest in such heavy and long-term areas.

The public sector invested in:

1. Steel plants.
2. Power generation.
3. Railways.
4. Civil aviation.
5. Petroleum.
6. Coal.
7. Heavy engineering.
8. Fertilisers.
9. Petrochemicals.
10. State trading.

### 24.2 Regional Balance

Industrial development before independence was concentrated in a few regions, especially port towns and already developed areas.

The government used public sector enterprises to develop backward areas by:

1. Setting up industries in less-developed regions.
2. Creating employment.
3. Developing ancillary industries.
4. Building infrastructure.
5. Reducing regional disparities.

### 24.3 Economies of Scale

Some industries require very large investment and large-scale operation to be economical.

Examples:

1. Electric power.
2. Natural gas.
3. Petroleum.
4. Telephone systems.
5. Heavy engineering.

The public sector could mobilise large capital and operate at a scale private firms could not easily manage at that time.

### 24.4 Check on Concentration of Economic Power

If only a few private business houses controlled heavy industries, wealth and economic power could become concentrated. Public sector helped reduce monopoly tendencies and spread benefits more widely.

### 24.5 Import Substitution

India needed self-reliance in heavy machinery, engineering goods, and basic industries. Public sector enterprises helped produce goods that would otherwise have to be imported.

Public enterprises also helped expand exports through state trading institutions.

## 25. Public Sector Policy Since 1991

The 1991 industrial policy changed the role of public sector. The new policy emphasized liberalisation, privatisation, and globalisation.

The broad policy direction included:

1. Restructuring and reviving viable public sector units.
2. Closing units that could not be revived.
3. Reducing government equity in non-strategic enterprises where needed.
4. Protecting worker interests during restructuring.
5. Encouraging efficiency and competition.

## 26. Main Public Sector Reforms After 1991

### 26.1 Reduction in Reserved Industries

Earlier, many industries were reserved for the public sector. The number was reduced from 17 to 8, and later to 3.

This meant private sector could enter many areas where only public sector had earlier operated.

Strategic areas retained for public sector included areas such as atomic energy, arms, and rail transport in the textbook context.

### 26.2 Disinvestment

Disinvestment means sale of government equity shares in selected public sector enterprises to private investors, institutions, employees, or the public.

Objectives of disinvestment:

1. Raise funds for the government.
2. Reduce public resources locked in non-strategic enterprises.
3. Reduce public debt and interest burden.
4. Transfer commercial risk to private investors.
5. Improve managerial performance.
6. Encourage wider ownership.
7. Introduce market discipline and corporate governance.

### 26.3 Policy for Sick Units

Sick public sector units were to be treated similarly to sick private units. Units could be restructured, revived, or closed depending on viability.

Worker protection measures included retraining, redeployment, compensation, voluntary separation, or voluntary retirement schemes.

### 26.4 Memorandum of Understanding System

Under the MoU system, public sector managements received more autonomy but were held accountable for agreed targets.

The MoU clarified:

1. Targets.
2. Performance expectations.
3. Autonomy granted.
4. Relationship between enterprise and ministry.
5. Accountability for results.

## 27. Public Sector in the Competitive Economy

After reforms, public sector enterprises were expected to:

1. Compete with private firms.
2. Improve efficiency.
3. Reduce losses.
4. Use resources better.
5. Focus on strategic and social priorities.
6. Be accountable for return on investment.
7. Avoid depending only on budgetary support.

Public and private sectors are now viewed as complementary parts of national development rather than completely separate rivals.

## 28. Global Enterprises

Global enterprises are huge business organisations that operate in several countries through branches, subsidiaries, affiliates, factories, offices, and marketing networks.

They are also called multinational corporations or MNCs.

### 28.1 Simple Meaning

A global enterprise is a company that carries on production, marketing, research, finance, or business operations in more than one country.

### 28.2 Main Character

Global enterprises are usually large in:

1. Capital.
2. Technology.
3. Product range.
4. Research capacity.
5. Marketing network.
6. International reach.
7. Brand power.

## 29. Features of Global Enterprises

### 29.1 Huge Capital Resources

Global enterprises have massive financial strength. They can raise funds through shares, debentures, bonds, banks, financial institutions, international markets, and retained profits.

Because of their financial strength, they can survive difficult market conditions better than small firms.

### 29.2 Foreign Collaboration

Global enterprises often collaborate with domestic companies for:

1. Technology transfer.
2. Brand use.
3. Production.
4. Marketing.
5. Distribution.
6. Research.

Such collaborations may benefit domestic firms but may also include restrictive conditions relating to technology, pricing, control, or payments.

### 29.3 Advanced Technology

MNCs often use advanced production methods, high-quality systems, modern machinery, and international standards.

This can improve productivity and product quality in host countries.

### 29.4 Product Innovation

Global enterprises often have strong research and development departments. They can invest heavily in new products and improved designs.

### 29.5 Strong Marketing Strategies

They use advanced market research, advertising, sales promotion, brand management, and distribution systems.

Their brands may already be known internationally, making market entry easier.

### 29.6 Expansion of Market Territory

Their operations extend beyond national borders. They operate through subsidiaries, branches, and affiliates in many countries.

### 29.7 Centralised Control

Headquarters in the home country usually controls broad policies, strategy, capital allocation, brand standards, and technology. Day-to-day operations may be handled locally.

## 30. Benefits and Concerns of Global Enterprises

### 30.1 Possible Benefits

1. Advanced technology.
2. Better quality standards.
3. Employment.
4. Exports.
5. Competition.
6. Wider product choice.
7. Investment.
8. Skill development.

### 30.2 Possible Concerns

1. Dominant market power.
2. Pressure on small domestic firms.
3. Restrictive collaboration terms.
4. Profit repatriation.
5. Excessive foreign control in sensitive areas.
6. Concentration of economic power.

## 31. Joint Ventures

A joint venture is an arrangement in which two or more businesses join together for a common purpose and mutual benefit.

The parties pool resources, share risks, share rewards, and contribute strengths such as technology, finance, distribution, brand, market access, or management skill.

### 31.1 Why Businesses Enter Joint Ventures

Businesses enter joint ventures to:

1. Expand business.
2. Enter new markets.
3. Develop new products.
4. Share technology.
5. Share capital burden.
6. Use another firm's distribution network.
7. Combine complementary strengths.
8. Reduce risk.

### 31.2 Joint Venture Agreement

A joint venture must be based on a clear agreement. The agreement should cover:

1. Objective of the venture.
2. Capital contribution.
3. Technology contribution.
4. Management structure.
5. Sharing of profit and loss.
6. Responsibilities of each party.
7. Duration.
8. Exit terms.
9. Dispute settlement.
10. Government approvals and licenses.

## 32. Types of Joint Ventures

The chapter explains two types:

1. Contractual joint venture.
2. Equity-based joint venture.

## 33. Contractual Joint Venture

In a contractual joint venture, no new jointly owned entity is created. The parties only enter into an agreement to work together.

### 33.1 Features

1. Two or more parties agree to run or support a business venture.
2. Each party brings inputs.
3. Parties exercise some control.
4. Relationship continues for a period and is not merely one transaction.
5. Ownership of a new entity is not created.

### 33.2 Example

A franchise arrangement may have features of a contractual joint venture. One party provides brand, system, and know-how; the other operates the outlet under the agreement.

## 34. Equity-Based Joint Venture

In an equity-based joint venture, a separate business entity is created or one party joins ownership of an existing entity.

### 34.1 Features

1. A separate entity is formed or shared.
2. Parties share ownership.
3. Parties share management.
4. Parties share capital responsibilities.
5. Parties share profits and losses according to agreement.
6. The entity may take the form of company, partnership, limited liability partnership, trust, or other permitted form.

## 35. Benefits of Joint Ventures

### 35.1 Increased Resources and Capacity

Parties combine financial, human, technological, and managerial resources. This helps the venture expand faster.

### 35.2 Access to New Markets

A foreign partner may get access to the Indian market, while an Indian partner may get access to global markets.

### 35.3 Access to Distribution Networks

Partners can use each other's dealers, distributors, retail outlets, logistics systems, and customer relationships.

### 35.4 Access to Technology

Joint ventures allow firms to use advanced technology without developing everything independently.

### 35.5 Innovation

Combining different skills and knowledge can lead to new products, improved processes, and better designs.

### 35.6 Lower Cost of Production

International firms may use India's skilled workforce, raw materials, and production base to produce quality goods at lower cost.

### 35.7 Established Brand Name

One party may benefit from the other party's established goodwill and brand recognition.

### 35.8 Risk Sharing

Large ventures are risky. Joint ventures reduce the burden on one firm by sharing investment and risk.

## 36. Public-Private Partnership

Public-private partnership, or PPP, is a relationship between public sector entities and private sector entities for providing infrastructure or public services by sharing tasks, responsibilities, risks, resources, and expertise.

### 36.1 Public Partners

Public partners may include:

1. Government ministries.
2. Government departments.
3. Municipal bodies.
4. State-owned enterprises.
5. Public authorities.

### 36.2 Private Partners

Private partners may include:

1. Domestic businesses.
2. Foreign businesses.
3. Investors.
4. Technical experts.
5. Service providers.
6. Infrastructure developers.

### 36.3 Community Stakeholders

Some PPPs may also involve non-government organisations or community-based groups when local people are directly affected.

## 37. Features of PPP

1. Public and private entities work together.
2. It is often used for infrastructure and public services.
3. Tasks and risks are allocated between partners.
4. Government ensures public welfare and social obligations.
5. Private partner brings efficiency, technology, management, and innovation.
6. Government may provide land, capital support, assets, approvals, or policy backing.
7. Private partner may design, build, finance, operate, or maintain the facility depending on the model.

## 38. Areas Where PPP May Be Used

PPP may be used in:

1. Roads.
2. Railways.
3. Power generation.
4. Power distribution.
5. Water supply.
6. Sanitation.
7. Waste management.
8. Pipelines.
9. Hospitals.
10. School buildings.
11. Stadiums.
12. Air traffic systems.
13. Housing.
14. Information technology systems.

## 39. PPP Model: Strengths and Weaknesses

### 39.1 Strengths

1. Transfers design and construction risk to the private party where structured that way.
2. May speed up infrastructure creation.
3. Uses private sector efficiency.
4. Allows government to focus on social obligations and policy.
5. Can improve service delivery.

### 39.2 Weaknesses

1. Conflict may arise between public welfare and private profit.
2. Environmental concerns may create disputes.
3. Private finance may not come easily in all models.
4. Poor contracts can create long-term problems.
5. Public accountability can become complex.

## 43. Short Answer Practice

### 43.1 Explain private sector and public sector.

Private sector consists of enterprises owned and controlled by private individuals or groups. Public sector consists of enterprises owned and managed wholly or partly by government. Private sector is usually profit-oriented, while public sector also pursues social, strategic, and developmental objectives.

### 43.2 State the forms of organisation in the private sector.

Forms in the private sector include sole proprietorship, partnership, joint Hindu family business, cooperative society, private company, and public company owned by private shareholders.

### 43.3 What are the forms of public sector enterprises?

The three main forms of public sector enterprises are departmental undertakings, statutory corporations, and government companies.

### 43.4 Why is government company form preferred in many cases?

Government company form is preferred because it is easier to form than a statutory corporation, has separate legal identity, works under company law, enjoys more business autonomy, can raise funds, and can compete with private companies while remaining under government control.

### 43.5 How does government maintain regional balance?

Government maintains regional balance by setting up public sector enterprises in backward areas, creating employment, developing infrastructure, encouraging ancillary industries, and preventing excessive concentration of industry in already developed regions.

### 43.6 State the meaning of public-private partnership.

Public-private partnership is an arrangement in which public sector and private sector entities work together to provide infrastructure or public services by sharing tasks, responsibilities, risks, resources, and expertise.

## 44. Long Answer Practice

### 44.1 Describe the 1991 industrial policy toward public sector.

The 1991 industrial policy reduced the dominance of public sector in many industries and increased the role of private sector. Its main features included reduction in industries reserved for public sector, disinvestment of selected public sector enterprises, similar treatment of sick public units and sick private units, and use of MoU system to give autonomy with accountability.

The policy aimed to improve efficiency, raise resources, reduce government burden, introduce competition, and focus public sector on strategic and priority areas.

### 44.2 What was the role of public sector before 1991?

Before 1991, public sector had a major role in building the industrial base of India. It developed infrastructure, created heavy industries, maintained regional balance, achieved economies of scale, checked concentration of economic power, and supported import substitution. Private sector lacked adequate capital and willingness for many long-term heavy investments, so government enterprises became important for planned development.

### 44.3 Can public sector companies compete with private sector in profit and efficiency?

Public sector companies can compete if they have operational autonomy, professional management, accountability, modern technology, financial discipline, and freedom from unnecessary interference. However, they may face delays, political influence, rigid procedures, and social obligations that reduce efficiency. Therefore, competition is possible but depends on reform, governance, and management quality.

### 44.4 Why are global enterprises considered superior in some respects?

Global enterprises are considered superior in some respects because they possess huge capital resources, advanced technology, strong research and development, product innovation, international brands, wide marketing networks, reliable market information systems, and operations across countries. These strengths allow them to produce high-quality goods, enter new markets, and compete globally.

### 44.5 What are the benefits of joint ventures and PPP?

Joint ventures provide increased resources, new markets, distribution networks, technology, innovation, lower production cost, established brand advantage, and risk sharing.

PPP helps combine public purpose with private efficiency. Government contributes public authority, land, capital support, social responsibility, and local knowledge. Private partners contribute expertise, innovation, finance, and efficient operations. PPP can accelerate infrastructure and improve service delivery when properly designed.

2. Funds come from government budget.
3. Revenue goes to treasury.
4. Employees are government servants.
5. It has no separate legal identity.

### 45.2 If the Question Mentions Special Act

Identify it as a statutory corporation if:

1. It is created by a special law.
2. Powers and functions are defined by the Act.
3. It has separate legal identity.
4. It has operational autonomy.
5. Employees are governed by the Act.

### 45.3 If the Question Mentions 51 Percent Government Shareholding

Identify it as a government company if:

1. At least 51 percent paid-up capital is held by government.
2. It is registered under company law.
3. It has separate legal identity.
4. It may be private or public company.

### 45.4 If the Question Mentions Operations in Several Countries

Identify it as a global enterprise if:

1. It has branches or subsidiaries in many countries.
2. It uses huge capital and advanced technology.
3. It follows global marketing strategy.
4. It is controlled broadly from headquarters.

### 45.5 If the Question Mentions Two Firms Pooling Resources

Identify it as a joint venture if:

1. Two or more firms agree to work together.
2. Resources and expertise are pooled.
3. Risks and rewards are shared.
4. The goal may be market entry, technology, product development, or expansion.

## 50. End Summary

Private sector enterprises are owned by private persons or groups, while public sector enterprises are owned wholly or partly by government. India is a mixed economy because both sectors operate together. Public sector enterprises may be organized as departmental undertakings, statutory corporations, or government companies. The public sector played a major role before 1991 in infrastructure, regional balance, economies of scale, reducing concentration of economic power, and import substitution. After 1991, policy shifted toward competition, disinvestment, restructuring, and accountability. Global enterprises operate in several countries with huge capital, technology, innovation, marketing strength, and centralised control. Joint ventures allow firms to share resources, risks, markets, technology, and brands. Public-private partnership combines public purpose with private efficiency for infrastructure and public services.
