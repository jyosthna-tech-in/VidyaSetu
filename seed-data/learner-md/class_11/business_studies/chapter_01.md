# Chapter 1: Business, Trade and Commerce

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

This chapter builds the foundation of Business Studies. It explains why human beings engage in economic work, how business differs from profession and employment, how industry and commerce are classified, why profit matters, why risk exists, and what choices must be made before starting a business.

The chapter also connects modern business with India's long commercial history. Ancient India had trade routes, port towns, manufacturing centers, indigenous banking, bills of exchange, merchant communities, and support services that performed many of the functions that modern commerce performs today.

By the end of this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. Explain the meaning of business in simple and technical language.
2. Distinguish between economic and non-economic work.
3. Compare business, profession, and employment.
4. Classify business work into industry and commerce.
5. Explain primary, secondary, and tertiary industries.
6. Explain trade and auxiliaries to trade.
7. Identify the hindrances removed by commerce.
8. Explain why profit is necessary but not the only objective of business.

## 3. Big Idea of the Chapter

Human needs are unlimited and recurring. People need food, clothing, shelter, transport, communication, education, entertainment, healthcare, and many other goods and services. These needs cannot be satisfied by isolated self-production in a modern economy.

Business exists because people specialize. One person or enterprise produces something, another transports it, another finances it, another stores it, another advertises it, and another sells it to the final consumer. Business connects these efforts into a system that creates utility and value.

In the broadest sense:

```text
Business = Industry + Commerce
Industry = Production or processing of goods and services
Commerce = Trade + support services that help trade
```

The chapter therefore studies business as a complete chain:

```text
Human needs
-> Economic work
-> Production of goods and services
-> Exchange in the market
-> Support services
-> Consumption
-> Profit, growth, and social usefulness
```

## 4. Meaning of Business

The word business comes from the idea of being busy. In Business Studies, it has a more specific meaning.

Business is an economic work in which people regularly produce, purchase, sell, or exchange goods and services with the aim of earning profit by satisfying human needs.

### 4.1 Simple Meaning

Business means producing or buying goods and services and selling them to others for a price.

### 4.2 Complete Meaning

Business includes all economic work connected with:

1. Production of goods.
2. Purchase of goods.
3. Sale of goods.
4. Exchange of goods.
5. Supply of services.
6. Distribution of goods from producers to consumers.
7. Earning profit through repeated dealings.

### 4.3 Examples

| Work | Is it business? | Reason |
|---|---:|---|
| A factory manufactures shoes for sale | Yes | It produces goods for the market |
| A shopkeeper sells grocery items daily | Yes | It regularly sells goods for profit |
| A transport company moves goods for a fee | Yes | It provides a service for income |
| A person sells an old phone once | No, usually not | It is an isolated personal sale |
| A teacher teaches in a school for salary | No, it is employment | The teacher works under a service contract |
| A doctor runs a clinic | Profession | It is based on expert knowledge and professional qualification |

## 5. Economic and Non-Economic Work

Human work can be divided into economic and non-economic work.

### 5.1 Economic Work

Economic work is performed to earn money, income, livelihood, or economic value.

Examples:

1. A farmer grows crops for sale.
2. A shopkeeper sells goods.
3. A doctor charges fees from patients.
4. A bank employee works for salary.
5. A transport company carries goods for payment.

Economic work directly affects production, income, wealth creation, or consumption.

### 5.2 Non-Economic Work

Non-economic work is performed because of love, sympathy, emotion, personal satisfaction, social duty, or patriotism, not for earning income.

Examples:

1. A parent cooks for the family.
2. A student helps a friend study without charging a fee.
3. A person donates blood voluntarily.
4. A citizen helps during a relief camp without payment.
5. A person plants trees in the neighborhood as voluntary service.

### 5.3 Same Work, Different Nature

The same type of work may be economic or non-economic depending on the motive.

| Work | Economic if | Non-economic if |
|---|---|---|
| Cooking | A chef cooks in a restaurant for income | A parent cooks for family members |
| Teaching | A tutor teaches for fees | A sibling teaches a younger child lovingly |
| Driving | A driver carries passengers for fare | A person drives a friend to hospital without payment |
| Cleaning | A worker cleans an office for wages | A person cleans their own room |

### 5.4 Is Barter Economic Work?

Barter means exchange of goods or services without using money.

Barter is economic work when it is done to obtain economic value or livelihood. For example, if a farmer gives wheat to a tailor in exchange for stitched clothes, both parties are exchanging value. The absence of money does not make it non-economic.

However, if two friends exchange personal items casually with no business or livelihood motive, it is not treated as business.

## 6. Three Main Forms of Economic Work

Economic work is commonly classified into:

1. Business.
2. Profession.
3. Employment.

### 6.1 Business

Business involves production or sale of goods and services with profit motive and risk.

Examples:

1. Running a factory.
2. Running a shop.
3. Operating an online store.
4. Running a logistics company.
5. Manufacturing furniture for sale.

### 6.2 Profession

A profession is an occupation requiring special knowledge, formal training, qualification, and membership or recognition by a professional body.

Examples:

1. Chartered accountancy.
2. Law.
3. Medicine.
4. Architecture.
5. Company secretary practice.

Professionals usually earn fees, follow a professional code, and provide expert personal services.

### 6.3 Employment

Employment is work performed by an employee under an employer in return for salary or wages.

Examples:

1. A bank clerk.
2. A teacher in a school.
3. A manager in a company.
4. A factory worker.
5. A government officer.

Employment is based on a service contract, appointment letter, service rules, and employer control.

## 7. Business, Profession, and Employment: Detailed Comparison

| Basis | Business | Profession | Employment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mode of entry | Decision of entrepreneur; legal formalities when required | Prescribed qualification, training, and professional recognition | Appointment by employer |
| Basic nature | Production, purchase, sale, or exchange of goods and services | Personal expert service | Work performed under service rules |
| Main objective | Profit through customer satisfaction | Fee through expert service | Salary or wages |
| Qualification | No fixed minimum qualification in most cases | Special qualification and training required | Qualification decided by employer |
| Capital | Needed according to size and nature of business | Limited capital may be needed | No capital investment by employee |
| Reward | Profit | Professional fee | Salary or wages |
| Risk | High; profit is uncertain | Moderate; fee may be more stable, but practice has risk | Low; return is generally fixed |
| Transfer of interest | Possible, subject to law and agreement | Usually not transferable | Not transferable |
| Code of conduct | No single universal professional code | Professional code must be followed | Employer rules must be followed |
| Example | Retail shop, factory, trading firm | Doctor, lawyer, chartered accountant | Clerk, teacher, engineer in a company |

### 7.1 Key Difference in One Line

Business earns profit by taking risk, profession earns fees by applying expert knowledge, and employment earns salary by serving an employer.

## 8. Characteristics of Business

Business has certain essential features. If these are absent, the work may be something else, but not business in the strict sense.

### 8.1 Business Is Economic Work

Business is carried out to earn money or livelihood. It is not performed only for affection, charity, or personal satisfaction.

Example:

A person who makes furniture for sale is doing business. A person who repairs a chair at home for personal use is not doing business.

### 8.2 Business Involves Production or Procurement

A business either produces goods itself or procures goods from others for resale.

Examples:

1. A bakery produces bread.
2. A retailer buys bread from a bakery and sells it to customers.
3. A software firm develops software services.
4. A wholesaler buys goods from manufacturers and sells them to retailers.

### 8.3 Business Involves Sale or Exchange

Goods or services must be sold or exchanged for value. Production for personal use is not business.

Example:

Growing vegetables in a home garden for family consumption is not business. Growing vegetables regularly for sale in the market is business.

### 8.4 Business Requires Regular Dealings

A single isolated transaction is normally not business. Business implies continuity or repeated dealings.

Example:

Selling an old bicycle once is not business. Buying and selling bicycles regularly is business.

### 8.5 Profit Motive Is Essential

The business person expects to earn profit. Profit is the reward for taking risk and organizing resources.

Profit motive does not mean unfair gain. A good business earns profit by satisfying customers and using resources efficiently.

### 8.6 Return Is Uncertain

Profit is not guaranteed. A business may earn high profit, low profit, no profit, or loss.

Reasons include:

1. Change in demand.
2. Change in price.
3. Competition.
4. Production failure.
5. Change in government rules.
6. Change in technology.

### 8.7 Risk Is Always Present

Every business faces the possibility of loss. Risk may arise due to fire, theft, price fall, customer default, poor management, natural events, or market changes.

### 8.8 Customer Satisfaction Is Central

Business survives only when it satisfies human needs. If customers do not value the goods or services, sales decline and profit becomes impossible.

## 9. Business in the Indian Commercial Tradition

India has a long history of trade, craft production, banking, maritime commerce, and market towns. Business was not a modern foreign import; it existed in organized forms in ancient and medieval India.

### 9.1 Ancient Economic Strength

Ancient India developed:

1. Agriculture.
2. Domestication of animals.
3. Weaving of cotton.
4. Dyeing of fabrics.
5. Pottery.
6. Metal utensils.
7. Handicrafts.
8. Sculpting.
9. Cottage industries.
10. Construction skills.

Many families worked in specialized crafts. Workshops and production centers produced goods for local and distant markets.

### 9.2 Trade Routes

Indian trade used both land and sea routes.

Land routes connected Indian trade centers with Central Asia, China, Rome, and other regions. Sea routes connected Indian ports with West Asia, South East Asia, China, and the Roman world.

Trade routes helped India export high-value products and import goods not easily available locally.

### 9.3 Major Exports

Common exports from India included:

1. Spices.
2. Wheat.
3. Sugar.
4. Indigo.
5. Sesame oil.
6. Cotton textiles.
7. Animal products.
8. Precious and semi-precious stones.
9. Handicrafts.

### 9.4 Major Imports

Common imports included:

1. Horses.
2. Chinese silk.
3. Linen.
4. Wine.
5. Gold.
6. Silver.
7. Copper.
8. Selected animal products.

### 9.5 Types of Towns

Ancient and medieval commercial life produced many types of towns:

| Type of town | Main feature |
|---|---|
| Port town | Sea trade, ship movement, storage, export and import |
| Manufacturing town | Production of textiles, crafts, metal goods, or other items |
| Mercantile town | Buying, selling, finance, and wholesale trade |
| Sacred center | Pilgrimage, religious tourism, offerings, services, and crafts |
| Administrative town | Royal power, tax collection, and organized markets |

### 9.6 Aids to Trade in Historical India

Trade needed more than buyers and sellers. It also needed:

1. Transportation.
2. Banking.
3. Finance.
4. Communication.
5. Storage.
6. Trust between merchants.
7. Instruments for safe transfer of money.

These support functions are similar to what modern commerce calls auxiliaries to trade.

## 10. Indigenous Banking and Hundi

Before modern banks became common, Indian traders used indigenous banking systems. One important instrument was the hundi.

### 10.1 Meaning of Hundi

A hundi was a traditional negotiable instrument used for transferring money and settling commercial payments. It allowed traders to avoid carrying large amounts of cash over risky routes.

It served many purposes:

1. Transfer of money from one place to another.
2. Settlement of trade payments.
3. Credit to traders.
4. Trust-based commercial exchange.
5. Reduction of theft and robbery risk during travel.

### 10.2 Basic Features of Hundi

A hundi generally involved:

1. A written instruction or promise.
2. Payment by one person to another.
3. A commercial relationship between parties.
4. Transferability through proper negotiation.
5. Trust in the reputation of merchants and bankers.

### 10.3 Why Hundi Was Important

Carrying cash over long distances was risky. Roads and sea routes involved danger from theft, robbery, loss, and uncertainty. Hundi made payment safer and helped trade expand over long distances.

### 10.4 Types of Hundi

| Type | Meaning in learner language |
|---|---|
| Dhani-jog Darshani | Payable to any person presenting it; payment is made on sight |
| Sah-jog Darshani | Payable to a specific respectable person; payer must be careful about the recipient |
| Firman-jog Darshani | Payable according to order; paid on sight |
| Dekhan-har Darshani | Payable to the presenter or bearer on sight |
| Dhani-jog Muddati | Payable to any person after a fixed period |
| Firman-jog Muddati | Payable to order after a fixed period |
| Jokhmi Muddati | Linked with goods in transit; payment depends on the goods reaching safely |

### 10.5 Modern Parallel

Hundi performed some functions similar to:

1. Bills of exchange.
2. Bank drafts.
3. Promissory instruments.
4. Trade credit instruments.
5. Remittance services.

The main lesson is that commerce requires trust, documentation, finance, and payment systems.

## 12. Business and Economic Development

Business contributes to the growth and development of an economy. It does not merely create private profit; it also creates employment, income, infrastructure, innovation, and tax revenue.

### 12.1 How Business Supports Development

Business supports economic development by:

1. Producing goods and services.
2. Creating employment.
3. Generating income for workers and owners.
4. Encouraging saving and investment.
5. Using natural and human resources productively.
6. Creating infrastructure demand.
7. Supporting banking, insurance, transport, warehousing, and communication.
8. Introducing innovation and new technology.
9. Increasing exports and foreign exchange earnings.
10. Improving standard of living.

### 12.2 Colonial Shift

During the colonial period, India increasingly became an exporter of raw materials and an importer of manufactured goods. This harmed many traditional industries and changed the structure of Indian trade.

### 12.3 Post-Independence Direction

After independence, India focused on planned development, self-reliance, public sector investment, basic industries, infrastructure, science, technology, and institution building.

Important areas included:

1. Heavy industries.
2. Public sector enterprises.
3. Scientific research.
4. Space and atomic energy institutions.
5. Education and technical training.
6. Infrastructure development.

### 12.4 Economic Challenges

India also faced several challenges:

1. Low capital formation.
2. Rapid population growth.
3. Weak financial system in many areas.
4. Inadequate infrastructure.
5. Defence expenditure.
6. Fiscal deficits.
7. Balance of payments pressure.

### 12.5 Liberalisation and Global Integration

In 1991, India adopted major economic reforms. These reforms aimed at stabilising the economy, restructuring industries, encouraging competition, reducing restrictions, and integrating India more deeply with the world economy.

Reform areas included:

1. Fiscal policy.
2. Monetary policy.
3. Trade policy.
4. Industrial policy.
5. Agriculture.
6. Infrastructure.
7. Foreign exchange.
8. Foreign investment.

### 12.6 Recent Business Environment

India's business environment has been shaped by:

1. Rising domestic consumption.
2. Young population.
3. Growing digital infrastructure.
4. Increased foreign investment interest.
5. Higher savings and investment potential.
6. Skill development initiatives.
7. Manufacturing and entrepreneurship initiatives.

## 13. Classification of Business Work

Business work is broadly divided into:

1. Industry.
2. Commerce.

```text
Business
-> Industry
   -> Primary industry
   -> Secondary industry
   -> Tertiary industry
-> Commerce
   -> Trade
   -> Auxiliaries to trade
```

## 14. Industry

Industry refers to economic work connected with production, processing, extraction, construction, or conversion of resources into useful goods and services.

In simple words, industry creates form utility by changing raw materials or resources into useful products.

### 14.1 Meaning of Form Utility

Form utility means making something useful by changing its form.

Examples:

1. Cotton is converted into cloth.
2. Sugarcane is converted into sugar.
3. Iron ore is converted into steel.
4. Wood is converted into furniture.
5. Components are assembled into a car.

### 14.2 Types of Industry

Industry is classified into:

1. Primary industry.
2. Secondary industry.
3. Tertiary industry.

## 15. Primary Industry

Primary industry is concerned with extraction, production, or development of natural resources and living organisms.

Primary industry is divided into:

1. Extractive industry.
2. Genetic industry.

### 15.1 Extractive Industry

Extractive industries take resources from nature.

Examples:

1. Farming.
2. Mining.
3. Fishing.
4. Lumbering.
5. Hunting.
6. Oil extraction.

Features:

1. Depend heavily on nature.
2. Provide raw materials to other industries.
3. Often affected by climate and natural conditions.
4. Form the base of many manufacturing industries.

### 15.2 Genetic Industry

Genetic industries involve breeding and reproduction of plants and animals.

Examples:

1. Plant nurseries.
2. Seed farms.
3. Cattle breeding.
4. Poultry farms.
5. Fish hatcheries.

Features:

1. Focus on growth and reproduction.
2. Need scientific care and selection.
3. Improve quality and quantity of plants or animals.
4. Support agriculture, food production, and allied sectors.

## 16. Secondary Industry

Secondary industry uses materials obtained from primary industry and converts them into finished or semi-finished goods.

Secondary industry includes:

1. Manufacturing industry.
2. Construction industry.

## 17. Manufacturing Industry

Manufacturing industry transforms raw materials or semi-finished goods into finished products.

Examples:

1. Cotton into cloth.
2. Wheat into flour.
3. Crude oil into petrol, diesel, and kerosene.
4. Components into a television.
5. Sugarcane into sugar.

### 17.1 Types of Manufacturing Industry

Manufacturing industries can be divided into:

1. Analytical.
2. Synthetical.
3. Processing.
4. Assembling.

### 17.2 Analytical Industry

An analytical industry separates one raw material into several products.

Example:

Crude oil is refined into petrol, diesel, kerosene, lubricating oil, and other products.

Key idea:

```text
One input -> Many outputs
```

### 17.3 Synthetical Industry

A synthetical industry combines different materials to make one new product.

Example:

Cement is produced by combining limestone, clay, gypsum, and other materials.

Key idea:

```text
Many inputs -> One output
```

### 17.4 Processing Industry

A processing industry passes raw material through different stages to produce the final product.

Examples:

1. Sugar industry.
2. Paper industry.
3. Textile industry.

Key idea:

```text
Input -> Stage 1 -> Stage 2 -> Stage 3 -> Final product
```

### 17.5 Assembling Industry

An assembling industry combines different components to make a final product.

Examples:

1. Car manufacturing.
2. Television assembly.
3. Computer assembly.
4. Mobile phone assembly.

Key idea:

```text
Many parts -> One complete product
```

## 18. Construction Industry

Construction industry is concerned with building infrastructure and structures.

Examples:

1. Buildings.
2. Bridges.
3. Roads.
4. Dams.
5. Tunnels.
6. Canals.

Features:

1. Products are fixed at one place.
2. Requires engineering and architectural knowledge.
3. Uses materials from many industries.
4. Creates long-term assets.
5. Supports trade, transport, housing, irrigation, and production.

## 19. Tertiary Industry

Tertiary industry provides services that support primary and secondary industries and trade.

Examples:

1. Transport.
2. Banking.
3. Insurance.
4. Warehousing.
5. Communication.
6. Packaging.
7. Advertising.

Tertiary industry overlaps with commerce because these services help goods move from producers to consumers.

## 20. Industry Classification Summary

| Industry type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Extractive | Takes resources from nature | Mining, fishing, farming |
| Genetic | Breeds plants or animals | Nursery, cattle breeding, poultry |
| Manufacturing | Converts materials into goods | Textile, cement, sugar, car assembly |
| Construction | Builds fixed assets | Roads, bridges, buildings |
| Tertiary | Provides support services | Transport, banking, insurance |

## 21. Commerce

Commerce includes all work necessary for the exchange of goods and services between producers and consumers.

Commerce has two parts:

1. Trade.
2. Auxiliaries to trade.

```text
Commerce = Trade + Auxiliaries to trade
```

Trade means buying and selling. Auxiliaries are support services that make buying and selling possible.

## 22. Trade

Trade refers to buying, selling, or exchange of goods and services.

### 22.1 Why Trade Is Needed

Producers usually do not sell directly to every final consumer. Goods must move through markets, wholesalers, retailers, agents, distributors, or online platforms.

Trade helps:

1. Producers sell large quantities.
2. Consumers obtain goods conveniently.
3. Markets expand beyond local areas.
4. Production becomes more specialized.
5. Goods reach the people who need them.

### 22.2 Internal and External Trade

Although the chapter mainly introduces trade in a general sense, learners should know the broad division:

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Internal trade | Buying and selling within the country |
| External trade | Buying and selling between countries |

## 23. Auxiliaries to Trade

Auxiliaries to trade are services that remove problems in buying and selling.

Major auxiliaries are:

1. Transport and communication.
2. Banking and finance.
3. Insurance.
4. Warehousing.
5. Advertising and public relations.

## 24. Hindrances Removed by Commerce

Commerce removes obstacles between producers and consumers.

| Hindrance | Meaning | Removed by |
|---|---|---|
| Hindrance of persons | Producer and consumer may not know each other | Trade |
| Hindrance of place | Goods are produced in one place but needed elsewhere | Transport |
| Hindrance of time | Goods may be produced before they are consumed | Warehousing |
| Hindrance of risk | Goods may be damaged, stolen, or destroyed | Insurance |
| Hindrance of finance | Funds may be needed for production and trade | Banking and finance |
| Hindrance of information | Consumers may not know about goods | Advertising and public relations |

## 25. Transport and Communication

Transport moves goods from one place to another. Communication transfers information between producers, traders, banks, customers, and other parties.

### 25.1 Importance of Transport

Transport helps by:

1. Moving raw materials to factories.
2. Moving finished goods to markets.
3. Expanding market reach.
4. Reducing regional shortages.
5. Supporting large-scale production.

### 25.2 Importance of Communication

Communication helps by:

1. Sending orders.
2. Confirming delivery.
3. Tracking shipments.
4. Exchanging payment information.
5. Handling customer queries.
6. Coordinating suppliers and distributors.

## 26. Banking and Finance

Business needs funds for land, buildings, machinery, raw materials, wages, advertising, transport, storage, and daily operations.

Banks and financial institutions help by:

1. Providing loans and advances.
2. Offering cash credit.
3. Allowing overdraft facilities.
4. Collecting cheques.
5. Remitting money.
6. Discounting bills.
7. Supporting export and import payments.
8. Helping enterprises raise capital.

Without finance, even profitable business ideas may fail to start or grow.

## 27. Insurance

Insurance protects business against certain risks by spreading risk among many persons or firms.

### 27.1 Risks Covered by Insurance

Insurance may cover:

1. Fire.
2. Theft.
3. Accident.
4. Damage to stock.
5. Goods in transit.
6. Employee-related risks.
7. Some liability risks.

### 27.2 How Insurance Works

A business pays premium to the insurance company. If an insured loss occurs, the insurance company compensates the business according to the policy terms.

Insurance does not eliminate risk, but it reduces the financial impact of certain losses.

## 28. Warehousing

Warehousing means storing goods safely until they are needed.

### 28.1 Need for Warehousing

Warehousing is important because:

1. Production and consumption may not happen at the same time.
2. Seasonal goods must be stored.
3. Goods may need protection from damage.
4. Continuous supply helps stabilize prices.
5. Large-scale production requires storage before distribution.

### 28.2 Examples

1. Food grains stored after harvest.
2. Finished goods stored before dispatch.
3. Cold storage for fruits, vegetables, milk, and medicines.
4. Imported goods stored before clearance or sale.

## 29. Advertising and Public Relations

Advertising informs and persuades customers about goods, services, brands, prices, features, and availability.

Public relations builds a favorable image of the business among customers, investors, employees, government, media, and society.

### 29.1 Difference Between Advertising and Public Relations

| Basis | Advertising | Public relations |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Paid communication | Planned image-building communication |
| Control | Business controls message and space | Less direct control over public response |
| Purpose | Promote goods, services, or ideas | Build trust and goodwill |
| Example | Newspaper ad, digital ad, billboard | Press note, community relation, public communication |

## 30. Objectives of Business

An objective is a desired end result. Every business needs objectives because objectives guide planning, decision-making, resource use, and performance measurement.

Profit is important, but a business cannot survive by looking only at profit. It must also satisfy customers, innovate, use resources efficiently, treat employees fairly, and act responsibly toward society.

## 31. Profit as an Objective

Profit is the excess of revenue over cost.

```text
Profit = Revenue - Cost
```

Revenue means the amount earned from selling goods or services. Cost means the expenditure incurred to produce and sell them.

### 31.1 Why Profit Is Necessary

Profit is necessary because it:

1. Provides income to business owners.
2. Rewards risk-taking.
3. Supplies funds for growth and expansion.
4. Indicates efficiency.
5. Shows that customers value the product.
6. Improves reputation and creditworthiness.
7. Helps the business survive difficult periods.

### 31.2 Why Profit Alone Is Not Enough

Excessive focus on profit may lead to:

1. Poor quality goods.
2. Exploitation of customers.
3. Neglect of employees.
4. Environmental harm.
5. Loss of public trust.
6. Weak long-term survival.

A business that ignores customers and society may earn short-term profit but lose long-term support.

## 32. Multiple Objectives of Business

Business objectives can be classified into several areas.

### 32.1 Market Standing

Market standing means the position, reputation, and goodwill of a business in the market.

A business improves market standing by:

1. Offering good quality.
2. Charging reasonable prices.
3. Delivering on time.
4. Giving good customer service.
5. Building brand trust.
6. Differentiating itself from competitors.

### 32.2 Innovation

Innovation means introducing new ideas, methods, products, designs, processes, or services.

Innovation may be:

1. Product innovation.
2. Process innovation.
3. Marketing innovation.
4. Service innovation.
5. Technological innovation.
6. Organizational innovation.

Innovation helps a business remain competitive and meet changing customer needs.

### 32.3 Productivity

Productivity means the relationship between output and input.

In simple terms:

```text
Productivity = Output obtained from given inputs
```

High productivity means more or better output from the same or fewer resources.

Ways to improve productivity:

1. Better technology.
2. Skilled employees.
3. Efficient processes.
4. Reduced waste.
5. Better planning.
6. Proper maintenance of machines.

### 32.4 Physical and Financial Resources

A business needs physical resources such as land, buildings, machines, tools, furniture, vehicles, computers, and raw materials.

It also needs financial resources such as owners' capital, loans, retained earnings, and working capital.

The objective is not only to acquire resources but to use them efficiently.

### 32.5 Earning Reasonable Profit

Profit should be reasonable in relation to the capital employed and risk taken.

Reasonable profit allows:

1. Survival.
2. Growth.
3. Expansion.
4. Innovation.
5. Reserves for uncertainty.
6. Better credit rating.

### 32.6 Social Responsibility

Social responsibility means that a business should contribute to society and avoid actions that harm public interest.

Examples:

1. Supplying quality goods.
2. Avoiding unfair trade practices.
3. Paying taxes honestly.
4. Protecting the environment.
5. Treating employees fairly.
6. Supporting community welfare.
7. Using resources responsibly.

## 33. Business Risk

Business risk is the possibility of inadequate profit or loss due to uncertainties and unexpected events.

Risk exists because the future is uncertain. A business makes decisions today, but demand, cost, price, technology, competition, and government rules may change tomorrow.

### 33.1 Examples of Business Risk

1. Demand may fall after goods are produced.
2. Raw material prices may increase suddenly.
3. Customers may not pay on time.
4. Competitors may sell at lower prices.
5. Machinery may break down.
6. Fire may destroy stock.
7. A new technology may make old products outdated.

## 34. Types of Risk

### 34.1 Speculative Risk

Speculative risk involves the possibility of gain as well as loss.

Examples:

1. Change in market price.
2. Change in demand.
3. Change in fashion.
4. Change in customer taste.
5. Change in exchange rate.

If price rises, a trader may gain. If price falls, the trader may lose.

### 34.2 Pure Risk

Pure risk involves the possibility of loss or no loss, but not gain.

Examples:

1. Fire.
2. Theft.
3. Accident.
4. Strike.
5. Natural disaster.

If fire occurs, the business loses. If fire does not occur, the business simply avoids loss; it does not gain because of the risk itself.

## 35. Nature of Business Risk

### 35.1 Risk Is an Essential Part of Business

No business can avoid risk completely. Even a stable business faces changes in customers, cost, competition, and technology.

### 35.2 Risk Arises from Uncertainty

Risk exists because future events cannot be predicted perfectly.

Uncertainty may relate to:

1. Demand.
2. Supply.
3. Cost.
4. Price.
5. Weather.
6. Technology.
7. Government policy.

### 35.3 Degree of Risk Depends on Nature and Size

Risk differs from business to business.

| Business type | Risk level tendency | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Daily necessity goods | Relatively lower | Demand is more stable |
| Fashion goods | Higher | Customer taste changes quickly |
| Large manufacturing | Higher capital risk | Heavy investment in plant and machinery |
| Small service business | Lower capital risk | Limited fixed investment |
| Export business | Additional risk | Exchange rate and foreign market changes |

### 35.4 Profit Is the Reward for Risk

Business persons take risk in the hope of earning profit. If there were no risk, there would be no special reward for entrepreneurship.

## 36. Causes of Business Risk

Business risk may arise from many causes.

### 36.1 Natural Causes

3. Lightning.
4. Heavy rain.
5. Drought.
6. Famine.
7. Storm.

These events can damage crops, stock, buildings, transport routes, and production.

## 37. Starting a Business

Starting a business requires careful decision-making. A good idea alone is not enough. The entrepreneur must study demand, resources, capital, location, law, workforce, production, and selling methods.

### 37.1 Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the process of setting up and running a business by identifying opportunities, arranging resources, taking risk, and creating value.

The person who does this is called an entrepreneur.

### 37.2 Role of Entrepreneur

An entrepreneur:

1. Identifies customer needs.
2. Develops a business idea.
3. Arranges capital.
4. Organizes land, labour, materials, and technology.
5. Takes risk.
6. Produces or procures goods and services.
7. Builds a market.
8. Creates employment.
9. Earns profit if the enterprise succeeds.

Entrepreneurship is important because it turns ideas into economic output.

## 38. Factors Before Starting a Business

### 38.1 Selection of Type of Business

The entrepreneur must decide what type of business to start.

Factors affecting this decision:

1. Profit possibility.
2. Customer demand.
3. Technical knowledge.
4. Personal interest.
5. Availability of resources.
6. Competition.
7. Legal restrictions.

Example:

A person trained in food processing may choose a bakery or packaged food unit if there is local demand and suitable finance.

### 38.2 Size of Business

The entrepreneur must decide whether the business will be small, medium, or large.

Factors affecting size:

2. Capital available.

5. Government rules.
6. Market area.
7. Economies of scale.

Small businesses require less capital and are easier to manage. Large businesses may enjoy lower cost per unit but require more capital and organization.

### 38.3 Location of Business

Location affects cost, customer service, transport, labour, and profitability.

## 40. High-Value Definitions

### 40.1 Business

Business is an economic work involving regular production, purchase, sale, or exchange of goods and services with the objective of earning profit by satisfying human needs.

### 40.2 Industry

Industry is concerned with production, processing, extraction, construction, or conversion of resources into useful goods and services.

### 40.3 Commerce

Commerce includes all work that helps in the exchange of goods and services, especially trade and auxiliaries to trade.

### 40.4 Trade

Trade means buying, selling, or exchange of goods and services.

### 40.5 Auxiliaries to Trade

Auxiliaries to trade are support services such as transport, banking, insurance, warehousing, advertising, and communication that make trade possible.

### 40.6 Business Risk

### 40.7 Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the process of identifying a business opportunity, arranging resources, taking risk, and creating value through a business enterprise.

## 41. Important Distinctions

### 41.1 Industry and Commerce

| Basis | Industry | Commerce |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Produces or processes goods and services | Facilitates exchange of goods and services |
| Main role | Creation of form utility | Removal of trade hindrances |
| Includes | Primary, secondary, tertiary industry | Trade and auxiliaries |
| Example | Textile mill | Wholesaler, transport, banking, insurance |

### 41.2 Trade and Commerce

|---|---|---|

| Function | Exchange of goods and services | Facilitates exchange from producer to consumer |
| Example | Retail sale | Retail sale plus transport, banking, storage, advertising |

### 41.3 Pure Risk and Speculative Risk

|---|---|---|

## 42. Case File: Innovation in Weaving

Consider a weaving enterprise where an entrepreneur develops a better method for inserting weft designs into fabric. The new method increases speed, improves quality, reduces manual effort, and allows more output without simply adding more looms.

### 42.1 Main Objective Shown

The main objective shown is innovation.

The entrepreneur introduced a new method that improved the production process and created better results.

### 42.2 Other Objectives Also Served

The innovation also supports:

1. Productivity, because output increases for the same or less effort.
2. Market standing, because better quality improves reputation.
3. Profit, because higher output and better products can improve earnings.
4. Efficient use of resources, because existing equipment can be used better.
5. Social usefulness, if the method supports local craft workers and uses eco-friendly materials.

### 42.3 Lesson from the Case

A business should not think only about selling more. It should also improve methods, reduce waste, raise quality, and create value for customers and workers.

## 45. Long Answer Practice

### 45.1 Explain the characteristics of business.

Business has the following characteristics:

1. It is economic work, because it is performed to earn income.
2. It involves production or procurement of goods and services.
3. It requires sale or exchange for value.
4. It involves regular dealings, not merely one isolated transaction.
5. It has profit motive.
6. It involves uncertainty of return.
7. It contains risk of loss.
8. It aims to satisfy human needs.

These features separate business from personal work, charity, employment, and casual sale of personal assets.

### 45.2 Compare business, profession, and employment.

Business is started by an entrepreneur to produce or sell goods and services for profit. It involves capital investment and risk. Profession requires specialized qualification and training. Professionals render expert services and earn fees. Employment is service under an employer. An employee follows service rules and receives salary or wages.

Thus, business is based mainly on risk and profit, profession on expert knowledge and fees, and employment on service contract and salary.

### 45.3 Explain the classification of industries.

Industries are classified into primary, secondary, and tertiary industries.

Primary industries are concerned with natural resources and living organisms. They include extractive industries such as mining and fishing, and genetic industries such as cattle breeding and plant nurseries.

Secondary industries use materials obtained from primary industries. They include manufacturing industries and construction industries. Manufacturing may be analytical, synthetical, processing, or assembling. Construction includes buildings, roads, bridges, dams, canals, and other fixed structures.

Tertiary industries provide support services such as transport, banking, insurance, warehousing, communication, and advertising.

### 45.4 Explain the role of commerce.

Commerce helps goods and services move from producers to consumers. It includes trade and auxiliaries to trade.

Trade removes the hindrance of persons by connecting buyers and sellers. Transport removes the hindrance of place. Warehousing removes the hindrance of time. Insurance reduces the hindrance of risk. Banking and finance remove the hindrance of funds. Advertising and public relations remove the hindrance of information.

Therefore, commerce makes production useful by ensuring that goods reach customers at the right time, place, and price.

### 45.5 Explain the objectives of business.

Business has several objectives:

1. Market standing: building reputation and a strong position in the market.
2. Innovation: introducing new products, methods, or ideas.
3. Productivity: obtaining better output from available inputs.
4. Efficient use of physical and financial resources.
5. Earning reasonable profit for survival and growth.
6. Social responsibility toward customers, employees, government, environment, and society.

Profit is essential, but a business must balance profit with long-term trust and social usefulness.

### 45.6 Explain business risk and its nature.

Business risk is the possibility of inadequate profit or loss due to uncertainty. Its nature can be explained as follows:

1. Risk is an essential part of business.
2. Risk arises because future events are uncertain.
3. The degree of risk depends on the nature and size of business.
4. Profit is the reward for taking risk.

Business can reduce risk through planning, insurance, market study, efficient management, and diversification, but it cannot remove risk completely.

### 45.7 Explain the causes of business risk.

Business risk may arise from:

1. Natural causes such as flood, earthquake, drought, lightning, and storm.
2. Human causes such as theft, dishonesty, negligence, strikes, riots, and inefficient management.
3. Economic causes such as change in demand, price, competition, interest rates, technology, tax, and customer default.
4. Other causes such as political disturbance, mechanical failure, legal change, and exchange rate fluctuation.

### 45.8 Explain the factors to be considered before starting a business.

Before starting a business, an entrepreneur must consider:

1. Type of business, based on demand, profit possibility, knowledge, and interest.
2. Size of business, based on capital, demand, technology, and risk capacity.
3. Location, based on raw materials, labour, power, transport, banking, and customers.
4. Finance, including fixed capital and working capital.
5. Physical facilities such as machinery, building, tools, storage, and utilities.
6. Workforce, including skilled workers, managers, sales staff, and technicians.
7. Tax planning, to understand and manage legal tax obligations.
8. Launching steps, including legal formalities, resource arrangement, production, and sales promotion.

## 50. End Summary

Business is a regular economic work involving production or exchange of goods and services for profit. It is wider than trade because it includes industry and commerce. Industry creates goods and services, while commerce helps those goods and services reach consumers. Commerce removes hindrances of persons, place, time, risk, finance, and information through trade and auxiliaries. Business requires profit for survival, but it must also pursue innovation, productivity, market standing, efficient resource use, and social responsibility. Since business operates under uncertainty, risk is unavoidable. Starting a business requires careful choice of type, size, location, finance, facilities, workforce, tax planning, and launch method.
