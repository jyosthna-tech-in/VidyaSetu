# Chapter 6: Social Responsibilities of Business and Business Ethics

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

Business exists within society. It uses society's resources, sells to society, hires from society, and operates under laws created by society. Therefore, business cannot focus only on profit. It must also act responsibly toward customers, workers, owners, government, community, and the environment.

This chapter explains:

1. Social responsibility of business.
2. Need for social responsibility.
3. Arguments for and against social responsibility.
4. Kinds of social responsibility.
5. Responsibility toward different interest groups.
6. Business and environmental protection.
7. Business ethics and its elements.

## 3. Core Idea

Business is an economic institution, but it is also a social institution.

It must earn profit to survive, but it must earn profit in a way that is acceptable to society.

```text
Good business = Profit + Responsibility + Ethics + Sustainability
```

## 4. Meaning of Social Responsibility

Social responsibility of business means the obligation of business to take decisions and perform actions that are desirable according to the objectives and values of society.

In simple words:

```text
Social responsibility = Duty of business to act in the interest of society
```

It means business should not harm society while earning profit. It should respect the expectations of society and contribute positively wherever possible.

## 5. Examples of Socially Undesirable Practices

Socially undesirable practices may increase short-term profit but harm society.

Examples:

1. Selling adulterated goods.
2. Misleading advertising.
3. Polluting air, water, or land.
4. Exploiting workers.
5. Not paying taxes honestly.
6. Using false weights and measures.
7. Charging unfair prices.
8. Ignoring customer complaints.
9. Unsafe working conditions.
10. Bribing officials.

## 6. Examples of Socially Desirable Practices

Socially desirable practices improve trust and long-term success.

Examples:

1. Supplying good quality goods.
2. Charging fair prices.
3. Paying taxes honestly.
4. Providing safe working conditions.
5. Installing pollution control devices.
6. Treating workers fairly.
7. Respecting consumer rights.
8. Responding to customer complaints.
9. Avoiding misleading advertisements.
10. Supporting community welfare.

## 7. Social Responsibility and Legal Responsibility

Legal responsibility means obeying the law.

Social responsibility is wider. It includes legal responsibility but also includes voluntary actions that society expects, even when not legally compulsory.

### 7.1 Difference Between Legal and Social Responsibility

| Basis | Legal responsibility | Social responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Duty to obey law | Duty to act according to social values and expectations |
| Scope | Narrower | Wider |
| Nature | Compulsory | Partly compulsory, partly voluntary |
| Enforced by | Courts, regulators, government | Society, conscience, public opinion, long-term business interest |
| Example | Paying tax as required by law | Paying tax honestly and also supporting community welfare |
| Minimum or higher standard | Minimum legal standard | Higher social and ethical standard |

## 8. Why Social Responsibility Is an Ethical Issue

Social responsibility asks:

1. What is right?
2. What is wrong?
3. What should business do when society is affected?
4. Should profit be the only goal?
5. How should business treat weaker groups?

These are moral questions. That is why social responsibility is linked with ethics.

## 9. Need for Social Responsibility

Business decisions affect many groups:

1. Owners.
2. Workers.
3. Consumers.
4. Suppliers.
5. Government.
6. Local community.
7. Natural environment.
8. Future generations.

Since business has power over resources and people, it must use this power responsibly.

## 10. Arguments for Social Responsibility

Major arguments in favour of social responsibility are:

1. Justification for existence and growth.
2. Long-term interest of the firm.
3. Avoidance of government regulation.
4. Maintenance of society.
5. Availability of resources with business.
6. Converting problems into opportunities.
7. Better environment for doing business.
8. Holding business responsible for social problems.

## 11. Justification for Existence and Growth

Business exists because society allows it to exist. It produces goods and services for society. Profit should be seen as a result of service to society, not as a right to exploit society.

A business that serves society earns trust, customers, cooperation, and long-term growth.

## 12. Long-Term Interest of the Firm

Socially responsible business gains goodwill and public image.

If workers, consumers, investors, government, and community feel exploited, they may withdraw cooperation.

Therefore, social responsibility protects the long-term interest of the firm.

## 13. Avoidance of Government Regulation

If business acts irresponsibly, government may impose stricter laws and controls.

Voluntary social responsibility can reduce the need for excessive regulation.

## 14. Maintenance of Society

Business cannot succeed in a society full of conflict, exploitation, pollution, disease, and unrest.

By helping maintain a healthy society, business creates a better environment for itself.

## 15. Availability of Resources With Business

Business has:

1. Capital.
2. Managers.
3. Technical knowledge.
4. Organisational skill.
5. Innovation ability.
6. Distribution networks.

These resources can help solve social problems.

## 16. Converting Problems Into Opportunities

Business can turn social problems into useful opportunities.

Examples:

1. Pollution problem can lead to clean technology.
2. Waste problem can lead to recycling business.
3. Rural unemployment can lead to skill training and rural enterprise.
4. Energy shortage can lead to renewable energy solutions.

## 17. Better Environment for Doing Business

A society with healthy citizens, educated workers, clean surroundings, fair markets, and stable institutions is better for business.

Social responsibility helps create such an environment.

## 18. Holding Business Responsible for Social Problems

Some social problems are created or worsened by business.

Examples:

1. Pollution.
2. Unsafe workplaces.
3. Misleading advertisements.
4. Discrimination.
5. Corruption.
6. Consumer exploitation.

If business contributes to these problems, it has responsibility to help solve them.

## 19. Arguments Against Social Responsibility

Major arguments against social responsibility are:

1. Violation of profit maximisation objective.
2. Burden on consumers.
3. Lack of social skills.
4. Lack of broad public support.

## 20. Violation of Profit Maximisation Objective

Some argue that business exists to earn profit. According to this view, spending resources on social welfare reduces efficiency and violates the objective of profit maximisation.

## 21. Burden on Consumers

Social programmes such as pollution control may be costly. Business may recover the cost by charging higher prices. Critics argue that consumers should not be forced to bear this burden indirectly.

## 22. Lack of Social Skills

Business managers are trained to solve business problems, not all social problems. Critics argue that specialized agencies should handle social issues.

## 23. Lack of Broad Public Support

Some people do not want business to interfere in social programmes. Without public confidence and cooperation, business may not succeed in such efforts.

## 24. Reality of Social Responsibility

Modern business increasingly accepts that it has social obligations. This acceptance may come from genuine concern, public pressure, legal expectations, brand image, or survival needs.

Business is no longer viewed as only a profit-making machine. It is expected to function as a responsible organ of society.

## 25. Forces Increasing Concern for Social Responsibility

The main forces are:

1. Threat of public regulation.
2. Pressure of labour movement.
3. Consumer consciousness.
4. Development of social standards.
5. Development of business education.
6. Link between social interest and business interest.
7. Growth of professional management.

## 26. Threat of Public Regulation

Governments in welfare states regulate business when it harms public interest. Fear of regulation encourages business to act responsibly.

## 27. Pressure of Labour Movement

Workers are more educated and organised than before. Strong labour movements have forced businesses to improve wages, working conditions, and worker welfare.

## 28. Consumer Consciousness

Education, media, competition, and consumer rights have made consumers more aware. The idea of "let the buyer beware" has weakened, and customer-oriented policies have become important.

## 29. Development of Social Standards

Society now expects business to earn profit through fair practices. Business is judged not only by profit but also by social conduct.

## 30. Development of Business Education

Business education has spread awareness about social responsibility, ethics, consumer rights, worker welfare, and environmental protection.

## 31. Link Between Social Interest and Business Interest

Business interest and social interest are not enemies. In the long run, business succeeds when society trusts it.

## 32. Professional Management

Professional managers are trained to balance the interests of several groups, not only owners. This has increased concern for social responsibility.

## 33. Kinds of Social Responsibility

Social responsibility can be divided into:

1. Economic responsibility.
2. Legal responsibility.
3. Ethical responsibility.
4. Discretionary responsibility.

## 34. Economic Responsibility

Business is an economic institution. Its first responsibility is to produce goods and services that society wants and sell them at a profit.

Without economic performance, business cannot survive or serve society.

## 35. Legal Responsibility

Business must obey the laws of the country.

Examples:

1. Pay taxes.
2. Follow labour laws.
3. Follow environmental laws.
4. Follow consumer protection laws.
5. Follow company law.

## 36. Ethical Responsibility

Ethical responsibility includes behaviour expected by society even if not written in law.

Examples:

1. Respect religious feelings in advertisements.
2. Avoid offensive content.
3. Avoid unfair manipulation.
4. Treat workers with dignity.
5. Avoid deceptive sales practices.

## 37. Discretionary Responsibility

Discretionary responsibility means voluntary responsibility accepted by business.

Examples:

1. Donations to education.
2. Relief during floods or earthquakes.
3. Skill development programmes.
4. Community health camps.
5. Support for art, sports, or culture.

## 38. Responsibility Toward Shareholders or Owners

Business must protect the interest of owners and investors.

Responsibilities include:

1. Fair return on capital.
2. Safety of investment.
3. Honest management.
4. Accurate information.
5. Regular financial reporting.
6. Long-term growth.
7. Avoiding reckless speculation.

## 39. Responsibility Toward Workers

Business depends on workers. It must treat them fairly.

Responsibilities include:

1. Fair wages.
2. Safe working conditions.
3. Meaningful work.
4. Job security where possible.
5. Training and development.
6. Respect for worker dignity.
7. Opportunity to form unions.
8. Fair grievance handling.
9. Protection against exploitation.

## 40. Responsibility Toward Consumers

Consumers are central to business survival.

Responsibilities include:

1. Right quality goods.
2. Right quantity.
3. Reasonable prices.
4. Honest advertising.
5. No adulteration.
6. No black marketing.
7. Proper packaging and labelling.
8. Customer complaint handling.
9. Safety of goods.
10. Correct product information.

## 41. Responsibility Toward Government and Community

Business must act as a good citizen.

Responsibilities include:

1. Obey laws.
2. Pay taxes honestly.
3. Avoid bribery.
4. Protect natural environment.
5. Support community welfare.
6. Avoid dirty and unsafe surroundings.
7. Cooperate with government programmes.
8. Maintain fair dealings with society.

## 42. Corporate Social Responsibility Under Companies Act 2013

In the textbook context, CSR under Companies Act, 2013 applies to companies meeting specified financial thresholds such as:

1. Annual turnover of Rs. 1,000 crore or more.
2. Net worth of Rs. 500 crore or more.
3. Net profit of Rs. 5 crore or more.

The framework includes:

1. CSR committee of board members.
2. Spending guideline of 2 percent of average net profit of preceding three years.
3. CSR areas listed in Schedule VII.
4. CSR work undertaken in India considered for this purpose.
5. Work meant only for employees and their families not treated as qualifying CSR.

This section should be written in exams according to the textbook wording unless the question asks for current legal updates.

## 43. Environment

Environment means the totality of human surroundings, both natural and man-made.

### 43.1 Natural Resources

Natural resources include:

1. Land.
2. Water.
3. Air.
4. Flora.
5. Fauna.
6. Raw materials.

### 43.2 Man-Made Resources

Man-made resources include:

1. Cultural heritage.
2. Socio-economic institutions.
3. Buildings.
4. Roads.
5. Factories.
6. Technology systems.

## 44. Environmental Pollution

Pollution means introduction of harmful substances into the environment.

Pollution changes the physical, chemical, or biological qualities of air, water, and land.

It harms:

1. Human health.
2. Animals.
3. Plants.
4. Natural resources.
5. Cultural monuments.
6. Quality of life.

## 45. Causes of Pollution

Business and industry can cause pollution through:

1. Smoke from factories.
2. Chemical waste.
3. Toxic by-products.
4. Dirty effluents.
5. Noise from machines.
6. Waste dumping.
7. Vehicle emissions.
8. Poor production technology.
9. Unsafe disposal methods.

## 46. Types of Pollution

The main types are:

1. Air pollution.
2. Water pollution.
3. Land pollution.
4. Noise pollution.

## 47. Air Pollution

Air pollution occurs when harmful gases, smoke, dust, or chemicals reduce air quality.

Examples:

1. Smoke from factories.
2. Carbon monoxide from vehicles.
3. Industrial fumes.
4. Chemical emissions.

Effects:

1. Breathing problems.
2. Global warming.
3. Ozone layer damage.
4. Harm to plants and animals.

## 48. Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances enter rivers, lakes, ponds, streams, groundwater, or seas.

Examples:

1. Chemical waste.
2. Industrial effluents.
3. Sewage.
4. Oil discharge.

Effects:

1. Death of aquatic animals.
2. Unsafe drinking water.
3. Disease.
4. Harm to agriculture.

## 49. Land Pollution

Land pollution occurs when toxic wastes or harmful substances are dumped on land.

Effects:

1. Soil damage.
2. Reduced fertility.
3. Unsafe crops.
4. Damage to plantation.
5. Difficulty in restoring land quality.

## 50. Noise Pollution

Noise pollution is unwanted or excessive sound from factories, vehicles, machines, and construction.

Effects:

1. Hearing loss.
2. Stress.
3. Heart problems.
4. Mental disturbance.
5. Reduced work efficiency.

## 51. Major Environmental Problems

Major environmental problems include:

1. Ozone depletion.
2. Global warming.
3. Solid and hazardous waste.
4. Water pollution.
5. Decline in freshwater quality and quantity.
6. Deforestation.
7. Land degradation.
8. Danger to biological diversity.

## 52. Need for Pollution Control

Pollution control is needed for:

1. Reduction of health hazards.
2. Reduced risk of liability.
3. Cost savings.
4. Improved public image.
5. Other social benefits.

## 53. Reduction of Health Hazards

Pollution causes diseases such as cancer, heart problems, lung complications, and water-borne illnesses. Pollution control supports healthier life.

## 54. Reduced Risk of Liability

A business may be held liable to compensate people harmed by toxic waste, polluted water, unsafe air, or hazardous operations. Pollution control reduces this risk.

## 55. Cost Savings

Pollution control can reduce waste, improve technology, lower cleaning costs, reduce disposal costs, and improve efficiency.

## 56. Improved Public Image

A firm that protects the environment earns reputation and public trust.

## 57. Other Social Benefits

Pollution control brings cleaner surroundings, clearer visibility, better buildings, better quality of life, and purer natural products.

## 58. Role of Business in Environmental Protection

Business should take the lead in protecting the environment because it has resources, technology, capital, and management skill.

## 59. Steps Business Can Take

### 59.1 Top Management Commitment

Top management must commit to environmental protection and pollution prevention.

### 59.2 Shared Commitment

All departments and employees should understand and support environmental goals.

### 59.3 Clear Policies and Programmes

Business should develop policies for:

1. Good quality raw materials.
2. Superior technology.
3. Scientific waste disposal.
4. Treatment of waste.
5. Employee training.
6. Pollution control skills.

### 59.4 Compliance With Laws

Business must follow government laws and regulations for pollution prevention.

### 59.5 Participation in Government Programmes

Business may participate in programmes related to:

1. Hazardous substance management.
2. Cleaning polluted rivers.
3. Tree plantation.
4. Checking deforestation.

### 59.6 Periodic Assessment

Pollution control programmes should be assessed for costs, benefits, and progress.

### 59.7 Education and Training

Business should educate employees, suppliers, dealers, and customers about environmental protection.

## 60. Business Ethics

Ethics means moral principles that judge what is right and wrong in human conduct.

Business ethics means socially determined moral principles that should govern business activities.

In simple words:

```text
Business ethics = Doing business in a fair, honest, and socially acceptable way
```

## 61. Examples of Business Ethics

1. Charging fair prices.
2. Using correct weights and measures.
3. Giving fair treatment to workers.
4. Avoiding misleading advertisements.
5. Earning reasonable profit.
6. Not bribing officials.
7. Respecting customer rights.
8. Reporting accounts honestly.
9. Keeping promises to suppliers.
10. Protecting confidential information.

## 62. Ethics and Law

Law is the minimum standard enforced by government. Ethics goes beyond law.

An act may be legal but still unethical.

Example:

An advertisement may technically avoid false statements but still mislead customers by hiding important facts. That may be unethical.

## 63. Why Business Ethics Is Important

Business ethics is important because it:

1. Builds trust.
2. Improves public image.
3. Reduces conflict.
4. Improves employee morale.
5. Strengthens customer loyalty.
6. Supports long-term profit.
7. Prevents legal problems.
8. Improves society.

Ethical business is good business in the long run.

## 64. Elements of Business Ethics

The main elements are:

1. Top management commitment.
2. Publication of code.
3. Establishment of compliance mechanisms.
4. Involving employees at all levels.
5. Measuring results.

## 65. Top Management Commitment

Top management must openly support ethical conduct. The CEO and senior managers should lead by example.

If top managers behave unethically, employees will not take ethics seriously.

## 66. Publication of a Code

A code of ethics is a written document stating principles of conduct for the organisation.

It may cover:

1. Honesty.
2. Compliance with law.
3. Product safety.
4. Product quality.
5. Workplace health and safety.
6. Conflict of interest.
7. Employment practices.
8. Fair selling.
9. Fair marketing.
10. Financial reporting.

## 67. Compliance Mechanisms

A business must ensure that actual decisions match ethical standards.

Mechanisms include:

1. Ethical hiring.
2. Training.
3. Ethics audit.
4. Reporting channels.
5. Internal controls.
6. Complaint systems.
7. Disciplinary action for unethical conduct.

## 68. Involving Employees at All Levels

Employees implement ethical policies. Therefore, they should be involved in designing, understanding, and improving ethical systems.

Employee groups can examine ethical issues and suggest improvements.

## 69. Measuring Results

It is difficult to measure ethics perfectly, but firms can audit compliance, track complaints, review decisions, and improve policies.

## 70. Universal Ground Rules of Ethics

Ethical conduct requires:

1. Be trustworthy.
2. Respect others.
3. Accept responsibility.
4. Be fair.
5. Care for others.
6. Be a good citizen.

## 72. Long Answer Practice

### 72.1 Give arguments for and against social responsibility.

Arguments for social responsibility include justification for existence and growth, long-term interest, avoidance of government regulation, maintenance of society, availability of resources, converting problems into opportunities, better business environment, and responsibility for social problems caused by business.

Arguments against social responsibility include violation of profit maximisation, burden on consumers, lack of social skills, and lack of broad public support.

### 72.2 Explain the forces increasing concern for social responsibility.

Concern for social responsibility has increased due to threat of public regulation, pressure of labour movement, consumer consciousness, development of social standards, business education, link between social and business interests, and growth of professional management.

### 72.3 Explain why business is a social institution and not merely a profit-making unit.

Business uses society's resources, sells goods and services to society, depends on workers and consumers, follows laws made by society, and affects the environment. Therefore, it cannot exist only for profit. It must also serve social needs, protect stakeholders, and act responsibly.

### 72.4 Why should enterprises adopt pollution control measures?

Enterprises should adopt pollution control measures to reduce health hazards, reduce liability risk, save costs, improve public image, protect resources, improve quality of life, and meet legal and social expectations.

### 72.5 What steps can enterprises take for environmental protection?

Enterprises can commit top management to environmental protection, share commitment across departments, frame clear policies, use better raw materials and technology, treat waste scientifically, obey environmental laws, participate in government programmes, assess pollution control efforts, and educate employees and stakeholders.

### 72.6 Explain elements of business ethics.

The elements of business ethics are top management commitment, publication of a code of ethics, compliance mechanisms, involvement of employees at all levels, and measurement of results. These elements help convert ethical values into actual business behaviour.

## 78. End Summary

Social responsibility means that business must act according to the values and expectations of society. It is wider than legal responsibility because it includes voluntary and ethical conduct beyond law. Business has responsibilities toward owners, workers, consumers, government, community, and the environment. Although some argue that social responsibility conflicts with profit, modern business increasingly accepts that long-term profit depends on public trust and social cooperation. Environmental protection is a major responsibility because industrial activity can cause air, water, land, and noise pollution. Business ethics means fair, honest, and socially acceptable conduct in business. Ethical business is supported by top management commitment, code of ethics, compliance systems, employee involvement, and measurement of results.
