# Chapter 7: Formation of a Company

## 1. Chapter Snapshot

The company form of organisation is suitable for medium and large businesses because it can raise large capital, spread risk, and create a separate legal entity. But a company cannot come into existence casually. It must pass through legal stages and submit prescribed documents to the Registrar of Companies.

This chapter explains how a company is formed from business idea to legal birth and, in case of a public company raising funds from the public, to capital subscription.

## 2. Learning Targets

By the end of this chapter, a learner should be able to:

1. State the stages in formation of a company.
2. Explain promotion of a company.
3. Explain the role and legal position of promoters.
4. Explain feasibility studies done by promoters.
5. List the steps in promotion.
6. Explain Memorandum of Association and its clauses.
7. Explain Articles of Association.
8. List documents required for incorporation.
9. Explain Certificate of Incorporation and its effect.
10. Explain preliminary contracts and provisional contracts.
11. Explain capital subscription by a public company.
12. Explain prospectus, minimum subscription, allotment, and return of allotment.
13. Distinguish Memorandum of Association and Articles of Association.

## 3. Big Idea

Formation of a company means completing all steps from business idea to legal readiness for business.

```text
Business idea
-> Promotion
-> Incorporation
-> Capital subscription, if public funds are raised
-> Commencement of business
```

## 4. Why Company Formation Is Important

A company has a separate legal identity. It can own property, enter contracts, raise capital, sue, and be sued in its own name.

Because it affects investors, creditors, government, customers, and the public, the law requires careful formation.

## 5. Stages in Formation of a Company

The three main stages are:

1. Promotion.
2. Incorporation.
3. Capital subscription.

### 5.1 Private Company

A private company generally goes through:

1. Promotion.
2. Incorporation.

It cannot invite the public to subscribe to its securities, so it does not need the public capital subscription process.

### 5.2 Public Company

A public company goes through:

1. Promotion.
2. Incorporation.
3. Capital subscription, if it raises funds from the public.

## 6. Promotion of a Company

Promotion is the first stage in company formation. It begins with identifying a business opportunity and taking steps to form a company to exploit that opportunity.

In simple words:

```text
Promotion = Discovering a business idea and preparing to form a company
```

## 7. Promoter

A promoter is a person who undertakes to form a company with reference to a business idea and takes necessary steps to set it going.

Promoters may be:

1. Individuals.
2. A group of persons.
3. An existing company.

## 8. Role of Promoters

Promoters:

1. Identify a business opportunity.
2. Study feasibility.
3. Arrange resources.
4. Choose company name.
5. Decide first members and directors.
6. Appoint professionals.
7. Prepare documents.
8. Submit documents for incorporation.
9. Take steps to bring the company into existence.

## 9. Functions of Promoters

The main functions are:

1. Identification of business opportunity.
2. Feasibility studies.
3. Name approval.
4. Fixing signatories to Memorandum of Association.
5. Appointment of professionals.
6. Preparation of necessary documents.

## 10. Identification of Business Opportunity

The promoter first identifies a business opportunity.

Examples:

1. A new product.
2. A new service.
3. A new technology.
4. A new method of distribution.
5. A market gap.
6. A large-scale expansion possibility.

The opportunity must have investment potential.

## 11. Feasibility Studies

Not every business idea can become a profitable company. Promoters study whether the idea is feasible.

Important feasibility studies are:

1. Technical feasibility.
2. Financial feasibility.
3. Economic feasibility.

## 12. Technical Feasibility

Technical feasibility asks whether the business idea can be executed technically.

It examines:

1. Availability of technology.
2. Availability of raw materials.
3. Availability of machines.
4. Availability of technical skill.
5. Production process.
6. Practical possibility of manufacturing or service delivery.

Example:

If a product needs a special metal that is not available and cannot be imported, the idea may be technically unfeasible.

## 13. Financial Feasibility

Financial feasibility asks whether required funds can be arranged.

It examines:

1. Total capital needed.
2. Fixed capital.
3. Working capital.
4. Borrowing possibilities.
5. Investor interest.
6. Cost of raising funds.

If required funds are too large and cannot be arranged, the idea may be dropped.

## 14. Economic Feasibility

Economic feasibility asks whether the idea is likely to be profitable.

Even if a project is technically possible and funds are available, it should not be launched if expected profit is too low or risk is too high.

It examines:

1. Demand.
2. Competition.
3. Cost.
4. Selling price.
5. Expected return.
6. Break-even possibility.
7. Long-term viability.

## 15. Name Approval

After deciding to form a company, promoters choose a name and apply to the Registrar of Companies for approval.

Usually multiple names are proposed in order of preference.

### 15.1 Undesirable Names

A proposed name may be rejected if:

1. It is identical or too similar to an existing company name.
2. It is misleading.
3. It wrongly suggests government patronage.
4. It violates rules relating to prohibited names, emblems, or official symbols.

## 16. Fixing Signatories to Memorandum

Promoters decide who will sign the Memorandum of Association.

These signatories are the first subscribers of the company.

Minimum subscribers:

1. Public company: at least seven.
2. Private company: at least two.

They usually also become first directors, subject to law and documents.

## 17. Appointment of Professionals

Promoters may appoint professionals to help prepare documents and complete legal formalities.

Examples:

1. Company secretary.
2. Chartered accountant.
3. Cost accountant.
4. Advocate.
5. Merchant banker.
6. Auditor.

Professional assistance does not automatically make these persons promoters if they act only in professional capacity.

## 18. Preparation of Necessary Documents

Promoters prepare documents required for incorporation.

Important documents include:

1. Memorandum of Association.
2. Articles of Association.
3. Consent of proposed directors.
4. Agreement with managing director, whole-time director, or manager, if any.
5. Statutory declaration.
6. Receipt of registration fee.
7. Notice of registered office address, if submitted at incorporation.

## 19. Memorandum of Association

Memorandum of Association is the most important document of a company. It defines the company's objectives and scope of powers.

No company can legally carry on activities beyond its memorandum.

In simple words:

```text
Memorandum = Charter of the company
```

## 20. Clauses of Memorandum of Association

The main clauses are:

1. Name clause.
2. Registered office clause.
3. Objects clause.
4. Liability clause.
5. Capital clause.
6. Subscription clause.

## 21. Name Clause

This clause contains the approved name of the company.

The company is known by this name in legal and business dealings.

## 22. Registered Office Clause

This clause states the state in which the registered office of the company will be situated.

The exact address may be notified to the Registrar within the prescribed time after incorporation, if not given earlier.

## 23. Objects Clause

This is the most important clause. It states the purpose for which the company is formed.

The company cannot legally undertake activities beyond its objects.

The objects clause protects:

1. Shareholders, by showing how their money will be used.
2. Creditors, by showing the company's permitted activities.
3. Public, by defining the company's scope.

## 24. Liability Clause

This clause states the liability of members.

In a company limited by shares, members' liability is limited to the unpaid amount on shares held by them.

Example:

If a shareholder owns 1,000 shares of Rs. 10 each and has paid Rs. 6 per share, unpaid amount is Rs. 4 per share. Maximum liability is:

```text
1,000 shares x Rs. 4 = Rs. 4,000
```

## 25. Capital Clause

This clause states the authorised share capital of the company and its division into shares.

Example:

Authorised capital may be Rs. 25,00,000 divided into 2,50,000 shares of Rs. 10 each.

The company cannot issue share capital beyond authorised capital unless it alters the capital clause according to law.

## 26. Subscription Clause

This clause contains the declaration by subscribers that they agree to form the company and take the number of shares written against their names.

## 27. Articles of Association

Articles of Association contain rules for internal management of the company.

In simple words:

```text
Articles = Internal rule book of the company
```

Articles are subordinate to:

1. Companies Act.
2. Memorandum of Association.

They cannot contradict the memorandum.

## 28. Matters Usually Covered in Articles

Articles may cover:

1. Share capital.
2. Issue of shares.
3. Allotment of shares.
4. Calls on shares.
5. Lien on shares.
6. Transfer and transmission of shares.
7. Forfeiture of shares.
8. Alteration of capital.
9. Buy-back.
10. Share certificates.
11. Dematerialisation.
12. Voting rights.
13. Proxies.
14. Meetings.
15. Directors.
16. Borrowing powers.
17. Dividends.
18. Accounts and audit.
19. Winding up.
20. Indemnity.

## 29. Consent of Proposed Directors

Each proposed director must give written consent to act as director.

If the articles require qualification shares, directors may also undertake to buy and pay for those shares.

## 30. Qualification Shares

Qualification shares are shares that directors may be required by the articles to hold so that they have a stake in the company.

## 31. Agreement With Key Managerial Person

If the company proposes to appoint a managing director, whole-time director, or manager under an agreement, that agreement may need to be submitted during registration.

## 32. Statutory Declaration

A statutory declaration states that all legal requirements for registration have been complied with.

It may be signed by prescribed professionals involved in formation and by a person named as director, manager, or secretary.

## 33. Receipt of Registration Fee

Registration fee must be paid with documents. Fee may depend on authorised share capital.

## 34. Legal Position of Promoters

Promoters perform important work before the company exists. Their legal position is special.

## 35. Promoters Are Not Agents

Promoters cannot be agents of the company before incorporation because the company does not yet exist.

Therefore, they may be personally liable for contracts made before incorporation.

## 36. Promoters Are Not Trustees

Promoters are not trustees in the strict sense because the company does not yet exist during promotion.

However, they stand in a fiduciary position and must act honestly for the proposed company.

## 37. Fiduciary Position

A fiduciary position means a position of trust and confidence.

Promoters must:

1. Avoid secret profits.
2. Disclose material facts.
3. Act in good faith.
4. Not misuse their position.

If they make secret profits, the company may rescind the contract or claim damages.

## 38. Remuneration of Promoters

Promoters do not have a legal right to claim expenses automatically.

But the company may choose to:

1. Reimburse preliminary expenses.
2. Pay lump sum remuneration.
3. Pay commission.
4. Allot shares or debentures.
5. Give option to buy securities later.

## 39. Preliminary Contracts

Preliminary contracts are contracts made by promoters with third parties before incorporation of the company.

They are also called pre-incorporation contracts.

### 39.1 Legal Effect

1. They are not legally binding on the company.
2. The company cannot ratify them because it did not exist when they were made.
3. The company may enter fresh contracts after incorporation on similar terms.
4. Promoters remain personally liable to third parties.

## 40. Provisional Contracts

Provisional contracts are contracts made after incorporation but before the company is entitled to commence business.

They are different from preliminary contracts because the company already exists at the time of contract.

## 41. Incorporation

Incorporation is the second stage in formation of a company. It is the stage at which the company is legally registered and comes into existence.

Promoters file an application with the Registrar of Companies of the state where the registered office will be situated.

## 42. Documents for Incorporation

The application is accompanied by:

1. Memorandum of Association duly stamped, signed, and witnessed.
2. Articles of Association duly stamped, signed, and witnessed, or adoption of model articles where allowed.
3. Written consent of proposed directors.
4. Agreement with managing director, manager, or whole-time director, if any.
5. Copy of name approval letter.
6. Statutory declaration of compliance.
7. Notice of exact registered office address, if submitted at this stage.
8. Evidence of payment of registration fee.

## 43. Scrutiny by Registrar

The Registrar checks whether documents are in order and legal formalities have been complied with.

When satisfied, the Registrar issues Certificate of Incorporation.

## 44. Certificate of Incorporation

Certificate of Incorporation is the legal document that confirms the birth of the company.

It is like the birth certificate of a company.

On the date printed on the certificate, the company becomes:

1. A separate legal entity.
2. Capable of entering valid contracts.
3. A body with perpetual succession.
4. Legally existing in its own name.

## 45. Effect of Certificate of Incorporation

Certificate of Incorporation is conclusive evidence that the company has been properly incorporated.

This means that once the certificate is issued, the legal existence of the company cannot normally be challenged on the ground of defects in registration formalities.

If illegal objects exist, the remedy is winding up according to law, not denying that the company was born.

## 46. Corporate Identity Number

Registrar of Companies allots a Corporate Identity Number, or CIN, to the company. It identifies the company in official records.

## 47. Director Identification Number

Every individual intending to be appointed as director must obtain Director Identification Number, or DIN, according to prescribed rules.

An individual should not obtain more than one DIN.

## 48. Commencement of Business

In the textbook context, both public and private companies are required to obtain certificate for commencement of business within 180 days of incorporation before undertaking business operations.

For exam answers, write according to the textbook and question wording.

## 49. Capital Subscription

Capital subscription is the stage in which a public company raises funds from the public by issuing securities such as shares or debentures.

Private companies do not invite the public to subscribe to securities.

## 50. Steps in Capital Subscription

The main steps are:

1. SEBI approval.
2. Filing of prospectus.
3. Appointment of bankers, brokers, and underwriters.
4. Minimum subscription.
5. Application to stock exchange.
6. Allotment of shares.
7. Return of allotment.

## 51. SEBI Approval

SEBI protects investors by requiring disclosure of material information.

A public company inviting funds from the public must follow SEBI guidelines and make adequate disclosure before issuing securities.

## 52. Filing of Prospectus

A prospectus is a document inviting the public to subscribe to securities or deposits of a company.

It contains information that helps investors decide whether to invest.

The prospectus must:

1. Disclose all material information.
2. Avoid misstatements.
3. Be filed with Registrar of Companies.
4. Protect investor interest.

## 53. Is Prospectus Necessary for Every Company?

No.

A private company does not issue a prospectus because it cannot invite public subscription.

A public company must issue a prospectus if it invites the public to subscribe. If it does not invite the public, it may have to file a statement or other document as required by law and textbook context.

## 54. Appointment of Bankers, Brokers, and Underwriters

### 54.1 Bankers

Bankers receive application money from applicants.

### 54.2 Brokers

Brokers distribute application forms and encourage investors to apply for shares.

### 54.3 Underwriters

Underwriters agree to buy unsubscribed shares if the public does not subscribe fully. They receive commission for this risk.

Underwriting is not compulsory in every case.

## 55. Minimum Subscription

Minimum subscription is the minimum amount that must be subscribed before allotment can proceed.

As per the textbook, SEBI guidelines require minimum subscription of 90 percent of the issue size.

If applications are received for less than 90 percent, allotment cannot be made and application money must be returned.

## 56. Application to Stock Exchange

The company applies to at least one stock exchange for permission to deal in its securities.

If permission is not granted within the prescribed time, allotment becomes void and application money must be returned.

## 57. Allotment of Shares

Allotment means acceptance of applications and distribution of shares to applicants.

Before allotment:

1. Application money should remain in a separate bank account.
2. It should not be used by the company.

After allotment:

1. Successful applicants receive allotment letters.
2. Excess application money is returned or adjusted.
3. Unsuccessful applicants receive refund.

## 58. Return of Allotment

Return of allotment is a statement filed with Registrar of Companies after shares are allotted.

It contains details such as:

1. Names of shareholders.
2. Number of shares allotted.
3. Amount payable.
4. Other prescribed details.

It is signed by a director or secretary and filed within the prescribed time.

## 59. Memorandum and Articles: Key Difference

| Basis | Memorandum of Association | Articles of Association |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Charter defining objects and powers of company | Rules for internal management |
| Position | Main document | Subsidiary document |
| Relation with law | Subordinate to Companies Act | Subordinate to Companies Act and memorandum |
| Relationship defined | Company and outsiders | Company and members; members inter se |
| Validity of acts beyond it | Invalid and cannot be ratified | May be ratified if not against memorandum or law |
| Necessity | Every company must file it | Public company may adopt model articles where allowed |
| Scope | External scope and purpose | Internal rules and procedures |

## 60. Company Formation Flowchart

```text
Business idea
-> Promoters
-> Feasibility studies
-> Name approval
-> Memorandum and articles
-> Other documents
-> Application to Registrar
-> Certificate of Incorporation
-> Capital subscription, for public issue
-> Allotment and return of allotment
-> Commencement of business
```

## 61. Case File: Avtar's Carburettor

Avtar has invented a carburettor that can reduce petrol consumption. He wants to manufacture it on a large scale and needs large funds. He rejects partnership because risk and capital need are high.

### 61.1 Suitable Form

Company form is suitable because:

1. Large capital can be raised.
2. Risk can be spread among shareholders.
3. Limited liability protects investors.
4. Company form suits large-scale production.
5. Separate legal entity supports contracts and expansion.

### 61.2 Steps He Should Know

He should understand:

1. Promotion.
2. Feasibility studies.
3. Name approval.
4. Preparation of memorandum and articles.
5. Incorporation.
6. Certificate of incorporation.
7. Capital subscription if public funds are raised.

## 62. Short Answer Practice

### 62.1 Name the stages in formation of a company.

The stages are promotion, incorporation, and capital subscription. A private company generally needs promotion and incorporation, while a public company raising funds from the public also goes through capital subscription.

### 62.2 List documents required for incorporation.

Documents include Memorandum of Association, Articles of Association, consent of proposed directors, agreement with managing director or manager if any, name approval letter, statutory declaration, notice of registered office address if submitted, and proof of payment of registration fee.

### 62.3 What is a prospectus?

A prospectus is a document issued by a public company inviting the public to subscribe to its securities or deposits. It contains material information for investors.

### 62.4 Is prospectus necessary for every company?

No. A private company cannot invite public subscription and therefore does not issue a prospectus. A public company issues a prospectus when it raises funds from the public.

### 62.5 What is return of allotment?

Return of allotment is a statement filed with Registrar of Companies after allotment of shares, containing details of shareholders and shares allotted.

### 62.6 At which stage does a company interact with SEBI?

A public company interacts with SEBI at the capital subscription stage when it proposes to raise funds from the public.

## 63. Long Answer Practice

### 63.1 Explain promotion and legal position of promoters.

Promotion is the first stage of company formation. It involves identifying a business opportunity, studying feasibility, arranging resources, selecting name, fixing subscribers, appointing professionals, and preparing documents.

Promoters are not agents because the company does not exist before incorporation. They are not trustees in strict sense, but they occupy a fiduciary position. They must not make secret profits, must disclose material facts, and may be personally liable for preliminary contracts. They have no automatic legal right to expenses, but the company may reimburse or remunerate them.

### 63.2 Explain steps in promotion of a company.

Steps in promotion include identifying a business opportunity, conducting technical, financial, and economic feasibility studies, obtaining name approval, fixing signatories to the memorandum, appointing professionals, and preparing documents such as memorandum, articles, consent of directors, agreements, statutory declaration, and fee receipt.

### 63.3 What is Memorandum of Association? Explain its clauses.

Memorandum of Association is the charter of a company. It defines the company's objectives and scope of powers.

Its clauses are:

1. Name clause: states company name.
2. Registered office clause: states the state of registered office.
3. Objects clause: states purposes of company.
4. Liability clause: states liability of members.
5. Capital clause: states authorised capital and share division.
6. Subscription clause: states subscribers' agreement to take shares.

### 63.4 Distinguish memorandum and articles.

Memorandum defines the objects and powers of the company, while articles define internal management rules. Memorandum is the main document and articles are subsidiary. Memorandum defines relationship with outsiders, while articles define relationship among company and members. Acts beyond memorandum are invalid and cannot be ratified, but acts beyond articles may be ratified if they do not violate memorandum or law.

### 63.5 What is Certificate of Incorporation?

Certificate of Incorporation is the legal certificate issued by Registrar of Companies after registration formalities are completed. It is conclusive evidence of the legal existence of the company and acts as its birth certificate.

### 63.6 Explain stages of formation of a company.

The stages are promotion, incorporation, and capital subscription. Promotion includes identifying opportunity, feasibility studies, name approval, subscribers, professionals, and documents. Incorporation involves filing documents with Registrar and obtaining Certificate of Incorporation. Capital subscription applies to a public company raising funds from the public and includes SEBI approval, prospectus, bankers, brokers, underwriters, minimum subscription, stock exchange application, allotment, and return of allotment.

## 68. End Summary

Formation of a company involves promotion, incorporation, and, where public funds are raised, capital subscription. Promotion begins with a business idea and includes feasibility studies, name approval, fixing subscribers, appointing professionals, and preparing documents. Memorandum of Association defines the company's objects and powers, while Articles of Association contain internal rules. Promoters are not agents or trustees before incorporation, but they occupy a fiduciary position and may be personally liable for preliminary contracts. Incorporation creates the company as a legal person through Certificate of Incorporation. A public company raising capital from the public must follow SEBI requirements, file prospectus, appoint bankers, brokers and underwriters where needed, receive minimum subscription, apply to stock exchange, allot shares, and file return of allotment.
