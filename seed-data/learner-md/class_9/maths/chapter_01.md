## Big Idea

A point on a line can be located using one number.

A point on a plane needs two numbers.

These two numbers are called coordinates.

The coordinate system lets us convert geometry into numbers and numbers into geometry.

| Situation | Coordinate Idea |
|---|---|
| A room floor plan | Locate furniture by distances from two walls. |
| A city grid | Locate street intersections using two road numbers. |
| A computer screen | Locate pixels by horizontal and vertical positions. |
| A graph | Locate a point using x-coordinate and y-coordinate. |

## 1.1 - Historical and Mathematical Background

The idea of locating places using grids is very old.

The raw chapter highlights several historical connections:

| Historical Link | Coordinate Idea |
|---|---|
| Sindhu-Sarasvati Civilisation | Streets arranged in North-South and East-West directions like a grid. |
| Baudhayana | Used geometric ideas involving perpendicular directions. |
| Ujjayini | Used as an important reference meridian in ancient astronomy and geography. |
| Aryabhata | Used trigonometric ideas to calculate positions of stars and cities. |
| Brahmagupta | Formalized zero and negative numbers, essential for modern coordinate axes. |
| Al-Biruni | Used Indian mathematical methods for geographic coordinates. |
| Omar Khayyam | Connected algebraic problems with geometry. |
| Rene Descartes | Formalized coordinate geometry in Europe. |

### Why Zero and Negative Numbers Matter

The origin has coordinate:

$$
(0,0)
$$

Without zero, there would be no natural starting point.

Without negative numbers, we could not represent:

- points to the left of the origin;
- points below the origin;
- the second, third, and fourth quadrants.

### Why a Floor Map Cannot Show Everything

A floor map shows positions on the floor.

It can show:

- door positions;
- table feet;
- bed corners;

It cannot fully show:

- objects at different vertical levels.

Reason:

> A floor map is two-dimensional. It records length and width, not height.

## 1.3 - The 2-D Cartesian Coordinate System

### From Number Line to Coordinate Plane

In earlier classes, you studied the number line.

A number line is one-dimensional:

```text
left/right only
```

The coordinate plane is two-dimensional:

```text
left/right and up/down
```

To locate a point on a plane, we use two perpendicular number lines.

---

## Coordinate Axes

| Axis | Direction | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **x-axis** | Horizontal | Measures left-right position. |
| **y-axis** | Vertical | Measures up-down position. |

The axes meet at the origin.

$$
O = (0,0)
$$

### Sign Convention

| Direction from Origin | Sign |
|---|---|
| Right | Positive x |
| Left | Negative x |
| Up | Positive y |
| Down | Negative y |

---

## Coordinates of a Point

A point is written as:

$$
P(x,y)
$$

The first number is the x-coordinate.

The second number is the y-coordinate.

### Meaning

| Coordinate | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(x\) | Horizontal distance from the y-axis, with sign showing left or right. |
| \(y\) | Vertical distance from the x-axis, with sign showing up or down. |

### Reading an Ordered Pair

For:

$$
S(3,-5)
$$

| Coordinate | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(3\) | Move 3 units right from the origin. |
| \(-5\) | Move 5 units down. |

So \(S(3,-5)\) lies in Quadrant IV.

---

## Points on the Axes

### Points on the x-axis

Any point on the x-axis has y-coordinate 0.

General form:

$$
(x,0)
$$

Examples:

| Point | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(B(4.5,0)\) | 4.5 units right of origin on x-axis. |
| \(E(-2.9,0)\) | 2.9 units left of origin on x-axis. |

### Points on the y-axis

Any point on the y-axis has x-coordinate 0.

General form:

$$
(0,y)
$$

Examples:

| Point | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(H(0,4)\) | 4 units above origin on y-axis. |
| \(G(0,-4.5)\) | 4.5 units below origin on y-axis. |

### Origin

The origin lies on both axes.

$$
O(0,0)
$$

---

## Quadrants

The axes divide the coordinate plane into four regions called quadrants.

| Quadrant | Sign of x | Sign of y | Example |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| **I** | + | + | \((3,4)\) |
| **II** | - | + | \((-5,3)\) |
| **III** | - | - | \((-2,-6)\) |
| **IV** | + | - | \((3,-5)\) |

### Quadrant Rule

To identify the quadrant:

1. Look at the sign of x.
2. Look at the sign of y.
3. Match the sign pair.

### Examples

| Point | Sign Pattern | Location |
|---|---|---|
| \(P(2,7)\) | \(+,+\) | Quadrant I |
| \(Q(-5,3)\) | \(-,+\) | Quadrant II |
| \(R(-4,-2)\) | \(-,-\) | Quadrant III |
| \(S(3,-5)\) | \(+,-\) | Quadrant IV |
| \(A(0,-2)\) | x is 0 | y-axis, not in a quadrant |
| \(B(4,0)\) | y is 0 | x-axis, not in a quadrant |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Points on the axes are not considered to be in any quadrant.

---

## Ordered Pairs: Why Order Matters

The point \((x,y)\) is usually different from \((y,x)\).

Example:

$$
(3,5) \ne (5,3)
$$

They are equal only when:

$$
x = y
$$

So:

$$
(x,y) = (y,x)\ \text{if and only if}\ x = y
$$

Example:

$$
(4,4) = (4,4)
$$

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Start at origin \(O(0,0)\). |
| **2** | Move horizontally according to x. |
| **3** | From there, move vertically according to y. |
| **4** | Mark the point. |
| **5** | Label it clearly. |

### Direction Guide

| Coordinate | Direction |
|---|---|
| Positive x | Right |
| Negative x | Left |
| Positive y | Up |
| Negative y | Down |

### Example: Plot \(Q(-5,3)\)

1. Start at origin.
2. Move 5 units left.
3. Move 3 units up.
4. Mark \(Q(-5,3)\).

## 1.4 - Distance Between Two Points in the 2-D Plane

### Distance Parallel to an Axis

If two points lie on a horizontal line, their y-coordinates are the same.

Distance between:

$$
(x_1,y)\quad \text{and}\quad (x_2,y)
$$

is:

$$
|x_2-x_1|
$$

If two points lie on a vertical line, their x-coordinates are the same.

Distance between:

$$
(x,y_1)\quad \text{and}\quad (x,y_2)
$$

is:

$$
|y_2-y_1|
$$

---

## Distance When Points Are Not Parallel to an Axis

For two general points, we use the Baudhayana-Pythagoras theorem.

Suppose:

$$
A(x_1,y_1)
$$

and:

$$
B(x_2,y_2)
$$

The horizontal change is:

$$
x_2-x_1
$$

The vertical change is:

$$
y_2-y_1
$$

These form the two legs of a right triangle.

Therefore, distance is:

$$
AB = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}
$$

---

## Worked Example 1 - Distance Between A and D

Find the distance between:

$$
A(3,4)
$$

and:

$$
D(7,1)
$$

### Step 1 - Horizontal Difference

$$
x_2-x_1 = 7-3 = 4
$$

### Step 2 - Vertical Difference

$$
y_2-y_1 = 1-4 = -3
$$

The length is 3 units vertically. Squaring removes the sign.

### Step 3 - Apply Distance Formula

$$
AD = \sqrt{4^2 + (-3)^2}
$$

$$
AD = \sqrt{16+9}
$$

$$
AD = \sqrt{25}
$$

$$
AD = 5\ \text{units}
$$

---

## Worked Example 2 - Distance Between D and M

Find the distance between:

$$
D(7,1)
$$

and:

$$
M(9,6)
$$

Horizontal difference:

$$
9-7 = 2
$$

Vertical difference:

$$
6-1 = 5
$$

Distance:

$$
DM = \sqrt{2^2+5^2}
$$

$$
DM = \sqrt{4+25}
$$

$$
DM = \sqrt{29}\ \text{units}
$$

---

## Worked Example 3 - Distance Between M and A

Find the distance between:

$$
M(9,6)
$$

and:

$$
A(3,4)
$$

Horizontal difference:

$$
9-3 = 6
$$

Vertical difference:

$$
6-4 = 2
$$

Distance:

$$
MA = \sqrt{6^2+2^2}
$$

$$
MA = \sqrt{36+4}
$$

$$
MA = \sqrt{40}\ \text{units}
$$

## Why Negative Differences Do Not Matter

In the distance formula, differences are squared.

Example:

$$
(-4)^2 = 16
$$

and:

$$
4^2 = 16
$$

So it does not matter whether \(x_2-x_1\) or \(y_2-y_1\) is positive or negative.

Distance is always non-negative.

## Reflection in the y-axis

When a point is reflected in the y-axis:

| Original Point | Reflected Point |
|---|---|
| \((x,y)\) | \((-x,y)\) |

The x-coordinate changes sign. The y-coordinate stays the same.

Examples:

| Original | Reflection in y-axis |
|---|---|
| \(A(3,4)\) | \(A'(-3,4)\) |
| \(D(7,1)\) | \(D'(-7,1)\) |
| \(M(9,6)\) | \(M'(-9,6)\) |

### What Remains the Same?

Distances remain the same under reflection.

The triangle changes position, but side lengths are preserved.

---

## Reflection in the x-axis

When a point is reflected in the x-axis:

| Original Point | Reflected Point |
|---|---|
| \((x,y)\) | \((x,-y)\) |

The y-coordinate changes sign. The x-coordinate stays the same.

$$
x_M = \frac{x_1+x_2}{2}
$$

and:

$$
y_M = \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}
$$

## Coordinate Geometry Toolkit

### To Find the Quadrant

| x Sign | y Sign | Location |
|---|---|---|
| + | + | Quadrant I |
| - | + | Quadrant II |
| - | - | Quadrant III |
| + | - | Quadrant IV |
| 0 | any | y-axis |
| any | 0 | x-axis |

### To Find Distance

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Label points as \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\). |
| **2** | Find \(x_2-x_1\). |
| **3** | Find \(y_2-y_1\). |
| **4** | Square both differences. |
| **5** | Add them. |
| **6** | Take the square root. |

### To Check if a Point Is on a Circle Centered at Origin

If circle center is:

$$
O(0,0)
$$

then distance of point \(P(x,y)\) from origin is:

$$
OP = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}
$$

Compare \(OP\) with radius \(r\):

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(OP < r\) | Point is inside the circle. |
| \(OP = r\) | Point is on the circle. |
| \(OP > r\) | Point is outside the circle. |

### To Check if Two Circles Intersect

If two circles have radii \(r_1\) and \(r_2\), and the distance between centers is \(d\):

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(d > r_1+r_2\) | Circles are separate. |
| \(d = r_1+r_2\) | Circles touch externally. |
| \(|r_1-r_2| < d < r_1+r_2\) | Circles intersect at two points. |
| \(d = |r_1-r_2|\) | Circles touch internally. |
| \(d < |r_1-r_2|\) | One circle lies inside the other without touching. |

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Writing \((y,x)\) instead of \((x,y)\). | Always write x-coordinate first, y-coordinate second. |
| Treating a point on an axis as a quadrant point. | Axis points are not in quadrants. |
| Saying distance can be negative. | Distance is always non-negative. |
| Forgetting absolute value for horizontal/vertical distance. | Use \(|x_2-x_1|\) or \(|y_2-y_1|\). |
| Mixing left/right and up/down. | x controls horizontal movement; y controls vertical movement. |
| Using only one coordinate to locate a point in a plane. | A plane location needs two coordinates. |
| Assuming \((3,5)\) and \((5,3)\) are same. | They are different unless both coordinates are equal. |
| Forgetting square root in distance formula. | Distance is the square root of the sum of squares. |

### Exercise 3 - RAMP

$$

Useful observations:

- \(A(0,-2)\) and \(M(-5,-2)\) have the same y-coordinate, so \(AM\) is horizontal.
- \(M(-5,-2)\) and \(P(-5,2)\) have the same x-coordinate, so \(MP\) is vertical.

Points:

$$
M(-5,-2)

and:

$$
P(-5,2)
$$

are mirror images of each other in the x-axis because their x-coordinates are the same and their y-coordinates have opposite signs.

### Exercise 6 - Checking Collinearity Without Plotting

Given:

$$
M(-3,-4),\quad A(0,0),\quad G(6,8)
$$

Notice:

From \(M\) to \(A\):

$$
\Delta x = 0-(-3)=3
$$

$$
\Delta y = 0-(-4)=4
$$

From \(A\) to \(G\):

$$
\Delta x = 6-0=6
$$

$$
\Delta y = 8-0=8
$$

The second movement is exactly twice the first:

$$
(6,8)=2(3,4)
$$

So the points lie on the same straight line.

---

### Exercise 7 - Same Method

Given:

$$
R(-5,-1),\quad B(-2,-5),\quad C(4,-12)
$$

From \(R\) to \(B\):

$$
\Delta x = -2-(-5)=3
$$

$$
\Delta y = -5-(-1)=-4
$$

From \(B\) to \(C\):

$$
\Delta x = 4-(-2)=6
$$

$$
\Delta y = -12-(-5)=-7
$$

The direction ratios are not proportional:

$$
(6,-7) \ne 2(3,-4)
$$

So the three points are not collinear.

### Exercise 9 - Midpoint Test

For \(S(x_1,y_1)\) and \(T(x_2,y_2)\), midpoint is:

$$
\left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)

Example:

For:

$$
S(-3,0),\quad T(3,0)

midpoint is:

$$
\left(\frac{-3+3}{2},\frac{0+0}{2}\right)
$$

$$
(0,0)
$$

So \(M(0,0)\) is the midpoint.

### Exercise 16 - Is ABCD a Square?

Given:

$$
A(2,1),\quad B(-1,2),\quad C(-2,-1),\quad D(1,-2)
$$

Find side \(AB\):

$$
AB=\sqrt{(-1-2)^2+(2-1)^2}
$$

$$
AB=\sqrt{(-3)^2+1^2}
$$

$$
AB=\sqrt{10}
$$

Similarly:

$$
BC=CD=DA=\sqrt{10}
$$

Now diagonal \(AC\):

$$
AC=\sqrt{(-2-2)^2+(-1-1)^2}
$$

$$
AC=\sqrt{(-4)^2+(-2)^2}
$$

$$
AC=\sqrt{20}
$$

Diagonal \(BD\):

$$
BD=\sqrt{(1-(-1))^2+(-2-2)^2}
$$

$$
BD=\sqrt{2^2+(-4)^2}
$$

$$
BD=\sqrt{20}
$$

All sides are equal and diagonals are equal. Therefore, \(ABCD\) is a square.

Area of square:

$$
\text{Area} = (\text{side})^2
$$

$$
\text{Area} = (\sqrt{10})^2 = 10
$$

So the area is:

$$
10\ \text{square units}
$$

## One-Page Summary

A coordinate system uses numbers to locate points exactly. On a line, one number is enough. On a plane, two numbers are needed. The Cartesian coordinate plane uses two perpendicular axes: the horizontal x-axis and the vertical y-axis. Their point of intersection is the origin, \(O(0,0)\).

A point is written as \((x,y)\), where \(x\) gives horizontal position and \(y\) gives vertical position. Positive x is to the right, negative x is to the left, positive y is upward, and negative y is downward. Points on the x-axis have the form \((x,0)\), and points on the y-axis have the form \((0,y)\).

The axes divide the plane into four quadrants. The sign patterns are: Quadrant I \((+,+)\), Quadrant II \((-,+)\), Quadrant III \((-,-)\), and Quadrant IV \((+,-)\).

The order of coordinates matters. Usually \((x,y)\) and \((y,x)\) are different points unless \(x=y\).

Distance between two points is found by using horizontal and vertical differences as the two sides of a right triangle. By the Baudhayana-Pythagoras theorem:

$$
d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}
$$

Coordinates are useful in room plans, maps, city grids, computer screens, geometry, and algebra.
