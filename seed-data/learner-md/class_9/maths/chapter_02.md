## Big Idea

A linear polynomial describes a quantity that changes by a constant amount when the variable changes by equal steps.

Examples:

| Situation | Linear Expression | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Square perimeter with side \(x\) | \(4x\) | Perimeter grows by 4 for each 1 unit increase in side. |
| Chess club fee | \(200+50m\) | Fixed fee plus 50 per match. |
| Pocket money spent daily | \(100-5n\) | Amount decreases by 5 each day. |
| Auto fare after first 2 km | \(15n-5\) | Fare grows by 15 per extra kilometre pattern. |
| Straight-line relationship | \(y=ax+b\) | \(a\) controls slope; \(b\) controls y-intercept. |

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Algebraic expression** | Expression made using numbers, variables, and operation symbols. |
| **Variable** | A letter that represents a number that can vary. |
| **Term** | Part of an expression separated by plus or minus signs. |
| **Coefficient** | Numerical factor multiplying a variable term. |
| **Constant** | Term with no variable. |
| **Polynomial** | Algebraic expression in one variable with non-negative integer powers of that variable. |
| **Univariate polynomial** | Polynomial in one variable. |
| **Degree** | Highest power of the variable in a polynomial. |
| **Constant polynomial** | Polynomial of degree 0, such as \(8\). |
| **Linear polynomial** | Polynomial of degree 1, such as \(2x+3\). |
| **Quadratic polynomial** | Polynomial of degree 2, such as \(x^2+5x+1\). |

## 2.0 - Introduction: From Expressions to Linear Polynomials

In earlier classes, you studied algebraic expressions.

This chapter focuses on a special kind of algebraic expression: **linear polynomials**.

Before reaching linear polynomials, we first review:

- terms;
- variables;
- coefficients;
- constants;
- powers;
- degree.

## 2.1 - Algebraic Expressions and Polynomials

### Example: Pens and Pencils

Suppose:

- each red box has 4 pens;
- each blue box has 5 pencils;
- Raju buys \(x\) red boxes and \(y\) blue boxes;
- he gets 3 extra pens free.

Total quantity:

$$
4x + 5y + 3
$$

### Parts of the Expression

| Part | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(4x\), \(5y\), \(3\) | Terms |
| \(x\), \(y\) | Variables |
| 4 | Coefficient of \(x\) |
| 5 | Coefficient of \(y\) |
| 3 | Constant term |

---

## Example: Garden Cost

A rectangular garden has:

- length \(l\) metres;
- width \(w\) metres;
- wire fence along length at Rs 100 per metre;
- wooden fence along width at Rs 80 per metre;
- seeds over the area at Rs 50 per square metre.

Cost of fencing along two lengths:

$$
2l \times 100 = 200l
$$

Cost of fencing along two widths:

$$
2w \times 80 = 160w
$$

Cost of seeds over the area:

$$
50 \times l \times w = 50lw
$$

Total cost:

$$
200l + 160w + 50lw
$$

This expression has two variables, \(l\) and \(w\). It is not a one-variable polynomial.

---

## Example: Rectangle from a 20 cm Wire

A wire of length 20 cm is bent to form rectangles.

If length is:

$$
x
$$

then total length plus width relation is:

$$
2(\text{length}+\text{width})=20
$$

So:

$$
\text{length}+\text{width}=10
$$

Width:

$$
10-x
$$

Area:

$$
x(10-x)
$$

Expanding:

$$
10x-x^2
$$

This is a polynomial in one variable, \(x\), but it is not linear because the highest power is 2.

## One-Variable Polynomials

A one-variable polynomial is an algebraic expression involving one variable and its non-negative integer powers.

Examples:

| Polynomial | Variable | Highest Power | Degree | Type |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| \(4x\) | \(x\) | 1 | 1 | Linear |
| \(x^2+1\) | \(x\) | 2 | 2 | Quadratic |
| \(2y-5\) | \(y\) | 1 | 1 | Linear |
| \(5y^3+y^2+2y-1\) | \(y\) | 3 | 3 | Cubic |
| \(3z+7\) | \(z\) | 1 | 1 | Linear |
| \(8\) | any chosen variable | 0 | 0 | Constant |

### Degree

The degree of a polynomial is the highest power of its variable.

Examples:

| Polynomial | Degree | Reason |
|---|---:|---|
| \(5y^3+y^2+2y-1\) | 3 | Highest power is \(y^3\). |
| \(x^2+5x+1\) | 2 | Highest power is \(x^2\). |
| \(z+7\) | 1 | Highest power is \(z^1\). |
| \(8\) | 0 | Can be written as \(8x^0\). |

---

## Polynomial Classification

| Degree | Name | Example |
|---:|---|---|
| 0 | Constant polynomial | \(9\), \(-5\), \(12\) |
| 1 | Linear polynomial | \(2x+3\), \(4z-3\) |
| 2 | Quadratic polynomial | \(x^2+5x+1\) |
| 3 | Cubic polynomial | \(y^3+2y-1\) |

## 2.2 - Linear Polynomials

A linear polynomial is a polynomial of degree 1.

General form:

$$
ax+b
$$

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(x\) | Variable |
| \(a\) | Coefficient of \(x\), not equal to 0 |
| \(b\) | Constant |

Examples:

| Linear Polynomial | Variable | Coefficient | Constant |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| \(4x\) | \(x\) | 4 | 0 |
| \(200+50m\) | \(m\) | 50 | 200 |
| \(2x+10\) | \(x\) | 2 | 10 |
| \(3z+7\) | \(z\) | 3 | 7 |

---

## Example: Perimeter of a Square

If side of a square is \(x\), perimeter is:

$$
4x
$$

This is a linear polynomial.

### Table

| Side \(x\) in cm | Perimeter \(4x\) in cm |
|---:|---:|
| 1 | 4 |
| 1.5 | 6 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 2.5 | 10 |
| 3 | 12 |

If the side increases by 0.5 cm, the perimeter increases by:

$$
4 \times 0.5 = 2\ \text{cm}
$$

---

## Example: Chess Club Fee

A chess club charges:

- joining fee Rs 200;
- Rs 50 for every match played.

If \(m\) is the number of matches, amount paid is:

$$
200+50m
$$

### Table

| Matches \(m\) | Amount \(200+50m\) |
|---:|---:|
| 1 | 250 |
| 2 | 300 |
| 3 | 350 |
| 4 | 400 |
| 5 | 450 |

The amount increases by Rs 50 for every extra match.

If a player paid Rs 750:

$$
200+50m=750
$$

$$
50m=550
$$

$$
m=11
$$

So the player played 11 matches.

---

## Linear Equation from a Linear Polynomial

When a linear polynomial is equated to a constant, it becomes a linear equation.

Example:

Two numbers add to 64. One number is 10 more than the other.

Let smaller number be:

$$
x
$$

Larger number:

$$
x+10
$$

Equation:

$$
x+(x+10)=64
$$

$$
2x+10=64
$$

$$
2x=54
$$

$$
x=27
$$

Numbers are:

$$
27\quad \text{and}\quad 37
$$

## Polynomials as Input-Output Processes

Consider:

$$
2x+3
$$

If \(x=4\):

$$
2(4)+3=11
$$

If \(x=-6\):

$$
2(-6)+3=-9
$$

The input is \(x\). The output is the value of the expression.

This idea is called a **function**.

| Input \(x\) | Output \(2x+3\) |
|---:|---:|
| 4 | 11 |
| -6 | -9 |
| 0 | 3 |

### Linear vs Quadratic Function

| Expression | Type | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| \(2x+3\) | Linear | Highest power of \(x\) is 1. |
| \(10x-x^2\) | Quadratic | Highest power of \(x\) is 2. |

## 2.3 - Exploring Linear Patterns

A linear pattern has a constant difference between consecutive terms.

Example tile pattern:

| Stage | Number of Tiles |
|---:|---:|
| 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 3 |
| 3 | 5 |
| 4 | 7 |
| 5 | 9 |
| 6 | 11 |
| 7 | 13 |

Difference:

$$
3-1=2,\quad 5-3=2,\quad 7-5=2
$$

The difference is constant.

### Rule

Number of tiles at Stage \(n\):

$$
2n-1
$$

Check:

For Stage 5:

$$
2(5)-1=9
$$

For Stage 15:

$$
2(15)-1=29
$$

For Stage 26:

$$
2(26)-1=51
$$

### Finding the Stage from Number of Tiles

If there are 21 tiles:

$$
2n-1=21
$$

$$
2n=22
$$

$$
n=11
$$

If there are 47 tiles:

$$
2n-1=47
$$

$$
2n=48
$$

$$
n=24
$$

---

## Example: Bela's Pocket Money

Bela has Rs 100 and spends Rs 5 every day.

Amount left after \(n\) days:

$$
100-5n
$$

### Table

| Day \(n\) | Amount Left |
|---:|---:|
| 0 | 100 |
| 1 | 95 |
| 2 | 90 |
| 3 | 85 |
| 4 | 80 |

When amount left is Rs 40:

$$
100-5n=40
$$

$$
5n=60
$$

$$
n=12
$$

So she has Rs 40 left after 12 days.

On the 15th day:

$$
100-5(15)=25
$$

All money is spent when:

$$
100-5n=0
$$

$$
n=20
$$

---

## Example: Auto-Rickshaw Fare

Fare:

- Rs 25 for the first 2 km;
- Rs 15 per km after that.

For \(n \ge 2\), fare is:

$$
25+15(n-2)
$$

Simplify:

$$
25+15n-30=15n-5
$$

For 10 km:

$$
15(10)-5=145
$$

If fare is Rs 130:

$$
15n-5=130
$$

$$
15n=135
$$

$$
n=9
$$

So the distance is 9 km.

---

## Linear Pattern Checklist

A pattern is linear if:

1. equal input steps give equal output changes;
2. the nth term can be written in the form \(an+b\);
3. the graph is a straight line.

## 2.4 - Linear Growth and Linear Decay

### Linear Growth

Linear growth means a quantity increases by a fixed amount over equal intervals.

General form:

$$
\text{quantity} = \text{starting value} + (\text{rate})(\text{time})
$$

Example:

Cost of journey:

$$
C(d)=100+60d
$$

Here:

- starting cost is Rs 100;
- cost increases by Rs 60 per km.

For 15 km:

$$
C(15)=100+60(15)=1000
$$

For cost Rs 700:

$$
100+60d=700
$$

$$
60d=600
$$

$$
d=10
$$

---

## Linear Decay

Linear decay means a quantity decreases by a fixed amount over equal intervals.

General form:

$$
\text{quantity} = \text{starting value} - (\text{rate})(\text{time})
$$

Example:

Water height in a tank:

$$
h(t)=3-0.5t
$$

Here:

- starting height is 3 m;
- height decreases by 0.5 m each month.

After 5 months:

$$
h(5)=3-0.5(5)=0.5
$$

Height is 0.5 m.

## 2.5 - Linear Relationships

A linear relationship between two variables \(x\) and \(y\) can be written as:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| \(x\) | Input variable |
| \(y\) | Output variable |
| \(a\) | Rate of change / slope |
| \(b\) | Starting value / y-intercept |

---

## Finding \(a\) and \(b\) from Two Data Points

Suppose a telecom bill follows:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

Given:

| Data Used \(x\) | Bill \(y\) |
|---:|---:|
| 10 GB | 350 |
| 20 GB | 550 |

Substitute:

$$
350=10a+b
$$

$$
550=20a+b
$$

Subtract first equation from second:

$$
200=10a
$$

$$
a=20
$$

Now substitute into:

$$
350=10a+b
$$

$$
350=10(20)+b
$$

$$
b=150
$$

So:

$$
y=20x+150
$$

### Meaning

| Number | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 20 | Cost per GB |
| 150 | Fixed monthly fee |

## 2.6 - Visualising Linear Relationships

Linear relationships of the form:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

are represented by straight lines on a coordinate plane.

To draw a line, two points are enough.

---

## Graphing \(y=2x+1\)

Choose two x-values.

| \(x\) | \(y=2x+1\) | Point |
|---:|---:|---|
| 0 | 1 | \((0,1)\) |
| 3 | 7 | \((3,7)\) |

Plot \((0,1)\) and \((3,7)\), then join them with a straight line.

Other points on the same line:

| \(x\) | \(y=2x+1\) |
|---:|---:|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 5 |
| 5 | 11 |
| 7 | 15 |
| 9 | 19 |
| 12 | 25 |
| 20 | 41 |

If a point lies on the line, its coordinates satisfy the equation.

Example:

For \((7,15)\):

$$
15=2(7)+1
$$

$$
15=15
$$

So \((7,15)\) lies on the line.

---

## Graphs Through the Origin

Equations of the form:

$$
y=ax
$$

always pass through the origin.

Reason:

If \(x=0\):

$$
y=a(0)=0
$$

So:

$$
(0,0)
$$

lies on the line.

### Examples

| Equation | Sample Points |
|---|---|
| \(y=3x\) | \((-1,-3),(0,0),(1,3),(3,9)\) |
| \(y=-2x\) | \((-2,4),(0,0),(2,-4)\) |
| \(y=x\) | \((-1,-1),(0,0),(2,2)\) |
| \(y=\frac{1}{2}x\) | \((0,0),(4,2)\) |

---

## Slope

In:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

the number \(a\) is called the slope.

It tells:

- whether the line rises or falls;
- how steep the line is;
- how much \(y\) changes when \(x\) increases by 1.

### Slope Sign

| Slope \(a\) | Graph Behavior | Pattern Type |
|---:|---|---|
| \(a>0\) | Line rises left to right | Linear growth |
| \(a<0\) | Line falls left to right | Linear decay |
| \(a=0\) | Horizontal line | Constant value |

### Comparing \(y=\frac{1}{2}x\), \(y=x\), \(y=2x\)

| Equation | Slope | Steepness |
|---|---:|---|
| \(y=\frac{1}{2}x\) | \(\frac{1}{2}\) | Less steep than \(y=x\) |
| \(y=x\) | 1 | Reference line |
| \(y=2x\) | 2 | Steeper than \(y=x\) |

---

## y-Intercept

In:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

the number \(b\) is called the y-intercept.

The line cuts the y-axis at:

$$
(0,b)
$$

Examples:

| Equation | y-intercept | Point on y-axis |
|---|---:|---|
| \(y=x+3\) | 3 | \((0,3)\) |
| \(y=2x+5\) | 5 | \((0,5)\) |
| \(y=3x-2\) | -2 | \((0,-2)\) |

---

## Parallel Lines

Lines with the same slope but different y-intercepts are parallel.

Example:

$$
y=2x-1
$$

$$
y=2x+1
$$

$$
y=2x+5
$$

All have slope 2, so they are parallel.

Their y-intercepts are different:

| Equation | Slope | y-intercept |
|---|---:|---:|
| \(y=2x-1\) | 2 | -1 |
| \(y=2x+1\) | 2 | 1 |
| \(y=2x+5\) | 2 | 5 |

## Formula and Rule Sheet

### Polynomial Degree

$$
\text{Degree} = \text{highest power of the variable}
$$

### Linear Polynomial

$$
ax+b,\quad a\ne 0
$$

### Linear Equation in Two Variables

$$
y=ax+b
$$

### Linear Growth

$$
\text{Quantity} = \text{initial value} + \text{constant increase}\times\text{time}
$$

### Linear Decay

$$
\text{Quantity} = \text{initial value} - \text{constant decrease}\times\text{time}
$$

### y-Intercept

For:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

the y-intercept is:

$$
b
$$

and the line cuts the y-axis at:

$$
(0,b)
$$

### Slope

For:

$$
y=ax+b
$$

the slope is:

$$
a
$$

## Graphing Toolkit

### To Graph \(y=ax+b\)

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Choose two convenient x-values. |
| **2** | Substitute each x-value to find y. |
| **3** | Write two points. |
| **4** | Plot the points on graph paper. |
| **5** | Join them with a ruler and extend the line. |

### Quick Choice of Points

Choose \(x=0\) first because:

$$
y=a(0)+b=b
$$

So \((0,b)\) is always easy to find.

Then choose another easy \(x\), such as 1, 2, or a value that avoids fractions.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Calling every algebraic expression a polynomial. | A polynomial in this chapter has one variable with non-negative integer powers. |
| Forgetting missing terms have coefficient 0. | If no \(z\)-term appears, coefficient of \(z\) is 0. |
| Saying constant polynomial has degree 1. | A non-zero constant polynomial has degree 0. |
| Treating \(10x-x^2\) as linear because it has \(x\). | It is quadratic because highest power is 2. |
| Confusing coefficient and constant. | Coefficient multiplies variable; constant has no variable. |
| Thinking linear growth must start at 0. | It can start at any value, such as \(100+60d\). |
| Forgetting a fixed fee in \(y=ax+b\). | Fixed fee is the \(b\) term. |
| Graphing a line using only one point. | Two points are needed to draw a unique line. |
| Confusing slope and y-intercept. | Slope is \(a\), y-intercept is \(b\). |
| Thinking same y-intercept means parallel. | Same slope means parallel. |

## One-Page Summary

An algebraic expression combines numbers, variables, and operations. Its parts are called terms. The number multiplying a variable is a coefficient, and a term without a variable is a constant.

A one-variable polynomial is an algebraic expression in one variable and its non-negative integer powers. The highest power of the variable is called the degree. Degree 0 polynomials are constant polynomials, degree 1 polynomials are linear polynomials, degree 2 polynomials are quadratic polynomials, and degree 3 polynomials are cubic polynomials.

A linear polynomial has the form \(ax+b\), where \(a\ne0\). Linear polynomials model situations where a quantity changes by a fixed amount. Such patterns are linear patterns. Linear growth means a quantity increases by a constant amount over equal intervals, while linear decay means it decreases by a constant amount.

A linear relationship between two variables can be written as \(y=ax+b\). Its graph is a straight line. The number \(a\) is the slope and shows the rate of change. The number \(b\) is the y-intercept, and the line cuts the y-axis at \((0,b)\). Lines with the same slope but different y-intercepts are parallel.
