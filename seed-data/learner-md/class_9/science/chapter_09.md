## 🌍 Introduction — From Atoms to Compounds

In Chapter 8, you studied atoms, electrons, protons, neutrons, shells, valency, and stability.

This chapter asks how atoms combine to form substances.

Example:

| Substance | Properties |
|---|---|
| Hydrogen gas | Combustible |
| Oxygen gas | Supports combustion |
| Water | Liquid at ordinary temperature; extinguishes fire |

Hydrogen and oxygen form water, but water does not behave like either hydrogen or oxygen. This shows that compounds have properties different from their elements.

However, when water forms, the mass of water equals the sum of masses of hydrogen and oxygen that combined.

## 9.1 — Law of Conservation of Mass

### Physical Change: Salt Dissolving in Water

In Activity 9.1, salt is added to water and dissolved. The total mass of the solution is equal to the sum of masses of water and salt.

| Before Dissolving | After Dissolving |
|---|---|
| Water + salt | Salt solution |
| Same total mass | Same total mass |

This shows mass does not change during a physical change.

### Chemical Change: Baking Soda and Vinegar

Baking soda reacts with vinegar to form carbon dioxide and other substances.

In an open setup, carbon dioxide gas escapes, so final measured mass may appear less.

In a closed setup with a balloon fixed to the flask, carbon dioxide is trapped, and the total mass remains the same.

| Setup | Observation | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Open system | Final mass may seem lower | Gas escapes |
| Closed system | Final mass equals initial mass | All products remain in system |

> [!NOTE]
> A **closed system** does not allow matter to enter or escape during the experiment.

### Law Statement

> [!NOTE]
> **Law of Conservation of Mass:** Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

This law was proposed by **Antoine Lavoisier** in 1789.

### Activity 9.3 — Sodium Sulfate and Barium Chloride

When sodium sulfate solution is mixed with barium chloride solution, a white precipitate of barium sulfate forms.

Word equation:

```text
Sodium sulfate + Barium chloride -> Barium sulfate + Sodium chloride
```

If all reactants and products are weighed together, total mass remains unchanged.

### Meet a Scientist — Antoine Lavoisier

Antoine Lavoisier is called the Father of Modern Chemistry. He proposed the law of conservation of mass and carefully studied chemical reactions quantitatively.

---

### 📐 Example 9.1 — Verifying Conservation of Mass

Reactants:

| Reactant | Mass |
|---|---:|
| Calcium carbonate | 4.0 g |
| Hydrochloric acid | 2.92 g |

Total mass of reactants:

$$
4.0+2.92
=
6.92\text{ g}
$$

Products:

| Product | Mass |
|---|---:|
| Carbon dioxide | 1.76 g |
| Water | 0.72 g |
| Calcium chloride | 4.44 g |

Total mass of products:

$$
1.76+0.72+4.44
=
6.92\text{ g}
$$

Since:

$$
\text{mass of reactants}
=
\text{mass of products}
$$

the law of conservation of mass is obeyed.

### 📐 Example 9.2 — Carbon Dioxide Formation

Given:

```text
12 g carbon + 32 g oxygen -> 44 g carbon dioxide
```

If 2.4 g carbon reacts completely:

$$
\text{carbon dioxide formed}
=
\frac{44}{12}
\times
2.4
=
8.8\text{ g}
$$

## 9.2 — Law of Constant Proportions

Water from rivers, borewells, oceans, or laboratories has the same chemical composition after purification.

In water:

| Element | Mass Ratio |
|---|---:|
| Hydrogen | 1 |
| Oxygen | 8 |

So 9 g water always contains 1 g hydrogen and 8 g oxygen.

> [!NOTE]
> **Law of Constant Proportions:** In a chemical compound, elements are always present in a fixed ratio by mass, irrespective of the source or method of preparation.

This is also called:

| Name |
|---|
| Law of Definite Proportions |
| Proust's Law |

### Cinnabar Example

Cinnabar contains mercury and sulfur in nearly fixed mass percentages:

| Element | Approximate Mass Percentage |
|---|---:|
| Mercury | 86.22% |
| Sulfur | 13.78% |

### 📐 Example 9.3 — Sodium Chloride Ratio

Sodium chloride contains sodium and chlorine in mass ratio:

$$
23:35.5
$$

If 46 g sodium reacts completely:

$$
\text{chlorine needed}
=
\frac{35.5}{23}
\times
46
=
71\text{ g}
$$

### Law Applies to Compounds, Not Mixtures

| Compound | Mixture |
|---|---|
| Fixed composition | Variable composition |
| Elements chemically combined | Components physically mixed |
| Example: water is always H:O = 1:8 by mass | Salt water may have different amounts of salt |

### Meet a Scientist — Joseph Proust

Joseph Louis Proust showed through careful experiments that compounds always contain elements in fixed mass proportions.

## 9.3 — Dalton's Atomic Theory

The laws of conservation of mass and constant proportions helped Dalton propose his atomic theory.

### Dalton's Postulates

| Postulate | Meaning |
|---|---|
| All matter is made of atoms | Atoms participate in chemical reactions |
| Atoms are indivisible in chemical reactions | They are not created or destroyed in ordinary chemical changes |
| Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and chemical properties | Same element has same kind of atoms, according to Dalton |
| Atoms of different elements differ | Different elements have different atoms |
| Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios | Explains formulae like H₂O and CO₂ |
| Relative number and kinds of atoms are constant in a compound | Explains law of constant proportions |

### How Dalton Explains the Laws

| Law | Dalton's Explanation |
|---|---|
| Conservation of mass | Atoms are not created or destroyed, only rearranged |
| Constant proportions | A compound has fixed kinds and numbers of atoms |

> [!NOTE]
> Later discoveries showed atoms are divisible into subatomic particles and isotopes exist, but Dalton's theory remains historically important.

### Meet a Scientist — John Dalton

John Dalton was an English scientist who presented his atomic theory in 1808. It became a turning point in the study of matter.

## 9.4 — How Atoms Combine?

Atoms combine to become more stable, usually by achieving a complete outer shell.

> [!NOTE]
> A **molecule** is an electrically neutral entity made of more than one atom, capable of independent existence, and showing the chemical properties of that substance.

Examples:

| Molecule Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Molecule of an element | `H₂`, `O₂`, `Cl₂`, `N₂` |
| Molecule of a compound | `HCl`, `H₂O`, `NH₃`, `CO₂` |

> [!NOTE]
> A **chemical bond** is the force that holds atoms together in a molecule or compound.

Atoms form bonds mainly by:

| Method | Bond Type |
|---|---|
| Sharing electrons | Covalent bond |
| Transferring electrons | Ionic bond |

---

## 9.4.1 — Bonding by Sharing of Electrons: Covalent Bond

> [!NOTE]
> A **covalent bond** is formed when atoms share one or more pairs of electrons.

### Hydrogen Molecule, H₂

Each hydrogen atom has one electron in the K-shell and needs one more electron to complete its duplet.

Two hydrogen atoms share one electron each.

```text
H + H -> H-H
```

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Shared electron pair | Holds both hydrogen atoms together |
| Bond type | Single covalent bond |
| Formula | `H₂` |

### Chlorine Molecule, Cl₂

Chlorine has 7 valence electrons and needs 1 more to complete its octet.

```text
Cl + Cl -> Cl-Cl
```

Two chlorine atoms share one pair of electrons, forming a single bond.

### Oxygen Molecule, O₂

Oxygen has 6 valence electrons and needs 2 more electrons.

Two oxygen atoms share two pairs of electrons.

```text
O + O -> O=O
```

| Shared Pairs | Bond |
|---:|---|
| 1 | Single bond |
| 2 | Double bond |
| 3 | Triple bond |

### Hydrogen Chloride, HCl

Hydrogen needs 1 electron to complete duplet. Chlorine needs 1 electron to complete octet.

They share one pair of electrons:

```text
H-Cl
```

### Water, H₂O

Oxygen needs 2 electrons; each hydrogen can share 1 electron.

So oxygen shares with two hydrogen atoms:

```text
H-O-H
```

Formula:

```text
H2O
```

### Common Covalent Molecule Patterns

| Molecule | Electron Sharing | Bond Representation |
|---|---|---|
| `H₂` | One shared pair | `H-H` |
| `Cl₂` | One shared pair | `Cl-Cl` |
| `O₂` | Two shared pairs | `O=O` |
| `N₂` | Three shared pairs | `N≡N` |
| `HCl` | One shared pair | `H-Cl` |
| `H₂O` | Two O-H single bonds | `H-O-H` |
| `NH₃` | Three N-H single bonds | `NH₃` |
| `CO₂` | Two C=O double bonds | `O=C=O` |

## Naming Covalent Compounds

For many binary covalent compounds, names use prefixes.

| Number | Prefix |
|---:|---|
| 1 | mono- |
| 2 | di- |
| 3 | tri- |
| 4 | tetra- |
| 5 | penta- |
| 6 | hexa- |

Rules:

| Rule | Example |
|---|---|
| First element keeps regular name | carbon, sulfur, phosphorus |
| Second element usually ends in `-ide` | oxide, chloride, sulfide |
| `mono-` is usually omitted for first element | `CO₂` is carbon dioxide |
| `mono-` is used for second element when needed | `CO` is carbon monoxide |
| Drop final vowel when needed | monoxide, pentoxide |

Examples:

| Formula | Name |
|---|---|
| `CO` | Carbon monoxide |
| `CO₂` | Carbon dioxide |
| `CS₂` | Carbon disulfide |
| `PCl₃` | Phosphorus trichloride |
| `SF₆` | Sulfur hexafluoride |
| `N₂O₄` | Dinitrogen tetroxide |
| `N₂O₅` | Dinitrogen pentoxide |
| `H₂S` | Hydrogen sulfide |
| `H₂O` | Water, common name |
| `NH₃` | Ammonia, common name |

## 9.4.2 — Bonding by Electron Transfer: Ionic Bond

Atoms with few valence electrons often lose electrons. Atoms with nearly complete valence shells often gain electrons.

> [!NOTE]
> An **ionic bond** is the electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by transfer of electrons.

### Cations and Anions

| Ion Type | Formation | Charge |
|---|---|---|
| **Cation** | Atom loses electron(s) | Positive |
| **Anion** | Atom gains electron(s) | Negative |

### Sodium Chloride Formation

Sodium has atomic number 11 and electronic configuration:

```text
2, 8, 1
```

It loses one electron:

```text
Na -> Na+ + e-
```

Now sodium has 11 protons and 10 electrons, so charge is +1.

Chlorine has atomic number 17 and electronic configuration:

```text
2, 8, 7
```

It gains one electron:

```text
Cl + e- -> Cl-
```

Now chloride has 17 protons and 18 electrons, so charge is −1.

Opposite ions attract:

```text
Na+ + Cl- -> NaCl
```

### Ionic Crystal Structure

Ionic compounds do not usually exist as separate molecules. They form large three-dimensional crystal structures.

In sodium chloride:

| Ion | Surrounding Ions |
|---|---|
| Each `Na⁺` | Surrounded by six `Cl⁻` ions |
| Each `Cl⁻` | Surrounded by six `Na⁺` ions |

> [!NOTE]
> A **crystal lattice** is a regular repeating three-dimensional arrangement of ions.

### Common Ions

#### Monoatomic Cations

| Ion | Formula | Valency |
|---|---|---:|
| Sodium | Na⁺ | 1 |
| Lithium | Li⁺ | 1 |
| Potassium | K⁺ | 1 |
| Silver | Ag⁺ | 1 |
| Calcium | Ca²⁺ | 2 |
| Barium | Ba²⁺ | 2 |
| Magnesium | Mg²⁺ | 2 |
| Zinc | Zn²⁺ | 2 |
| Aluminium | Al³⁺ | 3 |
| Ferrous | Fe²⁺ | 2 |
| Ferric | Fe³⁺ | 3 |
| Cuprous | Cu⁺ | 1 |
| Cupric | Cu²⁺ | 2 |

#### Monoatomic Anions

| Ion | Formula | Valency |
|---|---|---:|
| Fluoride | F⁻ | 1 |
| Chloride | Cl⁻ | 1 |
| Bromide | Br⁻ | 1 |
| Iodide | I⁻ | 1 |
| Oxide | O²⁻ | 2 |
| Sulfide | S²⁻ | 2 |

#### Polyatomic Ions

| Ion | Formula | Valency |
|---|---|---:|
| Hydroxide | OH⁻ | 1 |
| Nitrate | NO₃⁻ | 1 |
| Hydrogencarbonate | HCO₃⁻ | 1 |
| Carbonate | CO₃²⁻ | 2 |
| Sulfate | SO₄²⁻ | 2 |
| Ammonium | NH₄⁺ | 1 |

> [!NOTE]
> **Polyatomic ions** are ions made of two or more atoms bonded together and carrying an overall charge.

## 9.5 — Writing Chemical Formulae

Chemical formulae show the kinds and numbers or ratios of atoms or ions in a compound.

---

## 9.5.1 — Writing Formulae of Covalent Compounds

### Steps

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Write symbols of elements. |
| **2** | Write their valencies. |
| **3** | Criss-cross valencies as subscripts. |
| **4** | Do not write subscript 1. |

Examples:

| Compound | Valencies | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen chloride | H = 1, Cl = 1 | `HCl` |
| Hydrogen sulfide | H = 1, S = 2 | `H₂S` |
| Carbon tetrachloride | C = 4, Cl = 1 | `CCl₄` |
| Sulfur dioxide | S = 4, O = 2; criss-cross reduces to 1:2 | `SO₂` |

> [!TIP]
> For common covalent compounds, the molecular formula is often learned from bonding and naming, not only from criss-crossing.

---

## 9.5.2 — Writing Formulae of Ionic Compounds

### Steps

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Write cation first, then anion. |
| **2** | Write charges or valencies under symbols. |
| **3** | Criss-cross only the numbers, not signs. |
| **4** | Reduce to simplest whole-number ratio if needed. |
| **5** | Use brackets around polyatomic ions when more than one is required. |

### Examples

| Compound | Ions | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium chloride | Ca²⁺, Cl⁻ | `CaCl₂` |
| Aluminium oxide | Al³⁺, O²⁻ | `Al₂O₃` |
| Magnesium oxide | Mg²⁺, O²⁻ | `MgO` |
| Magnesium hydroxide | Mg²⁺, OH⁻ | `Mg(OH)₂` |
| Calcium carbonate | Ca²⁺, CO₃²⁻ | `CaCO₃` |
| Aluminium hydroxide | Al³⁺, OH⁻ | `Al(OH)₃` |
| Aluminium sulfate | Al³⁺, SO₄²⁻ | `Al₂(SO₄)₃` |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Write `Al(OH)₃`, not `AlOH₃`. The bracket shows there are three hydroxide ions.

### Charge Neutrality

Every ionic compound is electrically neutral overall.

Example:

| Formula | Charge Balance |
|---|---|
| `CaCl₂` | `Ca²⁺` balances two `Cl⁻` |
| `Al₂O₃` | two `Al³⁺` give +6; three `O²⁻` give −6 |
| `Mg(OH)₂` | `Mg²⁺` balances two `OH⁻` |

## 9.6 — Properties of Ionic and Covalent Compounds

Activity 9.4 compares compounds such as camphor, sodium chloride, copper sulfate, sugar, and naphthalene.

### General Comparison

| Property | Ionic Compounds | Covalent Compounds |
|---|---|---|
| Usual formation | Metal + non-metal | Non-metal + non-metal |
| Bonding | Electron transfer | Electron sharing |
| Particles | Ions in crystal lattice | Molecules |
| Solubility in water | Often soluble | Often insoluble, but exceptions like sugar |
| Solubility in kerosene/petrol | Usually insoluble | Many dissolve |
| Conductivity in solid state | Do not conduct | Do not conduct |
| Conductivity in aqueous solution | Conduct if soluble | Usually do not conduct |
| Conductivity in molten state | Conduct | Usually do not conduct |
| Melting/boiling points | Generally high | Generally low |

### Why Ionic Compounds Conduct in Solution

In solid ionic compounds, ions are fixed in place. They cannot move freely, so the solid does not conduct electricity.

When dissolved in water or melted, ions become mobile. Moving ions carry charge, so the substance conducts electricity.

### Why Sugar Solution Does Not Conduct

Sugar is covalent. It dissolves in water but does not form ions. Therefore, sugar solution does not conduct electricity.

## 9.7 — Molecular Mass of Covalent Compounds

> [!NOTE]
> **Molecular mass** is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms present in one molecule.

### Example 9.4 — Water, H₂O

Atomic masses:

| Atom | Atomic Mass |
|---|---:|
| H | 1 u |
| O | 16 u |

$$
\text{Molecular mass of H}_2\text{O}
=
(1\times2)
+
(16\times1)
=
18\text{ u}
$$

### Example 9.5 — Carbon Dioxide, CO₂

Atomic masses:

| Atom | Atomic Mass |
|---|---:|
| C | 12 u |
| O | 16 u |

$$
\text{Molecular mass of CO}_2
=
(12\times1)
+
(16\times2)
=
44\text{ u}
$$

### Practice

Nitric acid, `HNO₃`:

$$
\text{Molecular mass}
=
1
+
14
+
(16\times3)
=
63\text{ u}
$$

Methane, `CH₄`:

$$
\text{Molecular mass}
=
12
+
(1\times4)
=
16\text{ u}
$$

## 9.8 — Formula Unit Mass of Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds do not form separate molecules. Their formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio of ions.

> [!NOTE]
> A **formula unit** is the simplest whole-number ratio of ions in an ionic compound.

> [!NOTE]
> **Formula unit mass** is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms present in one formula unit.

### Example 9.6 — Sodium Oxide, Na₂O

Atomic masses:

| Atom | Atomic Mass |
|---|---:|
| Na | 23 u |
| O | 16 u |

$$
\text{Formula unit mass of Na}_2\text{O}
=
(23\times2)
+
(16\times1)
=
62\text{ u}
$$

### Example 9.7 — Calcium Nitrate, Ca(NO₃)₂

Atomic masses:

| Atom | Atomic Mass |
|---|---:|
| Ca | 40 u |
| N | 14 u |
| O | 16 u |

$$
\text{Formula unit mass of Ca(NO}_3)_2
=
(40\times1)
+
\{(14\times1)+(16\times3)\}\times2
$$

$$
=
40
+
(14+48)\times2
=
40+124
=
164\text{ u}
$$

### Practice

Potassium chloride, `KCl`:

$$
39+35.5
=
74.5\text{ u}
$$

Magnesium hydroxide, `Mg(OH)₂`:

$$
24
+
\{16+1\}\times2
=
24+34
=
58\text{ u}
$$

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **Mass disappears when gas escapes.** | Mass is conserved, but escaped gas is not measured in an open system. |
| **Compounds can have any ratio of elements.** | Compounds have fixed mass ratios. |
| **Dalton's theory is fully correct today.** | It was foundational, but later discoveries modified it. |
| **All substances made of atoms are molecules.** | Ionic compounds form crystal lattices, not molecules. |
| **Ionic compounds conduct electricity in solid state.** | They conduct only when ions are mobile, such as molten or dissolved. |
| **Sugar solution conducts because sugar dissolves.** | Dissolving is not enough; mobile ions are needed. |
| **Criss-cross method means final formula can keep common factors.** | Ionic formulae must be simplest whole-number ratios. |
| **Brackets are optional for polyatomic ions.** | Brackets are required when more than one polyatomic ion is present. |

### The Quest Continues

In ordinary chemical reactions, mass is conserved because atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed. In nuclear reactions, mass and energy are connected more deeply. You will study this more advanced idea in higher classes.
