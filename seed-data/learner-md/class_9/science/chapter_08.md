## 🌍 Introduction — Why Journey Inside the Atom?

Everything around us is made of matter, and matter is made of atoms.

Living beings, bricks, houses, proteins, cells, silicates, organs, and walls are all ultimately built from atoms. But atoms are too small to see with the naked eye.

This chapter asks:

| Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Are atoms truly indivisible? | Explains whether matter has deeper structure |
| What are atoms made of? | Leads to electron, proton, neutron, nucleus |
| How are atomic parts arranged? | Explains models of atom |
| Why are atoms stable? | Leads to Bohr's shells |
| Why are atoms of different elements different? | Leads to atomic number |

> [!NOTE]
> An **atom** is the smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical identity of that element, but it is not indivisible.

### Core Inquiry Questions

|---|---|
| **1** | Are atoms the smallest indivisible particles? |

## 8.1 — Rediscovering the Roots of Atomic Theory

The idea that matter is made of tiny particles is very old.

### Ancient Indian View — Acharya Kanada

Acharya Kanada suggested that if matter is divided repeatedly, a stage is reached where particles cannot be divided further. He called these particles **parmanus**.

| Idea | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Dravya** | Matter |
| **Parmanu** | Infinitely small particle, not perceived by senses |
| **Dyad** | Combination of two parmanus |
| **Triad** | Combination of three parmanus |

These ideas were recorded in the **Vaisesika Sutras**.

### Ancient Greek View — Leucippus and Democritus

Greek philosophers Leucippus and Democritus proposed indivisible particles called **atomos**, meaning indivisible.

### Dalton's Atomic Theory

In 1808, **John Dalton** proposed a scientific atomic theory based on experimental evidence available at that time.

Dalton proposed:

| Dalton's Idea | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Matter is made of atoms | Atoms are basic building blocks |
| Atoms are indivisible | Atoms cannot be broken into smaller parts |
| Atoms of one element are alike | Same element has same kind of atoms |
| Atoms of different elements differ | Different elements have different atoms |

Dalton's theory was not fully correct, but it became the starting point for modern atomic structure.

> [!TIP]
> Ancient atomic ideas were mostly philosophical. Dalton's atomic theory was scientific because it was linked to experiments and chemical laws.

## 8.2 — A Short Historical Journey Through Atomic Models

Scientific models change when new evidence appears.

| Model | Main Idea | Why It Changed |
|---|---|---|
| **Dalton** | Atom is indivisible solid particle | Subatomic particles were discovered |
| **Thomson** | Electrons embedded in positive sphere | Gold foil experiment showed central nucleus |
| **Rutherford** | Dense positive nucleus with electrons orbiting | Could not explain atomic stability |
| **Bohr** | Electrons in fixed energy levels | Later replaced by quantum mechanical model |
| **Modern model** | Electron clouds and probability regions | More accurate for atomic behaviour |

> [!NOTE]
> A scientific model is useful when it explains observations. It is modified when new evidence cannot be explained by the old model.

## 8.2.1 — Thomson's Model of an Atom

In 1897, **J. J. Thomson** studied electric current through gases at very low pressure using a cathode ray tube.

### Cathode Ray Observations

| Observation | Inference |
|---|---|
| Rays moved from cathode to anode | Rays carried negative charge |
| Rays were affected by electric and magnetic fields | They were made of charged particles |
| Nature of rays did not depend on cathode material or gas | These particles are present in all atoms |

These negatively charged particles were later called **electrons**.

> [!NOTE]
> The electron was the first subatomic particle to be identified.

The charge of an electron is:

$$
-1.602\times10^{-19}\text{ C}
$$

For convenience, its relative charge is taken as:

$$
-1
$$

### Thomson's Problem

Atoms are electrically neutral. If atoms contain negative electrons, they must also contain positive charge.

### Plum Pudding Model

Thomson proposed:

| Part of Atom | Thomson's Idea |
|---|---|
| Positive charge | Spread throughout atom as a sphere |
| Electrons | Embedded throughout the positive sphere |
| Overall atom | Neutral because positive and negative charges balance |

This model is called the **plum pudding model**.

Indian classroom analogy:

| Analogy | Represents |
|---|---|
| Watermelon red pulp | Positive charge spread throughout |
| Watermelon seeds | Electrons embedded in positive matter |

### Why Thomson's Model Was Important

| Importance |
|---|
| It included electrons in atomic structure |
| It explained electrical neutrality |
| It was the first real internal model of atom |
| It was later tested and replaced by stronger evidence |

### Meet a Scientist — J. J. Thomson

J. J. Thomson discovered the electron and received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1906 for work on electrical conductivity of gases.

## 8.2.2 — Testing Thomson's Model: The Gold Foil Experiment

In 1911, **Geiger and Marsden**, working under **Ernest Rutherford**, tested Thomson's model.

They fired a narrow beam of alpha particles at a very thin gold foil.

> [!NOTE]
> An **alpha particle** is a positively charged particle. It is actually the nucleus of a helium atom, containing two protons and two neutrons.

### Expected Result from Thomson's Model

If positive charge were spread throughout the atom, alpha particles should:

| Expected Observation |
|---|
| Mostly pass straight through |
| Show only small deflections |
| Not bounce back sharply |

### Actual Observations

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Most alpha particles passed through undeflected | Most of atom is empty space |
| Some alpha particles deflected slightly | Positive charge affects their path |
| A few alpha particles deflected sharply or bounced back | Positive charge and mass are concentrated in a tiny dense nucleus |

This deflection is called **scattering**. Therefore, the experiment is also called the **alpha-particle scattering experiment**.

---

## Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Rutherford concluded:

| Conclusion | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Most of atom is empty space | Most alpha particles passed through |
| Positive charge is concentrated in nucleus | Some particles deflected strongly |
| Nucleus is small and dense | Only a few particles hit or came close |
| Most mass lies in nucleus | Strong deflection required concentrated mass |
| Electrons revolve around nucleus | Atom has negative electrons outside positive nucleus |

This model is called the **planetary model** because electrons were imagined to orbit the nucleus like planets around the Sun.

### Size Comparison

Rutherford estimated:

| Structure | Approximate Diameter |
|---|---:|
| Atom | `10⁻¹⁰ m` |
| Nucleus | `10⁻¹⁵ m` |

The nucleus is about:

$$
\frac{10^{-10}}{10^{-15}}
=
10^5
$$

That means the atom is about **one lakh times wider than its nucleus**.

If an atom were the size of a cricket ground, the nucleus would be like a tiny black pepper grain at the centre.

### Atoms in a Sheet of Paper

If paper thickness is `0.1 mm = 10⁻⁴ m`, and atom diameter is about `10⁻¹⁰ m`:

$$
\text{Number of atoms}
\approx
\frac{10^{-4}}{10^{-10}}
=
10^6
$$

About **one million atoms** stacked together make that thickness.

---

## Limitation of Rutherford's Model

Rutherford's model could not explain atomic stability.

In circular motion, an electron changes direction continuously. That means it accelerates. According to classical ideas, an accelerating charged particle should lose energy.

If electrons lost energy while orbiting:

| Step | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Electron loses energy | Orbit becomes smaller |
| Orbit shrinks | Electron spirals inward |
| Electron reaches nucleus | Atom collapses |

But atoms do not collapse. Matter is stable. Therefore, Rutherford's model was incomplete.

### Discovery of the Proton

Rutherford showed that the nucleus carries positive charge due to **protons**.

| Particle | Charge | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Proton | +1 | Much heavier than electron |
| Electron | −1 | Very light |

For a neutral atom:

$$
\text{number of protons}
=
\text{number of electrons}
$$

Examples:

| Atom | Protons | Electrons | Neutral? |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Helium | 2 | 2 | Yes |
| Sodium | 11 | 11 | Yes |

### Meet a Scientist — Ernest Rutherford

Ernest Rutherford discovered the atomic nucleus and is known as the Father of Nuclear Physics. He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1908 for work on radioactive elements.

## 8.2.3 — Bohr's Model of the Atom

In 1913, **Niels Bohr** proposed a model that explained atomic stability better.

### Bohr's Main Ideas

| Bohr's Idea | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Electrons move in fixed circular paths | These paths are called shells, orbits, stationary states, or energy levels |
| Electrons do not lose energy in fixed shells | This explains atomic stability |
| Electrons cannot exist between shells | Only allowed energy levels are possible |
| Shells have definite energies | Energy increases farther from nucleus |
| Electron can jump shells | It must absorb or release fixed energy equal to energy difference |

### Shell Names

| Shell | Number |
|---|---:|
| K | `n = 1` |
| L | `n = 2` |
| M | `n = 3` |
| N | `n = 4` |

Energy order:

$$
K<L<M<N
$$

The K-shell is closest to the nucleus and has the least energy.

### Why K, L, M, N?

The names came from early X-ray experiments by Charles Barkla. He named the first observed X-ray line K, leaving space for possible earlier series. Bohr adopted this notation for shells.

### Meet a Scientist — Niels Bohr

Niels Bohr was a Danish physicist. He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1922 for his work on atomic structure.

## 8.3 — What Components Contribute to the Mass of an Atom?

Rutherford's model showed that most atomic mass is in the nucleus. Electrons are very light, so their mass is usually ignored in simple atomic mass calculations.

But a puzzle remained.

| Atom | Protons | Expected Mass from Protons Only | Actual Pattern |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Hydrogen | 1 | About 1 unit | Fits |
| Helium | 2 | About 2 units | Mass is about 4 units |

This suggested that something else existed in the nucleus: mass without charge.

## 8.3.1 — Discovery of the Neutron

In 1932, **James Chadwick** discovered the **neutron**.

> [!NOTE]
> A **neutron** is a subatomic particle with no electrical charge and mass nearly equal to that of a proton.

Neutrons are found in the nucleus of all atoms except the most common hydrogen atom.

### Subatomic Particles

| S. No. | Particle | Symbol | Relative Charge | Location | Relative Mass |
|---|---|---|---:|---|---:|
| **1** | Electron | `e⁻` | −1 | Outside nucleus | Very small |
| **2** | Proton | `p⁺` | +1 | Nucleus | About 1 |
| **3** | Neutron | `n⁰` | 0 | Nucleus | About 1 |

### Why Heavy Nuclei Need More Neutrons

Protons repel each other because they all have positive charge. Neutrons help stabilize the nucleus by contributing to nuclear binding and spacing between protons.

Examples:

| Element | Protons | Neutrons |
|---|---:|---:|
| Carbon | 6 | 6 |
| Oxygen | 8 | 8 |
| Iron | 26 | 30 |
| Uranium | 92 | 146 |

### India’s Scientific Contributions — BARC

The **Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC)** in Mumbai uses neutron-scattering experiments to study materials such as superconductors, battery electrodes, and drug molecules. This supports advances in medicines, energy storage, and industrial materials.

### Meet a Scientist — James Chadwick

James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932 and received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1935.

## 8.4 — Symbols of Elements

Scientists need a standard way to represent elements.

### Dalton's Symbols

John Dalton introduced pictorial symbols for elements in 1803, but they were not convenient for worldwide use.

### Berzelius and Modern Symbols

In 1813, Berzelius suggested alphabetic symbols based on element names, often Latin names.

Today, names and symbols are approved by **IUPAC**.

### Rules for Writing Element Symbols

| Rule | Example |
|---|---|
| First letter is capital | `H`, `C`, `O` |
| Second letter, if present, is lowercase | `Al`, `Co`, `Cl` |
| Do not write both letters capital | `Co`, not `CO` |
| Some symbols come from Latin, Greek, or German names | `Fe`, `Na`, `K`, `W` |

### Common Element Symbols

| Element | Symbol | Element | Symbol | Element | Symbol |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aluminium | Al | Copper / Cuprum | Cu | Nitrogen | N |
| Argon | Ar | Fluorine | F | Oxygen | O |
| Barium | Ba | Gold / Aurum | Au | Potassium / Kalium | K |
| Boron | B | Hydrogen | H | Silicon | Si |
| Bromine | Br | Iodine | I | Silver / Argentum | Ag |
| Calcium | Ca | Iron / Ferrum | Fe | Sodium / Natrium | Na |
| Carbon | C | Lead / Plumbum | Pb | Sulfur | S |
| Chlorine | Cl | Magnesium | Mg | Uranium | U |
| Cobalt | Co | Neon | Ne | Zinc | Zn |

> [!TIP]
> `Co` is cobalt, while `CO` would be read as carbon monoxide in chemical formula context. Case matters.

## 8.5 — Atomic Number

> [!NOTE]
> **Atomic number** is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It is denoted by `Z`.

$$
Z
=
\text{number of protons}
$$

For a neutral atom:

$$
\text{number of electrons}
=
\text{number of protons}
=
Z
$$

Atomic number determines:

| Determines | Why |
|---|---|
| Element identity | Different elements have different proton numbers |
| Number of electrons in neutral atom | Electrons equal protons |
| Chemical behaviour | Electron arrangement depends on proton number |

Examples:

| Element | Atomic Number | Protons | Electrons in Neutral Atom |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| Helium | 2 | 2 | 2 |
| Lithium | 3 | 3 | 3 |

---

## 8.6 — Mass Number

> [!NOTE]
> **Mass number** is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus. It is denoted by `A`.

Protons and neutrons are together called **nucleons**.

$$
A
=
\text{number of protons}
+
\text{number of neutrons}
$$

Since:

$$
Z
=
\text{number of protons}
$$

Then:

$$
\text{number of neutrons}
=
A-Z
$$

### Examples

| Element | Protons | Neutrons | Mass Number |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| Helium | 2 | 2 | 4 |
| Lithium | 3 | 4 | 7 |

### Atomic Notation

An atom can be written using mass number, atomic number, and symbol:

$$
{}^{A}_{Z}X
$$

For carbon with atomic number 6 and mass number 12:

$$
{}^{12}_{6}\text{C}
$$

### Quick Calculation Rules

| Given | Find |
|---|---|
| Atomic number `Z` | Protons = `Z` |
| Neutral atom with atomic number `Z` | Electrons = `Z` |
| Mass number `A` and atomic number `Z` | Neutrons = `A − Z` |

Example:

An atom has atomic number 26 and 56 nucleons.

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---:|
| Protons | 26 |
| Electrons, if neutral | 26 |
| Neutrons | `56 − 26 = 30` |

## 8.7 — How Are Electrons Distributed in Different Energy Levels?

Bohr and Bury suggested rules for filling electrons in shells.

### Bohr-Bury Rules

| Rule | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Maximum electrons in shell = `2n²` | `n` is shell number |
| Outermost shell can have maximum 8 electrons | Except first shell, which holds maximum 2 |
| Shells fill stepwise | K fills before L; L before M; M before N |

### Maximum Electrons by Shell

$$
\text{maximum electrons}
=
2n^2
$$

| Shell | `n` | Maximum Electrons by `2n²` |
|---|---:|---:|
| K | 1 | 2 |
| L | 2 | 8 |
| M | 3 | 18 |
| N | 4 | 32 |

For the first 18 elements, the common filling pattern is:

| Atomic Number Range | Shell Filling |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | K shell |
| 3–10 | K then L |
| 11–18 | K, L, then M |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> For Class 9 first-18-element configurations, use the shell distribution shown in the textbook table.

## 8.8 — Combining Capacity of an Atom: Valency

Atoms combine to form molecules and compounds.

> [!NOTE]
> **Valency** is the combining capacity of an atom. It is the number of electrons an atom gains, loses, or shares to achieve a stable electronic configuration.

### Valence Shell and Valence Electrons

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Valence shell** | Outermost shell containing electrons |
| **Valence electrons** | Electrons in the valence shell |
| **Octet** | Stable outer shell with 8 electrons |
| **Duplet** | Stable first shell with 2 electrons, as in helium |

Atoms with complete octet or duplet are usually stable and largely unreactive.

### How to Predict Valency

| Valence Electrons | Usual Tendency | Valency |
|---:|---|---:|
| 1 | Lose 1 electron | 1 |
| 2 | Lose 2 electrons | 2 |
| 3 | Lose 3 electrons | 3 |
| 4 | Share 4 electrons | 4 |
| 5 | Gain 3 electrons | 3 |
| 6 | Gain 2 electrons | 2 |
| 7 | Gain 1 electron | 1 |
| 8 | Already stable | 0 |

Examples:

| Element | Electronic Configuration | Valence Electrons | Valency | Reason |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Sodium | 2, 8, 1 | 1 | 1 | Loses 1 electron |
| Oxygen | 2, 6 | 6 | 2 | Gains 2 electrons |
| Carbon | 2, 4 | 4 | 4 | Shares 4 electrons |
| Neon | 2, 8 | 8 | 0 | Stable octet |

### Combining Capacity Examples

| Compound | Combining Capacity |
|---|---|
| `H₂O` | Oxygen combines with 2 hydrogen atoms, so oxygen valency is 2 |
| `NH₃` | Nitrogen combines with 3 hydrogen atoms, so nitrogen valency is 3 |
| `MgCl₂` | Magnesium combines with 2 chlorine atoms, so magnesium valency is 2 |

## 8.9.1 — Isotopes

Dalton thought all atoms of an element have identical mass. Later, scientists discovered atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons.

> [!NOTE]
> **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with the same atomic number but different mass numbers.

### Hydrogen Isotopes

| Isotope | Symbol | Protons | Neutrons | Electrons |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Protium | `¹₁H` | 1 | 0 | 1 |
| Deuterium | `²₁H` | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| Tritium | `³₁H` | 1 | 2 | 1 |

All hydrogen isotopes have one proton and one electron, so they show similar chemical behaviour.

### Carbon Isotopes

| Isotope | Protons | Neutrons | Electrons |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Carbon-12 | 6 | 6 | 6 |
| Carbon-13 | 6 | 7 | 6 |
| Carbon-14 | 6 | 8 | 6 |

### Why Isotopes Have Similar Chemical Properties

Chemical properties depend mainly on valence electrons.

Isotopes of an element have:

| Same | Different |
|---|---|
| Atomic number | Neutron number |
| Proton number | Mass number |
| Electron number in neutral atoms | Some physical properties |
| Electronic configuration | Mass |
| Chemical properties | Stability/radioactivity in some cases |

---

## Uses of Isotopes

| Isotope | Use |
|---|---|
| Uranium-235 | Fuel in nuclear reactors |
| Cobalt-60 | Radiation treatment for cancer |
| Iodine-131 | Treatment of goitre and thyroid cancer |
| Carbon-14 | Dating ancient fossils and artefacts |

> [!NOTE]
> Some isotopes are radioactive and can be useful in medicine, energy, archaeology, and geology when handled safely.

## Average Atomic Mass

Atoms are too tiny to measure conveniently in grams or kilograms, so atomic masses are measured in **unified atomic mass unit**, written as **u**.

### Chlorine Example

Chlorine occurs mainly as:

| Isotope | Mass | Natural Abundance |
|---|---:|---:|
| `³⁵Cl` | 35 u | 75% |
| `³⁷Cl` | 37 u | 25% |

Simple average:

$$
\frac{35+37}{2}
=
36\text{ u}
$$

But this ignores abundance.

Weighted average:

$$
\text{Average atomic mass}
=
35\times\frac{75}{100}
+
37\times\frac{25}{100}
$$

$$
=
26.25
+
9.25
=
35.5\text{ u}
$$

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Average atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5 u. This does not mean one chlorine atom has mass 35.5 u. It means natural chlorine is a mixture of isotopes whose weighted average mass is 35.5 u.

### General Weighted Average Formula

$$
\text{Average atomic mass}
=
\sum
\left(
\text{isotope mass}
\times
\frac{\text{percentage abundance}}{100}
\right)
$$

## 8.9.2 — Isobars

> [!NOTE]
> **Isobars** are atoms of different elements that have the same mass number but different atomic numbers.

Examples with mass number 40:

| Element | Atomic Number | Mass Number | Protons | Neutrons |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Argon | 18 | 40 | 18 | 22 |
| Potassium | 19 | 40 | 19 | 21 |
| Calcium | 20 | 40 | 20 | 20 |

### Isotopes vs Isobars

| Feature | Isotopes | Isobars |
|---|---|---|
| Same element? | Yes | No |
| Atomic number | Same | Different |
| Mass number | Different | Same |
| Protons | Same | Different |
| Neutrons | Different | Usually different |
| Example | `¹²C`, `¹³C`, `¹⁴C` | `⁴⁰Ar`, `⁴⁰K`, `⁴⁰Ca` |

## Modern Atomic View

Bohr's model was later improved. In the modern quantum mechanical model:

| Bohr Model | Modern Model |
|---|---|
| Electrons move in fixed circular orbits | Electrons exist in probability regions |
| Electron path is well-defined | Exact path is not defined |
| Shells explain basic stability | Orbitals/electron clouds explain deeper behaviour |

Electron microscopes such as **Scanning Tunnelling Microscopes (STM)** and **Transmission Electron Microscopes (TEM)** can reveal atomic-level details of materials.

### Meet a Scientist — Homi Jehangir Bhabha

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **Atoms are indivisible.** | Atoms contain electrons, protons, and neutrons. |
| **Thomson's model is correct because atoms are neutral.** | It explained neutrality but failed to explain gold foil observations. |
| **Most of an atom is filled with matter.** | Most of an atom is empty space. |
| **The nucleus is large compared with the atom.** | The nucleus is extremely small compared with the atom. |
| **Electrons in Rutherford's model are stable.** | Rutherford's model could not explain stability. |
| **Atomic number and mass number are the same.** | Atomic number is protons; mass number is protons plus neutrons. |
| **All atoms of an element have the same mass.** | Isotopes of an element can have different masses. |
| **Isotopes are different elements.** | Isotopes are atoms of the same element. |
| **Average atomic mass means every atom has that mass.** | It is a weighted average of naturally occurring isotopes. |
| **Valency is always equal to valence electrons.** | For more than 4 valence electrons, valency is often `8 − valence electrons`. |

## 🧭 Problem-Solving Toolkit

### Atomic Number and Mass Number Workflow

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Identify atomic number `Z`. |
| **2** | Protons = `Z`. |
| **3** | For a neutral atom, electrons = `Z`. |
| **4** | Identify mass number `A`. |
| **5** | Neutrons = `A − Z`. |
| **6** | Use electron count to write electronic configuration. |
| **7** | Use valence electrons to predict valency. |

### Isotope vs Isobar Check

| Question | If Yes |
|---|---|
| Same atomic number but different mass number? | Isotopes |
| Different atomic number but same mass number? | Isobars |
| Same proton number? | Same element |
| Different proton number? | Different elements |

### Valency Workflow

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Write electronic configuration. |
| **2** | Count valence electrons. |
| **3** | If valence electrons are 1–4, valency often equals that number. |
| **4** | If valence electrons are 5–7, valency often equals `8 − valence electrons`. |
| **5** | If outer shell is complete, valency is 0. |
