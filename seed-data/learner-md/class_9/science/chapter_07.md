## 🌍 Introduction — Why Work and Energy?

In Chapters 4 and 6, you learnt to describe motion using kinematics and Newton's laws. But when forces change with time or act in complicated ways, directly using Newton's laws can become difficult.

The ideas of **work**, **energy**, and **power** often provide a simpler route.

| Idea | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| **Work** | Measures energy transferred by force and displacement |
| **Energy** | Tracks capacity to do work |
| **Power** | Measures how quickly work is done |
| **Simple machines** | Help perform tasks with less effort or more convenience |

> [!NOTE]
> **Energy** is the capacity to do work.

Examples:

| Source | Energy Use |
|---|---|
| Food | Provides energy to walk |
| Electricity | Runs fans and lights |
| Fuel | Moves vehicles |
| Stored water in dams | Can generate electricity |

### Core Questions

| # | Question |
|---|---|
| **1** | What will be the speed of a child at the bottom of a slide? |
| **2** | Will two children of different masses reach the bottom of the same slide with the same speed? |
| **3** | Which slide gives the largest speed at the bottom? |

## 7.1 — Work Done by a Constant Force

In science, work is not just any effort. Work is done only when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force.

> [!NOTE]
> Work done by a constant force is the product of force and displacement in the direction of the force.

For force and displacement in the same direction:

$$
W
=
F\times s
\tag{7.2}
$$

| Symbol | Meaning | SI Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `W` | Work done | joule (J) |
| `F` | Force | newton (N) |
| `s` | Displacement in direction of force | metre (m) |

### Lifting Bags Example

| Situation | Force | Height | Work Compared to One Bag Lifted 1 m |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lift one 5 kg bag by 1 m | `mg` | 1 m | `W` |
| Lift three bags one by one by 1 m | `mg` each | 1 m each | `3W` |
| Lift three bags together by 1 m | `3mg` | 1 m | `3W` |
| Lift one bag by 3 m | `mg` | 3 m | `3W` |

Work increases if:

| Change | Effect on Work |
|---|---|
| Force increases for same displacement | Work increases |
| Displacement increases for same force | Work increases |

### Unit of Work

$$
1\text{ J}
=
1\text{ N}
\times
1\text{ m}
$$

Since:

$$
1\text{ N}
=
1\text{ kg m s}^{-2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
1\text{ J}
=
1\text{ kg m}^2\text{ s}^{-2}
$$

> [!NOTE]
> One joule of work is done when a constant force of 1 N displaces an object by 1 m in the direction of the force.

### Work from Force-Displacement Graph

If force is plotted on the y-axis and displacement on the x-axis:

> [!NOTE]
> Work done is equal to the area under the force-displacement graph.

For constant force, the graph is a rectangle:

$$
\text{Work}
=
\text{Area}
=
\text{force}
\times
\text{displacement}
$$

## 7.1.1 — When Is Work Done Equal to Zero?

Work done is zero in three important cases.

| Case | Why Work Is Zero | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Force is zero | `F = 0` | No force applied |
| Displacement is zero | `s = 0` | Pushing a rigid wall that does not move |
| Force is perpendicular to displacement | No displacement in direction of force | Carrying a box horizontally while force on box is upward |

### Why Pushing a Wall Feels Tiring

If you push a wall and it does not move, no work is done **on the wall** in the scientific sense.

But your muscles use internal energy while contracting and relaxing, so you feel tired.

> [!TIP]
> Work is always stated with respect to a force and an object: "work done by which force on which object?"

## 7.1.2 — Positive and Negative Work Done

Work can be positive or negative.

| Relative Direction | Work Sign | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Force and displacement in same direction | Positive | Pushing wheelchair forward |
| Force and displacement in opposite directions | Negative | Goalkeeper stopping a ball |
| Force perpendicular to displacement | Zero | Carrying a box horizontally |

Work has no direction, but it can have positive or negative sign.

### Example 7.1 — Lifting and Lowering a Dumbbell

When a girl lifts a dumbbell upward:

| Force by Girl | Displacement | Work by Girl |
|---|---|---|
| Upward | Upward | Positive |

When she slowly lowers it:

| Force by Girl | Displacement | Work by Girl |
|---|---|---|
| Upward | Downward | Negative |

### Example 7.2 — Goalkeeper Stopping a Ball

A goalkeeper applies a force of **200 N** while the hand moves back by **15 cm**.

Convert displacement:

$$
15\text{ cm}
=
0.15\text{ m}
$$

The force applied by goalkeeper is opposite to ball's displacement, so displacement in direction of force is negative:

$$
s
=
-0.15\text{ m}
$$

Work done:

$$
W
=
F\times s
=
200
\times
(-0.15)
=
-30\text{ J}
$$

The goalkeeper does **−30 J** of work on the ball.

> [!NOTE]
> The ball does positive work on the goalkeeper's hands, while the goalkeeper does negative work on the ball.

## 7.2 — The Work-Energy Theorem

When work is done on an object, its energy can change.

Examples:

| Situation | Energy Change |
|---|---|
| Fielder throws ball | Ball gains energy |
| Moving ball hits wickets | Ball transfers energy to wickets |
| Flowerpot raised to height | Pot gains stored energy |
| Falling pot hits ground | Stored energy changes into motion and impact |

> [!NOTE]
> An object that has the capacity to do work possesses energy.

### Work-Energy Theorem

> [!NOTE]
> **Work-energy theorem:** Work done on an object or system is equal to the change in its energy.

$$
W
=
\Delta E
\tag{7.3}
$$

The SI unit of energy is also the **joule (J)**.

### Energy Transfer

Energy can be transferred by:

| Mode | Example |
|---|---|
| Mechanical work | A bat hits a ball |
| Heat | Hot object warms a colder object |
| Radiation | Sun's energy reaches Earth |
| Electric circuit | Battery powers a bulb |
| Sound waves | Vibrations transfer energy |
| Nuclear reactions | Sun produces energy |

### Example 7.3 — Carrom Collisions

In a carrom shot:

| Interaction | Positive Work | Negative Work |
|---|---|---|
| Striker hits white coin | Striker does positive work on white coin | White coin does negative work on striker |
| White coin hits black coin | White coin does positive work on black coin | Black coin does negative work on white coin |

Energy transfers from one moving object to another through collisions.

### Meet a Scientist — James Prescott Joule

The unit **joule** is named after **James Prescott Joule**, who studied the relationship between mechanical energy and thermal energy.

## 7.4 — Mechanical Energy

> [!NOTE]
> **Mechanical energy** is energy possessed by an object due to its motion or position.

Two main forms:

| Mechanical Energy Type | Due To |
|---|---|
| **Kinetic energy** | Motion |
| **Potential energy** | Position, arrangement, or deformation |

---

## 7.4.1 — Kinetic Energy

> [!NOTE]
> **Kinetic energy** is the energy possessed by an object due to its motion.

All moving objects have kinetic energy:

| Object | Kinetic Energy? |
|---|---|
| Moving bicycle | Yes |
| Rolling ball | Yes |
| Moving car | Yes |
| Book lying still | No kinetic energy if at rest |

### Kinetic Energy Formula

For an object of mass `m` moving with velocity `v`:

$$
K
=
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
\tag{7.6}
$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `K` | Kinetic energy | J |
| `m` | Mass | kg |
| `v` | Velocity | m s⁻¹ |

Kinetic energy has no direction.

### Change in Kinetic Energy

For velocity changing from `u` to `v`:

$$
W
=
\frac{1}{2}m(v^2-u^2)
\tag{7.5}
$$

This is a form of the work-energy theorem.

### Example 7.4 — Doubling Speed

Initial kinetic energy:

$$
K_1
=
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

If speed doubles to `2v`:

$$
K_2
=
\frac{1}{2}m(2v)^2
=
4\left(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\right)
$$

So kinetic energy becomes **4 times** the original value.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Kinetic energy depends on the square of speed. Doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy.

### Example 7.5 — Cricket Ball Kinetic Energy

Mass of ball:

$$
m
=
0.2\text{ kg}
$$

Speed:

$$
154.8\text{ km h}^{-1}
=
43\text{ m s}^{-1}
$$

Kinetic energy:

$$
K
=
\frac{1}{2}
\times
0.2
\times
43^2
=
184.9\text{ J}
$$

### Example 7.6 — Aircraft Carrier Landing

A jet aircraft of mass `15000 kg` is stopped by a wire over `100 m`. The wire exerts a backward force of `367500 N`.

Work done by wire:

$$
W
=
367500
\times
(-100)
=
-36750000\text{ J}
$$

This equals the change in kinetic energy:

$$
0
-
\frac{1}{2}(15000)v^2
=
-36750000
$$

Solving:

$$
v^2
=
4900
$$

$$
v
=
70\text{ m s}^{-1}
=
252\text{ km h}^{-1}
$$

## 7.4.2 — Potential Energy

Potential energy is stored energy due to:

| Cause | Example |
|---|---|
| Deformation | Stretched rubber band, compressed spring |
| Relative position | Ball raised above ground |
| Magnetic arrangement | Separated unlike poles |
| Electric arrangement | Separated charges |

> [!NOTE]
> **Potential energy** is energy stored by an object due to deformation or by a system due to relative positions of its parts.

### Elastic Potential Energy

| Example | What Happens When Released |
|---|---|
| Stretched rubber band | Shoots object forward |
| Bent bow | Launches arrow |
| Compressed spring | Pushes object |

### Gravitational Potential Energy

Near Earth's surface, when an object of mass `m` is raised to height `h`:

$$
U
=
mgh
\tag{7.8}
$$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `U` | Potential energy |
| `m` | Mass |
| `g` | Acceleration due to gravity |
| `h` | Height above chosen zero level |

The zero level for potential energy is chosen for convenience, often the ground.

**Aim:** To connect height with gravitational potential energy.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Fill a container with loose sand. |
| **2** | Drop a heavy ball from 1 m. |
| **3** | Observe depression in sand. |

Inference:

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Higher drop gives deeper depression | Greater height means more potential energy |
| Ball can do more work on sand | More energy transferred during impact |

### Example 7.7 — Cricket Ball Potential Energy

Mass of ball:

$$
m
=
0.2\text{ kg}
$$

Height:

$$
h
=
10\text{ m}
$$

Using `g = 10 m s⁻²`:

$$
U
=
mgh
=
0.2
\times
10
\times
10
=
20\text{ J}
$$

## 7.4.3 — Conservation of Mechanical Energy

> [!NOTE]
> **Mechanical energy** is the sum of kinetic energy and potential energy.

$$
\text{Mechanical energy}
=
K+U
$$

For an object falling freely under gravity, ignoring air resistance:

| Position | Kinetic Energy | Potential Energy | Mechanical Energy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Top | 0 | `mgh` | `mgh` |
| Middle | Increasing | Decreasing | `mgh` |
| Just before ground | Maximum | 0 | `mgh` |

> [!NOTE]
> **Conservation of mechanical energy:** If no external dissipative force such as friction or air resistance acts, total mechanical energy remains constant.

### Simple Pendulum Energy

| Point | Kinetic Energy | Potential Energy |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme left | Zero | Maximum |
| Lowest point | Maximum | Minimum |
| Extreme right | Zero | Maximum |

In real life, a pendulum eventually slows because some energy is transferred due to air resistance and friction at the support.

---

### Example 7.8 — Speed at Bottom of a Slide

At the top of height `h`, potential energy is:

$$
U
=
mgh
$$

At the bottom, if friction is neglected, this becomes kinetic energy:

$$
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
=
mgh
$$

Cancel `m`:

$$
v^2
=
2gh
$$

So:

$$
v
=
\sqrt{2gh}
$$

> [!IMPORTANT]
> If friction is neglected, speed at the bottom depends on height, not on mass or slide shape.

### Example 7.9 — Escape Ramp for Truck

Truck mass:

$$
m
=
10000\text{ kg}
$$

Speed:

$$
72\text{ km h}^{-1}
=
20\text{ m s}^{-1}
$$

Initial kinetic energy:

$$
K
=
\frac{1}{2}
\times
10000
\times
20^2
=
2000000\text{ J}
$$

For a 30° incline, the truck rises 1 m for every 2 m travelled, so for ramp distance `d`, height gained is `d/2`.

Final potential energy:

$$
U
=
mg\frac{d}{2}
=
10000
\times
10
\times
\frac{d}{2}
=
50000d
$$

Work done by sand:

$$
W_{\text{sand}}
=
-50000d
$$

Using the work-energy theorem:

$$
-50000d
=
50000d
-
2000000
$$

So:

$$
2000000
=
100000d
$$

$$
d
=
20\text{ m}
$$

The ramp must be at least **20 m** long.

## 7.5 — Power

Doing the same work in less time requires more power.

> [!NOTE]
> **Power** is the rate at which work is done.

$$
P
=
\frac{W}{t}
\tag{7.11}
$$

| Symbol | Meaning | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `P` | Power | watt (W) |
| `W` | Work | joule (J) |
| `t` | Time | second (s) |

$$
1\text{ W}
=
1\text{ J s}^{-1}
$$

Another unit:

$$
1\text{ horsepower}
=
746\text{ W}
$$

### Example 7.10 — Weightlifter Power

A weightlifter lifts 75 kg by 2 m in 5 s. Take `g = 10 m s⁻²`.

Work:

$$
W
=
mgh
=
75
\times
10
\times
2
=
1500\text{ J}
$$

Power:

$$
P
=
\frac{1500}{5}
=
300\text{ W}
$$

### Example 7.11 — Car Engine Power

A 1000 kg car starts from rest and reaches 72 km h⁻¹ in 10 s.

Convert speed:

$$
72\text{ km h}^{-1}
=
20\text{ m s}^{-1}
$$

Work done equals change in kinetic energy:

$$
W
=
\frac{1}{2}mv^2
=
\frac{1}{2}
\times
1000
\times
20^2
=
200000\text{ J}
$$

Power:

$$
P
=
\frac{200000}{10}
=
20000\text{ W}
$$

## 7.6 — Simple Machines

Simple machines help us do tasks more conveniently.

> [!NOTE]
> **Simple machines** are devices that make work easier by changing the magnitude or direction of the force applied.

Important terms:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Effort** | Force applied by us to a machine |
| **Load** | Force that must be overcome |
| **Mechanical advantage** | Ratio of load to effort |

$$
\text{Mechanical advantage}
=
\frac{\text{Load}}{\text{Effort}}
\tag{7.12}
$$

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Simple machines do not reduce total work in ideal conditions. If force decreases, distance increases.

---

## 7.6.1 — Pulley

A pulley is a wheel with a groove that guides a rope.

| Pulley Type | Effect |
|---|---|
| Fixed pulley | Changes direction of force; mechanical advantage 1 |
| Movable pulley | Can reduce effort; mechanical advantage greater than 1 |
| Pulley system | Can lift heavier loads with smaller effort |

Examples:

| Use | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Flag hoisting | Pull down to raise flag up |
| Cranes | Lift heavy loads |
| Elevators | Mechanical support and direction change |

---

## 7.6.2 — Inclined Plane

An inclined plane helps raise a load by applying a smaller force over a longer distance.

### Activity 7.3 — Pulling a Cart Up a Plank

| Setup | Force Required | Distance |
|---|---|---|
| Lift vertically | Larger force | Smaller distance |
| Pull up steep plank | Smaller than vertical lift | Longer distance |
| Pull up shallow plank | Even smaller force | Even longer distance |

### Mechanical Advantage of Inclined Plane

For load `mg`, height `h`, and plane length `L`:

Work input:

$$
F'\times L
$$

Potential energy gained:

$$
mgh
$$

Ignoring friction:

$$
F'L
=
mgh
$$

So:

$$
\frac{mg}{F'}
=
\frac{L}{h}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\text{Mechanical advantage}
=
\frac{L}{h}
\tag{7.13}
$$

Since `L > h`, the effort is less than the load.

### Example 7.12 — Ramp Mechanical Advantage

Ramp height is 30 cm and base is 40 cm. By the 3-4-5 right triangle relation, ramp length is 50 cm.

$$
\text{Mechanical advantage}
=
\frac{L}{h}
=
\frac{50}{30}
=
1.67
$$

## 7.6.3 — Lever

A lever is a rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point.

### Parts of a Lever

| Part | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Fulcrum** | Fixed point about which lever rotates |
| **Load** | Force to be overcome |
| **Effort** | Force applied |
| **Load arm** | Distance of load from fulcrum |
| **Effort arm** | Distance of effort from fulcrum |

### Lever Work Relation

If effort `F_1` moves distance `d_1` and load force `F_2` moves distance `d_2`:

$$
F_1d_1
=
F_2d_2
\tag{7.14}
$$

For balance:

$$
\text{effort}
\times
\text{effort arm}
=
\text{load}
\times
\text{load arm}
\tag{7.15}
$$

Mechanical advantage:

$$
\text{Mechanical advantage}
=
\frac{\text{Load}}{\text{Effort}}
=
\frac{\text{Effort arm}}{\text{Load arm}}
\tag{7.16}
$$

> [!TIP]
> Increasing the effort arm reduces the effort needed to lift the same load.

### Activity 7.4 — Scale, Pencil, and Erasers

This activity shows that a lighter object can lift a heavier object if it is placed farther from the fulcrum.

### Activity 7.5 — Beam Balance

For coins on two sides:

$$
n_1L_1
=
n_2L_2
$$

This is the same idea as:

$$
\text{effort}
\times
\text{effort arm}
=
\text{load}
\times
\text{load arm}
$$

### Example 7.13 — Seesaw Balance

Seats A and E are 2 m from fulcrum C. Seats B and D are 1 m from C.

Let 15 kg child sit at 2 m. For 30 kg child:

$$
15
\times
2
=
30
\times
L
$$

$$
L
=
1\text{ m}
$$

So the 30 kg child should sit 1 m from the fulcrum.

### Classes of Levers

| Lever Class | Arrangement | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Class I** | Fulcrum between effort and load | Seesaw, scissors, crowbar, pliers, balance scale |
| **Class II** | Load between fulcrum and effort | Wheelbarrow, bottle opener, lemon squeezer |
| **Class III** | Effort between fulcrum and load | Tweezers, broom, tongs, oar |

## 🌉 Bridging Science and Society — Water Mills

In Himalayan regions, flowing water has gravitational potential energy at height. As it flows downhill, this energy becomes kinetic energy.

Traditional **gharat** or **panchakki** water mills use this kinetic energy to rotate a wheel connected to a grinding stone.

Modern hydroelectric power plants use a similar energy chain:

| Stage | Energy Form |
|---|---|
| Water stored high in dam | Gravitational potential energy |
| Water flows down | Kinetic energy |
| Turbine rotates | Mechanical energy |
| Generator works | Electrical energy |

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **Work is done whenever effort is applied.** | Scientific work needs displacement in direction of force. |
| **Carrying a box horizontally always involves work on the box by upward force.** | Upward force is perpendicular to displacement, so that force does zero work. |
| **Energy disappears when an object stops.** | Energy transforms or transfers, often into thermal energy due to friction. |
| **Kinetic energy doubles when speed doubles.** | It becomes four times because `K` depends on `v²`. |
| **Potential energy belongs only to one object.** | It often belongs to a system, such as Earth-object system. |
| **Simple machines reduce work.** | Ideal simple machines reduce effort by increasing distance or changing direction; total work is not reduced. |
| **A machine can run forever without energy input.** | Real machines lose energy to friction and other effects. |

### The Quest Continues

Scientists study mysterious **dark energy** because the expansion of the Universe is accelerating. Dark energy does not behave like ordinary forms of energy that easily exchange with mechanical, thermal, or electrical energy, but its effects may influence the future of the Universe.
