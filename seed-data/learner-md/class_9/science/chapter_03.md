## 🌱 Introduction — Why Do Living Bodies Need Tissues?

Life begins when a single cell divides repeatedly and gives rise to many cells. As development continues, these cells do not remain identical in function. They gradually organise into groups that perform specialised roles.

Some cells help form **skin**, which protects the body. Some form **muscles**, which enable movement. Some form **bones**, which support the body. Some form **nerves**, which coordinate and control responses.

This organisation is one of nature's most remarkable designs.

> [!NOTE]
> A **tissue** is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function.

In a multicellular organism, organisation happens in levels:

| Level | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cell** | Basic unit of life | Muscle cell |
| **Tissue** | Similar cells working together | Muscle tissue |
| **Organ** | Different tissues working together | Heart |
| **Organ system** | Different organs working together | Circulatory system |
| **Organism** | Complete living being | Human, mango tree |

In **unicellular organisms**, such as Amoeba, one cell performs all life functions. In **multicellular organisms**, different tissues divide work among themselves.

> [!NOTE]
> **Division of labour** means that different cells, tissues, organs, or organ systems perform different specialised functions.

### Examples of Division of Labour

| Organism | Tissue / Structure | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Animal | Muscle tissue | Movement |
| Animal | Nervous tissue | Carries messages |
| Plant | Xylem | Transports water and minerals |
| Plant | Phloem | Transports food |
| Plant | Epidermis | Protection |

> [!TIP]
> Whenever you study a tissue, always ask two questions: **What is its structure?** and **How does that structure help its function?**

## 3.1 — Why Are Plant and Animal Tissues Different?

Plant and animal tissues are different because plants and animals live differently.

Most plants remain fixed in one place. They need firm support to stay upright against wind, gravity, and environmental stress. Plant cells have a **cell wall**, which gives rigidity and strength.

Animals usually move from place to place. Their cells do not have rigid cell walls. This allows animal cells and tissues to be more flexible, which supports movement and locomotion.

### Plant Tissues vs Animal Tissues

| Feature | Plant Tissues | Animal Tissues |
|---|---|---|
| Movement | Plants are generally fixed in one place | Animals usually show locomotion |
| Cell wall | Present in plant cells | Absent in animal cells |
| Rigidity | More rigid because of cell walls | More flexible |
| Nutrition | Tissues help in photosynthesis and transport | Tissues help in digestion, absorption, transport, and movement |
| Growth pattern | Growth occurs in specific regions called meristems | Growth is usually more uniform in early development and then regulated |
| Transport | Xylem and phloem transport water, minerals, and food | Blood transports gases, nutrients, wastes, and hormones |

> [!NOTE]
> Plant tissues are strongly adapted for **support**, **photosynthesis**, and **transport**, while animal tissues are strongly adapted for **movement**, **coordination**, **protection**, and **internal transport**.

## 3.2 — Tissues for Growth in Plants

Plants grow in several ways:

| Type of Growth | Example |
|---|---|
| **Increase in length** | Stem grows taller; roots grow deeper |
| **Increase in girth** | Stem becomes thicker |
| **Regrowth after cutting** | Grass grows again after grazing or mowing |
| **Branching** | New branches arise from nodes |

These forms of growth depend on actively dividing cells.

> [!NOTE]
> **Meristematic tissue** is plant tissue made of actively dividing cells. It is responsible for growth in plants.

Meristematic tissues are found in specific regions of the plant.

| Type of Meristem | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Apical meristem** | Tips of roots and shoots | Increases length |
| **Lateral meristem** | Along the sides / circumference of stem | Increases girth |
| **Intercalary meristem** | At nodes or base of internodes, especially in grasses | Helps regrowth after cutting or grazing |

## 3.2.1 — Apical Meristem: How Do Plants Grow in Length?

Apical meristems are found at the tips of roots and shoots. Their cells divide repeatedly, adding new cells and helping the plant grow longer.

## 3.2.2 — Lateral Meristem: How Do Plants Grow in Girth?

Many dicot plants do not only grow taller; their stems also become thicker with time. This increase in thickness is called growth in **girth**.

If you observe the cut trunk of a tree, you may see ring-like patterns. These are called **annual growth rings**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Annual growth rings** are ring-like patterns seen in the cut trunk of many trees. They reflect growth during different years.

### What Annual Rings Tell Us

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Wide annual rings | Favourable growth conditions |
| Narrow annual rings | Less favourable growth conditions |
| Number of rings | Approximate age of the tree |
| Pattern of rings | Clues about past climate and growth conditions |

Growth in girth occurs due to actively dividing cells arranged in a ring in the stem.

> [!NOTE]
> **Lateral meristem** is meristematic tissue arranged along the sides or circumference of the stem. It divides to increase the diameter or girth of the stem.

Lateral meristem produces new cells toward the inside and outside in a concentric manner, making the stem thicker.

## 3.2.3 — Intercalary Meristem: How Do Plants Regrow After Cutting?

If the tip of a young stem is cut, the stem may stop growing in length from that tip. However, new branches may arise from the nodes.

> [!NOTE]
> A **node** is a point on the plant stem where leaves or branches arise.

> [!NOTE]
> An **internode** is the part of the stem between two nodes.

Intercalary meristem is located at the base of internodes or just above nodes. It helps plants regrow after cutting or grazing.

### Everyday Examples

| Observation | Tissue Responsible |
|---|---|
| Grass grows again after being mowed | Intercalary meristem |
| Grass regrows after grazing by animals | Intercalary meristem |
| Garden hedge becomes bushy after trimming | Meristematic activity near nodes |
| New branches arise after cutting | Meristematic tissue near nodes |

> [!NOTE]
> **Intercalary meristem** is meristematic tissue found near nodes or at the base of internodes. It helps in regrowth and branching, especially in grasses.

### Characteristics of Meristematic Cells

| Feature | Why It Helps Cell Division |
|---|---|
| Small cell size | Allows rapid division |
| Thin cell wall | Easier expansion and division |
| Dense cytoplasm | High metabolic activity |
| Large prominent nucleus | Controls active division |
| Many organelles | Supports rapid growth |
| Vacuoles absent or very small | More space for cytoplasm and nucleus |
| Tightly packed cells | Forms active growth zones |

> [!TIP]
> Meristematic cells generally lack large vacuoles because they need dense cytoplasm and active nuclei for repeated cell division.

### From Meristematic Tissue to Permanent Tissue

Some newly formed cells remain meristematic. Others lose the ability to divide and become specialised for functions such as support, storage, protection, or transport.

> [!NOTE]
> **Differentiation** is the process by which cells become specialised in structure and function.

> [!NOTE]
> **Permanent tissues** are tissues formed from meristematic cells that have lost the ability to divide and have become specialised.

## 3.2.4 — Permanent Tissues

Permanent tissues are specialised plant tissues. They may be made of one type of cell or more than one type of cell.

| Type of Permanent Tissue | Composition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple permanent tissue** | One type of cell | Parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma |
| **Complex permanent tissue** | More than one type of cell working together | Xylem, phloem |

When a transverse section of a sunflower stem is observed under a microscope, different groups of cells can be seen. These groups differ in shape, size, arrangement, and function.

This shows that a plant body contains multiple tissues specialised for different roles.

## Protective Tissue — Epidermis

The outermost layer of the plant body is the **epidermis**.

> [!NOTE]
> The **epidermis** is the outermost protective tissue of the plant body.

### Functions of Epidermis

| Function | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Protection | Protects against mechanical injury |
| Prevents water loss | Cuticle reduces evaporation |
| Defence | Helps prevent entry of harmful microorganisms and parasites |
| Absorption | Root hairs absorb water and minerals |
| Gas exchange | Stomata allow exchange of gases |
| Transpiration | Stomata allow water vapour to leave |

### Cuticle

Epidermal cells are often covered by a waxy layer called **cuticle**.

> [!NOTE]
> The **cuticle** is a waxy layer of cutin present on the epidermis. It reduces water loss and protects against injury and parasites.

In plants living in dry habitats, the cuticle may be thick. This helps reduce water loss.

### Root Hairs

In roots, hair-like projections from epidermal cells are called **root hairs**.

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| **Root hair** | Increases surface area for absorption of water and minerals |

### Stomata

In leaves and some stems, the epidermis contains pores called **stomata**.

| Function of Stomata | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Gas exchange | Allows carbon dioxide and oxygen exchange |
| Transpiration | Allows evaporation of water vapour |
| Water transport support | Transpiration pull helps water movement through xylem |
| Waste removal | Helps eliminate some wastes from the plant body |

## Supporting Tissues — Simple Permanent Tissues

Simple permanent tissues provide support, storage, flexibility, hardness, and sometimes photosynthesis.

There are three major simple permanent tissues:

| Tissue | Cell Type | Main Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Parenchyma** | Living cells with thin walls | Storage, photosynthesis, floating in aquatic plants |
| **Collenchyma** | Living cells with unevenly thickened corners | Flexible support |
| **Sclerenchyma** | Mostly dead cells with thick lignified walls | Strength and hardness |

---

### Parenchyma

**Parenchyma** consists of living cells with thin walls. The cells are loosely packed and usually have intercellular spaces.

| Feature | Function |
|---|---|
| Living cells | Can carry out metabolic activities |
| Thin walls | Allow storage and exchange |
| Intercellular spaces | Help gas exchange |
| Chlorophyll in green parts | Allows photosynthesis |
| Air spaces in aquatic plants | Helps plants float |

> [!NOTE]
> **Parenchyma** is a simple permanent tissue made of living thin-walled cells. It mainly stores food and may perform photosynthesis in green parts.

Specialised parenchyma with air spaces in aquatic plants helps them float.

---

### Collenchyma

**Collenchyma** consists of living cells with unevenly thickened corners. The thickening is due to deposition of **pectin**, which gives flexibility.

| Feature | Function |
|---|---|
| Living cells | Active tissue |
| Uneven thickening at corners | Provides support |
| Pectin deposition | Gives flexibility |
| Found in stems and tendrils | Allows bending without breaking |

> [!NOTE]
> **Collenchyma** provides flexible support to plant parts, allowing them to bend without breaking.

Example: young stems and leaf stalks bend during wind because of flexible support from collenchyma.

---

### Sclerenchyma

**Sclerenchyma** cells have thick walls due to **lignin** deposition. Most sclerenchyma cells are dead at maturity.

| Feature | Function |
|---|---|
| Thick lignified walls | Provides hardness and strength |
| Mostly dead cells | Forms strong structural tissue |
| Found in stems, leaf veins, seed coats, nut shells | Mechanical support and protection |

> [!NOTE]
> **Sclerenchyma** is a strong supporting tissue made of thick-walled, lignified cells. It gives hardness and strength.

Examples:

| Example | Reason |
|---|---|
| Coconut husk | Rich in tough sclerenchyma fibres |
| Walnut shell | Hard due to sclerenchyma |
| Seed coats | Protective hardness |
| Leaf veins | Mechanical support |

## Conducting Tissues — Complex Permanent Tissues

Plants need to transport water, minerals, and food across long distances.

They use two complex permanent tissues:

| Conducting Tissue | Main Function |
|---|---|
| **Xylem** | Transports water and minerals from roots to other parts |
| **Phloem** | Transports food from leaves to other parts |

> [!NOTE]
> **Complex permanent tissues** are made of more than one type of cell working together to perform a common function.

---

### Xylem

**Xylem** transports water and minerals from the roots to the rest of the plant. It also provides strength.

| Xylem Component | Nature | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Tracheids** | Dead, tubular, thick-walled | Water transport and support |
| **Vessels** | Dead, tubular, thick-walled | Efficient water transport |
| **Xylem parenchyma** | Living | Storage and lateral transport |
| **Xylem fibres** | Dead, sclerenchymatous | Strength |

> [!NOTE]
> In xylem, **xylem parenchyma** is the living component. Tracheids, vessels, and xylem fibres are mostly dead and strengthen water transport pathways.

---

### Phloem

**Phloem** transports food prepared in leaves to other parts of the plant.

| Phloem Component | Nature | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Sieve tubes** | Living tubular cells | Transport food |
| **Companion cells** | Living specialised parenchyma | Regulate sieve tube activity |
| **Phloem parenchyma** | Living | Stores food, resin, tannins, latex |
| **Phloem fibres** | Mostly dead sclerenchymatous cells | Provide strength |

> [!NOTE]
> **Companion cells** help control loading and unloading of sugars in sieve tubes.

### Xylem vs Phloem

| Feature | Xylem | Phloem |
|---|---|---|
| Main material transported | Water and minerals | Food / sugars |
| Main direction | Mostly upward from roots | From leaves to growing or storage parts |
| Living or dead | Mostly dead, except xylem parenchyma | Mostly living, except phloem fibres |
| Support function | Strong support | Some support through fibres |
| Major components | Tracheids, vessels, xylem parenchyma, xylem fibres | Sieve tubes, companion cells, phloem parenchyma, phloem fibres |

---

## Plant Tissue Systems

Plant tissues do not work alone. They are organised into tissue systems.

| Tissue System | Includes | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Dermal tissue system** | Epidermis and related protective tissues | Outer covering, protection, reduced water loss |
| **Ground tissue system** | Parenchyma, collenchyma, sclerenchyma | Storage, support, photosynthesis |
| **Vascular tissue system** | Xylem and phloem | Transport of water, minerals, and food |

## Cork and Bark

In young plants, the outer protective layer is usually a single-layered epidermis. As plants grow older, cells below the epidermis may regain the ability to divide and form **cork cambium**.

Cork cambium produces **cork cells**.

| Feature of Cork Cells | Importance |
|---|---|
| Dead cells | Protective layer |
| Compactly arranged | Prevents gaps |
| Impermeable to water and gases | Reduces water loss and protects inner tissues |
| Forms bark | Protects older stems |

|---|---|

## 3.3.1 — Epithelial Tissues: Structure and Function

**Epithelial tissue** forms the outer covering of the body and lines internal organs such as the mouth, lungs, blood vessels, and intestine.

> [!NOTE]
> **Epithelial tissue** is made of closely packed cells with very little intercellular space. It forms protective coverings and linings.

### Why Epithelial Cells Are Closely Packed

| Feature | Advantage |
|---|---|
| Very little space between cells | Prevents entry of germs |
| Continuous sheet-like arrangement | Covers and protects surfaces |
| Thin layers in some places | Allows rapid exchange |
| Specialised cells in glands | Allows secretion |
| Cilia or sensory cells in some regions | Helps sensing or movement of materials |

### Types / Functions of Epithelial Tissues

| Function | Structure | Location |
|---|---|---|
| **Exchange** | Single layer of thin, flat cells | Lining of blood vessels and lungs |
| **Protection** | Many layers of cells; outer cells flat and tightly packed | Skin, mouth, oesophagus |
| **Secretion** | Cells specialised for releasing mucus, enzymes, hormones, sweat, saliva | Salivary glands, sweat glands, stomach lining |
| **Sensory function** | Specialised receptor cells, often with hair-like cilia | Nostrils, taste buds, inner ear |
| **Absorption** | Single layer of tall pillar-like cells, often with hair-like structures | Lining of small intestine |

> [!TIP]
> The form of epithelial tissue depends on the function: thin for exchange, layered for protection, glandular for secretion, tall for absorption, specialised for sensation.

## 3.3.2 — How Are Various Parts Connected in Our Body?

Tissues that connect and support other tissues are called **connective tissues**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Connective tissue** connects, supports, binds, or transports materials in the body.

Blood and bone are both connective tissues, but they look very different because their **matrix** is different.

> [!NOTE]
> The **matrix** is the non-living material between cells in a connective tissue. It may be fluid, jelly-like, soft, or hard.

### Connective Tissues and Their Matrix

| Connective Tissue | Matrix | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Blood** | Fluid plasma | Transports gases, nutrients, hormones, wastes |
| **Bone** | Hard matrix with calcium and phosphorus compounds | Support, protection, strength |
| **Cartilage** | Soft jelly-like matrix | Flexibility and cushioning |
| **Tendon** | Tough fibrous matrix | Connects muscle to bone |
| **Ligament** | Elastic fibrous matrix | Connects bone to bone |

|---|---|

> [!NOTE]
> The red colour of blood is due to **haemoglobin**, an iron-rich protein present in RBCs.

RBCs live for about **4 months** and are replaced regularly.

## 3.3.3 — Can We Control Movement in Our Body?

Some movements are under conscious control. Others happen automatically.

| Movement Type | Meaning | Example | Muscle Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Voluntary movement** | Under conscious control | Running, writing, lifting objects | Skeletal muscle |
| **Involuntary movement** | Not under conscious control | Food movement in intestine, heartbeat | Smooth muscle, cardiac muscle |

---

### Skeletal Muscle

Skeletal muscles are attached to bones and help in voluntary movement.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell shape | Long, cylindrical fibres |
| Branching | Unbranched |
| Nuclei | Many nuclei |
| Striations | Present; light and dark bands |
| Control | Voluntary |
| Function | Movement of skeleton |

---

### Smooth Muscle

Smooth muscles are found in organs such as the stomach and intestine.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell shape | Spindle-shaped |
| Nuclei | Single nucleus |
| Striations | Absent |
| Control | Involuntary |
| Function | Slow, continuous movements such as digestion |

---

### Cardiac Muscle

Cardiac muscle is found only in the heart.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Cell shape | Cylindrical and branched |
| Nuclei | Single nucleus |
| Striations | Faint striations |
| Control | Involuntary |
| Special feature | Works rhythmically throughout life without fatigue |
| Function | Heartbeat |

### Skeletal vs Smooth vs Cardiac Muscle

| Feature | Skeletal Muscle | Smooth Muscle | Cardiac Muscle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Control | Voluntary | Involuntary | Involuntary |
| Location | Attached to bones | Walls of internal organs | Heart |
| Shape | Long cylindrical fibres | Spindle-shaped cells | Branched cylindrical fibres |
| Nucleus | Many | One | One |
| Striations | Present | Absent | Faint |
| Main function | Body movement | Organ movement | Heartbeat |

## 3.3.4 — How Does the Body Sense, Communicate, and Respond?

When you touch something hot, your hand moves away quickly. When you remember a song, your brain recalls stored information.

These functions depend on **nervous tissue**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Nervous tissue** forms the body's control and coordination network. It receives, processes, and transmits messages.

The brain acts as the control centre. Muscles do not work independently; they receive signals from nervous tissue.

### Neuron

The cells of nervous tissue are called **neurons** or **nerve cells**.

> [!NOTE]
> A **neuron** is a specialised nerve cell that receives, processes, and transmits messages.

| Part of Neuron | Function |
|---|---|
| **Cell body** | Contains nucleus; controls cell activities |
| **Dendrites** | Receive signals from other neurons |
| **Axon** | Long fibre that carries messages away from cell body |
| **Axon terminals** | Pass messages to other cells |

### Message Pathway in a Neuron

| Step | Direction of Message |
|---|---|
| **1** | Dendrites receive signal |
| **2** | Cell body processes signal |
| **3** | Axon carries signal |
| **4** | Axon terminals transmit signal to another cell |

## 3.4 — The Musculoskeletal System

The **musculoskeletal system** is made of bones, muscles, joints, cartilage, tendons, and ligaments.

> [!NOTE]
> The **musculoskeletal system** helps the body stand upright, move, maintain posture, and protect delicate organs.

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| **Bones** | Support and protection |
| **Muscles** | Produce movement |
| **Joints** | Allow movement between bones |
| **Cartilage** | Cushions joints |
| **Tendons** | Attach muscle to bone |
| **Ligaments** | Attach bone to bone |

The musculoskeletal system works under the control of the nervous system.

Muscles pull on bones to produce movement. Tendons transmit the pulling force of muscles to bones. Movement usually occurs at joints.

## 3.4.1 — The Musculoskeletal System in Action

Different body parts move differently because they have different types of joints.

> [!NOTE]
> A **joint** is a junction between two or more bones.

Joints allow movement, but joints do not move bones by themselves. Bones move when muscles contract and pull through tendons.

## 3.5.1 — Ball and Socket Joint

The shoulder joint allows movement in many directions because the rounded head of the upper arm bone fits into a shallow socket of the shoulder bone.

> [!NOTE]
> A **ball and socket joint** allows movement in many directions, including forward, backward, sideways, and circular movement.

| Joint | Movement |
|---|---|
| Shoulder | Free arm movement in many directions |
| Hip | Leg movement in many directions |

The shoulder, together with the collarbone, forms the shoulder girdle, which connects the arm to the skeleton.

---

## 3.5.2 — Hinge Joint

The elbow bends and straightens like a door hinge.

> [!NOTE]
> A **hinge joint** allows movement mainly in one plane.

| Joint | Movement |
|---|---|
| Elbow | Bending and straightening |
| Knee | Bending and straightening |
| Ankle | Up-down movement |

The knee contains a small bone called the **kneecap**, which protects the joint.

---

## 3.5.3 — Pivot Joint

When you shake your head to say "no", your neck uses a pivot joint.

> [!NOTE]
> A **pivot joint** allows side-to-side turning movement.

The skull is connected to the backbone through a pivot joint, allowing the head to turn.

---

## 3.5.4 — Fixed Joints

The skull protects the brain, eyes, and ears. The bones of the skull are connected by **fixed joints**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Fixed joints** do not allow movement. They provide protection and stability.

| Joint Type | Movement | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Ball and socket** | Many directions | Shoulder, hip |
| **Hinge** | One plane | Elbow, knee |
| **Pivot** | Side-to-side rotation | Neck |
| **Fixed** | No movement | Skull bones |

## 🩺 Science in Medicine — Bone Marrow Transplant

Bone marrow contains special cells called **stem cells**. These cells can divide and produce new blood cells.

In a **bone marrow transplant**, stem cells from a healthy person may be given to a patient whose own bone marrow cannot produce healthy blood cells properly.

| Medical Context | Why Bone Marrow Stem Cells Matter |
|---|---|
| **Leukemia** | Blood-forming cells become cancerous; healthy stem cells may help restore blood cell formation after treatment |
| **Thalassemia** | Blood cells or haemoglobin are affected; healthy marrow cells can help produce healthier blood cells |
| **Blood cell replacement** | Stem cells can divide and form different kinds of blood cells |

> [!NOTE]
> This example connects tissue study with human welfare. Understanding tissues is not only about classification; it also helps medicine, diagnosis, treatment, and biotechnology.

## 3.6 — Skeletal System

The skeletal system is the framework of bones in the body.

> [!NOTE]
> The **skeletal system** provides support, strength, protection, posture, and movement support.

Major parts include:

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| **Skull** | Protects brain, eyes, and ears |
| **Vertebral column / backbone / spine** | Supports body and protects spinal cord |
| **Rib cage** | Protects heart and lungs |
| **Limbs** | Enable movement |

### Vertebral Column

The backbone is made of small bones called **vertebrae**.

Between vertebrae are cartilage discs that act as cushions. These discs allow flexibility, so we can bend and twist without injuring the spinal cord.

### Rib Cage

Humans have **12 pairs of ribs**. Together, they form the rib cage.

| Feature | Function |
|---|---|
| Ribs attached to spine at back | Support and anchoring |
| Ribs attached to sternum at front | Forms protective cage |
| Flexible cartilage | Allows expansion and contraction during breathing |
| Rib cage movement | Helps air move in and out of lungs |

Injury to ribs can make breathing painful and difficult.

## Think as a Scientist — From One Cell to an Organism: Totipotency

In 1958, **F. C. Steward** showed that even single cells from vascular phloem of carrot could regenerate whole plants under suitable conditions.

He grew carrot phloem cells in a nutrient medium containing simple sugars and hormones. Under appropriate conditions, these cells divided and formed a mass of cells. The cells then differentiated into a complete plant.

> [!NOTE]
> **Totipotency** is the ability of a cell to divide and differentiate into a complete organism under suitable conditions.

### Key Processes in Steward's Experiment

| Process | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Dedifferentiation** | Mature cells regain the ability to divide |
| **Cell division** | Cells multiply |
| **Redifferentiation** | Cells become specialised again |
| **Regeneration** | A complete plant develops |

### Steward's Carrot Cell Culture: Concept Flow

| Step | What Happened |
|---|---|
| **1** | Small fragments of carrot root were cultured in nutrient medium |
| **2** | Stirring caused single cells to separate into the liquid |
| **3** | Single cells began to divide |
| **4** | Embryonic plant developed |
| **5** | Plantlet was grown on agar medium |
| **6** | Plantlet was later planted in soil |
| **7** | Adult plant developed |

### Effect of Light, Air, and Nutrient Medium

| Light | Air | Nutrient Medium | Effect on Fresh Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Present | Absent | Solid medium + nutrients | Reduced |
| Present | Present | Liquid medium + nutrients | 20% increased |
| Absent | Present | Liquid medium + nutrients | Reduced |

> [!TIP]
> Plant cells often show greater regeneration ability than animal cells. This is why plant tissue culture is widely used in agriculture and biotechnology.

## 🌉 Bridging Science and Society — Crown Gall Disease and Genetic Engineering

Plant pathologists observed a disease called **crown gall disease**, in which tumour-like swellings develop on plant stems due to rapid and uncontrolled cell division.

The disease is caused by the bacterium **Agrobacterium tumefaciens**.

Instead of only trying to cure the disease, scientists studied how the bacterium transfers genetic material into plant cells. This knowledge later became useful in plant tissue culture and genetic engineering.

Today, **Agrobacterium** is used as a tool to introduce useful genes into plants.

Applications include:

| Application | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Improved crops | Better yield or quality |
| Disease-resistant plants | Reduced crop loss |
| Production of valuable phytochemicals | Useful plant-based chemicals |
| Genetic engineering | Introduction of useful genes |

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **All plant cells keep dividing forever.** | Only meristematic cells actively divide; many cells differentiate into permanent tissues. |
| **Permanent tissues are useless because they do not divide.** | Permanent tissues perform specialised functions such as support, protection, transport, and storage. |
| **Xylem is fully living tissue.** | Most xylem components are dead; xylem parenchyma is living. |
| **Phloem and xylem transport the same material.** | Xylem transports water/minerals; phloem transports food. |
| **All connective tissues are hard.** | Blood is a fluid connective tissue; bone is hard; cartilage is flexible. |
| **Tendons and ligaments are the same.** | Tendons connect muscle to bone; ligaments connect bone to bone. |
| **All muscles are under conscious control.** | Skeletal muscles are voluntary; smooth and cardiac muscles are involuntary. |
| **All joints allow movement.** | Fixed joints, such as those in the skull, do not allow movement. |
| **Animals can regenerate whole bodies from most mature cells like plants.** | Plant cells often show greater totipotency; animal cells are more restricted. |
