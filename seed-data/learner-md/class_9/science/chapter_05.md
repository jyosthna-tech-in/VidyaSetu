## 🌍 Introduction — Why Study Mixtures?

Many important everyday and industrial processes depend on separating mixtures.

Think about these questions:

| Question | Hidden Science |
|---|---|

| How can ink colours be separated? | Paper chromatography |

Mixtures are everywhere: air, soil, milk, blood, seawater, medicines, paints, food, perfumes, fuels, and sewage water.

> [!NOTE]
> A **mixture** contains two or more substances physically combined. The components can often be separated using physical methods.

## 5.1 — How Can We Classify Mixtures?

Mixtures can be classified by how uniformly their components are distributed.

### Homogeneous Mixtures

> [!NOTE]
> A **homogeneous mixture** has uniform composition throughout.

Examples:

| Homogeneous Mixture | Components |
|---|---|
| Sugar solution | Sugar and water |
| Salt solution | Salt and water |
| Vinegar | Acetic acid in water |
| Soda / aerated drink | Carbon dioxide dissolved in water |
| Brass | Copper and zinc |

In a well-stirred sugar solution, the first sip and last sip are equally sweet because the sugar is uniformly distributed.

### Heterogeneous Mixtures

> [!NOTE]
> A **heterogeneous mixture** does not have uniform composition throughout.

Examples:

| Heterogeneous Mixture | Why It Is Heterogeneous |
|---|---|
| Sand and water | Sand particles are visible and settle |
| Oil and water | Separate layers form |
| Smoke | Solid particles dispersed in air |
| Fog | Tiny liquid droplets dispersed in air |
| Muddy water | Solid particles suspended in water |

### Homogeneous vs Heterogeneous

| Feature | Homogeneous Mixture | Heterogeneous Mixture |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | Uniform throughout | Not uniform |
| Visible parts | Usually not visible separately | Often visible or separable |
| Examples | Salt solution, vinegar, brass | Muddy water, oil-water, smoke |
| Layers possible? | No | Yes, in some cases |

## 5.2 — Solutions

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture.

> [!NOTE]
> A **solute** is the substance that dissolves. A **solvent** is the substance that dissolves the solute.

Examples:

| Solution | Solute | Solvent |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar solution | Sugar | Water |
| Salt solution | Salt | Water |
| Vinegar | Acetic acid | Water |
| Soda water | Carbon dioxide | Water |

> [!TIP]
> In most common liquid solutions, the component present in larger amount is the solvent.

---

## 5.2.1 — Concentration of a Solution

Two solutions can contain the same solute and solvent but in different proportions.

For example, Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) must contain correct amounts of salt and sugar in a fixed amount of water. If the amounts are wrong, the solution is not proper ORS.

Similarly, pesticides must be mixed in the correct proportion with water. Too little pesticide may not protect crops; too much may harm crops, soil, water, and the environment.

> [!NOTE]
> **Concentration** is the amount of solute dissolved in a given amount of solvent or solution.

### Why Concentration Matters

| Field | Why Correct Concentration Matters |
|---|---|
| Medicine | Dose and safety depend on concentration |
| Agriculture | Pesticide or fertiliser strength must be controlled |
| Food | Taste, preservation, nutrition, and safety |
| Cosmetics | Effectiveness and skin safety |
| Laboratory work | Experiments need reproducible amounts |
| Household use | Cleaning solutions, tea, juices, syrups |

### Meet a Scientist — Dilip Mahalanabis

**Dilip Mahalanabis**, an Indian paediatrician, developed and implemented ORS treatment for dehydration caused by diseases such as diarrhoea and cholera. ORS became a life-saving rehydration therapy after global adoption.

## 5.2.2 — How Do We Express Concentration?

One common way to express concentration is **percentage**.

This chapter uses three percentage methods:

| Method | Used When | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Mass by mass percentage** | Solid mixture or mass-based composition | grams solute per 100 g solution |
| **Mass by volume percentage** | Solute mass dissolved to make a measured volume of solution | grams solute per 100 mL solution |
| **Volume by volume percentage** | Two miscible liquids | mL solute per 100 mL solution |

---

### A. Mass by Mass Percentage

Mass by mass percentage tells how many grams of solute are present in 100 grams of solution.

$$
\text{Mass by mass percentage}
=
\frac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Mass of solution}}
\times
100
\tag{5.1}
$$

It is written as `% m/m` or `% w/w`.

> [!NOTE]
> In many industries, weight by weight percentage `% w/w` is used. Numerically, `% m/m` and `% w/w` are treated the same at this level.

#### Example 5.1 — Salt Solution

If 10 g salt is dissolved in 90 g water:

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---:|
| Mass of solute | 10 g |
| Mass of solvent | 90 g |
| Mass of solution | 100 g |

$$
\%\,m/m
=
\frac{10}{100}
\times
100
=
10\%
$$

---

### B. Mass by Volume Percentage

Mass by volume percentage tells how many grams of solute are present in 100 mL of solution.

$$
\text{Mass by volume percentage}
=
\frac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}}
\times
100
\tag{5.2}
$$

It is written as `% m/v` or `% w/v`.

This is common in medicines and laboratories because liquid volume is often easier to measure than liquid mass.

#### Example 5.2 — Glucose Solution

If 5 g glucose is dissolved in water to make 100 mL solution:

$$
\%\,m/v
=
\frac{5}{100}
\times
100
=
5\%
$$

So the solution is **5% m/v glucose solution**.

---

### C. Volume by Volume Percentage

Volume by volume percentage tells how many millilitres of solute are present in 100 mL of solution.

$$
\text{Volume by volume percentage}
=
\frac{\text{Volume of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution}}
\times
100
\tag{5.3}
$$

It is written as `% v/v`.

This is useful when two miscible liquids are mixed, such as in perfumes, cosmetics, vinegar, and pesticide sprays.

#### Example 5.3 — Pesticide Spray

If 1 mL liquid pesticide is mixed with water to make 100 mL spray:

$$
\%\,v/v
=
\frac{1}{100}
\times
100
=
1\%
$$

So the pesticide spray is **1% v/v**.

## 5.2.3 — Solubility of Substances

> [!NOTE]
> **Solubility** is the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a fixed amount of solvent at a given temperature.

The solvent quantity is often taken as **100 g** or **100 mL**.

> [!NOTE]
> A **saturated solution** is a solution that cannot dissolve any more solute at a given temperature.

### Why Temperature Is Mentioned

Solubility depends on temperature.

| Type of Solute | General Effect of Increasing Temperature |
|---|---|
| Solid dissolved in liquid | Solubility usually increases |
| Gas dissolved in liquid | Solubility usually decreases |

Example:

| Example | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Hot water dissolves more of many solids | Useful in crystallization |
| Warm soda loses fizz faster | Gas solubility decreases with temperature |

### Saline Drip

A common saline drip is **0.9% m/v sodium chloride** in water.

This means:

$$
0.9\text{ g sodium chloride}
\text{ is present in }
100\text{ mL solution}
$$

This concentration is safe for blood and helps replace lost fluids.

### 🔬 Activity 5.2 — Reading a Solubility Curve

A graph of solubility versus temperature is called a **solubility curve**.

| Axis | Quantity |
|---|---|
| X-axis | Temperature in °C |
| Y-axis | Solubility in g per 100 g water |

Questions to answer from a solubility curve:

| Question | What to Look For |

| Does solubility increase with temperature? | See whether curve rises |
| Which compound's solubility increases more sharply? | Compare steepness |
| What happens when a hot saturated solution cools? | Excess solute may crystallize out |

## 5.3 — Separation of Components of Homogeneous Mixtures

Homogeneous mixtures can often be separated by using physical properties such as:

| Property | Separation Method |
|---|---|
| Different solubility at different temperatures | Crystallization |
| Different boiling points | Distillation |
| Different movement rates through paper and solvent | Paper chromatography |

---

## 5.3.1 — Crystallization

If a hot saturated solution is cooled, the solubility of the solute may decrease. The extra solute separates out as crystals.

> [!NOTE]
> **Crystallization** is the process of forming crystals from a saturated solution.

> [!NOTE]
> A **crystal** is a solid whose particles are arranged in a regular geometric pattern.

### Everyday Crystals

| Crystal | Where You May See It |
|---|---|
| Rock salt crystals | Salt deposits |
| Sugar crystals | Mishri / candy sugar |
| Snowflakes | Freezing water vapour |
| Frost | Ice crystals on cold surfaces |
| Quartz | Natural mineral deposits |

### Principle of Crystallization

Crystallization depends on the difference in solubility at different temperatures.

Example:

| Temperature | Solubility of Compound B |
|---|---:|
| 60 °C | 287 g per 100 g water |
| 40 °C | 241 g per 100 g water |

If a saturated solution at 60 °C is cooled to 40 °C:

$$
\text{Mass crystallized}
=
287-241
=
46\text{ g}
$$

### Why Crystallization Is Useful

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Purification of solids | Impurities remain in solution or are filtered out |
| Separation of a solid from solution | Pure crystals can be obtained |
| Preparation of well-shaped crystals | Slow cooling allows better crystal growth |

### 🔬 Activity 5.3 — Preparing Copper Sulfate Crystals

**Aim:** To prepare crystals from a saturated copper sulfate solution.

#### Safety

| Substance / Step | Safety Point |
|---|---|
| Copper sulfate | Toxic; do not touch with bare hands |
| Dilute sulfuric acid | Teacher should handle; corrosive |
| Heating | Use water bath and supervision |

#### Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Take 1 g copper sulfate in a 100 mL beaker. |
| **2** | Add 25 mL water and one drop of dilute sulfuric acid. |
| **3** | Heat gently in a water bath while stirring. |
| **4** | Add more copper sulfate gradually until solution becomes saturated. |
| **5** | Filter the hot solution to remove insoluble impurities. |
| **6** | Collect filtrate in a clean beaker and cover with a watch glass. |
| **7** | Allow solution to cool slowly without disturbance. |
| **8** | Filter crystals, rinse with cold water, and dry on watch glass. |

#### Why Slow Cooling Helps

| Cooling Type | Crystal Quality |
|---|---|
| Slow cooling at room temperature | Larger, shiny, well-shaped crystals |
| Rapid cooling in ice-cold water | Smaller, less well-formed crystals |

> [!TIP]
> Slow cooling gives particles time to arrange into a regular crystal structure.

Salt crystals can be obtained from seawater by evaporation.

| Stage | What Happens |
|---|---|

| Solution becomes concentrated | More salt remains per amount of water |
| Saturated solution forms | No more salt can remain dissolved |
| Salt crystals form | Salt separates as solid crystals |

| Salt Type | Traditional Method |
|---|---|
| **Panga salt** | Obtained by boiling concentrated sea brines |

## 5.3.2 — Distillation

Sometimes we want to recover the solvent, not just the solute. Distillation helps.

> [!NOTE]
> **Distillation** is a method of separating components of a liquid mixture by vaporising the lower-boiling component and then condensing it back to liquid.

### When Distillation Is Used

| Mixture Type | Example |
|---|---|
| Two miscible liquids with sufficiently different boiling points | Acetone and water |
| Liquid containing dissolved solid | Salt solution, if water is to be recovered |

The boiling point difference should usually be about **25 °C or more** for simple distillation to work well.

### Distillation of Acetone and Water

| Liquid | Boiling Point |
|---|---:|
| Acetone | About 56 °C |
| Water | 100 °C |

Since acetone has a lower boiling point, it vaporises first. Its vapours pass through a condenser, cool, and become liquid acetone again.

### Main Parts of Distillation Setup

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Distillation flask | Holds mixture to be heated |
| Thermometer | Monitors temperature |
| Condenser | Cools vapour into liquid |
| Water inlet | Brings cold water into condenser |
| Water outlet | Removes warmed water |
| Receiver / conical flask | Collects distillate |
| Burner / heat source | Supplies heat |

### Distillation Flow

| Step | Process |
|---|---|
| **1** | Heat mixture |
| **2** | Lower-boiling liquid vaporises |
| **3** | Vapour enters condenser |
| **4** | Vapour cools and condenses |
| **5** | Distillate is collected separately |
| **6** | Higher-boiling component remains behind |

### Fractional Distillation

> [!NOTE]
> **Fractional distillation** separates components of a mixture whose boiling points are close to each other.

Raw petroleum is separated into useful fractions such as:

| Fraction | Use |
|---|---|
| Petroleum gas | LPG fuel |
| Petrol | Vehicles |
| Kerosene / aviation fuel | Fuel |
| Diesel | Vehicles and generators |
| Lubricating oil | Lubrication |
| Bitumen | Road surfacing |

### India’s Scientific Contributions — Mitti ka Ittar

Kannauj in Uttar Pradesh is known as the perfume capital of India. The pleasant earthy smell after the first rain is captured as **Mitti ka Ittar** using a traditional distillation method called the **Deg-Bhapka method**.

## 5.3.3 — Paper Chromatography

Have you seen ink spread when water falls on paper? Black ink may separate into different colours.

> [!NOTE]
> **Paper chromatography** separates components of a mixture because different components move at different rates through paper with a solvent.

### Activity 5.5 — Separating Ink Colours

**Aim:** To separate colours present in black sketch pen ink.

#### Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Take a strip of chromatography paper or filter paper. |
| **2** | Draw a pencil line 2 cm from the bottom. |
| **3** | Put a black ink spot at the centre of the line. |
| **4** | Add a thin layer of water to a jar or beaker. |
| **5** | Place the paper vertically so the lower end dips in water. |
| **6** | Keep the water level below the ink spot. |
| **7** | Observe the solvent rising and colours separating. |

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The solvent level must remain **below** the sample spot. If the spot dips directly into the solvent, the sample may dissolve into the liquid instead of separating on paper.

### Why Separation Happens

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Solubility in solvent | More soluble components move faster |
| Attraction to paper | More strongly held components move slower |
| Movement with solvent | Components separate into bands or spots |

### Uses

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| Separate dyes | Black ink into colours |
| Separate plant pigments | Spinach leaf extract |
| Study food colours | Green, orange, yellow colours |
| Separate flower pigments | Petal extracts |

> [!NOTE]
> The word **chromatography** comes from Greek words meaning "colour" and "to write".

## 5.4.1 — Separation of Two Immiscible Liquids

> [!NOTE]
> **Immiscible liquids** are liquids that do not mix with each other and form separate layers.

Examples:

| Mixture | Layers |
|---|---|
| Oil and water | Oil usually upper layer; water lower layer |
| Mustard oil and water | Mustard oil upper layer; water lower layer |

### Separating Funnel

A separating funnel is used to separate immiscible liquids based on different densities.

#### Activity 5.6 — Separating Mustard Oil and Water

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Pour 5 mL mustard oil and 20 mL water into a separating funnel. |
| **2** | Let the mixture stand undisturbed. |
| **3** | Observe two layers: oil above, water below. |
| **4** | Open stopcock slowly and collect lower water layer. |
| **5** | Close stopcock near the boundary. |
| **6** | Discard any mixed boundary portion if needed. |
| **7** | Collect oil separately. |

> [!TIP]
> The denser liquid forms the lower layer. Water is denser than mustard oil, so water comes out first.

### Heterogeneous Mixtures with Gas

Most gas-gas mixtures are homogeneous because gases mix easily. However, mixtures containing solids or liquids dispersed in gases may be heterogeneous.

| Mixture | Components |
|---|---|
| Smoke | Solid particles in air |
| Fog | Liquid water droplets in air |
| Dusty air | Solid dust particles in air |

## 5.4.2 — Sublimation

Some solids change directly into vapour on heating without becoming liquid.

> [!NOTE]
> **Sublimation** is the change of a solid directly into vapour below its melting point, without passing through the liquid state.

> [!NOTE]
> **Deposition** is the change of vapour directly into solid without becoming liquid.

### Activity 5.7 — Separating Camphor and Sand

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Put crushed camphor and sand in a china dish. |
| **2** | Place an inverted funnel over the dish. |
| **3** | Plug funnel stem with cotton. |
| **4** | Heat gently. |
| **5** | Camphor sublimes and vapour rises. |
| **6** | Camphor deposits on the cooler inner funnel wall. |
| **7** | Sand remains in the china dish. |

### Sublimable Substances

| Substance | Use / Example |
|---|---|
| Camphor | Sublimes on heating |
| Naphthalene | Used in mothballs |
| Dry ice | Solid carbon dioxide used for cooling |

### Sublimation vs Evaporation

| Feature | Sublimation | Evaporation |
|---|---|---|
| Starting state | Solid | Liquid |
| Ending state | Vapour | Vapour |
| Liquid stage involved? | No | Yes |
| Example | Camphor to vapour | Water drying from a surface |

## Alloys

Metals do not normally dissolve in each other at room temperature. But when melted at high temperature, metals can mix and then solidify into a uniform mixture.

> [!NOTE]
> An **alloy** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.

Examples:

| Alloy | Approximate Composition |
|---|---|
| Brass | 80% copper, 20% zinc |
| Bronze | 80% copper, 20% tin |
| Stainless steel | Iron with carbon, chromium, nickel, molybdenum |

Physical methods cannot easily separate the components of an alloy.

## 5.4.3 — Suspensions

> [!NOTE]
> A **suspension** is a heterogeneous mixture in which undissolved solid particles remain suspended in the medium.

Examples:

| Suspension | Suspended Particles |
|---|---|
| Sand in water | Sand particles |
| Sawdust in water | Sawdust particles |
| Tea leaves in water | Tea leaf particles |
| Muddy water | Mud particles |

### Properties of Suspensions

| Property | Suspension |
|---|---|
| Nature | Heterogeneous |
| Particle size | Large, more than 1000 nm |
| Visibility | Particles visible to naked eye |
| Settling | Particles settle on standing |
| Filtration | Often separable by filtration |
| Tyndall effect | Shows scattering of light |

## Separating Fine Suspended Particles

Sometimes filtration alone is not enough. Muddy water may still look cloudy after filtering because very fine particles remain.

Two useful techniques are:

| Technique | Main Idea |
|---|---|
| **Centrifugation** | Rapid spinning separates heavier particles |
| **Coagulation** | Small particles clump together and settle |

---

### A. Centrifugation

> [!NOTE]
> **Centrifugation** separates heavier particles from lighter liquid by spinning the mixture at high speed.

During centrifugation:

| Component | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Heavier particles | Move outward and settle |
| Lighter liquid | Remains above |

Uses:

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| Medical laboratories | Separating blood components |
| Chemical industries | Separating suspended particles |
| Rural health technology | Paperfuge for blood sample separation |

### Bridging Science and Society — Paperfuge

A **paperfuge** is a hand-powered centrifuge inspired by a common spinning toy. It can spin samples at high speed without electricity. Such low-cost tools can help detect diseases such as malaria and anaemia in remote areas.

### Activity 5.8 — Make a Centrifuge Model

| Material | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Cardboard disc | Rotating body |
| Thick thread | Spinning mechanism |
| Small sample holder | Model of centrifuge tube |

This model helps show how heavier particles move outward during rapid spinning.

---

### B. Coagulation

> [!NOTE]
> **Coagulation** is the process in which small suspended particles clump together to form larger particles that can settle.

Example: Add powdered alum to muddy water.

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Alum is added | Fine particles begin clumping |
| Clumps become larger | They settle due to gravity |
| Clearer water remains above | Water can be decanted or filtered |

Everyday example:

| Process | Coagulant |
|---|---|
| Paneer formation from milk | Lemon juice or vinegar |

## 5.4.4 — Colloids

Blood, milk, tomato sauce, and ice cream are not true solutions. Their particles are larger than solution particles, but they do not settle like suspensions.

> [!NOTE]
> A **colloid** is a mixture with particle size between that of a solution and a suspension. Colloid particles do not settle on standing and can scatter light.

### Particle Size Comparison

| Mixture Type | Particle Size |
|---|---|
| Solution | Less than 1 nm |
| Colloid | 1–1000 nm |
| Suspension | More than 1000 nm |

### Blood as a Colloid

Blood is not a simple solution or true suspension. Blood components are too small to see with the naked eye, and they do not settle like sand in water under normal conditions.

Blood can be separated into components by centrifugation:

| Blood Component | Role |
|---|---|
| Plasma | Liquid medium |
| Red blood cells | Oxygen transport |
| White blood cells | Defence |
| Platelets | Clotting |

### Bridging Science and Society — Blood Donation

Blood donation saves lives. Donated blood is tested, grouped, separated into components, stored safely in blood banks, and supplied to patients during emergencies, surgeries, and serious illnesses.

The body replaces donated blood naturally within a few weeks.

## 5.5 — Tyndall Effect

In Activity 5.1, the laser path was:

| Mixture | Was Path Visible? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Salt solution | No | Particles too small to scatter light visibly |
| Chalk suspension | Yes | Large suspended particles scatter light |
| Milk-water colloid | Yes | Colloid particles scatter light |

> [!NOTE]
> The **Tyndall effect** is the scattering of light by particles in a colloid or suspension.

It was first explained by scientist **John Tyndall**.

### Everyday Examples of Tyndall Effect

| Example | Scattering Particles |
|---|---|
| Sunlight beam visible in dusty room | Dust and smoke particles |
| Sun rays through dense trees | Dust, mist, or tiny particles in air |
| Floodlights in stadium | Dust or water droplets in air |
| Hazy polluted city air | Smoke and dust particles |
| Foggy headlights | Water droplets |

> [!TIP]
> Transparent true solutions do not show the Tyndall effect. Colloids and suspensions do.

## Dispersed Phase and Dispersion Medium

In a colloid:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Dispersed phase** | Solute-like component; dispersed particles |
| **Dispersion medium** | Solvent-like component in which particles are dispersed |

Example:

| Colloid | Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium |
|---|---|---|
| Milk | Fat droplets | Water-based liquid |
| Fog | Water droplets | Air |
| Smoke | Solid particles | Air |
| Blood | Cells and proteins | Plasma |

---

## Emulsions

Some colloids have both dispersed phase and dispersion medium as liquids. These are called **emulsions**.

> [!NOTE]
> An **emulsion** is a colloid in which one liquid is dispersed in another liquid.

### Types of Emulsions

| Type | Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Oil-in-water emulsion** | Oil | Water | Milk, vanishing cream |
| **Water-in-oil emulsion** | Water | Oil | Butter, cold cream, body lotion |

Emulsions may need **emulsifying agents** for stability. Proteins in milk and butter act as emulsifying agents.

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **All mixtures are visibly non-uniform.** | Homogeneous mixtures appear uniform throughout. |
| **Milk is a true solution.** | Milk is a colloid. |
| **Colloids settle like suspensions.** | Colloid particles do not settle on standing. |
| **Solutions show Tyndall effect.** | True solutions do not show Tyndall effect. |
| **Evaporation and crystallization are the same.** | Evaporation removes solvent; crystallization forms pure crystals from saturated solution. |
| **Distillation can separate any two liquids.** | Simple distillation works well when boiling points differ sufficiently. |
| **The solvent level in chromatography can cover the ink spot.** | The solvent level must remain below the spot. |
| **Sublimation and evaporation are the same.** | Sublimation starts from a solid; evaporation starts from a liquid. |
| **Alloys are heterogeneous mixtures.** | Alloys are usually homogeneous mixtures. |
