## Big Idea

Every organism has a limited life span. Reproduction is the biological process that allows life to continue after individual organisms die.

There are two broad methods:

| Method | Parent Involvement | Offspring |
|---|---|---|
| **Asexual reproduction** | One parent | Usually genetically identical to parent. |
| **Sexual reproduction** | Two gametes, usually from two individuals | Genetically varied offspring. |

The chapter follows reproduction from simple organisms to flowering plants, animals, and humans.

## 11.0 - Introduction: Reproduction and Continuity of Life

Living beings are born, grow, mature, reproduce, and eventually die.

Reproduction ensures that life continues from one generation to the next.

Examples:

| Parent Organism | New Individual |
|---|---|
| Mango tree | New mango plants from seeds. |
| Cow | Calf |
| Dog | Puppy |
| Cat | Kitten |
| Human | Child |

### Why Reproduction Matters

Reproduction:

- maintains the population of a species;
- transfers genetic information to the next generation;
- allows variation in sexually reproducing organisms;
- helps species adapt over many generations.

### Asexual and Sexual Reproduction

| Feature | Asexual Reproduction | Sexual Reproduction |
|---|---|---|
| Number of parents | One | Usually two gametes; often two parents |
| Gamete fusion | Absent | Present |
| Speed | Usually faster | Usually slower |
| Genetic similarity | Offspring are almost identical to parent | Offspring show variation |
| Common in | Bacteria, amoeba, yeast, hydra, many plants | Flowering plants, most animals |
| Advantage | Rapid multiplication | Variation and better adaptability |
| Limitation | Low variation | Requires more energy and coordination |

## 11.1 - Asexual Reproduction

### Main Concept

Asexual reproduction involves only one parent and produces offspring that are usually genetically identical to the parent.

This is common in:

- bacteria;
- amoeba;
- yeast;
- hydra;
- sponge;
- many plants.

### Why Offspring Are Identical

In asexual reproduction, the central cell division process is often mitosis.

Mitosis produces daughter cells with the same number of chromosomes and the same genetic information as the parent cell.

Therefore, asexual offspring are called **clones**.

---

## Asexual Reproduction in Plants: Vegetative Propagation

Vegetative propagation means new plants grow from vegetative parts such as:

- stem;
- root;
- leaf;
- bud.

### Examples

| Plant | Vegetative Part Used | How New Plant Forms |
|---|---|---|
| Potato | Underground stem tuber | Buds or "eyes" sprout into new plants. |
| Ginger | Underground stem rhizome | Buds develop into new shoots. |
| Money plant | Stem cutting | Stem grows roots and shoots. |
| Sugarcane | Stem cutting | Nodes produce roots and shoots. |
| Bryophyllum | Leaf | Leaf margins produce small plantlets. |

### Key Point

Vegetative propagation is asexual because only one parent plant is involved.

---

## 11.1.1 - How Vegetative Propagation Helps Agriculture

Farmers, gardeners, scientists, and horticulturists use vegetative propagation to multiply useful plants.

### Advantages

| Advantage | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Fast multiplication** | Many plants can be produced quickly. |
| **Uniform quality** | New plants retain desirable traits of the parent. |
| **Useful for seedless plants** | Plants that do not produce viable seeds can still be propagated. |
| **Early yield** | Some vegetatively propagated plants mature faster. |
| **Disease-free plantlets** | Tissue culture can produce healthy plantlets under controlled conditions. |

### Limitation

Because the plants are genetically similar, a disease that affects one plant may affect many others in the crop.

This activity asks learners to observe real propagation methods used by gardeners or farmers.

### Cutting

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Select a healthy shoot cutting. | The cutting should have nodes and internodes. |
| **2** | Remove leaves from the lower half. | Reduces water loss and prepares the cutting for planting. |
| **3** | Insert the cutting partly into compost-rich soil. | Nodes can form roots and shoots. |

| **5** | Observe growth. | New roots and shoots indicate successful propagation. |

### Grafting

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Stock** | Rooted plant that provides the root system. |
| **Scion** | Stem piece from another plant with desired traits. |
| **Graft union** | Joined region where stock and scion grow together. |

Grafting joins two plants so desirable features can be combined.

Example:

| Plant A | Plant B | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Rooted wild rose | Yellow or pink rose stem piece | A rooted plant carrying the desired rose variety. |

### Layering

Layering is done by bending a flexible branch and burying part of it under soil while it remains attached to the parent plant.

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Branch is buried partly in soil. | Buried region remains moist. |
| Roots develop from buried part. | Branch becomes capable of independent growth. |
| Branch is cut from parent. | New plant can grow separately. |

### Tissue Culture

Tissue culture uses small plant tissues to produce many plantlets under sterile and controlled conditions.

In banana farming, tissue culture can provide large numbers of healthy plantlets and reduce spread of virus-infected plants.

## Asexual Reproduction by Spores

Fungi such as bread mould reproduce by spores.

Spores are:

- tiny;
- usually single-celled;
- light enough to float in air;
- produced in huge numbers;
- able to germinate when moisture, warmth, and nutrients are available.

### Activity 11.3 - Bread Mould

| Step | Action | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Moisten bread or roti lightly. | Moisture supports mould growth. |
| **2** | Keep it in a moist chamber. | Prevents drying. |
| **3** | Keep it warm and dark. | Fungal spores grow well in warm, moist conditions. |
| **4** | Observe without touching directly. | Safety and hygiene. |
| **5** | Use a magnifying glass or microscope. | Thread-like hyphae and spore structures may be seen. |

### Where Does Bread Mould Come From?

Mould does not arise spontaneously from non-living matter. Spores already present in the air settle on the moist bread or roti and grow when conditions are suitable.

### Conditions for Mould Growth

| Condition | Effect |
|---|---|
| Moisture | Helps spores germinate. |
| Warmth, about 25-35 °C | Speeds growth. |
| Nutrients | Bread or roti provides food. |
| Low temperature | Slows or stops reproduction. |

This is why refrigeration helps preserve food.

## Louis Pasteur and Pre-Existing Life

For a long time, people believed living things could arise spontaneously from non-living matter.

Louis Pasteur's experiments helped show that new life comes from pre-existing life.

His work supported:

- germ theory of disease;
- the idea that cells arise from pre-existing cells;
- sterilisation of food and instruments.

## Mitosis and Clones in Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction usually depends on mitosis.

| Feature | Mitosis in Asexual Reproduction |
|---|---|
| Chromosome number | Remains the same. |
| Genetic information | Copied accurately. |
| Daughter cells | Genetically identical. |
| Offspring | Clones of parent. |

### Advantage

Asexual reproduction is fast and useful when environmental conditions are favourable.

### Risk

Low variation means a sudden disease or environmental change can affect many individuals similarly.

## 11.2 - Sexual Reproduction

### Main Concept

Sexual reproduction involves the formation and fusion of gametes.

The offspring receives genetic material from two gametes, usually from two different parents.

### Why a Special Cell Division Is Needed

If each parent gave the full chromosome number to the offspring, chromosome number would double every generation.

To prevent this, gametes are formed by meiosis.

Meiosis halves the chromosome number in gametes.

---

## 11.2.1 - How Meiosis Creates Variation

Each species has a fixed number of chromosomes.

Humans have:

$$
23\ \text{pairs of chromosomes} = 46\ \text{chromosomes}
$$

Gametes have:

$$
23\ \text{chromosomes}
$$

When sperm and egg fuse:

$$
23 + 23 = 46
$$

This restores the chromosome number in the zygote.

### Diploid and Haploid

| Term | Meaning | Human Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Diploid** | Full chromosome set in body cells | 46 chromosomes |
| **Haploid** | Half chromosome set in gametes | 23 chromosomes |

### Meiosis

Meiosis is a special type of cell division that:

- produces gametes;
- halves chromosome number;
- separates chromosomes from each pair;
- creates new combinations of genetic information.

### Why Variation Matters

Variation helps:

- individuals differ from parents and siblings;
- some individuals survive better in changing environments;
- species adapt over long periods;
- evolution occur across generations.

Examples of human variation:

- ability to tolerate low oxygen at high altitudes;
- ability of some adults to digest milk;
- differences in height, skin tone, hair form, and many other traits.

## Activity 11.6 - Investigating Pollination in Pea Plants

This activity uses flower covering and stamen removal to test whether pollen transfer is necessary for fruit formation.

### Main Idea

If pollen cannot reach the stigma, fruit formation may not occur.

### Treatments

| Treatment | What Is Done | Expected Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Flower bud covered with muslin cloth | Prevents outside pollen. | Tests whether self-pollination can occur inside bud. |
| Bud with stamens removed and covered | Removes pollen source before maturity. | Fruit should not form if pollen is unavailable. |
| Open flower with stamens removed and covered | Tests whether pollination already occurred. | Fruit may form if pollination happened before removal. |
| Fresh flower covered | Prevents outside pollen but leaves its stamens. | Tests self-pollination. |
| Fresh flower uncovered | Allows natural pollination. | Fruit formation likely. |

### Inference

The transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma is necessary for fruit formation.

## 11.2.3 - Pollination

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma.

### Types of Pollination

| Type | Description | Genetic Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-pollination** | Pollen reaches the stigma of the same flower or another flower on the same plant. | Less variation. |
| **Cross-pollination** | Pollen moves from one plant to another plant of the same type. | More variation. |

### Why Pollination Is Important

Pollination is needed before fertilisation in flowering plants. Without pollination:

- pollen may not reach the stigma;
- male gamete may not reach the egg;
- fertilisation may not occur;
- seeds and fruits may not form.

---

## 11.2.4 - Pollination Strategies and Reproductive Success

Plants use different agents for pollination.

| Pollination Agent | Plant Examples | Flower / Pollen Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Wind** | Wheat, maize, rice | Light pollen, large numbers of pollen grains, feathery stigma. |
| **Water** | Vallisneria, Hydrilla | Pollen carried by water currents. |
| **Insects** | Sunflower, hibiscus, marigold | Bright petals, fragrance, nectar, sticky or spiny pollen. |
| **Birds** | Coral tree, hibiscus | Showy flowers; nectar may attract birds such as sunbirds. |

### Wind vs Insect Pollination

| Feature | Wind Pollination | Insect Pollination |
|---|---|---|
| Pollen number | Very large | Fewer than wind-pollinated plants |
| Pollen size | Small and light | Larger, sticky, or spiny |
| Stigma | Often long and feathery | Often sticky |
| Efficiency | Many pollen grains wasted | More targeted transfer |
| Attraction | Not dependent on colour or fragrance | Often depends on colour, nectar, fragrance |

## 11.2.5 - Fertilisation and Seed Formation

After a compatible pollen grain lands on the stigma, the next stage begins.

### Sequence

| Step | Event |
|---|---|
| **1** | Pollen grain lands on stigma. |
| **2** | Pollen grain germinates. |
| **3** | Pollen tube grows down through style. |
| **4** | Male gamete travels through pollen tube. |
| **5** | Male gamete reaches ovule. |
| **6** | Male gamete fuses with egg cell. |
| **7** | Zygote forms. |
| **8** | Zygote develops into embryo. |
| **9** | Ovule becomes seed. |
| **10** | Ovary becomes fruit. |

### After Fertilisation

| Before Fertilisation | After Fertilisation |
|---|---|
| Egg cell | Zygote, then embryo |
| Ovule | Seed |
| Ovary | Fruit |

### Seed Dispersal

Seeds may be dispersed by:

- wind;
- water;
- animals.

When conditions such as water, air, and temperature are suitable, the seed germinates and grows into a new plant.

## 11.3 - Sexual Reproduction in Animals

Animals may reproduce sexually, asexually, or both depending on the organism. In many animals, sexual reproduction involves sperm and egg.

### External Fertilisation

External fertilisation occurs outside the body.

Examples:

- frogs;
- many fish;
- many aquatic animals.

Process:

1. female releases eggs into water;
2. male releases sperm over or near the eggs;
3. sperm and egg fuse outside the body.

### Internal Fertilisation

Internal fertilisation occurs inside the female body.

Examples:

- reptiles;
- birds;
- mammals.

### Comparison

| Feature | External Fertilisation | Internal Fertilisation |
|---|---|---|
| Place of fertilisation | Outside body, often in water | Inside female body |
| Number of eggs | Usually many | Usually fewer |
| Protection | Lower | Higher |
| Survival chances | Often lower | Often higher |
| Examples | Fish, frogs | Reptiles, birds, mammals |

---

## 11.4 - Variations in Reproduction in Animals

Animals use different reproductive strategies to ensure gametes meet and young ones survive.

### Reproductive Strategies

| Animal | Habitat | Fertilisation | Number of Eggs | Survival of Young Ones |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fish | Water | External | Hundreds to thousands | Low |
| Frog | Water / land | External | 5,000-50,000 | Low |
| Lizard | Land | Internal | 2-20 | Moderate |
| Bird | Water / land | Internal | 1-15 | Moderate to high |

### Why External Fertilisation Often Produces Many Eggs

Many eggs are produced because:

- eggs may be carried away by water currents;
- eggs or young ones may be eaten by predators;
- fertilisation is less protected;
- survival rate is low.

### Larval Stage

Some animals produce eggs with enough yolk only for early development. The young hatch as larvae and then feed actively.

Examples:

- butterfly larvae;
- frog tadpoles;
- many insects.

### Internal Development and Care

In birds and reptiles, eggs contain enough yolk to support development until hatching.

In mammals, the zygote develops inside the female body, and young ones are often nourished after birth by milk.

## 11.5.4 - How Reproductive Cells Are Made

The formation of gametes is called gametogenesis.

It occurs in:

- testes in males;
- ovaries in females.

Gametes are formed by meiosis.

### Chromosome Number

| Cell Type | Chromosome Number in Humans |
|---|---|
| Body cell | 46 |
| Sperm | 23 |
| Egg | 23 |
| Zygote | 46 |

### Sperm and Egg Comparison

| Feature | Sperm | Egg |
|---|---|---|
| Size | Very small | Large |
| Number produced | Millions | Few |
| Stored nutrients | Absent | Present |
| Motility | Actively motile | Non-motile |
| Genetic material | 23 chromosomes | 23 chromosomes |

This difference between male and female gametes is common in many animals.

## 11.5.5 - What Happens When a Sperm Meets an Egg?

### Ovulation

From puberty onwards, usually one mature egg is released from one ovary during a menstrual cycle.

This release is called ovulation.

### Fertilisation in Humans

| Step | Event |
|---|---|
| **1** | Ovary releases an egg. |
| **2** | Egg enters the oviduct. |
| **3** | Sperm enter the female reproductive tract during sexual intercourse. |
| **4** | Sperm swim toward the egg. |
| **5** | One sperm may fuse with the egg. |
| **6** | Zygote forms. |
| **7** | Zygote divides by mitosis while moving toward uterus. |
| **8** | Embryo implants in uterine lining. |
| **9** | Pregnancy begins. |

### Implantation

Implantation is the attachment of the developing embryo to the inner lining of the uterus.

The uterine lining is thick and rich in blood vessels, so it can nourish the embryo.

---

## IVF: In-Vitro Fertilisation

In-vitro fertilisation, or IVF, is a medical technique in which an egg and sperm are combined outside the female body, usually in a laboratory dish.

The fertilised egg is then placed in the uterus to begin a possible pregnancy.

### Important Clarification

The common phrase "test tube baby" is misleading. The process usually occurs in a culture dish, not inside a test tube.

### Indian Context

India has an important place in IVF history. In 1978, Subhash Mukhopadhyay of Kolkata carried out experimental IVF work that led to India's first test tube baby, Kanupriya Agarwal, also known as Durga.

---

## 11.5.6 - What Happens When an Egg Is Not Fertilised?

If the egg is not fertilised, it remains viable for about a day and then degenerates.

The thick uterine lining is no longer needed. It breaks down and leaves the body through the vagina along with some blood.

This process is called menstruation, or a period.

### Menstrual Cycle

The cycle usually repeats every 21-35 days, often around 28 days.

| Stage | Approximate Days | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Menstruation | Day 1-5 | Uterine lining is shed. |
| Lining rebuilds | Day 6-14 | Uterine lining gradually thickens; egg matures. |
| Ovulation | Around Day 14 in a typical 28-day cycle | Mature egg is released. |
| Lining becomes richer | Day 15-28 | Uterus prepares for possible implantation. |
| If no fertilisation | Around end of cycle | Lining breaks down and menstruation begins. |

### Important

Ovulation does not always happen exactly on day 14 for every person. Cycle length varies.

## Biological Sex Determination

Humans have sex chromosomes.

| Biological Sex Chromosome Pattern | Chromosomes |
|---|---|
| Typically female | XX |
| Typically male | XY |

The egg from the mother always carries an X chromosome.

The sperm from the father may carry:

- X chromosome; or
- Y chromosome.

Therefore:

| Egg | Sperm | Chromosome Pattern of Baby |
|---|---|---|
| X | X | XX |
| X | Y | XY |

Scientific conclusion:

> The sperm chromosome determines whether the zygote receives XX or XY chromosomes.

This is a biological mechanism and should never be used for blame or discrimination.

## 11.5.7 - Pregnancy and Childbirth

Human pregnancy lasts about nine months and is divided into three trimesters.

### Pregnancy Stages

| Stage | Main Development |
|---|---|
| **First trimester** | Fertilised egg develops into embryo; major organs begin forming. |
| **From about ninth week** | Developing embryo is called a foetus. |
| **Second trimester** | Foetus grows larger and stronger; mother may feel movements. |
| **Third trimester** | Baby grows rapidly and prepares for life outside the womb. |

### Childbirth

During childbirth, strong contractions of the uterus help push the baby through the birth canal.

If vaginal birth is not possible or safe, doctors may use medical or surgical procedures to deliver the baby safely.

### Newborn Care

Newborns need:

- warmth;
- gentle handling;
- breastfeeding when possible;
- vaccination on time;
- medical attention when needed.

Breast milk provides nutrition and helps protect infants from many diseases.

## 11.5.8 - Mother's Health During Pregnancy

A mother's health is closely connected to the baby's growth and safety.

Recommended care includes:

| Care Area | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Balanced diet | Provides proteins, vitamins, and minerals for mother and baby. |
| Regular check-ups | Tracks health and development. |
| Rest | Supports recovery and growth. |

| Avoid medicines without medical advice | Some medicines may affect pregnancy. |

### ASHA Workers

Accredited Social Health Activist workers, or ASHA workers, are trained community health workers under India's National Health Mission.

They support:

- maternal care;
- safe deliveries;
- immunisation;
- hygiene awareness;
- family planning;
- health advice in communities, especially rural areas.

### Post-Partum Depression

Some mothers experience anxiety, fatigue, low mood, or feeling overwhelmed after delivery.

This condition can be post-partum depression. It is a recognized health condition and can be treated. Mothers should be encouraged to speak with a doctor, nurse, ASHA worker, or other healthcare professional.

## 11.5.9 - What Does It Mean to Be Sexually Mature?

Sexual maturity means the body becomes capable of reproduction.

However:

- physical maturity is gradual;
- emotional maturity takes time;
- responsible decision-making is important;
- being biologically capable of reproduction does not mean a person is ready for adult responsibilities.

Emotional maturity includes:

- understanding feelings;
- communicating clearly;
- respecting boundaries;
- making thoughtful decisions;
- considering health, safety, and consequences.

## 11.5.10 - Preventing Unwanted Pregnancies and Infections

Responsible choices help prevent:

- unplanned pregnancies;
- sexually transmitted infections;
- harmful social and health consequences.

### Sexually Transmitted Infections

STIs can spread through sexual contact with an infected person.

Examples include:

- gonorrhoea;
- herpes;
- syphilis;
- genital warts;
- HIV, which can lead to AIDS.

Some STIs are difficult to cure or not curable yet, so prevention is important.

### Condoms

Condoms are barrier devices.

They help:

- reduce the spread of STIs;
- prevent sperm from reaching the egg;
- reduce the chance of pregnancy.

### Contraceptive Methods

| Method | How It Works |
|---|---|
| Condoms / barrier methods | Stop sperm from reaching egg; also reduce STI risk. |
| Oral contraceptive pills | Alter hormones and can affect egg release. |
| Intra-Uterine Devices such as Copper-T | Placed in uterus by a medical professional to prevent pregnancy. |
| Surgical methods | Block vas deferens in males or fallopian tubes in females so gametes cannot meet. |

### Important Comparison

| Method | Prevents Pregnancy? | Helps Reduce STI Risk? |
|---|---|---|
| Condoms | Yes | Yes |
| Oral pills | Yes | No |
| IUD | Yes | No |
| Surgical methods | Yes | No |

Therefore, if a couple uses oral pills but not condoms, pregnancy risk may be reduced, but STI risk remains.

## Abortion and Prenatal Sex Determination

Abortion is a medical procedure to end a pregnancy. The chapter notes that it is generally done only within the first trimester when legally and medically permitted.

Prenatal sex determination for sex-selective abortion is strictly prohibited by law in India.

Why?

- Sex-selective abortion can distort the sex ratio.
- It supports gender discrimination.
- A balanced sex ratio is important for a healthy society.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Asexual reproduction always happens only in unicellular organisms. | Many plants and some multicellular organisms also reproduce asexually. |
| Vegetative propagation produces genetically different plants. | It usually produces genetically identical plants. |
| Mould appears from non-living bread by itself. | Mould grows from spores already present in the air. |

## One-Page Summary

Reproduction is the biological process by which organisms produce new individuals of their own kind. It ensures continuity of life and transfer of genetic information.

Asexual reproduction involves one parent and usually produces genetically identical offspring called clones. It occurs through methods such as vegetative propagation in plants, budding in yeast and hydra, and spore formation in fungi. It is fast and useful in favourable conditions, but it produces little variation.

Sexual reproduction involves gametes and fertilisation. Meiosis forms gametes with half the chromosome number, so fertilisation restores the correct chromosome number in the zygote. Sexual reproduction creates variation, which helps species adapt over generations.

In flowering plants, the stamen produces pollen grains and the pistil contains the ovary and ovules. Pollination transfers pollen from anther to stigma. After pollination, a pollen tube carries the male gamete to the ovule. Fertilisation forms the zygote. The ovule becomes the seed and the ovary becomes the fruit.

Animals may show external or internal fertilisation. External fertilisation is common in many aquatic animals and usually involves many eggs with lower survival. Internal fertilisation offers more protection and often involves fewer young ones.

In humans, testes produce sperm and ovaries produce eggs. Fertilisation usually occurs in the oviduct. If fertilisation occurs, the zygote divides and implants in the uterus, beginning pregnancy. If fertilisation does not occur, the uterine lining is shed as menstruation.

Reproductive health includes menstrual hygiene, care during pregnancy, prevention of unwanted pregnancy, and protection from sexually transmitted infections. Condoms help reduce both pregnancy risk and STI transmission risk, while many other contraceptive methods mainly prevent pregnancy.
