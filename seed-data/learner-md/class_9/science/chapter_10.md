## Big Idea

Sound is produced when something vibrates.

Those vibrations disturb nearby particles of a material medium such as air, water, or metal. The particles do not travel from the source to the listener. Instead, they vibrate about their mean positions and pass the disturbance forward. This travelling disturbance is a sound wave.

The chapter connects four levels of understanding:

| Level | What You Observe | Scientific Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Daily life** | You hear speech, music, thunder, claps, and noise. | Vibrating sources produce sound. |
| **Particle level** | Air particles are alternately crowded and spread out. | Compressions and rarefactions move through the medium. |
| **Graph level** | Density varies periodically with distance or time. | Crests and troughs represent maximum and minimum density. |
| **Formula level** | Speed, wavelength, frequency, and time period are measurable. | Sound wave quantities are related mathematically. |

Everyday examples include:

|---|---|

|---|---|

## 10.1 - Production of Sound

### Main Concept

Sound is produced by vibrating objects.

A vibrating object moves to and fro repeatedly. This motion disturbs the nearby medium and creates sound waves.

### Simple Observations

| Observation | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| A plucked rubber band makes sound. | The stretched band vibrates. |
| Sound stops when the rubber band stops vibrating. | Sound depends on vibration. |
| A struck metal object rings. | Metal vibrates after being struck. |
| A flute produces sound when air is blown through it. | The air column inside vibrates. |
| Touching the throat while speaking shows vibrations. | Vocal cords vibrate to produce voice. |

### Activity 10.1 - Rubber Band and Cardboard Box

| Step | Action | What to Notice |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Stretch a rubber band across the open side of a cardboard box. | The box helps make the sound louder. |
| **2** | Pluck the rubber band. | Sound is heard. |
| **3** | Watch the rubber band carefully. | It vibrates while sound is produced. |
| **4** | Wait until the band stops vibrating. | Sound stops. |
| **5** | Change the tension and pluck again. | The nature of sound changes. |
| **6** | Remove the band from the box and pluck near the ear. | Sound is produced, but it may be less loud. |

### Conclusion from Activity 10.1

The activity shows:

- a vibrating object produces sound;
- sound stops when vibration stops;
- the loudness may depend on how vibrations are supported or amplified;
- changing tension can change the frequency and pitch of the sound.

### Vibration

Vibration means periodic to-and-fro motion about a mean position.

Examples:

| Vibrating Part | Source of Sound |
|---|---|
| Stretched string | Sitar, guitar, ektara, tanpura |
| Membrane | Tabla, mridangam, drum |
| Air column | Flute, bansuri, whistle |
| Metal body | Bell, tuning fork, metal plate |
| Vocal cords | Human voice |

### Source of Sound

The object or system that produces sound is called the **source** of sound.

Important point:

> The source must vibrate, but the vibration may not always be clearly visible to the eye.

## 10.2.1 - Sound Needs a Medium to Propagate

Sound cannot travel through vacuum.

A vacuum is a space with no matter, or almost no matter. Since sound needs particles of a medium to pass the disturbance forward, sound cannot propagate where there are no particles.

### Vacuum Bell Jar Experiment

| Stage | Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Electric bell rings in normal air. | Sound is clearly heard. | Air carries sound from bell to listener. |
| Air is pumped out gradually. | Sound becomes fainter. | Fewer particles are available to carry sound. |
| Near vacuum is reached. | Bell may be seen ringing, but almost no sound is heard. | Sound cannot travel without a medium. |
| Air is allowed back in. | Sound becomes louder again. | Restored air carries sound. |

### Astronaut Example

Outer space is close to vacuum. Therefore:

- astronauts cannot directly hear each other speaking through space;
- metal objects clanking outside a spacecraft cannot be heard directly through vacuum;
- astronauts communicate using devices in their suits, where sound is converted into electrical signals and transmitted.

### Assertion-Reason Check

| Assertion | Reason |
|---|---|
| We cannot hear the sound of a bell in a closed jar after most air is pumped out. | Sound requires a medium to travel. |

Correct understanding:

- The assertion is true.
- The reason is true.
- The reason correctly explains the assertion.

## 10.3 - Sound Waves

### Main Concept

Sound travels as a wave through a medium.

A sound wave is a travelling disturbance made of alternating compressions and rarefactions.

### Direction of Sound Propagation

Sound can spread in many directions from a source. For a small source producing sound equally in all directions, sound spreads out as spherical waves.

For easier understanding, the chapter first studies sound moving in one direction.

## Compressions and Rarefactions

Imagine a piston moving back and forth in a long tube filled with air.

### Compression

When the piston moves forward:

1. nearby air particles are pushed forward;
2. particles come closer together;
3. density increases in that region;
4. this high-density region moves ahead.

This high-density region is called a **compression**.

### Rarefaction

When the piston moves backward:

1. nearby air particles move backward;
2. particles become more spread out;
3. density decreases in that region;
4. this low-density region moves ahead.

This low-density region is called a **rarefaction**.

### Sound Wave

A sound wave is a series of alternating compressions and rarefactions travelling through a medium.

Important:

> The disturbance travels forward, but the medium particles only vibrate about their mean positions.

---

## Particle Motion vs Wave Motion

This is one of the most important ideas in the chapter.

| Question | Correct Idea |
|---|---|
| Do air particles move from a speaker all the way to your ear? | No. They vibrate about their mean positions. |
| What reaches your ear? | Energy carried by the sound wave. |
| What moves through the medium? | Compressions and rarefactions. |
| What does not move forward continuously? | Individual particles of the medium. |

### Common Mistake

Incorrect:

> Sound travels because air particles move from the source to the listener.

Correct:

> Sound travels because particles vibrate and pass the disturbance to neighboring particles.

This kind of wave is called a **longitudinal wave**.

| Feature | Sound Wave |

| Direction of wave propagation | Forward from source |
| Direction of particle vibration | Back and forth parallel to propagation |
| Type | Longitudinal |
| Needs medium? | Yes |

Sound needs a material medium. Therefore, sound is a **mechanical wave**.

| Wave Type | Needs Medium? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanical wave** | Yes | Sound wave, seismic wave |
| **Non-mechanical wave** | No | Light wave |

### Sound vs Light

| Feature | Sound | Light |
|---|---|---|
| Needs material medium? | Yes | No |
| Can travel in vacuum? | No | Yes |
| Wave type in this chapter | Mechanical longitudinal wave | Transverse electromagnetic wave |
| Example | Voice through air | Sunlight reaching Earth |

## Longitudinal and Transverse Waves

| Feature | Longitudinal Wave | Transverse Wave |
|---|---|---|
| Particle vibration direction | Parallel to wave direction | Perpendicular to wave direction |
| Common example | Sound in air | Light; waves on a string |
| Regions | Compressions and rarefactions | Crests and troughs |
| Medium needed? | For sound, yes | Depends on wave type |

### Earthquake Connection

Earthquakes produce seismic waves that travel through Earth. Some are longitudinal and some are transverse. Longitudinal seismic waves are usually detected first by seismographs.

## 10.5 - Graphical Representation of a Sound Wave

Sound waves can be represented using graphs.

Because sound in air involves density changes, one useful graph shows how density varies with distance at a particular instant.

### Density-Distance Graph

| Graph Part | Meaning in Sound Wave |
|---|---|
| Density above average | Compression region |
| Density below average | Rarefaction region |
| Highest point | Crest, maximum density |
| Lowest point | Trough, minimum density |
| Distance between consecutive crests | Wavelength |
| Distance between consecutive troughs | Wavelength |

### Crest

A crest is the highest point on the graph. For a sound-density graph, it represents maximum density.

### Trough

A trough is the lowest point on the graph. For a sound-density graph, it represents minimum density.

### Compression and Rarefaction on a Graph

| Region | Density | Graph Position |
|---|---|---|
| Compression | Higher than average | Above average-density line |
| Rarefaction | Lower than average | Below average-density line |

## 10.6.1 - Wavelength, Frequency, and Time Period

### Wavelength

Wavelength is the distance between:

- two consecutive crests; or
- two consecutive troughs.

It is represented by the Greek letter lambda.

$$
\lambda = \text{wavelength}
$$

SI unit:

$$
\text{metre (m)}
$$

### Frequency

Frequency is the number of complete density oscillations per unit time at a fixed point.

It is represented by the Greek letter nu.

$$
\nu = \text{frequency}
$$

SI unit:

$$
\text{hertz (Hz)}
$$

Since one hertz means one oscillation per second:

$$
1\ \text{Hz} = 1\ \text{s}^{-1}
$$

### Time Period

Time period is the time taken for one complete oscillation.

It is represented by:

$$
T = \text{time period}
$$

SI unit:

$$
\text{second (s)}
$$

### Relation Between Frequency and Time Period

Frequency and time period are inversely related.

$$
\nu = \frac{1}{T}
$$

$$
T = \frac{1}{\nu}
$$

Equivalent form:

$$
T\nu = 1
$$

### Meaning of the Relation

| Situation | Frequency | Time Period |
|---|---|---|
| Oscillations happen quickly | High | Small |
| Oscillations happen slowly | Low | Large |

## Worked Example 10.1 - Frequency and Time Period

**Question:** If there are 10 density oscillations in 2 seconds at a given position, calculate:

1. frequency of the sound wave;
2. time period of the wave.

### Given

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| Number of oscillations | 10 |
| Time taken | 2 s |

### Step 1 - Frequency

$$
\text{frequency} = \frac{\text{number of oscillations}}{\text{time taken}}
$$

$$
\nu = \frac{10}{2\ \text{s}} = 5\ \text{Hz}
$$

### Step 2 - Time Period

$$
T = \frac{\text{time taken}}{\text{number of oscillations}}
$$

$$
T = \frac{2\ \text{s}}{10} = 0.2\ \text{s}
$$

### Answer

| Part | Answer |
|---|---|
| Frequency | 5 Hz |
| Time period | 0.2 s |

## 10.6.2 - Amplitude and Intensity of Sound Waves

### Amplitude

The amplitude of a sound wave is the maximum change in density from the average density.

For a sound-density graph:

| Graph Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Larger height above/below average density | Larger amplitude |
| Smaller height above/below average density | Smaller amplitude |

### Amplitude and Energy

| Amplitude | Energy Carried | Perceived Sound |
|---|---|---|
| Large | More energy | Louder sound, generally |
| Small | Less energy | Softer sound, generally |

In Activity 10.6, striking a plate harder transfers more energy to the surrounding air. This produces larger particle displacements and makes the grains jump higher.

### Intensity

Intensity is the amount of sound energy passing through unit area per second in a direction perpendicular to the wave's propagation.

Simplified meaning:

> Intensity tells how much sound energy is crossing a given area each second.

### Why Intensity Decreases with Distance

As sound travels away from a source, it spreads over a larger area.

The same sound energy becomes distributed over more space, so intensity decreases.

| Near Source | Far from Source |
|---|---|
| Energy spread over smaller area. | Energy spread over larger area. |
| Higher intensity. | Lower intensity. |
| Sound is usually louder. | Sound is usually softer. |

## 10.6.3 - Speed of Sound

### Definition

The speed of sound describes how fast a sound wave propagates through a medium.

It can be defined as:

$$
\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance travelled}}{\text{time taken}}
$$

For wave motion:

$$
v = \frac{\lambda}{T}
$$

Since:

$$
\nu = \frac{1}{T}
$$

We get:

$$
v = \lambda\nu
$$

In words:

$$
\text{speed} = \text{wavelength} \times \text{frequency}
$$

### Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning | SI Unit |
|---|---|---|
| \(v\) | Speed of sound | m s\(^{-1}\) |
| \(\lambda\) | Wavelength | m |
| \(\nu\) | Frequency | Hz or s\(^{-1}\) |
| \(T\) | Time period | s |

---

## Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound speed depends on the medium.

General order:

$$
\text{solids} > \text{liquids} > \text{gases}
$$

At about 15 °C and atmospheric pressure:

| State | Medium | Approximate Speed of Sound |
|---|---|---|
| Solid | Steel | 5000 m s\(^{-1}\) |
| Liquid | Water | 1500 m s\(^{-1}\) |
| Gas | Air | 340 m s\(^{-1}\) |

### Why Sound Travels Faster in Solids

In solids, particles are closely packed and strongly connected. Vibrations can be passed along quickly.

In gases, particles are farther apart, so the disturbance travels more slowly.

### Effect of Temperature and Humidity

For air:

| Condition | Effect on Speed |
|---|---|
| Higher temperature | Speed increases. |
| Higher humidity | Speed increases. |

Examples from the chapter:

| Air Condition | Approximate Speed |
|---|---|
| Dry air at 0 °C | 331 m s\(^{-1}\) |
| Air at 22 °C | 344 m s\(^{-1}\) |

### Important Concept

In ordinary media such as air, if the source frequency changes:

- speed remains nearly fixed for that medium and condition;
- wavelength changes according to \(v = \lambda\nu\).

$$
0.0172\ \text{m} = 1.72\ \text{cm}

| Frequency | Wavelength |
|---|---|
| 20 Hz | 17.2 m |
| 20 kHz | 0.0172 m = 1.72 cm |

|---|---|

## Tone, Note, Timbre, and Octave

### Tone

A tone is a sound of a single frequency.

Examples:

- tuning fork sound;
- careful oral whistling.

### Musical Note

A musical note is usually a combination of:

- a fundamental frequency;
- higher frequencies called overtones.

This combination makes musical notes richer than a pure tone.

### Timbre

Timbre is the quality that helps us distinguish different sources even if they play the same note at the same loudness.

Example:

> A flute, ektara, and tabla may produce sounds with the same fundamental note, but they still sound different because their overtones differ.

### Octave

An octave is the interval between two notes where one has double the frequency of the other.

Example:

| Note 1 | Note 2 | Relation |
|---|---|---|
| 200 Hz | 400 Hz | 400 Hz is one octave above 200 Hz. |

## Science and Society: C. V. Raman and Acoustics

Sir C. V. Raman is widely known for the Raman Effect in light, for which he received India's first Nobel Prize in Science. He also studied acoustics and the sounds of Indian percussion instruments such as the tabla and mridangam.

Indian drums often contain a black central patch called **syaahi**. This patch changes how the membrane vibrates, helping the drum produce rich and controlled sounds.

## 10.7 - Reflection of Sound

Sound waves can bounce from surfaces. This is called reflection of sound.

Sound follows laws of reflection similar to light:

1. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
2. The incident sound direction, reflected sound direction, and normal to the surface lie in the same plane.

### Reflection Surfaces

| Surface Type | Effect on Sound |
|---|---|
| Hard and smooth | Strong reflection; clearer echo possible. |
| Soft and porous | Absorbs sound; reduces reflection. |
| Rough | Scatters sound in many directions. |

---

## 10.7.1 - Echo

An echo is a reflected sound heard separately after the original sound.

Examples:

- shouting near a mountain;
- clapping in a long empty corridor;
- sound reflected from a cliff.

### Condition for Hearing Echo

For the human ear to hear two sounds separately, the time gap should be at least about:

$$
0.1\ \text{s}
$$

At speed of sound \(340\ \text{m s}^{-1}\):

$$
\text{distance travelled by sound in } 0.1\ \text{s} = 340 \times 0.1 = 34\ \text{m}
$$

This 34 m is the total distance to the reflecting surface and back.

Therefore, the minimum distance of the reflecting surface should be:

$$
\frac{34}{2} = 17\ \text{m}
$$

### Minimum Echo Distance

At ordinary room temperature, the reflecting surface should be about **17 m or more** away to hear a distinct echo.

---

## Echo Formula

For echo problems, sound travels to the reflector and comes back.

If the object is at distance \(d\), total distance travelled is:

$$
2d
$$

Using:

$$
\text{distance} = \text{speed} \times \text{time}
$$

We get:

$$
2d = vt
$$

So:

$$
d = \frac{vt}{2}
$$

Use this formula for echo and sonar when the given time is the total return time.

## Worked Example 10.5 - Echo in a Corridor

**Question:** You clap in an empty corridor and hear an echo after 0.5 s. If the speed of sound in air is 340 m s\(^{-1}\), calculate your distance from the wall.

### Given

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| Speed | 340 m s\(^{-1}\) |
| Echo time | 0.5 s |

### Formula

$$
d = \frac{vt}{2}
$$

### Calculation

$$
d = \frac{340\ \text{m s}^{-1} \times 0.5\ \text{s}}{2}
$$

$$
d = \frac{170\ \text{m}}{2}
$$

$$
d = 85\ \text{m}
$$

### Answer

The wall is **85 m** away.

## 10.7.2 - Reverberation

Reverberation is the persistence of sound due to multiple reflections.

It occurs when reflected sounds arrive so quickly that they mix with the original sound instead of being heard as separate echoes.

### Echo vs Reverberation

| Feature | Echo | Reverberation |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Sound reflects and returns after a clear delay. | Many reflections arrive close together. |
| Time gap | At least about 0.1 s for clear separate hearing. | Very small gaps; often less than about 0.05 s. |
| What listener hears | Repeated sound. | Sound lingering or blending. |
| Example | Shouting near a cliff. | Sound persisting in a large hall. |

### Auditorium Design

Good auditoriums control reverberation.

| Design Element | Effect |
|---|---|
| Sound-absorbing panels | Reduce unwanted reflections. |
| Upholstered chairs | Absorb sound. |
| Curtains | Reduce excessive reverberation. |
| Curved surfaces | Direct sound toward audience areas. |
| Carefully shaped ceilings | Improve clarity and distribution. |

Too much reverberation can make speech unclear. A useful amount of reverberation can make music sound fuller.

### Cultural Connection

Some historical monuments show deep understanding of acoustics. The Whispering Gallery of Gol Gumbaz in Bijapur, Karnataka, is known for sound reflections that allow faint sounds to be heard across the dome.

## 10.8.1 - Echolocation

Echolocation is locating objects using reflected sound.

### How Bats Use Echolocation

| Step | Process |
|---|---|
| **1** | Bat emits short bursts of ultrasonic waves. |
| **2** | Waves reflect from objects or prey. |
| **3** | Bat detects returning echoes. |
| **4** | Bat estimates position, distance, and movement of objects. |

Other animals such as dolphins, whales, and some birds also use echolocation.

---

## Sonar

Sonar stands for sound navigation and ranging.

It uses sound waves, usually ultrasonic waves, to locate underwater objects.

### How Sonar Works

| Step | Process |
|---|---|
| **1** | A ship sends an ultrasonic signal into water. |
| **2** | The signal reflects from an object or seabed. |
| **3** | The echo returns to the ship. |
| **4** | Time taken is measured. |
| **5** | Distance is calculated using speed of sound in water. |

### Sonar Formula

If the signal returns after total time \(t\):

$$
d = \frac{vt}{2}
$$

The division by 2 is necessary because the signal travels to the object and back.

## Worked Problem Patterns

### Pattern 1 - Frequency from Oscillations

Use when a question gives number of oscillations and time.

$$
\nu = \frac{N}{t}
$$

Example:

If 20 compressions pass a point in 4 seconds:

$$
\nu = \frac{20}{4} = 5\ \text{Hz}
$$

### Pattern 2 - Time Period from Frequency

Use:

$$
T = \frac{1}{\nu}
$$

Example:

If frequency is 20 Hz:

$$
T = \frac{1}{20} = 0.05\ \text{s}
$$

### Pattern 3 - Wavelength from Speed and Frequency

Use:

$$
\lambda = \frac{v}{\nu}
$$

Example:

If \(v = 344\ \text{m s}^{-1}\) and \(\nu = 100\ \text{Hz}\):

$$
\lambda = \frac{344}{100} = 3.44\ \text{m}
$$

### Pattern 4 - Echo / Sonar Distance

Use when sound goes to a surface or object and returns.

$$
d = \frac{vt}{2}
$$

Example:

If echo returns in 4 s and sound speed is 1500 m s\(^{-1}\):

$$
d = \frac{1500 \times 4}{2} = 3000\ \text{m}
$$

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Sound can travel through empty space. | Sound needs a material medium and cannot travel through vacuum. |
| Air particles travel from speaker to listener. | Air particles vibrate locally; the disturbance travels. |
| Loudness and pitch are the same. | Loudness relates mostly to amplitude; pitch relates to frequency. |
| Higher frequency always means faster sound. | In a given medium, sound speed is mostly fixed; wavelength changes when frequency changes. |
| Echo and reverberation are identical. | Echo is heard separately; reverberation is blended persistence. |
| Ultrasound means very loud sound. | Ultrasound means frequency above 20 kHz, not necessarily louder sound. |
| Infrasound means very soft sound. | Infrasound means frequency below 20 Hz. |
| The formula \(d = vt/2\) is used for all distance problems. | Divide by 2 only when the sound travels to a target and returns. |
| A louder sound must have higher pitch. | Loudness and pitch are different perceptions. |

Distance:

$$
d = 1720\ \text{m}
$$

At 22 °C:

$$
t_{22} = \frac{1720}{344} = 5.0\ \text{s}
$$

At 0 °C:

$$
t_{0} = \frac{1720}{331} \approx 5.20\ \text{s}
$$

Extra time:

$$
5.20 - 5.00 = 0.20\ \text{s}
$$

The thunder takes about **0.20 s** extra at 0 °C.

Use the wavelength shown in the graph.

and:

then:

$$
\nu = \frac{v}{\lambda}
$$

$$
\nu = \frac{340}{0.08} = 4250\ \text{Hz}
$$

When answering from the textbook figure, first clearly state the wavelength read from the graph.

### Q15 - Ratio of Speed in Air and Water

The sound travels the same total distance in both cases.

For the same distance:

$$
v \propto \frac{1}{t}
$$

If time for sound in air is 4.5 times the time for sound in water:

$$
t_{\text{air}} = 4.5t_{\text{water}}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\frac{v_{\text{air}}}{v_{\text{water}}} = \frac{t_{\text{water}}}{t_{\text{air}}}
$$

$$
\frac{v_{\text{air}}}{v_{\text{water}}} = \frac{1}{4.5}
$$

So:

$$
v_{\text{air}} : v_{\text{water}} = 1 : 4.5
$$

## One-Page Summary

Sound is produced by vibrating objects. The object producing sound is called its source. The vibrations disturb particles of a material medium such as air, water, or steel. These particles do not travel from the source to the listener; they vibrate about their mean positions and pass the disturbance forward.

Sound travels as a wave made of alternating compressions and rarefactions. A compression is a high-density region, while a rarefaction is a low-density region. Since particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation, sound is a longitudinal wave. Since it needs a medium, it is also a mechanical wave.

Sound waves carry energy. They can make other objects vibrate, as shown by grains jumping on a stretched sheet when a loud sound is produced nearby.

Sound waves can be described using wavelength, frequency, time period, amplitude, intensity, and speed. Frequency and time period are inversely related. Wave speed is given by:

$$
v = \lambda\nu
$$

Sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases. In air, speed increases with temperature and humidity.

Humans perceive frequency as pitch and amplitude mainly as loudness. The human audible range is about 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Sounds below this range are infrasonic; sounds above it are ultrasonic.

Sound reflects from surfaces. A reflected sound heard separately is called an echo. If many reflections blend together, the result is reverberation. Sonar and echolocation use reflected sound to locate objects.
