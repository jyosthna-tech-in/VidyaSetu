## 🌍 Introduction — Why Study Cells?

It is widely accepted in the scientific community that life originated in water. Some researchers believe that early life may have originated not only in oceans, but also in small water pools where environmental conditions kept changing.

Hot springs are examples of such environments. In India, the hot springs of **Puga Valley in Ladakh** maintain very high temperatures, nearly at the boiling point of water, even in a cold climate. These conditions appear similar to those on early Earth about **3.5 billion years ago**.

The organisms living in such hot springs are mostly heat-loving bacteria called **thermophiles**. These organisms are **unicellular**, meaning each organism consists of only one cell.

Scientists from the **Birbal Sahni Institute of Palaeosciences, Lucknow** studied these hot springs and found that calcium carbonate formed rapidly around them. These deposits may have protected early organic molecules from harmful radiation and extreme conditions. They may also have helped in the formation of the first protective membrane — the barrier that defines a cell.

> [!NOTE]
> A **cell** is the basic unit at which life exists. Every living organism is made up of one or more cells.

Some organisms, such as bacteria and yeast, consist of only one cell. They are called **unicellular organisms**. Other organisms, such as plants, fish, birds, and humans, are made up of millions or trillions of cells that work together. They are called **multicellular organisms**.

The organisation of life can be understood as a hierarchy:

| Level | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cell** | Smallest living structural and functional unit | A cheek cell |
| **Tissue** | Group of similar cells performing a similar function | Muscle tissue |
| **Organ** | Structure made of different tissues working together | Lung |
| **Organ System** | Group of organs performing a major body function | Respiratory system |
| **Organism** | Complete living being | Human, neem tree, bacterium |

For example, the **nasal pores**, **nasal cavity**, **trachea**, and **lungs** together form the **respiratory system**.

Even when cells are organised into tissues, organs, and organ systems, the cell remains the fundamental unit of structure and function in all living organisms.

## 2.1 — How to Study Cells?

Human eyes cannot see everything. Two tiny dots drawn on paper may appear separate when they are far enough apart, but if they are moved very close together, they appear as a single point.

> [!NOTE]
> The **limit of resolution** is the ability to see two very close objects as separate and distinct.

For the human eye, when viewed from about **25 cm** away, two points must be separated by about **0.1 mm** to be seen as distinct. If they are closer than this, they appear as one point.

Most cells are smaller than what the unaided human eye can clearly resolve. This is why scientists use microscopes.

### 🔬 Why Microscopes Are Needed

| Tool | What It Helps Us See |
|---|---|
| **Unaided eye** | Larger objects such as a chicken egg or fish egg |
| **Light microscope** | Most plant and animal cells, nuclei, many bacteria |
| **Electron microscope** | Mitochondria, ribosomes, viruses, proteins, membranes, very fine cell details |

### 🧪 How a Light Microscope Works

A light microscope uses a **convex lens** or a combination of lenses to magnify an object.

| Part | Role |
|---|---|
| **Eyepiece** | Lens through which the observer looks |
| **Objective lens** | Lens close to the specimen; provides magnification |
| **Stage** | Platform where the slide is placed |
| **Mirror / light source** | Directs light through the specimen |
| **Coarse adjustment knob** | Moves the tube/stage quickly for rough focusing |
| **Fine adjustment knob** | Sharpens the image carefully |
| **Base** | Supports the microscope |
| **Handle / arm** | Used to hold or carry the microscope |

### 📜 Robert Hooke and the First Cells

In **1665**, **Robert Hooke** used a self-designed microscope capable of about **200–300X magnification**. While observing a thin slice of cork, he saw small box-like compartments and named them **cells**.

> [!NOTE]
> The word **cell** was first used by Robert Hooke after observing cork under a microscope.

### 🔬 Activity 2.1 — Estimating the Size of a Cell

**Aim:** To estimate the real size of an onion peel cell using the field of view of a microscope.

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Transparent ruler with millimetre markings | To measure the microscope field of view |
| Microscope | To observe the ruler and onion peel cells |
| Onion peel slide | To observe cells |
| Adjustment knobs | To focus the image |

#### Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Place a transparent ruler on the microscope stage. |
| **2** | Focus the ruler using the adjustment knob. |
| **3** | Observe the diameter of the circular field of view through the eyepiece. |
| **4** | Measure this diameter in millimetres. |
| **5** | Convert millimetres into micrometres. |
| **6** | Remove the ruler and place an onion peel slide on the stage. |
| **7** | Focus the onion peel cells. |
| **8** | Count the number of cells present along the diameter of the field of view in a straight line. |
| **9** | Estimate the size of one cell using the formula below. |

#### Unit Conversion

$$
1\text{ millimetre (mm)} = 1000\text{ micrometre }(\mu\text{m})
$$

#### Formula

$$
\text{Estimated size of one cell}
=
\frac{\text{Diameter of visible field in micrometre}}{\text{Number of cells along the diameter}}
$$

#### Worked Example

Suppose the diameter of the visible field is **5 mm**.

$$
5\text{ mm} = 5 \times 1000 = 5000\ \mu\text{m}
$$

Suppose **25 cells** are seen along the diameter.

$$
\text{Size of one onion cell}
=
\frac{5000\ \mu\text{m}}{25}
=
200\ \mu\text{m}
$$

$$
\boxed{\text{Estimated size of one onion peel cell} = 200\ \mu\text{m}}
$$

#### Total Magnification

The total magnification of a microscope depends on both the eyepiece and the objective lens.

$$
\text{Total magnification}
=
\text{Eyepiece magnification}
\times
\text{Objective lens magnification}
$$

If both the eyepiece and objective lens have magnifying power **10X**, then:

$$
10X \times 10X = 100X
$$

$$
\boxed{\text{Total magnification} = 100X}
$$

This means a cell of estimated size **200 µm** appears **100 times larger** under the microscope.

> [!TIP]
> A microscope improves three important features: **magnification**, **resolution**, and **contrast**.

### Microscope Improvements

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Magnification** | Makes an object appear larger |
| **Resolution** | Makes fine details appear clearer |
| **Contrast** | Makes different parts of an object easier to distinguish by brightness difference |

Apart from light microscopes, scientists use **electron microscopes**. Electron microscopes use a beam of electrons instead of light and can reveal structures at the **nanometre scale**.

> [!NOTE]
> A **nanometre** is one-billionth of a metre. Electron microscopes allow scientists to observe very fine cell structures that cannot be clearly seen with ordinary light microscopes.

## 2.2 — Structure of a Cell

Cells are organised into specialised tissues and organs. For cells to function as living units, they must interact with one another and with their surroundings.

These interactions occur at the **cell boundary**, where substances move between the cell and the external environment.

Even single-celled organisms exchange materials and respond to their surroundings through the **cell membrane**.

---

## 2.2.1 — Cell Membrane: The Universal Feature of a Cell

The **cell membrane** is a thin boundary that surrounds a cell and protects its contents. It defines the individuality of a cell.

The cell membrane is also called the **plasma membrane**.

> [!NOTE]
> The **cell membrane** or **plasma membrane** is selectively permeable. This means it allows some substances to pass through it while blocking others.

### Why Selective Permeability Matters

| Cell Need | How the Cell Membrane Helps |
|---|---|
| Take in useful materials | Allows entry of substances such as water and some dissolved materials |
| Remove wastes | Allows exit of some unwanted substances |
| Maintain internal conditions | Controls what enters and leaves |
| Communicate with surroundings | Helps cells respond to external changes |

You have studied how oxygen and carbon dioxide move across the membranes of alveoli in the lungs. A similar principle applies at the cell membrane: movement of substances depends on the membrane structure and concentration differences.

---

### 🔬 Activity 2.2 — Potato Osmosis Experiment

**Aim:** To observe how water moves in and out of plant cells through a selectively permeable membrane.

| Material | Use |
|---|---|
| Potato | Source of plant cells |
| Kitchen knife | To cut potato pieces |
| Weighing balance | To measure initial and final weights |
| Beaker A | Contains plain water |
| Beaker B | Contains 20% salt or sugar solution |

#### Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Carefully cut a potato into two pieces of roughly equal size. |
| **2** | Measure and record the initial weight of both pieces. |
| **3** | Put one potato piece in Beaker A with plain water. |
| **4** | Put the other potato piece in Beaker B with 20% salt or sugar solution. |
| **5** | Leave them undisturbed for about an hour or until visible changes are seen. |
| **6** | Measure and record the final weight of each piece. |
| **7** | Calculate the difference between initial and final weights. |

#### Expected Observations

| Beaker | Liquid | Observation | Weight Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A** | Plain water | Potato piece swells | Weight increases |
| **B** | 20% salt/sugar solution | Potato piece shrinks | Weight decreases |

#### Explanation

The cell membrane allows water to move in and out of the cell, but it does not allow all sugar or salt molecules to pass freely.

Water moves from a region with **more water and less solute** to a region with **less water and more solute** until the concentrations become more balanced.

> [!NOTE]
> **Osmosis** is the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.

> [!NOTE]
> **Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration. It can happen even without a membrane.

### Diffusion vs Osmosis

| Feature | Diffusion | Osmosis |
|---|---|---|
| What moves? | Particles of a substance | Water molecules |
| Requires membrane? | Not always | Yes, a selectively permeable membrane |
| Direction | Higher concentration to lower concentration | From more water / dilute solution to less water / concentrated solution |
| Example | Fragrance spreading in air | Water entering root cells |

In plants, water from the soil enters root cells through osmosis.

---

### Types of External Solutions

When a cell is placed in salt or sugar solutions of different concentrations, the movement of water depends on the concentration of the solution outside the cell.

| Type of Solution | Comparison with Cell Interior | Water Movement | Effect on Cell |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Isotonic solution** | Solute concentration outside = solute concentration inside | No net water movement | Cell remains about the same |
| **Hypotonic solution** | Solute concentration outside < solute concentration inside | Water enters the cell | Cell swells |
| **Hypertonic solution** | Solute concentration outside > solute concentration inside | Water leaves the cell | Cell shrinks |

> [!TIP]
> Think of water as moving toward the side with more solute. A concentrated solution pulls water out of cells.

---

### Fluid-Mosaic Model of the Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is extremely thin, about **7 to 10 nanometres (nm)** thick.

It is mainly made of **lipids** and **proteins**.

> [!NOTE]
> The **fluid-mosaic model** explains the structure of the cell membrane as a lipid bilayer with proteins embedded in it.

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Lipid bilayer** | Two layers of special fat molecules |
| **Water-attracting heads** | Face outward toward watery surroundings |
| **Water-repelling tails** | Face inward away from water |
| **Embedded proteins** | Help substances pass through and help in communication |
| **Fluid nature** | Molecules can move sideways, flip, and rotate |
| **Mosaic nature** | Molecules are arranged like tiles in a mosaic |

Proteins in the membrane act like **gatekeepers**. They help control the passage of substances across the membrane.

All living cells communicate with their surroundings and neighbouring cells through the cell membrane.

> [!NOTE]
> In plant cells, the rigid cell wall maintains the outer shape even when the cell membrane and inner contents shrink.

## 2.2.2 — Cell Wall: The Outer Covering of Cells

Plants generally cannot move from place to place. They need a rigid structure to withstand environmental stresses such as wind and rain.

Therefore, plant cells have an additional covering outside the cell membrane called the **cell wall**.

> [!NOTE]
> The **cell wall** is a rigid outer covering found outside the cell membrane in plant cells. It helps provide shape, support, and protection.

### Functions of the Cell Wall

| Function | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Support** | Helps plants stay upright |
| **Shape** | Helps leaves and flowers remain firm |
| **Protection** | Helps cells withstand environmental stress |
| **Permeability** | Allows water and some dissolved minerals to pass through |
| **Absorption support** | Works with the cell membrane to help roots absorb water and nutrients |

The cell wall is **permeable**, while the cell membrane is **selectively permeable**.

### Cell Wall and Osmosis

When plant cells are placed in a concentrated sugar solution:

| Part | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **Water inside the cell** | Moves out by osmosis |
| **Cell membrane and inner contents** | Shrink away from the cell wall |
| **Cell wall** | Maintains the cell's general shape |

Animal cells, such as cheek cells, do not have a cell wall. Therefore, when placed in a concentrated sugar solution, they lose water and shrink.

### Cell Wall Composition

The plant cell wall is mainly made of **cellulose**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Cellulose** is a carbohydrate made of many glucose units linked together. In our diet, cellulose acts as roughage and helps digestion.

Some microorganisms, such as **fungi** and **bacteria**, also have cell walls. Their cell walls provide protection and structural support.

### Plant Cell vs Animal Cell Boundary

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Cell membrane | Present | Present |
| Cell wall | Present | Absent |
| Shape | Usually more regular and rigid | More flexible and irregular |
| Response to concentrated solution | Inner contents shrink but wall maintains outer shape | Entire cell shrinks |

## 2.3 — The Cell Interior: A Coordinated Working System

Most cells have three basic parts:

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cell membrane / plasma membrane** | Selectively permeable boundary |
| **Cytoplasm** | Semi-fluid, jelly-like substance inside the cell |
| **Nucleus** | Prominent structure that contains genetic information in eukaryotic cells |

In addition to the nucleus, the cytoplasm contains sub-cellular components called **organelles**. Most organelles are visible only with an electron microscope.

> [!NOTE]
> **Organelles** are specialised structures inside a cell that perform specific functions.

### Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

A bacterial cell lacks a well-defined nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Such cells are called **prokaryotic cells**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Prokaryotic cells** do not have a membrane-bound nucleus. Their genetic material is present in a region called the **nucleoid**.

Plant and animal cells have a well-defined nucleus and several membrane-bound organelles. Such cells are called **eukaryotic cells**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Eukaryotic cells** have a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

### Table 2.2 — Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

| Characteristic | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
|---|---|---|
| Primitive nucleus / nucleoid | Present | Absent |
| Typical diameter | 1 to 10 µm | 10 to 100 µm |
| Number of cells in organism | Usually unicellular | Can be unicellular or multicellular |
| Membrane-bound organelles | Absent | Present |
| Membrane-bound nucleus | Absent | Present |

### Cytoskeleton and Cell Inclusions

In eukaryotic cells, a network of fine fibres forms the **cytoskeleton**.

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| **Cytoskeleton** | Provides structural support, maintains cell shape, helps cell movement and internal transport |
| **Cell inclusions** | Stored materials such as starch, calcium oxalate crystals, or silica crystals |

The cytoskeleton is visible as a separate entity only under an electron microscope.

### Ready to Go Beyond — Acellular Infectious Agents

Viruses, viroids, and prions are **acellular** infectious agents. This means they do not have cells.

| Agent | Main Feature |
|---|---|
| **Virus** | Genetic material surrounded by a protein coat |
| **Viroid** | Genetic material without a protein coat |
| **Prion** | Misfolded protein without genetic material |

## 2.3.1 — Why Do Eukaryotic Cells Need Organelles?

Eukaryotic cells perform many life processes at the same time. Organelles help divide the work inside the cell.

They help in:

| Cell Task | Organelles Help By... |
|---|---|
| Building new materials | Synthesising proteins, lipids, and other molecules |
| Removing waste | Breaking down unwanted materials |
| Producing energy | Releasing and storing energy from food |
| Transporting materials | Packaging and moving substances inside or outside the cell |
| Storing substances | Holding water, minerals, sugars, and waste |

> [!TIP]
> A eukaryotic cell can be imagined as a tiny living factory. Each organelle performs a specialised job, but all organelles work together.

---

### 🧬 Nucleus — House of Coded Instructions

The **nucleus** has a double-layered covering called the **nuclear membrane**. This membrane has pores that allow material to move between the nucleus and cytoplasm.

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| **Nuclear membrane** | Double-layered covering around the nucleus |
| **Nuclear pores** | Allow exchange of materials between nucleus and cytoplasm |
| **Nucleolus** | Site where ribosomal subunits are synthesised |
| **Chromatin** | Thread-like DNA-protein material in a non-dividing cell |
| **Chromosome** | Organised rod-shaped structure visible when a cell is about to divide |
| **DNA** | Molecule containing genetic information |
| **Gene** | Functional segment of DNA |

> [!NOTE]
> **DNA** stands for **Deoxyribonucleic acid**. It contains the genetic information inherited from parents.

When a cell is not dividing, DNA is present as part of **chromatin material**. When the cell is about to divide, chromatin becomes organised into **chromosomes**.

### Special Case — Mature Human Red Blood Cells

Mature **Red Blood Cells (RBCs)** in humans do not have a nucleus. They are **enucleate**.

| Advantage | Limitation |
|---|---|
| More space for haemoglobin, so more oxygen can be transported | RBCs cannot repair or divide themselves |

Because they lack a nucleus, mature human RBCs have a short lifespan of approximately **120 days**.

Prokaryotic cells do not have a well-defined nucleus. Their DNA is present as a single circular molecule associated with specific proteins. The region containing this genetic material is called the **nucleoid**.

### 🏭 Ribosomes — Protein Factories

Ribosomes are tiny structures present either freely in the cytoplasm or attached to the endoplasmic reticulum.

> [!NOTE]
> **Ribosomes** are the sites of protein synthesis.

| Location of Ribosomes | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Free in cytoplasm | Make proteins used inside the cell |
| Attached to ER | Make proteins that may be secreted or sent to membranes/organelles |

---

### 🏭 Endoplasmic Reticulum — Manufacturing Factory

The **Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)** is a large organelle that spreads like a network within the cytoplasm.

The ER is continuous with the outer membrane of the nuclear envelope.

### Rough ER vs Smooth ER

| Feature | Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER) | Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER) |
|---|---|---|
| Ribosomes on surface | Present | Absent |
| Appearance | Rough under electron microscope | Smooth under electron microscope |
| Main function | Protein synthesis and secretion | Synthesis and storage of fats and hormones |
| Example context | Gland cells such as pancreatic cells | Specialised cells that make lipids or hormones |

---

### 📦 Golgi Apparatus — Packaging and Shipping Centre

The **Golgi apparatus** is made of stacks of flattened, sac-like structures.

It is functionally linked to the ER, the cell membrane, and other organelles.

| Golgi Function | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Modify** | Changes proteins or lipids after they are made |
| **Sort** | Directs materials to proper destinations |
| **Package** | Packs materials into vesicles |
| **Transport / secretion** | Helps move substances inside or outside the cell |
| **Lysosome formation** | Helps form lysosomes |

> [!NOTE]
> The Golgi apparatus acts like the cell's post office: it modifies, sorts, packages, and sends materials.

### Meet a Scientist — Camillo Golgi

The Golgi apparatus was first observed in **1898** by the Italian scientist **Camillo Golgi** in nerve cells of a barn owl. He used special staining techniques and observed a thread-like network. Later, electron microscope observations confirmed the structure clearly, and it was named the **Golgi apparatus** in his honour.

### 🧹 Lysosomes — The Clean-Up System

Cells produce waste materials and damaged or worn-out organelles during their activities.

**Lysosomes** help prevent waste from accumulating inside the cell.

> [!NOTE]
> **Lysosomes** are single membrane-bound sacs filled with enzymes that break down unwanted materials.

Lysosomes can break down:

| Material | Broken Down By Lysosomal Enzymes |
|---|---|
| Proteins | Yes |
| Carbohydrates | Yes |
| Fats | Yes |
| Damaged organelles | Yes |
| Foreign particles | Yes |

The products formed by breakdown are released into the cytoplasm and may be reused in cellular processes.

Human sperm cells contain lysosomal enzymes. When a sperm meets an egg, these enzymes help break down the outer layer of the egg, allowing fertilisation to take place.

---

### ⚡ Mitochondria — Powerhouses of the Cell

Mitochondria are often called the **powerhouses of the cell** because they supply energy needed for most cellular activities.

Each mitochondrion is surrounded by two membranes.

| Part | Feature |
|---|---|
| **Outer membrane** | Smooth and porous |
| **Inner membrane** | Folded into finger-like projections called cristae |
| **Cristae** | Increase surface area for chemical reactions and energy production |

In mitochondria, glucose and other molecules are broken down to release energy during **cellular respiration**.

The energy released is stored in a molecule called **Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP)**.

> [!NOTE]
> **ATP** acts as the energy currency of the cell.

---

### 🌿 Plastids — Centres for Food Synthesis and Storage

Animals obtain food from their surroundings. Plants synthesise food in the presence of sunlight.

Plants use special organelles called **plastids** for food synthesis and storage.

### Chloroplasts

**Chloroplasts** are green plastids involved in photosynthesis.

| Structure / Substance | Function |
|---|---|
| **Chlorophyll** | Green pigment that absorbs sunlight |
| **Stroma** | Semi-fluid substance inside chloroplasts |
| **Disc-shaped membrane structures** | Contain chlorophyll and absorb light energy |
| **Starch granules** | Store sugars made during photosynthesis |

Chloroplasts are double-membrane-bound organelles, like mitochondria.

### Similarities Between Mitochondria and Plastids

Mitochondria and plastids share some features with certain bacteria.

| Similar Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Own DNA | They carry some genetic information |
| Own ribosomes | They can make some of their own proteins |
| Double membrane | They have two surrounding membranes |

These similarities suggest that mitochondria and plastids share an evolutionary history with certain single-celled organisms.

### Types of Plastids

| Plastid Type | Colour / Pigment | Main Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chloroplast** | Green chlorophyll | Photosynthesis | Green leaves |
| **Chromoplast** | Yellow, orange, or red pigments | Gives colour to flowers and fruits | Flower petals, fruits |
| **Leucoplast** | Colourless | Stores food materials such as starch, oils, or proteins | Potato and taro cells |

Bright colours in flowers and fruits help attract pollinators and fruit-eating animals. Pollinators help in pollination, while fruit-eating animals may help in seed dispersal.

---

### 🫧 Vacuoles — Storage and Support Organelles

In mature plant cells, there is usually one large central **vacuole** surrounded by a single selectively permeable membrane.

The vacuole is filled with a watery fluid called **cell sap**.

| Vacuole Function | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Stores water | Helps keep plant cells firm |
| Stores minerals | Holds dissolved materials |
| Stores sugars | Helps with temporary storage |
| Stores waste materials | Keeps some waste away from active cytoplasm |
| Provides support | Maintains pressure inside the cell |

When a plant does not get enough water, the vacuole loses water. The cells become less firm, and the plant wilts.

Animal cells may also have vacuoles, but they are usually smaller than plant vacuoles and help with temporary storage.

### Ready to Go Beyond — Synthetic DNA Experiment

In **2010**, scientist **J. Craig Venter** and his team made an important discovery in synthetic biology.

They studied the complete DNA sequence of a simple bacterium called **Mycoplasma mycoides** using computer programming. They then chemically synthesised an exact copy of this DNA in the laboratory.

Next, they took another closely related bacterium and removed its DNA, while keeping the rest of the cell, including cytoplasm and cell membrane, intact. They inserted the synthetic DNA into this cell.

After this, the cell started to grow and divide according to the instructions from the newly inserted synthetic DNA.

> [!NOTE]
> This experiment showed that DNA controls the structure and activities of a cell. However, scientists did not create a completely new cell from scratch. Only the DNA was synthetic; the rest of the cell came from an already existing living cell.

## 2.4 — How Do Normal Cells Grow and Divide?

When you get a small cut on your skin, it heals after a few days. When hair falls out, new hair grows back.

This happens because cells in the body can grow and divide to replace old, dead, or damaged cells.

When our body grows, it is not only because cells get bigger. Cells can grow only up to a certain size. Growth mostly happens because cells divide to form new cells.

### 🔬 Activity 2.5 — Studying Onion Root Tip Cells

**Aim:** To observe dividing cells in freshly growing onion root tips.

#### Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1** | Take a jar and fill it with plain water. |
| **2** | Place an onion bulb over the jar so that its base with roots is immersed in water. |
| **3** | Leave the setup for 5–6 days and observe root growth. |
| **4** | Cut 2–3 cm of freshly grown roots. |
| **5** | Transfer the root tips to freshly prepared aceto-alcohol. |
| **6** | Keep root tips in aceto-alcohol for 24 hours. |
| **7** | Transfer them to 70% ethanol for preservation. |
| **8** | Wash one or two preserved roots in water and place them on a clean slide. |
| **9** | Add one drop of dilute hydrochloric acid to soften the tissue. |
| **10** | Rinse the roots after 10–15 minutes. |
| **11** | Add 2–3 drops of aceto-carmine stain. |
| **12** | Leave for 5–10 minutes and gently warm the slide with caution. |
| **13** | Cut the tip portion of the root on the slide and place a coverslip. |
| **14** | Gently squash the coverslip with your thumb to spread the cells. |
| **15** | Observe the slide under a microscope. |

#### Expected Observation

Cells in the growing tip of an onion root divide continuously. Therefore, different cells may show different structures corresponding to different stages of cell division.

> [!NOTE]
> **Cell division** is the process by which new cells are formed from pre-existing cells.

Every day, an estimated hundreds of billions of cells in the human body are replaced. This is almost **1% of the total number of cells** in the body.

Both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells divide. Eukaryotic cells divide in a more controlled and orderly manner through the **cell cycle**.

## 2.4.1 — Cell Division

Cell division allows organisms to:

| Purpose | Example |
|---|---|
| Grow | A child grows into an adult |
| Repair tissues | A cut on the skin heals |
| Replace old cells | Dead skin cells are replaced |
| Reproduce | New organisms or reproductive cells are formed |

There are two major types of cell division:

| Type | Main Role |
|---|---|
| **Mitosis** | Growth, repair, maintenance, and asexual reproduction |
| **Meiosis** | Sexual reproduction and creation of genetic diversity |

---

### Mitosis

Every human begins life as a single fertilised egg. This one cell divides repeatedly to form trillions of cells in the body.

> [!NOTE]
> **Mitosis** produces two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell.

In mitosis:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Number of daughter cells | 2 |
| Genetic similarity | Daughter cells are genetically identical to the parent cell |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent cell |
| Importance | Growth, repair, maintenance, asexual reproduction |

Mitosis ensures that genetic information is largely maintained across body cells.

---

### Meiosis

**Meiosis** occurs only in cells of reproductive organs.

In animals, including humans:

| Organ | Gamete Produced |
|---|---|
| Testes of males | Sperm |
| Ovaries of females | Eggs |

In plants:

| Plant Part | Gamete-Related Structure |
|---|---|
| Anthers | Pollen grains that later produce sperm cells |
| Ovaries | Egg cells |

> [!NOTE]
> **Meiosis** produces gametes and creates variation and diversity among living organisms.

In meiosis, the parent cell divides twice, one after the other, to form four daughter cells.

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Number of divisions | 2 |
| Number of daughter cells | 4 |
| Chromosome number | Half of the parent cell |
| Genetic similarity | Daughter cells are not identical |
| Importance | Sexual reproduction and variation |

During fertilisation, gametes from two individuals combine and the original chromosome number is restored.

---

### Mitosis vs Meiosis

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| Main purpose | Growth, repair, maintenance | Gamete formation |
| Where it occurs | Body cells | Reproductive organs |
| Number of divisions | One | Two |
| Daughter cells produced | Two | Four |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent cell | Half of parent cell |
| Genetic variation | No major variation | Produces variation |
| Example | Skin healing | Formation of sperm or egg |

---

### Cell Culture

Scientists have developed methods to grow plant and animal cells outside the body in special conditions. This is called **cell culture**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Cell culture** is the process of growing cells outside the body in a nutrient-rich medium under suitable conditions.

For cell culture, scientists maintain:

| Condition | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Nutrient-rich medium | Provides food and materials for growth |
| Right temperature | Supports normal cell activity |
| Proper acidic or alkaline conditions | Maintains suitable chemical environment |
| Moisture | Prevents drying |
| Sterile conditions | Prevents contamination |

Cell culture is important for studying how cells work and for producing biochemicals, food, medicines, vaccines, and more.

### 🌉 Bridging Science and Society — Errors in Cell Division

The processes of mitosis and meiosis must occur properly and in a controlled manner.

| Error Type | Possible Consequence |
|---|---|
| Error in mitosis | Uncontrolled cell division, tumour formation, abnormal chromosome number in body cells |
| Error in meiosis | Genetic disorders, developmental problems, distinctive physical features, early pregnancy loss, reduced fertility |

## 2.5 — Cell Theory: The Unifying Principle of Biology

An important observation about living organisms is that all organisms are made up of cells.

### Historical Development

| Year | Scientist | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **1838** | **Matthias Schleiden** | Reported that all plants are made up of cells |
| **1839** | **Theodor Schwann** | Found that all animals are made up of cells |
| **1855** | **Rudolf Virchow** | Stated that new cells arise only from pre-existing cells |

Together, their work led to the formulation of the **Cell Theory**.

### Classical Cell Theory

> [!NOTE]
> According to classical **Cell Theory**:
> - All living organisms are made up of one or more cells.
> - The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in living beings.
> - All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Cell Theory unifies biology from bacteria to humans and explains the continuity of life through cell division.

---

## 2.5.1 — Do Cells Grow and Reproduce Forever?

Cells grow and divide in a controlled way. They stay in the right place, perform their functions, and eventually die when no longer needed.

Dead cells are replaced by new cells that perform the same function.

Thus, every cell has a definite lifespan.

If cells do not die when they should, or if they die too early, problems can arise in the body.

### Contact Inhibition

In many animal cells, cell division usually stops when cells come in contact with neighbouring cells. This process is called **contact inhibition**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Contact inhibition** is the process by which many animal cells stop dividing when they touch neighbouring cells.

Cancer cells lose this control and keep dividing uncontrollably, leading to the formation of tumours.

Plant cells grow differently. Because of their rigid cell walls, plant cells do not show contact inhibition in the same way and follow a different pattern of growth.

---

### Cancer Cells and Tumours

Normal cells grow, age, and die in a controlled manner. Sometimes this control system breaks down, and abnormal cells start growing and dividing uncontrollably.

This may result in **tumours**.

| Tumour Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Benign tumour** | Does not invade nearby tissues aggressively |
| **Malignant / cancerous tumour** | Can invade nearby tissues and spread to other body parts |

Cancerous tumours can spread to other parts of the body and form new tumours.

---

### Programmed Cell Death

Cells have natural ways of dying to maintain balance.

> [!NOTE]
> **Programmed Cell Death (PCD)** is a genetically regulated and organised process of selective cell destruction.

PCD is essential for:

| Role | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Normal development | Helps shape body structures |
| Cellular quality control | Removes damaged or unnecessary cells |
| Immune function | Helps maintain healthy body defence |

During embryo development, PCD helps form fingers by eliminating cells between digits. Without it, hands would remain webbed.

---

### Meet a Scientist — Gottlieb Haberlandt

In **1902**, Austrian botanist **Gottlieb Haberlandt** proposed that any living plant cell, even a fully mature cell from a permanent tissue, can develop into a complete plant if it is given suitable nutrients and favourable conditions.

This special ability of plant cells is called **totipotency**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Totipotency** is the ability of a plant cell to form different types of cells and potentially develop into a complete plant under suitable conditions.

Haberlandt's idea laid the foundation for **Plant Tissue Culture Technology**.

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| **All cells have a nucleus.** | Prokaryotic cells do not have a membrane-bound nucleus. Mature human RBCs also lack a nucleus. |
| **Diffusion and osmosis are the same.** | Osmosis is a special case of diffusion involving water across a selectively permeable membrane. |
| **Plant cells shrink fully like animal cells in concentrated solution.** | The inner contents shrink, but the rigid cell wall maintains the outer shape. |
| **Only green parts of plants contain plastids.** | Roots may contain plastids such as leucoplasts for food storage. |
| **Mitochondria are found only in animal cells.** | Both plant and animal cells have mitochondria. |
| **Meiosis repairs body tissues.** | Body repair mainly uses mitosis. Meiosis forms gametes. |
| **Cancer means all tumours are spreading.** | Some tumours are benign; malignant tumours can invade and spread. |
