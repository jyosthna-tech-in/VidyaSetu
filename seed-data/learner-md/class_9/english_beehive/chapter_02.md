## Big Ideas

This chapter asks learners to look at work with respect.

It shows that craft is not just labour. Craft carries:

- history;
- identity;
- community memory;
- discipline;
- skill;
- beauty;
- livelihood;
- responsibility.

The prose lesson focuses on one young girl's desire to become a pot maker.

Together, the texts ask:

- Who keeps traditional skills alive?
- Why should handmade work be respected?
- What happens when economic pressure threatens old crafts?

## Before Reading

Think about:

1. What handmade objects are used in your home?
2. Do you know anyone who practises a traditional craft?
3. Why are some crafts disappearing?
4. Is a craft valuable only if it earns a lot of money?
5. Should a family skill be passed on to the next generation?

These questions prepare you for the conflict in the story: the difference between passion and practical livelihood.

---

## Short Summary

Sentila is a young girl who dreams of becoming a pot maker like her mother and grandmother. Her mother, Arenla, wants her to become a weaver instead because pottery is physically exhausting, messy, seasonal, and poorly paid. Sentila secretly watches expert potters and becomes fascinated by the way clay turns into beautiful pots.

The village council is worried that Arenla is not passing on the traditional skill. They remind Mesoba, Sentila's father, that pot making is not merely a private family skill. It is part of the people's tradition and history.

Arenla later teaches Sentila, but Sentila struggles for a long time. She cannot shape the clay properly and feels ashamed. When Sentila stays in a girls' dormitory, Onula notices her effort and gently teaches her. Onula also tells her to observe her mother carefully, especially the shaping of the mouth of the pot.

During a later pot-making session, Arenla leaves Sentila to continue the work. Sentila suddenly finds rhythm and skill in her hands. She makes many pots, almost matching her mother's count. When she enters the house, she finds that Arenla has died. Sentila feels guilty, but Onula understands that something extraordinary has happened. Seeing two rows of pots - one made by Arenla and one by Sentila - Onula realises that a new pot maker has been born.

## Process of Pot Making

Sentila observes that pot making is a long, skilled process.

| Step | Process | Skill Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Dig grey and red clay from the riverbank. | Strength and knowledge of material. |
| **2** | Carry the clay uphill in a basket. | Physical endurance. |
| **3** | Soak the clay in a trough. | Preparation and patience. |
| **4** | Stuff clay into bamboo cylinders in the right proportion. | Experience with consistency. |
| **5** | Pound clay until it becomes soft and malleable. | Labour and timing. |
| **6** | Shape clay with hand and spatula. | Dexterity and rhythm. |
| **7** | Add finishing touches and test shape. | Precision. |
| **8** | Dry pots in the sun. | Weather awareness. |
| **9** | Load pots into the kiln with hay and bamboo. | Careful arrangement. |
| **10** | Fire the kiln correctly. | Judgement; overfiring or underfiring can ruin the batch. |

---

## Why Arenla Preferred Weaving

Arenla does not reject pottery because she lacks skill. She rejects it because she knows its hardships.

| Pot Making | Weaving |
|---|---|
| Requires clay from a distant riverbank. | Can be done indoors. |
| Involves heavy carrying. | Less physically harsh. |
| Needs pounding, shaping, drying, firing. | More manageable in all seasons. |
| Messy and tiring. | Cleaner. |
| Takes months for one batch. | One shawl takes less time. |
| Gives low income. | Gives better return. |

This makes Arenla's character realistic. She is not against tradition; she is worried about livelihood and hardship.

---

## Role of the Village Council

The village council warns Mesoba that:

- Arenla must pass the skill on;
- pot making serves the needs of the people;
- it symbolises community tradition and history;
- expert potters must teach those who wish to learn;
- a craft cannot be treated as private property only.

This idea is central to the story.

Craft belongs to:

- the maker;
- the family;
- the community;
- the past;
- the future.

---

## Role of Onula

Onula is a turning point in Sentila's journey.

She helps in three ways:

1. She notices that Sentila's failure is partly due to tension.
2. She teaches calmly and gives confidence.
3. She tells Sentila exactly what to observe in Arenla's technique.

Onula's support matters because she does not merely demonstrate skill. She changes Sentila's mental state. Sentila moves from fear and frustration to confidence and attention.

---

## Meaning of the Ending

The final line means that Sentila has truly become a pot maker.

This is not only about making pots. It means:

- the family craft has survived;
- the community tradition continues;
- Arenla's skill has passed into Sentila's hands;
- Sentila's passion has become ability;
- a new identity has been born.

The ending is both sad and powerful. Arenla dies, but her craft does not die.

## Check Your Understanding: Model Answers

### 1. Is pot making easy?

No. Pot making is difficult because it involves collecting clay from a far riverbank, carrying heavy loads, pounding clay, shaping it with skill, drying it properly, and firing it carefully in a kiln. One mistake can ruin an entire batch.

### 2. Would Sentila fulfil her dream?

Yes. Her passion, observation, Onula's support, and her mother's final opportunity help her become a pot maker by the end.

### 3. Would Mesoba and Arenla support Sentila?

Mesoba appears willing to support the passing on of the skill after the council's warning. Arenla is reluctant at first but later teaches Sentila, which shows a complicated form of support.

### 4. Did Onula's support help Sentila?

Yes. Onula's calm instruction gives Sentila confidence. She also tells Sentila what to observe in her mother's hand movements, which becomes crucial to her success.

### 5. What does Sentila observing her mother tell us?

It shows that Sentila is serious, patient, and willing to learn through close attention. She understands that craft is learned through the eyes, hands, and repeated practice.

## Short Answer Questions: Model Points

### 1. Describe the process of pot making.

Expert pot makers dig clay from the riverbank, carry it home, soak it, pound it in bamboo cylinders, soften it into malleable dough, shape it with the left hand and a spatula, finish the mouth and rim, dry the pots in the sun, arrange them in the kiln, and fire them carefully.

### 2. What warning was given to Mesoba?

The council warned him that Arenla must pass on the pottery skill because it was a traditional community skill and not private property.

### 3. How did Sentila feel after failing for a year?

She felt ashamed, frustrated, and discouraged because she loved pottery but could not shape the clay successfully.

### 4. Explain Onula's reaction to the two rows of pots.

Onula realises that the rows show a major transformation. One row belongs to Arenla's skill, and the other proves Sentila's newly awakened skill. She sees the moment as a profound revelation.

### 5. What does the idea that tradition does not belong to an individual symbolise?

It symbolises community ownership of heritage. Traditional knowledge must be shared and preserved because it carries the people's history and identity.

### 6. What is the significance of "A new pot maker was born"?

It means Sentila has finally inherited the skill. The craft will continue through her, even after Arenla's death.

### 7. What role does perseverance play in pursuing dreams?

Sentila's journey shows that passion must be supported by patience, repeated practice, observation, and guidance. She fails many times but does not stop trying.

# Unit 2C: Grammar

## Noun Clauses

A noun clause is a subordinate clause that acts like a noun.

It can function as:

- subject;
- object;
- complement.

### Examples

| Sentence | Noun Clause | Function |
|---|---|---|
| She realised that the pot was ready. | that the pot was ready | Object of "realised" |
| Onula sensed that something important had happened. | that something important had happened | Object of "sensed" |

### Complete the Sentences

| Incomplete Sentence | Suitable Completion |
|---|---|
| The elders emphasised that... | the craft must be passed on to the next generation. |
| Mesoba explained why... | Arenla had delayed teaching Sentila. |
| Onula's promise was that... | she would help Sentila learn to make a pot. |
| Sentila observed her mother carefully when she was shaping the mouth of the pot, which... | helped her understand the correct technique. |
| The kiln, where... | the pots were fired, required careful attention. |

---

## Relative Clauses

A relative clause describes a noun.

It often begins with:

- who;
- whom;
- whose;
- which;
- that;
- where.

### Examples

| Sentence | Relative Clause | Noun Described |
|---|---|---|
| Arenla took Sentila to the riverbank where the clay was found. | where the clay was found | riverbank |
| Pot making, which served the people, carried tradition. | which served the people | pot making |

### Complete the Sentences

| Incomplete Sentence | Suitable Completion |
|---|---|
| Sentila, whose... | dream was to become a pot maker, practised the craft diligently. |
| The village council, where... | elders discussed community matters, sought an explanation. |
| The potter's hands, which... | moved with rhythm and control, shaped the clay beautifully. |
| Arenla wanted Sentila to learn weaving, which... | could provide better income. |
| Mesoba discussed the matter with Arenla, who... | later began teaching Sentila. |

---

## Determiners

Determiners come before nouns and tell us about number, quantity, ownership, or definiteness.

### Types of Determiners

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Articles** | a, an, the |
| **Demonstratives** | this, that, these, those |
| **Possessives** | my, our, your, his, her, its, their |
| **Definite numerals** | one, two, three |
| **Indefinite numerals** | some, any, no, all, many, few, several, little |
| **Distributives** | each, every, either, neither |

### Examples from Craft Context

| Determiner | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **the** | The kiln required careful attention. |
| **some** | Sentila took out some clay. |
| **her** | Her mother watched silently. |
| **many** | Many pots were arranged in rows. |
| **each** | Each craft has its own discipline. |

## Reflective Writing: Skills and Passion

Reflective writing is personal but organised. It explains what you feel, what you have learnt, and how an experience shaped you.

### Structure

| Step | What to Write |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Name your passion or skill. |
| **Describe the skill** | Explain what you do to practise it. |
| **Connect to profession** | Explain whether it can become a career. |
| **Examples** | Give a real incident or achievement. |
| **Reflection** | Explain what you learnt about yourself. |
| **Conclusion** | Summarise your goal. |

### Model Reflective Paragraph

I have always enjoyed sketching buildings and natural scenes. At first, I treated it as a hobby, but gradually I realised that drawing helps me observe details more carefully. I practise by sketching objects at home, watching tutorials, and attending an art class on weekends. This skill can help me in fields such as design, architecture, animation, or illustration. Once, I made a poster for a school event, and my teacher appreciated the clarity of the layout. That experience showed me that my interest could become useful to others. I now understand that passion becomes meaningful when it is developed with discipline.

## Short Summary of the Poem

The speaker listens to Bharat celebrating its many vocations. Craftspersons, artisans, carpenters, electricians, boatmen, shoemakers, cooks, designers, and masons are presented as people whose work carries rhythm, skill, identity, and beauty. The poem honours everyday workers and suggests that every vocation contributes to society in a unique way.

---

## Central Idea

The poem celebrates the dignity of labour.

It says that work is not just a way of earning money. A vocation carries:

- identity;
- pride;
- rhythm;
- skill;
- beauty;
- social contribution.

The poem asks readers to respect all forms of useful work.

| Pattern in many lines | Many lines name a vocation and then describe its action or contribution. |

### 2. Speaker

The speaker appears to be an observer-listener who hears the many working voices of Bharat and turns them into a celebration.

### 3. Tone and Mood

| Feature | Answer |
|---|---|
| Tone | Celebratory and reverential. |

### 4. Imagery

The poem uses:

- visual imagery: workers, tools, colours, nets, wood, cables, shoes;
- auditory imagery: singing, humming, lutes, rhythm, voices.

### 5. Metaphor

The phrase about the cook's singing connects taste and sound. It suggests that the cook's work is pleasing and artistic, just like enjoyable music or delicious food.

### 6. Personification

The poem personifies vocations by giving them voices and identity. It suggests that work itself can speak through the worker.

### 7. Repetition

The poem begins and ends with a similar celebratory line because repetition:

- frames the poem;
- gives unity;
- creates musicality;
- reinforces the central idea that Bharat is alive with varied work.

### 8. Alliteration

Possible examples include repeated consonant sounds in phrases about craft, colours, work, walking, or varied voices. Learners should identify exact examples from their copy of the poem.

## Short Answer Questions: Model Points

### 1. Why does the poet say that Bharat is celebrating varied vocations?

The poet hears the sounds and rhythms of different workers and sees them as a collective celebration of skill, identity, and contribution.

### 2. What does the electrician humming suggest?

It suggests joy, readiness, confidence, and rhythm in work. It makes labour appear lively and meaningful.

### 3. Explain the line about vocation and identity.

The line means that a person's work is often connected to who they are, what they know, what they contribute, and how society recognises them.

### 4. Are all vocations important in daily life?

Yes. Every vocation fulfils a need. Farmers provide food, electricians provide power, carpenters build useful objects, cooks nourish people, and craftspersons preserve culture.

### 5. Why does the poet celebrate all vocations?

The poet celebrates them because each worker contributes uniquely to society. The poem rejects the idea that only some occupations deserve respect.

### 6. How does sensory imagery show the beauty of work?

The poem uses sights, sounds, movement, colour, and rhythm to make everyday work feel artistic and alive.

## Final Chapter Summary

- "The Pot Maker" is about Sentila's desire to become a pot maker.
- Arenla wants Sentila to learn weaving because pottery is tiring and poorly paid.
- The village council believes traditional craft belongs to the community, not only to one family.
- Sentila struggles for a year but continues trying.
- Onula's kindness and guidance help Sentila gain confidence.
- Sentila finally makes pots with skill, proving that the craft has passed to her.
- The ending shows continuity of tradition even after personal loss.
- The poem "Gifts of Grace" celebrates workers and vocations across Bharat.
- It uses free verse, imagery, metaphor, repetition, personification, and symbolism.
- The grammar section focuses on noun clauses, relative clauses, and determiners.
- The writing tasks build reflective writing and poster-making skills.
- The whole chapter teaches dignity of labour, respect for traditional skill, and the importance of pursuing one's passion with perseverance.

## Study Strategy for Learners

For the story:

1. Track Sentila's emotional journey: desire, secrecy, failure, guidance, success.
2. Understand Arenla's reasons instead of judging her too quickly.
3. Remember that pottery represents heritage, not only income.
4. Use the two rows of pots as the key symbol of the ending.

For the poem:

1. Learn which vocation is connected with which contribution.
2. Focus on sound and movement words.
3. Explain free verse and repetition clearly.
4. Connect every answer to dignity of labour.

For grammar:

1. Noun clause: acts like a noun.
2. Relative clause: describes a noun.
3. Determiner: comes before a noun and limits or specifies it.
