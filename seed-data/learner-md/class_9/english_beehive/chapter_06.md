## Big Ideas

This chapter is about music, but it is also about communication.

Shruti loves music and respects her father. Her father, Nabin Sharma, loves classical Hindustani music and worries that fusion music may weaken tradition. Their conflict is not between good and bad people. It is between two sincere fears:

- Shruti fears disappointing her father.
- Nabin fears losing the seriousness and purity of music.

The play resolves the conflict by showing that tradition and innovation need not destroy each other. A musician can honour classical roots while exploring new forms.

The poem "A Friend Found in Music" carries the emotional side of music. It shows music as rhythm, therapy, comfort, and companionship.

## Act II: Dinner Conversation

Act II takes place after dinner at Shruti's home. Shruti is distracted and struggles to speak. Her mother notices her worry and jokes gently, but Nabin becomes serious when Shruti asks him not to get angry.

Before Shruti can fully explain, Nabin says no. He believes too many performances can harm artistic seriousness. When he learns the concert is an Indo-Western fusion performance, he becomes more upset. He says his views on fusion are already known and refuses to consider it.

Shruti requests him to attend one practice before deciding, but he mocks fusion music. Leela later scolds Shruti for hiding the rehearsals from her parents. Shruti promises not to hide things again.

Act III returns to Iqbal's house. Shruti arrives and considers practising one last time before telling her friends that she cannot perform.

## Long Answer Question Bank

### 1. Justify the title "Twin Melodies".

The title is suitable because the play brings together two musical streams: classical Hindustani music and Indo-Western fusion. These are the "twin melodies" of tradition and modernity. The title also suggests the relationship between father and daughter, who may seem different but share the same love for music. By the end, the two melodies harmonise instead of clashing.

### 2. How does the play explore tradition and modernity?

Nabin represents tradition because he believes strongly in classical Hindustani music, ragas, and aalaaps. Shruti represents modern exploration because she wants to perform fusion music. The play does not reject tradition or blindly celebrate modernity. Instead, it shows that new forms can grow from strong roots if the artist remains sincere.

### 3. What do Peter, Iqbal, and Avinash reveal through their advice?

| Character | Perspective | What It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Peter** | Practical and encouraging | He wants Shruti to speak and move forward. |
| **Iqbal** | Reassuring and emotionally confident | He believes family love can lead to understanding. |
| **Avinash** | Direct and humorous | He thinks Shruti is making things worse by hiding. |

All three friends support communication instead of secrecy.

### 4. Analyse Nabin Sharma's character.

Nabin begins as strict and traditional. He believes fusion music may reduce the dignity of the violin and classical art. However, he is not cruel. He is serious about music and afraid of artistic compromise. When he hears Shruti's rehearsal, he recognises her discipline and skill. He remembers his own past rebellion against family expectations. His attitude evolves from rejection to trust.

### 5. How does Shruti's internal conflict explore family duty and personal passion?

Shruti wants to perform fusion music, but she does not want her father to feel disrespected. Her conflict is internal because she values both personal passion and family duty. She is not rebelling carelessly; she is searching for a respectful way to express her artistic choice. This makes her conflict believable and mature.

### 6. Is the play's conclusion effective?

Yes, the conclusion is effective because it resolves the conflict through experience rather than argument. Nabin changes after hearing the performance and after remembering his own past. The solution is realistic within the emotional world of the play because love, memory, and music influence his decision.

### 7. How does cultural diversity contribute to the storyline?

The fusion group itself is culturally diverse: violin, tabla, flute, and keyboard come together. The names of the children also suggest a mixed social environment. The play uses this diversity to show that music can connect different traditions, communities, and styles.

# Unit 6D: Drama Vocabulary and Grammar

## Aside

In drama, an **aside** is used when a character speaks directly to the audience or reveals thoughts that other characters on stage do not hear.

### What Is Aside Used For?

Correct answer:

**(iv) (i), (ii), and (iii)**

It:

- enables a character to comment on the story;
- shows what a character thinks or feels;
- creates a direct link between audience and character.

## Non-Lexical Fillers

Words such as **ahem-ahem**, **er**, **um**, and **uh** fill pauses while a speaker gathers thoughts.

| Filler | Usual Feeling / Function |
|---|---|
| **ahem-ahem** | drawing attention or clearing throat |
| **err** | hesitation or uncertainty |
| **arrgh** | frustration or irritation |
| **hmmm** | thinking or considering |
| **whew** | relief or tiredness after stress |

## Mini Model Script

### Title: A Beat of Her Own

**Characters**

- Anuradha: teenage tabla player
- Mr. Sen: her father, a vocalist
- Meera: her mother
- Rohan: her brother

**Setting**

The living room. A tabla rests near a low table. A tanpura leans against the wall.

**Anuradha:** (aside) The invitation is in my hand, but the courage is not.

**Rohan:** (whispering) Tell him now. If you wait, the event will be over before your speech begins.

**Anuradha:** Papa... I have been invited to perform tabla at the World Music Day event.

**Mr. Sen:** (firmly) Tabla? Again? Your voice is your real gift, Anuradha.

**Anuradha:** I respect vocal music, Papa. But rhythm speaks to me. I do not want to abandon singing; I want to grow as a musician.

**Meera:** (gently) Let her play once. You taught her that music needs discipline. She has shown discipline.

**Rohan:** And she practises more than I study, which is saying something.

**Mr. Sen:** (after a pause) One performance. I will listen before I decide.

**Anuradha:** (with relief) That is all I ask.

**Curtain**

## Stage Conventions

The play uses speaker names, colons, and brackets instead of quotation marks.

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| **Why are inverted commas not used?** | A play script already shows dialogue through speaker names and colons. |
| **Where do we use colons?** | After the speaker's name, before the spoken line. |
| **What is written in brackets?** | Stage directions: tone, action, expression, movement, or delivery. |
| **How does it help actors?** | It tells them how to speak, move, pause, or react. |

## Rhythm Activity

In groups, create rhythm using classroom objects:

- desk tap;
- pencil roll;
- clap;
- foot tap;
- bottle shake;
- notebook beat.

Rules:

1. Keep a steady beat.
2. Let each person take one sound.
3. Start softly, then build a crescendo.
4. End together.
