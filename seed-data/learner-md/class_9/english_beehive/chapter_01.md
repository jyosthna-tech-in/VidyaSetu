## Before Reading

Think about these questions before studying the lesson:

1. Which languages do your grandparents or elderly relatives speak?
2. Do they read books, newspapers, magazines, or religious texts?
3. What do they depend on younger people for?
4. How can young people help older people learn something new?
5. Why does reading give a person confidence?

These questions prepare you for the emotional centre of the story: a grandmother who realises that not being able to read makes her dependent.

---

## Short Summary

The narrator remembers her childhood in a village in north Karnataka, where she lived with her grandparents. Newspapers and magazines arrived by bus, and the whole household waited for them eagerly. Her grandmother Krishtakka, who had never gone to school, loved listening to the Kannada serial novel `Kashi Yatre`, which the young narrator read aloud to her every week.

The grandmother identified deeply with the old woman in the novel, who wanted to go to Kashi but finally gave away her savings to help an orphan girl. Because the grandmother could not read, she depended on the narrator to know what happened next.

One week, the narrator went to a wedding and stayed away longer than expected. During her absence, the magazine arrived, but the grandmother could not read it. She felt ashamed, helpless, and dependent. This experience made her decide to learn the Kannada alphabet at the age of sixty-two.

The narrator first laughed at the idea, but the grandmother remained determined. She studied seriously, practised reading and writing, and worked hard. By Saraswati Puja during Dassara, she could read the novel on her own. She respected the narrator as her teacher and touched her feet, reversing the usual age-based custom. The narrator realised that her first student had succeeded fully.

## Setting and Social Background

The story is set in a village in north Karnataka at a time when:

- transport was slow;
- printed magazines arrived late;
- serialised fiction was eagerly followed;
- girls' education was not always considered essential;
- elderly women often had heavy domestic responsibilities;
- family and temple spaces were central to social life.

The rural setting matters because the grandmother cannot instantly access another reader, a library, or digital help. Her dependence becomes very visible when the narrator is away.

---

## Central Themes

### 1. Literacy as Independence

The grandmother has money and family support, but she still feels dependent because she cannot read. The story shows that literacy is not just a school skill. It is a form of personal freedom.

Reading allows a person to:

- access information;
- enjoy literature;
- act privately;
- avoid embarrassment;
- make decisions independently;
- participate more fully in society.

### 2. Learning Has No Age Limit

The grandmother is sixty-two, but she learns because her motivation is strong. Her example challenges the assumption that only children can learn.

The key lesson is:

> Determination matters more than age.

### 3. Respect for Teachers

The grandmother touches the narrator's feet not because the narrator is older, but because she has become the teacher. This shifts respect from age alone to the value of knowledge.

### 4. Intergenerational Learning

Usually elders teach children. Here, the child teaches the elder. The story shows that learning can move both ways between generations.

### 5. Gender and Education

The grandmother did not study because, in her youth, girls' education was not treated as important. The story quietly criticises that social attitude and shows its lifelong consequences.

### 6. Literature as Emotional Companion

The novel `Kashi Yatre` becomes meaningful to the grandmother because she sees her own longings in it. Stories help people understand their own lives.

---

## Importance of Kashi Yatre in the Story

`Kashi Yatre` is important at two levels.

### In the Grandmother's Life

The grandmother identifies with the old woman in the novel because:

- both are elderly women;
- both have religious and emotional longings;
- both think about Kashi;
- both value sacrifice and goodness;
- both are emotionally moved by another person's happiness.

The novel gives her joy, discussion, and anticipation.

### In the Main Story

The serial becomes the reason the grandmother realises how dependent she is. Without it, she may not have felt the urgency to learn. The story-within-the-story therefore triggers the grandmother's transformation.

## Short Answer Questions: Model Points

### 1. Why was the grandmother embarrassed to ask someone else to read?

She had always been respected in her family and community. Asking someone else to read the magazine would expose her inability and make her feel dependent. Her embarrassment came from wounded self-respect.

### 2. Why does the narrator initially laugh?

The narrator is only twelve and thinks like a child. She believes that learning the alphabet at sixty-two is unrealistic because her grandmother is old, busy, and physically aged. She later realises that this assumption was wrong.

### 3. What significance does `Kashi Yatre` have?

It is emotionally important to the grandmother because she identifies with the old woman in the novel. It is structurally important because the grandmother's inability to read the new episode becomes the turning point of the story.

### 4. What does the grandmother's desire to learn reflect?

It reflects her self-respect, courage, independence, and belief that learning is possible at any age.

### 5. What lessons can we infer from the grandmother touching the narrator's feet?

The action shows that knowledge deserves respect regardless of the teacher's age or gender. It also shows humility, gratitude, and the sacred role of a teacher.

### 6. What broader theme appears in the line about determination overcoming obstacles?

The broader theme is that sincere purpose and disciplined effort can overcome social, physical, and emotional barriers.

### 7. How does the story show the value of education?

The story shows that education is not only for marks or employment. It gives independence, confidence, dignity, and access to the world of ideas.

# Unit 1C: Grammar - Simple Past and Past Perfect

## Basic Idea

When two actions happened in the past, the earlier action often uses the past perfect tense.

The later action uses simple past.

### Structure

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple past** | Verb 2 form | She arrived. |
| **Past perfect** | had + past participle | She had arrived. |

### Key Rule

Use past perfect for the action that happened first.

Example:

By the time I reached the station, the train had left.

| Action | Time | Tense |
|---|---|---|
| Train left | Earlier | Past perfect |
| I reached | Later | Simple past |

Last year, my parents and I **took** a financial planning course. When we **reviewed** our expenses, we realised we **had spent** too much on unnecessary purchases. ## Common Grammar Errors

| Error | Correction | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| When I had reached, he left. | When I reached, he had left. | Earlier action: he left. |
| After she finished, she had slept. | After she had finished, she slept. | Finishing happened first. |
| By the time they came, we ate. | By the time they came, we had eaten. | Eating happened before they came. |
| I had saw the book. | I had seen the book. | Use past participle after "had". |

# Unit 1D: Speaking and Writing

## Speaking Activity: Turncoat Debate

A turncoat debate is a solo debate where the same speaker argues both for and against a topic.

### Topic 1

It is important to learn a new language apart from your mother tongue.

#### Points For

- It helps us communicate with more people.
- It improves employment and travel opportunities.
- It helps us understand other cultures.
- It strengthens memory and thinking skills.
- It promotes national integration in a multilingual country.

#### Points Against

- Mother tongue should not be neglected.
- Some learners may feel overloaded.
- Language learning needs time and practice.
- Forced language learning may reduce interest.
- Cultural identity begins with one's first language.

### Topic 2

Learning can happen only when you are young.

#### Points For

- Children often learn faster.
- Young learners usually have more time.
- Schools provide structured learning.
- Habits are easier to build early.

#### Points Against

- Adults learn when they have strong motivation.
- Experience can make adult learning deeper.
- New skills are needed throughout life.
- The grandmother's example proves learning has no age limit.

### Useful Turncoat Phrases

| Purpose | Phrases |
|---|---|
| Begin for the topic | To begin with, I support the topic because... |
| Add point | Moreover, I would like to add... |
| Switch side | On the other hand... |
| Challenge earlier view | However, this is not the complete picture. |
| Conclude | To sum up, both sides show that... |

## Writing Task: Letter to the Editor

### Topic

Students can contribute significantly to promoting adult literacy.

### Format

1. Sender's address
2. Date
3. Receiver's designation and address
4. Subject
5. Salutation
6. Body in three paragraphs
7. Complimentary close
8. Name

### Model Letter

```text
24, Green Park Colony
Mysuru

23 May 2026

The Editor
The City Herald
Mysuru

Subject: Student Participation In Adult Literacy Camps

Sir/Madam,

Through the columns of your newspaper, I wish to draw attention to the need for greater student participation in adult literacy camps. Many adults in our communities still struggle to read forms, signs, messages, bank instructions, and basic public information. This affects their confidence and independence.

Students can play a meaningful role in addressing this issue. By volunteering a few hours each week, they can help adults learn basic reading, writing, numeracy, and digital skills. Such work benefits society, but it also develops empathy, patience, communication skills, and civic responsibility among students.

Schools, resident welfare associations, libraries, and local organisations should work together to run evening and weekend literacy sessions. Awareness campaigns, student volunteer groups, and simple learning kits can encourage more participation. I hope this letter is published so that more young people are motivated to join this important cause.

Yours truly,
Ananya Sharma
```

### Why This Letter Works

| Feature | How It Appears |
|---|---|
| Clear issue | Adult literacy is identified as a social need. |
| Public purpose | It addresses the community, not a private complaint. |
| Student role | It explains how students can help. |
| Benefits | It mentions benefits for adults and students. |
| Solutions | It suggests camps, partnerships, awareness, and learning kits. |
| Formal tone | It uses respectful, clear language. |

## Important Note on Studying the Poem

The poem is an ode. It praises India in a grand and emotional style.

Do not read it as a list of geographical facts only. Read it as a poem of admiration. The poet selects symbols such as mountains, rivers, scriptures, warriors, sages, music, and spiritual teachers to represent India's greatness.

---

## Short Summary of the Poem

The poet praises India as a land of unmatched greatness. He celebrates the Himalayas, the Ganga, the Upanishads, brave warriors, sages, divine music, Brahma-knowledge, and the teachings of the Buddha. These references show India as a land of natural beauty, spiritual wisdom, courage, culture, and ancient heritage. The repeated praise of India creates a strong sense of devotion and national pride.

---

## Stanza-Wise Explanation

### Stanza 1

The poet praises India's mighty mountains, graceful river, sacred scriptures, and golden land. These images create a picture of India as physically beautiful and spiritually rich.

Key ideas:

- Himavant symbolises strength and permanence.
- Ganga symbolises purity, generosity, and grace.
- Upanishads symbolise deep spiritual wisdom.
- The golden land suggests richness, warmth, and glory.

### Stanza 2

The poet praises the people and traditions of India. Warriors, sages, divine music, and auspicious things show that India has courage, wisdom, art, spirituality, and cultural depth.

Key ideas:

- Warriors represent bravery.
- Sages represent wisdom and holiness.
- Music represents refined culture.
- Auspiciousness suggests sacredness and blessing.

### Stanza 3

The poet highlights India's philosophical and spiritual contributions. Brahma-knowledge and the Buddha's teachings show India as a centre of self-realisation, compassion, and enlightenment.

Key ideas:
