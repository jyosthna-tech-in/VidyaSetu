## Big Ideas

This chapter asks learners to rethink ability, competition, and victory.

The prose interview teaches that disability does not define a person's potential. Dr. Deepa Malik's life shows that a setback can become a turning point when it is met with courage, support, discipline, and purpose.

The poem "Nine Gold Medals" expands the idea of sportsmanship. The runners train to win, but they choose to help a fallen athlete instead of racing past him. The poem suggests that the highest victory is sometimes shared humanity.

Together, the texts teach:

- ability can exist beyond visible limitation;
- inclusion is not charity, but justice and dignity;
- sports can challenge stereotypes;
- empathy is stronger than selfish competition;
- success is greater when it creates space for others.

## Short Summary

The interview begins by introducing Paralympics as a global platform where athletes with disabilities challenge stereotypes through skill, strength, and competition. India first participated in the Paralympics in 1968 and won its first medal in 1972.

Dr. Deepa Malik explains that at the age of 29 she was diagnosed with a spinal tumour. After surgery, she was paralysed waist down and told she would use a wheelchair for the rest of her life. Instead of wasting life in regret, she chose to transform it.

Because she loved sports and had been a swimmer, she moved towards para-athletics. Her breakthrough came at the 2016 Rio Paralympic Games, where she won a silver medal in shot put. She sees this not only as a personal victory but also as a way to change public perceptions about disability.

She explains that one of her biggest challenges was society's tendency to underestimate disabled people. Family support, mental resilience, and fortitude helped her turn obstacles into stepping stones.

She also speaks about advocacy. She works for emotional health, women's empowerment through outdoor sports, support for persons from lower socio-economic backgrounds, and sports awareness sessions in schools and colleges. Her advice is clear: every setback can become an opportunity to prove strength.

## Long Answer Question Bank

### 1. How do Dr. Malik's achievements challenge societal perceptions?

Dr. Malik's achievements challenge the belief that disability limits ambition, strength, or public contribution. By winning a Paralympic medal and becoming a recognised para-athlete, she proves that persons with disabilities can perform at elite levels. Her success also challenges gender stereotypes because she becomes a leading woman athlete in a field where women with disabilities may face double discrimination.

### 2. What can be the long-term impact of involving youth in disability advocacy?

Involving youth can create long-term social change. Students who learn about inclusion early are more likely to reject stereotypes, support accessible spaces, respect persons with disabilities, and build fair institutions in the future. Youth can spread awareness through schools, colleges, sports clubs, and digital platforms.

### 3. Rationalise the title with reference to Dr. Malik's attitude and achievements.

The title is appropriate because Dr. Malik refuses to define life by loss. After paralysis, she chooses sport, wins medals, earns national recognition, and becomes an advocate. Her life shows that possibility opens when determination, support, and courage meet. The "limitless" in the title refers not to the absence of difficulty, but to the refusal to let difficulty become final.

### 4. "Every setback is an opportunity to prove your strength." Explain as a life lesson.

This lesson applies to everyone because setbacks are part of all lives. Failure, illness, rejection, or loss can either stop a person or teach resilience. A setback tests patience, courage, creativity, and discipline. If a person responds with effort and support, the difficulty can become a turning point.

### 5. How does Dr. Malik's global recognition contribute to gender equality in sports?

Her recognition shows that women, including women with disabilities, deserve visibility in sports. It challenges the idea that high achievement in athletics belongs mainly to able-bodied men. Her example encourages girls and women to enter sports, demand access, and see themselves as capable of national and international success.

### 6. How can "ability beyond disability" guide future para-athletes?

The phrase reminds para-athletes that disability is only one part of identity. It encourages them to focus on training, skill, strategy, self-belief, and discipline. It also asks society to provide equipment, coaching, accessibility, and fair opportunities so that ability can be developed fully.

### 7. What can you learn from this interview and implement in life?

The interview teaches resilience, self-belief, discipline, and empathy. In daily life, these lessons can be implemented by not giving up after failure, supporting classmates who face challenges, avoiding stereotypes, and treating every person with dignity.

## Direct to Reported Speech: Changes

### Example 1

Direct:

Interviewer: You have been listed as one of the ten most inspirational women para-athletes globally.

Reported:

The interviewer said that she had been listed as one of the ten most inspirational women para-athletes globally.

Changes:

- reporting verb "said" is added;
- "that" is added;
- "you" changes to "she";
- "have been" changes to "had been";
- quotation marks are removed.

### Example 2

Direct:

Dr. Malik: I love sports and had been a swimmer too, and so I decided to switch to para-athletics.

Reported:

Dr. Malik said that she loved sports and had been a swimmer too, and so she had decided to switch to para-athletics.

Changes:

- "said that" is added;
- "I" changes to "she";
- "love" changes to "loved";
- "decided" changes to "had decided";
- quotation marks are removed;
- the sentence becomes part of the reporting sentence.

### Example 3

Direct:

Interviewer: Your story is indeed a testament to the power of determination.

Reported:

The interviewer said that her story was indeed a testament to the power of determination.

Changes:

- "said that" is added;
- "your" changes to "her";
- "is" changes to "was";
- quotation marks are removed.

### Example 4

Direct:

Dr. Malik: I believe youth is the voice of tomorrow.

Reported:

Dr. Malik says that she believes youth is the voice of tomorrow.

Changes:

- "says that" is used because the statement is treated as a continuing belief;
- "I" changes to "she";
- "believe" changes to "believes";
- "is" remains "is" because the reporting verb is present tense and the idea is still relevant.

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Siya said that she watched... last night. | Siya said that she had watched... the previous night. |

|---|---|

## Interview Preparation Checklist

- Write 5-6 clear questions.
- Begin with greeting and introduction.
- Ask one question at a time.
- Listen without interrupting.
- Ask a follow-up question if needed.
- Thank the interviewee.
- Keep the tone suitable to the person.

## Writing Skill: Notice

### What Is a Notice?

A notice is a short formal message written to inform a specific group about an event, activity, competition, meeting, lost item, or important instruction.

### Notice Format

| Part | What To Write |
|---|---|
| **Name of organisation** | School or institution name. |
| **NOTICE** | Written clearly at the top. |
| **Date** | Date of issuing the notice. |
| **Heading** | Short title of the notice. |
| **Body** | What, when, where, who, and action required. |
| **Signature / Name / Designation** | Person issuing the notice. |

## Model Notice

```text
ABC PUBLIC SCHOOL
NOTICE
12 August 2026

Inter-School Athletic Meet Selection

The Sports Club will hold selection trials for the Inter-School Athletic Meet on
16 August 2026 at 8:00 a.m. on the school ground. Interested students must
register with their House Sports Captain by 14 August 2026.

Riya Sharma
Sports Captain
```

## Notice Writing Checklist

- Keep within the word limit.
- Use formal language.
- Mention date, time, venue, and purpose.
- Say who should respond.
- Give the registration deadline.
- End with name and designation.
- Do not use unnecessary description.

## Short Summary

Nine athletes prepare for a race after months of training. The crowd is excited, and the runners are ready at the starting line. When the signal is given, the runners charge ahead, but the smallest athlete stumbles, falls, and cries out in anguish.

Instead of continuing toward individual victory, the other eight runners stop. They turn back, help him up, join hands, and walk together to the finish line. All nine receive gold medals, and the crowd gives them a standing ovation.

The poem shows that empathy can be more meaningful than winning alone.

They had trained for weeks and months and wanted to prove themselves. The race was the final event, and the chance to win medals created excitement and anticipation.

It symbolises the victory of compassion over competition. A dash is about speed and individual success, while the walk shows togetherness, patience, and support. The transformation shows that the runners choose humanity over personal medals.

### 5. How would the poem differ if it focused only on individual achievement?

If the poem focused only on individual achievement, it would become a simple sports poem about winning. It would lose its emotional depth and message of inclusion. The power of the poem comes from the athletes choosing to help instead of leaving someone behind.

### 6. How does language and tone engage the reader?

The poet uses simple narrative language, strong action verbs, and vivid images. The tone changes from excitement to tension and then to admiration. This keeps the reader emotionally involved and makes the ending inspiring.

### 7. What might be the poet's purpose?

The poet's purpose is to show that true victory is not only about crossing the line first. It is also about kindness, empathy, and shared dignity. The poem encourages readers to value people above prizes.
