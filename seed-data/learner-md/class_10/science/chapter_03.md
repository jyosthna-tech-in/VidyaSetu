## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Metal** | Element that generally loses electrons, forms positive ions, conducts heat/electricity, and is malleable and ductile. |
| **Non-metal** | Element that generally gains or shares electrons, forms acidic/neutral oxides, and is usually non-lustrous, brittle, and a poor conductor. |
| **Metallic lustre** | Shining surface of a metal in its pure or freshly cut state. |
| **Malleability** | Ability of a substance to be beaten into thin sheets. |
| **Ductility** | Ability of a substance to be drawn into thin wires. |
| **Sonorous** | Producing a ringing sound when struck. |
| **Amphoteric oxide** | Oxide that reacts with both acids and bases to form salt and water. |
| **Reactivity series** | List of metals arranged in decreasing order of reactivity. |
| **Displacement reaction** | Reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. |
| **Cation** | Positively charged ion formed by loss of electrons. |
| **Anion** | Negatively charged ion formed by gain of electrons. |
| **Ionic compound** | Compound formed by transfer of electrons and held by electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. |
| **Mineral** | Naturally occurring element or compound found in the earth's crust. |
| **Ore** | Mineral from which a metal can be profitably extracted. |
| **Gangue** | Unwanted earthy impurities such as sand and soil present with ore. |
| **Metallurgy** | Process of extracting metals from ores and refining them for use. |
| **Roasting** | Heating sulphide ore strongly in excess air to convert it into oxide. |
| **Calcination** | Heating carbonate ore strongly in limited air to convert it into oxide. |
| **Reduction** | Removal of oxygen or gain of electrons; in metallurgy, converting metal oxide into metal. |
| **Electrolytic reduction** | Extraction of highly reactive metals by electrolysis of molten compounds. |
| **Electrolytic refining** | Purification of impure metal using electrolysis. |
| **Corrosion** | Slow damage of a metal surface by reaction with air, moisture, chemicals, or gases. |
| **Rusting** | Corrosion of iron in the presence of air and water. |
| **Galvanisation** | Coating iron or steel with zinc to prevent rusting. |
| **Alloy** | Homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal. |

## Big Ideas

Metals and non-metals can first be compared using physical properties, but physical properties alone are not enough because there are many exceptions.

Chemical behaviour gives a clearer classification. Metals usually lose electrons and form positive ions. Non-metals usually gain electrons and form negative ions, or share electrons in other compounds.

The reactivity series is the central tool of this chapter. It helps decide:

- which metal reacts faster with water or acid;
- which metal can displace another metal from salt solution;
- which extraction method is suitable;
- why some metals are found free in nature and others are found only as compounds.

Metallurgy is applied chemistry. The more reactive the metal, the harder it is to extract from its compounds.

Corrosion is unwanted reaction of metals, but it can be controlled through coating, alloying, and surface treatment.

Classifying elements as metals and non-metals helps us predict:

- whether they conduct electricity;
- whether they form cations or anions;
- whether their oxides are basic or acidic;
- whether they react with acids;
- how they can be extracted from nature.

# Unit 3B: Physical Properties of Metals

## 1. Metallic Lustre

Metals usually have a shining surface in pure state.

Freshly cut or freshly cleaned metal surfaces show this property clearly.

Examples:

- iron becomes shiny after rubbing with sandpaper;
- copper has a reddish-brown shine;
- aluminium has a silvery shine;
- magnesium ribbon looks shiny after cleaning.

## 2. Hardness

Metals are generally hard, but hardness varies.

| Metal Type | Observation |
|---|---|
| Iron, copper, aluminium | Hard and difficult to cut. |
| Zinc, lead | Softer than iron. |
| Sodium, potassium | Very soft; can be cut with a knife. |

## 3. Malleability

Malleability is the ability of a metal to be beaten into thin sheets.

Examples:

- gold leaf;
- aluminium foil;
- silver foil;
- metal sheets used in construction and utensils.

Gold and silver are among the most malleable metals.

## 4. Ductility

Ductility is the ability of a metal to be drawn into thin wires.

Examples:

- copper wires;
- aluminium wires;
- gold wire;
- iron wire.

Gold is extremely ductile. A very long wire can be drawn from a small mass of gold.

## 5. Conductivity of Heat

Metals are generally good conductors of heat.

This is why cooking vessels are commonly made from metals like:

- aluminium;
- copper;
- stainless steel;
- iron.

| Conductor Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Very good heat conductors | Silver, copper |
| Good heat conductors | Aluminium, iron |
| Poorer metallic conductors | Lead, mercury |

## 6. Conductivity of Electricity

Metals generally conduct electricity well.

Copper and aluminium are widely used in electrical wiring.

Electric wires are coated with PVC or rubber-like material because these materials are electrical insulators. The coating prevents electric shock and short circuits.

## 7. Sonority

Metals are sonorous. They produce a ringing sound when struck.

This is why school bells are made of metals.

## Summary Table: Physical Properties of Metals

| Property | Meaning | Common Metal Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Lustre | Shiny appearance | Usually shiny when clean. |
| Hardness | Resistance to cutting/scratching | Generally hard, except alkali metals. |
| Malleability | Can be beaten into sheets | Usually malleable. |
| Ductility | Can be drawn into wires | Usually ductile. |
| Heat conductivity | Transfers heat | Usually good conductors. |
| Electrical conductivity | Allows current | Usually good conductors. |
| Sonority | Produces ringing sound | Usually sonorous. |

# Unit 3C: Physical Properties of Non-metals

## General Physical Properties

Non-metals usually show properties opposite to metals.

| Property | General Non-metal Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Lustre | Usually dull. |
| Hardness | Usually soft or brittle if solid. |
| Malleability | Not malleable. |
| Ductility | Not ductile. |
| Heat conductivity | Usually poor conductors. |
| Electrical conductivity | Usually poor conductors. |
| Sonority | Not sonorous. |
| State | Many are gases or solids; bromine is liquid. |

## Examples of Non-metals

| Non-metal | State at Room Temperature |
|---|---|
| Oxygen | Gas |
| Hydrogen | Gas |
| Nitrogen | Gas |
| Chlorine | Gas |
| Carbon | Solid |
| Sulphur | Solid |
| Iodine | Solid |
| Bromine | Liquid |

## Important Exceptions

Physical properties alone cannot classify every element perfectly.

| Exception | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Mercury | Metal but liquid at room temperature. |
| Gallium and caesium | Metals with very low melting points; can melt near palm temperature. |
| Sodium, potassium, lithium | Metals but very soft and can be cut with a knife. |
| Iodine | Non-metal but lustrous. |
| Graphite | Non-metal allotrope of carbon but conducts electricity. |
| Diamond | Non-metal allotrope of carbon but extremely hard and has very high melting point. |

## Allotropes of Carbon

Allotropes are different physical forms of the same element.

Carbon has important allotropes:

| Allotrope | Important Property |
|---|---|
| Diamond | Hardest natural substance; very high melting and boiling point. |
| Graphite | Conducts electricity; soft and slippery. |

## Why Chemical Properties Are Needed

Because of exceptions, elements are more clearly classified by chemical behaviour:

- metals generally lose electrons and form cations;
- non-metals generally gain electrons and form anions;
- metals usually form basic oxides;
- non-metals usually form acidic or neutral oxides.

# Unit 3D: Burning Metals and Non-metals in Air

## Metal Oxides

Almost all metals combine with oxygen to form metal oxides.

General reaction:

$$
Metal + Oxygen \rightarrow Metal\ oxide
$$

## Magnesium Burning in Air

Magnesium burns with a dazzling white flame and forms magnesium oxide.

$$
2Mg + O_2 \rightarrow 2MgO
$$

Magnesium oxide dissolves slightly in water to form magnesium hydroxide, which is basic.

$$
MgO + H_2O \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2
$$

So the solution turns red litmus blue.

## Copper Heated in Air

Copper does not burn easily, but when heated strongly in air it forms black copper(II) oxide.

$$
2Cu + O_2 \rightarrow 2CuO
$$

Copper oxide is basic in nature.

## Aluminium in Air

Aluminium forms aluminium oxide.

$$
4Al + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2Al_2O_3
$$

Aluminium oxide forms a thin protective coating on aluminium. This prevents further corrosion.

## Non-metal Oxides

Non-metals generally form acidic or neutral oxides.

Sulphur burns in oxygen to form sulphur dioxide.

$$
S + O_2 \rightarrow SO_2
$$

Sulphur dioxide dissolves in water to form sulphurous acid.

$$
SO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow H_2SO_3
$$

The solution is acidic and turns blue litmus red.

## Metal Oxides vs Non-metal Oxides

| Oxide Type | Usual Nature | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Metal oxide | Basic | `MgO`, `CuO`, `Na2O` |
| Non-metal oxide | Acidic or neutral | `SO2`, `CO2`, `H2O`, `CO` |

# Unit 3E: Amphoteric Oxides

## Meaning

Some metal oxides react with both acids and bases. These are called amphoteric oxides.

Important examples:

- aluminium oxide, `Al2O3`;
- zinc oxide, `ZnO`.

## Aluminium Oxide with Acid

$$
Al_2O_3 + 6HCl \rightarrow 2AlCl_3 + 3H_2O
$$

## Aluminium Oxide with Base

$$
Al_2O_3 + 2NaOH \rightarrow 2NaAlO_2 + H_2O
$$

Here, sodium aluminate is formed.

## Zinc Oxide with Acid

$$
ZnO + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2O
$$

## Zinc Oxide with Base

$$
ZnO + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2ZnO_2 + H_2O
$$

Here, sodium zincate is formed.

## Exam Definition

Amphoteric oxides are oxides that react with both acids and bases to form salt and water.

# Unit 3F: Different Metals React Differently with Oxygen

## Very Reactive Metals

Sodium and potassium react vigorously with oxygen and may catch fire in air.

They are stored under kerosene oil to prevent contact with air and moisture.

## Metals with Protective Oxide Layers

Magnesium, aluminium, zinc, and lead develop a thin oxide layer on their surface.

This layer prevents further oxidation.

## Iron, Copper, Silver, and Gold

| Metal | Reaction with Oxygen |
|---|---|
| Iron | Does not burn as a piece, but iron filings burn when sprinkled in flame. |
| Copper | Forms black copper(II) oxide on heating. |
| Silver | Does not react with oxygen easily. |
| Gold | Does not react with oxygen even at high temperature. |

## Anodising

Anodising is a process of forming a thick oxide layer on aluminium.

During anodising:

- aluminium article is made the anode;
- dilute sulphuric acid is used as electrolyte;
- oxygen evolved at the anode reacts with aluminium;
- a thicker aluminium oxide layer forms;
- the layer can be dyed to give an attractive finish.

Anodising increases corrosion resistance.

# Unit 3G: Reaction of Metals with Water

## General Pattern

Metals may react with water to form metal oxide or metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

$$
Metal + Water \rightarrow Metal\ oxide + Hydrogen
$$

If the metal oxide dissolves in water:

$$
Metal\ oxide + Water \rightarrow Metal\ hydroxide
$$

Not all metals react with water. Reactivity decides the speed and conditions.

## Potassium and Sodium with Cold Water

Potassium and sodium react violently with cold water. The reaction is so exothermic that hydrogen may catch fire.

$$
2K + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2KOH + H_2 + Heat
$$

$$
2Na + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH + H_2 + Heat
$$

## Calcium with Water

Calcium reacts less violently with water.

$$
Ca + 2H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2 + H_2
$$

Calcium starts floating because hydrogen bubbles stick to its surface.

## Magnesium with Hot Water

Magnesium does not react with cold water. It reacts with hot water to form magnesium hydroxide and hydrogen.

$$
Mg + 2H_2O \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2 + H_2
$$

Hydrogen bubbles may make magnesium float.

## Aluminium, Iron, and Zinc with Steam

Aluminium, iron, and zinc do not react with cold or hot water, but they react with steam.

Aluminium:

$$
2Al + 3H_2O(g) \rightarrow Al_2O_3 + 3H_2
$$

Iron:

$$
3Fe + 4H_2O(g) \rightarrow Fe_3O_4 + 4H_2
$$

Zinc:

$$
Zn + H_2O(g) \rightarrow ZnO + H_2
$$

## Metals That Do Not React with Water

Lead, copper, silver, and gold do not react with water under ordinary conditions.

## Water Reaction Summary

| Metal | Reaction with Water |
|---|---|
| K, Na | Violent with cold water. |
| Ca | Reacts with cold water, less violently. |
| Mg | Reacts with hot water. |
| Al, Zn, Fe | React with steam. |
| Pb, Cu, Ag, Au | Do not react with water. |

# Unit 3H: Reaction of Metals with Acids

## General Pattern

Metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series react with dilute acids to form salt and hydrogen gas.

$$
Metal + Dilute\ acid \rightarrow Salt + Hydrogen
$$

## Examples with Dilute Hydrochloric Acid

Magnesium:

$$
Mg + 2HCl \rightarrow MgCl_2 + H_2
$$

Aluminium:

$$
2Al + 6HCl \rightarrow 2AlCl_3 + 3H_2
$$

Zinc:

$$
Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2
$$

Iron:

$$
Fe + 2HCl \rightarrow FeCl_2 + H_2
$$

## Reactivity Order from Acid Reaction

In the activity described in the raw chapter, the observed order is:

```text
Mg > Al > Zn > Fe
```

Magnesium reacts fastest and most exothermically. Copper does not react with dilute hydrochloric acid.

## Why Copper Does Not React with Dilute HCl

Copper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series. Therefore, it cannot displace hydrogen from dilute hydrochloric acid.

## Nitric Acid Exception

Hydrogen gas is usually not evolved when a metal reacts with nitric acid.

Reason:

- nitric acid is a strong oxidising agent;
- it oxidises the hydrogen produced to water;
- nitric acid itself gets reduced to nitrogen oxides such as `NO`, `NO2`, or `N2O`.

Exception:

- magnesium and manganese can produce hydrogen with very dilute nitric acid.

## Aqua Regia

Aqua regia is a freshly prepared mixture of concentrated hydrochloric acid and concentrated nitric acid in the ratio 3:1.

It can dissolve gold and platinum, even though neither acid alone can dissolve gold.

It is highly corrosive and fuming.

# Unit 3I: Displacement Reactions in Salt Solutions

## General Rule

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

$$
Metal\ A + Salt\ solution\ of\ B \rightarrow Salt\ solution\ of\ A + Metal\ B
$$

This happens only if metal A is more reactive than metal B.

## Iron Nail in Copper Sulphate Solution

Iron is more reactive than copper, so iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

$$
Fe + CuSO_4 \rightarrow FeSO_4 + Cu
$$

Observations:

| Observation | Reason |
|---|---|
| Blue copper sulphate solution turns green | Iron(II) sulphate forms. |
| Brown deposit appears on iron nail | Copper metal is deposited. |
| Iron nail surface changes | Iron dissolves into solution as `Fe2+`. |

## Copper Wire in Iron Sulphate Solution

Copper is less reactive than iron, so copper cannot displace iron from iron sulphate solution.

No reaction occurs.

## Displacement Test Logic

| Observation | Conclusion |
|---|---|
| Metal A displaces B from salt solution | A is more reactive than B. |
| Metal A does not displace B | A is less reactive than B or similar in reactivity. |

# Unit 3J: Reactivity Series

## Activity Series of Metals

The reactivity series arranges metals in decreasing order of reactivity.

```text
K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au
```

## Table Form

| Position | Element | Reactivity |
|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Potassium, K | Most reactive |
| 2 | Sodium, Na | Very reactive |
| 3 | Calcium, Ca | Very reactive |
| 4 | Magnesium, Mg | Reactive |
| 5 | Aluminium, Al | Reactive |
| 6 | Zinc, Zn | Moderately reactive |
| 7 | Iron, Fe | Moderately reactive |
| 8 | Lead, Pb | Less reactive |
| 9 | Hydrogen, H | Reference point |
| 10 | Copper, Cu | Below hydrogen |
| 11 | Mercury, Hg | Low reactivity |
| 12 | Silver, Ag | Very low reactivity |
| 13 | Gold, Au | Least reactive |

## What the Series Helps You Predict

| Question | Use of Reactivity Series |
|---|---|
| Will a metal react with dilute acid? | Metals above hydrogen usually displace hydrogen. |
| Will displacement occur in salt solution? | Higher metal displaces lower metal. |
| Is metal found free in nature? | Low-reactivity metals may occur free. |
| How is a metal extracted? | High-reactivity metals need electrolysis; lower ones need simpler reduction. |
| Which metal corrodes easily? | More reactive metals generally corrode more easily. |

# Unit 3K: How Metals and Non-metals React

## Stable Electronic Configuration

Atoms react to attain a stable electronic configuration, usually like noble gases.

Noble gases have completely filled outer shells and are therefore very unreactive.

## Metals Lose Electrons

Metals have 1, 2, or 3 valence electrons in many common cases. They tend to lose these electrons.

When a metal atom loses electrons, it forms a positive ion called a cation.

Example: sodium atom to sodium ion.

$$
Na \rightarrow Na^+ + e^-
$$

Sodium electronic configuration:

```text
Na atom: 2,8,1
Na+ ion: 2,8
```

## Non-metals Gain Electrons

Non-metals often have 5, 6, or 7 valence electrons. They tend to gain electrons to complete the outer shell.

When a non-metal atom gains electrons, it forms a negative ion called an anion.

Example: chlorine atom to chloride ion.

$$
Cl + e^- \rightarrow Cl^-
$$

Chlorine electronic configuration:

```text
Cl atom: 2,8,7
Cl- ion: 2,8,8
```

## Formation of Sodium Chloride

Sodium transfers one electron to chlorine.

Step 1:

$$
Na \rightarrow Na^+ + e^-
$$

Step 2:

$$
Cl + e^- \rightarrow Cl^-
$$

Then oppositely charged ions attract:

$$
Na^+ + Cl^- \rightarrow NaCl
$$

Sodium chloride does not exist as simple molecules. It exists as a giant arrangement of oppositely charged ions.

## Formation of Magnesium Chloride

Magnesium has two valence electrons and loses both.

$$
Mg \rightarrow Mg^{2+} + 2e^-
$$

Each chlorine atom gains one electron.

$$
2Cl + 2e^- \rightarrow 2Cl^-
$$

The ions combine:

$$
Mg^{2+} + 2Cl^- \rightarrow MgCl_2
$$

## Ions Present in Magnesium Chloride

| Compound | Cation | Anion |
|---|---|---|
| `MgCl2` | `Mg2+` | `Cl-` |

## Ionic or Electrovalent Compounds

Compounds formed by transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal are called ionic compounds or electrovalent compounds.

They are held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions.

# Unit 3L: Electron-Dot Structures

## How to Represent Valence Electrons

Electron-dot structures show valence electrons as dots around the symbol of an element.

For text notes, we can represent them simply:

| Element | Electronic Configuration | Valence Electrons | Dot Idea |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Sodium, Na | 2,8,1 | 1 | `Na.` |
| Oxygen, O | 2,6 | 6 | `O` with 6 dots |
| Magnesium, Mg | 2,8,2 | 2 | `Mg:` |

## Formation of Sodium Oxide, `Na2O`

Oxygen needs two electrons to complete its octet. Two sodium atoms each lose one electron.

Sodium:

$$
2Na \rightarrow 2Na^+ + 2e^-
$$

Oxygen:

$$
O + 2e^- \rightarrow O^{2-}
$$

Combination:

$$
2Na^+ + O^{2-} \rightarrow Na_2O
$$

Ions present:

| Compound | Cation | Anion |
|---|---|---|
| `Na2O` | `Na+` | `O2-` |

## Formation of Magnesium Oxide, `MgO`

Magnesium loses two electrons. Oxygen gains two electrons.

Magnesium:

$$
Mg \rightarrow Mg^{2+} + 2e^-
$$

Oxygen:

$$
O + 2e^- \rightarrow O^{2-}
$$

Combination:

$$
Mg^{2+} + O^{2-} \rightarrow MgO
$$

Ions present:

| Compound | Cation | Anion |
|---|---|---|
| `MgO` | `Mg2+` | `O2-` |

# Unit 3M: Properties of Ionic Compounds

## 1. Physical Nature

Ionic compounds are usually:

- solid;
- hard;
- crystalline;
- brittle.

They are hard because oppositely charged ions attract strongly.

They are brittle because shifting layers can bring like charges close together, causing repulsion and breaking.

## 2. High Melting and Boiling Points

Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points because strong electrostatic attraction holds ions together.

A large amount of energy is needed to separate the ions.

Examples from the raw chapter:

| Ionic Compound | Melting Point (K) | Boiling Point (K) |
|---|---:|---:|
| NaCl | 1074 | 1686 |
| LiCl | 887 | 1600 |
| CaCl2 | 1045 | 1900 |
| CaO | 2850 | 3120 |
| MgCl2 | 981 | 1685 |

## 3. Solubility

Ionic compounds are generally:

- soluble in water;
- insoluble in kerosene and petrol.

Water can separate ions better than non-polar solvents like kerosene and petrol.

## 4. Electrical Conductivity

Ionic compounds conduct electricity only when ions can move.

| State | Conducts Electricity? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Solid | No | Ions are fixed in a rigid lattice. |
| Molten | Yes | Ions are free to move. |
| Aqueous solution | Yes | Ions are free to move in water. |

## Key Explanation

Electrical conduction in ionic substances requires mobile charged particles. Solid ionic crystals contain ions, but those ions are not free to move. Molten or dissolved ionic compounds have mobile ions.

# Unit 3N: Occurrence of Metals

## Sources of Metals

The earth's crust is the major source of metals.

Seawater also contains soluble salts such as:

- sodium chloride;
- magnesium chloride;
- other dissolved salts.

## Minerals

Minerals are elements or compounds that occur naturally in the earth's crust.

## Ores

Ores are minerals from which metals can be extracted profitably.

All ores are minerals, but all minerals are not ores.

## Gangue

Gangue is unwanted impurity mixed with ore.

Examples:

- sand;
- soil;
- clay;
- rocky material.

Before metal extraction, gangue must be removed from the ore.

This process is called concentration or enrichment of ore.

## Free and Combined State

| Metal Type | Occurrence |
|---|---|
| Very low reactivity metals | Often found in free state. |
| Moderately reactive metals | Found mainly as oxides, sulphides, or carbonates. |
| Highly reactive metals | Found only as compounds, never as free elements in nature. |

Examples:

| Reactivity Level | Metals | Common Occurrence |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Au, Ag, Pt, sometimes Cu | Free/native state. |
| Middle | Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu | Oxides, sulphides, carbonates. |
| High | K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al | Stable compounds. |

# Unit 3O: Enrichment of Ores

## Why Enrichment Is Needed

Ores mined from earth contain gangue. The gangue must be removed before extraction because it wastes fuel, reduces efficiency, and interferes with chemical reactions.

## How Enrichment Is Done

The method depends on differences between ore and gangue in:

- density;
- magnetic property;
- solubility;
- chemical behaviour;
- surface properties.

The raw chapter does not require detailed methods here, but the key concept is that ore is cleaned/concentrated before chemical extraction.

# Unit 3P: Extraction of Metals Based on Reactivity

## The Main Principle

The extraction method depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series.

| Position in Series | Examples | Extraction Method |
|---|---|---|
| Low reactivity | Hg, Cu, Ag, Au | Heating ores; simple reduction; some found free. |
| Middle reactivity | Zn, Fe, Pb | Convert sulphides/carbonates to oxides, then reduce with carbon or other reducing agent. |
| High reactivity | K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al | Electrolytic reduction of molten compounds. |

## Why Oxides Are Important

It is easier to extract many metals from oxides than from sulphides or carbonates.

Therefore:

- sulphide ores are converted into oxides by roasting;
- carbonate ores are converted into oxides by calcination.

# Unit 3Q: Extracting Metals Low in the Activity Series

## Low Reactivity Metals

Metals low in the activity series are less reactive. Their oxides can often be reduced by heating.

Examples:

- mercury;
- copper;
- silver;
- gold.

## Mercury from Cinnabar

Cinnabar is mercury sulphide, `HgS`.

First, cinnabar is heated in air to form mercuric oxide.

$$
2HgS + 3O_2 \xrightarrow{Heat} 2HgO + 2SO_2
$$

Then mercuric oxide decomposes on further heating to form mercury.

$$
2HgO \xrightarrow{Heat} 2Hg + O_2
$$

## Copper from Copper Sulphide

Copper is found as copper(I) sulphide, `Cu2S`, in some ores.

On heating in air:

$$
2Cu_2S + 3O_2 \xrightarrow{Heat} 2Cu_2O + 2SO_2
$$

Then copper(I) oxide reacts with copper(I) sulphide to form copper.

$$
2Cu_2O + Cu_2S \xrightarrow{Heat} 6Cu + SO_2
$$

## Learner Note

Low-reactivity metals need less forceful chemical extraction because they do not hold oxygen or sulphur as strongly as highly reactive metals do.

# Unit 3R: Extracting Metals in the Middle of the Activity Series

## Middle Reactivity Metals

Examples:

- zinc;
- iron;
- lead;
- copper.

These metals are often found as:

- sulphides;
- carbonates;
- oxides.

## Roasting

Roasting is heating sulphide ore strongly in excess air to convert it into oxide.

Example: zinc sulphide to zinc oxide.

$$
2ZnS + 3O_2 \xrightarrow{Heat} 2ZnO + 2SO_2
$$

## Calcination

Calcination is heating carbonate ore strongly in limited air to convert it into oxide.

Example: zinc carbonate to zinc oxide.

$$
ZnCO_3 \xrightarrow{Heat} ZnO + CO_2
$$

## Reduction with Carbon

The metal oxide is then reduced to metal using carbon.

Example:

$$
ZnO + C \rightarrow Zn + CO
$$

Here, carbon removes oxygen from zinc oxide.

## Reduction as a Concept

Obtaining a metal from its oxide is a reduction process.

In the example:

- zinc oxide is reduced to zinc;
- carbon is oxidised to carbon monoxide.

## Displacement as a Reduction Method

Highly reactive metals such as aluminium can reduce oxides of less reactive metals.

Example: manganese dioxide with aluminium.

$$
3MnO_2 + 4Al \rightarrow 3Mn + 2Al_2O_3 + Heat
$$

In this reaction:

| Substance | Change |
|---|---|
| Aluminium | Oxidised to aluminium oxide. |
| Manganese dioxide | Reduced to manganese. |

## Thermit Reaction

The thermit reaction uses aluminium to reduce iron(III) oxide.

$$
Fe_2O_3 + 2Al \rightarrow 2Fe + Al_2O_3 + Heat
$$

This reaction is highly exothermic. The iron produced is molten and can be used to join railway tracks or repair cracked machine parts.

# Unit 3S: Extracting Metals High in the Activity Series

## Highly Reactive Metals

Examples:

- potassium;
- sodium;
- calcium;
- magnesium;
- aluminium.

These metals cannot be extracted by heating their oxides with carbon.

## Why Carbon Reduction Fails

Highly reactive metals have a stronger affinity for oxygen than carbon does.

Carbon cannot remove oxygen from their oxides effectively.

## Electrolytic Reduction

Highly reactive metals are extracted by electrolysis of molten compounds.

For sodium from molten sodium chloride:

At cathode:

$$
Na^+ + e^- \rightarrow Na
$$

At anode:

$$
2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2 + 2e^-
$$

## Electrode Rule

| Electrode | Charge in Electrolysis | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Cathode | Negative | Metal cations gain electrons and form metal. |
| Anode | Positive | Non-metal anions lose electrons and form non-metal. |

For molten sodium chloride:

- sodium metal is deposited at the cathode;
- chlorine gas is liberated at the anode.

# Unit 3T: Refining of Metals

## Why Refining Is Needed

Metals obtained after reduction are often impure.

Impurities may affect:

- strength;
- conductivity;
- appearance;
- corrosion resistance;
- usefulness in machines or wires.

## Electrolytic Refining

Electrolytic refining is the most widely used method for refining many metals.

Examples:

- copper;
- zinc;
- tin;
- nickel;
- silver;
- gold.

## Setup for Electrolytic Refining

| Part | Material Used |
|---|---|
| Anode | Impure metal |
| Cathode | Thin strip of pure metal |
| Electrolyte | Solution of a soluble salt of the same metal |

For copper refining:

| Part | Material |
|---|---|
| Anode | Impure copper |
| Cathode | Pure copper strip |
| Electrolyte | Acidified copper sulphate solution |

## What Happens During Refining

At the anode:

$$
Cu \rightarrow Cu^{2+} + 2e^-
$$

At the cathode:

$$
Cu^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu
$$

Pure copper is deposited on the cathode.

## Anode Mud

Insoluble impurities settle at the bottom of the anode. This deposit is called anode mud.

Sometimes anode mud contains valuable metals such as silver and gold.

# Unit 3U: Corrosion

## Meaning

Corrosion is the slow damage of a metal surface due to reaction with substances in the environment.

## Examples of Corrosion

| Metal | Corrosion Observation | Compound/Layer |
|---|---|---|
| Silver | Turns black | Silver sulphide |
| Copper | Green coating | Basic copper carbonate |
| Iron | Brown flaky rust | Hydrated iron(III) oxide |

## Silver Tarnishing

Silver reacts with sulphur compounds in air to form black silver sulphide.

$$
2Ag + H_2S \rightarrow Ag_2S + H_2
$$

This is a simplified representation of tarnishing.

## Copper Corrosion

Copper reacts slowly with moist carbon dioxide and oxygen in air to form a green layer of basic copper carbonate.

The green layer is commonly represented as:

$$
CuCO_3.Cu(OH)_2
$$

## Rusting of Iron

Iron rusts in the presence of both air and water.

Rust is hydrated iron(III) oxide, commonly represented as:

$$
Fe_2O_3.xH_2O
$$

## Conditions Needed for Rusting

Iron rusts when both are present:

- oxygen/air;
- water/moisture.

## Test Tube Experiment Logic

| Test Tube | Condition | Rusting? | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Iron nail with water and air | Yes | Both air and water are present. |
| B | Boiled water plus oil layer | No | Water is present, but dissolved air is removed and oil blocks air. |
| C | Dry air with anhydrous calcium chloride | No | Air is present, but moisture is absent. |

## Conclusion

Iron rusts only when both oxygen and moisture are available.

# Unit 3V: Prevention of Corrosion

## Common Methods

Rusting of iron can be prevented by:

- painting;
- oiling;
- greasing;
- galvanising;
- chrome plating;
- anodising;
- alloying.

## How Coatings Work

Coatings prevent contact between iron and:

- oxygen;
- moisture;
- corrosive gases;
- salts.

## Galvanisation

Galvanisation is coating iron or steel with a thin layer of zinc.

Zinc protects iron because:

- it forms a protective layer on the surface;
- it is more reactive than iron;
- even if scratched, zinc corrodes preferentially and protects iron.

## Chrome Plating

Chrome plating gives a protective and shiny chromium coating.

It is used on:

- bicycle parts;
- bathroom fittings;
- vehicle parts;
- decorative metal objects.

## Anodising

Anodising forms a thick protective oxide layer on aluminium.

It improves corrosion resistance and can give coloured finishes.

## Alloying

Alloying changes the properties of a metal by mixing it with other elements.

Example: stainless steel is made by mixing iron with chromium and nickel.

Stainless steel is hard and does not rust easily.

# Unit 3W: Alloys

## Definition

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.

## Why Alloys Are Made

Alloying improves properties such as:

- hardness;
- strength;
- corrosion resistance;
- melting point;
- electrical resistance;
- appearance.

## Common Alloys

| Alloy | Composition | Use/Property |
|---|---|---|
| Brass | Copper and zinc | Harder than copper; used in fittings and instruments. |
| Bronze | Copper and tin | Strong and corrosion-resistant. |
| Solder | Lead and tin | Low melting point; used to join electrical wires. |
| Stainless steel | Iron, chromium, nickel, carbon | Hard and corrosion-resistant. |
| Amalgam | Alloy containing mercury | Special uses depending on metal mixed. |

## 24 Carat and 22 Carat Gold

Pure gold is called 24 carat gold. It is very soft and not suitable for making durable jewellery.

In India, 22 carat gold is commonly used for ornaments.

Meaning:

```text
22 parts gold + 2 parts copper or silver
```

The added metal makes gold harder.

## Electrical Conductivity and Melting Point of Alloys

The electrical conductivity and melting point of an alloy are generally lower than those of the pure metals.

Example:

- copper is used in electrical circuits because it conducts well;
- brass and bronze are poorer conductors than pure copper.

$$

$$

## Carbonate Ore to Oxide

$$
Metal\ carbonate \xrightarrow{Heat} Metal\ oxide + Carbon\ dioxide
$$

Example:

$$
ZnCO_3 \xrightarrow{Heat} ZnO + CO_2
$$

| Forgetting the hydrogen reference in the reactivity series. | Metals above hydrogen displace hydrogen from dilute acids. |
| Predicting displacement without the reactivity series. | A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from salt solution. |
| Saying ionic compounds conduct electricity in solid state. | Solid ionic compounds do not conduct because ions cannot move. |
| Confusing roasting and calcination. | Roasting is for sulphide ore in excess air; calcination is for carbonate ore in limited air. |
| Saying rusting needs only air. | Rusting of iron needs both air and water. |
| Saying galvanisation fails if scratched. | Zinc still protects iron because it is more reactive and corrodes first. |

Understand:

- non-metals gain electrons and form anions;
- ionic compounds conduct only when ions can move;
- displacement depends on relative reactivity;
- extraction becomes harder as metal reactivity increases;
- rusting requires both oxygen and moisture;
- alloying changes properties deliberately.

## Best Exam Answer Pattern

For reaction-prediction questions:

1. Locate both metals in the reactivity series.
2. Identify which metal is more reactive.
3. State whether displacement occurs.
4. Write the balanced equation if reaction occurs.
5. Mention the observation if asked.

Example:

```text
Iron is more reactive than copper, so iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.
Fe + CuSO4 -> FeSO4 + Cu
The blue solution turns green and copper is deposited.
```

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Metals are generally lustrous, hard, malleable, ductile, sonorous, and good conductors of heat and electricity. Non-metals generally show opposite properties, but important exceptions exist, such as graphite, iodine, diamond, mercury, and alkali metals.

Metals usually form basic oxides. Non-metals usually form acidic or neutral oxides. Aluminium oxide and zinc oxide are amphoteric because they react with both acids and bases.

The reactivity series is:

```text
K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au
```

Metals above hydrogen displace hydrogen from dilute acids. More reactive metals displace less reactive metals from salt solutions.

Metals and non-metals often form ionic compounds by electron transfer. Ionic compounds are hard, brittle, have high melting points, dissolve in water, and conduct electricity in molten or aqueous state.

Metals occur as minerals and ores. Low-reactivity metals may occur free. Middle-reactivity metals are extracted by converting ores to oxides and reducing them. High-reactivity metals are extracted by electrolysis.

Corrosion damages metals. Iron rusts only when both air and water are present. Rusting can be prevented by painting, oiling, greasing, galvanising, chrome plating, anodising, and alloying.
