# Unit 10A: Human Eye

## Eye as an Optical Instrument

The human eye works somewhat like a camera.

It uses a lens system to form an image on a light-sensitive screen called the retina.

## Main Parts and Functions

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Cornea | Transparent bulge at front; does most refraction of incoming light. |
| Iris | Controls pupil size. |
| Pupil | Controls amount of light entering eye. |
| Eye lens | Fine-adjusts focus so image forms on retina. |
| Ciliary muscles | Change curvature of eye lens. |
| Retina | Light-sensitive screen where image forms. |
| Light-sensitive cells | Convert light into electrical signals. |
| Optic nerve | Sends signals to brain. |
| Brain | Interprets signals so we perceive objects correctly. |

## Image Formed by Eye

The eye lens forms an image on the retina that is:

- real;
- inverted;
- smaller than object.

The brain interprets the signals, so we perceive the object upright.

## Size of Eyeball

The eyeball is approximately spherical.

Its diameter is about:

```text
2.3 cm
```

## Cornea vs Eye Lens

Most refraction happens at the cornea.

The crystalline lens mainly provides fine adjustment of focal length.

# Unit 10B: Power of Accommodation

## Meaning

Power of accommodation is the ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length so that objects at different distances can be focused clearly on the retina.

## Seeing Distant Objects

When looking at distant objects:

- ciliary muscles relax;
- eye lens becomes thin;
- focal length increases;
- distant object is focused on retina.

## Seeing Nearby Objects

When looking at nearby objects:

- ciliary muscles contract;
- lens becomes thicker;
- curvature increases;
- focal length decreases;
- nearby object is focused on retina.

## Near Point

Near point is the minimum distance at which an object can be seen clearly without strain.

For a young adult with normal vision:

```text
Near point = 25 cm
```

It is also called least distance of distinct vision.

## Far Point

Far point is the farthest point up to which the eye can see clearly.

For a normal eye:

```text
Far point = infinity
```

## Normal Range of Clear Vision

A normal young adult eye can see clearly from:

```text
25 cm to infinity
```

# Unit 10C: Cataract

## Meaning

Cataract is a condition in which the crystalline lens becomes milky and cloudy, usually in old age.

## Effect

Cataract can cause:

- blurred vision;
- partial loss of vision;
- complete loss of vision in severe cases.

## Correction

Vision can often be restored through cataract surgery.

---

# Unit 10D: Myopia

## Meaning

Myopia is near-sightedness.

A person with myopia can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects distinctly.

## Image Formation

In myopia, image of a distant object forms in front of the retina instead of on the retina.

## Causes

Myopia may occur due to:

- excessive curvature of eye lens;
- elongation of eyeball.

## Correction

Myopia is corrected using a concave lens of suitable power.

## Why Concave Lens Works

A concave lens diverges incoming rays before they enter the eye.

This shifts the image backward onto the retina.

---

# Unit 10E: Hypermetropia

## Meaning

Hypermetropia is far-sightedness.

A person with hypermetropia can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects distinctly.

## Image Formation

In hypermetropia, light from a nearby object focuses behind the retina.

## Causes

Hypermetropia may occur due to:

- focal length of eye lens being too long;
- eyeball being too small.

## Correction

Hypermetropia is corrected using a convex lens of suitable power.

## Why Convex Lens Works

A convex lens converges light rays before they enter the eye.

It provides additional focusing power and brings the image onto the retina.

---

# Unit 10F: Presbyopia

## Meaning

Presbyopia is an age-related defect in which the power of accommodation decreases.

## Cause

It occurs due to:

- weakening of ciliary muscles;
- reduced flexibility of eye lens.

## Effect

Near point moves farther away.

The person finds it difficult to see nearby objects clearly.

## Bifocal Lenses

Some people suffer from both myopia and hypermetropia.

They may use bifocal lenses:

| Part of Lens | Type | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Upper part | Concave | Distant vision |
| Lower part | Convex | Near vision |

Contact lenses and surgery can also correct some refractive defects.

# Unit 10G: Refraction Through a Prism

## Triangular Glass Prism

A triangular glass prism has:

- two triangular bases;
- three rectangular lateral surfaces;
- refracting surfaces inclined to each other.

## Angle of Prism

The angle between two refracting faces of the prism is called the angle of the prism.

## Ray Path Through Prism

Important rays:

| Symbol/Name | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Incident ray | Ray entering prism. |
| Refracted ray | Ray inside prism. |
| Emergent ray | Ray leaving prism. |

At first surface:

- light enters from air to glass;
- bends towards normal.

At second surface:

- light enters from glass to air;
- bends away from normal.

## Angle of Deviation

Because prism faces are inclined, the emergent ray is not parallel to the incident ray.

The angle between direction of incident ray and emergent ray is called angle of deviation.

## Difference from Glass Slab

| Glass Slab | Prism |
|---|---|
| Opposite faces are parallel. | Refracting faces are inclined. |
| Emergent ray is parallel to incident ray. | Emergent ray deviates from original direction. |
| Causes lateral displacement. | Causes angular deviation and dispersion. |

# Unit 10H: Dispersion of White Light

## Meaning

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its component colours.

## Spectrum

The band of colours formed by dispersion is called spectrum.

Sequence:

```text
Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red
```

Shortcut:

```text
VIBGYOR
```

## Why Dispersion Occurs

Different colours of light bend by different amounts in a prism.

| Colour | Bending |
|---|---|
| Red | Least |
| Violet | Most |

Therefore, colours separate and form a spectrum.

## Newton's Prism Experiment

Isaac Newton used a prism to split sunlight into a spectrum.

He then used a second inverted prism to recombine the colours into white light.

Conclusion:

White light is made of seven colours.

# Unit 10I: Rainbow Formation

## Meaning

A rainbow is a natural spectrum formed in the sky after rain.

## Conditions

A rainbow is seen when:

- sunlight is present;
- tiny water droplets are in the atmosphere;
- observer faces the water droplets with the Sun behind them.

## Role of Water Droplets

Water droplets act like small prisms.

In each droplet:

1. Sunlight enters and refracts.
2. White light disperses into colours.
3. Light reflects internally.
4. Light refracts again as it leaves.
5. Different colours reach the observer's eye.

## Direction

A rainbow is formed in a direction opposite to the Sun.

You can also see rainbow-like colours near waterfalls or fountains with Sun behind you.

# Unit 10J: Atmospheric Refraction

## Meaning

Atmospheric refraction is refraction of light by the earth's atmosphere.

The atmosphere has layers of changing density and refractive index.

## Wavering Above Fire

Hot air above fire is less dense and has lower refractive index than cooler air above it.

Since air conditions keep changing, light bends irregularly.

This makes objects seen through hot air appear to waver.

## Twinkling of Stars

Stars twinkle because:

- starlight passes through atmosphere of changing refractive index;
- light bends continuously;
- apparent position of star changes slightly;
- amount of light entering eye changes;
- star appears brighter and fainter rapidly.

## Why Stars Are Affected Strongly

Stars are very far away and appear as point sources of light.

Small changes in light path cause noticeable brightness changes.

## Why Planets Do Not Twinkle Much

Planets are closer and appear as extended sources.

They can be treated as many point sources together.

Brightness variations from different points average out.

Therefore, planets generally do not twinkle.

## Advanced Sunrise and Delayed Sunset

The Sun is visible about 2 minutes before actual sunrise and about 2 minutes after actual sunset.

Reason:

Atmospheric refraction bends sunlight, making the Sun appear above the horizon even when it is actually slightly below it.

## Flattened Sun at Sunrise/Sunset

The apparent flattening of the Sun's disc at sunrise and sunset is also due to atmospheric refraction.

# Unit 10K: Scattering of Light

## Meaning

Scattering is the spreading of light in different directions by particles.

Atmosphere contains:

- air molecules;
- dust particles;
- smoke;
- tiny water droplets;
- suspended particles.

## Tyndall Effect

Tyndall effect is scattering of light by colloidal particles, making the path of light visible.

Examples:

- sunlight entering a smoke-filled room through a small hole;
- sunlight passing through mist in a dense forest;
- light beam visible in dusty air.

## Particle Size and Colour

| Particle Size | Scattered Light |
|---|---|
| Very fine particles | Mainly blue light |
| Larger particles | Longer wavelengths too |
| Very large particles | Scattered light may appear white |

# Unit 10L: Why the Sky Is Blue

## Explanation

Air molecules and very fine particles in the atmosphere are smaller than the wavelength of visible light.

They scatter shorter wavelengths more strongly than longer wavelengths.

Blue light has shorter wavelength than red light.

Therefore, blue light is scattered more and reaches our eyes from all directions.

So the clear sky appears blue.

## If Earth Had No Atmosphere

There would be no scattering.

The sky would appear dark.

This is why the sky appears dark to astronauts or passengers at very high altitudes where scattering is weak.

## Why Danger Signals Are Red

Red light has longer wavelength and is scattered least by fog, smoke, and dust.

Therefore, red light can travel longer distances and remain visible.

That is why danger signals are red.

## Red Sun at Sunrise and Sunset

At sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels a longer path through the atmosphere.

Much of the blue light is scattered away.

The red/orange light reaches our eyes more strongly, so the Sun appears reddish.

# Unit 10M: Eye Donation Note

## Key Ideas

Eye donation can help people with corneal blindness.

Important points from the chapter:

- eyes should be removed within 4-6 hours after death;
- people of many ages and backgrounds can donate eyes;
- even people who use spectacles or had cataract surgery may donate;
- eye removal does not disfigure the face;
- donated eyes are evaluated by an eye bank;
- unsuitable eyes may still help research and education.

Some infections or diseases can prevent donation, so eye banks follow medical standards.

## Exercise 5: Lens Powers Are -5.5 D for Distant Vision and +1.5 D for Near Vision. Find Focal Lengths.

$$

|---|---:|---:|

## Exercise 6: Far Point of Myopic Person Is 80 cm. Find Nature and Power of Lens.

## Idea

For a distant object, the corrective lens should form a virtual image at the person's far point.

Given:

```text
u = -infinity
v = -80 cm = -0.80 m
```

## Working

Lens formula:

$$
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}
$$

For object at infinity:

$$
\frac{1}{u} = 0
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{v}
$$

$$
f = v = -0.80\ m
$$

Power:

$$
P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{-0.80} = -1.25D
$$

## Answer

The required lens is concave.

Power:

```text
-1.25 D
```

---

## Exercise 7: Hypermetropic Eye Near Point Is 1 m. Find Power of Corrective Lens.

## Idea

The person should be able to read at normal near point:

```text
u = -25 cm = -0.25 m
```

The lens should form a virtual image at the person's near point:

```text
v = -1 m
```

## Working

Lens formula:

$$
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{-1} - \frac{1}{-0.25}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{f} = -1 + 4 = 3
$$

$$
f = \frac{1}{3} = 0.333\ m
$$

Power:

$$
P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{0.333} = +3D
$$

## Answer

The required lens is convex.

Power:

```text
+3 D
```

## Diagram Description

Draw:

1. Hypermetropic eye with nearby object focusing behind retina.
2. A convex lens placed before the eye.
3. Convex lens converging rays slightly before they enter eye.
4. Eye lens then focuses image on retina.

## Exercise 8: Why Can't a Normal Eye See Objects Closer Than 25 cm Clearly?

## Answer

The eye lens has a limit to accommodation.

For very close objects, ciliary muscles cannot make the lens thick enough to reduce focal length further.

Therefore, image cannot be focused clearly on the retina and the eye feels strain.

---

## Exercise 9: What Happens to Image Distance in Eye When Object Distance Increases?

## Answer

Image distance in the eye remains nearly constant because the distance between eye lens and retina is fixed.

When object distance changes, the eye changes focal length of its lens to keep the image on the retina.

---

## Exercise 10: Why Do Stars Twinkle?

## Answer

Stars twinkle due to atmospheric refraction.

Starlight passes through layers of air with changing refractive index.

The path of light changes slightly and continuously.

Since stars appear as point sources, small changes in light path cause changes in apparent position and brightness.

Thus stars appear to twinkle.

---

## Exercise 11: Why Do Planets Not Twinkle?

## Answer

Planets are much closer than stars and appear as extended sources of light.

They can be considered as many point sources close together.

The brightness changes from different points average out.

Therefore, planets do not twinkle noticeably.

---

## Exercise 12: Why Does Sky Appear Dark Instead of Blue to an Astronaut?

## Answer

Blue sky is caused by scattering of sunlight by air molecules.

In space, there is no atmosphere to scatter sunlight.

Therefore, the sky appears dark to an astronaut.

# Unit 10P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying the eye image forms on the cornea. | Image forms on the retina. |
| Confusing pupil and iris. | Pupil is opening; iris controls pupil size. |
| Saying eye lens does most refraction. | Most refraction occurs at cornea; lens fine-adjusts focus. |
| Mixing myopia and hypermetropia. | Myopia: distant blur, concave lens. Hypermetropia: near blur, convex lens. |
| Using centimetres in power formula. | Convert focal length to metres for `P = 1/f`. |
| Saying prism creates colours. | Prism separates colours already present in white light. |
| Saying stars twinkle because they move. | Twinkling is due to atmospheric refraction. |
| Saying planets never refract through atmosphere. | Planet light refracts too, but variations average out because planets are extended sources. |
| Saying sky is blue because air is blue. | Sky is blue because shorter wavelength blue light scatters more. |

## One-Page Chapter Summary

The human eye forms a real inverted image on the retina. The brain interprets signals from the retina so that we perceive objects correctly. The cornea does most refraction, while the eye lens fine-adjusts focus by accommodation.

Myopia makes distant objects blurred and is corrected by a concave lens. Hypermetropia makes nearby objects blurred and is corrected by a convex lens. Presbyopia is age-related loss of accommodation. Cataract is clouding of the lens.

A prism refracts light through inclined surfaces and causes deviation. White light disperses into seven colours because different colours bend by different amounts. Rainbow forms when raindrops refract, disperse, internally reflect, and refract sunlight.

Atmospheric refraction causes twinkling of stars, advanced sunrise, delayed sunset, and apparent flattening of the Sun. Scattering causes the blue sky; red light scatters least, so danger signals are red.
