## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Acid** | Substance that gives hydrogen ions, `H+`, in aqueous solution. Acids usually taste sour, but tasting chemicals is unsafe. |
| **Base** | Substance that gives hydroxide ions, `OH-`, in aqueous solution or neutralises acids. Many bases feel soapy and taste bitter, but direct testing by touch/taste is unsafe. |
| **Alkali** | A base that is soluble in water. All alkalis are bases, but all bases are not alkalis. |
| **Indicator** | A substance that changes colour or smell depending on acidic/basic nature. |
| **Litmus** | Natural indicator obtained from lichens; red in acid and blue in base. |
| **Olfactory indicator** | Substance whose smell changes in acidic or basic medium, such as onion, vanilla, or clove. |
| **Neutralisation** | Reaction between an acid and a base to produce salt and water. |
| **Salt** | Ionic compound formed when the replaceable hydrogen of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion. |
| **pH** | A number that tells how acidic or basic a solution is. |
| **Hydronium ion** | `H3O+`, formed when `H+` combines with water. |
| **Universal indicator** | Mixture of indicators showing different colours for different pH values. |
| **Water of crystallisation** | Fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a crystalline salt. |
| **Brine** | Concentrated aqueous solution of sodium chloride. |
| **Chlor-alkali process** | Electrolysis of brine to produce chlorine, hydrogen, and sodium hydroxide. |
| **Bleaching powder** | Calcium oxychloride/calcium hypochlorite type compound used for bleaching and disinfecting. |
| **Baking soda** | Sodium hydrogencarbonate, `NaHCO3`. |
| **Washing soda** | Sodium carbonate decahydrate, `Na2CO3.10H2O`. |
| **Plaster of Paris** | Calcium sulphate hemihydrate, `CaSO4.1/2H2O`. |

## Big Ideas

Acids and bases are not just laboratory chemicals. They appear in food, digestion, soil, rainwater, cleaning materials, medicines, baking, and building materials.

Their behaviour depends strongly on ions. Acids show acidic character because they produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions in water. Bases show basic character because they produce `OH-` ions in water.

The pH scale is a compact way to express acidity and basicity. It helps us compare lemon juice, blood, stomach acid, soap solution, milk, rainwater, and soil.

Salts connect acid-base chemistry with industry and everyday life. Common salt is not only a food ingredient; it is also the starting material for sodium hydroxide, chlorine, hydrogen, bleaching powder, baking soda, washing soda, and other useful compounds.

# Unit 2B: Indicators

## What Is an Indicator?

An indicator is a substance that gives a visible or detectable change in acidic or basic medium. The change may be:

- a colour change;
- a smell change;
- a pH-colour scale change.

## Litmus

Litmus is a natural indicator obtained from lichens.

| Medium | Effect on Litmus |
|---|---|
| Acidic solution | Blue litmus turns red. |
| Basic solution | Red litmus turns blue. |
| Neutral solution | No change in red or blue litmus. |

## Other Common Indicators

| Indicator | In Acid | In Base | Learner Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Turmeric** | Yellow | Reddish brown | Used in household testing of soap/basic substances. |
| **Phenolphthalein** | Colourless | Pink | Very common laboratory indicator. |
| **Methyl orange** | Red | Yellow | Useful for acid-base titration. |
| **Universal indicator** | Red/orange/yellow shades for acids | Blue/violet shades for bases | Gives approximate pH value. |

## Olfactory Indicators

Olfactory indicators are substances whose smell changes in acidic or basic medium.

Examples:

- onion;
- vanilla;
- clove.

Typical idea: the smell may be retained in one medium and reduced or lost in another. This allows identification without relying only on colour.

## Testing Unknown Solutions with Only Red Litmus

If only red litmus paper is available:

1. Dip red litmus in the first solution.
2. If it turns blue, that solution is basic.
3. Use the blue litmus paper produced in step 2 to test other solutions.
4. A solution that turns blue litmus red is acidic.
5. A solution that changes neither red nor blue litmus is neutral.

This is a common reasoning question because it checks whether you understand indicator colour changes, not just memorised facts.

# Unit 2C: Reaction of Acids with Metals

## General Reaction

When an acid reacts with a suitable metal, salt and hydrogen gas are formed.

$$
Acid + Metal \rightarrow Salt + Hydrogen
$$

## Example: Zinc with Dilute Sulphuric Acid

$$
Zn + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow ZnSO_4 + H_2
$$

Word equation:

```text
Zinc + Sulphuric acid -> Zinc sulphate + Hydrogen
```

## Example: Zinc with Hydrochloric Acid

$$
Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2
$$

## How to Test Hydrogen Gas

Hydrogen burns with a pop sound.

Procedure idea:

1. Bring a burning splint near the mouth of the test tube.
2. If hydrogen is present, it burns with a small pop sound.

## Important Observations

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Bubbles appear | A gas is evolving. |
| Gas burns with pop sound | The gas is hydrogen. |
| Test tube becomes warm | Reaction may be exothermic. |
| Metal slowly disappears | Metal is reacting to form salt solution. |

## Why Some Metals React More Vigorously

Metals differ in reactivity. Reactive metals like zinc and magnesium react more readily with dilute acids than less reactive metals.

In this chapter, focus on the pattern:

$$
Metal + Dilute\ acid \rightarrow Salt + H_2
$$

# Unit 2D: Reaction of Bases with Metals

Some bases react with certain metals to produce salt and hydrogen gas.

## Example: Sodium Hydroxide with Zinc

$$
2NaOH + Zn \rightarrow Na_2ZnO_2 + H_2
$$

In this reaction:

| Part | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `NaOH` | Sodium hydroxide, a strong base. |
| `Zn` | Zinc metal. |
| `Na2ZnO2` | Sodium zincate. |
| `H2` | Hydrogen gas. |

## Learner Note

This reaction shows that hydrogen gas is not produced only by acids. Under suitable conditions, some metals also react with strong bases to produce hydrogen.

# Unit 2E: Reaction of Acids with Metal Carbonates and Hydrogencarbonates

## General Pattern

Acids react with metal carbonates and metal hydrogencarbonates to produce:

- salt;
- carbon dioxide;
- water.

## Acid + Metal Carbonate

$$
Metal\ carbonate + Acid \rightarrow Salt + Carbon\ dioxide + Water
$$

Example:

$$
Na_2CO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2O + CO_2
$$

Word equation:

```text
Sodium carbonate + Hydrochloric acid -> Sodium chloride + Water + Carbon dioxide
```

## Acid + Metal Hydrogencarbonate

$$
Metal\ hydrogencarbonate + Acid \rightarrow Salt + Carbon\ dioxide + Water
$$

Example:

$$
NaHCO_3 + HCl \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O + CO_2
$$

Word equation:

```text
Sodium hydrogencarbonate + Hydrochloric acid -> Sodium chloride + Water + Carbon dioxide
```

## Why Effervescence Occurs

Effervescence means brisk bubbling. It occurs because carbon dioxide gas is released.

If you add dilute acid to a carbonate or hydrogencarbonate and bubbles appear, suspect that `CO2` may be forming.

## Test for Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide turns lime water milky.

$$
Ca(OH)_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + H_2O
$$

Here, insoluble calcium carbonate forms. It appears as a milky white suspension.

## What Happens with Excess Carbon Dioxide?

If excess carbon dioxide is passed through lime water, the milkiness disappears because soluble calcium hydrogencarbonate forms.

$$
CaCO_3 + H_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow Ca(HCO_3)_2
$$

## Diagnostic Summary

| Test | Positive Observation | Inference |
|---|---|---|
| Acid added to carbonate/hydrogencarbonate | Effervescence | A gas is released. |
| Gas passed through lime water | Lime water turns milky | Gas is carbon dioxide. |
| Excess gas passed through milky lime water | Milkiness disappears | Calcium hydrogencarbonate forms. |

# Unit 2F: Neutralisation

## Meaning

Neutralisation is the reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.

$$
Acid + Base \rightarrow Salt + Water
$$

## Example: Hydrochloric Acid and Sodium Hydroxide

$$
HCl + NaOH \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O
$$

In words:

```text
Hydrochloric acid + Sodium hydroxide -> Sodium chloride + Water
```

## Ionic Meaning of Neutralisation

The key ionic reaction is:

$$
H^+(aq) + OH^-(aq) \rightarrow H_2O(l)
$$

The acid supplies `H+` ions. The base supplies `OH-` ions. These ions combine to form water.

## Why Neutralisation Matters

Neutralisation is used in:

- treating acidity in the stomach;
- correcting soil pH;
- treating acidic stings;
- preparing salts;
- controlling industrial waste before disposal.

# Unit 2G: Reaction of Acids with Metallic Oxides

## Metallic Oxides Are Basic in Nature

Metallic oxides generally behave like bases. They react with acids to form salt and water.

## General Reaction

$$
Metallic\ oxide + Acid \rightarrow Salt + Water
$$

## Example: Copper Oxide with Hydrochloric Acid

$$
CuO + 2HCl \rightarrow CuCl_2 + H_2O
$$

Observations:

| Observation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Black copper oxide dissolves | It reacts with hydrochloric acid. |
| Blue-green solution forms | Copper chloride solution is formed. |
| Water forms | Oxide ion part combines with hydrogen from acid. |

## Important Inference

Since metallic oxides react with acids to form salt and water, they are basic oxides.

# Unit 2H: Reaction of Bases with Non-Metallic Oxides

## Non-Metallic Oxides Are Acidic in Nature

Non-metallic oxides generally react with bases to form salt and water. Therefore, many non-metallic oxides are acidic oxides.

## General Reaction

$$
Nonmetallic\ oxide + Base \rightarrow Salt + Water
$$

## Example: Carbon Dioxide with Calcium Hydroxide

$$
CO_2 + Ca(OH)_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + H_2O
$$

This is the same reaction used in the lime water test for carbon dioxide.

## Concept Link

Because carbon dioxide reacts with a base to form salt and water, carbon dioxide is acidic in nature.

# Unit 2I: What Do Acids and Bases Have in Common?

## Acids Produce Hydrogen Ions in Water

Acids show acidic behaviour because they produce hydrogen ions in aqueous solution.

Example:

$$
HCl \rightarrow H^+ + Cl^-
$$

But free `H+` ions do not exist alone in water. They combine with water molecules to form hydronium ions.

$$
H^+ + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+
$$

So hydrochloric acid in water is better represented as:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

## Why HCl Is Acidic but Glucose and Alcohol Are Not

Hydrochloric acid, nitric acid, and sulphuric acid produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions in water.

Glucose and alcohol contain hydrogen atoms, but they do not produce hydrogen ions in water. Therefore, they are not acids.

| Substance | Contains Hydrogen? | Produces `H+` in Water? | Acidic? |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| HCl | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| HNO3 | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| H2SO4 | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Glucose | Yes | No | No |
| Alcohol | Yes | No | No |

## Why Acid Solutions Conduct Electricity

Acid solutions conduct electricity because they contain ions.

For example, aqueous hydrochloric acid contains:

- `H3O+`;
- `Cl-`.

These ions move through the solution and carry electric charge.

## Why Dry HCl Gas Does Not Turn Dry Blue Litmus Red

Dry hydrogen chloride gas does not show acidic behaviour in the absence of water because it does not produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions without water.

With water:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

Therefore:

| Condition | Effect on Blue Litmus |
|---|---|
| Dry HCl gas + dry blue litmus | No colour change. |
| HCl gas + moist blue litmus | Blue litmus turns red. |

## Bases Produce Hydroxide Ions in Water

Bases show basic behaviour because they produce hydroxide ions in aqueous solution.

Examples:

$$
NaOH(s) \rightarrow Na^+(aq) + OH^-(aq)
$$

$$
KOH(s) \rightarrow K^+(aq) + OH^-(aq)
$$

$$
Ca(OH)_2(s) \rightarrow Ca^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^-(aq)
$$

## Alkalis

Bases soluble in water are called alkalis.

Examples:

| Alkali | Formula |
|---|---|
| Sodium hydroxide | `NaOH` |
| Potassium hydroxide | `KOH` |
| Calcium hydroxide | `Ca(OH)2` |

Important distinction:

- all alkalis are bases;
- not all bases dissolve in water;
- therefore, not all bases are alkalis.

# Unit 2J: Dilution of Acids and Bases

## What Is Dilution?

Dilution means decreasing the concentration of a solution by adding water.

When water is added to an acid or base solution, the concentration of ions per unit volume decreases.

## Dilution of Acid

An acid solution contains `H3O+` ions. On dilution, the number of `H3O+` ions per unit volume decreases.

This means the solution becomes less acidic.

## Dilution of Base

A base solution contains `OH-` ions. On dilution, the number of `OH-` ions per unit volume decreases.

This means the solution becomes less basic.

## Safe Dilution Rule for Acids

Always add acid slowly to water while stirring.

Do not add water directly to concentrated acid.

## Why This Rule Matters

Dilution of concentrated acid is highly exothermic. If water is added to concentrated acid, heat may cause the mixture to splash out. This can cause burns.

Correct method:

```text
Acid -> Water
```

Unsafe method:

```text
Water -> Concentrated acid
```

## Effect of Adding Excess Base to an Acid

If excess base is added to an acid, `OH-` ion concentration increases after all available acid has been neutralised.

This makes the final solution basic.

# Unit 2K: Strength of Acids and Bases

## Strong and Weak Acids

The strength of an acid depends on how many hydrogen ions it produces in water.

| Acid Type | Ion Production | Example Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Strong acid | Produces a large amount of `H+`/`H3O+` ions | HCl |
| Weak acid | Produces a smaller amount of `H+`/`H3O+` ions | Acetic acid |

## Strong and Weak Bases

The strength of a base depends on how many hydroxide ions it produces in water.

| Base Type | Ion Production | Example Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Strong base | Produces a large amount of `OH-` ions | NaOH |
| Weak base | Produces a smaller amount of `OH-` ions | NH4OH |

## Important Distinction: Strength vs Concentration

Strength and concentration are related but not identical.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Strength** | How completely the acid/base ionises in water. |
| **Concentration** | Amount of acid/base dissolved per unit volume. |

For Class 10 NCERT-style questions, the key idea is:

- more `H+`/`H3O+` ions means stronger acidity;
- more `OH-` ions means stronger basicity.

# Unit 2L: pH Scale

## What Is pH?

pH is a scale used to express how acidic or basic a solution is.

The pH scale usually runs from 0 to 14 for school-level aqueous solutions.

| pH Value | Nature of Solution |
|---:|---|
| Less than 7 | Acidic |
| 7 | Neutral |
| Greater than 7 | Basic |

## pH and Hydrogen Ion Concentration

Higher hydrogen ion concentration means lower pH.

Lower hydrogen ion concentration means higher pH.

## pH Scale Summary

| pH Range | Nature | Example Type |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-3 | Strongly acidic | Strong acids, gastric acid |
| 4-6 | Weakly acidic | Some food acids, milk becoming sour |
| 7 | Neutral | Pure water |
| 8-10 | Weakly basic | Mild basic solutions |
| 11-14 | Strongly basic | Strong alkalis |

## Universal Indicator

Universal indicator is a mixture of many indicators. It gives different colours at different pH values.

| Colour Region | Approximate Meaning |
|---|---|
| Red | Strong acid |
| Orange/yellow | Weak acid |
| Green | Neutral |
| Blue | Weak base |
| Violet/purple | Strong base |

## Comparing Two Solutions

If solution A has pH 6 and solution B has pH 8:

| Solution | pH | Nature | Hydrogen Ion Concentration |
|---|---:|---|---|
| A | 6 | Acidic | More than B |
| B | 8 | Basic | Less than A |

So solution A has higher `H+` concentration.

## Can a Basic Solution Have Hydrogen Ions?

Yes. A basic aqueous solution still contains some `H+` ions because water itself contains a very small amount of `H+` and `OH-`.

But in a basic solution:

$$
[OH^-] > [H^+]
$$

In an acidic solution:

$$
[H^+] > [OH^-]
$$

In a neutral solution:

$$
[H^+] = [OH^-]
$$

# Unit 2M: Importance of pH in Everyday Life

## 1. pH in the Human Body

The human body works within a narrow pH range, approximately 7.0 to 7.8.

Large pH changes can disturb biological processes.

## 2. pH in Digestion

The stomach produces hydrochloric acid. This acid helps digestion and kills many harmful microorganisms.

Sometimes excess acid causes indigestion, pain, or irritation.

Antacids are mild bases that neutralise excess stomach acid.

Example antacid:

- milk of magnesia, `Mg(OH)2`;
- baking soda in some simple contexts, though medicines should be taken properly.

Neutralisation idea:

$$
Acid + Antacid\ base \rightarrow Salt + Water
$$

## 3. pH and Tooth Decay

Tooth enamel is made mainly of calcium phosphate. It begins to corrode when mouth pH falls below about 5.5.

Why pH drops:

- bacteria in the mouth feed on sugar;
- acids are produced;
- acids lower pH;
- enamel is attacked.

Prevention:

- clean teeth regularly;
- reduce sugary food frequency;
- use basic toothpaste to neutralise acid.

## 4. pH and Soil

Plants need suitable soil pH.

| Soil Condition | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Too acidic | Add quicklime, slaked lime, or chalk. |
| Too basic | Add organic matter/compost that releases mild acids during decomposition. |

Acidic soil treatment examples:

- quicklime, `CaO`;
- slaked lime, `Ca(OH)2`;
- chalk, `CaCO3`.

## 5. pH and Acid Rain

Rainwater is called acid rain when its pH falls below about 5.6.

Acid rain can:

- damage crops;
- harm aquatic life;
- corrode buildings and monuments;
- disturb soil and water pH.

## 6. pH and Stings

Bee stings and nettle stings contain acidic substances such as methanoic acid.

Relief can be obtained by applying a mild base, such as baking soda paste.

The traditional use of dock plant leaves for nettle stings is connected with neutralising the acidic effect.

## Daily-Life pH Map

| Situation | Chemistry |
|---|---|
| Indigestion | Excess acid neutralised by antacid. |
| Tooth decay | Acid lowers mouth pH below safe level. |
| Acidic soil | Mild bases correct soil pH. |
| Bee sting | Acidic venom treated with mild base. |
| Acid rain | Low pH harms living and non-living systems. |

# Unit 2N: Salts

## What Is a Salt?

A salt is formed when an acid reacts with a base.

$$
Acid + Base \rightarrow Salt + Water
$$

Examples:

| Acid | Base | Salt Formed |
|---|---|---|
| HCl | NaOH | NaCl |
| H2SO4 | KOH | K2SO4 |
| HNO3 | NaOH | NaNO3 |

## Families of Salts

Salts can be grouped by common positive or negative radicals.

## Same Positive Radical

| Salt | Common Positive Radical |
|---|---|
| Sodium chloride, `NaCl` | `Na+` |
| Sodium nitrate, `NaNO3` | `Na+` |
| Sodium sulphate, `Na2SO4` | `Na+` |

These are sodium salts.

## Same Negative Radical

| Salt | Common Negative Radical |
|---|---|
| Sodium chloride, `NaCl` | `Cl-` |
| Potassium chloride, `KCl` | `Cl-` |
| Calcium chloride, `CaCl2` | `Cl-` |

These are chloride salts.

## pH of Salt Solutions

The pH of a salt solution depends on the acid and base from which the salt is formed.

| Acid Used | Base Used | Salt Solution Nature | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong acid | Strong base | Neutral | `NaCl` from HCl and NaOH |
| Strong acid | Weak base | Acidic | `NH4Cl` type |
| Weak acid | Strong base | Basic | `CH3COONa` type |

For Class 10, remember the simple rule:

- strong acid + strong base -> neutral salt;
- strong acid + weak base -> acidic salt solution;
- weak acid + strong base -> basic salt solution.

# Unit 2O: Common Salt

## Sodium Chloride

Common salt is sodium chloride, `NaCl`.

- sea water;

# Unit 2P: Chlor-Alkali Process

## Meaning

The chlor-alkali process is the electrolysis of concentrated sodium chloride solution, called brine.

It is called chlor-alkali because:

- chlorine gas is produced;
- an alkali, sodium hydroxide, is produced.

## Main Reaction

$$
2NaCl(aq) + 2H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2NaOH(aq) + Cl_2(g) + H_2(g)
$$

## Products and Their Locations

| Product | Formula | Where Formed |
|---|---|---|
| Chlorine gas | `Cl2` | At the anode |
| Hydrogen gas | `H2` | At the cathode |
| Sodium hydroxide solution | `NaOH` | Near the cathode |

## Uses of Chlorine

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Water treatment | Disinfects drinking water. |
| PVC manufacture | Used in making polyvinyl chloride. |
| Bleaching powder | Reacts with slaked lime. |
| Disinfectants | Kills microorganisms. |
| Chemicals | Used in many industrial products. |

## Uses of Hydrogen

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Fuels | Hydrogen can be used as fuel. |
| Ammonia production | Used in the Haber process. |
| Hydrogenation | Used to convert oils into fats in industry. |

## Uses of Sodium Hydroxide

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Soap and detergents | Important industrial alkali. |
| Paper | Used in processing wood pulp. |
| Artificial fibres | Used in rayon-type processes. |
| Degreasing metals | Removes grease before treatment. |

# Unit 2Q: Bleaching Powder

## Preparation

Bleaching powder is prepared by passing chlorine gas over dry slaked lime.

School-level equation commonly used:

$$
Ca(OH)_2 + Cl_2 \rightarrow CaOCl_2 + H_2O
$$

The raw source also represents bleaching powder using calcium hypochlorite-type notation:

$$
2Ca(OH)_2 + 2Cl_2 \rightarrow Ca(ClO)_2 + CaCl_2 + 2H_2O
$$

For exam answers, `CaOCl2` is commonly accepted as the school formula for bleaching powder, while the chapter source notes calcium hypochlorite-type representation as well.

## Reactants

| Reactant | Formula |
|---|---|
| Chlorine | `Cl2` |
| Dry slaked lime | `Ca(OH)2` |

## Uses of Bleaching Powder

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Textile industry | Bleaches cotton and linen. |
| Paper industry | Bleaches wood pulp. |
| Laundry | Bleaches washed clothes. |
| Oxidising agent | Used in chemical industries. |
| Disinfectant | Makes drinking water free from germs. |

## Common Question

The common name of `Ca(ClO)2` in the raw chapter context is bleaching powder.

# Unit 2R: Baking Soda

## Identity

Baking soda is sodium hydrogencarbonate.

| Name | Formula |
|---|---|
| Baking soda | `NaHCO3` |
| Chemical name | Sodium hydrogencarbonate |

## Preparation

It is prepared using sodium chloride as one of the raw materials.

$$
NaCl + H_2O + CO_2 + NH_3 \rightarrow NH_4Cl + NaHCO_3
$$

## Properties

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Mildly basic | It neutralises acids gently. |
| Non-corrosive | Safer than strong bases for many household uses. |
| Releases carbon dioxide on heating or acid reaction | Useful in baking and extinguishers. |

## Heating Baking Soda

When sodium hydrogencarbonate is heated, it forms sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide.

$$
2NaHCO_3 \rightarrow Na_2CO_3 + H_2O + CO_2
$$

## Baking Powder

Baking powder is a mixture of baking soda and a mild edible acid.

When mixed with water or heated, it releases carbon dioxide. The `CO2` bubbles make cakes and bread rise.

General idea:

$$
NaHCO_3 + H^+ \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O + Sodium\ salt
$$

## Uses of Baking Soda

| Use | Chemistry Behind It |
|---|---|
| Baking powder | Produces carbon dioxide, making food soft and spongy. |
| Antacid | Neutralises excess stomach acid. |
| Soda-acid fire extinguisher | Produces carbon dioxide, which helps extinguish fire. |

## Why Baking Soda Helps Delay Milk Curdling

Fresh milk has pH around 6. A milkman may add a small amount of baking soda to make it slightly alkaline.

Then, when bacteria produce lactic acid, the acid first neutralises the added base. Because of this, it takes longer for the milk to become acidic enough to set as curd.

# Unit 2S: Washing Soda

## Identity

Washing soda is sodium carbonate decahydrate.

| Name | Formula |
|---|---|
| Washing soda | `Na2CO3.10H2O` |
| Chemical name | Sodium carbonate decahydrate |

## Preparation from Sodium Carbonate

Sodium carbonate is recrystallised with water to form washing soda.

$$
Na_2CO_3 + 10H_2O \rightarrow Na_2CO_3.10H_2O
$$

## Uses of Washing Soda

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Cleaning agent | Used for domestic cleaning. |
| Softening hard water | Removes permanent hardness. |
| Glass industry | Raw material for glass manufacture. |
| Soap industry | Used in manufacture of soaps. |
| Paper industry | Used in paper-related industrial processes. |
| Sodium compounds | Used in making compounds such as borax. |

## Why It Softens Hard Water

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium ions. Sodium carbonate can remove these ions as insoluble carbonates.

General idea:

$$
Ca^{2+} + CO_3^{2-} \rightarrow CaCO_3
$$

$$
Mg^{2+} + CO_3^{2-} \rightarrow MgCO_3
$$

When calcium and magnesium ions are removed, water becomes softer.

# Unit 2T: Water of Crystallisation

## Meaning

Water of crystallisation is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt crystal.

## Example: Copper Sulphate

Blue copper sulphate crystals are hydrated copper sulphate:

$$
CuSO_4.5H_2O
$$

The five water molecules are water of crystallisation.

## Heating Copper Sulphate

When blue copper sulphate crystals are heated, they lose water of crystallisation and become white anhydrous copper sulphate.

$$
CuSO_4.5H_2O \xrightarrow{heat} CuSO_4 + 5H_2O
$$

| Before Heating | After Heating |
|---|---|
| Blue | White |
| Hydrated copper sulphate | Anhydrous copper sulphate |

## Restoring Blue Colour

When water is added to white anhydrous copper sulphate, it becomes blue again because water of crystallisation is restored.

$$
CuSO_4 + 5H_2O \rightarrow CuSO_4.5H_2O
$$

## Why This Matters

The colour change proves that water molecules are part of the crystal structure of hydrated salts.

# Unit 2U: Gypsum and Plaster of Paris

## Gypsum

Gypsum is calcium sulphate dihydrate.

$$
CaSO_4.2H_2O
$$

## Preparation of Plaster of Paris

When gypsum is heated at about 373 K, it loses part of its water of crystallisation and becomes Plaster of Paris.

$$
CaSO_4.2H_2O \xrightarrow{373K} CaSO_4.\frac{1}{2}H_2O + \frac{3}{2}H_2O
$$

Plaster of Paris is calcium sulphate hemihydrate.

$$
CaSO_4.\frac{1}{2}H_2O
$$

It is called hemihydrate because two formula units of calcium sulphate share one molecule of water. In simple school notation, this is written as `1/2 H2O` per `CaSO4`.

## Reaction with Water

Plaster of Paris reacts with water to form gypsum again. It sets into a hard mass.

$$
CaSO_4.\frac{1}{2}H_2O + \frac{3}{2}H_2O \rightarrow CaSO_4.2H_2O
$$

## Why Plaster of Paris Must Be Stored in a Moisture-Proof Container

Plaster of Paris absorbs moisture from air. If it absorbs water before use, it slowly changes into gypsum and sets.

Once it sets, it loses its useful powder form.

## Uses of Plaster of Paris

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Fracture support | Used by doctors to support broken bones. |
| Toys | Used for moulded articles. |
| Decorative materials | Used for ceiling designs and statues. |
| Smooth surfaces | Used for making casts and moulds. |

| Base + non-metallic oxide | Base + non-metallic oxide -> salt + water | Non-metallic oxide is acidic |

| Gas | Test | Positive Result |

# Unit 2W: Solved In-Text Questions

## Question 1: You Have Three Test Tubes. One Contains Distilled Water, One Acidic Solution, and One Basic Solution. Only Red Litmus Paper Is Available. How Will You Identify Them?

## Answer

Dip red litmus paper into each solution.

- The solution that turns red litmus blue is basic.
- Now use that blue litmus paper to test the remaining solutions.
- The solution that turns blue litmus red is acidic.
- The solution that shows no change with either red or blue litmus is distilled water/neutral solution.

## Reasoning

Red litmus directly detects a base. Once it becomes blue in a basic solution, it can be used as blue litmus to detect an acid.

---

## Question 2: Why Should Curd and Sour Substances Not Be Kept in Brass or Copper Vessels?

## Answer

Curd and sour substances contain acids. These acids can react with copper or brass to form harmful metal salts.

Such salts may contaminate the food and make it unsafe.

---

## Question 3: Which Gas Is Usually Liberated When an Acid Reacts with a Metal? How Will You Test It?

## Answer

Hydrogen gas is liberated.

Test:

- bring a burning splint near the gas;
- hydrogen burns with a pop sound.

Example:

$$
Zn + 2HCl \rightarrow ZnCl_2 + H_2
$$

---

## Question 4: A Metal Compound A Reacts with Dilute Hydrochloric Acid to Produce Effervescence. The Gas Extinguishes a Burning Candle. If One Product Is Calcium Chloride, Identify A and Write the Reaction.

## Answer

The gas that extinguishes a burning candle is carbon dioxide.

The product calcium chloride indicates that compound A contains calcium.

Therefore, A is calcium carbonate, `CaCO3`.

Reaction:

$$
CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2
$$

## Explanation

Calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid to form calcium chloride, water, and carbon dioxide. The carbon dioxide causes effervescence and extinguishes the candle.

---

## Question 5: Why Do HCl and HNO3 Show Acidic Character in Aqueous Solution, While Glucose and Alcohol Do Not?

## Answer

HCl and HNO3 produce hydrogen ions in water, so they show acidic character.

Glucose and alcohol contain hydrogen, but they do not produce `H+` ions in water. Therefore, they are not acids.

For HCl:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

---

## Question 6: Why Does an Aqueous Solution of an Acid Conduct Electricity?

## Answer

An aqueous acid solution contains ions. These ions move and carry electric charge, so the solution conducts electricity.

Example:

$$
HCl(aq) \rightarrow H^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)
$$

In water, the hydrogen ion exists mainly as hydronium:

$$
H^+ + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+
$$

---

## Question 7: Why Does Dry HCl Gas Not Change the Colour of Dry Litmus Paper?

## Answer

Dry HCl gas does not produce hydrogen ions because water is absent. Without `H+` or `H3O+` ions, it does not show acidic behaviour.

Therefore, dry HCl gas does not change dry litmus paper.

In the presence of water:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

---

## Question 8: While Diluting an Acid, Why Is It Recommended That Acid Should Be Added to Water and Not Water to Acid?

## Answer

Dilution of concentrated acid is highly exothermic. If water is added to concentrated acid, the heat released can cause the acid to splash out.

To reduce this risk, acid is added slowly to water with constant stirring.

---

## Question 9: How Is the Concentration of Hydronium Ions Affected When an Acid Is Diluted?

## Answer

When an acid is diluted, the concentration of hydronium ions per unit volume decreases.

The solution becomes less acidic, and its pH increases toward 7.

---

## Question 10: How Is the Concentration of Hydroxide Ions Affected When Excess Base Is Dissolved in a Solution of Sodium Hydroxide?

## Answer

Adding excess base increases the concentration of hydroxide ions, `OH-`, in the solution.

The solution becomes more basic.

---

## Question 11: Two Solutions A and B Have pH 6 and pH 8. Which Has More Hydrogen Ion Concentration?

## Answer

Solution A, with pH 6, has more hydrogen ion concentration.

Reason:

- lower pH means higher `H+` concentration;
- pH 6 is acidic;
- pH 8 is basic.

---

## Question 12: What Effect Does the Concentration of `H+` Ions Have on the Nature of a Solution?

## Answer

Higher `H+` ion concentration makes a solution more acidic.

Lower `H+` ion concentration makes it less acidic or more basic, depending on the amount of `OH-` ions present.

---

## Question 13: Do Basic Solutions Also Have `H+` Ions? If Yes, Why Are They Basic?

## Answer

Yes, basic aqueous solutions also contain `H+` ions, but their `OH-` ion concentration is greater than their `H+` ion concentration.

That is why they behave as bases.

$$
[OH^-] > [H^+]
$$

---

## Question 14: What Should a Farmer Add to Acidic Soil?

## Answer

The farmer should add a basic substance such as:

- quicklime, `CaO`;
- slaked lime, `Ca(OH)2`;
- chalk, `CaCO3`.

These neutralise excess acid in the soil.

Dry slaked lime, `Ca(OH)2`, reacts with chlorine to produce bleaching powder.

## Exercise 3: 10 mL of NaOH Is Neutralised by 8 mL of HCl. How Much HCl Is Required to Neutralise 20 mL of the Same NaOH Solution?

## Answer

16 mL of HCl is required.

## Working

For the same solutions, volume required is directly proportional.

```text
10 mL NaOH needs 8 mL HCl
```

So:

$$
Required\ HCl = 8 \times \frac{20}{10} = 16\ mL

## Exercise 6: Why Do Acids Conduct Electricity but Glucose and Alcohol Solutions Do Not?

## Answer

Acid solutions conduct electricity because acids produce ions in water.

Glucose and alcohol do not produce ions in water, even though they contain hydrogen. Therefore, their solutions do not conduct electricity like acid solutions.

## Explanation

For hydrochloric acid:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

The ions carry charge.

Glucose and alcohol remain mostly as neutral molecules in water, so they do not provide mobile ions.

---

## Exercise 7: Why Does Distilled Water Not Conduct Electricity, Whereas Rainwater Does?

## Answer

Distilled water does not conduct electricity because it has almost no dissolved ions.

Rainwater conducts electricity because it contains dissolved gases, salts, and acidic substances from the atmosphere. These form ions in water.

## Explanation

Electric current in solution requires mobile ions. Rainwater has more ions than distilled water.

## Exercise 8: Why Does Dry HCl Gas Not Show Acidic Behaviour in the Absence of Water?

## Answer

Dry HCl gas does not show acidic behaviour because it cannot produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions without water.

With water:

$$
HCl + H_2O \rightarrow H_3O^+ + Cl^-
$$

Without water, this ionisation does not occur effectively, so dry HCl does not turn dry litmus red.

# Final Study Strategy

## What to Memorise

Memorise:

- indicator colour changes;
- pH rule: less than 7 acid, 7 neutral, greater than 7 base;
- gas tests for hydrogen and carbon dioxide;
- formulas of baking soda, washing soda, gypsum, and Plaster of Paris;
- chlor-alkali products and electrodes;
- acid/base reaction patterns.

## What to Understand

Understand:

- acids need water to produce `H+`/`H3O+` ions;
- bases produce `OH-` ions in water;
- neutralisation is really `H+ + OH- -> H2O`;
- lower pH means higher hydrogen ion concentration;
- common salts are industrial starting materials, not just table substances.

## Best Exam Answer Pattern

For reaction questions, write answers in this order:

1. Name the reactants.
2. State the observation.
3. Identify the product or gas.
4. Write the balanced equation.
5. Add one reason if asked.

Example:

```text
When calcium carbonate reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid, carbon dioxide gas is evolved with effervescence. The gas turns lime water milky.
CaCO3 + 2HCl -> CaCl2 + H2O + CO2
```

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Acids produce `H+` or `H3O+` ions in water, turn blue litmus red, react with metals to produce hydrogen, react with carbonates/hydrogencarbonates to produce carbon dioxide, and neutralise bases to form salts and water.

Bases produce `OH-` ions in water, turn red litmus blue, and neutralise acids. Water-soluble bases are called alkalis.

pH measures acidity/basicity. pH below 7 is acidic, pH 7 is neutral, and pH above 7 is basic. A lower pH means greater hydrogen ion concentration.

Common salt, `NaCl`, is the starting point for many useful chemicals. Electrolysis of brine gives sodium hydroxide, chlorine, and hydrogen. Chlorine is used to prepare bleaching powder. Baking soda is `NaHCO3`; washing soda is `Na2CO3.10H2O`. Gypsum is `CaSO4.2H2O`; Plaster of Paris is `CaSO4.1/2H2O`.
