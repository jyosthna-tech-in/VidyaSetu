## Big Ideas

Living organisms must constantly maintain their internal order. Even during sleep or rest, cells keep repairing, transporting, synthesising, breaking down, and removing materials.

Life processes need energy. Food supplies energy and raw materials. Respiration converts food energy into usable cellular energy in the form of ATP.

As organisms become larger and more complex, diffusion alone becomes insufficient. They need specialised organs and transport systems.

Plants and animals solve the same basic problems differently:

- plants make food by photosynthesis; animals obtain food from other organisms;
- plants use xylem and phloem; humans use blood, heart, and vessels;
- plants remove wastes by storage, diffusion, transpiration, and shedding parts; humans use kidneys and nephrons.

# Unit 5A: What Makes Something Alive?

## Visible Signs of Life

Common signs include:

- movement;
- breathing;
- growth;
- response to surroundings;
- taking food;
- producing wastes;
- reproduction.

But visible movement is not enough.

Examples:

- a sleeping animal is alive even when it is not moving much;
- a plant may not visibly grow every moment but is still alive;
- many cellular processes are invisible to the naked eye.

## Molecular Movement

Living organisms are highly organised structures made of cells, tissues, organs, and molecules.

This organisation tends to break down due to environmental effects and internal chemical reactions.

Therefore, organisms must constantly:

- repair structures;
- replace molecules;
- move substances;
- obtain energy;
- remove wastes.

These invisible molecular movements are essential for life.

## Viruses and the Life Question

Viruses do not show independent molecular movement until they infect a living cell.

This is one reason there is debate about whether viruses are truly living.

For Class 10, the key idea is: living systems must maintain molecular activity and internal organisation.

# Unit 5B: Life Processes

## Meaning

Life processes are the maintenance processes that keep organisms alive.

They continue even when the organism is resting or sleeping.

## Main Life Processes

| Life Process | Main Function |
|---|---|
| Nutrition | Supplies energy and raw materials. |
| Respiration | Breaks down food to release usable energy. |
| Transportation | Moves food, gases, wastes, hormones, and other substances inside the body. |
| Excretion | Removes harmful metabolic wastes. |

## Why Energy Is Needed

Energy is needed for:

- repair;
- growth;
- movement;
- protein synthesis;
- transport across membranes;
- nerve impulses;
- muscle contraction;
- maintaining body organisation.

## Why Transport Is Needed in Multicellular Organisms

In unicellular organisms, the entire cell surface is in contact with the environment.

In multicellular organisms:

- many cells are deep inside the body;
- all cells are not in direct contact with air, water, or food;
- distances are larger;
- oxygen and food demand is higher;
- wastes must be collected from many tissues.

Therefore, diffusion alone is insufficient and transport systems are required.

# Unit 5C: Nutrition

## Why Organisms Need Food

Food provides:

- energy;
- carbon-based raw materials;
- molecules for growth;
- materials for repair;
- substances needed to make proteins, fats, carbohydrates, and other cellular components.

## Two Main Modes of Nutrition

| Mode | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Autotrophic | Organism makes its own food from simple inorganic substances. | Green plants, some bacteria. |
| Heterotrophic | Organism depends on other organisms for complex food. | Animals, fungi, many bacteria. |

## Autotrophs

Autotrophs use simple substances such as:

- carbon dioxide;
- water;
- minerals.

They build complex organic molecules using energy from sunlight or chemical sources.

## Heterotrophs

Heterotrophs use complex food made by other organisms.

Because complex food cannot usually be absorbed directly, it must be broken down by enzymes into simpler molecules.

Heterotrophs depend directly or indirectly on autotrophs.

# Unit 5D: Photosynthesis

## Meaning

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants and some bacteria convert carbon dioxide and water into carbohydrates using sunlight and chlorophyll.

General equation:

$$
6CO_2 + 6H_2O \xrightarrow{Sunlight,\ Chlorophyll} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2
$$

## Raw Materials and Conditions

| Requirement | Source/Role |
|---|---|
| Carbon dioxide | Enters leaves through stomata. |
| Water | Absorbed by roots from soil. |
| Sunlight | Provides energy. |
| Chlorophyll | Absorbs light energy. |
| Minerals | Taken from soil; help build proteins and other molecules. |

## Main Events of Photosynthesis

The chapter lists three major events:

1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll.
2. Conversion of light energy into chemical energy and splitting of water into hydrogen and oxygen.
3. Reduction of carbon dioxide to carbohydrates.

These steps do not always occur immediately one after another in every plant.

Example:

- desert plants may take in carbon dioxide at night;
- they prepare an intermediate compound;
- during the day, sunlight energy is used to complete carbohydrate formation.

## Storage of Food

Carbohydrates made during photosynthesis are used for energy.

Excess carbohydrate may be stored as starch in plants.

In humans and animals, some food energy is stored as glycogen.

# Unit 5E: Leaf Structure, Chlorophyll, and Stomata

## Chloroplasts

Leaf cells contain green organelles called chloroplasts.

Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, which absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.

## Variegated Leaf Starch Test

A variegated leaf has green and non-green areas.

Experiment idea:

1. Keep plant in dark for three days to remove stored starch.
2. Keep it in sunlight.
3. Boil a leaf in water.
4. Heat it in alcohol using a water bath to remove chlorophyll.
5. Add iodine solution.

Observation:

| Leaf Area | Iodine Result | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Green area | Blue-black | Starch is present; photosynthesis occurred. |
| Non-green area | No blue-black colour | Starch absent; photosynthesis did not occur. |

Conclusion:

Chlorophyll is necessary for photosynthesis.

## Stomata

Stomata are tiny pores on leaf surfaces.

Functions:

- allow carbon dioxide to enter;
- allow oxygen to leave;
- allow water vapour to escape during transpiration.

## Guard Cells

Guard cells control stomatal opening and closing.

| Guard Cell Condition | Stomatal Pore |
|---|---|
| Guard cells swell with water | Pore opens |
| Guard cells lose water and shrink | Pore closes |

Plants close stomata when carbon dioxide is not needed or when they must reduce water loss.

## Carbon Dioxide Requirement Test

In the bell-jar experiment:

- one plant is kept with potassium hydroxide, `KOH`;
- `KOH` absorbs carbon dioxide;
- both plants are placed in sunlight after destarching;
- leaves are tested for starch.

Observation:

| Setup | Result |
|---|---|
| With `KOH` | Little/no starch because carbon dioxide is absorbed. |
| Without `KOH` | Starch present. |

Conclusion:

Carbon dioxide is necessary for photosynthesis.

## Sunlight Requirement Test

To show sunlight is necessary:

1. Destarch a plant by keeping it in darkness.
2. Cover part of a leaf with black paper.
3. Keep the plant in sunlight.
4. Test the leaf with iodine.

Observation:

- exposed part turns blue-black;
- covered part does not.

Conclusion:

Sunlight is necessary for photosynthesis.

# Unit 5F: Heterotrophic Nutrition

## Types of Heterotrophic Nutrition

| Type | Method | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Saprophytic | Food is broken down outside the body and then absorbed. | Bread mould, yeast, mushrooms. |
| Holozoic | Food is taken into the body and digested internally. | Humans, Amoeba, animals. |
| Parasitic | Organism obtains food from a living host without immediately killing it. | Cuscuta, ticks, lice, leeches, tapeworms. |

## Why Nutrition Differs Among Organisms

Nutrition depends on:

- food availability;
- whether food is mobile or stationary;
- body design;
- digestive organs;
- environment;
- method of food capture.

Examples:

- cow eats grass and has adaptations for plant material;
- lion eats prey and has adaptations for meat;
- fungi absorb digested food;
- parasites obtain nutrition from host organisms.

# Unit 5G: Nutrition in Unicellular Organisms

## Amoeba

Amoeba takes food using temporary finger-like extensions called pseudopodia.

Steps:

1. Amoeba detects food.
2. Pseudopodia surround the food particle.
3. A food vacuole forms.
4. Enzymes digest complex food into simpler substances.
5. Digested food diffuses into cytoplasm.
6. Undigested material moves to the surface and is thrown out.

## Paramecium

Paramecium has a definite shape.

Food enters at a specific spot.

Cilia move food particles toward this feeding region.

## Comparison

| Feature | Amoeba | Paramecium |
|---|---|---|
| Shape | Irregular | Definite |
| Food intake | Pseudopodia surround food | Cilia move food to a fixed spot |
| Digestion | Food vacuole | Food vacuole |
| Egestion | Through cell surface | Through a specific region |

The alimentary canal is a long tube from mouth to anus.

Main parts:

1. Mouth

4. Small intestine

Food contains complex molecules such as:

- starch;

Digestion breaks them into simpler molecules:

| Complex Food | Final Simpler Form |
|---|---|
| Carbohydrates | Glucose/simple sugars |

# Unit 5I: Digestion in the Mouth

## Mechanical Digestion

Teeth crush and grind food into smaller pieces.

This increases surface area for enzyme action.

## Saliva

Saliva is secreted by salivary glands.

Functions:

- wets food;
- makes swallowing easier;
- begins chemical digestion of starch;
- helps form a soft bolus.

## Salivary Amylase

Saliva contains salivary amylase.

It breaks starch into simpler sugars.

## Starch-Saliva-Iodine Activity

Two test tubes:

| Test Tube | Contents |
|---|---|
| A | Starch solution + saliva |
| B | Starch solution only |

After 20-30 minutes, iodine is added.

Expected result:

| Test Tube | Observation | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| A | Little/no blue-black colour | Salivary amylase breaks starch down. |
| B | Blue-black colour | Starch remains present. |

Conclusion:

Saliva helps digest starch.

## Role of Tongue

The muscular tongue:

- mixes food with saliva;
- helps chewing;
- moves food for swallowing;
- helps taste.

# Unit 5J: Peristalsis and Stomach Digestion

## Peristalsis

Peristalsis is rhythmic contraction and relaxation of muscles in the alimentary canal.

It pushes food forward in a regulated manner.

Peristalsis occurs throughout the gut.

## Oesophagus

Food passes from mouth to stomach through the oesophagus, also called the food pipe.

## Stomach

The stomach is a large muscular organ.

Functions:

- stores food temporarily;
- churns food;
- mixes food with gastric juice;
- begins protein digestion.

## Gastric Glands

Gastric glands secrete:

| Secretion | Function |
|---|---|
| Hydrochloric acid | Creates acidic medium and kills many germs. |
| Pepsin | Digests proteins. |
| Mucus | Protects stomach lining from acid. |

## Role of Acid

Hydrochloric acid:

- provides acidic medium for pepsin;
- kills many microorganisms entering with food.

## Acidity

Excess acid or disturbance of normal protection can cause discomfort called acidity.

Mucus normally protects the stomach lining.

## Sphincter Muscle

The exit of food from the stomach is controlled by a sphincter muscle.

It releases partly digested food into the small intestine in small amounts.

# Unit 5K: Small Intestine, Liver, Pancreas, and Absorption

## Small Intestine

The small intestine is the longest part of the alimentary canal.

It is highly coiled, so it fits inside the body.

It is the site of complete digestion of:

- carbohydrates;
- proteins;
- fats.

## Length and Diet

| Animal Type | Small Intestine |
|---|---|
| Herbivores | Longer, because cellulose-rich plant food is harder to digest. |
| Carnivores | Shorter, because meat is easier to digest. |

## Bile

Bile is produced by the liver and stored in the gall bladder.

Functions:

- makes acidic food from stomach alkaline;
- emulsifies fats.

## Emulsification of Fats

Fats enter the small intestine as large globules.

Bile salts break large fat globules into smaller droplets.

This increases surface area for enzyme action.

## Pancreatic Juice

Pancreas secretes pancreatic juice.

Important enzymes:

| Enzyme | Function |
|---|---|
| Trypsin | Digests proteins. |
| Lipase | Digests emulsified fats. |

## Intestinal Juice

Intestinal glands secrete intestinal juice.

Its enzymes complete digestion:

| Food Type | Final Products |
|---|---|
| Proteins | Amino acids |
| Complex carbohydrates | Glucose |
| Fats | Fatty acids and glycerol |

## Absorption by Villi

The inner lining of the small intestine has many finger-like projections called villi.

Villi increase surface area for absorption.

Each villus has blood vessels that carry absorbed food to body cells.

## Assimilation

Absorbed food is used by cells for:

- energy;
- growth;
- repair;
- building new tissues.

## Large Intestine

Unabsorbed food passes into the large intestine.

Main function:

- absorbs water from undigested material.

Remaining waste is removed through anus.

The anal sphincter regulates exit of faeces.

# Unit 5L: Dental Caries

## Meaning

Dental caries means tooth decay.

It causes gradual softening of enamel and dentine.

## Cause

Bacteria act on sugars and produce acids.

These acids demineralise tooth enamel.

## Plaque

Plaque is a sticky layer of bacterial cells and food particles on teeth.

When plaque covers teeth, saliva cannot easily reach the tooth surface to neutralise acid.

## Prevention

Brush teeth after eating.

This removes plaque before bacteria produce too much acid.

If untreated, microorganisms may reach the pulp and cause inflammation and infection.

# Unit 5M: Respiration

## Meaning

Respiration is the process by which food is broken down in cells to release energy.

This energy is used to make ATP.

## Lime Water Test for Exhaled Air

When we blow exhaled air through lime water, it turns milky faster than ordinary air passed through lime water.

Reason:

Exhaled air contains more carbon dioxide than inhaled air.

Test reaction:

$$
Ca(OH)_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + H_2O
$$

## First Step of Glucose Breakdown

Glucose is a six-carbon molecule.

It first breaks down into pyruvate, a three-carbon molecule.

This occurs in the cytoplasm.

Simplified:

$$
Glucose \rightarrow Pyruvate + Energy
$$

---

# Unit 5N: Pathways of Glucose Breakdown

## Pathway 1: Anaerobic Respiration in Yeast

In absence of oxygen, pyruvate is converted into ethanol and carbon dioxide.

This happens in yeast during fermentation.

$$
Glucose \rightarrow Pyruvate \rightarrow Ethanol + CO_2 + Energy
$$

Lime water turns milky in fermentation experiments because carbon dioxide is produced.

## Pathway 2: Aerobic Respiration

In presence of oxygen, pyruvate is broken down completely in mitochondria.

Products:

- carbon dioxide;
- water;
- large amount of energy.

Overall equation:

$$
C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + Energy
$$

Aerobic respiration releases much more energy than anaerobic respiration.

## Pathway 3: Lactic Acid Formation in Muscles

When oxygen is insufficient in muscle cells during sudden activity, pyruvate is converted into lactic acid.

$$
Glucose \rightarrow Pyruvate \rightarrow Lactic\ acid + Energy
$$

Lactic acid build-up causes muscle cramps.

## Comparison

| Pathway | Oxygen? | Site | Products | Energy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Yeast fermentation | No | Cytoplasm | Ethanol + `CO2` | Low |
| Aerobic respiration | Yes | Mitochondria | `CO2` + `H2O` | High |
| Muscle anaerobic pathway | Low/absent | Muscle cells | Lactic acid | Low |

# Unit 5O: ATP - Energy Currency of the Cell

## What Is ATP?

ATP is adenosine triphosphate.

It is called the energy currency of the cell.

## Formation of ATP

Energy released during respiration is used to make ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate.

$$
ADP + P_i + Energy \rightarrow ATP
$$

## Use of ATP

When ATP breaks down, usable energy is released.

$$
ATP + H_2O \rightarrow ADP + P_i + Energy
$$

Energy released from breaking the terminal phosphate linkage is about:

```text
30.5 kJ/mol
```

ATP powers:

- muscle contraction;
- protein synthesis;
- nerve impulse conduction;
- active transport;
- many endothermic cell reactions.

Plants exchange gases mainly through:

# Unit 5Q: Respiration in Animals

## Aquatic Organisms

Aquatic organisms use oxygen dissolved in water.

Dissolved oxygen in water is much less than oxygen in air.

Therefore, aquatic organisms often breathe faster.

Fish take water through the mouth and force it over gills, where oxygen is absorbed into blood.

## Terrestrial Organisms

Terrestrial organisms use oxygen from air.

Advantage:

Air contains more oxygen than water, so terrestrial organisms can obtain oxygen more easily than aquatic organisms.

## Respiratory Surface Requirements

An effective respiratory surface is:

- thin;
- moist;
- delicate;
- large in area;
- richly supplied with blood or transport fluid.

Because it is delicate, it is usually protected inside the body.

# Unit 5R: Human Respiratory System

## Air Pathway

Air travels through:

1. Nostrils
2. Nasal passage
3. Throat/pharynx
4. Trachea/windpipe
5. Bronchi
6. Smaller tubes/bronchioles
7. Alveoli

## Filtering Air

The nasal passage has:

- fine hairs;
- mucus;
- cilia.

These trap dust, germs, and harmful particles.

## Cartilage Rings

Rings of cartilage keep the air passage open and prevent collapse.

## Alveoli

Alveoli are balloon-like structures at the ends of tiny air tubes.

They provide a large surface area for gas exchange.

If the alveolar surface were spread out, it would cover about 80 m2.

## Breathing Mechanism

During inhalation:

- ribs move up and out;
- diaphragm flattens;
- chest cavity becomes larger;
- air is sucked into lungs.

During exhalation:

- ribs move down and in;
- diaphragm relaxes and curves upward;
- chest cavity becomes smaller;
- air is pushed out.

## Gas Exchange in Alveoli

| Gas | Direction |
|---|---|
| Oxygen | From alveolar air into blood. |
| Carbon dioxide | From blood into alveoli. |

Blood brings carbon dioxide from body tissues to the lungs.

Oxygen from alveoli is absorbed into blood and transported to cells.

## Residual Volume

Lungs always contain some residual air.

This gives enough time for oxygen absorption and carbon dioxide release during breathing cycles.

## Haemoglobin

Haemoglobin is present in red blood cells.

It has high affinity for oxygen and carries oxygen from lungs to tissues.

Carbon dioxide is more soluble in water than oxygen, so it is transported mostly in dissolved form in blood plasma.

## Tobacco Harm

Smoking and tobacco use damage the respiratory system.

Smoking destroys cilia, allowing dust, smoke, germs, and harmful chemicals to enter lungs more easily.

This can cause infection, cough, lung disease, and cancer.

# Unit 5T: Human Heart and Double Circulation

## Heart Structure

The heart is a muscular organ about the size of a fist.

It has four chambers:

| Chamber | Main Role |
|---|---|
| Right atrium | Receives deoxygenated blood from body. |
| Right ventricle | Pumps deoxygenated blood to lungs. |
| Left atrium | Receives oxygenated blood from lungs. |
| Left ventricle | Pumps oxygenated blood to body. |

## Why Ventricles Have Thicker Walls

Ventricles pump blood out of the heart.

The left ventricle especially pumps blood to the entire body, so it has thick muscular walls.

## Valves

Valves prevent backward flow of blood.

They ensure one-way flow during contraction and relaxation.

## Blood Flow Path

```text
Body -> Right atrium -> Right ventricle -> Lungs -> Left atrium -> Left ventricle -> Body
```

## Double Circulation

In one complete cycle, blood passes through the heart twice:

1. Heart to lungs and back to heart.
2. Heart to body and back to heart.

This is called double circulation.

## Why Separation Is Necessary

Mammals and birds need high energy to maintain constant body temperature.

Complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood ensures:

- efficient oxygen supply;
- high energy production;
- no mixing of oxygen-rich and carbon dioxide-rich blood.

## Other Vertebrates

| Group | Heart Type | Circulation Note |
|---|---|---|
| Fish | Two chambers | Blood passes once through heart per body cycle. |
| Amphibians/reptiles | Usually three chambers | Some mixing tolerated. |
| Birds/mammals | Four chambers | Complete separation; efficient oxygen supply. |

# Unit 5U: Blood Vessels, Blood Pressure, Platelets, and Lymph

## Arteries

Arteries carry blood away from the heart.

They have thick, elastic walls because blood flows under high pressure.

## Veins

Veins carry blood back to the heart.

They have thinner walls and valves that prevent backflow.

## Capillaries

Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels.

Their walls are one-cell thick.

Function:

- exchange of oxygen, carbon dioxide, food, wastes, and other materials between blood and tissues.

## Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is the force exerted by blood against vessel walls.

It is higher in arteries than in veins.

Normal adult blood pressure is about:

```text
120/80 mm Hg
```

| Pressure | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Systolic | Pressure during ventricular contraction. |
| Diastolic | Pressure during ventricular relaxation. |

High blood pressure is called hypertension.

## Platelets

Platelets help clot blood at injury sites.

Clotting:

- reduces blood loss;
- prevents loss of pressure;
- blocks entry of pathogens.

## Lymph

Lymph is also called tissue fluid.

It forms when some plasma, proteins, and blood cells escape through capillary walls into intercellular spaces.

Properties:

- colourless;
- similar to plasma;
- contains less protein than plasma.

Functions:

- drains excess fluid back into blood;
- transports absorbed fat from intestine;
- helps in body defence.

# Unit 5V: Transportation in Plants

## Why Plants Need Transport

Plants need to move:

- water from roots to leaves;
- minerals from soil to growing parts;
- food from leaves to roots, stems, fruits, seeds, and buds;
- hormones and other soluble substances.

Large plants cannot depend only on diffusion.

## Plant Transport Tissues

| Tissue | Main Material Transported | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Xylem | Water and minerals | Mainly upward |
| Phloem | Food and other soluble products | Upward and downward |

## Why Plant Transport Can Be Slower

Plants:

- do not move from place to place;
- have lower energy needs than animals;
- have many dead cells in tissues;
- can use slower transport systems.

---

# Unit 5W: Xylem Transport of Water and Minerals

## Xylem Structure

Xylem contains vessels and tracheids.

These form a continuous system of water-conducting channels through roots, stems, and leaves.

## Root Absorption

Root cells actively take up mineral ions from soil.

This creates a concentration difference between root and soil.

Water moves into roots to reduce this difference.

This creates root pressure and pushes water into xylem.

## Transpiration Pull

During the day, water evaporates from leaf cells through stomata.

This creates suction that pulls water upward through xylem.

This suction is called transpiration pull.

## Importance of Transpiration

Transpiration helps:

- upward movement of water;
- upward movement of dissolved minerals;
- absorption from roots;
- temperature regulation in plants.

## Root Pressure vs Transpiration Pull

| Force | More Important When? | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Root pressure | Night or low transpiration | Pushes water upward from roots. |
| Transpiration pull | Day when stomata are open | Major force pulling water upward. |

---

# Unit 5X: Phloem Transport of Food

## Translocation

Translocation is the transport of soluble products of photosynthesis through phloem.

Materials transported:

- sucrose;
- amino acids;
- other soluble substances.

## Direction

Phloem transport can occur both upward and downward.

It depends on the plant's needs.

Examples:

- food from leaves to roots;
- stored sugar from roots/stems to buds in spring;
- food to fruits and seeds;
- amino acids to growing regions.

## How Phloem Transport Works

1. Sucrose is transferred into phloem using energy from ATP.
2. This increases osmotic pressure inside phloem.
3. Water enters phloem.
4. Pressure moves food solution toward tissues with lower pressure.
5. Food is unloaded where needed.

## Why Energy Is Needed

Unlike xylem transport, phloem translocation uses ATP.

It is an active process.

---

# Unit 5Y: Excretion

## Meaning

Excretion is the biological process of removing harmful metabolic wastes from the body.

## Why Excretion Is Necessary

Metabolic reactions produce wastes such as:

- carbon dioxide;
- nitrogenous wastes;
- excess water;
- excess salts;
- other harmful by-products.

If wastes accumulate, they can poison cells and disturb body functions.

## Excretion in Unicellular Organisms

Many unicellular organisms remove wastes by simple diffusion through the body surface into surrounding water.

## Excretion in Complex Multicellular Organisms

Complex organisms use specialised organs because diffusion is not enough.

Humans use kidneys as major excretory organs for nitrogenous wastes.

---

# Unit 5Z: Human Excretory System

## Main Parts

| Organ | Function |
|---|---|
| Kidneys | Filter blood and form urine. |
| Ureters | Carry urine from kidneys to urinary bladder. |
| Urinary bladder | Stores urine. |
| Urethra | Carries urine out of body. |

## Nitrogenous Wastes

Important nitrogenous wastes:

- urea;
- uric acid.

These are removed from blood by kidneys.

## Nephron

Nephron is the basic filtration unit of the kidney.

Each kidney contains many nephrons.

## Parts of a Nephron

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Glomerulus | Cluster of thin-walled blood capillaries where filtration begins. |
| Bowman's capsule | Cup-shaped structure that collects filtrate. |
| Tubule | Reabsorbs useful substances and water; forms urine. |
| Collecting duct | Carries urine toward ureter. |

## Urine Formation

Main steps:

1. Filtration of blood in glomerulus.
2. Collection of filtrate in Bowman's capsule.
3. Selective reabsorption of useful substances.
4. Secretion of some wastes into tubule.
5. Final urine passes into ureter.

## Reabsorbed Substances

Useful substances reabsorbed include:

- glucose;
- amino acids;
- salts;
- major amount of water.

## Regulation of Urine Amount

Urine amount depends on:

- how much excess water is in the body;
- how much dissolved waste must be excreted;
- how much water is reabsorbed in kidney tubules;
- hydration level;
- body needs.

## Urinary Bladder Control

Urine is stored in the urinary bladder.

The bladder is muscular and under nervous control, so we can usually control urination.

---

# Unit 5AA: Artificial Kidney and Dialysis

## When Dialysis Is Needed

Kidneys may fail due to:

- infection;
- injury;
- restricted blood flow;
- disease.

Kidney failure causes poisonous wastes to accumulate in blood.

## Artificial Kidney

An artificial kidney removes nitrogenous wastes from blood through dialysis.

## Dialysis Working

1. Patient's blood passes through tubes with semipermeable lining.
2. Tubes are surrounded by dialysing fluid.
3. Dialysing fluid has same osmotic pressure as blood but no nitrogenous wastes.
4. Wastes diffuse from blood into dialysing fluid.
5. Purified blood is returned to patient.

## Difference from Natural Kidney

Dialysis removes wastes by diffusion, but it does not perform selective reabsorption like a natural nephron does.

## Filtrate Fact

A healthy adult may form about 180 L of initial filtrate per day.

Only about 1-2 L urine is excreted daily because most filtrate is reabsorbed.

---

# Unit 5AB: Excretion in Plants

## Plant Waste Strategies

Plants do not have an excretory system like animals.

They remove or manage wastes in several ways.

## Methods

| Waste Method | Example |
|---|---|
| Gas diffusion | Oxygen from photosynthesis and carbon dioxide from respiration diffuse out. |
| Transpiration | Excess water is lost as vapour. |
| Vacuole storage | Wastes stored inside cell vacuoles. |
| Leaf fall | Wastes stored in leaves are removed when leaves fall. |
| Old xylem storage | Wastes stored as resins and gums. |
| Soil excretion | Some wastes are released into surrounding soil. |

## Oxygen as Waste

Oxygen produced in photosynthesis may be considered a waste product of that process if it is not used immediately by the plant.

## Question 1: Why Is Diffusion Insufficient to Meet Oxygen Requirements of Multicellular Organisms Like Humans?

## Answer

Diffusion is too slow for large multicellular organisms.

Reasons:

- all cells are not in direct contact with the environment;
- oxygen has to travel long distances;
- human cells need oxygen continuously;
- body size is large compared with surface area;
- many tissues are deep inside the body.

Therefore, humans need respiratory and circulatory systems to deliver oxygen rapidly.

## Question 11: What Are the Different Ways in Which Glucose Is Oxidised to Provide Energy?

## Question 17: How Are Water and Minerals Transported in Plants?

## Answer

Water and minerals are transported through xylem.

Process:

1. Root cells actively absorb mineral ions.
2. Water enters roots by osmosis.
3. Root pressure pushes water into xylem.
4. Transpiration from leaves creates suction.
5. Transpiration pull draws water upward to leaves.

---

## Question 18: How Is Food Transported in Plants?

## Answer

Food is transported through phloem by translocation.

Sucrose is loaded into phloem using ATP.

This increases osmotic pressure, water enters, and pressure moves food solution to areas where it is needed.

Phloem transport can be upward or downward.

---

## Question 19: Describe the Structure and Functioning of Nephrons.

## Answer

Nephrons are filtration units of kidneys.

Structure:

- glomerulus: cluster of blood capillaries;
- Bowman's capsule: cup-shaped structure around glomerulus;
- tubule: long coiled tube;
- collecting duct: carries urine onward.

Function:

1. Blood is filtered in the glomerulus.
2. Filtrate enters Bowman's capsule.
3. Useful substances such as glucose, amino acids, salts, and water are reabsorbed.
4. Wastes remain in the tubule.
5. Urine flows to collecting ducts, ureters, bladder, and urethra.

---

## Question 20: What Methods Do Plants Use to Get Rid of Excretory Products?

## Answer

Plants remove wastes by:

- diffusion of gases through stomata and surfaces;
- transpiration of excess water;
- storing wastes in vacuoles;
- storing wastes in leaves that fall off;
- storing wastes as resins and gums in old xylem;
- excreting some wastes into soil.

---

## Question 21: How Is the Amount of Urine Produced Regulated?

## Answer

Urine amount is regulated by selective reabsorption in kidney tubules.

It depends on:

- amount of excess water in the body;
- amount of dissolved waste to be removed;
- hydration state;
- body requirements.

If the body has excess water, less water is reabsorbed and more urine forms.

If the body needs water, more water is reabsorbed and less urine forms.

## Exercise 11: Describe Double Circulation in Human Beings. Why Is It Necessary?

## Answer

Double circulation means blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circuit.

## Pulmonary Circulation

```text
Right ventricle -> Lungs -> Left atrium
```

Deoxygenated blood goes to lungs and becomes oxygenated.

```text
Left ventricle -> Body -> Right atrium
```

Oxygenated blood goes to body tissues and returns deoxygenated.

- separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood;
- supplies oxygen efficiently;
- supports high energy needs;
- helps maintain constant body temperature in mammals.

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Life processes maintain living organisms. Nutrition supplies food and raw materials, respiration releases energy, transportation moves materials, and excretion removes wastes.

Plants use photosynthesis to make carbohydrates from carbon dioxide and water using sunlight and chlorophyll. Animals and fungi use heterotrophic nutrition.

In humans, digestion begins in the mouth with salivary amylase. The stomach uses acid and pepsin. The small intestine completes digestion using bile, pancreatic juice, and intestinal juice. Villi absorb digested food.

Respiration breaks glucose into pyruvate in the cytoplasm. Pyruvate may form ethanol and carbon dioxide in yeast, lactic acid in muscles during oxygen shortage, or carbon dioxide and water in mitochondria during aerobic respiration.

Humans exchange gases through alveoli. Oxygen is carried by haemoglobin, and carbon dioxide is mostly dissolved in blood. The heart, blood, and vessels transport materials. Double circulation separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

Plants transport water and minerals through xylem and food through phloem. Humans excrete nitrogenous wastes using kidneys and nephrons. Plants remove wastes by diffusion, transpiration, storage, leaf fall, resins, gums, and soil excretion.
