# Unit 9A: Light and Ray Optics

## Why We See Objects

We see objects because light from them reaches our eyes.

Objects may:

- emit light, like the Sun or a lamp;
- reflect light, like a book, chair, tree, or face;
- transmit light, like glass or water.

## Straight-Line Propagation

In ordinary situations, light seems to travel in straight lines.

Evidence:

- sharp shadows from small light sources;
- rays drawn in mirror and lens diagrams;
- light beams through small holes.

## Limits of Ray Optics

When obstacles are extremely small, light can bend around them. This is diffraction.

Ray optics does not explain diffraction well.

For this Class 10 chapter, we use the ray model to study reflection and refraction.

---

# Unit 9B: Reflection of Light

## Meaning

Reflection is the bouncing back of light from a surface.

A highly polished surface reflects most of the light falling on it.

## Laws of Reflection

1. Angle of incidence is equal to angle of reflection.
2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

These laws apply to:

- plane mirrors;
- concave mirrors;
- convex mirrors;
- all reflecting surfaces.

## Plane Mirror Image Properties

Image formed by a plane mirror is:

- virtual;
- erect;
- same size as object;
- laterally inverted;
- as far behind the mirror as the object is in front.

# Unit 9C: Spherical Mirrors

## Meaning

A spherical mirror has a reflecting surface that forms part of a sphere.

## Types

| Mirror | Surface | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Concave mirror | Reflecting surface curves inward | Converging mirror |
| Convex mirror | Reflecting surface curves outward | Diverging mirror |

## Spoon Analogy

The inner side of a shining spoon behaves roughly like a concave mirror.

The outer bulging side behaves roughly like a convex mirror.

## Important Terms

| Term | Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Pole | `P` | Centre of reflecting surface. |
| Centre of curvature | `C` | Centre of sphere of which mirror is a part. |
| Radius of curvature | `R` | Distance `PC`. |
| Principal axis | - | Line through `P` and `C`. |
| Principal focus | `F` | Point where parallel rays meet or appear to meet. |
| Focal length | `f` | Distance `PF`. |
| Aperture | - | Diameter of reflecting surface. |

## Concave Mirror Focus

Parallel rays to the principal axis actually meet at the focus after reflection.

Therefore, focus of a concave mirror is real and lies in front of the mirror.

## Convex Mirror Focus

Parallel rays appear to come from the focus after reflection.

Therefore, focus of a convex mirror is virtual and lies behind the mirror.

## Relation Between `R` and `f`

For spherical mirrors of small aperture:

$$
R = 2f
$$

So the focus lies midway between pole and centre of curvature.

# Unit 9D: Concave Mirror Image Formation

## Image Table for Concave Mirror

| Object Position | Image Position | Size | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus `F` | Highly diminished, point-sized | Real and inverted |
| Beyond `C` | Between `F` and `C` | Diminished | Real and inverted |
| At `C` | At `C` | Same size | Real and inverted |
| Between `C` and `F` | Beyond `C` | Enlarged | Real and inverted |
| At `F` | At infinity | Highly enlarged | Image not obtained on nearby screen |
| Between `P` and `F` | Behind mirror | Enlarged | Virtual and erect |

## Key Pattern

Concave mirror can form:

- real, inverted images;
- virtual, erect, enlarged image when object is between pole and focus.

## Uses of Concave Mirrors

| Use | Reason |
|---|---|
| Torches/searchlights/headlights | Produce strong parallel beam when source is near focus. |
| Shaving mirror | Gives enlarged erect image when face is between `P` and `F`. |
| Dentist mirror | Gives enlarged image of teeth. |
| Solar furnace | Concentrates sunlight at focus to produce heat. |

---

# Unit 9E: Convex Mirror Image Formation

## Image Table for Convex Mirror

| Object Position | Image Position | Size | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At focus behind mirror | Highly diminished | Virtual and erect |
| Between infinity and pole | Between `P` and `F` behind mirror | Diminished | Virtual and erect |

## Key Pattern

A convex mirror always forms an image that is:

- virtual;
- erect;
- diminished;
- behind the mirror.

## Uses of Convex Mirrors

Convex mirrors are used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles because:

- they always give erect images;
- images are diminished, so more area is visible;
- they have a wider field of view.

# Unit 9F: Ray Rules for Spherical Mirrors

## Rule 1: Parallel Ray

A ray parallel to the principal axis:

- after reflection from a concave mirror, passes through focus;
- after reflection from a convex mirror, appears to come from focus.

## Rule 2: Focus Ray

A ray passing through the focus of a concave mirror reflects parallel to the principal axis.

A ray directed toward the focus of a convex mirror reflects parallel to the principal axis.

## Rule 3: Centre of Curvature Ray

A ray passing through centre of curvature of a concave mirror reflects back along the same path.

A ray directed toward centre of curvature of a convex mirror reflects back along the same path.

Reason:

It strikes the mirror normally.

## Rule 4: Pole Ray

A ray incident at the pole reflects according to the laws of reflection.

Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection.

| Rule | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Origin | Pole `P` is origin. |
| Axis | Principal axis is x-axis. |
| Object position | Object is placed to the left of mirror. |
| Distances | All distances measured from pole. |
| Right of pole | Positive. |
| Left of pole | Negative. |
| Above principal axis | Positive height. |
| Below principal axis | Negative height. |

## Mirror Focal Length Signs

| Mirror | Focal Length Sign |
|---|---|
| Concave mirror | Negative |
| Convex mirror | Positive |

## Image Distance Signs for Mirrors

| Image Type/Position | Sign of `v` |
|---|---|
| Real image in front of mirror | Negative |
| Virtual image behind mirror | Positive |

# Unit 9H: Mirror Formula and Magnification

## Mirror Formula

$$
\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}
$$

Where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `u` | Object distance from pole |
| `v` | Image distance from pole |
| `f` | Focal length |

## Magnification

$$
m = \frac{h'}{h}
$$

For mirrors:

$$
m = -\frac{v}{u}
$$

## Interpreting Magnification for Mirrors

| Magnification | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `m > 0` | Virtual and erect image. |
| `m < 0` | Real and inverted image. |
| `|m| > 1` | Enlarged image. |
| `|m| < 1` | Diminished image. |
| `|m| = 1` | Same size image. |

# Unit 9I: Refraction of Light

## Meaning

Refraction is the change in direction of light when it travels obliquely from one transparent medium to another.

It happens because speed of light changes in different media.

## Everyday Examples

| Observation | Cause |
|---|---|
| Pencil partly in water looks bent | Refraction at air-water boundary. |
| Coin in water appears raised | Refraction makes apparent position different. |
| Letters under glass slab appear raised | Refraction through glass. |
| Lemon in water appears bigger | Refraction and magnification through curved water-glass surfaces. |

## Rarer to Denser

When light travels from optically rarer medium to optically denser medium:

- speed decreases;
- ray bends towards the normal.

Example:

```text
Air -> Glass
```

## Denser to Rarer

When light travels from optically denser medium to optically rarer medium:

- speed increases;
- ray bends away from the normal.

Example:

```text
Glass -> Air
```

## Normal Incidence

If a ray enters normally to the surface, it does not bend because angle of incidence is zero.

It may change speed, but direction remains same.

---

# Unit 9J: Refraction Through a Rectangular Glass Slab

## What Happens

At first surface:

- light goes from air to glass;
- it bends towards the normal.

At second surface:

- light goes from glass to air;
- it bends away from the normal.

## Emergent Ray

For a rectangular glass slab:

- emergent ray is parallel to incident ray;
- ray is shifted sideways.

This happens because opposite faces of the slab are parallel, so bending at the two surfaces is equal and opposite.

## Lateral Displacement

The sideways shift between incident ray direction and emergent ray is called lateral displacement.

---

# Unit 9K: Laws of Refraction

## First Law

Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

## Second Law: Snell's Law

For light of a given colour and a given pair of media:

$$
\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = constant
$$

The constant is the refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first.

## Validity

In the school-level statement, this is considered for:

```text
0 degrees < i < 90 degrees
```

# Unit 9L: Refractive Index and Optical Density

## Relative Refractive Index

If light travels from medium 1 to medium 2:

$$
n_{21} = \frac{Speed\ of\ light\ in\ medium\ 1}{Speed\ of\ light\ in\ medium\ 2}
$$

Using speeds:

$$
n_{21} = \frac{v_1}{v_2}
$$

## Absolute Refractive Index

If first medium is vacuum or air:

$$
n = \frac{c}{v}
$$

Where:

- `c` is speed of light in air/vacuum;
- `v` is speed of light in the medium.

## Speed of Light

Speed of light in vacuum:

$$
c = 3 \times 10^8\ m/s
$$

## Optical Density

Optical density depends on refractive index, not mass density.

| Higher Refractive Index | Lower Refractive Index |
|---|---|
| Optically denser | Optically rarer |
| Light speed lower | Light speed higher |

Example:

Kerosene is optically denser than water because its refractive index is higher, even though its mass density is lower.

# Unit 9M: Spherical Lenses

## Meaning

A lens is a transparent material bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is spherical.

## Types

| Lens | Shape | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Convex lens | Thicker at middle, thinner at edges | Converges light |
| Concave lens | Thinner at middle, thicker at edges | Diverges light |

## Convex Lens

A convex lens is also called a converging lens.

Parallel rays converge at its focus after refraction.

## Concave Lens

A concave lens is also called a diverging lens.

Parallel rays appear to diverge from its focus after refraction.

## Lens Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Centres of curvature `C1`, `C2` | Centres of spheres forming lens surfaces. |
| Principal axis | Line passing through both centres of curvature. |
| Optical centre `O` | Central point of lens. |
| Principal foci `F1`, `F2` | Focus points on two sides of lens. |
| Focal length `f` | Distance between optical centre and principal focus. |
| Aperture | Effective diameter of circular outline of lens. |

## Optical Centre Rule

A ray passing through the optical centre of a thin lens emerges without deviation.

# Unit 9N: Image Formation by Convex Lens

## Convex Lens Image Table

| Object Position | Image Position | Size | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At `F2` | Highly diminished | Real and inverted |
| Beyond `2F1` | Between `F2` and `2F2` | Diminished | Real and inverted |
| At `2F1` | At `2F2` | Same size | Real and inverted |
| Between `F1` and `2F1` | Beyond `2F2` | Enlarged | Real and inverted |
| At `F1` | At infinity | Highly enlarged | Image not formed on nearby screen |
| Between `F1` and `O` | Same side as object | Enlarged | Virtual and erect |

## Key Pattern

A convex lens can form:

- real and inverted images for object outside focus;
- virtual, erect, enlarged image when object is between focus and optical centre.

This is why a convex lens can act as a magnifying glass.

---

# Unit 9O: Image Formation by Concave Lens

## Concave Lens Image Table

| Object Position | Image Position | Size | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| At infinity | At `F1` on same side | Highly diminished | Virtual and erect |
| Between infinity and optical centre | Between `F1` and `O` on same side | Diminished | Virtual and erect |

## Key Pattern

A concave lens always forms an image that is:

- virtual;
- erect;
- diminished;
- on the same side as the object.

---

# Unit 9P: Ray Rules for Lenses

## Rule 1: Parallel Ray

For convex lens:

- ray parallel to principal axis passes through focus on other side after refraction.

For concave lens:

- ray parallel to principal axis appears to diverge from focus on same side.

## Rule 2: Focus Ray

For convex lens:

- ray passing through focus emerges parallel to principal axis.

For concave lens:

- ray directed towards focus on other side emerges parallel to principal axis.

## Rule 3: Optical Centre Ray

A ray passing through optical centre of a lens emerges without deviation.

---

# Unit 9Q: Sign Convention for Lenses

## Main Rule

Sign convention for lenses is similar to mirrors, but distances are measured from optical centre `O`.

## Lens Focal Length Signs

| Lens | Focal Length Sign |
|---|---|
| Convex lens | Positive |
| Concave lens | Negative |

## Image Distance Signs for Lenses

| Image Position | Sign of `v` |
|---|---|
| Image on right side of lens | Positive |
| Image on left side of lens | Negative |

For a real image formed by a convex lens on the other side, `v` is positive.

For a virtual image on the same side as object, `v` is negative.

## Lens Formula

$$
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}
$$

## Lens Magnification

$$
m = \frac{h'}{h}
$$

For lenses:

$$
m = \frac{v}{u}

| Magnification | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `m > 0` | Virtual and erect image. |
| `m < 0` | Real and inverted image. |

# Unit 9S: Power of a Lens

## Meaning

Power of a lens measures its ability to converge or diverge light.

Shorter focal length means greater power.

## Formula

$$
P = \frac{1}{f}
$$

Here `f` must be in metres.

## Unit

SI unit of power is dioptre, symbol `D`.

1 dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre.

$$
1D = 1m^{-1}
$$

## Positive and Negative Power

| Lens | Power Sign |
|---|---|
| Convex lens | Positive |
| Concave lens | Negative |

## Power of Lens Combination

For lenses in contact:

$$
P = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + ...
$$

This is why opticians use power values conveniently.

# Unit 9T: Solved In-Text Questions

## Question 1: Define Principal Focus of a Concave Mirror.

## Answer

The principal focus of a concave mirror is the point on the principal axis where rays parallel to the principal axis meet after reflection.

It is represented by `F`.

---

## Question 2: Radius of Curvature of a Spherical Mirror Is 20 cm. What Is Its Focal Length?

## Answer

For a spherical mirror:

$$
R = 2f
$$

So:

$$
f = \frac{R}{2} = \frac{20}{2} = 10\ cm
$$

Focal length is 10 cm.

---

## Question 3: Name a Mirror That Can Give an Erect and Enlarged Image.

## Answer

A concave mirror can give an erect and enlarged image when the object is placed between the pole and the principal focus.

---

## Question 4: Why Is a Convex Mirror Preferred as Rear-View Mirror?

## Answer

A convex mirror is preferred because:

- it always forms an erect image;
- image is diminished, so more area is visible;
- it has a wider field of view.

This helps drivers see traffic behind them over a larger area.

---

## Question 5: Find the Focal Length of a Convex Mirror of Radius 32 cm.

## Answer

For a convex mirror:

$$
f = \frac{R}{2}
$$

$$
f = \frac{32}{2} = 16\ cm
$$

Since it is convex, focal length is positive:

```text
f = +16 cm
```

---

## Question 6: A Concave Mirror Produces Three Times Magnified Real Image of an Object 10 cm in Front. Where Is the Image?

## Given

```text
u = -10 cm
m = -3
```

Real image has negative magnification.

## Working

For mirrors:

$$
m = -\frac{v}{u}
$$

Substitute:

$$
-3 = -\frac{v}{-10}
$$

$$
-3 = \frac{v}{10}
$$

$$
v = -30\ cm
$$

## Answer

The image is located 30 cm in front of the mirror.

It is real, inverted, and enlarged.

---

## Question 7: A Ray of Light Travelling in Air Enters Water Obliquely. Does It Bend Towards or Away from Normal?

## Answer

It bends towards the normal.

Reason:

Air is optically rarer than water. When light travels from rarer to denser medium, its speed decreases and it bends towards the normal.

---

## Question 8: Light Enters Glass of Refractive Index 1.50. Find Speed of Light in Glass.

## Given

```text
n = 1.50
c = 3 x 10^8 m/s
```

## Working

$$
n = \frac{c}{v}
$$

$$
v = \frac{c}{n}
$$

$$
v = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{1.50} = 2 \times 10^8\ m/s
$$

## Answer

Speed of light in glass is:

```text
2 x 10^8 m/s
```

---

## Question 9: Which Medium Has Highest and Lowest Optical Density in the Table?

## Answer

Highest optical density:

- diamond, refractive index 2.42.

Lowest optical density:

- air, refractive index 1.0003.

---

## Question 10: In Kerosene, Turpentine, and Water, Where Does Light Travel Fastest?

## Answer

Refractive indices:

| Medium | Refractive Index |
|---|---:|
| Water | 1.33 |
| Kerosene | 1.44 |
| Turpentine oil | 1.47 |

Lower refractive index means higher speed of light.

Therefore, light travels fastest in water.

---

## Question 11: Refractive Index of Diamond Is 2.42. What Does This Mean?

## Answer

It means:

$$
n = \frac{c}{v} = 2.42
$$

So speed of light in air/vacuum is 2.42 times the speed of light in diamond.

Light travels much slower in diamond than in air.

---

## Question 12: Define 1 Dioptre.

## Answer

1 dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre.

$$
1D = 1m^{-1}
$$

---

## Question 13: Convex Lens Forms Real Inverted Image of Needle at 50 cm, Equal in Size. Find Object Position and Power.

## Reasoning

For a convex lens, real image equal to object size is formed when:

```text
Object at 2F1 and image at 2F2
```

Given image distance:

```text
v = 50 cm = 2f
```

Therefore:

$$
f = 25\ cm = 0.25\ m
$$

Object is also at:

```text
2f = 50 cm
```

So needle is placed 50 cm in front of the lens.

Power:

$$
P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{0.25} = +4D
$$

## Answer

Object is placed 50 cm in front of the lens.

Power is `+4 D`.

---

## Question 14: Find Power of Concave Lens of Focal Length 2 m.

## Answer

For concave lens:

```text
f = -2 m
```

$$
P = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{-2} = -0.5D
$$

Power is `-0.5 D`.

## Answer

Use a convex lens of focal length 5 cm.

A convex lens of focal length 5 cm.

## Exercise 7: Erect Image Using Concave Mirror of Focal Length 15 cm

## Answer

For a concave mirror to form an erect image, object must be between pole `P` and focus `F`.

Therefore object distance must be:

```text
less than 15 cm from the mirror
```

## Nature of Image

The image is:

- virtual;
- erect;
- enlarged;
- formed behind the mirror.

## Ray Diagram Description

Draw:

1. Concave mirror with `P`, `F`, and `C`.
2. Object between `P` and `F`.
3. One ray parallel to principal axis reflecting through `F`.
4. One ray to pole reflecting by equal angles.
5. Extend reflected rays behind mirror.
6. Their extensions meet behind mirror, giving enlarged virtual erect image.

## Answer

| Situation | Mirror | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Headlights of car | Concave mirror | Produces strong parallel beam when bulb is at focus. |
| Side/rear-view mirror | Convex mirror | Gives erect diminished image and wide field of view. |
| Solar furnace | Concave mirror | Concentrates sunlight at focus to produce heat. |

## Exercise 9: Half of a Convex Lens Is Covered. Will It Produce Complete Image?

## Answer

Yes, it will produce a complete image.

However, the image will be less bright.

## Reason

Each part of a convex lens can refract rays from the whole object and form the full image.

Covering half the lens reduces the amount of light passing through, so brightness decreases.

## Experimental Verification

Form a sharp image of a candle using a convex lens on a screen.

Cover half the lens with black paper.

Observation:

- complete image still forms;
- image becomes dimmer.

## Exercise 10: Object 5 cm Long, 25 cm from Convex Lens of Focal Length 10 cm

## Given

```text
h = +5 cm
u = -25 cm
f = +10 cm
```

## Find Image Distance

Lens formula:

$$
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{-25} = \frac{1}{10}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{25} = \frac{1}{10}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{v} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{25}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{v} = \frac{5 - 2}{50} = \frac{3}{50}
$$

$$

$$

## Find Image Size

$$
m = \frac{v}{u} = \frac{16.67}{-25} = -0.667
$$

$$
h' = mh = -0.667 \times 5 = -3.33\ cm
$$

## Answer

Image forms 16.67 cm on the other side of the lens.

Image is:

- real;
- inverted;
- diminished;
- about 3.33 cm tall.

# Unit 9V: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Using mirror and lens formula interchangeably. | Mirror: `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`; Lens: `1/v - 1/u = 1/f`. |
| Forgetting object distance is usually negative. | Object is on left side, so `u` is negative in this convention. |
| Treating all erect images as real. | Erect images in mirrors/lenses here are usually virtual. |
| Saying convex mirror magnifies. | Convex mirror always gives diminished image. |
| Saying concave lens can form real image of real object. | Concave lens always forms virtual, erect, diminished image for real object. |
| Confusing optical density with mass density. | Optical density depends on refractive index, not mass density. |
| Forgetting metres in lens power. | Use focal length in metres for dioptres. |
| Missing sign of focal length. | Concave mirror and concave lens have negative `f`; convex mirror and convex lens have positive `f`. |

|---|---|

| Travel Direction | Speed Change | Bending |
|---|---|---|
| Rarer to denser | Slows down | Towards normal |
| Denser to rarer | Speeds up | Away from normal |
| Along normal | Speed changes | No bending |

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Light travels in straight lines in ray optics. Reflection occurs from polished surfaces and follows two laws: angle of incidence equals angle of reflection, and incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in one plane.

Spherical mirrors are concave or convex. Concave mirrors can form real/inverted or virtual/erect images depending on object position. Convex mirrors always form virtual, erect, diminished images and are used as rear-view mirrors.

Refraction is bending of light when it travels obliquely from one transparent medium to another due to speed change. Rarer to denser bends towards normal; denser to rarer bends away from normal. Refractive index compares speed of light in different media.

Convex lenses converge light and can form real or virtual images depending on object position. Concave lenses diverge light and always form virtual, erect, diminished images for real objects.

Mirror formula is `1/v + 1/u = 1/f`; lens formula is `1/v - 1/u = 1/f`. Lens power is `P = 1/f` with `f` in metres and unit dioptre.
