# Unit 11A: Electric Current and Circuit

## What Is Electric Current?

Electric current is the flow of electric charge.

In a metal wire, the moving charges are mainly electrons. When these electrons move in an organised way through the wire, current is said to flow.

The amount of current depends on:

| Factor | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Charge passing** | More charge passing a point means larger current. |
| **Time taken** | The same charge passing in less time means larger current. |

The mathematical definition is:

$$
I = \frac{Q}{t}
$$

where:

| Symbol | Meaning | SI Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `I` | electric current | ampere, A |
| `Q` | electric charge | coulomb, C |
| `t` | time | second, s |

## Electric Circuit

An electric circuit is a continuous and closed conducting path through which current can flow.

For example, a torch bulb glows only when the cells, switch, wires, and bulb form a complete path. If the switch is open, the path breaks and current stops.

| Circuit State | Current Flow? | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Closed circuit** | Yes | Bulb can glow or device can work. |
| **Open circuit** | No | Bulb does not glow or device stops. |

## Role of a Switch

A switch controls whether the conducting path is complete.

| Switch Position | Circuit Condition | Current |
|---|---|---|
| **On** | Closed | Current flows. |
| **Off** | Open | Current stops. |

## Direction of Current

In metallic conductors, electrons move from the negative terminal toward the positive terminal.

However, by convention, electric current is taken in the opposite direction:

| Flow Type | Direction Outside the Cell |
|---|---|
| **Electron flow** | Negative terminal to positive terminal. |
| **Conventional current** | Positive terminal to negative terminal. |

This convention was accepted before electrons were discovered, and it is still used in circuit diagrams and textbooks.

## Unit of Current

The SI unit of current is ampere.

One ampere is the current produced when one coulomb of charge flows through a conductor in one second.

$$
1 \ \text{A} = \frac{1 \ \text{C}}{1 \ \text{s}}
$$

Small currents are often expressed as:

| Unit | Symbol | Value in Ampere |
|---|---|---|
| milliampere | mA | `10^-3 A` |
| microampere | microampere or `mu A` | `10^-6 A` |

## Charge in One Coulomb

The charge of one electron has magnitude:

$$
e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \ \text{C}
$$

The number of electrons making one coulomb of charge is:

$$
n = \frac{1}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}}
$$

$$
n = 6.25 \times 10^{18}
$$

So, one coulomb contains the charge of about `6.25 x 10^18` electrons.

## Ammeter

An ammeter measures current.

Important rules:

| Rule | Reason |
|---|---|
| Connect ammeter in series. | The same current must pass through the meter. |
| Use correct range. | Too much current can damage the meter. |
| Ammeter has very low resistance. | It should not significantly reduce circuit current. |

## Worked Example: Charge Flow

**Question:** A current of `0.5 A` flows through a bulb for `10 min`. Find the charge that flows through the circuit.

Given:

$$
I = 0.5 \ \text{A}
$$

$$
t = 10 \ \text{min} = 600 \ \text{s}
$$

Using:

$$
Q = It
$$

Substitute:

$$
Q = 0.5 \times 600
$$

$$
Q = 300 \ \text{C}
$$

**Answer:** `300 C` of charge flows through the circuit.

# Unit 11B: Electric Potential and Potential Difference

## Why Charges Flow

Charges do not flow through a conductor by themselves. A potential difference is needed to push charges through the circuit.

The idea is similar to water flow:

| Water Flow | Electric Flow |
|---|---|
| Water flows when there is a pressure difference. | Charges flow when there is a potential difference. |
| Pump maintains pressure difference. | Cell or battery maintains potential difference. |
| Water moves through pipe. | Charges move through conductor. |

## Electric Potential Difference

Potential difference between two points is the work done to move a unit charge from one point to the other.

$$
V = \frac{W}{Q}
$$

where:

| Symbol | Meaning | SI Unit |
|---|---|---|
| `V` | potential difference | volt, V |
| `W` | work done | joule, J |
| `Q` | charge | coulomb, C |

## Unit of Potential Difference

The SI unit of potential difference is volt.

One volt is the potential difference between two points when one joule of work is done to move one coulomb of charge between them.

$$
1 \ \text{V} = 1 \ \text{J C}^{-1}
$$

## Role of Cell or Battery

A cell uses chemical energy to create potential difference across its terminals.

When a cell is connected in a closed circuit:

1. The cell creates potential difference.
2. The potential difference pushes charges.
3. Current flows through the circuit.
4. Chemical energy changes into electrical energy and then into other forms such as heat, light, or motion.

## Voltmeter

A voltmeter measures potential difference.

Important rules:

| Rule | Reason |
|---|---|
| Connect voltmeter in parallel. | It compares energy difference between two points. |
| Voltmeter has high resistance. | It should draw very little current. |
| Connect across the component. | It measures the potential difference of that component. |

## Worked Example: Work Done

**Question:** How much work is done in moving a charge of `2 C` across two points having a potential difference of `12 V`?

Given:

$$
Q = 2 \ \text{C}
$$

$$
V = 12 \ \text{V}
$$

Using:

$$
W = VQ
$$

Substitute:

$$
W = 12 \times 2
$$

$$
W = 24 \ \text{J}
$$

**Answer:** The work done is `24 J`.

# Unit 11C: Circuit Diagrams

## Why Circuit Diagrams Are Used

Real circuits can be messy to draw. Circuit diagrams use standard symbols to represent electrical components clearly.

A good circuit diagram should show:

| Feature | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Source** | Shows where potential difference comes from. |
| **Closed path** | Shows whether current can flow. |
| **Components** | Shows resistors, bulbs, switches, meters, and wires. |
| **Connections** | Shows whether parts are in series or parallel. |
| **Meter placement** | Prevents wrong measurement and wrong conclusions. |

## Common Components

| Component | Learner Description |
|---|---|
| **Cell** | Single source of potential difference. |
| **Battery** | Two or more cells connected together. |
| **Plug key or switch** | Opens or closes the circuit. |
| **Wire joint** | Point where wires are connected. |
| **Wires crossing without joining** | Wires cross but are not electrically connected. |
| **Resistor** | Provides resistance. |
| **Variable resistance or rheostat** | Changes resistance to regulate current. |
| **Ammeter** | Measures current in series. |
| **Voltmeter** | Measures potential difference in parallel. |

## Correct Meter Placement

| Meter | Measures | Correct Connection |
|---|---|---|
| Ammeter | Current | Series with the component or branch. |
| Voltmeter | Potential difference | Parallel across the component. |

## Common Error

Do not connect an ammeter in parallel across a component. Because an ammeter has very low resistance, this can allow a very large current to flow and may damage the meter.

# Unit 11D: Ohm's Law

## Relationship Between Voltage and Current

For a metallic conductor at constant temperature, current increases when the potential difference increases.

Experiments with a nichrome wire show that:

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `V/I` remains constant. | Resistance is constant for the conductor at fixed temperature. |
| V-I graph is a straight line through origin. | Current is directly proportional to potential difference. |

## Statement of Ohm's Law

Ohm's law states:

The potential difference across the ends of a given metallic conductor is directly proportional to the current flowing through it, provided its temperature remains constant.

In symbols:

$$
V \propto I
$$

Therefore:

$$
\frac{V}{I} = R
$$

So:

$$
V = IR
$$

## Resistance

Resistance is the property of a conductor by which it opposes the flow of charge.

$$
R = \frac{V}{I}
$$

The SI unit of resistance is ohm.

$$
1 \ \Omega = 1 \ \text{V A}^{-1}
$$

If a potential difference of `1 V` produces a current of `1 A` through a conductor, its resistance is `1 ohm`.

## Meaning of Larger Resistance

For the same potential difference:

| Resistance | Current |
|---|---|
| Low resistance | Large current. |
| High resistance | Small current. |

From Ohm's law:

$$
I = \frac{V}{R}
$$

If resistance doubles and voltage remains the same, current becomes half.

## Rheostat

A rheostat is a variable resistor used to change current in a circuit.

It is useful when we want to:

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Increase current | Decrease resistance. |
| Decrease current | Increase resistance. |
| Study Ohm's law | Change current gradually and record readings. |

## Conductors, Resistors, and Insulators

| Material Type | Resistance | Current Flow |
|---|---|---|
| Good conductor | Very low | Easy. |
| Resistor | Appreciable | Controlled. |
| Poor conductor | High | Difficult. |
| Insulator | Very high | Negligible under normal conditions. |

# Unit 11E: Resistance of a Conductor

## Why Different Wires Have Different Resistance

The resistance of a conducting wire depends on:

1. Length of the wire.
2. Area of cross-section.
3. Nature of material.
4. Temperature.

## Dependence on Length

For the same material and same thickness:

$$
R \propto l
$$

A longer wire has greater resistance because electrons face more collisions while moving through it.

| Wire Length | Resistance |
|---|---|
| Short wire | Lower resistance. |
| Long wire | Higher resistance. |

## Dependence on Area of Cross-Section

For the same material and same length:

$$
R \propto \frac{1}{A}
$$

A thicker wire has lower resistance because charges get more conducting path.

| Wire Thickness | Area | Resistance |
|---|---|---|
| Thin wire | Small | High. |
| Thick wire | Large | Low. |

## Dependence on Material

Different materials resist current differently.

| Material Type | Resistivity | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Good conductor | Low | Silver, copper, aluminium. |
| Alloy | Higher than pure metals | Nichrome, manganin, constantan. |
| Insulator | Very high | Glass, rubber, ebonite. |

## Resistance Formula

Combining the length and area relations:

$$
R = \rho \frac{l}{A}
$$

where `rho` is electrical resistivity.

## Resistivity

Resistivity is a characteristic property of a material.

It tells how strongly a material opposes electric current, independent of the shape of a particular sample.

$$
\rho = \frac{RA}{l}
$$

The SI unit of resistivity is:

$$
\Omega \ \text{m}
$$

## Selected Resistivity Values

You do not need to memorise all values, but you should understand their order.

| Material | Type | Approximate Resistivity at 20 degrees C |
|---|---|---|
| Silver | conductor | `1.60 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Copper | conductor | `1.62 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Aluminium | conductor | `2.63 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Tungsten | conductor | `5.20 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Iron | conductor | `10.0 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Mercury | conductor | `94.0 x 10^-8 ohm m` |
| Manganese | conductor | `1.84 x 10^-6 ohm m` |
| Constantan | alloy | `49 x 10^-6 ohm m` |
| Nichrome | alloy | `100 x 10^-6 ohm m` |
| Glass | insulator | about `10^10` to `10^14 ohm m` |
| Hard rubber | insulator | about `10^13` to `10^16 ohm m` |

## Why Alloys Are Used in Heating Devices

Alloys such as nichrome are used in electric heaters, toasters, and irons because:

| Property | Usefulness |
|---|---|
| High resistivity | Produces more heat for a given size. |
| High melting point | Does not melt easily when hot. |
| Does not oxidise readily | Lasts longer at high temperature. |

## Why Copper and Aluminium Are Used in Transmission Lines

Copper and aluminium have low resistivity, so they allow current to flow with less energy loss.

They are also ductile, meaning they can be drawn into wires.

# Unit 11F: Resistors in Series

## Meaning of Series Connection

Resistors are in series when they are connected end to end and current has only one path.

In a series circuit:

| Quantity | Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Current | Same through every resistor. |
| Potential difference | Divides across resistors. |
| Equivalent resistance | Sum of individual resistances. |

## Current in Series

Since there is only one path:

$$
I = I_1 = I_2 = I_3
$$

## Potential Difference in Series

The total potential difference equals the sum of potential differences across individual resistors.

$$
V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3
$$

## Equivalent Resistance in Series

Using Ohm's law:

$$
V = IR_s
$$

For each resistor:

$$
V_1 = IR_1
$$

$$
V_2 = IR_2
$$

$$
V_3 = IR_3
$$

Since:

$$
V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3
$$

we get:

$$
IR_s = IR_1 + IR_2 + IR_3
$$

Therefore:

$$
R_s = R_1 + R_2 + R_3
$$

## Important Result

The equivalent resistance in series is greater than any individual resistance.

Example:

$$
R_s = 2 + 3 + 6 = 11 \ \Omega
$$

## Voltage Division

In series, the resistor with higher resistance gets a larger share of voltage.

For the same current:

$$
V = IR
$$

So, if `R` is larger, `V` is larger.

## Disadvantages of Series Circuits in Homes

Series circuits are not used for domestic wiring because:

| Problem | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Same current through all appliances | Appliances need different currents. |
| One failure breaks circuit | If one device fails, all stop working. |
| Voltage divides | Each appliance may not get rated voltage. |
| Independent control is difficult | Switching one appliance can affect others. |

## Worked Example: Series Lamp and Resistor

**Question:** A lamp of resistance `20 ohm` and a conductor of resistance `4 ohm` are connected in series to a `6 V` battery. Find total resistance, circuit current, and potential difference across each.

Total resistance:

$$
R_s = 20 + 4
$$

$$
R_s = 24 \ \Omega
$$

Current:

$$
I = \frac{V}{R_s}
$$

$$
I = \frac{6}{24}
$$

$$
I = 0.25 \ \text{A}
$$

Voltage across lamp:

$$
V_1 = IR_1
$$

$$
V_1 = 0.25 \times 20
$$

$$
V_1 = 5 \ \text{V}
$$

Voltage across conductor:

$$
V_2 = IR_2
$$

$$
V_2 = 0.25 \times 4
$$

$$
V_2 = 1 \ \text{V}
$$

Check:

$$
V_1 + V_2 = 5 + 1 = 6 \ \text{V}
$$

# Unit 11G: Resistors in Parallel

## Meaning of Parallel Connection

Resistors are in parallel when they are connected between the same two points.

In a parallel circuit:

| Quantity | Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Potential difference | Same across every branch. |
| Current | Divides into branches. |
| Equivalent resistance | Less than the smallest individual resistance. |

## Voltage in Parallel

Since each resistor is connected across the same two points:

$$
V = V_1 = V_2 = V_3
$$

## Current in Parallel

The total current is the sum of branch currents.

$$
I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3
$$

## Equivalent Resistance in Parallel

Using Ohm's law:

$$
I = \frac{V}{R_p}
$$

For branches:

$$
I_1 = \frac{V}{R_1}
$$

$$
I_2 = \frac{V}{R_2}
$$

$$
I_3 = \frac{V}{R_3}
$$

Since:

$$
I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{V}{R_p} = \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} + \frac{V}{R_3}
$$

Cancel `V`:

$$
\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}
$$

## Important Result

Adding more resistors in parallel gives extra paths for current, so equivalent resistance decreases.

For example:

$$
\frac{1}{R_p} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{6}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{R_p} = 1
$$

$$
R_p = 1 \ \Omega
$$

## Why Parallel Circuits Are Used in Homes

Domestic appliances are connected in parallel because:

| Advantage | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Same voltage | Each appliance gets the supply voltage. |
| Independent operation | One appliance can be switched off without stopping others. |
| Different current needs | Each appliance draws current according to its resistance and power. |
| Fault isolation | Failure of one appliance does not break the whole circuit. |

## Worked Example: Three Parallel Resistors

**Question:** Resistors `5 ohm`, `10 ohm`, and `30 ohm` are connected in parallel to a `12 V` battery. Find branch currents, total current, and equivalent resistance.

Current through `5 ohm`:

$$
I_1 = \frac{12}{5}
$$

$$
I_1 = 2.4 \ \text{A}
$$

Current through `10 ohm`:

$$
I_2 = \frac{12}{10}
$$

$$
I_2 = 1.2 \ \text{A}
$$

Current through `30 ohm`:

$$
I_3 = \frac{12}{30}
$$

$$
I_3 = 0.4 \ \text{A}
$$

Total current:

$$
I = 2.4 + 1.2 + 0.4
$$

$$
I = 4.0 \ \text{A}
$$

Equivalent resistance:

$$
R_p = \frac{V}{I}
$$

$$
R_p = \frac{12}{4}
$$

$$
R_p = 3 \ \Omega
$$

## Worked Example: Mixed Combination

**Question:** `R1 = 10 ohm` and `R2 = 40 ohm` are in parallel. `R3 = 30 ohm`, `R4 = 20 ohm`, and `R5 = 60 ohm` are in parallel. The two groups are connected in series to a `12 V` battery. Find total resistance and total current.

First group:

$$
\frac{1}{R'} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{40}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{R'} = \frac{4}{40} + \frac{1}{40} = \frac{5}{40}
$$

$$
R' = 8 \ \Omega
$$

Second group:

$$
\frac{1}{R''} = \frac{1}{30} + \frac{1}{20} + \frac{1}{60}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{R''} = \frac{2}{60} + \frac{3}{60} + \frac{1}{60}
$$

$$
\frac{1}{R''} = \frac{6}{60}
$$

$$
R'' = 10 \ \Omega
$$

Total resistance:

$$
R = R' + R''
$$

$$
R = 8 + 10 = 18 \ \Omega
$$

Total current:

$$
I = \frac{V}{R}
$$

$$
I = \frac{12}{18}
$$

$$
I = 0.67 \ \text{A}
$$

# Unit 11H: Heating Effect of Electric Current

## Where Electrical Energy Goes

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat.

This is why:

| Observation | Reason |
|---|---|
| Electric iron becomes hot. | Current heats the resistance element. |
| Toaster glows red. | Its heating wire reaches high temperature. |
| Fan motor becomes warm after long use. | Some electrical energy is lost as heat. |

If the circuit is purely resistive, the electrical energy supplied by the source is mainly converted into heat.

## Derivation of Heating Formula

Work done to move charge `Q` through potential difference `V`:

$$
W = VQ
$$

Since:

$$
Q = It
$$

we get:

$$
W = VIt
$$

This energy appears as heat:

$$
H = VIt
$$

Using Ohm's law:

$$
V = IR
$$

Therefore:

$$
H = I^2Rt
$$

This is Joule's law of heating.

## Joule's Law of Heating

Heat produced in a resistor is:

| Relation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Directly proportional to `I^2` | Doubling current makes heat four times, if `R` and `t` are constant. |
| Directly proportional to `R` | Larger resistance produces more heat for same current and time. |
| Directly proportional to `t` | Longer current flow produces more heat. |

## Formula Selection

| Given Data | Best Formula |
|---|---|
| `V`, `I`, `t` | `H = VIt` |
| `I`, `R`, `t` | `H = I^2Rt` |
| `V`, `R`, `t` | `H = V^2t/R` |

## Practical Applications

| Device | How Heating Effect Is Used |
|---|---|
| Electric iron | Heating element converts electrical energy into heat. |
| Electric toaster | Nichrome wire heats bread. |
| Electric kettle | Heating coil heats water. |
| Electric oven | Heating coil produces high temperature. |
| Electric bulb | Filament becomes hot and emits light. |
| Fuse | Wire melts when current exceeds safe value. |

## Tungsten in Bulb Filaments

Tungsten is used in electric bulb filaments because:

| Property | Importance |
|---|---|
| Very high melting point | It can become white hot without melting. |
| Can be drawn into thin wire | Useful for filament construction. |
| Emits light at high temperature | Converts part of heat into visible light. |

Bulbs are often filled with inactive gases like nitrogen and argon to reduce evaporation of tungsten and increase filament life.

## Electric Fuse

A fuse is a safety device connected in series with an appliance or circuit.

When current exceeds the safe value:

1. Fuse wire heats up.
2. Fuse wire melts.
3. Circuit breaks.
4. Current stops.
5. Appliance and wiring are protected.

Fuse ratings such as `1 A`, `2 A`, `5 A`, and `10 A` tell the maximum safe current.

## Choosing Fuse Rating

For a device of power `P` connected to voltage `V`:

$$
I = \frac{P}{V}
$$

For a `1000 W` iron connected to `220 V`:

$$
I = \frac{1000}{220}
$$

$$
I = 4.54 \ \text{A}
$$

So a `5 A` fuse is suitable.

# Unit 11I: Electric Power

## Meaning of Electric Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or converted into another form.

$$
P = \frac{E}{t}
$$

For an electric circuit:

$$
P = VI
$$

Using Ohm's law:

$$
P = I^2R
$$

and:

$$
P = \frac{V^2}{R}
$$

## Unit of Electric Power

The SI unit of power is watt.

One watt is the power consumed when a current of `1 A` flows through a device at a potential difference of `1 V`.

$$
1 \ \text{W} = 1 \ \text{V} \times 1 \ \text{A}
$$

## Larger Unit: Kilowatt

$$
1 \ \text{kW} = 1000 \ \text{W}
$$

Household appliances often have power ratings in watts or kilowatts.

| Appliance | Typical Meaning of Rating |
|---|---|
| `100 W` bulb | Consumes `100 J` of energy each second. |
| `1000 W` heater | Consumes `1000 J` of energy each second. |
| `400 W` refrigerator | Uses energy at a rate of `400 W` while operating. |

## Electrical Energy

Electrical energy consumed:

$$
E = Pt
$$

If `P` is in watt and `t` is in second, energy is in joule.

If `P` is in kilowatt and `t` is in hour, energy is in kilowatt hour.

## Commercial Unit of Electrical Energy

The commercial unit is kilowatt hour, commonly called one unit.

$$
1 \ \text{kW h} = 1000 \ \text{W} \times 3600 \ \text{s}
$$

$$
1 \ \text{kW h} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \ \text{J}
$$

## Important Clarification

Electrons are not consumed in a circuit. Electrical energy is consumed.

The electricity bill charges for energy supplied to make charges move and run appliances.

## Worked Example: Power of Bulb

**Question:** An electric bulb is connected to a `220 V` generator. Current is `0.50 A`. Find the power of the bulb.

Using:

$$
P = VI
$$

Substitute:

$$
P = 220 \times 0.50
$$

$$
P = 110 \ \text{W}
$$

**Answer:** Power of bulb is `110 W`.

## Worked Example: Cost of Refrigerator Energy

**Question:** A refrigerator rated `400 W` operates `8 hours/day`. Find the cost for `30 days` at `Rs 3.00 per kW h`.

Convert power:

$$
400 \ \text{W} = 0.4 \ \text{kW}
$$

Energy per day:

$$
E = 0.4 \times 8
$$

$$
E = 3.2 \ \text{kW h}
$$

Energy in 30 days:

$$
E = 3.2 \times 30
$$

$$
E = 96 \ \text{kW h}
$$

Cost:

$$
\text{Cost} = 96 \times 3
$$

$$
\text{Cost} = \text{Rs } 288
$$

**Answer:** Cost is `Rs 288`.

P = I^2R
$$

$$
P = 5^2 \times 44
$$

$$
P = 25 \times 44
$$

$$
P = 1100 \ \text{W}
$$

**Answer:** Heat is developed at `1100 W` or `1.1 kW`.

## Exercise 18: Explain the Following

### Part A: Why Is Tungsten Used for Filaments?

Tungsten has a very high melting point. It can become white hot and emit light without melting easily.

### Part B: Why Are Heating Device Conductors Made of Alloy?

Alloys have high resistivity, high melting point, and do not oxidise readily at high temperature. Therefore they can produce heat efficiently and last longer.

### Part C: Why Is Series Arrangement Not Used for Domestic Circuits?

Series arrangement is not used because:

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Same current | Appliances need different currents. |
| Voltage division | Appliances may not get full rated voltage. |
| One failure stops all | If one appliance fails, circuit breaks. |
| No independent control | Switching one affects the whole circuit. |

### Part D: How Does Resistance Vary with Area of Cross-Section?

Resistance is inversely proportional to area of cross-section.

$$
R \propto \frac{1}{A}
$$

If area increases, resistance decreases. If area becomes four times, resistance becomes one-fourth.

### Part E: Why Are Copper and Aluminium Wires Used for Transmission?

Copper and aluminium have low resistivity, so energy loss is less. They are also ductile and can be drawn into long wires.

# Unit 11M: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Thinking current is used up. | Current is same in a series circuit; energy is converted, not current. |
| Connecting ammeter in parallel. | Ammeter must be connected in series. |
| Connecting voltmeter in series. | Voltmeter must be connected in parallel. |
| Forgetting to convert minutes to seconds. | Use seconds when formula needs SI units. |
| Treating series and parallel formulae as interchangeable. | Series uses direct sum; parallel uses reciprocal sum. |
| Believing higher resistance always means higher heat. | It depends on what is constant: current, voltage, or time. |
| Using watt as unit of energy. | Watt is power; watt hour or joule is energy. |
| Forgetting that `kW h` is energy. | `1 kW h = 3.6 x 10^6 J`. |
| Using `V = I/R`. | Correct Ohm's law is `V = IR`. |
| Forgetting current is same in series. | In series, same current flows through all components. |
| Forgetting voltage is same in parallel. | In parallel, each branch has the same potential difference. |

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge. A closed circuit is required for current to flow. A cell or battery maintains potential difference, which does work on charges. The SI unit of current is ampere and the SI unit of potential difference is volt.

Ohm's law states that potential difference across a metallic conductor is directly proportional to current through it, provided temperature remains constant. Resistance opposes current and is measured in ohm. Resistance of a wire increases with length, decreases with area of cross-section, and depends on material through resistivity.

In series combination, the same current flows through all resistors and equivalent resistance is the sum of individual resistances. In parallel combination, the same voltage exists across each branch and the reciprocal of equivalent resistance is the sum of reciprocals of individual resistances.

When current passes through a resistance, heat is produced. Joule's law gives `H = I^2Rt`. This principle is used in heaters, irons, toasters, and fuses. Electric power is the rate of electrical energy consumption and can be calculated using `P = VI`, `P = I^2R`, or `P = V^2/R`. Domestic electrical energy is measured in kilowatt hour, where `1 kW h = 3.6 x 10^6 J`.
