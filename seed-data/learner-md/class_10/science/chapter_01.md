## Big Ideas

Chemical reactions are happening constantly: food cooking, milk souring, iron rusting, digestion, respiration, fermentation, and burning.

A chemical equation is a compact language for these changes. It must be balanced because atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Different reactions have different patterns. Once you recognise the pattern, you can predict and explain the reaction more clearly.

# Unit 1B: Chemical Equations

## Word Equation

Example: burning of magnesium.

```text
Magnesium + Oxygen -> Magnesium oxide
```

Reactants are written on the left side. Products are written on the right side. The arrow shows direction of reaction.

## Chemical Equation

Using formulae, the word equation becomes:

$$
Mg + O_2 \rightarrow MgO
$$

This is short, but it is not balanced.

## Balanced Form

$$
2Mg + O_2 \rightarrow 2MgO
$$

Now magnesium atoms and oxygen atoms are equal on both sides.

## Reactants and Products

| Side | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **LHS** | Reactants | `Mg`, `O2` |
| **RHS** | Products | `MgO` |

# Unit 1C: Balanced Chemical Equations

## Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.

Therefore, the number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides of a chemical equation.

## Balanced Example: Zinc and Sulphuric Acid

$$
Zn + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow ZnSO_4 + H_2
$$

Atom count:

| Element | Reactants | Products |
|---|---:|---:|
| Zn | 1 | 1 |
| H | 2 | 2 |
| S | 1 | 1 |
| O | 4 | 4 |

This equation is already balanced.

## Balancing Example: Iron and Steam

Skeletal equation:

$$
Fe + H_2O \rightarrow Fe_3O_4 + H_2
$$

Balanced equation:

$$
3Fe + 4H_2O \rightarrow Fe_3O_4 + 4H_2
$$

With state symbols:

$$
3Fe(s) + 4H_2O(g) \rightarrow Fe_3O_4(s) + 4H_2(g)
$$

## Hit-and-Trial Balancing Method

1. Write the correct formulae.
2. Count atoms of each element on both sides.
3. Start with the compound having the maximum number of atoms.
4. Balance one element at a time using coefficients.
5. Never change the formula inside a compound.
6. Use smallest whole-number coefficients.
7. Recount atoms to check.
8. Add state symbols if required.

## State Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `(s)` | Solid |
| `(l)` | Liquid |
| `(g)` | Gas |
| `(aq)` | Aqueous solution in water |

## Reaction Conditions

Conditions such as heat, light, pressure, catalyst, or chlorophyll can be written above or below the arrow.

Example: photosynthesis.

$$
6CO_2(aq) + 12H_2O(l) \xrightarrow[\text{chlorophyll}]{\text{sunlight}} C_6H_{12}O_6(aq) + 6O_2(aq) + 6H_2O(l)
$$

# Unit 1D: Combination Reactions

## Definition

A reaction in which two or more reactants combine to form a single product is called a combination reaction.

General form:

```text
A + B -> AB
```

## Quick Lime and Water

$$
CaO(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2(aq) + \text{Heat}
$$

Calcium oxide reacts with water to form calcium hydroxide, also called slaked lime.

## More Examples

Burning of coal:

$$
C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)
$$

Formation of water:

$$
2H_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2H_2O(l)
$$

Burning of magnesium:

$$
2Mg(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2MgO(s)
$$

## Exothermic Reactions

Reactions in which heat is released along with products are called exothermic reactions.

Examples:

- burning of natural gas;
- respiration;
- formation of slaked lime;
- decomposition of vegetable matter into compost.

Respiration:

$$
C_6H_{12}O_6(aq) + 6O_2(aq) \rightarrow 6CO_2(aq) + 6H_2O(l) + \text{energy}
$$

## Whitewashing

Slaked lime reacts with carbon dioxide in air to form calcium carbonate, which gives a shiny finish.

$$
Ca(OH)_2(aq) + CO_2(g) \rightarrow CaCO_3(s) + H_2O(l)
$$

# Unit 1E: Decomposition Reactions

## Definition

A reaction in which a single reactant breaks down into two or more simpler products is called a decomposition reaction.

General form:

```text
AB -> A + B
```

## Thermal Decomposition

Decomposition caused by heat is called thermal decomposition.

Ferrous sulphate:

$$
2FeSO_4(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} Fe_2O_3(s) + SO_2(g) + SO_3(g)
$$

Calcium carbonate:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Lead nitrate:

$$
2Pb(NO_3)_2(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} 2PbO(s) + 4NO_2(g) + O_2(g)
$$

Brown fumes in this reaction are nitrogen dioxide.

## Electrolytic Decomposition

Decomposition caused by electricity.

Electrolysis of water:

$$
2H_2O(l) \xrightarrow{\text{Electricity}} 2H_2(g) + O_2(g)
$$

Hydrogen gas collected is twice the volume of oxygen because water has hydrogen and oxygen in a 2:1 ratio.

## Photolytic Decomposition

Decomposition caused by light.

Silver chloride:

$$
2AgCl(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Sunlight}} 2Ag(s) + Cl_2(g)
$$

Silver bromide:

$$
2AgBr(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Sunlight}} 2Ag(s) + Br_2(g)
$$

These reactions are used in black-and-white photography.

## Endothermic Reactions

Reactions in which energy is absorbed are called endothermic reactions.

Decomposition reactions often need heat, light, or electricity.

# Unit 1F: Displacement Reactions

## Definition

A reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound is called a displacement reaction.

General form:

```text
A + BC -> AC + B
```

## Iron and Copper Sulphate

When iron nails are dipped in copper sulphate solution, the blue colour fades and iron nails become brownish.

$$
Fe(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow FeSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)
$$

Iron displaces copper because iron is more reactive than copper.

## More Examples

$$
Zn(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)
$$

$$
Pb(s) + CuCl_2(aq) \rightarrow PbCl_2(aq) + Cu(s)
$$

---

# Unit 1G: Double Displacement and Precipitation

## Double Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which two compounds exchange ions is called a double displacement reaction.

General form:

```text
AB + CD -> AD + CB
```

## Sodium Sulphate and Barium Chloride

$$
Na_2SO_4(aq) + BaCl_2(aq) \rightarrow BaSO_4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
$$

Barium sulphate is an insoluble white precipitate.

## Precipitation Reaction

Any reaction that produces an insoluble solid is called a precipitation reaction.

Lead nitrate and potassium iodide:

$$
Pb(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2KI(aq) \rightarrow PbI_2(s) + 2KNO_3(aq)
$$

Lead iodide is a yellow precipitate.

# Unit 1H: Oxidation and Reduction

## Oxidation

A substance is oxidised if it:

- gains oxygen; or
- loses hydrogen.

## Reduction

A substance is reduced if it:

- loses oxygen; or
- gains hydrogen.

## Copper to Copper Oxide

Heating copper powder in air forms black copper(II) oxide.

$$
2Cu + O_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} 2CuO
$$

Copper gains oxygen, so copper is oxidised.

## Copper Oxide and Hydrogen

$$
CuO + H_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} Cu + H_2O
$$

| Substance | Change | Result |
|---|---|---|
| `CuO` | loses oxygen | reduced to `Cu` |
| `H2` | gains oxygen | oxidised to `H2O` |

## More Redox Examples

$$
ZnO + C \rightarrow Zn + CO
$$

Carbon is oxidised to carbon monoxide. Zinc oxide is reduced to zinc.

$$
MnO_2 + 4HCl \rightarrow MnCl_2 + 2H_2O + Cl_2
$$

Hydrochloric acid is oxidised to chlorine. Manganese dioxide is reduced to manganese chloride.

# Unit 1I: Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

## Corrosion

When a metal is attacked by substances such as moisture, acids, or air, it corrodes. The process is called corrosion.

Examples:

| Metal | Corrosion Product / Appearance |
|---|---|
| Iron | Reddish-brown rust |
| Copper | Green coating |
| Silver | Black coating |

Corrosion damages bridges, cars, ships, railings, and iron structures.

## Rancidity

When fats and oils are oxidised, their smell and taste change. This is called rancidity.

Ways to slow rancidity:

- use airtight containers;
- add antioxidants;
- refrigerate food;
- flush packets with nitrogen;
- reduce exposure to air and light.

Nitrogen is used in chips packets because it is relatively unreactive and prevents oxidation of fats/oils.

## 9. Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Exothermic** | Releases heat. | `CaO + H2O -> Ca(OH)2 + heat` |
| **Endothermic** | Absorbs energy. | `CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2` on heating |

## 10. Why Respiration Is Exothermic

Respiration releases energy from glucose. Since energy is released, it is exothermic.

$$
C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{energy}
$$

## 11. Decomposition Opposite of Combination

Combination: two or more substances form one product.

$$
CaO + H_2O \rightarrow Ca(OH)_2
$$

Decomposition: one substance breaks into two or more products.

$$
CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2
$$

## 12. Decomposition by Heat, Light, Electricity

Heat:

$$
CaCO_3(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} CaO(s) + CO_2(g)
$$

Light:

$$
2AgCl(s) \xrightarrow{\text{Sunlight}} 2Ag(s) + Cl_2(g)
$$

Electricity:

$$
2H_2O(l) \xrightarrow{\text{Electricity}} 2H_2(g) + O_2(g)
$$

## 13. Displacement vs Double Displacement

| Feature | Displacement | Double Displacement |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern | One element replaces another. | Ions exchange between two compounds. |
| General form | `A + BC -> AC + B` | `AB + CD -> AD + CB` |
| Example | `Fe + CuSO4 -> FeSO4 + Cu` | `Na2SO4 + BaCl2 -> BaSO4 + 2NaCl` |

## 14. Recovery of Silver

$$
Cu(s) + 2AgNO_3(aq) \rightarrow Cu(NO_3)_2(aq) + 2Ag(s)
$$

Copper displaces silver from silver nitrate solution.

## 15. Precipitation Reaction

A precipitation reaction forms an insoluble solid.

$$
Na_2SO_4(aq) + BaCl_2(aq) \rightarrow BaSO_4(s) + 2NaCl(aq)
$$

`BaSO4` is the precipitate.

## 16. Oxidation and Reduction

Oxidation: gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen.

Examples:

$$
2Cu + O_2 \rightarrow 2CuO
$$

$$
C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2
$$

Reduction: loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen.

Examples:

$$
CuO + H_2 \rightarrow Cu + H_2O
$$

$$
ZnO + C \rightarrow Zn + CO
$$

## 17. Shiny Brown Element

Element `X` is copper. On heating in air, it forms black copper(II) oxide.

$$
2Cu + O_2 \rightarrow 2CuO
$$

## 18. Why Paint Iron Articles?

Paint prevents iron from coming in contact with air and moisture. This slows or prevents rusting.

## 19. Why Flush Oily Food with Nitrogen?

Nitrogen prevents oxidation of oils and fats. This slows rancidity and keeps food fresh longer.

## 20. Corrosion and Rancidity

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Corrosion** | Metal damage due to air, moisture, acids, etc. | Rusting of iron. |
| **Rancidity** | Spoilage of fats and oils by oxidation. | Old chips smell and taste bad. |

## Final Chapter Summary

- Chemical reactions form new substances.
- Chemical reactions can be observed through colour change, gas evolution, precipitate formation, temperature change, or state change.
- Chemical equations represent reactions symbolically.
- Equations must be balanced because mass is conserved.
- Combination reactions form one product from two or more reactants.
- Decomposition reactions break one reactant into simpler products.
- Exothermic reactions release heat; endothermic reactions absorb energy.
- Displacement reactions involve a more reactive element replacing a less reactive one.
- Double displacement reactions involve exchange of ions.
- Precipitation reactions form insoluble solids.
- Oxidation is gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen.
- Reduction is loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen.
- Corrosion and rancidity are everyday effects of oxidation.
