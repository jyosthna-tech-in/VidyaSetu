# Unit 13A: Environment and Ecosystem

## Meaning of Environment

Environment means the surroundings in which organisms live.

It includes:

| Component | Examples |
|---|---|
| Living things | Plants, animals, humans, bacteria, fungi. |
| Non-living things | Air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature, minerals. |
| Human-made surroundings | Houses, roads, farms, industries, waste systems. |

The environment is not only a background. It actively affects living organisms, and organisms also change it.

## Ecosystem

An ecosystem is formed when living organisms in an area interact with one another and with the non-living components around them.

Examples:

| Ecosystem | Type | Main Components |
|---|---|---|
| Forest | Natural | Trees, animals, soil, rainfall, microorganisms. |
| Pond | Natural | Aquatic plants, fish, insects, water, sunlight, dissolved oxygen. |
| Lake | Natural | Water, algae, fish, birds, decomposers, minerals. |
| Garden | Artificial or human-managed | Grass, flowers, insects, birds, soil, water. |
| Crop field | Artificial | Crop plants, weeds, insects, soil, irrigation, fertilisers. |
| Aquarium | Artificial | Fish, water, oxygen pump, aquatic plants, food, decomposers if present. |

## Biotic and Abiotic Components

Every ecosystem has two broad types of components.

| Component Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Biotic | Living components | Plants, animals, fungi, bacteria. |
| Abiotic | Non-living components | Air, water, soil, temperature, minerals, rainfall, wind, sunlight. |

The growth, reproduction, movement, and survival of organisms depend on both biotic and abiotic components.

## Aquarium as an Ecosystem

An aquarium is a useful example because it shows how a system needs balance.

For fish to survive, an aquarium needs:

| Need | Why It Is Required |
|---|---|
| Water | Medium in which fish live. |
| Space | Allows movement and reduces stress. |
| Oxygen | Needed for respiration. |
| Food | Provides energy and materials for growth. |
| Aquatic plants | Can release oxygen and provide shelter. |
| Decomposers | Help break down wastes and dead matter. |
| Cleaning | Prevents waste build-up if the system is not self-sustaining. |

Natural ponds and lakes do not need cleaning in the same way because they usually have larger, more balanced systems with producers, consumers, decomposers, and natural circulation.

# Unit 13B: Producers, Consumers, and Decomposers

## Producers

Producers are organisms that make organic food from inorganic substances.

Most producers use:

1. Sunlight.
2. Carbon dioxide.
3. Water.
4. Chlorophyll.

Green plants and some bacteria are producers.

They convert solar energy into chemical energy stored in food.

## Why Producers Are Important

Producers are the entry point of energy into most ecosystems.

Without producers:

1. Herbivores would have no plant food.
2. Carnivores would lose prey.
3. Decomposers would eventually lose dead organic matter.
4. The ecosystem would collapse.

## Consumers

Consumers cannot make their own food from inorganic materials.

They depend directly or indirectly on producers.

| Consumer Type | Food Source | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Herbivore | Plants | Deer, goat, rabbit, grasshopper. |
| Carnivore | Animals | Lion, snake, frog, eagle. |
| Omnivore | Plants and animals | Human, crow, bear. |
| Parasite | Host organism | Tapeworm, lice, Cuscuta. |

## Decomposers

Decomposers break down dead organisms and waste materials.

Important decomposers include:

1. Bacteria.
2. Fungi.

They convert complex organic substances into simpler inorganic substances that return to soil and water.

## Role of Decomposers

| Role | Importance |
|---|---|
| Break down dead bodies | Prevents accumulation of dead plants and animals. |
| Break down wastes | Helps clean the environment naturally. |
| Recycle nutrients | Returns minerals to soil for plants. |
| Maintain soil fertility | Supports growth of producers. |
| Complete material cycles | Keeps ecosystem functioning. |

If decomposers were absent, dead organisms and wastes would accumulate and nutrients would not return to soil efficiently.

# Unit 13C: Food Chains and Trophic Levels

## Food Chain

A food chain is a series of organisms in which each organism is eaten by the next organism.

Example:

`Grass -> Grasshopper -> Frog -> Snake -> Eagle`

This means:

1. Grass makes food.
2. Grasshopper eats grass.
3. Frog eats grasshopper.
4. Snake eats frog.
5. Eagle eats snake.

## Trophic Level

Each step in a food chain is called a trophic level.

| Trophic Level | Organism Type | Example in Chain |
|---|---|---|
| First trophic level | Producer | Grass |
| Second trophic level | Primary consumer or herbivore | Grasshopper |
| Third trophic level | Secondary consumer | Frog |
| Fourth trophic level | Tertiary consumer | Snake |
| Fifth trophic level | Top consumer | Eagle |

## More Examples of Food Chains

| Ecosystem | Food Chain |
|---|---|
| Grassland | Grass -> Deer -> Tiger |
| Pond | Phytoplankton -> Zooplankton -> Small fish -> Large fish |
| Crop field | Wheat -> Rat -> Snake -> Eagle |
| Forest | Leaves -> Caterpillar -> Bird -> Hawk |

## Why Producers Form the First Trophic Level

Producers capture solar energy and convert it into food.

All other trophic levels depend on this food directly or indirectly.

Therefore producers always form the first trophic level.

# Unit 13D: Energy Flow and Food Webs

## Energy Flow in Ecosystem

Energy enters most ecosystems through sunlight.

Green plants capture only about `1 percent` of the sunlight falling on their leaves and convert it into food energy.

This energy then moves through food chains.

## The 10 Percent Transfer

When one trophic level is eaten by the next, only about `10 percent` of the available energy is transferred to the next level.

The rest is lost mainly as:

1. Heat.
2. Energy used in movement.
3. Energy used in respiration.
4. Energy used in digestion.
5. Waste materials.

## Energy Transfer Example

If producers have `10000 units` of energy:

| Trophic Level | Organism Type | Energy Available |
|---|---|---:|
| First | Producers | 10000 units |
| Second | Herbivores | 1000 units |
| Third | Secondary consumers | 100 units |
| Fourth | Tertiary consumers | 10 units |
| Fifth | Top consumers | 1 unit |

This explains why long food chains are rare.

After three or four transfers, very little usable energy remains.

## Unidirectional Flow of Energy

Energy flow in an ecosystem is unidirectional.

This means energy flows in one direction:

`Sun -> Producers -> Consumers -> Decomposers/Environment`

Energy does not flow back from herbivores to plants or from plants to the Sun.

## Food Web

In nature, organisms usually do not belong to only one simple food chain.

One organism may be eaten by more than one organism, and one consumer may eat many kinds of food.

A food web is a network of interconnected food chains.

Example:

| Organism | Can Be Eaten By |
|---|---|
| Grass | Grasshopper, goat, rabbit. |
| Grasshopper | Frog, bird. |
| Frog | Snake, bird. |
| Snake | Eagle. |
| Rabbit | Fox, eagle. |

Food webs make ecosystems more stable than single food chains because organisms may have alternative food sources.

# Unit 13E: Biological Magnification

## Entry of Harmful Chemicals into Food Chains

Pesticides and other chemicals used in agriculture can enter soil and water.

They may be:

1. Washed into water bodies.
2. Absorbed by plants with water and minerals.
3. Taken up by aquatic plants and animals.
4. Passed along food chains.

## Non-Biodegradable Chemicals

Some chemicals do not break down easily.

They remain in organisms and accumulate in tissues.

When one organism eats another, these chemicals move to the next trophic level.

## Biological Magnification

Biological magnification is the progressive increase in concentration of non-biodegradable harmful chemicals at successive trophic levels.

It is strongest in top consumers.

## Why Humans Are at Risk

Humans often occupy top positions in many food chains.

For example:

`Pesticides in water -> Aquatic plants -> Fish -> Humans`

or:

`Pesticides in soil -> Crops -> Humans`

Since humans eat grains, vegetables, fruits, meat, fish, and milk products, pesticide residues can enter the human body through many routes.

## Important Point

Washing food may remove some chemicals from the surface, but it cannot always remove chemicals already absorbed into tissues of plants or animals.

# Unit 13F: Human Activities and Environment

## Humans as Part of the Environment

Human beings are not separate from the environment.

Changes in the environment affect us, and our activities change the environment.

Examples:

| Human Activity | Environmental Effect |
|---|---|
| Excess pesticide use | Soil and water contamination, biological magnification. |
| Burning fossil fuels | Air pollution and greenhouse gas increase. |
| Cutting forests | Habitat loss, soil erosion, biodiversity loss. |
| Throwing plastic waste | Soil and water pollution, animal injury. |
| Untreated sewage | Water pollution and disease spread. |
| Industrial waste discharge | Toxic pollution of soil and water. |

## Two Major Problems Discussed in This Chapter

1. Ozone layer depletion.
2. Waste disposal.

Both show how human activities can disturb natural systems.

# Unit 13G: Ozone Layer and Depletion

## Oxygen and Ozone

Normal oxygen gas is `O2`.

Ozone is `O3`, a molecule made of three oxygen atoms.

At ground level, ozone can be harmful and poisonous.

But high in the atmosphere, ozone is extremely useful.

## Function of Ozone Layer

The ozone layer protects life on Earth by absorbing harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun.

Excess ultraviolet radiation can:

| Effect | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Cause skin cancer | UV can damage DNA in skin cells. |
| Damage eyes | UV exposure can contribute to eye problems. |
| Harm plants | Photosynthesis and growth can be affected. |
| Harm aquatic life | Plankton and early life stages of organisms are sensitive. |
| Disturb ecosystems | Damage to producers affects food chains. |

## Formation of Ozone

High-energy ultraviolet radiation splits oxygen molecules into free oxygen atoms.

The free oxygen atoms combine with oxygen molecules to form ozone.

In words:

`Oxygen molecule -> oxygen atoms`

`oxygen atom + oxygen molecule -> ozone`

## Ozone Depletion

The amount of ozone in the atmosphere began to fall sharply in the 1980s.

This was linked to synthetic chemicals called chlorofluorocarbons, or CFCs.

CFCs were used in:

1. Refrigerators.
2. Air conditioners.
3. Fire extinguishers.
4. Aerosol sprays in older systems.

## Control of CFCs

International action was taken to reduce CFC production and use.

The raw chapter notes that in 1987, the United Nations Environment Programme helped create an agreement to freeze CFC production at 1986 levels.

Today, manufacturers are required to make CFC-free refrigerators and cooling systems.

## Why Ozone Damage Is a Serious Issue

Ozone depletion is not local only.

The atmosphere is shared globally, and UV radiation affects humans, animals, plants, and aquatic ecosystems.

So ozone layer protection needs international cooperation.

# Unit 13H: Managing the Garbage We Produce

## Waste in Daily Life

Daily waste may include:

| Waste Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Kitchen waste | Vegetable peels, spoilt food, tea leaves. |
| Paper waste | Newspapers, cartons, notebooks. |
| Plastic waste | Packets, wrappers, bottles, packaging. |
| Textile waste | Old clothes, torn fabric. |
| Medical packaging | Strips, bottles, bubble packs. |
| E-waste | Old phones, batteries, wires, circuit boards. |

## Biodegradable Waste

Biodegradable substances can be broken down by biological processes.

Examples:

1. Vegetable peels.
2. Fruit peels.
3. Paper.
4. Cotton cloth.
5. Food waste.
6. Leaves.
7. Wood.
8. Leather.

These substances are acted on by decomposers such as bacteria and fungi.

## Non-Biodegradable Waste

Non-biodegradable substances do not break down easily by biological processes.

Examples:

1. Plastic bags.
2. Synthetic fibres.
3. Glass.
4. Metal cans.
5. Some chemical containers.
6. Electronic waste.

These can persist in the environment for a long time.

## Why Some Substances Are Non-Biodegradable

Biological breakdown depends on enzymes made by organisms.

Enzymes are specific. They break down particular kinds of substances.

Many human-made materials, such as plastics, have structures that common decomposer enzymes cannot break down easily.

Therefore they persist for a long time.

## Effects of Biodegradable Waste

Biodegradable waste is not always harmless.

If produced in excess or dumped carelessly, it can:

| Effect | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Produce foul smell | Rotting organic matter releases unpleasant gases. |
| Attract pests | Flies, rats, and stray animals may gather. |
| Spread disease | Pathogens may multiply in waste. |
| Reduce oxygen in water | Decomposition in water uses dissolved oxygen. |
| Release methane | Anaerobic decomposition can produce methane. |

But if managed properly, biodegradable waste can be composted and returned to soil.

## Effects of Non-Biodegradable Waste

Non-biodegradable waste can:

| Effect | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Persist for years | It does not decompose easily. |
| Block drains | Plastic and packaging can clog drainage. |
| Harm animals | Animals may eat plastic or get trapped. |
| Pollute soil and water | Chemicals can leach into surroundings. |
| Cause biological magnification | Some toxic chemicals accumulate in food chains. |
| Occupy landfills | Waste volume remains for long periods. |

## Waste Segregation

Waste should be separated at source.

| Waste Category | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Wet biodegradable waste | Composting or biogas production. |
| Dry recyclable waste | Recycling of paper, metal, glass, some plastics. |
| Hazardous waste | Safe collection and special treatment. |
| E-waste | Authorised recycling and recovery. |
| Biomedical waste | Separate safe disposal. |

## Reducing Waste Disposal Problem

Useful methods include:

1. Reduce unnecessary consumption.
2. Reuse containers, bags, and materials.
3. Recycle paper, metal, glass, and suitable plastics.
4. Refuse single-use plastic when possible.
5. Compost kitchen waste.
6. Carry cloth bags while shopping.
7. Repair items instead of discarding them quickly.
8. Segregate waste into wet and dry categories.
9. Send e-waste to authorised collection centres.

## Disposable Cups Example

Different disposable cup materials have different environmental costs.

| Cup Type | Advantage | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| Plastic cup | Cheap, waterproof | Non-biodegradable and persistent. |
| Clay kulhad | Biodegradable and traditional | Large-scale production can remove fertile topsoil. |
| Paper cup | Often more biodegradable than plastic | Uses paper resources and may have coating. |

The best solution is not only changing material but also reducing unnecessary disposables.

# Unit 13I: Solved In-Text Questions

## Question 1: What Are Trophic Levels? Give an Example.

Trophic levels are the feeding levels in a food chain.

Example:

`Grass -> Grasshopper -> Frog -> Snake`

| Trophic Level | Organism | Role |
|---|---|---|
| First | Grass | Producer |
| Second | Grasshopper | Primary consumer |
| Third | Frog | Secondary consumer |
| Fourth | Snake | Tertiary consumer |

## Question 2: What Is the Role of Decomposers in the Ecosystem?

Decomposers break down dead plants, dead animals, and organic wastes.

They convert complex organic substances into simple inorganic substances that return to soil and water.

Their roles are:

1. Cleaning dead remains and wastes.
2. Recycling nutrients.
3. Maintaining soil fertility.
4. Supporting producers.
5. Completing material cycles in nature.

## Question 3: Why Are Some Substances Biodegradable and Some Non-Biodegradable?

Some substances are biodegradable because decomposers have enzymes that can break them down.

Some substances are non-biodegradable because decomposers do not have suitable enzymes to break them down under normal environmental conditions.

Natural organic materials are generally biodegradable. Many synthetic materials such as plastics are usually non-biodegradable.

## Question 4: Give Two Ways in Which Biodegradable Substances Affect the Environment.

Two effects:

1. They are decomposed and recycle nutrients back into soil.
2. If accumulated in large amounts, they may produce foul smell, attract pests, and spread disease.

## Question 5: Give Two Ways in Which Non-Biodegradable Substances Affect the Environment.

Two effects:

1. They persist for a long time and pollute soil and water.
2. Toxic non-biodegradable chemicals may enter food chains and cause biological magnification.

## Question 6: What Is Ozone and How Does It Affect Any Ecosystem?

Ozone is a molecule made of three oxygen atoms, written as `O3`.

At higher levels of the atmosphere, ozone forms a protective layer that absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun.

This protects organisms from UV damage.

If the ozone layer is depleted, more UV radiation reaches Earth, harming plants, animals, microorganisms, aquatic life, and humans.

## Question 7: How Can You Help in Reducing the Problem of Waste Disposal? Give Any Two Methods.

Two methods:

1. Separate biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste at home so that composting and recycling become easier.
2. Reduce use of single-use items by carrying cloth bags, reusing containers, and avoiding unnecessary packaging.

Other valid methods include composting kitchen waste, recycling paper and metal, and sending e-waste to authorised recyclers.

# Unit 13J: Solved Exercise Questions

## Exercise 1: Groups Containing Only Biodegradable Items

Options:

| Option | Items | Only Biodegradable? |
|---|---|---|
| (a) | Grass, flowers, leather | Yes |
| (b) | Grass, wood, plastic | No, plastic is non-biodegradable |
| (c) | Fruit peels, cake, lime juice | Yes |
| (d) | Cake, wood, grass | Yes |

**Answer:** `(a)`, `(c)`, and `(d)`.

## Exercise 2: Which Constitutes a Food Chain?

A food chain must show a feeding relationship from producer to consumer.

| Option | Correct? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| (a) Grass, wheat, mango | No | All are producers. |
| (b) Grass, goat, human | Yes | Grass is eaten by goat, goat products/meat may be consumed by humans. |
| (c) Goat, cow, elephant | No | These are mostly herbivores; not a feeding chain. |
| (d) Grass, fish, goat | No | This is not a natural feeding sequence. |

**Answer:** `(b) Grass, goat and human`.

## Exercise 3: Environment-Friendly Practices

All three practices are environment-friendly:

1. Carrying cloth bags reduces plastic use.
2. Switching off unnecessary lights and fans saves energy.
3. Walking to school reduces fuel use and pollution.

**Answer:** `(d) All of the above`.

## Exercise 4: What Happens If We Kill All Organisms in One Trophic Level?

The food chain becomes disturbed.

Effects:

1. Organisms at the next higher trophic level may lose food and decline.
2. Organisms at the lower trophic level may increase because their consumers are removed.
3. Energy flow is disrupted.
4. Food web balance is disturbed.
5. The ecosystem may become unstable.

Example:

If all frogs are removed from `grass -> grasshopper -> frog -> snake`, grasshopper numbers may increase and snakes may decline.

## Exercise 5: Is the Impact Different for Different Trophic Levels?

Yes, the impact differs for different trophic levels.

Removing producers is usually the most damaging because producers are the base of the ecosystem.

Removing herbivores, carnivores, or decomposers also causes imbalance, but the exact effect depends on the food web.

No trophic level can be removed without causing damage because all levels are interconnected.

## Exercise 6: What Is Biological Magnification?

Biological magnification is the increase in concentration of non-biodegradable harmful chemicals at successive trophic levels in a food chain.

The concentration is different at different trophic levels.

It is lowest in producers and highest in top consumers.

Example:

`Pesticide in water -> aquatic plants -> small fish -> large fish -> humans`

Humans or top carnivores receive the highest concentration.

## Exercise 7: Problems Caused by Non-Biodegradable Wastes

Problems include:

1. They persist in the environment for long periods.
2. They occupy landfills and open spaces.
3. They may block drains and cause waterlogging.
4. Animals may eat plastic and die.
5. Toxic substances may pollute soil and water.
6. Some chemicals enter food chains and cause biological magnification.
7. Burning them may release poisonous gases.

## Exercise 8: If All Waste Is Biodegradable, Will It Have No Impact?

No.

Even biodegradable waste can harm the environment if generated in large amounts or dumped carelessly.

Possible impacts:

1. Foul smell.
2. Growth of disease-causing organisms.
3. Attraction of flies, rats, and stray animals.
4. Oxygen depletion in water bodies during decomposition.
5. Methane production under anaerobic conditions.

However, biodegradable waste can be useful if managed through composting or biogas production.

## Exercise 9: Why Is Damage to Ozone Layer a Cause for Concern?

Damage to the ozone layer allows more ultraviolet radiation to reach Earth's surface.

This can:

1. Cause skin cancer.
2. Damage eyes.
3. Reduce plant growth.
4. Harm plankton and aquatic ecosystems.
5. Disturb food chains and ecosystems.

Steps taken to limit damage:

1. Reduction and control of CFC production.
2. International agreements to restrict ozone-depleting substances.
3. Use of CFC-free refrigerators and cooling systems.
4. Safer alternatives in aerosols, fire extinguishers, and refrigeration.
5. Public awareness and regulation of ozone-depleting chemicals.

# Unit 13L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Calling only animals biotic components. | Plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, and humans are all biotic. |
| Thinking producers are most important only because they are many. | Producers are essential because they introduce food energy into ecosystems. |
| Treating food chains as isolated in nature. | Most food chains are interconnected as food webs. |
| Assuming all energy passes to next level. | Only about 10 percent passes to next trophic level. |
| Saying energy cycles in ecosystem. | Materials cycle, but energy flows in one direction and is lost as heat. |
| Thinking biodegradable waste has no impact. | Excess biodegradable waste can still pollute and spread disease. |
| Thinking plastic disappears when broken into pieces. | It may become smaller but can still persist and harm organisms. |
| Saying ozone is always good. | Ozone high in atmosphere is protective; ground-level ozone can be harmful. |
| Thinking washing removes all pesticides. | Some chemicals may be absorbed inside tissues. |
| Calling recycling the only solution. | Reduce and reuse are often better before recycling. |

## One-Page Chapter Summary

The environment includes living and non-living surroundings. An ecosystem consists of organisms interacting with each other and with abiotic components. Producers make food using sunlight, consumers depend on producers directly or indirectly, and decomposers recycle nutrients by breaking down dead matter and wastes.

Food chains show feeding relationships, and each step is a trophic level. Energy flows from the Sun to producers and then to consumers. Only about 1 percent of sunlight is captured by plants, and only about 10 percent of energy passes from one trophic level to the next. Therefore food chains are usually short. In nature, food chains connect to form food webs.

Non-biodegradable chemicals such as some pesticides can accumulate in organisms and increase at higher trophic levels. This is biological magnification. Human activities also damage the environment through waste generation and ozone layer depletion. Ozone in the upper atmosphere protects life from harmful UV radiation, but CFCs have damaged the ozone layer. Waste must be reduced, segregated, reused, recycled, composted, and safely treated to protect ecosystems.
