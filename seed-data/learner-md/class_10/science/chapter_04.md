## Big Ideas

Carbon is central to living systems and to many materials we use daily. Food, clothes, paper, medicines, fuels, plastics, oils, soaps, and many household substances contain carbon compounds.

Carbon forms covalent bonds because gaining or losing four electrons is difficult. Sharing electrons allows carbon to complete its outer shell.

Carbon forms millions of compounds mainly because:

- it is tetravalent;
- it shows catenation strongly;
- carbon-carbon bonds are strong;
- carbon also bonds strongly with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur, chlorine, and other elements.

The chemical behaviour of an organic compound is strongly controlled by its functional group.

Soap cleans because each soap molecule has two different ends: one attaches to water and one attaches to oil/grease. This dual nature allows oily dirt to be lifted into water.

# Unit 4B: Covalent Bonding

## Why Carbon Does Not Normally Form `C4+` or `C4-`

Carbon has atomic number 6.

Electronic configuration:

```text
Carbon: 2,4
```

Carbon has four valence electrons.

To achieve a noble gas configuration, carbon would need either to gain four electrons or lose four electrons.

## Why Gaining Four Electrons Is Difficult

If carbon gained four electrons, it would become:

```text
C4-
```

But the nucleus has only six protons. Holding ten electrons strongly would be difficult.

## Why Losing Four Electrons Is Difficult

If carbon lost four electrons, it would become:

```text
C4+
```

But removing four electrons needs a very large amount of energy.

## Carbon's Solution: Sharing

Carbon shares electrons with other atoms. This forms covalent bonds.

In a covalent bond, shared electrons are counted in the outer shells of both bonded atoms.

## Covalent Compounds: General Properties

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Low melting/boiling points | Intermolecular forces are usually weak. |
| Poor electrical conductivity | No free ions are formed by bonding. |
| Molecular nature | Many covalent compounds exist as molecules. |
| Strong bonds inside molecule | Atoms are strongly joined by shared electron pairs. |

# Unit 4C: Simple Covalent Molecules

## Hydrogen Molecule, `H2`

Hydrogen has one electron and needs one more to complete its first shell.

Two hydrogen atoms share one pair of electrons.

$$
H + H \rightarrow H_2
$$

Structural representation:

```text
H-H
```

This is a single covalent bond.

## Chlorine Molecule, `Cl2`

Chlorine has electronic configuration:

```text
2,8,7
```

Each chlorine atom needs one electron to complete its octet. Two chlorine atoms share one pair.

```text
Cl-Cl
```

This is also a single covalent bond.

## Oxygen Molecule, `O2`

Oxygen has electronic configuration:

```text
2,6
```

Each oxygen atom needs two electrons to complete its octet. Two oxygen atoms share two pairs.

```text
O=O
```

This is a double covalent bond.

## Nitrogen Molecule, `N2`

Nitrogen has electronic configuration:

```text
2,5
```

Each nitrogen atom needs three electrons to complete its octet. Two nitrogen atoms share three pairs.

```text
N#N
```

This is a triple covalent bond.

In many textbooks, the triple bond is shown as three lines between nitrogen atoms.

## Water Molecule, `H2O`

Oxygen forms two single bonds with two hydrogen atoms.

```text
H-O-H
```

The molecule has single covalent bonds.

## Ammonia Molecule, `NH3`

Nitrogen forms three single bonds with hydrogen atoms.

```text
   H
   |
H-N-H
```

Nitrogen completes its octet by sharing one electron with each hydrogen.

# Unit 4D: Methane and Carbon Tetravalency

## Methane Formula

Methane has the formula:

```text
CH4
```

Carbon has valency 4. Hydrogen has valency 1.

Carbon shares one electron each with four hydrogen atoms.

## Structure of Methane

```text
    H
    |
H - C - H
    |
    H
```

All four bonds are single covalent bonds.

## Why Methane Is Important

Methane is:

- one of the simplest carbon compounds;
- a major component of natural gas;
- a major component of biogas;
- present in CNG fuel mixtures.

# Unit 4E: Allotropes of Carbon

## Meaning of Allotropy

Allotropes are different physical forms of the same element.

Carbon has several allotropes with very different physical properties.

## Diamond

In diamond, each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms.

This creates a rigid three-dimensional network.

Properties:

- hardest natural substance;
- very high melting point;
- does not conduct electricity;
- used in cutting tools and jewellery.

## Graphite

In graphite, each carbon atom is bonded to three other carbon atoms in the same plane.

The atoms form hexagonal layers. These layers can slide over one another.

Properties:

- smooth and slippery;
- conducts electricity;
- used in pencil leads, lubricants, and electrodes.

## Fullerenes

Fullerenes are carbon allotropes with cage-like structures.

The first identified fullerene was `C60`, shaped like a football.

It is called buckminsterfullerene because its structure resembles geodesic domes designed by Buckminster Fuller.

## Allotrope Comparison

| Allotrope | Bonding Arrangement | Important Property |
|---|---|---|
| Diamond | Each C bonded to 4 C atoms in 3D | Extremely hard |
| Graphite | Each C bonded to 3 C atoms in layers | Conducts electricity and is slippery |
| Fullerene | Cage-like carbon structure | Molecular form of carbon |

# Unit 4F: Versatile Nature of Carbon

## Why Carbon Forms So Many Compounds

Carbon forms a huge number of compounds because of:

- catenation;
- tetravalency;
- strong carbon-carbon bonds;
- ability to bond with many other elements.

## Catenation

Catenation is the ability of carbon to bond with itself.

Carbon atoms can form:

- long chains;
- branched chains;
- rings;
- single bonds;
- double bonds;
- triple bonds.

## Why Carbon Shows Strong Catenation

Carbon-carbon bonds are strong and stable because carbon atoms are small.

The small size lets the nucleus hold shared electron pairs strongly.

## Tetravalency

Carbon has valency 4.

It can form four covalent bonds with:

- carbon;
- hydrogen;
- oxygen;
- nitrogen;
- sulphur;
- chlorine;
- bromine;
- many other elements.

## Organic Compounds

Carbon compounds were once believed to be formed only by living systems.

This idea was disproved when Friedrich Wohler prepared urea from ammonium cyanate in 1828.

Today, organic chemistry studies most carbon compounds, except compounds such as:

- carbides;
- oxides of carbon;
- carbonates;
- hydrogencarbonates.

# Unit 4G: Saturated and Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

## Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen.

Examples:

- methane, `CH4`;
- ethane, `C2H6`;
- ethene, `C2H4`;
- ethyne, `C2H2`;
- benzene, `C6H6`.

## Saturated Compounds

Saturated carbon compounds have only single bonds between carbon atoms.

Example: ethane.

```text
H   H
|   |
H-C-C-H
|   |
H   H
```

Condensed formula:

```text
CH3-CH3
```

Molecular formula:

```text
C2H6
```

Saturated hydrocarbons are called alkanes.

## Unsaturated Compounds

Unsaturated compounds contain at least one carbon-carbon double or triple bond.

## Ethene

Ethene has a double bond.

```text
CH2=CH2
```

Molecular formula:

```text
C2H4
```

Ethene is an alkene.

## Ethyne

Ethyne has a triple bond.

```text
HC#CH
```

Molecular formula:

```text
C2H2
```

Ethyne is an alkyne.

## Saturated vs Unsaturated

| Feature | Saturated | Unsaturated |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon-carbon bond | Single only | At least one double or triple bond |
| Reactivity | Less reactive | More reactive |
| Hydrocarbon class | Alkane | Alkene or alkyne |
| Example | Ethane, `C2H6` | Ethene, `C2H4`; ethyne, `C2H2` |

# Unit 4I: Chains, Branches, Rings, and Isomerism

## Straight Chains

Carbon atoms can form straight chains.

Example: normal butane.

```text
CH3-CH2-CH2-CH3
```

## Branched Chains

The same number of carbon atoms can also form branched structures.

Example: isobutane.

```text
    CH3
     |
CH3-C-H
     |
    CH3
```

Both normal butane and isobutane have the same molecular formula:

```text
C4H10
```

But their structures differ.

## Structural Isomers

Structural isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements.

Example:

| Formula | Isomers |
|---|---|
| `C4H10` | Normal butane and isobutane |
| `C5H12` | Three structural isomers of pentane |

## Rings

Carbon atoms can form rings.

Example: cyclohexane.

```text
C6H12
```

Cyclohexane is a saturated ring compound.

## Benzene

Benzene has the formula:

```text
C6H6
```

It is a ring compound with alternating double-bond character in school-level structural representation.

## Hydrocarbon Structure Types

| Type | Example | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| Straight chain | Butane | `C4H10` |
| Branched chain | Isobutane | `C4H10` |
| Saturated ring | Cyclohexane | `C6H12` |
| Aromatic ring | Benzene | `C6H6` |

# Unit 4J: Functional Groups

## What Is a Functional Group?

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms that gives a carbon compound its characteristic chemical properties.

In a hydrocarbon, one or more hydrogen atoms can be replaced by heteroatoms or groups.

## Heteroatoms

Common heteroatoms include:

- chlorine;
- bromine;
- oxygen;
- nitrogen;
- sulphur.

## Important Functional Groups

| Class | Functional Group | Example Name |
|---|---|---|
| Haloalkane | `-Cl`, `-Br` | Chloropropane, bromopropane |
| Alcohol | `-OH` | Ethanol |
| Aldehyde | `-CHO` | Ethanal |
| Ketone | `>C=O` inside chain | Propanone |
| Carboxylic acid | `-COOH` | Ethanoic acid |

## Why Functional Groups Matter

The functional group decides many chemical properties.

For example:

- `CH3OH`, `C2H5OH`, `C3H7OH`, and `C4H9OH` all behave like alcohols because they contain `-OH`;
- `HCOOH`, `CH3COOH`, and `C2H5COOH` all behave like carboxylic acids because they contain `-COOH`.

# Unit 4K: Homologous Series

## Definition

A homologous series is a family of organic compounds with:

- same functional group;
- same general formula;
- similar chemical properties;
- successive members differing by `-CH2-`;
- gradual change in physical properties.

## Example: Alkanes

| Compound | Formula |
|---|---|
| Methane | `CH4` |
| Ethane | `C2H6` |
| Propane | `C3H8` |
| Butane | `C4H10` |

Each successive member differs by:

```text
-CH2-
```

Molecular mass difference:

$$
12 + 2(1) = 14\ u
$$

## Example: Alcohols

| Alcohol | Formula |
|---|---|
| Methanol | `CH3OH` |
| Ethanol | `C2H5OH` |
| Propanol | `C3H7OH` |
| Butanol | `C4H9OH` |

All contain the `-OH` functional group, so they show similar chemical properties.

## Physical Properties in a Homologous Series

As molecular mass increases:

- melting point usually changes gradually;
- boiling point usually increases;
- solubility may change gradually;
- density and other physical properties may show gradation.

Chemical properties remain similar because the functional group remains the same.

# Unit 4M: Combustion

## Meaning

Combustion is burning in oxygen with release of heat and light.

Carbon and most carbon compounds burn in oxygen to form carbon dioxide and water.

## Carbon Combustion

$$
C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + Heat + Light
$$

## Methane Combustion

Unbalanced form:

$$
CH_4 + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O
$$

Balanced form:

$$
CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O + Heat + Light
$$

## Ethanol Combustion

Unbalanced form:

$$
CH_3CH_2OH + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + H_2O
$$

Balanced form:

$$
CH_3CH_2OH + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2CO_2 + 3H_2O + Heat + Light
$$

## Clean and Sooty Flames

| Flame Type | Cause | Observation |
|---|---|---|
| Clean blue flame | Complete combustion with enough oxygen | Less soot, efficient burning |
| Yellow sooty flame | Incomplete combustion due to limited oxygen or unsaturated compound | Black soot deposit |

## Saturated vs Unsaturated Flame

| Compound Type | Flame |
|---|---|
| Saturated hydrocarbons | Usually clean flame if oxygen is sufficient |
| Unsaturated hydrocarbons | Yellow flame with black smoke/soot |

## Blackened Cooking Vessels

If the bottom of a cooking vessel becomes black, the fuel is not burning completely.

Usually this means:

- air holes are blocked;
- oxygen supply is insufficient;
- fuel is being wasted;
- soot is depositing on the vessel.

## Pollutants from Coal and Petroleum

Coal and petroleum may contain nitrogen and sulphur.

On combustion, they can form:

- oxides of nitrogen;
- oxides of sulphur.

These are major environmental pollutants.

## Why Coal and Petroleum Are Fossil Fuels

Coal and petroleum formed from ancient biomass buried under earth layers for millions of years.

Because they come from remains of ancient plants and animals, they are called fossil fuels.

# Unit 4N: Oxidation of Carbon Compounds

## Combustion Is Complete Oxidation

When carbon compounds burn completely, carbon is oxidised to carbon dioxide.

## Oxidation of Alcohols

Alcohols can be oxidised to carboxylic acids.

Example: ethanol to ethanoic acid.

$$
CH_3CH_2OH + 2[O] \rightarrow CH_3COOH + H_2O
$$

Here `[O]` represents oxygen supplied by an oxidising agent.

## Oxidising Agents

Oxidising agents are substances that add oxygen to another substance or remove hydrogen from it.

Common oxidising agents in this chapter:

- alkaline potassium permanganate, `KMnO4`;
- acidified potassium dichromate, `K2Cr2O7`.

## Potassium Permanganate Observation

When alkaline potassium permanganate is added drop by drop to warm ethanol:

- initially, its colour disappears because it oxidises ethanol;
- after all ethanol is oxidised, excess permanganate is no longer consumed;
- then the colour persists.

# Unit 4O: Addition Reaction

## Meaning

An addition reaction occurs when atoms add across a carbon-carbon double or triple bond.

Unsaturated hydrocarbons undergo addition reactions.

## Hydrogenation

Hydrogenation is addition of hydrogen to an unsaturated compound in the presence of a catalyst such as nickel, palladium, or platinum.

Example:

$$
CH_2=CH_2 + H_2 \xrightarrow{Ni} CH_3-CH_3
$$

Ethene becomes ethane.

## Hydrogenation of Vegetable Oils

Vegetable oils usually contain long unsaturated carbon chains.

On hydrogenation, they become more saturated and more solid.

This process is used in the manufacture of vanaspati ghee.

## Health Note

Animal fats generally contain more saturated fatty acids. Oils with unsaturated fatty acids are often preferred for cooking.

# Unit 4P: Substitution Reaction

## Meaning

A substitution reaction is a reaction in which one atom or group replaces another atom or group in a molecule.

## Methane with Chlorine

In sunlight, chlorine replaces hydrogen atoms in methane.

$$
CH_4 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{Sunlight} CH_3Cl + HCl
$$

The product shown is chloromethane.

## Why This Is Substitution

One hydrogen atom of methane is substituted by one chlorine atom.

In higher alkanes, many products may form because chlorine can replace hydrogen atoms at different positions.

# Unit 4Q: Ethanol

## Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| Common name | Alcohol |
| Chemical name | Ethanol |
| Formula | `CH3CH2OH` or `C2H5OH` |
| Functional group | Alcohol group, `-OH` |

## Physical Properties

Ethanol:

- is a liquid at room temperature;
- is soluble in water in all proportions;
- is a good solvent;
- has a characteristic smell;
- is volatile compared with many water-like liquids.

## Uses of Ethanol

| Use | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Alcoholic drinks | Active ingredient, though consumption has health risks. |
| Medicines | Used in tincture iodine, cough syrups, and tonics. |
| Solvent | Dissolves many organic substances. |
| Fuel/additive | Can be blended with petrol in some countries. |

## Health Effects of Ethanol

Small quantities of dilute ethanol can cause drunkenness.

Large quantities can:

- slow metabolic processes;
- depress the central nervous system;
- reduce coordination;
- cause mental confusion;
- cause drowsiness;
- reduce judgement and timing;
- lead to serious health problems with long-term use.

Pure ethanol, called absolute alcohol, can be dangerous if consumed.

## Methanol Warning

Methanol is highly poisonous.

In the liver, methanol is oxidised to methanal. Methanal damages cells and can cause blindness or death.

Industrial alcohol is often denatured by adding poisonous substances such as methanol and dyes so it cannot be misused for drinking.

# Unit 4R: Reactions of Ethanol

## 1. Reaction with Sodium

Ethanol reacts with sodium to form sodium ethoxide and hydrogen gas.

$$
2Na + 2CH_3CH_2OH \rightarrow 2CH_3CH_2ONa + H_2
$$

Hydrogen gas can be tested by bringing a burning splint near it. It burns with a pop sound.

## Comparison with Other Reactions Producing Hydrogen

Hydrogen is also produced when some metals react with:

- dilute acids;
- water;
- alcohols such as ethanol.

## 2. Dehydration of Ethanol

When ethanol is heated at 443 K with excess concentrated sulphuric acid, it loses water and forms ethene.

$$
CH_3CH_2OH \xrightarrow[443K]{Hot\ conc.\ H_2SO_4} CH_2=CH_2 + H_2O
$$

Concentrated sulphuric acid acts as a dehydrating agent.

## Meaning of Dehydrating Agent

A dehydrating agent removes water from a compound.

In this reaction, ethanol loses:

- `H` from one carbon;
- `OH` from the neighbouring carbon;
- together these form water.

The remaining carbon atoms form a double bond.

# Unit 4T: Reactions of Ethanoic Acid

## 1. Esterification

Ethanoic acid reacts with ethanol in the presence of concentrated sulphuric acid to form an ester.

$$
CH_3COOH + CH_3CH_2OH \xrightarrow{Acid} CH_3COOCH_2CH_3 + H_2O
$$

The ester formed is ethyl ethanoate.

## Esters

Esters are usually sweet-smelling substances.

Uses:

- perfumes;
- flavouring agents;
- fragrant products.

## 2. Saponification

When an ester reacts with sodium hydroxide, it forms alcohol and the sodium salt of a carboxylic acid.

$$
CH_3COOCH_2CH_3 + NaOH \rightarrow CH_3COONa + CH_3CH_2OH
$$

This reaction is called saponification because it is used in soap preparation.

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.

## 3. Reaction with Base

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium hydroxide to form sodium ethanoate and water.

$$
CH_3COOH + NaOH \rightarrow CH_3COONa + H_2O
$$

This is a neutralisation reaction.

## 4. Reaction with Carbonates

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium carbonate to form sodium ethanoate, water, and carbon dioxide.

$$
2CH_3COOH + Na_2CO_3 \rightarrow 2CH_3COONa + H_2O + CO_2
$$

## 5. Reaction with Hydrogencarbonates

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium hydrogencarbonate to form sodium ethanoate, water, and carbon dioxide.

$$
CH_3COOH + NaHCO_3 \rightarrow CH_3COONa + H_2O + CO_2
$$

Pass the gas through lime water.

$$
Ca(OH)_2 + CO_2 \rightarrow CaCO_3 + H_2O
$$

Lime water turns milky.

# Unit 4U: Soaps and Detergents

## Why Oil and Water Do Not Mix

Most dirt is oily.

Oil does not dissolve in water, so water alone cannot remove oily dirt effectively.

## Soap Molecules

Soap molecules are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.

Each soap molecule has two ends:

| Part | Nature | Attracted To |
|---|---|---|
| Ionic head | Hydrophilic | Water |
| Long hydrocarbon tail | Hydrophobic | Oil/grease |

## Micelle Formation in Water

In water, soap molecules arrange themselves into micelles.

The hydrophobic tails go inward, away from water, and attach to oil/grease.

The hydrophilic ionic heads face outward toward water.

## Cleaning Action

1. Soap molecules surround oily dirt.
2. Hydrophobic tails enter the oil/grease.
3. Hydrophilic heads remain in water.
4. The oily dirt breaks into small droplets surrounded by soap molecules.
5. These droplets remain suspended as an emulsion.
6. Rinsing washes the suspended dirt away.

## Why Soap Solution Looks Cloudy

Soap micelles are large enough to scatter light.

Therefore, soap solution appears cloudy.

## Soap at the Surface of Water

At the surface of water:

- ionic heads stay in water;
- hydrophobic tails point out of water.

Inside water:

- tails cluster inside the micelle;
- heads face water outside.

# Unit 4V: Hard Water, Scum, and Detergents

## Hard Water

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium salts.

Examples of ions causing hardness:

- `Ca2+`;
- `Mg2+`.

## Soap in Hard Water

Soap reacts with calcium and magnesium ions to form insoluble salts.

These insoluble salts appear as a curdy white precipitate called scum.

General idea:

$$
2RCOONa + Ca^{2+} \rightarrow (RCOO)_2Ca + 2Na^+
$$

Here, `(RCOO)2Ca` is insoluble calcium soap.

## Why More Soap Is Needed in Hard Water

Some soap is wasted in forming scum before cleaning can begin.

Therefore, more soap is needed to form enough lather.

## Detergents

Detergents are cleansing agents that remain effective in hard water.

They are often:

- sodium salts of sulphonic acids;
- ammonium salts with chloride or bromide ions;
- long-chain compounds with hydrophilic and hydrophobic parts.

## Why Detergents Work in Hard Water

The charged ends of detergents do not form insoluble precipitates with calcium and magnesium ions.

Therefore, detergents form lather in hard water and clean effectively.

## Can Detergents Test Water Hardness?

No. Detergents do not form scum with hard water, so they cannot be used to check hardness in the way soap can.

Soap is better for detecting hardness because it forms scum and less foam in hard water.

- hydrophobic tail, which dissolves in oil/grease.

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Carbon has four valence electrons and forms covalent bonds by sharing electrons. Its tetravalency and catenation allow it to form a huge number of stable compounds.

Hydrocarbons contain carbon and hydrogen only. Alkanes are saturated and have only single bonds. Alkenes and alkynes are unsaturated and contain double or triple bonds.

Functional groups such as `-OH`, `-CHO`, `>C=O`, and `-COOH` give carbon compounds their characteristic chemical properties. A homologous series contains compounds with the same functional group and successive members differing by `-CH2-`.

Carbon compounds burn to release heat and light. Ethanol can be oxidised to ethanoic acid, reacts with sodium to produce hydrogen, and dehydrates to ethene. Ethanoic acid forms esters with ethanol, reacts with bases to form salts, and reacts with carbonates/hydrogencarbonates to produce carbon dioxide.

Soap molecules have hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails. They form micelles in water and remove oily dirt by emulsification. In hard water, soap forms scum with calcium and magnesium ions, while detergents remain effective.
