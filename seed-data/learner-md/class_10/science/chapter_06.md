## Big Ideas

Control and coordination connect three stages:

1. Detect a change.
2. Process information.
3. Produce the correct response.

Animals use two major systems:

- nervous control: fast, electrical, short-lived, targeted;
- hormonal control: slower, chemical, longer-lasting, widespread.

Plants do not have nerves or muscles, but they still coordinate responses through electrical-chemical signals, water movement in cells, growth changes, and hormones.

# Unit 6A: Why Control and Coordination Are Needed

## Movement as a Response

Living organisms move in response to changes around them.

Examples:

| Situation | Stimulus | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Cat sees a mouse | Visual stimulus | Cat runs. |
| Hand touches hot object | Heat/pain stimulus | Hand withdraws. |
| Bright light falls on eyes | Light stimulus | Pupil size changes/blinking may occur. |
| Plant shoot receives light from one side | Light direction | Shoot bends toward light. |
| Tendril touches support | Touch stimulus | Tendril coils around support. |

## Why Response Must Be Controlled

Different stimuli need different responses.

Examples:

- whispering in class instead of shouting;
- pulling hand away from heat;
- bending a shoot toward light;
- increasing heartbeat during fear.

Incorrect or delayed responses can harm the organism.

## Specialised Tissues

Multicellular organisms use specialised tissues for control and coordination.

In animals:

- nervous tissue detects and transmits information;
- muscular tissue produces movement;
- endocrine glands produce hormones.

In plants:

- hormones and growth changes coordinate responses;
- some rapid responses involve changes in water content of cells.

# Unit 6B: Animal Nervous System

## Receptors

Receptors are specialised nerve endings or cells that detect stimuli.

| Receptor Type | Detects | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Gustatory receptors | Taste | Tongue |
| Olfactory receptors | Smell | Nose |
| Photoreceptors | Light | Eyes |
| Thermoreceptors | Temperature | Skin |
| Mechanoreceptors | Touch/pressure | Skin, inner ear |

## Why Taste Changes When Nose Is Blocked

Taste and smell work together to create the full flavour of food.

When the nose is blocked:

- olfactory receptors receive fewer smell signals;
- flavour perception becomes weaker;
- food seems less tasty.

This is common during a cold.

# Unit 6C: Neuron Structure and Impulse Transmission

## Parts of a Neuron

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Dendrite | Receives information from receptor or another neuron. |
| Cell body | Contains nucleus and cytoplasm; maintains neuron. |
| Axon | Carries electrical impulse away from cell body. |
| Nerve ending | Releases chemicals at synapse. |
| Synapse | Gap where signal passes chemically to next cell. |

## Text Diagram of a Neuron

```text
Dendrites -> Cell body -> Axon -> Nerve endings -> Synapse
```

## Direction of Impulse

Information generally travels in this direction:

```text
Dendrite -> Cell body -> Axon -> Nerve ending
```

At the nerve ending, the electrical impulse causes release of chemicals.

These chemicals cross the synapse and start a new electrical impulse in the next neuron.

## Electrical and Chemical Nature

Inside a neuron:

- signal travels as an electrical impulse.

Across a synapse:

- signal travels as a chemical messenger.

Then in the next neuron:

- chemical signal starts another electrical impulse.

## Why Synapses Matter

Synapses allow:

- one-way transmission;
- connection between neurons;
- connection between neuron and muscle;
- connection between neuron and gland;
- control and processing of information.

# Unit 6D: Reflex Action

## Meaning

A reflex action is a sudden, automatic response to a stimulus.

It occurs without conscious thinking.

Examples:

- pulling hand away from a hot object;
- blinking when something approaches the eye;
- knee-jerk response;
- mouth watering at sight/smell of food;
- pupil size change in bright light.

## Why Reflexes Are Needed

Some situations are urgent.

If you touch a flame, waiting for conscious thinking may take too long.

A fast pathway protects the body from damage.

## Reflex Arc

A reflex arc is the nerve pathway followed in a reflex action.

Typical pathway:

```text
Receptor -> Sensory neuron -> Spinal cord -> Motor neuron -> Effector
```

Effector means the muscle or gland that produces the response.

## Example: Touching a Hot Object

1. Heat/pain receptors in skin detect stimulus.
2. Sensory neuron carries impulse to spinal cord.
3. Relay neuron in spinal cord passes signal to motor neuron.
4. Motor neuron carries signal to arm muscle.
5. Muscle contracts.
6. Hand is pulled away.
7. Information also travels to brain, so we become aware of pain.

## Why Reflex Arcs Are Often in the Spinal Cord

Nerves from the body pass through the spinal cord on their way to the brain.

The spinal cord is the first place where sensory and motor neurons can connect quickly.

This saves time.

# Unit 6E: Human Brain

## Central Nervous System

The brain and spinal cord form the central nervous system.

They:

- receive information;
- integrate information;
- make decisions;
- coordinate responses.

## Peripheral Nervous System

The peripheral nervous system includes:

- cranial nerves from brain;
- spinal nerves from spinal cord.

It connects the central nervous system with the rest of the body.

## Major Brain Regions

| Brain Region | Main Functions |
|---|---|
| Forebrain | Thinking, memory, interpretation of sensory information, voluntary actions, hunger centre. |
| Midbrain | Some involuntary actions and relay functions. |
| Hindbrain | Involuntary actions, posture, balance, coordination. |

## Forebrain

The forebrain is the main thinking part of the brain.

It has areas for:

- hearing;
- smell;
- sight;
- touch;
- taste;
- association and interpretation;
- voluntary movement decisions.

It also contains centres related to hunger and feeling full.

## Medulla

The medulla is part of the hindbrain.

It controls many involuntary actions, including:

- blood pressure;
- salivation;
- vomiting;
- breathing-related control;
- heartbeat-related regulation.

## Cerebellum

The cerebellum is part of the hindbrain.

It maintains:

- posture;
- balance;
- precision of voluntary actions.

Examples:

- walking in a straight line;
- riding a bicycle;
- picking up a pencil accurately.

## Brain Protection

The brain is delicate and vital.

- skull/cranium;

## Spinal Cord Protection

# Unit 6G: How Nervous Tissue Causes Muscle Action

## From Signal to Movement

Nervous tissue does not directly move the body.

It sends information to muscles.

Muscle tissue performs the movement.

## Muscle Cell Shortening

When a nerve impulse reaches muscle cells:

- special proteins in muscle cells change shape and arrangement;
- muscle fibres shorten;
- the muscle contracts;
- movement occurs.

## Voluntary and Involuntary Muscles

| Type | Control | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Voluntary muscles | Under conscious control | Arm and leg muscles. |
| Involuntary muscles | Not under direct conscious control | Muscles of gut, heart-related action. |

# Unit 6H: Voluntary, Involuntary, and Reflex Actions

## Voluntary Actions

Voluntary actions are actions we decide to perform.

Examples:

- writing;
- walking intentionally;
- clapping;
- moving a chair;
- speaking.

They involve conscious decision-making by the brain.

## Involuntary Actions

Involuntary actions occur without conscious control.

Examples:

- heartbeat;
- salivation;
- vomiting;
- blood pressure regulation;
- digestion-related muscle movement.

Many involuntary actions are controlled by the midbrain and hindbrain.

## Reflex Actions

Reflex actions are quick automatic responses to stimuli.

They often involve spinal cord pathways and are protective.

## Comparison

| Feature | Voluntary Action | Involuntary Action | Reflex Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conscious control | Yes | No | No |
| Speed | Usually slower than reflex | Varies | Very fast |
| Control centre | Forebrain | Brainstem/other centres | Mostly spinal cord, brain also informed |
| Example | Picking up a book | Heartbeat | Pulling hand from flame |

# Unit 6I: Coordination in Plants

## Plants Respond Without Nerves

Plants do not have:

- nervous system;
- muscles.

But plants still respond to stimuli.

They use:

- electrical-chemical signals;
- cell-to-cell communication;
- changes in water content of cells;
- plant hormones;
- controlled growth.

## Two Types of Plant Movement

| Type | Growth Involved? | Speed | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Immediate response | No | Fast | Folding of Mimosa leaves on touch. |
| Growth movement | Yes | Slow | Shoot bending toward light. |

# Unit 6J: Immediate Response in Sensitive Plant

## Mimosa Leaf Folding

When leaves of the sensitive plant are touched, they fold and droop quickly.

This movement is not due to growth.

## How It Happens

1. Touch stimulus is detected.
2. Information is transmitted cell to cell by electrical-chemical means.
3. Cells at the base of leaflets change water content.
4. Some cells lose water and shrink.
5. Change in turgor pressure causes leaf movement.

## Key Difference from Animal Movement

Animals use muscle proteins that shorten.

Mimosa uses water movement in cells to change cell shape.

---

# Unit 6K: Growth Movements in Plants

## Tendril Movement

Tendrils of pea plants are sensitive to touch.

When a tendril touches support:

- cells on the contact side grow more slowly;
- cells on the opposite side grow faster;
- the tendril bends and coils around the support.

## Tropic Movements

Tropic movements are directional growth movements in response to a stimulus.

They may be:

- positive: toward the stimulus;
- negative: away from the stimulus.

## Phototropism

Phototropism is growth response to light.

Shoots generally show positive phototropism.

Roots often show negative phototropism.

## Geotropism

Geotropism is growth response to gravity.

| Plant Part | Response |
|---|---|
| Root | Positive geotropism; grows downward. |
| Shoot | Negative geotropism; grows upward. |

## Hydrotropism

Hydrotropism is growth response to water.

Roots usually grow toward moisture.

## Chemotropism

Chemotropism is growth response to chemicals.

Example:

Pollen tube grows toward the ovule due to chemical signals.

# Unit 6L: Auxin and Phototropism

## Auxin

Auxin is a plant hormone made at the shoot tip.

It helps cells grow longer.

## How Shoot Bends Toward Light

When light comes from one side:

1. Auxin moves toward the shady side of the shoot.
2. Cells on the shady side receive more auxin.
3. These cells elongate more.
4. The shady side grows faster than the lit side.
5. The shoot bends toward light.

## Activity 6.2 Conclusion

In the flask-and-window experiment:

- shoots bend toward light;
- roots grow away from light;
- when flask is turned, new growth changes direction according to the new light direction.

Conclusion:

Plant growth responds directionally to light.

# Unit 6M: Plant Hormones

## Meaning

Plant hormones are chemical substances produced in one part of a plant and transported/diffused to another part where they regulate growth, development, and response to stimuli.

## Important Plant Hormones

| Hormone | Main Function |
|---|---|
| Auxin | Cell elongation; phototropism; tendril bending. |
| Gibberellin | Stem growth and growth promotion. |
| Cytokinin | Cell division; high in fruits and seeds. |
| Abscisic acid | Growth inhibition; wilting; stress response. |

## Growth Promoters and Inhibitors

Growth-promoting hormones:

- auxin;
- gibberellin;
- cytokinin.

Growth-inhibiting hormone:

- abscisic acid.

# Unit 6N: Electrical vs Chemical Communication

## Electrical Impulses

Advantages:

- very fast;
- useful for quick responses;
- precise.

Limitations:

- reach only cells connected by nervous tissue;
- cannot be generated continuously without reset time.

## Chemical Communication

Advantages:

- can reach many cells through diffusion or blood;
- can act for a longer time;
- useful for growth, metabolism, and long-term coordination.

Limitations:

- slower than nerve impulses.

## Why Multicellular Organisms Use Both

Fast reactions need nervous control.

Long-lasting and widespread changes need hormonal control.

# Unit 6O: Hormones in Animals

## Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into blood.

Blood carries hormones to target organs.

## Target Organs

Hormones act only on target cells/organs that have receptors for that hormone.

## Adrenaline: Emergency Hormone

Adrenaline is secreted by adrenal glands during fear, anger, stress, or emergency.

It prepares the body for fight or flight.

Effects:

- heart beats faster;
- breathing rate increases;
- more oxygen reaches muscles;
- blood supply to digestive system and skin decreases;
- blood is diverted to skeletal muscles;
- body becomes ready for quick action.

## Thyroxin

Thyroxin is secreted by the thyroid gland.

Iodine is needed to make thyroxin.

Functions:

- regulates carbohydrate metabolism;
- regulates protein metabolism;
- regulates fat metabolism;
- supports growth and development.

Iodine deficiency can cause goitre, with swelling in the neck region.

## Growth Hormone

Growth hormone is secreted by the pituitary gland.

Function:

- regulates growth and development of the body.

Deficiency in childhood can cause dwarfism.

Excess secretion in childhood can lead to excessive growth.

## Sex Hormones

| Hormone | Gland | Main Role |
|---|---|---|
| Testosterone | Testes | Development of male sex organs and male secondary sexual characters. |
| Oestrogen | Ovaries | Development of female sex organs, female secondary sexual characters, and menstrual cycle regulation. |

These hormones cause many changes during puberty.

## Insulin

Insulin is secreted by the pancreas.

Function:

- regulates blood sugar level.

If insulin is not secreted in proper amount, blood sugar rises and causes diabetes-related harmful effects.

Some diabetic patients are treated with insulin injections.

## Hypothalamus and Releasing Hormones

The hypothalamus helps control hormone release.

Example:

When growth hormone level is low, the hypothalamus releases growth hormone releasing factor, which stimulates the pituitary gland to release growth hormone.

---

# Unit 6P: Feedback Mechanism

## Meaning

A feedback mechanism regulates the timing and amount of hormone secretion.

## Insulin Feedback Example

When blood sugar rises:

1. Pancreatic cells detect high sugar level.
2. Pancreas secretes more insulin.
3. Insulin helps reduce blood sugar.
4. As blood sugar falls, insulin secretion decreases.

This prevents both excessive and insufficient hormone action.

## Why Feedback Is Important

Hormones are powerful even in small amounts.

Precise regulation is needed to keep the body balanced.

## Question 2: What Happens at the Synapse Between Two Neurons?

## Answer

At the synapse:

1. Electrical impulse reaches the end of the axon.
2. Chemicals are released from the nerve ending.
3. These chemicals cross the synaptic gap.
4. Chemicals reach the dendrite of the next neuron.
5. A new electrical impulse starts in the next neuron.

## Question 10: Design an Experiment to Demonstrate Hydrotropism.

## Answer

1. Place germinating seeds in a tray with moist cotton.
2. Keep a porous pot or small source of water on one side.
3. Keep the rest of the region comparatively dry.
4. Allow roots to grow for a few days.
5. Observe the direction of root growth.

Expected observation:

Roots bend toward the water source.

Conclusion:

Roots show positive hydrotropism.

## Exercise 6: How Does Phototropism Occur in Plants?

## Answer

Phototropism occurs because of unequal distribution of auxin.

When light comes from one side:

1. Auxin moves to the shaded side of the shoot.
2. Shaded side cells elongate more.
3. Lit side grows less.
4. Shoot bends toward light.

This is positive phototropism in shoots.

## Exercise 7: Which Signals Are Disrupted in Spinal Cord Injury?

## Answer

Spinal cord injury can disrupt signals between brain and body.

Affected signals may include:

- sensory signals from body to brain;
- motor signals from brain to muscles;
- reflex pathways below or at the injury level;
- autonomic signals controlling some involuntary functions.

The exact effect depends on the location and severity of injury.

- cytokinin promotes cell division;

| Growth involved | No, for Mimosa leaf folding | No, for ordinary leg movement |
| Control | Plant cell signalling | Nervous system control |

# Unit 6T: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying all plant movement is growth. | Mimosa leaf folding is not growth; shoot bending is growth. |
| Saying reflex action is controlled only after brain thinks. | Reflex response is usually coordinated by spinal cord first. |
| Confusing axon and dendrite. | Dendrite receives impulse; axon carries impulse away from cell body. |
| Saying synapse is a solid connection. | Synapse is a gap crossed by chemicals. |
| Saying cerebrum/cerebellum same thing. | Cerebellum maintains posture, balance, and precision. |
| Saying hormones act on every cell equally. | Hormones act on target cells with suitable receptors. |
| Saying auxin makes the lit side grow more. | In phototropism, auxin accumulates on the shaded side of the shoot. |
| Calling insulin a plant hormone. | Insulin is an animal hormone from pancreas. |
| Saying iodine is a hormone. | Iodine is needed to make thyroxin; it is not the hormone itself. |

## Best Exam Answer Pattern

For coordination questions:

1. Identify the stimulus.
2. Identify the receptor or detecting region.
3. State the signal pathway.
4. Name the responding tissue/organ.
5. Explain the final response.

Example:

```text
When a shoot receives light from one side, auxin moves to the shaded side. Cells on the shaded side elongate more than cells on the lit side. This unequal growth bends the shoot toward light.
```

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Control and coordination help organisms detect changes and respond correctly. In animals, receptors detect stimuli and neurons transmit impulses. Signals travel from dendrite to cell body to axon, and chemicals cross synapses.

Reflex actions are fast automatic responses usually coordinated through the spinal cord. The brain controls thinking, voluntary actions, many involuntary actions, balance, posture, and coordination. The brain and spinal cord are protected by skull, cerebrospinal fluid, and vertebral column.

Plants do not have nerves or muscles, but they respond through electrical-chemical signals, water movement, growth, and hormones. Mimosa leaf folding is a quick turgor-based response, while shoot bending toward light is a growth movement.

Plant hormones include auxin, gibberellin, cytokinin, and abscisic acid. Animal hormones include adrenaline, thyroxin, growth hormone, testosterone, oestrogen, and insulin. Hormone secretion is regulated by feedback mechanisms.
