# Unit 12A: Electricity and Magnetism

## Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

Electric current can produce a magnetic field.

This means a wire carrying current behaves like a magnet in the region around it.

The basic evidence is simple:

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| A compass needle stays in its north-south direction when no current flows nearby. | No strong nearby magnetic effect from the wire. |
| The compass needle deflects when current flows through the wire. | Current produces a magnetic field around the wire. |
| The needle deflects in the opposite direction when current direction is reversed. | Direction of magnetic field depends on direction of current. |

## Oersted's Observation

Hans Christian Oersted observed that a compass needle gets deflected when placed near a current-carrying wire.

This showed that electricity and magnetism are connected.

The discovery became the foundation of electromagnetism and later technologies such as motors, generators, speakers, microphones, and electromagnetic instruments.

## Important Idea

Current produces magnetism.

The reverse effect is also possible: changing magnetic fields can produce electric current. That idea is called electromagnetic induction and is used in generators, though this chapter focuses mainly on magnetic effects of current and domestic circuits.

# Unit 12B: Magnetic Field and Field Lines

## Compass Needle as a Small Magnet

A compass needle is itself a small magnet.

| End of Needle | Name |
|---|---|
| End pointing approximately north | North-seeking pole or north pole |
| End pointing approximately south | South-seeking pole or south pole |

Like poles repel and unlike poles attract.

So:

| Poles Brought Together | Result |
|---|---|
| North and north | Repulsion |
| South and south | Repulsion |
| North and south | Attraction |

## Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic force can be detected.

If a compass needle changes direction near a magnet, it means the compass is experiencing the magnetic field of the magnet.

## Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines used to represent magnetic field.

They show:

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direction of line | Direction in which a free north pole would move. |
| Closeness of lines | Strength of magnetic field. |
| Shape of lines | Pattern of the magnetic field. |

## Field Lines Around a Bar Magnet

Outside a bar magnet, magnetic field lines emerge from the north pole and enter the south pole.

Inside the magnet, field lines go from south pole to north pole.

Therefore, magnetic field lines form closed curves.

## Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Field lines emerge from north pole and merge at south pole outside the magnet. | This gives conventional direction outside the magnet. |
| Inside the magnet, lines go from south pole to north pole. | This completes the closed curve. |
| Magnetic field lines are closed curves. | They do not start or end in empty space. |
| Direction at a point is shown by tangent to field line. | A compass north pole points along this direction. |
| Lines are closer where field is stronger. | Crowded lines near poles show stronger field. |
| No two field lines intersect. | Intersection would mean two directions at one point, which is impossible. |

## Why Field Lines Do Not Intersect

If two field lines crossed, a compass placed at the crossing point would have to point in two directions at the same time.

That is impossible.

So magnetic field lines never intersect.

## Uniform Magnetic Field

A uniform magnetic field has the same strength and direction throughout a region.

It is represented by straight, parallel, equally spaced field lines.

# Unit 12C: Magnetic Field Around a Straight Current-Carrying Wire

## Field Pattern

A straight wire carrying current produces magnetic field lines in the form of concentric circles around the wire.

The wire passes through the centre of these circles.

| Observation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Iron filings form circles around the wire. | Field lines are circular. |
| Compass direction changes around the wire. | Field direction is tangential to the circular field line. |
| Reversing current reverses compass deflection. | Magnetic field direction reverses. |

## Effect of Current

At a fixed distance from the wire:

| Current | Magnetic Field |
|---|---|
| Smaller current | Weaker field. |
| Larger current | Stronger field. |

So increasing current increases magnetic field strength.

## Effect of Distance

For the same current:

| Distance from Wire | Magnetic Field |
|---|---|
| Near the wire | Stronger field. |
| Far from the wire | Weaker field. |

So magnetic field becomes weaker as distance from the wire increases.

## Field Direction

The direction of field around a straight current-carrying conductor is found using the right-hand thumb rule.

# Unit 12D: Right-Hand Thumb Rule

## Statement

Imagine holding a straight current-carrying conductor in your right hand so that your thumb points in the direction of current.

Then the curled fingers show the direction of magnetic field lines around the conductor.

## How to Use the Rule

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Identify direction of conventional current. |
| 2 | Point right thumb in that direction. |
| 3 | Curl fingers naturally. |
| 4 | The curled fingers show magnetic field direction. |

## Common Direction Cases

| Current Direction | Field Direction |
|---|---|
| Upward through vertical wire | Anticlockwise when viewed from above. |
| Downward through vertical wire | Clockwise when viewed from above. |
| Toward observer | Anticlockwise around wire. |
| Away from observer | Clockwise around wire. |

## Worked Example: Horizontal Power Line

**Question:** Current through a horizontal power line flows from east to west. What is the direction of magnetic field at a point directly below it and directly above it?

Use right-hand thumb rule:

1. Point right thumb from east to west.
2. Curled fingers show circular field around the wire.
3. At points above and below the wire, field is tangential to the circular field line.

When viewed from the east end, the field circles clockwise.

When viewed from the west end, the field circles anticlockwise.

This is the safest learner answer: mention that the field forms circles around the wire and use the viewpoint clearly.

# Unit 12E: Magnetic Field Due to a Current Through a Circular Loop

## Circular Loop Field Pattern

If a straight wire is bent into a circular loop and current flows through it, each small part of the loop produces a magnetic field.

At the centre of the loop:

| Contribution | Effect |
|---|---|
| Field from each part of loop | Points in the same direction. |
| Combined field | Becomes strong at the centre. |
| Field lines near centre | Appear nearly straight and parallel. |

## Direction of Field at Centre

Use right-hand thumb rule for each part of the loop, or use this shortcut:

| Current Around Loop as Seen by You | Magnetic Field Through Centre |
|---|---|
| Anticlockwise | Toward you or out of the page. |
| Clockwise | Away from you or into the page. |

If a circular loop lies flat on a table and current is clockwise when viewed from above, the magnetic field inside the loop is downward into the table.

## Effect of Number of Turns

If a circular coil has `n` turns, the magnetic field at the centre becomes `n` times the field due to one turn, provided current is the same.

Reason:

Each turn carries current in the same direction and each turn adds its magnetic field in the same direction.

# Unit 12F: Magnetic Field Due to a Current in a Solenoid

## Solenoid

A solenoid is a coil of many circular turns of insulated copper wire wound closely in the shape of a cylinder.

When current passes through a solenoid, it behaves like a bar magnet.

## Field Pattern of Solenoid

| Region | Field Pattern |
|---|---|
| Inside solenoid | Straight, parallel, closely spaced field lines. |
| Outside solenoid | Field pattern resembles that around a bar magnet. |
| Ends of solenoid | One end acts as north pole and the other as south pole. |

## Uniform Magnetic Field Inside Solenoid

The field inside a long current-carrying solenoid is nearly uniform.

This is shown by straight, parallel, equally spaced field lines inside it.

## Solenoid as a Bar Magnet

A current-carrying solenoid has:

| Bar Magnet Feature | Solenoid Equivalent |
|---|---|
| North pole | One end of solenoid behaves as north pole. |
| South pole | Other end behaves as south pole. |
| Field outside | Similar field line pattern. |
| Field inside | Lines go from south end to north end. |

## Electromagnet

A solenoid can be used to make an electromagnet.

Process:

1. Wind insulated copper wire into a coil.
2. Place soft iron inside the coil.
3. Pass current through the coil.
4. The magnetic field of solenoid magnetises the soft iron.

## Why Soft Iron Is Used

Soft iron is commonly used in electromagnets because it becomes strongly magnetised when current flows and loses most of its magnetism when current is switched off.

## Uses of Electromagnets

| Use | Reason |
|---|---|
| Electric bell | Magnetism can be switched on and off. |
| Cranes for lifting scrap iron | Strong temporary magnet can lift and release iron. |
| Relays | Small current can control a larger circuit. |
| Loudspeakers | Magnetic effect of current produces motion. |

# Unit 12G: Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

## Basic Idea

A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field.

If this conductor is placed inside another magnetic field, the two magnetic fields interact.

As a result, the conductor experiences a force.

## What the Force Depends On

The force on a current-carrying conductor depends on:

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Current in conductor | Larger current gives larger force. |
| Strength of magnetic field | Stronger magnet gives larger force. |
| Length of conductor in field | Longer conductor in field gives larger force. |
| Angle between current and magnetic field | Force is largest when current is perpendicular to magnetic field. |

## Direction of Force

The direction of force depends on:

1. Direction of current.
2. Direction of magnetic field.

If either current direction or magnetic field direction is reversed, the force direction reverses.

## Fleming's Left-Hand Rule

Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of your left hand so that all three are mutually perpendicular.

| Finger | Represents |
|---|---|
| Forefinger | Magnetic field |
| Middle finger | Current |
| Thumb | Force or motion |

This rule is used for motors and for force on a current-carrying conductor.

## Memory Aid

Use `FBI` in left hand:

| Letter | Meaning |
|---|---|
| F | Force - thumb |
| B | Magnetic field - forefinger |
| I | Current - middle finger |

## Electron Motion Warning

Fleming's left-hand rule uses direction of conventional current.

For electrons:

| Quantity | Direction |
|---|---|
| Electron motion | Actual movement of electrons. |
| Conventional current | Opposite to electron motion. |

Always convert electron direction into conventional current direction before applying the rule.

## Worked Example: Electron in Magnetic Field

**Question:** An electron enters a magnetic field at right angles. Which direction is the force?

Important reasoning:

1. Electron is negatively charged.
2. Conventional current direction is opposite to electron motion.
3. Apply Fleming's left-hand rule using current direction, not electron direction.

In the textbook example, the correct force direction is into the page.

# Unit 12H: Domestic Electric Circuits

## Electric Supply in Homes

In Indian domestic circuits, the usual supply potential difference is `220 V` and frequency is `50 Hz`.

The supply commonly has three wires:

| Wire | Textbook Colour Convention | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Live wire | Red | Carries current at high potential. |
| Neutral wire | Black | Completes circuit and is near zero potential. |
| Earth wire | Green | Safety wire connected to earth. |

## Potential Difference

The potential difference between live and neutral wires is about `220 V`.

Appliances are connected across live and neutral so that each gets the required voltage.

## Main Components in Domestic Circuit

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Electricity meter | Measures electrical energy used. |
| Main fuse | Protects circuit from excessive current. |
| Main switch | Allows the entire supply to be switched off. |
| Separate circuits | Divide load into lighting/fan and high-power appliance circuits. |
| Appliance switch | Controls individual appliance. |
| Earth wire | Provides safety path for leakage current. |

## 5 A and 15 A Circuits

Domestic wiring often uses separate circuits:

| Circuit Rating | Typical Use |
|---|---|
| `5 A` | Bulbs, fans, small appliances. |
| `15 A` | Geyser, air cooler, heater, high-power appliances. |

High-power appliances should not be forced into low-current sockets.

## Why Appliances Are Connected in Parallel

Appliances in homes are connected in parallel because:

| Advantage | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Same voltage | Each appliance gets about `220 V`. |
| Independent switching | One appliance can be switched off without affecting others. |
| Different current needs | Each appliance draws current according to its power. |
| Fault isolation | One failed appliance does not stop all appliances. |

## Earth Wire

The earth wire is connected to a metal plate buried deep in the ground.

It is connected to the metallic body of appliances such as:

1. Electric iron.
2. Toaster.
3. Refrigerator.
4. Table fan.
5. Electric heater.

If current leaks to the metallic body, the earth wire provides a low-resistance path to ground.

This keeps the appliance body at earth potential and reduces the chance of severe electric shock.

## Fuse

A fuse is connected in series with the circuit.

When current becomes too high:

1. Fuse wire heats up.
2. Fuse wire melts.
3. Circuit breaks.
4. Current stops.
5. Appliance and wiring are protected.

## Short Circuit

A short circuit occurs when live and neutral wires come into direct contact.

This can happen when:

| Cause | Result |
|---|---|
| Wire insulation is damaged | Live and neutral touch. |
| Appliance has internal fault | Current finds very low resistance path. |
| Loose or exposed wiring | Direct contact becomes possible. |

During a short circuit, resistance becomes very small and current increases heavily.

## Overloading

Overloading occurs when current drawn by the circuit exceeds its safe rating.

Common causes:

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Too many appliances in one socket | Total current becomes too high. |
| High-power appliance on low-rating circuit | Current exceeds safe limit. |
| Sudden rise in supply voltage | Appliances may draw excessive current. |
| Faulty wiring | Can cause abnormal current flow. |

## Avoiding Overloading

Precautions:

1. Do not connect too many appliances to one socket.
2. Use proper fuse or MCB rating.
3. Use separate circuits for high-power appliances.
4. Replace damaged insulation and loose plugs.
5. Use three-pin plugs with proper earthing for metallic appliances.

# Unit 12I: Solved In-Text Questions

## Question 1: Why Does a Compass Needle Get Deflected Near a Bar Magnet?

A compass needle is a small magnet.

When it is brought near a bar magnet, it experiences a magnetic force due to the magnetic field of the bar magnet.

Therefore it turns and gets deflected.

## Question 2: Draw Magnetic Field Lines Around a Bar Magnet.

In a correct diagram:

| Feature | What to Show |
|---|---|
| Outside magnet | Lines emerge from north pole and enter south pole. |
| Inside magnet | Lines go from south pole to north pole. |
| Near poles | Lines are closer together. |
| Shape | Lines form closed curves. |
| Arrows | Direction shown from north to south outside magnet. |

## Question 3: List Properties of Magnetic Field Lines.

Properties:

1. Magnetic field lines are closed curves.
2. Outside a magnet, they go from north pole to south pole.
3. Inside a magnet, they go from south pole to north pole.
4. The tangent to a field line at a point gives direction of magnetic field.
5. Field lines are closer where magnetic field is stronger.
6. No two field lines intersect.

## Question 4: Why Don't Two Magnetic Field Lines Intersect?

If two field lines intersected, the magnetic field at the point of intersection would have two directions.

A compass needle cannot point in two directions at the same time.

Therefore two magnetic field lines never intersect.

## Question 5: Circular Loop on Table with Clockwise Current

If the loop lies in the plane of the table and current is clockwise when viewed from above:

| Region | Magnetic Field Direction |
|---|---|
| Inside the loop | Downward, into the table. |
| Outside the loop | Upward, out of the table. |

This follows from the right-hand thumb rule.

## Question 6: The Magnetic Field in a Region Is Uniform. Draw It.

Represent a uniform magnetic field by straight, parallel, equally spaced lines with arrows in the same direction.

## Question 7: Choose Correct Option - Magnetic Field Inside Long Straight Solenoid

Inside a long current-carrying solenoid, the magnetic field is nearly uniform.

So it is the same at all points inside.

**Answer:** `(d) is the same at all points`.

## Question 8: Which Property of a Proton Can Change in a Magnetic Field?

A proton moving freely in a magnetic field experiences a force perpendicular to its motion.

This force can change direction of motion but not speed.

| Property | Can Change? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Mass | No | Magnetic field does not change mass. |
| Speed | No | Magnetic force is perpendicular, so it does no work. |
| Velocity | Yes | Direction of velocity changes. |
| Momentum | Yes | Momentum depends on velocity. |

**Answer:** `(c) velocity` and `(d) momentum`.

## Question 9: Effect on Displacement of Rod AB

The displacement of rod AB increases if:

| Change | Effect |
|---|---|
| Current in rod increases | Force increases. |
| Stronger horse-shoe magnet is used | Magnetic field increases, so force increases. |
| Length of rod in field increases | More conductor interacts with field, so force increases. |

## Question 10: Alpha Particle Projected West Deflected North

An alpha particle is positively charged.

Using Fleming's left-hand rule:

| Direction | Quantity |
|---|---|
| West | Current or motion of positive charge |
| North | Force |
| Upward | Magnetic field |

**Answer:** `(d) upward`.

## Question 11: Name Two Safety Measures in Electric Circuits and Appliances.

Two common safety measures are:

1. Electric fuse or MCB.
2. Earthing of metallic appliance bodies.

## Question 12: Electric Oven of 2 kW in 220 V, 5 A Circuit

Power of oven:

`P = 2 kW = 2000 W`

Supply voltage:

`V = 220 V`

Current drawn:

`I = P/V = 2000/220 = 9.09 A`

The circuit rating is only `5 A`.

Since `9.09 A` is greater than `5 A`, the circuit will be overloaded. The fuse may blow or the circuit may trip.

## Question 13: Precaution to Avoid Overloading

Precautions:

1. Do not connect many appliances to a single socket.
2. Use separate circuits for high-power appliances.
3. Use proper fuse or MCB ratings.
4. Do not use damaged wires or loose connections.
5. Use sockets and plugs suitable for the current rating of the appliance.

# Unit 12J: Solved Exercise Questions

## Exercise 1: Magnetic Field Near a Long Straight Wire

The magnetic field around a long straight current-carrying wire consists of concentric circles centred on the wire.

**Answer:** `(d) The field consists of concentric circles centred on the wire.`

## Exercise 2: Current During Short Circuit

During a short circuit, live and neutral wires come into direct contact and resistance becomes very low.

So current increases heavily.

**Answer:** `(c) increases heavily`.

## Exercise 3: True or False

### Part A

The field at the centre of a long circular coil carrying current will be parallel straight lines.

**Answer:** True.

At the centre of a circular coil, field lines appear nearly straight and parallel.

### Part B

A wire with green insulation is usually the live wire of electric supply.

**Answer:** False.

Green insulation is used for earth wire in the textbook convention. Live wire is usually red.

## Exercise 4: List Two Methods of Producing Magnetic Fields.

Two methods:

1. Use a permanent magnet such as a bar magnet.
2. Pass electric current through a conductor, coil, or solenoid.

## Exercise 5: When Is Force on Current-Carrying Conductor Largest?

The force is largest when the direction of current is perpendicular to the direction of magnetic field.

That means the angle between current and magnetic field is `90 degrees`.

## Exercise 6: Electron Beam Deflected to Your Right

Situation:

| Quantity | Direction |
|---|---|
| Electron beam | From back wall toward front wall. |
| Deflection | To your right. |
| Conventional current | Opposite to electron motion, from front wall toward back wall. |

Using Fleming's left-hand rule with conventional current, the magnetic field must be upward.

**Answer:** The magnetic field is directed upward.

## Exercise 7: State the Rules

### Part I: Magnetic Field Around Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

Use the right-hand thumb rule.

Hold the conductor in right hand with thumb pointing in the direction of current. Curled fingers show direction of magnetic field lines.

### Part II: Force on Current-Carrying Conductor in Magnetic Field

Use Fleming's left-hand rule.

Stretch thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of left hand mutually perpendicular. Forefinger shows magnetic field, middle finger shows current, and thumb shows force or motion.

### Part III: Induced Current in Coil Rotating in Magnetic Field

Use Fleming's right-hand rule.

Stretch thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of right hand mutually perpendicular. Forefinger shows magnetic field, thumb shows motion of conductor, and middle finger shows induced current.

## Exercise 8: When Does an Electric Short Circuit Occur?

An electric short circuit occurs when live and neutral wires come into direct contact.

This usually happens due to damaged insulation, loose wiring, or a fault inside an appliance.

Because resistance becomes very low, current increases suddenly and dangerously.

## Exercise 9: Function of Earth Wire

The earth wire provides a low-resistance path for leakage current to flow safely into the ground.

It is necessary to earth metallic appliances because if a live wire touches the metal body, the body can become dangerous. Earthing keeps the metal body at earth potential and reduces the risk of electric shock.

# Unit 12L: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying field lines start at north and end at south permanently. | Field lines are closed curves; outside they go N to S, inside S to N. |
| Drawing intersecting magnetic field lines. | Field lines never intersect. |
| Forgetting that a compass needle is a magnet. | Compass deflects because it experiences magnetic force. |
| Applying right-hand thumb rule with left hand. | Use right hand for magnetic field around straight conductor. |
| Using electron direction as current direction. | Conventional current is opposite to electron motion. |
| Confusing Fleming's left and right rules. | Left hand is for motor force; right hand is for induced current. |
| Saying field inside solenoid is zero. | Field inside long solenoid is nearly uniform and strong. |
| Calling green wire live. | Green is earth wire in the textbook convention. |
| Connecting appliances in series in homes. | Domestic appliances are connected in parallel. |

## One-Page Chapter Summary

A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field. Oersted's compass experiment showed the connection between electricity and magnetism. Magnetic field lines represent magnetic field; they form closed curves, show direction, are crowded where field is strong, and never intersect.

Around a straight current-carrying wire, magnetic field lines are concentric circles. Their direction is found by the right-hand thumb rule. A current-carrying circular loop produces a strong field at its centre, and a coil with many turns produces a stronger field. A current-carrying solenoid behaves like a bar magnet and has a nearly uniform magnetic field inside it. With a soft iron core, it becomes an electromagnet.

A current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force. The force is largest when current and magnetic field are perpendicular. Fleming's left-hand rule gives the direction of force. Domestic circuits use live, neutral, and earth wires. Appliances are connected in parallel. Fuse and earthing protect users and appliances from overloading, short circuit, and leakage current.
