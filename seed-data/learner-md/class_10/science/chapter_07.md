## Big Ideas

Reproduction is not essential for the survival of one individual, but it is essential for the survival of a species.

New organisms resemble their parents because DNA is copied and passed on. They are not always exact copies because DNA copying is not perfectly error-free.

Asexual reproduction usually produces offspring very similar to the parent. Sexual reproduction combines DNA from two individuals and creates more variation.

Variation may not always help one individual, but it can help a population survive when the environment changes.

# Unit 7A: Why Do Organisms Reproduce?

## Reproduction and Individual Survival

An organism can remain alive through life processes such as:

- nutrition;
- respiration;
- transport;
- excretion.

Reproduction is different. It is not required to keep one individual alive.

## Reproduction and Species Survival

Reproduction is necessary because:

- it produces new individuals;
- it maintains populations;
- it continues the species over generations;
- it allows inherited body designs to persist;
- it creates variation that can help survival over time.

## Why Similar Organisms Are Recognised as One Species

Individuals of the same species usually look similar because their body design is similar.

Body design is controlled by inherited information stored in DNA.

---

# Unit 7B: DNA Copying and Body Design

## DNA as Blueprint

Chromosomes in the nucleus contain DNA.

DNA carries information for making proteins.

Proteins influence:

- cell structure;
- enzymes;
- body design;
- body functions;
- inherited features.

Simple flow:

```text
DNA information -> Proteins -> Cell features -> Body design
```

## What Happens During Reproduction

Basic events:

1. DNA is copied.
2. Additional cellular apparatus is made.
3. DNA copies separate.
4. Cell divides or reproductive cells fuse.
5. New individual receives genetic information.

## Why DNA Copying Must Be Accurate

Accurate DNA copying preserves body design.

If copying is too inaccurate, new cells may not survive because their proteins and cell functions may fail.

## Why DNA Copying Is Not Perfect

No biochemical reaction is perfectly reliable.

Therefore, small errors occur during DNA copying.

These errors create variation.

## Similar but Not Identical

Offspring resemble parents because DNA is copied.

Offspring are not always identical because DNA copying can include small changes.

# Unit 7C: Importance of Variation

## Meaning

Variation means differences among individuals of the same species.

Examples:

- height differences;
- heat tolerance in bacteria;
- disease resistance;
- flower colour differences;
- seed size differences.

## Why Variation Helps Species

Environments can change due to:

- temperature changes;
- water availability;
- disease;
- predators;
- competition;
- natural disasters;
- climate change.

If all individuals are exactly the same, one harmful change may wipe out the whole population.

If some individuals have useful variations, they may survive and reproduce.

## Example: Heat-Resistant Bacteria

If bacteria live in cool water and the water becomes warmer:

- most bacteria may die;
- a few heat-resistant variants may survive;
- those variants reproduce and continue the population.

## Variation and Individual Benefit

A variation may not benefit the individual immediately.

It may even be neutral or harmful in one environment.

But at the species level, variation increases the chance that at least some individuals survive future changes.

# Unit 7E: Fission

## Meaning

Fission is asexual reproduction in unicellular organisms by cell division.

## Binary Fission

Binary fission means one parent cell divides into two daughter cells.

Examples:

- many bacteria;
- Amoeba;
- Leishmania.

## Binary Fission in Amoeba

In Amoeba, division can occur in any plane because Amoeba has an irregular shape.

Basic steps:

1. DNA duplicates.
2. Nucleus divides.
3. Cytoplasm divides.
4. Two daughter Amoebae form.

## Binary Fission in Leishmania

Leishmania has a whip-like structure at one end.

Because of this more organised body structure, binary fission occurs in a definite orientation.

## Multiple Fission

Multiple fission means one parent cell divides into many daughter cells at the same time.

Example:

- Plasmodium, the malarial parasite.

## Binary vs Multiple Fission

| Feature | Binary Fission | Multiple Fission |
|---|---|---|
| Daughter cells formed | Two | Many |
| Example | Amoeba, Leishmania | Plasmodium |
| Division pattern | One cell splits into two | Nucleus divides repeatedly before many cells form |

# Unit 7F: Budding

## Budding in Yeast

Yeast reproduces by budding.

Process:

1. A small bud appears on parent yeast cell.
2. Nucleus divides.
3. One nucleus enters the bud.
4. Bud grows.
5. Bud separates and becomes a new yeast cell.

## Budding in Hydra

Hydra also reproduces by budding.

Process:

1. Repeated cell division occurs at one specific site.
2. A bud develops as an outgrowth.
3. Bud grows into a tiny Hydra.
4. Mature bud detaches.
5. New Hydra lives independently.

## Key Point

Budding creates a new organism as an outgrowth from the parent.

---

# Unit 7G: Fragmentation

## Meaning

Fragmentation occurs when a simple multicellular organism breaks into pieces and each piece grows into a new individual.

Example:

- Spirogyra.

## Fragmentation in Spirogyra

Spirogyra is a filamentous alga.

When mature filaments break into fragments, each fragment can grow into a new filament.

## Why Fragmentation Works in Simple Organisms

It works when:

- body organisation is simple;
- cells are not highly specialised into many complex organs;
- each fragment can grow independently.

## Why Fragmentation Does Not Work in Complex Organisms

Complex multicellular organisms have:

- specialised cells;
- tissues;
- organs;
- definite body organisation.

Random fragments cannot recreate the whole body easily because each part depends on organised structure.

---

# Unit 7H: Regeneration

## Meaning

Regeneration is the ability to regrow lost body parts.

In some simple animals, body pieces can grow into complete organisms.

Examples:

- Hydra;
- Planaria.

## Regeneration in Planaria

If Planaria is cut into pieces, many pieces can develop into complete Planaria.

## How Regeneration Occurs

1. Specialised cells at the cut region divide.
2. A mass of cells forms.
3. Cells differentiate into different cell types.
4. Tissues and body parts develop in an organised sequence.

## Regeneration Is Not Always Reproduction

Regeneration can replace lost parts.

But most organisms do not normally reproduce by being cut into pieces.

Therefore, regeneration is not the same as normal reproduction in all organisms.

# Unit 7I: Vegetative Propagation

## Meaning

Vegetative propagation is asexual reproduction in plants using vegetative parts such as:

- roots;
- stems;
- leaves;
- buds.

## Examples

| Plant/Method | Reproductive Part or Method |
|---|---|
| Sugarcane | Stem cutting |
| Rose | Cutting/layering/grafting |
| Grapes | Cutting/grafting |
| Banana | Vegetative part, often sucker |
| Orange | Grafting |
| Jasmine | Layering/cutting |
| Bryophyllum | Leaf buds |
| Potato | Stem tuber buds or "eyes" |
| Money plant | Stem cutting with node/leaf region |

## Bryophyllum

Bryophyllum leaves have buds in notches along the leaf margin.

When these buds fall on soil, they can grow into new plants.

## Potato Eyes

Potato has buds called eyes.

Pieces with buds can form new shoots and roots.

Pieces without buds usually do not form new plants.

## Money Plant Activity

Money plant cuttings with a node/leaf region can grow new roots and leaves in water.

Pieces taken between nodes usually do not grow well.

Conclusion:

Nodes/buds are important for vegetative propagation.

## Advantages of Vegetative Propagation

Vegetative propagation is practised because:

- plants grow faster than from seeds;
- flowers and fruits may appear earlier;
- seedless plants can be propagated;
- desirable traits of parent are preserved;
- many similar plants can be produced;
- plants like banana, orange, rose, and jasmine can be propagated efficiently.

## Tissue Culture

Tissue culture is a modern method of vegetative propagation.

Process:

1. Tissue or cells are taken from growing tip.
2. Cells are placed in artificial nutrient medium.
3. Cells divide to form callus.
4. Callus is transferred to medium with hormones.
5. Plantlets develop.
6. Plantlets are transferred to soil.

Advantages:

- many plants from one parent;
- disease-free conditions;
- useful for ornamental and crop plants.

# Unit 7J: Spore Formation

## Rhizopus/Bread Mould

Bread mould has thread-like hyphae.

The reproductive structures are sporangia.

Sporangia contain spores.

## Spore Formation Process

1. Sporangia develop on hyphae.
2. Spores form inside sporangia.
3. Spores are released.
4. Spores disperse by air.
5. Under moist suitable conditions, spores germinate into new Rhizopus.

## Benefits of Spores

Spores are useful because:

- they have thick protective walls;
- they survive unfavourable conditions;
- they spread easily;
- they can remain inactive until conditions become suitable;
- many spores are produced at once.

# Unit 7K: Sexual Reproduction

## Meaning

Sexual reproduction involves two parents or two gametes.

Male and female gametes fuse to form a zygote.

## Why Sexual Reproduction Produces More Variation

In sexual reproduction:

- DNA from two individuals combines;
- each individual already carries accumulated variations;
- new combinations form in offspring;
- meiosis creates gametes with half the chromosome number;
- fertilisation restores the chromosome number.

## Why Chromosome Number Must Be Halved

If gametes had the full chromosome number, the zygote would have double the DNA every generation.

This would disturb cell functioning.

Meiosis solves this by producing gametes with half the chromosome number.

## Gamete Specialisation

| Gamete | General Feature |
|---|---|
| Male gamete | Smaller and motile in many organisms. |
| Female gamete | Larger and contains stored food in many organisms. |

## Zygote

The zygote forms by fusion of male and female gametes.

It divides repeatedly and develops into a new organism.

# Unit 7M: Pollination and Fertilisation

## Pollination

Pollination is transfer of pollen from anther to stigma.

## Self-Pollination

Pollen is transferred to stigma of the same flower or another flower of the same plant.

## Cross-Pollination

Pollen is transferred from one flower to a flower on another plant of the same species.

Agents:

- wind;
- water;
- insects;
- birds;
- other animals.

## Fertilisation in Flower

After pollen lands on a suitable stigma:

1. Pollen grain germinates.
2. Pollen tube grows through the style.
3. Pollen tube reaches ovary.
4. Male gamete reaches ovule.
5. Male gamete fuses with egg cell.
6. Zygote forms.

## After Fertilisation

| Structure | Develops Into |
|---|---|
| Zygote | Embryo |
| Ovule | Seed |
| Ovary | Fruit |

Petals, sepals, stamens, style, and stigma often shrivel and fall off.

## Germination

The seed contains the embryo.

Under suitable conditions, the embryo grows into a seedling.

This process is called germination.

## Pollination vs Fertilisation

| Feature | Pollination | Fertilisation |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma | Fusion of male and female gametes |
| Location | Stigma | Ovule inside ovary |
| Result | Pollen reaches female part | Zygote forms |
| Type of process | Transfer process | Fusion process |

# Unit 7N: Human Puberty and Sexual Maturation

## Puberty

Puberty is the period during adolescence when reproductive tissues mature.

It does not occur at the exact same age for everyone.

Changes happen slowly over months and years.

## Changes Common to Boys and Girls

Common changes:

- increase in height;
- hair growth in armpits and genital area;
- thinner hair on arms and legs;
- skin becomes oilier;
- pimples may appear;
- increased awareness of body and emotions.

## Changes in Girls

Changes include:

- breast size increases;
- skin around nipples darkens;
- menstruation begins;
- hips may broaden;
- reproductive organs mature.

## Changes in Boys

Changes include:

- facial hair begins to grow;
- voice cracks and deepens;
- penis and testes mature;
- sperm production begins;
- shoulders may broaden.

## Why Puberty Happens After Childhood Growth

During early growth, body resources are mainly used to increase body size.

As general body growth slows, reproductive tissues begin to mature.

# Unit 7O: Male Reproductive System

## Main Parts

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Testes | Produce sperms and testosterone. |
| Scrotum | Holds testes outside abdominal cavity at lower temperature. |
| Vas deferens | Carries sperms from testes. |
| Seminal vesicles | Add fluid that nourishes sperms. |
| Prostate gland | Adds fluid that helps sperm transport. |
| Urethra | Common passage for urine and semen. |
| Penis | Transfers semen into female reproductive tract. |

## Why Testes Are Outside the Abdomen

Sperm formation needs a temperature lower than normal body temperature.

The scrotum keeps testes outside the abdominal cavity and helps maintain suitable temperature.

## Testosterone

Testosterone:

- regulates sperm formation;
- causes puberty changes in boys;
- supports development of male reproductive organs and secondary sexual characters.

## Sperm Structure

Sperms are tiny cells containing:

- genetic material;
- a long tail for movement.

# Unit 7P: Female Reproductive System

## Main Parts

| Part | Function |
|---|---|
| Ovaries | Produce eggs and female hormones. |
| Oviducts/Fallopian tubes | Carry egg from ovary to uterus; site of fertilisation. |
| Uterus | Supports development of embryo/foetus. |
| Cervix | Lower opening of uterus into vagina. |
| Vagina | Receives sperms and acts as birth passage. |

## Eggs

At birth, ovaries already contain thousands of immature eggs.

After puberty, usually one egg matures and is released by one ovary each month.

## Fertilisation in Humans

1. Sperms enter through vagina.
2. Sperms travel upward.
3. Egg is released from ovary into fallopian tube.
4. Fertilisation may occur in fallopian tube.
5. Zygote forms.
6. Zygote divides to form embryo.
7. Embryo implants in uterine lining.

## Implantation

Implantation is attachment of the embryo to the lining of uterus.

The uterus lining is thick, spongy, and blood-rich to support the embryo.

# Unit 7Q: Placenta, Pregnancy, and Birth

## Placenta

Placenta is a special tissue between embryo and mother.

It is embedded in the uterine wall.

## Placental Structure

The embryo side has villi.

The mother side has blood spaces around the villi.

This creates a large surface area for exchange.

## Functions of Placenta

Placenta allows:

- glucose to pass from mother to embryo;
- oxygen to pass from mother to embryo;
- wastes from embryo to pass into mother's blood;
- nourishment of developing embryo.

## Pregnancy Duration

Development of the child inside mother's body takes about nine months.

Birth occurs due to rhythmic contractions of uterine muscles.

# Unit 7R: Menstruation

## What Happens If Egg Is Not Fertilised?

The egg lives for about one day after release.

If fertilisation does not occur:

- thick uterine lining is no longer needed;
- lining breaks down slowly;
- blood and mucus pass out through vagina.

This is menstruation.

## Menstrual Cycle

Menstruation occurs roughly once a month.

It usually lasts about two to eight days.

## Why Uterus Prepares Every Month

The uterus prepares to receive and nourish a possible embryo.

If pregnancy does not occur, the prepared lining is removed.

# Unit 7S: Reproductive Health

## Meaning

Reproductive health means physical, emotional, and social well-being in matters related to reproduction.

Sexual maturity does not automatically mean a person is physically, mentally, emotionally, or socially ready for pregnancy or parenting.

People may face pressure from:

- friends;

Sexually transmitted infections can be bacterial or viral.

Examples:

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|

| Viral | Warts, HIV-AIDS |

Using a condom during sex helps reduce transmission of many sexually transmitted infections.

## Pregnancy and Health

Pregnancy makes major physical and emotional demands on the body and mind.

# Unit 7T: Contraception

## Meaning

Contraception means methods used to prevent pregnancy.

## Barrier Methods

Barrier methods prevent sperm from reaching the egg.

Examples:

- condom worn on penis;
- barrier coverings used in vagina.

Condoms also help reduce the risk of STIs.

## Hormonal Methods

Oral pills change hormonal balance.

They may prevent egg release and fertilisation.

They should be used with medical guidance because they can have side effects.

## Intrauterine Devices

Devices such as loop or copper-T are placed in the uterus to prevent pregnancy.

They may cause side effects due to irritation of the uterus in some people.

## Surgical Methods

Surgical methods block gamete transport.

| Method | What Is Blocked | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Vasectomy | Vas deferens in male | Sperm transfer is prevented. |
| Tubectomy | Fallopian tubes in female | Egg cannot reach uterus; fertilisation is prevented. |

Surgical methods are effective long-term methods but require proper medical care.

## Copper-T and STI Protection

Copper-T helps prevent pregnancy.

It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.

Condoms are needed to reduce STI transmission risk.

## Sex-Selective Abortion and Sex Ratio

Illegal sex-selective abortion harms society and reduces the female-male sex ratio.

Prenatal sex determination for sex selection is prohibited by law.

A healthy society must value all children equally.

# Unit 7V: Solved In-Text Questions

## Question 1: What Is the Importance of DNA Copying in Reproduction?

## Answer

DNA copying is important because DNA carries the information for body design.

During reproduction, DNA must be copied so that offspring receive inherited information from the parent.

DNA copying also produces small variations, which are important for evolution and survival of species.

---

## Question 2: Why Is Variation Beneficial to the Species but Not Necessarily to the Individual?

## Answer

A variation may or may not help an individual in its current environment.

It may even be harmful to that individual.

But variation is beneficial to the species because if the environment changes, some individuals may have variations that help them survive and reproduce.

Thus, variation improves survival chances of the population over time.

---

## Question 3: How Does Binary Fission Differ from Multiple Fission?

## Answer

| Feature | Binary Fission | Multiple Fission |
|---|---|---|
| Number of daughter cells | Two | Many |
| Example | Amoeba, Leishmania | Plasmodium |
| Process | One cell divides into two | Nucleus divides many times before cytoplasm divides into many cells |

---

## Question 4: How Is an Organism Benefited If It Reproduces Through Spores?

## Answer

Spore reproduction is useful because spores:

- have thick protective walls;
- survive dry or unfavourable conditions;
- disperse easily through air;
- are produced in large numbers;
- germinate when moisture and suitable conditions return.

---

## Question 5: Why Can More Complex Organisms Not Give Rise to New Individuals Through Regeneration?

## Answer

Complex organisms have highly specialised tissues and organs arranged in definite positions.

Their body is not a simple mass of similar cells.

Random pieces cannot usually reorganise into a complete body with all required organs.

Therefore, complex organisms use specialised reproductive organs and cells instead of regeneration for reproduction.

---

## Question 6: Why Is Vegetative Propagation Practised for Growing Some Types of Plants?

## Answer

Vegetative propagation is practised because:

- it produces plants genetically similar to parent;
- desirable traits are preserved;
- plants may bear flowers and fruits earlier;
- seedless plants can be grown;
- many plants can be produced quickly;
- it is useful for crops and ornamental plants such as banana, rose, jasmine, grapes, and orange.

---

## Question 7: Why Is DNA Copying Essential in Reproduction?

## Answer

DNA copying is essential because offspring must receive genetic information from the parent.

Without DNA copying, the new individual would not inherit body design instructions.

DNA copying also allows some variation, which is important for survival and evolution.

---

## Question 8: How Is Pollination Different from Fertilisation?

## Answer

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma.

Fertilisation is fusion of male gamete with female gamete to form a zygote.

Pollination happens before fertilisation.

---

## Question 9: What Is the Role of Seminal Vesicles and Prostate Gland?

## Answer

Seminal vesicles and prostate gland add fluid to sperms.

This fluid:

- makes sperm transport easier;
- provides nutrition to sperms;
- forms semen.

---

## Question 10: What Changes Are Seen in Girls at Puberty?

## Answer

Changes include:

- breast size increases;
- skin around nipples darkens;
- menstruation begins;
- hair grows in armpits and genital region;
- skin may become oily;
- pimples may appear;
- reproductive organs mature;
- body shape changes.

---

## Question 11: How Does the Embryo Get Nourishment Inside the Mother's Body?

## Answer

The embryo gets nourishment through the placenta.

Placenta has villi on the embryo side and blood spaces on the mother's side.

This large surface area allows glucose and oxygen to pass from mother's blood to embryo.

Embryo wastes pass into mother's blood through the placenta.

---

## Question 12: If a Woman Uses Copper-T, Will It Protect Her from Sexually Transmitted Diseases?

## Answer

No.

Copper-T helps prevent pregnancy, but it does not protect against sexually transmitted diseases.

Condoms are used to reduce the risk of STI transmission.

## Answer

Sexual reproduction has these advantages:

## Exercise 6: Why Does Menstruation Occur?

## Answer

Every month, the uterus prepares a thick, blood-rich lining to nourish a possible embryo.

If the egg is not fertilised, this lining is not needed.

The lining breaks down and passes out through the vagina as blood and mucus.

This process is menstruation.

## Exercise 9: How Are Modes of Reproduction Different in Unicellular and Multicellular Organisms?

## Answer

| Feature | Unicellular Organisms | Multicellular Organisms |
|---|---|---|
| Body design | Single cell | Many specialised cells, tissues, organs |
| Common modes | Fission, budding | Fragmentation, regeneration, vegetative propagation, sexual reproduction |
| Complexity | Simple division can create new individual | Reproduction often needs specialised cells/organs |
| Example | Amoeba by binary fission | Humans by sexual reproduction; plants by seeds/vegetative propagation |

Unicellular organisms often reproduce by simple cell division because one cell is the whole organism.

Multicellular organisms need more organised reproductive methods because their bodies contain specialised structures.

## Exercise 10: How Does Reproduction Help Provide Stability to Populations of Species?

## Answer

Reproduction maintains populations by producing new individuals.

DNA copying preserves body design features that allow organisms to survive in their niche.

At the same time, small variations help some individuals survive environmental changes.

Thus reproduction provides both:

- stability through similarity;
- adaptability through variation.

# Unit 7X: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying reproduction is essential for individual survival. | It is essential for species survival, not individual life maintenance. |
| Saying DNA copies are always perfectly identical. | DNA copying is mostly accurate but small variations occur. |
| Confusing binary and multiple fission. | Binary gives two cells; multiple gives many. |
| Saying regeneration and reproduction are always the same. | Regeneration can repair; only some organisms can form whole individuals from pieces. |
| Saying vegetative propagation produces very different plants. | It usually produces plants genetically similar to parent. |
| Confusing pollination and fertilisation. | Pollination is pollen transfer; fertilisation is gamete fusion. |
| Saying anther contains ovules. | Anther contains pollen grains; ovary contains ovules. |
| Saying fertilisation occurs in uterus in humans. | Fertilisation usually occurs in fallopian tube. |
| Saying Copper-T protects from STIs. | Copper-T prevents pregnancy, not STIs. Condoms reduce STI risk. |

## Best Exam Answer Pattern

For reproduction-mode questions:

1. Name the mode.
2. State number of parents.
3. Explain the steps.
4. Give one example.
5. Mention advantage if asked.

Example:

```text
In binary fission, one unicellular organism divides into two daughter organisms. The DNA duplicates, the nucleus divides, and the cytoplasm separates. Amoeba reproduces by binary fission.
```

## One-Page Chapter Summary

Reproduction produces new individuals and maintains species. DNA copying transfers body-design information from parent to offspring. Copying is mostly accurate but produces variation, which helps species survive environmental changes.

Asexual reproduction involves one parent and includes fission, budding, fragmentation, regeneration, vegetative propagation, and spore formation. It is fast and produces offspring similar to the parent.

Sexual reproduction combines DNA from two individuals through gamete fusion. Meiosis forms gametes with half the chromosome number, and fertilisation restores the chromosome number in the zygote.

In flowers, stamens produce pollen and pistil contains ovules. Pollination transfers pollen to stigma, and fertilisation forms zygote. Ovule becomes seed and ovary becomes fruit.

In humans, testes produce sperms and testosterone. Ovaries produce eggs and hormones. Fertilisation usually occurs in fallopian tube. The embryo implants in uterus and is nourished through placenta. If fertilisation does not occur, menstruation removes the uterine lining.

Reproductive health includes STI prevention, responsible contraception, pregnancy planning, and maintaining a healthy sex ratio. Condoms help reduce STI transmission; Copper-T prevents pregnancy but does not protect from STIs.
