## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Biodiversity supports human survival

Plants, animals, and microorganisms maintain the quality of air, water, and soil. Without them, agriculture, health, climate balance, and human survival are impossible.

### 2. Humans are only one part of the ecological system

Human beings often behave as if nature exists only for human use. The chapter corrects this idea. Humans are part of a wider ecological web and depend on it.

### 3. Forests are primary producers

Forests produce biomass and support other forms of life. They provide habitat, food, fuel, medicine, fibres, timber, biodiversity, and climate regulation.

### 4. Depletion is not only a biological problem

Loss of forests and wildlife also causes loss of culture, livelihood, health, food security, and community identity, especially among forest-dependent people.

### 5. Conservation must include local communities

Government laws are important, but conservation becomes stronger when local people participate because they understand local ecosystems and depend on them directly.

# Unit 2A: Biodiversity and Human Life

## Core Idea

Biodiversity means the variety of living organisms on Earth. It includes plants, animals, microorganisms, bacteria, fungi, forests, grasslands, wetlands, rivers, and the relationships among them.

The chapter begins by reminding us that humans share the planet with millions of other living beings, from microorganisms and lichens to banyan trees, elephants, and blue whales.

## Why Biodiversity Matters

Biodiversity is important because it supports the basic systems of life.

| Life-Support Function | How Biodiversity Helps |
| --- | --- |
| Air | Plants and microorganisms help maintain air quality. |
| Water | Forests and wetlands help recharge water and filter pollutants. |
| Soil | Microorganisms and decomposers help form fertile soil. |
| Food | Crops, wild plants, pollinators, fisheries, and livestock depend on biodiversity. |
| Medicine | Many medicines come from plants, animals, and microorganisms. |
| Climate | Forests absorb carbon and regulate temperature and rainfall. |
| Livelihood | Forest-dependent communities use forest produce, grazing, fishing, and medicinal plants. |
| Culture | Many communities connect identity, rituals, and traditions with nature. |

## Forests as Primary Producers

Forests are called primary producers because plants convert solar energy into biomass through photosynthesis. This biomass supports herbivores, carnivores, decomposers, and human beings.

In simple terms:

**Forests support food chains, water cycles, soil fertility, climate balance, and human livelihoods.**

## India and Biodiversity

India is one of the world's richest countries in terms of biological diversity.

Important facts from the chapter:

- India has nearly 8 percent of the total number of species in the world.
- Over 81,000 species of fauna are found in India.
- Over 47,000 species of flora are found in India.
- About 15,000 flowering plant species are endemic to India.

## What "Endemic" Means Here

An endemic species is native to a particular region and not naturally found everywhere. For example, some species found in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands or Arunachal Pradesh may be restricted to those areas.

## Answer Frame: Importance of Biodiversity

Biodiversity is important because plants, animals, and microorganisms maintain the quality of air, water, and soil. Forests act as primary producers and support food chains. Biodiversity provides food, medicines, fuel, fodder, timber, livelihoods, and cultural value. Human beings depend on biodiversity for survival, so its loss threatens life-support systems.

# Unit 2C: Threatened Species and Vanishing Forests

## Threatened Biodiversity in India

The chapter mentions serious signs of decline:

- At least 10 percent of India's recorded wild flora is on the threatened list.
- About 20 percent of India's mammals are threatened.
- 79 species of mammals, 44 birds, 15 reptiles, and 3 amphibians are threatened.
- Nearly 1,500 plant species are considered endangered.
- Flowering plants and vertebrate animals have become extinct at a rate much higher than the expected natural rate.

These facts show that biodiversity loss is not a small or isolated issue. It is a national ecological crisis.

## Vanishing Forests

The chapter states that India's forest and tree cover was estimated at 79.42 million hectares, which was 24.16 percent of the total geographical area.

It also gives these categories:

| Forest Cover Type | Share Mentioned in Chapter |
| --- | --- |
| Dense forest | 12.2 percent |
| Open forest | 9.14 percent |
| Mangrove | 0.14 percent |

The State of Forest Report 2015 mentioned an increase in dense forest cover by 3,775 sq km since 2013, but the chapter explains that this increase was linked to conservation measures, management interventions, and plantations.

## Important Understanding

An increase in plantation does not always mean natural forest recovery. A natural forest has multiple species, layers, habitats, and ecological relationships. A plantation may have only one or a few species and may not support the same biodiversity.

# Unit 2D: Causes of Depletion of Flora and Fauna

## Main Causes

The depletion of forests and wildlife has many causes.

| Cause | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Agricultural expansion | Forests are cleared to create farmland. |
| Colonial forest policies | Forests were exploited for railways, commercial forestry, agriculture, mining, and revenue. |
| Shifting cultivation | In some tribal belts, slash-and-burn agriculture has degraded forest areas. |
| Large development projects | River valley projects, dams, roads, and industries clear forests. |
| Mining | Mining destroys habitats, creates waste, and blocks animal movement. |
| Habitat destruction | Species lose their homes and breeding grounds. |
| Hunting and poaching | Animals are killed for trade, skins, bones, meat, or sport. |
| Over-exploitation | Excessive extraction of timber, medicinal plants, fuel, fodder, and other products. |
| Pollution | Air, water, and soil pollution weaken ecosystems. |
| Poisoning | Chemicals and toxins kill plants and animals. |
| Forest fires | Fires destroy vegetation, animals, nests, seeds, and soil organisms. |
| Unequal consumption | Rich and powerful groups consume more resources and cause greater ecological damage. |

## Colonial Period

The chapter says that great damage was inflicted on Indian forests during the colonial period because of:

- expansion of railways
- expansion of agriculture
- commercial forestry
- scientific forestry
- mining activities

## After Independence

Even after Independence, agricultural expansion continued to be a major cause of forest depletion.

Between 1951 and 1980, over 26,200 sq km of forest area was converted into agricultural land in India.

## Shifting Cultivation

Substantial parts of tribal belts, especially in north-eastern and central India, were deforested or degraded by shifting cultivation, also called jhum.

The exam point is not to blame tribal communities simplistically. The broader issue is pressure on land, shortened fallow cycles, poverty, forest policy, and lack of alternatives.

## Large Development Projects

Since 1951, over 5,000 sq km of forest was cleared for river valley projects.

The chapter gives the example of the Narmada Sagar Project in Madhya Pradesh, which would inundate 40,000 hectares of forest.

## Mining

Mining is a major cause of deforestation. The chapter gives the example of Buxa Tiger Reserve in West Bengal, which is threatened by dolomite mining.

Mining harms forests by:

- destroying vegetation
- disturbing animal habitats
- blocking migration routes
- creating dust and waste
- polluting water

The chapter specifically mentions that dolomite mining in Buxa has disturbed the habitat of several species and blocked migration routes, including that of the great Indian elephant.

## Enrichment Plantation and Monoculture

Some colonial and commercial forest policies promoted a few commercially valuable species. This was called enrichment plantation, but it often damaged natural forests.

Examples:

| Region | Plantation Problem |
| --- | --- |
| South India | Teak monoculture damaged natural forest. |
| Himalayas | Chir pine plantations replaced Himalayan oak and rhododendron forests. |

## Why Monoculture Is Harmful

Monoculture means growing one species over a large area. It reduces biodiversity because:

- many native species are removed
- animals lose varied habitats
- soil quality changes
- forest becomes more vulnerable to disease and fire
- local communities lose diverse forest produce

# Unit 2E: Case Studies from the Chapter

## Case Study 1: Asiatic Cheetah

The cheetah is described as the world's fastest land mammal. It can run at about 112 km per hour.

The Asiatic cheetah was once found in parts of Asia and Africa. In India, it declined because of:

- loss of habitat
- decline of prey
- hunting
- pressure on grassland ecosystems

The chapter states that the species was declared extinct in India in 1952.

## Key Identification

The cheetah is often confused with the leopard. Its distinguishing marks include long tear-shaped lines from the corners of the eyes toward the mouth.

## Case Study 2: Himalayan Yew

The Himalayan yew is a medicinal plant found in Himachal Pradesh and Arunachal Pradesh.

A chemical compound called taxol is extracted from its bark, needles, twigs, and roots. Taxol has been used in cancer treatment.

The plant is under threat because of over-exploitation. Thousands of yew trees have dried up in parts of Himachal Pradesh and Arunachal Pradesh.

## Why the Himalayan Yew Example Matters

It shows that plants can be endangered too, not only large animals. It also shows that medicinal value can become a reason for over-exploitation if harvesting is not regulated.

# Unit 2F: Environmental Destruction and Social Inequality

## Biological Loss and Cultural Loss

The chapter makes a very important point:

**The destruction of forests and wildlife is not just a biological issue.**

It is also connected with the loss of cultural diversity.

Many indigenous and forest-dependent communities depend on forests for:

- food
- drinking water
- medicine
- fuel
- fodder
- livelihood
- culture
- spirituality
- rituals
- identity

When forests are destroyed, these communities lose not just resources, but also ways of life.

## Why Poor People Suffer More

Poor communities often depend directly on forests, land, and water for survival. When these resources are degraded, they have fewer alternatives.

Environmental destruction can lead to:

- loss of livelihood
- food insecurity
- longer work hours
- health problems
- displacement
- poverty
- droughts and floods

The chapter says poverty can be a direct outcome of environmental destruction.

## Why Women Are Affected More

In many societies, women are responsible for collecting:

- fuel
- fodder
- water
- minor forest produce

When forests and water sources degrade, women may have to walk long distances, sometimes more than 10 km, to collect basic resources. This increases their workload, causes health problems, reduces time for children and household care, and deepens social hardship.

## Unequal Consumption

The chapter challenges the idea that environmental degradation is caused only by population growth in poor countries.

It gives a comparison:

- An average American consumes many times more resources than an average Somalian.
- The richest sections of Indian society cause more ecological damage through high consumption than the poorest sections.

## Important Question

For environmental justice, we must ask:

**Who is consuming what, from where, and how much?**

This question helps us see that environmental responsibility is not equal for everyone. Those who consume more must take greater responsibility.

# Unit 2G: Conservation of Forest and Wildlife in India

## Why Conservation Is Necessary

Conservation is essential because:

1. Forests and wildlife are declining rapidly.
2. They maintain ecological balance.
3. They support water, air, and soil systems.
4. They preserve genetic diversity.
5. They support agriculture, fisheries, medicine, and livelihoods.
6. They protect cultural and community life.

## Conservation Preserves Life-Support Systems

| Life-Support System | Role of Conservation |
| --- | --- |
| Water | Forests recharge groundwater, protect streams, and reduce floods. |
| Air | Trees absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen. |
| Soil | Roots bind soil and prevent erosion. |
| Genetic diversity | Different species and varieties help breeding, adaptation, and resilience. |
| Agriculture | Traditional crop varieties and pollinators support farming. |
| Fisheries | Aquatic biodiversity supports fish breeding and survival. |

## Wildlife Protection Act, 1972

The Indian Wildlife Protection Act was implemented in 1972.

Its main features:

- legal protection to habitats
- ban on hunting many species
- restrictions on trade in wildlife
- protection of endangered species
- publication of an all-India list of protected species
- creation and strengthening of national parks and wildlife sanctuaries

## Animal Protection Projects

The government started projects to protect threatened animals, including:

- tiger
- one-horned rhinoceros
- Kashmir stag or hangul
- freshwater crocodile
- saltwater crocodile
- gharial
- Asiatic lion
- Indian elephant
- black buck
- great Indian bustard
- snow leopard

## Conservation Is Now Wider

Earlier conservation often focused on big animals. The chapter says conservation is now moving toward broader biodiversity protection.

This includes:

- insects
- butterflies
- moths
- beetles
- dragonflies
- plants

In 1991, plants were added to the protected list for the first time, starting with six species.

# Unit 2H: Project Tiger

## Why Tigers Matter

The tiger is a key species in the faunal web. Protecting tigers also protects large habitats and many other species living in those habitats.

## Launch

Project Tiger was launched in 1973.

It became one of the best-known wildlife conservation programmes in the world.

## Why It Was Needed

The tiger population had fallen sharply. The chapter says that in 1973, the tiger population had dwindled to 1,827 from an estimated 55,000 at the turn of the century.

## Major Threats to Tigers

| Threat | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Poaching | Tigers are killed illegally for skins, bones, and body parts. |
| Illegal trade | Tiger parts are traded for profit, including in traditional medicine markets. |
| Shrinking habitat | Forests are cleared or fragmented. |
| Depletion of prey | If deer and other prey decline, tigers cannot survive. |
| Growing human population | Expanding settlements, farming, and roads increase pressure on tiger habitats. |

## Tiger Population Figures Mentioned

| 1973 | 1,827 |
| 1985 | 4,002 |
| 1989 | 4,334 |
| 1993 | 3,600 |

## Tiger Reserves Mentioned

| Reserve | State |
| --- | --- |
| Corbett National Park | Uttarakhand |
| Sunderbans National Park | West Bengal |
| Bandhavgarh National Park | Madhya Pradesh |
| Sariska Wildlife Sanctuary | Rajasthan |

## Deeper Meaning of Project Tiger

Project Tiger is not only about saving one animal. It is also about preserving large biotypes, forests, prey species, water systems, and biodiversity.

## Answer Frame: Project Tiger

Project Tiger was launched in 1973 to protect tigers from poaching, habitat loss, prey depletion, and illegal trade. It aimed to save tigers and preserve their habitats. Tiger reserves such as Corbett, Sunderbans, Bandhavgarh, Sariska, Manas, and Periyar became important conservation areas. The project is significant because tiger conservation also protects many other species and forest ecosystems.

# Unit 2I: Types and Distribution of Forest and Wildlife Resources

In India, much of the forest and wildlife resources are owned or managed by the government through the Forest Department or other departments.

Forests are classified into three categories:

1. Reserved forests
2. Protected forests
3. Unclassed forests

## Forest Categories

| Category | Meaning | Importance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reserved Forests | More than half of total forest land; considered most valuable for conservation. | Strongest conservation value. |
| Protected Forests | Almost one-third of total forest area; protected from further depletion. | Prevents further damage. |
| Unclassed Forests | Other forests and wastelands belonging to government, private individuals, or communities. | Often managed locally or outside strict reserved/protected categories. |

## Permanent Forest Estates

Reserved and protected forests are also called permanent forest estates.

They are maintained for:

- timber production
- other forest produce
- protective functions
- conservation

## Regional Distribution

| Region or State | Forest Category Pattern |
| --- | --- |
| Madhya Pradesh | Largest area under permanent forests; about 75 percent of its total forest area. |
| Jammu and Kashmir, Andhra Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, Maharashtra | Large percentage of reserved forests. |
| Bihar, Haryana, Punjab, Himachal Pradesh, Odisha, Rajasthan | Large share under protected forests. |
| North-eastern states and parts of Gujarat | High percentage of unclassed forests, often managed by local communities. |

## Exam Tip

If asked to match the forest categories:

- Reserved forests = most valuable for conservation.
- Protected forests = protected from further depletion.
- Unclassed forests = other forests and wastelands belonging to government, private individuals, or communities.

# Unit 2J: Community and Conservation

## Core Idea

Conservation is not new in India. Many communities have protected forests, animals, water sources, and sacred landscapes for generations.

The chapter argues that local communities should be at the centre of natural resource management.

## Why Communities Matter

Local communities matter because they:

- live close to forests
- depend on forests for livelihood
- know local plants and animals
- have traditional conservation practices
- suffer directly when forests are destroyed
- can monitor illegal activities
- can restore degraded land through collective work

## Example 1: Sariska Tiger Reserve

In Sariska Tiger Reserve, Rajasthan, villagers fought against mining by citing the Wildlife Protection Act.

This example shows that local people can use laws to protect forests and wildlife.

## Example 2: Bhairodev Dakav Sonchuri

In Alwar district of Rajasthan, inhabitants of five villages declared 1,200 hectares of forest as Bhairodev Dakav Sonchuri.

They created their own rules:

- no hunting
- protection of wildlife
- prevention of outside encroachment

This is a strong example of community-led conservation.

## Example 3: Sacred Groves

Sacred groves are forest patches protected because of religious and cultural beliefs.

They are often called forests of gods and goddesses.

In these areas:

- trees are not cut
- animals are protected
- human interference is restricted
- rare species may survive

## Sacred Trees and Community Beliefs

| Community or Region | Protected Tree or Practice |
| --- | --- |
| Mundas and Santhals of Chota Nagpur | Worship mahua and kadamba trees |
| Tribals of Odisha and Bihar | Worship tamarind and mango trees during weddings |
| Many Indian communities | Consider peepal and banyan sacred |
| Temples in many regions | Macaques and langurs are fed and protected |
| Bishnoi villages in Rajasthan | Blackbuck, chinkara, nilgai, and peacocks are protected |

## Example 4: Chipko Movement

The Chipko movement in the Himalayas resisted deforestation. It also showed that community afforestation with indigenous species can be successful.

Key points:

- people resisted tree cutting
- women played an important role
- local needs and ecology were central
- indigenous species were valued

## Example 5: Beej Bachao Andolan and Navdanya

Farmers' and citizens' groups such as Beej Bachao Andolan in Tehri and Navdanya promoted:

- traditional seeds
- diversified crop production
- ecological farming
- reduced dependence on synthetic chemicals
- economically viable conservation-based agriculture

## Example 6: Joint Forest Management

Joint Forest Management, or JFM, involves local communities in the management and restoration of degraded forests.

It formally began in 1988 when Odisha passed the first resolution for Joint Forest Management.

## How JFM Works

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Local institutions | Village-level groups protect forests. |
| Forest department role | Forest land remains under department management. |
| Community role | Local people protect degraded forest land. |
| Benefits | Communities receive non-timber forest produce and a share in timber after successful protection. |

## Core Lesson

The chapter's key lesson is that local communities must be involved in natural resource management. Conservation cannot succeed fully if people who live with forests are excluded from decision-making.

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. What is biodiversity? Why is biodiversity important for human lives?

Biodiversity is the variety of living organisms, including plants, animals, microorganisms, and their relationships in ecosystems. It is important because it maintains the quality of air, water, and soil. It supports agriculture, forests, fisheries, medicines, food, fuel, fodder, and livelihoods. Human beings depend on biodiversity for survival, so its loss threatens life-support systems.

## Q2. How have human activities affected the depletion of flora and fauna?

Human activities have depleted flora and fauna through agricultural expansion, deforestation, mining, large development projects, habitat destruction, hunting, poaching, pollution, forest fires, and over-exploitation of forest products. Colonial forest policies and commercial plantations also damaged natural forests. Overconsumption by richer groups and unequal access to resources have increased ecological damage.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Describe how communities have conserved and protected forests and wildlife in India.

Communities in India have protected forests and wildlife through local rules, sacred beliefs, movements, and participation in forest management. In Sariska Tiger Reserve, villagers fought against mining by using the Wildlife Protection Act. In Alwar district of Rajasthan, five villages declared 1,200 hectares of forest as Bhairodev Dakav Sonchuri and made rules against hunting and encroachment.

Sacred groves have preserved forest patches because many communities consider them the forests of gods and goddesses. The Mundas and Santhals worship mahua and kadamba trees, while many communities protect peepal and banyan trees. Bishnoi villages in Rajasthan protect blackbuck, chinkara, nilgai, and peacocks.

The Chipko movement resisted deforestation in the Himalayas and promoted community afforestation with indigenous species. Beej Bachao Andolan and Navdanya promoted traditional seeds and ecological farming. Joint Forest Management involves local communities in protecting and restoring degraded forests in return for benefits such as non-timber forest produce and a share in timber after successful protection.

## Q2. Write a note on good practices toward conserving forests and wildlife.

Good conservation practices include protecting natural forests, preventing hunting and poaching, controlling illegal trade in wildlife, regulating mining and development projects, restoring degraded forests, and involving local communities in decision-making. Sacred groves, community forest rules, Chipko movement, Beej Bachao Andolan, Navdanya, and Joint Forest Management show that people can conserve nature effectively.

Government measures are also important. The Wildlife Protection Act of 1972 protects habitats and species, restricts trade, and bans hunting of protected species. National parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and projects such as Project Tiger help conserve threatened animals and their habitats. Conservation should be people-centric, environment-friendly, and economically rewarding.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing biodiversity means only animals. | Biodiversity includes plants, animals, microorganisms, and ecosystems. |
| Ignoring microorganisms. | Microorganisms help soil fertility, decomposition, and ecological balance. |
| Confusing endangered and extinct. | Endangered species still exist but are in danger; extinct species are no longer found. |
| Confusing endemic and endangered. | Endemic means found only in a particular area; it may or may not be endangered. |
| Writing only population growth as the cause of environmental damage. | The chapter stresses unequal consumption and responsibility. |
| Treating conservation as only a government task. | Community participation is a major theme. |
| Writing plantations are always equal to forests. | Plantations may lack the biodiversity of natural forests. |
| Forgetting examples. | This chapter is example-heavy; answers need specific examples. |
