## How to Understand This Chapter

Manufacturing means producing goods in large quantities after processing raw materials into more valuable products.

Examples:

- wood becomes paper
- sugarcane becomes sugar
- iron ore becomes iron and steel
- bauxite becomes aluminium
- yarn becomes cloth

This chapter explains:

1. Why manufacturing is important for development.
2. Why industries are located in particular places.
3. How industries are classified.
4. What the major industries of India are.
5. How industries pollute the environment.
6. How industrial pollution can be controlled.

The central idea is:

**Industrial development is necessary, but it must be efficient, competitive, employment-generating, regionally balanced, and environmentally responsible.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Manufacturing adds value

Manufacturing increases the value of raw materials. Cotton becomes cloth, sugarcane becomes sugar, iron ore becomes steel, and bauxite becomes aluminium.

### 2. Manufacturing supports agriculture

Industries provide farmers with irrigation pumps, fertilizers, insecticides, pesticides, plastic pipes, machines, and tools. Agro-industries also buy agricultural raw materials.

### 3. Industry reduces pressure on agriculture

Manufacturing creates jobs in the secondary and tertiary sectors, reducing dependence on farming.

### 4. Location is based on least cost

Industries locate where raw materials, labour, power, capital, transport, market, and services are available at low cost.

### 5. Industry and urbanisation go together

Industries create towns and cities. Cities provide markets, transport, banking, insurance, labour, and business services.

### 6. Pollution control is part of development

Industrial growth cannot be called successful if it destroys air, water, land, health, and livelihoods.

# Unit 6A: Meaning and Importance of Manufacturing

## Meaning

Manufacturing is the production of goods in large quantities after processing raw materials into more valuable products.

## Examples

| Raw Material | Manufactured Product |
| --- | --- |
| Wood | Paper |
| Sugarcane | Sugar |
| Iron ore | Iron and steel |
| Bauxite | Aluminium |
| Cotton fibre | Yarn and cloth |
| Petroleum | Petrochemicals, plastics, synthetic fibres |

## Secondary Sector

Manufacturing belongs to the secondary sector because it transforms primary materials into finished goods.

Examples of workers in the secondary sector:

- steel factory workers
- textile workers
- bakery workers
- automobile workers
- brewery workers
- machine operators

## Why Manufacturing Is Important

Manufacturing is considered the backbone of development because:

| Importance | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Modernises agriculture | Provides machines, tools, fertilizers, pesticides, pumps, and pipes. |
| Reduces dependence on agriculture | Creates jobs in secondary and tertiary sectors. |
| Reduces poverty and unemployment | Industrial jobs expand non-farm employment. |
| Reduces regional disparities | Public sector industries were set up in tribal and backward areas. |
| Expands trade | Manufactured exports increase trade and commerce. |
| Earns foreign exchange | Export of manufactured goods brings foreign currency. |
| Adds value | Raw materials become higher-value finished goods. |
| Supports national prosperity | Countries that process raw materials into finished goods become more prosperous. |

## Agriculture and Industry Move Together

Agriculture and industry are not separate from each other.

Industries help agriculture by providing:

- irrigation pumps
- fertilizers
- insecticides
- pesticides
- plastic and PVC pipes
- machines
- tools

Agriculture helps industries by supplying:

- cotton
- jute
- sugarcane
- oilseeds
- rubber
- tea
- coffee
- food for workers

## Competitiveness in Globalisation

In a globalised world, self-sufficiency is not enough. Indian manufactured goods must match international quality so they can compete in global markets.

# Unit 6B: Contribution of Industry to the National Economy

## Manufacturing Share

The chapter states that the share of manufacturing had stagnated at about 17 percent of GDP, within the broader industry sector.

This was lower than many East Asian economies, where manufacturing contributed around 25 to 35 percent.

## Growth Target

The desired manufacturing growth rate was about 12 percent over the next decade.

Since 2003, manufacturing again grew at about 9 to 10 percent per annum.

## NMCC

The National Manufacturing Competitiveness Council was set up to improve manufacturing productivity and competitiveness.

## Why Manufacturing Growth Matters

Higher manufacturing growth can:

- create employment
- increase exports
- improve technology
- strengthen infrastructure
- reduce pressure on agriculture
- support urban and rural markets
- raise national income

# Unit 6C: Industrial Location

## Core Idea

Industrial location depends on least cost. Industries try to locate where inputs and services are available cheaply or can be arranged efficiently.

## Factors Affecting Industrial Location

| Factor | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Raw material | Bulky or perishable raw material encourages location near source. |
| Labour | Skilled and cheap labour reduces production cost. |
| Capital | Investment is needed for land, machinery, buildings, and technology. |
| Power | Industries need reliable energy. |
| Market | Nearby market reduces transport cost and helps sales. |
| Transport | Roads, railways, ports, and waterways move raw materials and goods. |
| Water | Many industries need water for processing, cooling, and cleaning. |
| Government policy | Policies can promote industries in certain regions. |
| Specialised labour | Some industries need trained workers. |
| Banking and insurance | Financial services support industrial operations. |

## Industry and Urbanisation

After an industry starts, urbanisation often follows. Industries are frequently located in or near cities because cities provide:

- markets
- labour
- transport
- banking
- insurance
- consultants
- financial advice
- communication
- repair services

## Agglomeration Economies

Many industries come together in urban centres to use shared advantages. This is called agglomeration economy.

Examples of advantages:

- shared transport
- shared labour pool
- large market
- supplier networks
- banking and insurance
- repair and maintenance services
- communication facilities

## Pre-Independence Industrial Location

Before Independence, many manufacturing units were located in port cities such as:

- Mumbai
- Kolkata
- Chennai

This was because overseas trade was important. These cities became industrial urban centres surrounded by agricultural hinterlands.

## Cotton Textiles

## Historical Background

In ancient India, cotton textiles were produced through hand spinning and handloom weaving.

After the 18th century, powerlooms came into use.

During the colonial period, traditional Indian textile industries suffered because they could not compete with mill-made cloth from England.

## First Successful Cotton Textile Mill

The first successful textile mill was established in Mumbai in 1854.

## Why Cotton Textile Industry Concentrated in Maharashtra and Gujarat

Early cotton textile mills were located in Maharashtra and Gujarat because of:

- availability of raw cotton
- market
- transport facilities
- port access
- labour
- moist climate

## Spinning and Weaving

| Process | Pattern in India |
| --- | --- |
| Spinning | More centralised in Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Tamil Nadu |
| Weaving | Highly decentralised |

Weaving is decentralised because it allows traditional skills and designs in cotton, silk, zari, embroidery, and other fabrics.

## Khadi

Handspun khadi provides large-scale employment to weavers in their homes as a cottage industry.

Mahatma Gandhi emphasized spinning yarn and weaving khadi because it supported self-reliance, employment, dignity of labour, and resistance to imported British cloth.

## India's Strength and Weakness

| Strength | Weakness |
| --- | --- |
| World-class spinning | Weaving, knitting, and processing are fragmented and often low quality |
| Large share in world cotton yarn trade | Garment trade share is low |
| Second largest installed spindle capacity after China in the chapter's data | Many units cannot use high-quality yarn |
| Competitive spinning mills | Some garment manufacturers import fabric |

## Main Problems of Cotton Textile Industry

- erratic power supply
- need to upgrade machinery
- low labour productivity
- competition from synthetic fibre industry
- mismatch between high-quality yarn and weaker weaving/processing
- fragmented small units in weaving and processing

## Jute Textiles

## Importance

India is the largest producer of raw jute and jute goods. It stands second as an exporter after Bangladesh.

## First Jute Mill

The first jute mill was set up near Kolkata at Rishra in 1855.

## Location

Most jute mills are located in West Bengal along the Hugli River, in a narrow belt about 98 km long and 3 km wide.

## Why Jute Mills Are Located in the Hugli Basin

| Factor | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Proximity to jute-growing areas | Reduces raw material transport cost. |
| Inexpensive water transport | Hugli River helps movement. |
| Railway, roadway, and waterway network | Helps movement of raw material and finished goods. |
| Abundant water | Needed for processing raw jute. |
| Cheap labour | Available from West Bengal, Bihar, Odisha, and Uttar Pradesh. |
| Kolkata | Provides banking, insurance, and port facilities. |

## Partition Impact

After Partition in 1947, jute mills remained in India, but three-fourths of jute-producing areas went to Bangladesh, then East Pakistan.

## Challenges

Jute industry faces:

- competition from synthetic substitutes
- competition from Bangladesh, Brazil, Philippines, Egypt, and Thailand
- need for product diversification
- cost competition

## Opportunities

Demand can grow because:

- government policy mandates use of jute packaging
- jute is biodegradable
- global concern for environment-friendly materials is increasing
- National Jute Policy 2005 aimed to increase productivity, quality, farmer prices, and yield

## Main Export Markets

- USA
- Canada
- Ghana
- Saudi Arabia
- UK
- Australia

## Sugar Industry

## Importance

India is the second largest producer of sugar in the world and first in production of gur and khandsari.

## Raw Material

Sugarcane is the raw material.

## Why Sugar Mills Should Be Near Sugarcane Fields

Sugarcane is bulky and its sucrose content decreases after cutting. Therefore, mills should be located close to sugarcane-growing areas to reduce transport delay and loss of sucrose.

## Major Producing States

- Uttar Pradesh
- Bihar
- Maharashtra
- Karnataka
- Tamil Nadu
- Andhra Pradesh
- Gujarat
- Punjab
- Haryana
- Madhya Pradesh

## Shift to Southern and Western States

Sugar mills have shifted toward southern and western states, especially Maharashtra, because:

- cane has higher sucrose content
- cooler climate gives longer crushing season
- cooperatives are more successful

## Challenges

- seasonal nature of industry
- old and inefficient production methods
- transport delay
- need to maximise use of bagasse

## Why Cooperative Sector Suits Sugar

Sugar industry is seasonal and depends on cane supplied by farmers. Cooperative ownership allows farmers to pool resources, share profit, coordinate supply, and reduce exploitation.

## Iron and Steel Industry

## Why It Is a Basic Industry

Iron and steel is called a basic industry because almost all other industries depend on it for machinery, tools, and construction material.

Steel is used in:

- engineering goods
- construction material
- defence equipment
- medical equipment
- telephones
- scientific equipment
- consumer goods
- transport
- machines

Production and consumption of steel are often regarded as an index of a country's development.

## Why It Is a Heavy Industry

Iron and steel is a heavy industry because:

- raw materials are heavy and bulky
- finished goods are heavy
- transport costs are high

## Raw Materials

Iron ore, coking coal, and limestone are needed in the approximate ratio:

**4 : 2 : 1**

Small quantities of manganese are also needed to harden steel.

## Ideal Location

Steel plants should be located near raw materials and transport routes because raw materials and finished goods are heavy.

## Chhotanagpur Plateau Concentration

The Chhotanagpur plateau region has maximum concentration of iron and steel industries because of:

- low-cost iron ore
- high-grade raw materials nearby
- cheap labour
- growth potential in the home market
- proximity to coal and other minerals

## Integrated Steel Plant

An integrated steel plant is large and handles everything in one complex:

- raw material assembly
- steel making
- rolling
- shaping

## Mini Steel Plant

A mini steel plant is smaller and usually has:

- electric furnaces
- steel scrap
- sponge iron
- re-rollers

It produces mild and alloy steel of specific requirements.

## Problems of the Iron and Steel Industry

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| High cost and limited availability of coking coal | Raises production cost. |
| Low productivity of labour | Reduces competitiveness. |
| Irregular energy supply | Interrupts production. |
| Poor infrastructure | Transport and logistics become inefficient. |
| Need for quality improvement | India imports some good quality steel. |

## Recent Developments

Production capacity has increased due to:

- liberalisation
- foreign direct investment
- private entrepreneurship
- better technology
- policy support
- need for research and development

## SAIL

Most public sector steel undertakings market their steel through Steel Authority of India Limited.

## Importance

Aluminium smelting is the second most important metallurgical industry in India.

## Raw Material

## Properties of Aluminium

Aluminium is:

- light
- resistant to corrosion
- a good conductor of heat
- malleable
- strong when mixed with other metals

## Uses

- aircraft

## Important States

# Unit 6I: Industrial Pollution and Environmental Degradation

Industries contribute to development, but they also cause serious environmental pollution.

Industries cause four major types of pollution:

1. Air pollution
2. Water pollution
3. Land pollution
4. Noise pollution

---

## Air Pollution

## Causes

Air pollution is caused by:

- sulphur dioxide
- carbon monoxide
- dust
- smoke
- sprays
- mist
- toxic gases
- burning fossil fuels

## Sources

| Source | Pollutants |
| --- | --- |
| Chemical factories | Smoke and toxic gases |
| Paper factories | Smoke and waste emissions |
| Brick kilns | Smoke and particulate matter |
| Refineries | Gases and smoke |
| Smelting plants | Smoke and metallic particles |
| Thermal power plants | Fly ash, smoke, gases |
| Factories burning fossil fuels | Smoke and gases |

## Effects

Air pollution affects:

- human health
- animals
- plants
- buildings
- atmosphere

Toxic gas leaks can be extremely dangerous and have long-term effects.

---

## Water Pollution

## Causes

Water pollution is caused by organic and inorganic industrial wastes and effluents discharged into rivers and water bodies.

## Major Polluting Industries

- paper
- pulp
- chemical
- textile
- dyeing
- petroleum refineries
- tanneries
- electroplating

## Pollutants

- dyes
- detergents
- acids
- salts
- heavy metals such as lead and mercury
- pesticides
- fertilizers
- synthetic chemicals
- plastics
- rubber

## Thermal Pollution

Thermal pollution occurs when hot water from factories and thermal power plants is released into rivers and ponds before cooling.

This can harm aquatic life because sudden temperature changes reduce oxygen and disturb aquatic ecosystems.

## Nuclear Waste Risk

Waste from nuclear power plants, nuclear production, and weapon facilities can cause cancers, birth defects, and miscarriages.

---

# Unit 6J: Control of Environmental Degradation

## Controlling Water Pollution

Industrial water pollution can be reduced by:

1. minimising water use in processing
2. reusing and recycling water in successive stages
3. harvesting rainwater for industrial water needs
4. treating hot water before releasing it
5. treating effluents before discharge
6. legally regulating overdrawing of groundwater

## Effluent Treatment

Industrial effluents can be treated in three stages:

| Stage | Process |
| --- | --- |
| Primary treatment | Mechanical processes such as screening, grinding, flocculation, and sedimentation |
| Secondary treatment | Biological process |
| Tertiary treatment | Biological, chemical, and physical processes, including recycling of wastewater |

## Controlling Air Pollution

Air pollution can be reduced by using:

- electrostatic precipitators
- fabric filters
- scrubbers
- inertial separators
- smoke stacks
- oil or gas instead of coal
- cleaner fuels
- pollution monitoring

## Controlling Noise Pollution

Noise pollution can be reduced by:

- fitting generators with silencers
- redesigning machinery for lower noise
- using noise-absorbing materials
- using earplugs and earphones
- improving equipment efficiency

## NTPC Example

NTPC is a major power corporation in India with ISO certification for Environment Management System 14001.

NTPC has taken steps such as:

- optimum utilisation of equipment
- adopting latest techniques
- upgrading existing equipment
- minimising waste generation
- maximising ash utilisation
- providing green belts
- afforestation through special purpose vehicles
- ash pond management
- ash water recycling
- liquid waste management
- ecological monitoring
- online database management for power stations

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. What is manufacturing?

Manufacturing is the production of goods in large quantities after processing raw materials into more valuable products. For example, sugar is made from sugarcane, paper from wood, steel from iron ore, and aluminium from bauxite.

## Q2. Name any three physical factors for the location of industry.

Three physical factors for industrial location are availability of raw materials, land, and water. Power and climate may also influence location depending on the industry.

## Q3. Name any three human factors for the location of industry.

Three human factors are labour, capital, and market. Transport, banking, insurance, government policy, and specialised labour also influence industrial location.

## Q4. What are basic industries? Give an example.

Basic or key industries are industries whose products are used as raw materials by other industries. Examples include iron and steel, copper smelting, and aluminium smelting.

## Q5. Name the important raw materials used in manufacturing cement.

Important raw materials and inputs for cement are limestone, silica, gypsum, coal, electric power, and transport facilities.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. How are integrated steel plants different from mini steel plants? What problems does the industry face? What recent developments have led to a rise in production capacity?

An integrated steel plant is a large plant that handles all processes in one complex, from assembling raw materials to steel making, rolling, and shaping. A mini steel plant is smaller and uses electric furnaces, steel scrap, sponge iron, and re-rollers to produce mild and alloy steel of specified quality.

The iron and steel industry faces problems such as high cost and limited availability of coking coal, low labour productivity, irregular energy supply, poor infrastructure, and need for quality improvement. India also imports some good quality steel.

Recent developments such as liberalisation, foreign direct investment, private entrepreneurship, policy support, and technology improvement have increased production capacity. More research and development is needed to make steel production more competitive.

## Q2. How do industries pollute the environment?

Industries pollute air, water, land, and the environment through noise. Air pollution is caused by gases such as sulphur dioxide and carbon monoxide, smoke, dust, sprays, mist, and toxic gases from factories, refineries, smelting plants, brick kilns, and thermal power plants.

Water pollution occurs when industrial effluents, dyes, detergents, acids, salts, heavy metals, pesticides, fertilizers, synthetic chemicals, plastics, and rubber wastes are discharged into rivers and water bodies. Thermal pollution occurs when hot water from factories and power plants is released without cooling.

Land pollution is caused by dumping industrial waste, glass, chemicals, packaging, salts, garbage, fly ash, phospho-gypsum, and iron and steel slags. Noise pollution from machinery, generators, drills, and construction causes stress, hearing problems, increased heart rate, and high blood pressure.

## Q3. Discuss steps to minimise environmental degradation by industry.

Industrial environmental degradation can be reduced by minimising water use, reusing and recycling water, harvesting rainwater, and treating hot water and effluents before release. Effluent treatment should include primary mechanical treatment, secondary biological treatment, and tertiary biological, chemical, and physical treatment.

Air pollution can be controlled by fitting smoke stacks with electrostatic precipitators, fabric filters, scrubbers, and inertial separators. Smoke can be reduced by using oil or gas instead of coal. Groundwater overuse by industries should be legally regulated.

Noise pollution can be reduced by fitting generators with silencers, redesigning machinery, using noise-absorbing materials, and using earplugs or earphones. Industries should also adopt cleaner technology, waste management, ash utilisation, green belts, recycling, ecological monitoring, and environmental management systems.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing manufacturing means any production. | Manufacturing specifically means processing raw materials into more valuable goods in large quantities. |
| Treating agriculture and industry as separate. | They support each other through raw materials, tools, fertilizers, machines, and markets. |
| Confusing agro-based and mineral-based industries. | Agro-based uses farm raw materials; mineral-based uses minerals and metals. |
| Calling iron and steel a consumer industry. | It is a basic industry because other industries use steel. |
| Forgetting least-cost location. | Industrial location depends on arranging inputs at lowest cost. |
| Writing only raw material for location. | Labour, capital, power, market, transport, water, policy, and services also matter. |
| Confusing integrated and mini steel plants. | Integrated plants are large and complete; mini plants are smaller and use scrap/sponge iron. |
| Ignoring pollution control in industry answers. | Sustainable development requires economic growth with environmental protection. |
