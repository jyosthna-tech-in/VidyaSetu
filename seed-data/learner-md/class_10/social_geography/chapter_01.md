## How This Chapter Works

This chapter begins with a simple idea: nature does not automatically give us "resources" in a ready-made form. A thing becomes a resource only when human beings know how to use it, have the technology to use it, can afford to use it, and accept its use socially and culturally.

From there, the chapter moves through five connected questions:

1. What is a resource?
2. How are resources classified?
3. Why does resource use need planning and conservation?
4. How is land used and degraded in India?
5. What are the major soil types of India and how can soil be conserved?

The full logic is:

**nature + human needs + technology + institutions + responsible planning = useful and sustainable resources**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. A thing becomes a resource only through use

A substance lying in nature is not automatically a resource. It becomes a resource when humans can use it to satisfy a need. For example, flowing water becomes a resource when it can be used for drinking, irrigation, hydroelectricity, transport, or industry.

### 2. Resources depend on knowledge and institutions

Technology helps people use resources. Institutions help organize their use. Laws, governments, local communities, markets, and scientific agencies all influence how resources are used.

### 3. Resource distribution is unequal

Some regions have minerals, some have forests, some have water, some have solar energy, and some have skilled people. Because of this uneven distribution, resource planning becomes essential.

### 4. Development without conservation creates crisis

If resources are used only for short-term profit, they get depleted and damaged. This creates ecological problems such as global warming, land degradation, pollution, water scarcity, and soil erosion.

### 5. Sustainable development is the correct direction

Development must meet present needs without reducing the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. This is why conservation, planning, and fair distribution matter.

# Unit 1A: What Is a Resource?

## Core Idea

A resource is not just a natural object. It is anything in the environment that can be used to satisfy human needs, but only if three conditions are fulfilled:

1. It must be technologically accessible.
2. It must be economically feasible.
3. It must be culturally acceptable.

So the correct definition is:

**Everything available in our environment which can be used to satisfy our needs, provided it is technologically accessible, economically feasible, and culturally acceptable, is called a resource.**

## Understanding the Three Conditions

| Condition | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Technologically accessible | Humans must have the knowledge and tools to use it. | Petroleum under the sea becomes useful only when drilling technology exists. |
| Economically feasible | Using it should not cost more than its benefit. | A mineral deposit may exist, but mining it may be too costly. |
| Culturally acceptable | Society must accept its use. | A resource use may be rejected if it harms sacred places, community rights, or local traditions. |

## Nature, Technology, and Institutions

The chapter emphasizes that resources are created through an interactive relationship among:

| Element | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Nature | Provides materials such as land, water, forests, minerals, animals, and sunlight. |
| Technology | Helps transform these materials into usable forms. |
| Institutions | Organize ownership, rules, management, distribution, and development. |

Human beings are central in this process. They identify needs, invent tools, create institutions, and transform natural materials into resources.

## Important Explanation

Resources are not simply "free gifts of nature." Nature provides materials, but human beings convert those materials into resources. A forest becomes a resource when people use it for timber, fruits, herbs, biodiversity, climate regulation, soil conservation, and livelihood. A river becomes a resource when people use it for water supply, irrigation, fishing, navigation, electricity, and cultural life.

## Answer Frame: Define Resource

Use this structure in exams:

1. Start with the definition.
2. Mention the three conditions.
3. Add the role of humans, technology, and institutions.
4. Give one example.

Example answer:

A resource is anything available in the environment that can satisfy human needs, if it is technologically accessible, economically feasible, and culturally acceptable. Human beings transform materials available in nature into resources through technology and institutions. For example, river water becomes a resource when it is used for drinking, irrigation, fishing, transport, and power generation.

# Unit 1B: Classification of Resources

Resources are classified in four main ways:

1. On the basis of origin
2. On the basis of exhaustibility
3. On the basis of ownership
4. On the basis of status of development

---

## 1. Classification on the Basis of Origin

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Biotic Resources | Resources obtained from the biosphere and having life. | Human beings, flora, fauna, fisheries, livestock |
| Abiotic Resources | Resources made of non-living things. | Rocks, metals, minerals, land |

### Easy Memory

**Biotic = living**

**Abiotic = non-living**

### Exam Tip

Do not write that all natural resources are biotic. Land, minerals, metals, and rocks are natural but abiotic.

---

## 2. Classification on the Basis of Exhaustibility

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Renewable Resources | Resources that can be renewed or reproduced by physical, chemical, or mechanical processes. | Solar energy, wind energy, water, forests, wildlife |
| Non-Renewable Resources | Resources that take millions of years to form and may get exhausted with use. | Coal, petroleum, natural gas, metallic minerals |

## Renewable Resources

Renewable resources can be replenished. Some are continuous or flow resources, such as:

- solar energy
- wind energy
- flowing water
- tidal energy

However, renewable does not mean unlimited in careless use. Forests and wildlife can be renewed only if they are protected and allowed to regenerate.

## Non-Renewable Resources

Non-renewable resources form over a very long geological period. Once exhausted, they cannot be replaced within a human lifetime.

Examples:

- coal
- petroleum
- natural gas
- metallic minerals

Some non-renewable resources, such as metals, can be recycled. But fossil fuels like coal and petroleum cannot be reused after burning.

### Common Mistake

Do not write that all non-renewable resources are non-recyclable. Many metals can be recycled, but fossil fuels cannot be reused after combustion.

---

## 3. Classification on the Basis of Ownership

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Individual Resources | Owned privately by individuals. | Houses, fields, orchards, plantation plots, private wells |
| Community-Owned Resources | Accessible to all members of a community. | Village ponds, grazing grounds, public parks, burial grounds, picnic spots |
| National Resources | Belong to the nation. | Land, forests, minerals, water resources, roads, railways, territorial waters |
| International Resources | Found beyond national boundaries and regulated by international institutions. | Oceanic resources beyond 200 nautical miles of the Exclusive Economic Zone |

## National Resources: Important Detail

All resources within a country's political boundaries are national resources. The oceanic area up to 12 nautical miles from the coast is also part of territorial water. Resources in this zone belong to the nation.

## International Resources: Important Detail

The oceanic resources beyond 200 nautical miles of the Exclusive Economic Zone cannot be used freely by any single country. International institutions regulate their use.

---

## 4. Classification on the Basis of Status of Development

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Potential Resources | Found in a region but not fully used. | Wind and solar energy in Rajasthan and Gujarat |
| Developed Resources | Surveyed, measured, and ready for use. | Coal fields, iron ore mines, developed river valley projects |
| Stock | Materials that can satisfy needs but cannot yet be used because technology is lacking. | Hydrogen and oxygen in water as a possible energy source, if technology is not available |
| Reserves | Part of stock that can be used with present technology but is saved for future use. | Water stored in dams, forests reserved for future use |

## Potential vs Developed Resources

| Point | Potential Resources | Developed Resources |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Use | Not fully used yet | Already being used |
| Survey | May not be fully surveyed | Surveyed and measured |
| Example | Solar energy in Rajasthan | Coal mining in developed coal fields |
| Key Idea | Possibility exists | Use has already begun |

## Stock vs Reserves

| Point | Stock | Reserves |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Technology | Present technology is not enough for use. | Present technology can use them. |
| Use | Cannot be used at present. | Can be used now, but may be saved. |
| Example | Water as a source of hydrogen energy when technology is not available. | Water stored in dams for future generation and irrigation. |

### Exam Tip

If the question asks for "status of development," write all four: potential, developed, stock, and reserves. Do not stop after only potential and developed.

# Unit 1C: Development of Resources

## Why Resources Are Important

Resources are necessary for:

- survival
- comfort
- economic development
- agriculture
- industry
- transport
- housing
- energy
- national development

But the chapter warns that resources must be used carefully. If a few people use too much, while others remain deprived, both society and the environment suffer.

## Problems Created by Indiscriminate Resource Use

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Resource depletion | Resources are exhausted when they are used faster than they can be renewed or replaced. |
| Concentration in few hands | Powerful groups may control resources, increasing inequality. |
| Global ecological crisis | Careless use causes pollution, global warming, ozone layer depletion, and land degradation. |
| Conflict | Unequal access to resources can create social tension and political conflict. |

## Why Equitable Distribution Matters

Fair distribution of resources is necessary for:

- quality of life
- social justice
- peace
- economic development
- ecological balance

If resources remain in the hands of a few people, development becomes unequal. If resources are used carelessly, future generations suffer.

# Unit 1D: Sustainable Development

## Meaning

Sustainable development means development that takes place without damaging the environment and without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

In simple words:

**Use resources today, but do not destroy tomorrow's options.**

## Why Sustainable Development Is Needed

Sustainable development is needed because:

1. Resources are limited.
2. Some resources take millions of years to form.
3. Overuse causes pollution and ecological damage.
4. Poor people often suffer most from environmental damage.
5. Future generations also have a right to resources.

## Rio de Janeiro Earth Summit, 1992

In June 1992, more than 100 heads of state met in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, for the first International Earth Summit.

The summit focused on:

- environmental protection
- socio-economic development
- global cooperation
- sustainable development

## Agenda 21

Agenda 21 was a declaration signed at the 1992 Earth Summit.

Its aims included:

- achieving global sustainable development
- combating environmental damage
- fighting poverty
- controlling disease
- encouraging cooperation among nations
- giving local governments a role in sustainable planning

The important idea was that every local government should draw its own local Agenda 21, because environmental problems are experienced locally even when they are connected globally.

## Answer Frame: Sustainable Development

Sustainable development is development that meets present needs without damaging the environment or reducing the ability of future generations to meet their needs. It is necessary because indiscriminate resource use causes depletion, pollution, global warming, land degradation, and social inequality. The 1992 Rio Earth Summit and Agenda 21 emphasized global cooperation for sustainable development.

# Unit 1E: Resource Planning in India

## Why Resource Planning Is Needed

Resource planning is necessary because resources are unevenly distributed across India. Some regions are rich in one resource but poor in another.

| Region | Resource Situation |
| --- | --- |
| Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh | Rich in minerals and coal deposits |
| Arunachal Pradesh | Abundant water resources, but lacks infrastructure development |
| Rajasthan and Gujarat | Great potential for wind and solar energy, but shortage of water in many areas |
| Ladakh | Rich cultural heritage, but deficient in water, infrastructure, and some minerals |

This proves that the availability of resources alone does not guarantee development.

## Why Resources Alone Are Not Enough

For resources to contribute to development, a region also needs:

- technology
- skilled human resources
- transport
- communication
- institutions
- investment
- planning
- historical experience of development

For example, a region may have minerals, but if there are no roads, no skilled workers, no power supply, and no institutions to regulate mining, the minerals cannot contribute properly to development.

## Process of Resource Planning

Resource planning is a complex process. It has three main stages:

| Stage | What Happens |
| --- | --- |
| Identification and inventory | Resources are identified across regions through surveying, mapping, and measurement. |
| Planning structure | Technology, skills, institutions, and financial systems are developed for resource use. |
| Matching with national plans | Resource development plans are linked with national development plans. |

## Stage 1: Identification and Inventory

This includes:

- surveying
- mapping
- qualitative estimation
- quantitative estimation
- measurement of resource quality
- measurement of resource quantity

## Stage 2: Creating a Planning Structure

This includes:

- developing appropriate technology
- training human skills
- creating institutions
- arranging investment
- building transport and communication

## Stage 3: Matching Resource Plans with Development Plans

Resource planning must fit into broader national development. India has made efforts in this direction since the First Five Year Plan.

## Core Conclusion

In India, resource development does not mean only the availability of resources. It also depends on technology, quality of human resources, institutions, and historical experiences.

# Unit 1F: Conservation of Resources

## Meaning

Conservation means the careful, judicious, and planned use of resources so that they do not get exhausted or damaged.

It does not mean stopping all use. It means using resources responsibly.

## Why Conservation Is Necessary

Conservation is necessary because:

1. Resources are limited.
2. Some resources are non-renewable.
3. Overuse creates environmental problems.
4. Unequal use creates social problems.
5. Future generations need resources too.

## Gandhi's View

Mahatma Gandhi warned against greedy and exploitative resource use. His idea can be understood through the line:

**There is enough for everybody's need, but not for anybody's greed.**

This means the earth can support human needs if resources are used fairly and moderately. But it cannot support unlimited greed, waste, and luxury at the cost of others.

## Important Milestones in Resource Conservation

| Year | Event | Importance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1968 | Club of Rome advocated resource conservation | Drew attention to limits of resource use and global ecological concerns |
| 1974 | E. F. Schumacher wrote Small is Beautiful | Emphasized people-centered and sustainable development |
| 1987 | Brundtland Commission Report | Popularized the idea of sustainable development |
| 1992 | Rio Earth Summit | Brought global attention to environmental protection and sustainable development |

## Conservation Answer Frame

Resource conservation is necessary because irrational consumption and overutilisation create socio-economic and environmental problems. Conservation ensures careful use, reduces waste, protects ecological balance, and safeguards resources for future generations. It requires planning, technology, public awareness, and fair distribution.

# Unit 1G: Land as a Resource

## Why Land Is Important

Land is one of the most important natural resources because it supports:

- natural vegetation
- wildlife
- human life
- agriculture
- settlements
- economic activities
- transport systems
- communication networks
- industries

Land is a fixed and limited resource. We cannot increase the total land area of the country, so land use must be planned carefully.

## India's Relief Features

India has three major relief divisions:

| Relief Feature | Share of Land Area | Importance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Plains | About 43 percent | Suitable for agriculture, settlements, roads, railways, and industries |
| Mountains | About 30 percent | Support forests, rivers, tourism, biodiversity, and ecological balance |
| Plateaus | About 27 percent | Rich in minerals, forests, and fossil fuels |

## Why Relief Matters

Relief affects land use. Plains are easier for cultivation and settlement. Mountains are less suitable for dense settlement but important for forests and rivers. Plateaus often contain minerals and forests.

# Unit 1H: Land Utilisation

Land in India is used in different ways. The main categories are:

## 1. Forests

Forests are land areas covered with trees and natural vegetation. They provide timber, fuelwood, biodiversity, rainfall support, soil conservation, and ecological balance.

## 2. Land Not Available for Cultivation

This includes:

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Barren and waste land | Land that cannot be cultivated easily. |
| Land put to non-agricultural uses | Land used for buildings, roads, railways, factories, canals, and other non-farming purposes. |

## 3. Other Uncultivated Land Excluding Fallow Land

This includes:

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Permanent pastures and grazing land | Land used for grazing animals. |
| Land under miscellaneous tree crops and groves | Land under trees not counted in net sown area. |
| Culturable waste land | Land left uncultivated for more than five agricultural years, though it can be improved and cultivated. |

## 4. Fallow Land

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Current fallow | Land left without cultivation for one agricultural year or less. |
| Other than current fallow | Land left uncultivated for one to five agricultural years. |

## 5. Net Sown Area

Net sown area is the land on which crops are grown in a given agricultural year.

## Gross Cropped Area

Gross cropped area is:

**net sown area + area sown more than once in an agricultural year**

For example, if a field grows wheat in winter and rice in the monsoon, the same land is counted once in net sown area but twice in gross cropped area.

# Unit 1I: Land Use Pattern in India

Land use is determined by both physical and human factors.

Punjab and Haryana have very high net sown area, while many north-eastern states and island regions have low net sown area. Forest area remains below the desired 33 percent because of population pressure, agriculture, settlements, industries, mining, and infrastructure expansion.

# Unit 1J: Land Degradation

## Meaning

Land degradation means decline in the quality, fertility, and productive capacity of land.

It may happen because of natural processes, but in this chapter the major focus is on human causes.

## Why Land Degradation Is Serious

Land degradation affects:

- agriculture
- forests
- grazing
- water availability
- biodiversity
- rural livelihoods
- food security
- ecological balance

If fertile land becomes degraded, farmers produce less. If forests are degraded, soil erosion increases. If mining damages land, local communities may lose both livelihood and environmental security.

## Major Causes of Land Degradation

| Cause | How It Degrades Land |
| --- | --- |
| Deforestation | Removes tree cover, exposes soil, increases erosion, reduces fertility. |
| Overgrazing | Animals remove vegetation faster than it can regrow, leaving soil exposed. |
| Mining and quarrying | Leave deep scars, pits, waste heaps, and dust pollution. |
| Over-irrigation | Causes waterlogging and increases salinity and alkalinity in soil. |
| Industrial effluents | Pollute land with chemicals and waste. |
| Mineral processing | Produces dust and waste that degrade surrounding land. |

## Regional Examples

| Region | Major Cause |
| --- | --- |
| Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh, Odisha | Mining and deforestation |
| Gujarat, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra | Overgrazing |
| Punjab, Haryana, western Uttar Pradesh | Over-irrigation causing waterlogging, salinity, and alkalinity |
| Industrial regions | Effluents and industrial waste |

## Measures to Control Land Degradation

| Measure | How It Helps |
| --- | --- |
| Afforestation | Restores vegetation and protects soil. |
| Proper management of grazing | Prevents overuse of grasslands. |
| Planting shelter belts | Reduces wind erosion. |
| Stabilising sand dunes with thorny bushes | Controls desert expansion and wind erosion. |
| Proper management of wasteland | Brings degraded land back into productive use. |
| Control of mining activities | Reduces pits, scars, dumping, and deforestation. |
| Treatment of industrial effluents | Prevents chemical pollution of land and water. |
| Proper disposal of industrial waste | Reduces contamination and land damage. |

## Answer Frame: Land Degradation

Land degradation is the decline in the quality and productivity of land. It is caused by deforestation, overgrazing, mining, over-irrigation, industrial effluents, and improper waste disposal. In Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh, and Odisha, mining has caused serious degradation. In Punjab, Haryana, and western Uttar Pradesh, over-irrigation has led to waterlogging and salinity. It can be controlled through afforestation, controlled grazing, shelter belts, mining regulation, wasteland management, and treatment of industrial waste.

# Unit 1K: Soil as a Resource

## Why Soil Is Important

Soil is one of the most important renewable natural resources. It supports plant growth and therefore supports agriculture, forests, animals, and human life.

Soil is a living system because it contains:

- minerals
- organic matter
- water
- air
- microorganisms
- plant roots

## Soil Formation

Soil formation is a slow process. It may take millions of years to form a few centimetres of soil.

## Factors of Soil Formation

| Factor | Role in Soil Formation |
| --- | --- |
| Parent rock | Provides the basic mineral material. |
| Relief | Affects drainage, erosion, and depth of soil. |
| Climate | Temperature and rainfall affect weathering and organic activity. |
| Vegetation | Adds organic matter and protects soil. |
| Other life forms | Microorganisms and animals help decompose organic matter. |
| Time | Longer time allows deeper and more mature soil to develop. |

## Natural Agents Involved

Soil formation is influenced by:

- temperature change
- running water
- wind
- glaciers
- decomposers
- chemical reactions
- organic activity

## What Soil Contains

Soil is a mixture of:

- inorganic minerals
- humus
- air
- water
- living organisms

Humus is very important because it improves fertility and water retention.

# Unit 1L: Major Soil Types of India

India has a wide variety of soils because of differences in relief, climate, vegetation, parent rock, and time.

The major soil types discussed in this chapter are:

1. Alluvial soil
2. Black soil
3. Red and yellow soil
4. Laterite soil
5. Arid soil
6. Forest and mountainous soil

---

## 1. Alluvial Soil

## Distribution

Alluvial soil is found mainly in:

- northern plains
- Indus river system areas
- Ganga river system areas
- Brahmaputra river system areas
- parts of Rajasthan and Gujarat through a narrow corridor
- eastern coastal plains, especially river deltas

Important deltas include deltas formed by rivers such as the Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri.

## Formation

Alluvial soil is formed by river deposits. Rivers carry sand, silt, and clay from the mountains and deposit them in plains and deltas.

## Texture

Alluvial soil contains different proportions of:

- sand
- silt
- clay

In upper river valleys near the foothills, the soil is often coarser. In lower plains and deltas, finer particles are common.

## Bangar and Khadar

| Type | Meaning | Fertility |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bangar | Older alluvial soil, found above flood plains, often containing kankar nodules. | Fertile but less fertile than khadar. |
| Khadar | Newer alluvial soil, renewed by floods. | More fertile than bangar. |

## Nutrient Quality

Alluvial soils are generally rich in:

- potash
- phosphoric acid
- lime

They are suitable for:

- sugarcane
- paddy
- wheat
- cereals
- pulses

## Exam Answer: Eastern Coastal Deltas

The river deltas of the eastern coast have alluvial soil. This soil is formed by river deposits of sand, silt, and clay. It is very fertile and contains nutrients such as potash, phosphoric acid, and lime. It supports crops such as paddy, sugarcane, wheat, cereals, and pulses.

---

## 2. Black Soil

## Other Names

Black soil is also called:

- regur soil
- black cotton soil

It is called black cotton soil because it is very suitable for growing cotton.

## Distribution

Black soil is found in:

- Maharashtra
- Saurashtra
- Malwa
- Madhya Pradesh
- Chhattisgarh
- parts of the Godavari valley
- parts of the Krishna valley

## Formation

Black soil is formed from basaltic rocks of the Deccan Trap region.

## Characteristics

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Colour | Black, due to parent rock and mineral composition. |
| Texture | Clayey. |
| Moisture | Holds moisture for a long time. |
| Cracks | Develops deep cracks in hot weather, which helps aeration. |
| Wet condition | Becomes sticky when wet and difficult to work. |

## Nutrient Quality

Black soil is rich in:

- calcium carbonate
- magnesium
- potash
- lime

It is generally poor in:

- phosphoric content

## Crops

Black soil is especially suitable for:

- cotton
- pulses
- millets
- oilseeds
- sugarcane in some areas

## Exam Answer: Black Soil

Black soil, also called regur or black cotton soil, is found mainly in Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Chhattisgarh, and parts of the Godavari and Krishna valleys. It is formed from basaltic rocks of the Deccan Trap. It is clayey, moisture-retentive, rich in calcium carbonate, magnesium, potash, and lime, but poor in phosphoric content. It is very suitable for cotton cultivation.

## Distribution

Red and yellow soils are found in:

- eastern and southern parts of the Deccan plateau
- Odisha
- Chhattisgarh
- southern parts of the middle Ganga plain
- piedmont zones of the Western Ghats

## Formation

These soils develop on crystalline igneous rocks in areas of low rainfall.

## Colour

| Colour | Reason |
| --- | --- |
| Red | Diffusion of iron in crystalline and metamorphic rocks. |
| Yellow | Hydration of iron compounds. |

## Important Point

These soils are generally less fertile than alluvial soil, but they can support cultivation with proper use of fertilizers and irrigation.

## 4. Laterite Soil

## Meaning

The word laterite comes from the Latin word later, meaning brick. Laterite soil often hardens when exposed and may look brick-like.

## Formation

Laterite soil develops under:

- high temperature
- heavy rainfall
- alternating wet and dry seasons
- intense leaching

Heavy rainfall washes away soluble nutrients, leaving behind less fertile material.

## Characteristics

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Leached | Nutrients are washed away by heavy rain. |
| Acidic | Generally acidic, often with pH below 6. |
| Nutrient deficient | Poor in plant nutrients unless managed carefully. |
| Variable humus | More humus under forests, less humus under sparse vegetation. |

## Distribution

Laterite soil is found in:

- southern states
- Western Ghats region of Maharashtra
- Odisha
- parts of West Bengal
- north-eastern regions

## Crops

With proper soil conservation and fertilizers, laterite soil can support:

- tea
- coffee
- cashew

Red laterite soils in Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and Kerala are especially suitable for cashew nut cultivation.

# Unit 1M: Soil Erosion and Soil Conservation

## Meaning of Soil Erosion

Soil erosion is the removal or washing away of soil cover, especially topsoil.

Topsoil is the most fertile layer. When it is removed, land becomes less productive.

## Causes of Soil Erosion

| Cause | How It Causes Erosion |
| --- | --- |
| Deforestation | Removes roots that bind soil. |
| Overgrazing | Removes grass cover and exposes soil. |
| Construction | Disturbs land surface and increases runoff. |
| Mining | Removes vegetation and creates loose debris. |
| Wrong ploughing | Allows water to flow quickly down slopes. |
| Wind | Blows away dry, loose soil. |
| Running water | Washes away topsoil. |
| Glaciers | Scrape and transport soil and rock material. |

## Gully Erosion

Gully erosion occurs when running water cuts deep channels into land. These channels make cultivation difficult or impossible.

Land affected by gully erosion is often called bad land.

The ravines of the Chambal basin are an important example.

## Sheet Erosion

Sheet erosion happens when water flows as a sheet over a large area and removes the topsoil.

It is dangerous because it may not look dramatic at first, but it slowly removes fertile soil from fields.

## Wind Erosion

Wind erosion occurs in dry and sandy regions where vegetation cover is weak. Strong winds blow away loose soil particles.

---

## Methods of Soil Conservation

| Method | Best Used In | How It Works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Contour ploughing | Sloping land | Ploughing along contour lines slows water flow. |
| Terrace cultivation | Hilly areas | Steps cut into slopes reduce runoff and erosion. |
| Strip cropping | Large fields exposed to wind | Strips of grass between crops reduce wind force. |
| Shelter belts | Dry and sandy areas | Rows of trees reduce wind speed. |
| Afforestation | Degraded land | Tree roots bind soil and reduce runoff. |
| Controlled grazing | Grasslands | Prevents removal of protective vegetation. |
| Stabilising sand dunes | Desert margins | Thorny bushes and grasses hold sand in place. |

## Contour Ploughing

Contour ploughing means ploughing along the contour lines of a slope. This creates natural barriers that slow down water flow and reduce soil erosion.

## Terrace Cultivation

Terrace cultivation means cutting steps on hill slopes. It is useful in hilly states such as Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, and parts of the north-eastern region.

## Strip Cropping

In strip cropping, large fields are divided into strips. Strips of grass are left between crops. These grass strips reduce the force of wind and protect soil.

## Shelter Belts

Shelter belts are rows of trees planted in dry areas to reduce wind speed. They are especially useful in desert and semi-arid regions.

## Restoration Example: Sukhomajri and Jhabua

The chapter gives the example of Sukhomajri and the district of Jhabua to show that land restoration is possible.

In Sukhomajri, tree density increased from 13 trees per hectare in 1976 to 1,272 trees per hectare in 1992. This shows that community participation, watershed management, and ecological restoration can improve both environment and local livelihoods.

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. Name three states having black soil and the crop mainly grown in it.

Three states having black soil are Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, and Gujarat. Black soil is especially suitable for growing cotton, so it is also called black cotton soil.

## Q2. What type of soil is found in the river deltas of the eastern coast?

Alluvial soil is found in the river deltas of the eastern coast. This soil is formed by river deposits of sand, silt, and clay. It is fertile and supports crops such as paddy, sugarcane, wheat, cereals, and pulses.

Soil erosion in hilly areas can be controlled by terrace cultivation, contour ploughing, afforestation, controlled grazing, and proper land management. Terrace cultivation reduces the speed of water flowing down slopes, while trees bind the soil with their roots.

## Q4. What are biotic and abiotic resources? Give examples.

Biotic resources are obtained from living things, such as plants, animals, human beings, fisheries, and livestock. Abiotic resources are made of non-living things, such as rocks, metals, land, and minerals.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Explain land use pattern in India and why forest area has not increased much since 1960-61.

Land use in India is determined by physical factors such as topography, climate, soil type, and water availability, and human factors such as population density, technology, culture, and economic needs. Land is used for forests, agriculture, pastures, settlements, industries, transport, mining, and wasteland.

Plains are generally used more for agriculture and settlement, while mountains support forests, rivers, and biodiversity. Plateaus often contain minerals and forests. Net sown area is very high in Punjab and Haryana, where more than 80 percent of land is under cultivation, but it is less than 10 percent in states such as Arunachal Pradesh, Mizoram, Manipur, and in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Forest area has not increased much because land is under pressure from agriculture, population growth, settlements, industries, mining, transport networks, and encroachment. In many regions, deforestation and land degradation have also reduced the quality of forest land.

## Q2. How has technical and economic development led to more consumption of resources?

Technical and economic development has increased resource consumption in many ways. Modern technology makes it possible to mine deeper, cultivate more intensively, build large industries, extract fossil fuels, and transport goods over long distances. Economic development increases demand for houses, roads, vehicles, electricity, machines, consumer goods, and services.

Industrialisation requires minerals, coal, petroleum, water, and land. Urbanisation increases demand for construction materials, energy, transport, and waste disposal. Higher incomes often increase consumption of manufactured goods and electricity. Therefore, as technology and the economy grow, resource extraction and use also increase. If this growth is not planned sustainably, it leads to depletion, pollution, land degradation, and ecological imbalance.

## Q3. Why is resource planning important in India?

Resource planning is important in India because resources are unevenly distributed across the country. Some regions are rich in minerals but may lack infrastructure. Some regions have water resources but limited technology or transport. Rajasthan and Gujarat have great potential for solar and wind energy but face water scarcity. Ladakh has rich cultural heritage but lacks water and infrastructure.

Resource planning helps identify resources, estimate their quantity and quality, develop technology and institutions, and match resource development with national development plans. It ensures that resources are used efficiently, fairly, and sustainably.

## Q4. Explain the problems caused by indiscriminate use of resources.

Indiscriminate use of resources creates several problems. First, it causes resource depletion, especially of non-renewable resources such as coal, petroleum, and minerals. Second, it concentrates resources in the hands of a few powerful groups, increasing inequality. Third, it causes environmental problems such as global warming, pollution, ozone layer depletion, land degradation, and soil erosion. Fourth, it creates social and economic conflict because many people are deprived of basic resources.

Therefore, resource use must be guided by conservation, equitable distribution, planning, and sustainable development.

## Q5. Explain the major causes and control measures of land degradation.

Land degradation is caused by deforestation, overgrazing, mining, quarrying, over-irrigation, industrial effluents, and improper waste disposal. In Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh, and Odisha, mining has caused deforestation and land damage. In Gujarat, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, and Maharashtra, overgrazing has degraded land. In Punjab, Haryana, and western Uttar Pradesh, over-irrigation has caused waterlogging, salinity, and alkalinity.

Land degradation can be controlled through afforestation, proper grazing management, planting shelter belts, stabilising sand dunes with thorny bushes, managing wasteland, controlling mining activities, and treating industrial effluents before discharge.
