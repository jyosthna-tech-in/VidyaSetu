## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Freshwater is limited

Most water on Earth is salty ocean water. Only a small part is freshwater, and much of that freshwater is locked in glaciers and ice sheets.

### 2. Water is renewable through the hydrological cycle

Water moves through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration, and groundwater recharge. This makes water renewable, but only if we do not overuse or pollute it.

### 3. Scarcity is not only a rainfall problem

Some areas may receive rainfall but still face scarcity because of pollution, over-extraction, high demand, unequal access, or poor management.

### 4. Big dams have both benefits and costs

Dams can provide irrigation, electricity, drinking water, flood control, navigation, and fish breeding. But they can also displace people, damage rivers, submerge forests, increase conflicts, and cause ecological problems.

### 5. Local water harvesting is often sustainable

Traditional systems such as tankas, johads, khadins, guls, kuls, and bamboo drip irrigation are adapted to local rainfall, soil, slopes, and community needs.

# Unit 3A: Water on Earth and Freshwater Availability

## Core Idea

Although water covers about three-fourths of the Earth's surface, only a small proportion is freshwater that humans can use.

Most water is in oceans and is salty. Freshwater is found in glaciers, ice sheets, groundwater, rivers, lakes, and atmospheric moisture.

## Important Water Facts

| Fact | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Earth's surface covered by water | About three-fourths |
| World's water in oceans | About 96.5 percent |
| World's water as freshwater | About 2.5 percent |
| Freshwater in ice sheets and glaciers | Nearly 70 percent of freshwater |
| Freshwater stored as groundwater | A little less than 30 percent |
| India's share of global precipitation | Nearly 4 percent |
| India's rank in water availability per person per year | 133 |
| India's total renewable water resources | About 1,897 sq km per annum |

## Why Freshwater Is Actually Limited

Freshwater is limited because:

1. Most water is salty ocean water.
2. A large part of freshwater is frozen in ice sheets and glaciers.
3. Groundwater is not equally available everywhere.
4. Rivers and lakes are unevenly distributed.
5. Pollution makes water unsafe.
6. Over-extraction reduces availability.

## Surface Runoff and Groundwater

Freshwater used by humans mainly comes from:

- surface runoff
- groundwater
- direct precipitation

Surface runoff flows across land into rivers, lakes, ponds, and reservoirs. Groundwater is stored below the ground and is recharged when rainwater infiltrates into soil and rock.

# Unit 3B: How Water Becomes a Renewable Resource

## The Hydrological Cycle

Water becomes renewable through the hydrological cycle. This cycle continuously moves water between oceans, land, air, rivers, lakes, ice, and groundwater.

## Main Steps

| Step | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Evaporation | Sun heats water and changes it into water vapour. |
| Transpiration | Plants release water vapour into the air. |
| Condensation | Water vapour cools and forms clouds. |
| Precipitation | Water falls as rain, snow, hail, or sleet. |
| Runoff | Water flows over land into rivers, lakes, and seas. |
| Infiltration | Water enters the ground. |
| Groundwater Recharge | Underground water stores are replenished. |

## Important Understanding

Water is renewable, but this does not mean unlimited. If groundwater is pumped faster than it is recharged, water tables fall. If rivers are polluted, water may exist physically but may not be usable.

## Exam Answer: How Water Becomes Renewable

Water becomes a renewable resource through the hydrological cycle. Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, rivers, and land, forms clouds through condensation, returns to Earth as precipitation, and then flows as surface runoff or seeps underground to recharge groundwater. This continuous circulation renews water, but overuse and pollution can still create scarcity.

# Unit 3C: Water Scarcity

## Meaning

Water scarcity means shortage of usable water for drinking, domestic use, agriculture, industry, sanitation, and ecosystem needs.

Water scarcity can be caused by low availability, but it can also happen when water is available but polluted or unequally distributed.

## Water Stress

According to Falkenmark, water stress occurs when water availability is between 1,000 and 1,600 cubic metres per person per year.

## Why Scarcity Can Exist Even with Rainfall

A region may receive rainfall and still face water scarcity if:

- population is very high
- demand is greater than supply
- water is polluted
- groundwater is overused
- storage is poor
- water is unequally distributed
- industries and cities consume large quantities
- agriculture uses too much irrigation water

## Main Causes of Water Scarcity

| Cause | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Low rainfall | Less water enters rivers, ponds, tanks, and groundwater. |
| Seasonal rainfall | Water is available during monsoon but scarce in dry months. |
| Over-exploitation | Water is withdrawn faster than it is recharged. |
| Excessive use | Agriculture, industries, and cities use large quantities. |
| Unequal access | Some social groups get water while others remain deprived. |
| Large population | More people need more water for domestic use and food production. |
| Intensive agriculture | Irrigation for dry-season agriculture consumes huge water. |
| Industrialisation | Industries use and pollute water. |
| Urbanisation | Cities pump groundwater and create high domestic demand. |
| Pollution | Domestic waste, industrial waste, chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers make water unsafe. |

## Water Scarcity and Agriculture

A large population needs more food. To produce more food, farmers often expand irrigation, especially for dry-season crops.

Irrigated agriculture is the largest consumer of water.

When wells and tube wells are used excessively:

- groundwater levels fall
- wells dry up
- water becomes harder to access
- food security is affected
- poor farmers suffer more

## Water Scarcity and Industry

Industries create water scarcity in two ways:

1. They use large amounts of water.
2. They pollute water with waste and chemicals.

Many industries also require power, and a part of India's electricity comes from hydroelectric power.

The chapter mentions that hydroelectric power contributes about 22 percent of India's total electricity production.

## Water Scarcity and Cities

Urban water demand rises because of:

- dense population
- urban lifestyles
- housing colonies
- industries
- offices and services
- groundwater pumping devices
- higher per capita consumption

Many urban colonies use private groundwater pumps. This often leads to over-extraction and falling groundwater levels.

## Water Quality Scarcity

Water scarcity is not only about quantity. It can also be about quality.

Even if water is present, it may be unusable because of:

- domestic sewage
- industrial waste
- agricultural chemicals
- pesticides
- fertilizers
- toxic pollutants

Rivers such as the Ganga and Yamuna are mentioned as examples of rivers facing pollution pressure.

## Answer Frame: Water Scarcity and Causes

Water scarcity is the shortage of usable water for domestic, agricultural, industrial, and ecological needs. It is caused by low rainfall, seasonal variation, over-exploitation, excessive irrigation, growing population, industrialisation, urbanisation, pollution, and unequal access. A region may have rainfall but still suffer scarcity if water is polluted or if demand is greater than supply.

# Unit 3D: Need for Water Conservation and Management

## Why Conservation Is Needed

Water must be conserved and managed because:

1. Freshwater is limited.
2. Population is growing.
3. Agriculture consumes large amounts of water.
4. Industries and cities are increasing demand.
5. Pollution reduces usable water.
6. Over-extraction lowers groundwater levels.
7. Water scarcity affects health, food security, and livelihoods.
8. Mismanagement can create ecological crises.

## What Water Conservation Protects

| Area | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Health | Clean water prevents disease. |
| Food security | Agriculture needs reliable water. |
| Livelihoods | Farmers, fishers, industries, and households need water. |
| Ecosystems | Rivers, wetlands, forests, and wildlife need water. |
| Social peace | Unequal water access can create conflict. |

## Local Water Conservation Ideas

Good local measures include:

- fixing leaking taps and pipes
- using buckets instead of running hoses
- collecting rainwater
- reusing greywater for gardening
- growing less water-intensive crops where suitable
- protecting ponds and lakes
- preventing waste dumping into water bodies
- using drip irrigation
- recharging groundwater
- planting trees in catchment areas

# Unit 3E: Dams and Multi-Purpose River Projects

## What Is a Dam?

A dam is a barrier across flowing water that obstructs, directs, or slows the flow of water. It often creates a reservoir, lake, or impoundment.

## Parts and Features

| Feature | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Reservoir | Stored water behind a dam. |
| Spillway or weir | Structure through which water can flow intermittently or continuously. |
| Dam wall or barrier | Structure that holds back or regulates water. |

## Classification of Dams

Dams may be classified by:

- structure
- material used
- purpose
- height

Based on material and structure, dams may be:

- timber dams
- embankment dams
- masonry dams

Based on height, dams may be:

- low dams
- medium height dams
- high dams
- large or major dams

## Traditional Purpose of Dams

Traditionally, dams were built to store river water and rainwater for irrigation.

## Modern Multi-Purpose Uses

Today dams are used for many purposes:

| Purpose | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Irrigation | Stored water is supplied to agricultural fields. |
| Electricity generation | Flowing water is used to produce hydroelectric power. |
| Domestic water supply | Water is supplied to towns and villages. |
| Industrial water supply | Industries receive water for production and cooling. |
| Flood control | River flow is regulated to reduce floods. |
| Recreation | Reservoirs may be used for boating and tourism. |
| Inland navigation | Waterways may support transport. |
| Fish breeding | Reservoirs may support fisheries. |

## Why They Are Called Multi-Purpose Projects

They are called multi-purpose projects because the same stored water is used for several purposes at the same time, such as irrigation, electricity, flood control, drinking water, and industrial supply.

## Examples

| Project | River Basin | Purposes Mentioned |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bhakra-Nangal Project | Sutluj-Beas basin | Hydel power and irrigation |
| Hirakud Project | Mahanadi basin | Water conservation and flood control |
| Sardar Sarovar Project | Narmada River, Gujarat | Water supply to drought-prone and desert areas of Gujarat and Rajasthan |

## Nehru's View

After Independence, multi-purpose river valley projects were seen as symbols of development. Jawaharlal Nehru called dams the temples of modern India because they were expected to connect agricultural development, village economy, industrialisation, and urban growth.

# Unit 3G: Problems of Multi-Purpose Projects and Large Dams

Large dams have benefits, but they have also come under criticism because of environmental, social, and economic problems.

## Environmental Problems

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Disturbed river flow | Dams regulate and reduce natural river flow. |
| Poor sediment flow | Sediment gets trapped in reservoirs instead of moving downstream. |
| Excessive sedimentation | Reservoir bottoms fill with silt, reducing storage capacity. |
| Rocky stream beds | Lack of sediment can make river beds rockier downstream. |
| Poor aquatic habitat | Fish and other aquatic organisms lose suitable habitat. |
| Blocked migration | Dams fragment rivers and prevent fish migration, especially for spawning. |
| Submergence | Reservoirs submerge forests, soil, farms, and settlements. |
| Decomposition | Submerged vegetation decomposes under water. |
| Soil salinisation | Over-irrigation can increase salt in soil. |
| Flood problems | Sudden water releases and reservoir sedimentation may worsen floods. |
| Water-borne diseases | Stored water can encourage diseases and pests. |
| Pollution | Excessive water use and stagnant water can create pollution issues. |

## Social Problems

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Displacement | Villages, farms, forests, and homes may be submerged. |
| Loss of livelihood | Farmers, fishers, pastoralists, and forest users may lose resources. |
| Unequal benefits | Benefits often go to large farmers, industries, and urban centres. |
| Local sacrifice | Local communities bear costs for national development. |
| Rehabilitation issues | Displaced people may not receive fair compensation or secure livelihood. |

## Economic and Political Problems

Large projects can create conflicts over:

- water sharing
- cost sharing
- benefit sharing
- priority between cities and villages
- irrigation versus drinking water
- upstream and downstream needs

## Environmental Movements

Multi-purpose projects and large dams led to several environmental movements, including:

- Narmada Bachao Andolan
- Tehri Dam Andolan

## Narmada Bachao Andolan

Narmada Bachao Andolan, or Save Narmada Movement, mobilised tribal people, farmers, environmentalists, and human rights activists against the Sardar Sarovar Dam on the Narmada River in Gujarat.

Initially, it focused on environmental issues such as trees that would be submerged. Later, it focused more strongly on rehabilitation of displaced people, especially poor citizens and oustees.

## Sardar Sarovar Project

The Sardar Sarovar Dam has been built on the Narmada River in Gujarat.

It covers four states:

- Maharashtra
- Madhya Pradesh
- Gujarat
- Rajasthan

The project aimed to supply water to drought-prone and desert areas of Gujarat and Rajasthan, including many villages and towns.

## Flood Control Criticism

Dams are often built for flood control, but they can sometimes worsen floods because:

- reservoirs fill with sediment
- storage capacity declines
- excessive rainfall may force sudden release of water
- flood plains are deprived of natural silt
- downstream erosion and flooding may increase

The chapter mentions floods in Maharashtra and Gujarat in 2006, where water release from dams during heavy rains aggravated flood situations.

## Inter-State Water Disputes

Water disputes arise when states disagree over river water sharing, project costs, or benefits.

The chapter mentions the Krishna-Godavari dispute. Objections were raised by Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh regarding diversion of more water at Koyna by Maharashtra for a multi-purpose project. The concern was that downstream flow would reduce, affecting agriculture and industry.

## Balanced Answer: Multi-Purpose Projects

Multi-purpose river projects have benefits such as irrigation, hydroelectricity, flood control, water supply, navigation, recreation, and fish breeding. However, they also cause displacement, loss of livelihood, submergence of forests and farms, sedimentation, disturbed river flow, blocked fish migration, salinisation, water disputes, and unequal distribution of benefits. Therefore, they must be planned carefully with environmental protection and fair rehabilitation.

# Unit 3H: Rainwater Harvesting

## Meaning

Rainwater harvesting means collecting and storing rainwater for future use or for recharging groundwater.

## Why Rainwater Harvesting Is Important

Rainwater harvesting is important because it:

- reduces dependence on groundwater
- recharges wells and aquifers
- provides drinking water
- supports agriculture
- reduces runoff
- reduces flooding
- suits local climate conditions
- is often cheaper and more sustainable than large projects

## Traditional Knowledge

People in different regions developed water harvesting systems according to:

- rainfall pattern
- soil type
- slope
- river behaviour
- groundwater condition
- local water needs

This local adaptation is the strength of traditional water systems.

## Guls and Kuls

Guls or kuls are diversion channels built in hill and mountainous regions of the western Himalayas. They carry water from streams to agricultural fields.

In Bengal flood plains, people developed inundation channels to carry floodwater into fields for irrigation.

## Khadins

Khadins are rain-fed storage structures used in Jaisalmer. Agricultural fields are designed to store rainwater, allowing water to stand and moisten the soil.

## Johads

Johads are traditional water storage structures found in parts of Rajasthan. They help store rainwater and recharge groundwater.

# Unit 3J: Rooftop Rainwater Harvesting in Rajasthan

## Where It Was Common

Rooftop rainwater harvesting was common in the semi-arid and arid regions of Rajasthan, especially in:

- Bikaner
- Phalodi
- Barmer
- towns and villages of the Thar

## What Is a Tanka?

A tanka is an underground tank used to store drinking water collected from rooftops.

Tankas were usually built:

- inside the main house
- in the courtyard
- underground
- connected to sloping roofs by pipes

## How the System Worked

1. Rain fell on sloping rooftops.
2. Water flowed down through a pipe.
3. The first rain was usually allowed to wash the roof and pipes and was not stored.
4. Later rainwater was collected in underground tankas.
5. Stored water was used for drinking during dry months.

## Why the First Rain Was Not Collected

The first spell of rain cleaned dust, leaves, bird droppings, and impurities from the roof and pipes. Later rainwater was cleaner and suitable for storage.

## Palar Pani

Rainwater is called palar pani in parts of Rajasthan. It is considered one of the purest forms of natural water.

## Size of Tankas

Tankas could be as large as a room. The chapter gives an example from Phalodi where one household had a tanka:

- 6.1 metres deep
- 4.27 metres long
- 2.44 metres wide

## Why Tankas Were Reliable

Rainwater stored in tankas could last until the next rainfall. This made them reliable during summer when other sources dried up.

## Underground Rooms Near Tankas

Some houses built underground rooms near tankas because the stored water helped keep the room cool in summer.

## Decline in Western Rajasthan

In western Rajasthan, rooftop rainwater harvesting declined after water became available through the perennial Indira Gandhi Canal. However, some houses still maintain tankas because they prefer the taste of tanka water.

## Exam Answer: Rajasthan Rainwater Harvesting

In semi-arid Rajasthan, rainwater harvesting was carried out through rooftop collection and underground tankas. Rain falling on sloping roofs was carried through pipes into underground tanks built inside houses or courtyards. The first rain was not collected because it cleaned the roofs and pipes. Later rainwater, called palar pani, was stored for drinking and could last through dry months. Tankas were especially important in Bikaner, Phalodi, Barmer, and the Thar region.

# Unit 3K: Modern Adaptations of Rainwater Harvesting

## Rooftop Harvesting System

A modern rooftop rainwater harvesting system may include:

1. rooftop catchment
2. PVC pipe
3. filter with sand and bricks
4. storage sump for immediate use
5. overflow pipe to a well
6. groundwater recharge
7. later withdrawal from the well

## Shillong, Meghalaya

Rooftop rainwater harvesting is common in Shillong.

This is interesting because Cherrapunji and Mawsynram, located about 55 km from Shillong, receive some of the highest rainfall in the world, yet Shillong faces acute water shortage.

Nearly every household in Shillong has a rooftop rainwater harvesting structure. About 15 to 25 percent of household water requirement comes from rooftop water harvesting.

## Gendathur, Karnataka

Gendathur, a village in Mysuru district of Karnataka, adapted rooftop rainwater harvesting.

Important facts:

- Nearly 200 households installed rooftop rainwater harvesting systems.
- The village earned the distinction of being rich in rainwater.
- Gendathur receives about 1,000 mm annual precipitation.
- With 80 percent collection efficiency and about 10 fillings, each house can collect about 50,000 litres annually.

## Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu was the first state in India to make rooftop rainwater harvesting compulsory for all houses.

It also made legal provisions to punish defaulters.

## Recharge Through Wells and Hand Pumps

Modern rainwater harvesting may recharge groundwater through:

- hand pumps
- abandoned dug wells
- recharge pits
- percolation tanks
- storage sumps

The main goal is to allow rainwater to enter underground aquifers instead of flowing away as waste runoff.

# Unit 3L: Bamboo Drip Irrigation in Meghalaya

## Meaning

Bamboo drip irrigation is a traditional system in Meghalaya that uses bamboo pipes to carry stream and spring water from hilltops to plant roots.

## Age

The system is about 200 years old.

## How It Works

1. Bamboo pipes divert water from perennial springs on hilltops.
2. Water flows by gravity through bamboo channels.
3. The channels carry water over long distances.
4. Smaller bamboo sections divide the water into branches.
5. Flow is controlled by adjusting pipe positions.
6. At the plant site, water is reduced to drops near the roots.

## Water Flow

About 18 to 20 litres of water may enter the bamboo pipe system, but by the time it reaches the plant, it may be reduced to 20 to 80 drops per minute.

## Why It Is Efficient

Bamboo drip irrigation is efficient because:

- it uses local materials
- it works by gravity
- it avoids water wastage
- it delivers water near roots
- it suits hill slopes
- it requires low external energy
- it is adapted to local ecology

## Exam Answer: Bamboo Drip Irrigation

Bamboo drip irrigation is a traditional water management system of Meghalaya. Bamboo pipes carry water from perennial springs on hilltops to lower agricultural fields by gravity. The flow is gradually reduced through smaller bamboo channels, and water finally reaches plant roots in drops. It is efficient, low-cost, locally adapted, and conserves water.

# Unit 3M: Water Disputes and Social Conflicts

## Why Water Creates Conflict

Water creates conflict because different groups want the same water for different uses.

Competing users include:

- farmers
- industries
- cities
- villages
- upstream states
- downstream states
- dam authorities
- displaced communities
- fishers
- pastoralists

## Common Conflict Questions

| Conflict | Core Issue |
| --- | --- |
| City versus village | Should water go first to urban supply or agriculture? |
| Upstream versus downstream | Does upstream diversion reduce downstream water? |
| Large farmer versus landless labourer | Who receives irrigation benefits? |
| Industry versus household | Should water support factories or drinking needs? |
| Project beneficiaries versus displaced people | Who gains and who sacrifices? |

## Sabarmati Basin Example

In Gujarat, Sabarmati-basin farmers became agitated when higher priority was given to water supply in urban areas, especially during droughts.

## Krishna-Godavari Dispute

The Krishna-Godavari dispute involved objections by Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh to Maharashtra's diversion of more water at Koyna for a multi-purpose project. They feared reduced downstream flow would harm agriculture and industry.

## Key Lesson

Water management must consider fairness, not just engineering. A project is not successful if it benefits cities, industries, or large farmers while displacing or depriving poor local communities.

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. Explain how water becomes a renewable resource.

Water becomes a renewable resource through the hydrological cycle. Water evaporates from oceans, rivers, lakes, and land, condenses to form clouds, returns as precipitation, and then flows as surface runoff or seeps underground to recharge groundwater. This continuous movement renews water. However, overuse and pollution can still create scarcity.

## Q2. What is water scarcity and what are its main causes?

Water scarcity is the shortage of usable water for domestic, agricultural, industrial, and ecological needs. Its main causes are low rainfall, seasonal variation, over-exploitation, excessive use, unequal access, growing population, intensive irrigation, industrialisation, urbanisation, and pollution by domestic waste, industrial waste, chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers.

## Q3. Compare the advantages and disadvantages of multi-purpose river projects.

Multi-purpose river projects provide irrigation, hydroelectricity, domestic and industrial water supply, flood control, fish breeding, recreation, and inland navigation. However, they also cause displacement of people, loss of livelihood, submergence of forests and farmland, disturbed river flow, sedimentation, blocked fish migration, salinisation, water-borne diseases, and water disputes.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Discuss how rainwater harvesting in semi-arid regions of Rajasthan is carried out.

In semi-arid regions of Rajasthan, especially Bikaner, Phalodi, Barmer, and the Thar region, people traditionally practised rooftop rainwater harvesting. Houses had sloping roofs connected to underground tanks called tankas through pipes. Rainwater falling on rooftops flowed into the pipes and was stored in tankas built inside the main house or courtyard.

The first spell of rain was usually not collected because it cleaned the roof and pipes. Water from later showers was stored. This rainwater, called palar pani, was considered pure and could be stored until the next rainfall. Tankas were very useful in summer when other water sources dried up. Some houses also built underground rooms near tankas to keep them cool during hot months.

## Q2. Describe how modern adaptations of traditional rainwater harvesting methods are being carried out to conserve and store water.

Modern rainwater harvesting adapts traditional methods by using rooftops, PVC pipes, filters, sumps, wells, recharge pits, and groundwater recharge systems. Rainwater from rooftops is carried through pipes, filtered through sand and bricks, stored in sumps for immediate use, and excess water is sent to wells or recharge structures.

In Shillong, Meghalaya, rooftop rainwater harvesting is widely used, and about 15 to 25 percent of household water requirements come from it. In Gendathur village of Mysuru, Karnataka, nearly 200 households installed rooftop harvesting systems and collect large quantities of rainwater annually. Tamil Nadu made rooftop rainwater harvesting compulsory for all houses. These adaptations help conserve water, reduce groundwater depletion, and improve local water security.

## Q3. Why have multi-purpose river projects faced opposition?

Multi-purpose river projects have faced opposition because they cause environmental and social problems. Dams disturb natural river flow, reduce sediment movement, fragment rivers, block fish migration, and submerge forests, farms, and settlements. Reservoirs may cause sedimentation, water-borne diseases, and decomposition of submerged vegetation.

They also displace local communities and reduce their access to land, forests, water, and livelihood. Often, benefits go to large farmers, industries, and urban centres, while local people bear the cost. Movements such as Narmada Bachao Andolan and Tehri Dam Andolan emerged because of these concerns, especially displacement and inadequate rehabilitation.

## Q4. Why can areas with high rainfall still suffer from water scarcity?

Areas with high rainfall can still suffer from water scarcity if population is large, demand is high, water is polluted, storage is poor, or access is unequal. Cities may receive rainfall but overuse groundwater and pollute rivers. Industrial waste, domestic sewage, fertilizers, and pesticides can make water unsafe. If water is not harvested and managed properly, rainwater flows away quickly and scarcity appears during dry months.
