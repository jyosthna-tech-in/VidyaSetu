## How to Understand This Chapter

Agriculture is a primary activity. It produces food and raw material for industries. In India, agriculture is especially important because a large part of the population depends on it for livelihood.

This chapter has four major parts:

1. **Types of farming** - how agriculture is practised.
2. **Cropping pattern** - when crops are grown.
3. **Major crops** - where crops are grown and what conditions they need.
4. **Agrarian change** - reforms, food security, and globalisation.

The most important study method for this chapter is:

**crop name -> season -> climate -> soil -> regions -> uses -> problems**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Agriculture depends on both nature and society

Climate, soil, relief, rainfall, irrigation, technology, labour, landholding size, markets, transport, and government policy all shape agriculture.

### 2. India has many farming systems

India has primitive subsistence farming, intensive subsistence farming, commercial farming, and plantation agriculture because regions differ in population, land, technology, rainfall, and market access.

### 3. Cropping seasons follow climate

Rabi, kharif, and zaid seasons are linked to monsoon, winter, and summer conditions.

### 4. Crop geography matters

Every crop has specific requirements of temperature, rainfall, soil, labour, and growing season.

### 5. Agriculture faces economic pressure

Small holdings, uncertain rainfall, falling public investment, market competition, input costs, water depletion, land degradation, and globalisation create difficulties for farmers.

### 6. Food security depends on farmers' security

There can be no real food security if small and marginal farmers are insecure, indebted, or pushed out of cultivation.

# Unit 4B: Types of Farming

India has different types of farming because land, rainfall, population, technology, culture, and markets vary from region to region.

The main types discussed in the chapter are:

1. Primitive subsistence farming
2. Intensive subsistence farming
3. Commercial farming
4. Plantation agriculture

---

## 1. Primitive Subsistence Farming

## Meaning

Primitive subsistence farming is practised on small patches of land with primitive tools and family or community labour.

## Main Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Land size | Small patches of land |
| Tools | Hoe, dao, digging sticks |
| Labour | Family or community labour |
| Inputs | Very low use of fertilizers, pesticides, and modern inputs |
| Dependence | Depends on monsoon, natural fertility, and local environmental conditions |
| Output | Low productivity |
| Purpose | Family subsistence |

## Slash and Burn Method

Primitive subsistence farming is often a slash and burn system:

1. Farmers clear a patch of forest.
2. Vegetation is burned.
3. Ash adds temporary fertility.
4. Crops are grown for family needs.
5. When soil fertility declines, farmers move to a new patch.
6. The old patch is left for natural regeneration.

## Names in India

| Region | Local Name |
| --- | --- |
| North-eastern India | Jhumming |
| Manipur | Pamlou |
| Bastar district of Chhattisgarh | Dipa |
| Andaman and Nicobar Islands | Dipa |
| Madhya Pradesh | Bewar or Dahiya |
| Andhra Pradesh | Podu or Penda |
| Odisha | Pama Dabi, Koman, or Bringa |
| Western Ghats | Kumari |
| South-eastern Rajasthan | Valre or Waltre |
| Himalayan belt | Khil |
| Jharkhand | Kuruwa |

## Names Outside India

## Rinjha Example

The textbook gives the example of Rinjha's family near Diphu in Assam. Her family clears, slashes, and burns a patch of land and uses water from a bamboo canal. This is an example of primitive subsistence farming or jhumming.

---

## 2. Intensive Subsistence Farming

## Meaning

Intensive subsistence farming is practised in areas of high population pressure on land. Farmers try to get maximum output from small landholdings.

## Main Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Population pressure | Very high |
| Landholding | Small and often fragmented |
| Labour | Labour-intensive |
| Inputs | High doses of biochemical inputs and irrigation |
| Aim | Maximum production from limited land |
| Problem | Landholdings become uneconomic due to inheritance and fragmentation |

## Why It Happens

The right of inheritance divides land among successive generations. Landholdings become smaller, but farmers continue cultivation because they often lack alternative employment.

## Regions

It is common in densely populated agricultural regions such as the fertile plains of India.

---

## 3. Commercial Farming

## Meaning

Commercial farming is farming mainly for sale in the market.

## Main Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Market sale |
| Inputs | HYV seeds, fertilizers, insecticides, pesticides, irrigation |
| Productivity | Higher productivity is the aim |
| Market link | Stronger connection with transport, markets, processing, and trade |

## Commercialisation Varies by Region

The same crop can be commercial in one region and subsistence in another.

Example:

- Rice is a commercial crop in Punjab and Haryana.
- Rice may be a subsistence crop in Odisha.

This depends on surplus production, irrigation, market access, and farming purpose.

---

## 4. Plantation Agriculture

## Meaning

Plantation is a type of commercial farming in which a single crop is grown on a large area.

## Main Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Crop pattern | Single crop over a large area |
| Nature | Interface of agriculture and industry |
| Land | Large estates or tracts |
| Inputs | Capital-intensive inputs |
| Labour | Often migrant labour |
| Processing | Produce is processed as industrial raw material |
| Market | Needs strong transport and communication network |

## Important Plantation Crops in India

- tea
- coffee
- rubber
- sugarcane
- banana

## Examples

| Crop | Region |
| --- | --- |
| Tea | Assam and North Bengal |
| Coffee | Karnataka |
| Rubber | Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka |

## Why Transport Matters

Plantation crops are produced for the market and often require quick processing. Therefore, roads, railways, communication, processing industries, and market links are essential.

# Unit 4C: Cropping Pattern

India has three cropping seasons:

1. Rabi
2. Kharif
3. Zaid

## Rabi Season

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Sowing time | October to December |
| Harvesting time | April to June |
| Climate | Winter season, cool growing period |
| Important crops | Wheat, barley, peas, gram, mustard |
| Important regions | Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Jammu and Kashmir, Uttarakhand, Uttar Pradesh |
| Important support | Western temperate cyclones bring winter precipitation |

## Why Rabi Crops Grow Well in North-West India

Rabi crops grow well in north and north-western India because:

- winter temperatures are suitable
- western temperate cyclones bring winter rainfall
- irrigation is available
- Green Revolution improved wheat production
- fertile alluvial soils support cultivation

## Kharif Season

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Sowing time | With onset of monsoon |
| Harvesting time | September-October |
| Climate | Monsoon season |
| Important crops | Paddy, maize, jowar, bajra, tur, moong, urad, cotton, jute, groundnut, soybean |
| Important rice regions | Assam, West Bengal, Odisha coast, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Maharashtra Konkan coast, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar |

## Aus, Aman, and Boro

In states such as Assam, West Bengal, and Odisha, three crops of paddy are grown in a year:

- Aus
- Aman
- Boro

## Zaid Season

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Time | Short summer season between rabi and kharif |
| Important crops | Watermelon, muskmelon, cucumber, vegetables, fodder crops |
| Special note | Sugarcane takes almost a year to grow |

## Rabi, Kharif, Zaid Comparison

| Point | Rabi | Kharif | Zaid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Season | Winter | Monsoon | Summer |
| Sowing | October-December | Onset of monsoon | After rabi, before kharif |
| Harvesting | April-June | September-October | Before monsoon |
| Examples | Wheat, gram, mustard | Rice, maize, cotton, jute | Watermelon, cucumber, fodder |
| Water source | Winter rain and irrigation | Monsoon rain | Irrigation often needed |

## Rice

## Importance

Rice is the staple food crop for a majority of people in India.

India is the second largest producer of rice in the world after China.

## Season

Rice is a kharif crop.

## Geographical Conditions

| Requirement | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Temperature | Above 25 degrees Celsius |
| Humidity | High humidity |
| Rainfall | Annual rainfall above 100 cm |
| Water | Irrigation needed in areas of less rainfall |
| Land | Plains, coastal areas, deltaic regions |

## Major Regions

Rice is grown in:

- plains of north India
- north-eastern India
- coastal areas
- deltaic regions
- Assam
- West Bengal
- coastal Odisha
- Andhra Pradesh
- Telangana
- Tamil Nadu
- Kerala
- Maharashtra, especially Konkan coast
- Uttar Pradesh
- Bihar
- Punjab and Haryana through irrigation
- western Uttar Pradesh
- parts of Rajasthan through irrigation

## Why Rice Grows in Punjab and Haryana

Punjab and Haryana receive less rainfall than traditional rice regions, but dense networks of canals and tubewells have made rice cultivation possible.

## Exam Answer: Conditions for Rice

Rice is a kharif crop and staple food for a majority of Indians. It requires high temperature above 25 degrees Celsius, high humidity, and annual rainfall above 100 cm. In areas of low rainfall, it is grown with irrigation. It is grown in the plains of north and north-eastern India, coastal areas, deltaic regions, Assam, West Bengal, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Punjab, and Haryana.

- Uttarakhand
- Meghalaya

## Exam Answer: Tea Conditions

Tea grows well in tropical and subtropical climates. It requires deep, fertile, well-drained soil rich in humus and organic matter. Tea bushes need a warm, moist, frost-free climate throughout the year and frequent showers evenly distributed over the year. Tea is labour-intensive and requires abundant, cheap, skilled labour. Major tea regions include Assam, Darjeeling and Jalpaiguri hills, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala.

# Unit 4J: Technological and Institutional Reforms

## Why Reforms Were Needed

Indian agriculture has been practised for thousands of years, but sustained use of land without suitable technological and institutional changes slowed agricultural development.

Problems included:

- dependence on monsoon
- small and fragmented landholdings
- low productivity
- lack of irrigation
- lack of credit
- exploitation by middlemen
- poverty among farmers
- natural disasters
- lack of storage and marketing facilities

## Institutional Reforms after Independence

After Independence, the following reforms were given priority:

| Reform | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Collectivisation | Promote collective agricultural effort. |
| Consolidation of holdings | Combine scattered land parcels into manageable holdings. |
| Cooperation | Encourage cooperative credit, marketing, and farming support. |
| Abolition of zamindari | Remove exploitative intermediaries. |
| Land reforms | Improve ownership and access to land. |

Land reform was the main focus of the First Five Year Plan.

# Unit 4L: Food Security

## Meaning

Food security means that all people have access to enough food that provides minimum nutritional levels.

If any group does not have access to sufficient food, that group lacks food security.

## Who Is More Vulnerable?

Food insecurity is more common in:

- economically less developed states
- regions with high poverty
- remote areas
- disaster-prone areas
- areas with uncertain food supply

## National Food Security System

India's national food security system has two main components:

1. Buffer stock
2. Public Distribution System

## Buffer Stock

Buffer stock is the stock of foodgrains maintained by the government to ensure food availability.

## PDS

Public Distribution System provides foodgrains and essential commodities at subsidised prices to Below Poverty Line families in rural and urban areas.

## FCI

Food Corporation of India procures and stocks foodgrains.

FCI buys foodgrains from farmers at the government-announced Minimum Support Price.

## Food Security Policy

The main objective is to ensure availability of foodgrains to common people at affordable prices.

## Problems with Food Security and Agriculture

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| BPL/APL categorisation | Some deserving poor are excluded from BPL. |
| Crop failure | APL families may slip into poverty after one crop failure. |
| MSP distortion | High MSP for wheat and paddy encourages overproduction of these crops. |
| Subsidy inefficiency | Fertilizer, power, and water subsidies may lead to wasteful use. |
| Environmental damage | Excessive fertilizers and water cause waterlogging, salinity, and loss of micronutrients. |
| Cereal shift | Shift from food crops to fruits, vegetables, oilseeds, and industrial crops reduces area under cereals and pulses. |
| Land competition | Housing and non-agricultural uses reduce net sown area. |
| Water scarcity | Periodic scarcity reduces irrigated area. |
| Poor storage and marketing | Farmers face distress sale and low bargaining power. |

## Why Small Farmers Matter

The chapter makes a strong point:

**There can be no food security without the security of small farmers.**

Small and marginal farmers suffer because:

- input costs are high
- market prices are uncertain
- they lack storage
- they lack bargaining power
- they are vulnerable to crop failure
- groundwater decline affects them first
- big farmers can use deeper tubewells

# Unit 4M: Globalisation and Indian Agriculture

## Globalisation Is Not New

The chapter explains that globalisation existed even during colonial times.

Historical examples:

| Period | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Nineteenth century | Indian spices were exported to many countries. |
| British period | Cotton was exported to Britain for textile industries in Manchester and Liverpool. |
| Champaran, 1917 | Farmers were forced to grow indigo for British textile needs. |

## Globalisation after 1990

After 1990, Indian farmers faced new challenges because of liberalisation and global competition.

## Challenges

| Challenge | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Competition with developed countries | Developed countries heavily subsidise agriculture, making their products cheaper. |
| Reduced import duties | Foreign agricultural goods can compete with Indian produce. |
| Falling investment | Farmers may withdraw investment due to uncertainty. |
| Input costs | HYV seeds, fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation can be expensive. |
| Price uncertainty | Farmers often cannot fix prices in their favour. |
| Land fragmentation | Holdings become smaller as population rises. |
| Environmental problems | Chemical overuse, aquifer depletion, and biodiversity loss. |

## Globalisation and Crop Diversification

Some economists argue that farmers should diversify from cereals to high-value crops such as:

- fruits
- vegetables
- flowers
- medicinal herbs
- bio-diesel crops such as jatropha and jojoba

These crops may increase income and require less irrigation than rice or sugarcane in some regions.

## Risk of Diversification

If India shifts too much from cereals to high-value crops, it may need to import foodgrains. This can be risky for food security.

## Gene Revolution

Gene revolution refers to the use of genetic engineering to create new hybrid or genetically modified seed varieties.

## Organic Farming

Organic farming is farming without factory-made chemical fertilizers and pesticides. It is considered environment-friendly because it reduces chemical pollution and soil degradation.

## Balanced View

Indian agriculture can benefit from global markets and high-value crops, but small farmers need protection, credit, infrastructure, storage, fair prices, and sustainable technology.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Suggest initiatives taken by the government to ensure an increase in agricultural production.

The government has taken many initiatives to increase agricultural production. After Independence, land reforms, consolidation of holdings, cooperation, and abolition of zamindari were introduced. The Green Revolution promoted HYV seeds, irrigation, fertilizers, pesticides, and modern technology, while the White Revolution increased milk production.

The government also introduced crop insurance against drought, flood, cyclone, fire, and disease. Grameen banks, cooperative societies, and cooperative banks provide credit at lower interest rates. Kisan Credit Card and Personal Accident Insurance Scheme support farmers. Weather bulletins and agricultural programmes on radio and television provide useful information. Minimum Support Price, procurement prices, and remunerative prices protect farmers from exploitation. ICAR, agricultural universities, veterinary services, animal breeding centres, horticulture development, meteorology, rural infrastructure, irrigation, and electricity have also helped modernise agriculture.

## Q2. Describe the impact of globalisation on Indian agriculture.

Globalisation has affected Indian agriculture in both historical and modern ways. During colonial times, Indian spices were exported, cotton was supplied to British textile industries, and farmers in Champaran were forced to grow indigo for British industry.

After 1990, Indian farmers faced new competition from global markets. Although India is an important producer of rice, cotton, rubber, tea, coffee, jute, and spices, its products often struggle to compete with subsidised agriculture in developed countries. Reduction in import duties has also increased competition. Farmers face high input costs, uncertain prices, declining public investment, and market pressure.

Globalisation has encouraged diversification toward high-value crops such as fruits, vegetables, flowers, medicinal herbs, and bio-diesel crops. However, excessive shift away from cereals can create food security risks. Therefore, globalisation must be balanced with farmer protection, infrastructure, credit, fair prices, and sustainable farming.

## Q3. Describe the geographical conditions required for the growth of rice.

Rice is a kharif crop and the staple food of a majority of Indians. It requires high temperature above 25 degrees Celsius, high humidity, and annual rainfall above 100 cm. In regions with less rainfall, rice is grown with irrigation.

Rice is grown in the plains of north and north-eastern India, coastal areas, and deltaic regions. Important rice-growing states include Assam, West Bengal, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar. Dense canal irrigation and tubewells have also made rice cultivation possible in Punjab, Haryana, western Uttar Pradesh, and parts of Rajasthan.
