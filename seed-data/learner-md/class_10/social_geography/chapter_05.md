## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter connects two major resource groups:

1. **Minerals** - materials found in rocks and the Earth's crust, such as iron ore, manganese, copper, bauxite, mica, limestone, and coal.
2. **Energy resources** - resources used to produce heat, light, electricity, transport power, and industrial energy.

The chapter's central logic is:

**minerals and energy support development, but they are limited, unevenly distributed, costly to extract, and environmentally sensitive.**

So the chapter is not only about where minerals are found. It is also about how they form, how they are extracted, how they support industries, and why conservation is necessary.

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Minerals are essential in daily life

Minerals are used in buildings, roads, railways, machines, vehicles, utensils, electrical goods, industries, agriculture, medicines, and even food.

### 2. Minerals are unevenly distributed

Minerals are found in specific geological structures. Peninsular India is rich in coal, metallic minerals, mica, and many non-metallic minerals. The northern alluvial plains have very few economic minerals.

### 3. Minerals become mines only when extraction is viable

A mineral deposit becomes a mine only when the mineral concentration, extraction ease, cost, and market access make extraction profitable.

### 4. Minerals are finite and non-renewable

Most mineral resources take millions of years to form. Their replenishment is extremely slow compared with current consumption.

### 5. Energy is the foundation of modern development

Agriculture, industry, transport, households, communication, and services all need energy.

### 6. Renewable energy is essential for the future

Coal, petroleum, and natural gas are limited and polluting. Solar, wind, tidal, geothermal, biogas, and other renewable sources are necessary for sustainable development.

# Unit 5A: What Is a Mineral?

## Core Idea

A mineral is a homogeneous, naturally occurring substance with a definable internal structure.

Minerals are found in rocks. Some rocks are made of one mineral, such as limestone. Most rocks contain several minerals in different proportions.

## Why Minerals Differ

Minerals differ because they form under different physical and chemical conditions.

These conditions affect:

- colour
- hardness
- crystal form
- lustre
- density
- internal structure

Geologists use these properties to classify minerals.

## Minerals in Daily Life

Minerals are used in:

- pins
- utensils
- buildings
- roads
- railways
- machines
- vehicles
- ships
- aeroplanes
- electrical goods
- toothpaste
- fertilizers
- medicines
- food nutrients

## Toothpaste Example

Toothpaste contains several mineral-related materials:

| Material | Mineral Source or Use |
| --- | --- |
| Silica, limestone, aluminium oxide, phosphate minerals | Abrasive minerals used for cleaning |
| Fluoride | Comes from fluorite and helps reduce cavities |
| Titanium oxide | Makes toothpaste white; comes from rutile, ilmenite, anatase |
| Mica | Adds sparkle in some toothpastes |
| Plastic tube and toothbrush | Made from petroleum-based plastics |

## Minerals and Life

Minerals are needed by living things. Even though mineral intake is a small part of total food intake, life processes cannot happen without minerals.

# Unit 5C: Mineral Ores and Economic Viability

## What Is an Ore?

An ore is an accumulation of a mineral mixed with other elements from which the mineral can be extracted commercially.

## When Does a Deposit Become a Mine?

A mineral deposit becomes a mine only when extraction is economically viable.

Factors affecting economic viability:

| Factor | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Concentration of mineral in ore | Higher concentration makes extraction more profitable. |
| Ease of extraction | Minerals near surface or easy structures are cheaper to mine. |
| Cost of extraction | Deep, difficult, or dangerous mining increases cost. |
| Closeness to market | Nearby markets reduce transport cost. |
| Technology | Better technology can make low-grade ores usable. |
| Demand | High demand makes extraction more attractive. |

# Unit 5D: Modes of Occurrence of Minerals

Minerals occur in several forms depending on rock type, formation process, and geological conditions.

## 1. In Igneous and Metamorphic Rocks

Minerals may occur in:

- cracks
- crevices
- faults
- joints

Smaller occurrences are called veins. Larger occurrences are called lodes.

## Formation

Minerals in liquid, molten, or gaseous form are forced upward through cavities toward the Earth's surface. They cool and solidify as they rise.

## Examples

Major metallic minerals obtained from veins and lodes include:

- tin
- copper
- zinc
- lead

## Exam Answer

In igneous and metamorphic rocks, minerals occur in cracks, crevices, faults, and joints. Smaller occurrences are called veins and larger ones are called lodes. They form when minerals in molten or gaseous form move upward through cavities, cool, and solidify. Tin, copper, zinc, and lead are obtained in this way.

---

## 2. In Sedimentary Rocks

Minerals in sedimentary rocks occur in beds or layers.

## Formation

They are formed through:

- deposition
- accumulation
- concentration in horizontal strata
- heat and pressure over long periods
- evaporation in arid regions

## Examples

| Process | Minerals |
| --- | --- |
| Heat and pressure on plant material | Coal |
| Sedimentary concentration | Some forms of iron ore |
| Evaporation in arid regions | Gypsum, potash salt, sodium salt |

---

## 3. Residual Mass from Decomposition of Surface Rocks

Some minerals form when surface rocks decompose and soluble materials are removed. The remaining weathered material contains ore.

## Example

Bauxite is formed this way.

## Exam Point

If the question asks which mineral forms by decomposition of rocks leaving residual mass, the answer is **bauxite**.

---

## 4. Alluvial or Placer Deposits

Some minerals occur as alluvial deposits in sands of valley floors and the base of hills.

These are called placer deposits.

They usually contain minerals that are not corroded by water.

## Examples

- gold
- silver
- tin
- platinum

---

## 5. Ocean Waters and Ocean Beds

Ocean water contains vast quantities of minerals, but most are too widely diffused to be economically useful.

However, some minerals are obtained from ocean water:

- common salt
- magnesium
- bromine

Ocean beds are rich in manganese nodules.

---

## Modes of Occurrence Summary

| Mode | Formation | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Veins and lodes | Minerals cool and solidify in cracks of igneous/metamorphic rocks | Tin, copper, zinc, lead |
| Beds and layers | Deposition and accumulation in sedimentary rocks | Coal, some iron ore, gypsum, potash salt, sodium salt |
| Residual mass | Decomposition of surface rocks and removal of soluble materials | Bauxite |
| Placer deposits | Alluvial deposits in valley floors and hill bases | Gold, silver, tin, platinum |
| Ocean water and beds | Diffused minerals in seawater and nodules on ocean beds | Salt, magnesium, bromine, manganese nodules |

Mineral distribution depends on:

# Unit 5J: Conservation of Minerals

## Why Conservation Is Necessary

Mineral resources must be conserved because:

1. They are finite.
2. They are non-renewable.
3. They took millions of years to form.
4. Their formation is extremely slow.
5. Current consumption is very rapid.
6. Workable mineral deposits are only a tiny fraction of the Earth's crust.
7. Continued extraction increases cost.
8. Deeper mining reduces quality and raises risk.
9. Future generations also need minerals.

## Important Fact

The total volume of workable mineral deposits is an insignificant fraction, about one percent of the Earth's crust.

## How to Conserve Minerals

| Method | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Planned use | Avoid wasteful extraction and use. |
| Sustainable mining | Mine carefully with environmental controls. |
| Improved technology | Use low-grade ores at lower cost. |
| Recycling | Reuse metals instead of extracting new ores. |
| Scrap metal use | Use metal waste as raw material. |
| Substitutes | Replace scarce minerals where possible. |
| Reduce consumption | Avoid unnecessary mineral use. |

## Exam Answer: Why Conserve Minerals

Minerals are finite and non-renewable resources that take millions of years to form. Their rate of replenishment is extremely slow compared with present consumption. Workable mineral deposits are a very small part of the Earth's crust. Continued extraction increases cost because minerals must be mined from greater depths and lower quality ores. Therefore, minerals must be conserved through planned use, recycling, scrap use, improved technology, and substitutes.

# Unit 5L: Coal

## Importance

Coal is the most abundantly available fossil fuel in India. It provides a large part of India's energy needs.

## Uses

Coal is used for:

- power generation
- industrial energy
- domestic energy
- commercial energy needs

India depends heavily on coal for commercial energy.

## Formation

Coal is formed by compression of plant material over millions of years.

Different forms of coal depend on:

- degree of compression
- depth of burial
- time of burial
- temperature and pressure

## Types of Coal

| Type | Feature |
| --- | --- |
| Peat | Produced from decaying plants in swamps; low carbon, high moisture, low heating capacity |
| Lignite | Low-grade brown coal; soft, high moisture; Neyveli in Tamil Nadu has major reserves |
| Bituminous | Buried deep and exposed to higher temperatures; most popular coal for commercial use |
| Metallurgical Coal | High-grade bituminous coal used for smelting iron in blast furnaces |
| Anthracite | Highest quality hard coal |

## Coal Distribution in India

Coal in India occurs mainly in two geological ages:

1. Gondwana coal - a little over 200 million years old.
2. Tertiary coal - about 55 million years old.

## Gondwana Coal

Major Gondwana coal resources are found in:

- Damodar Valley
- Jharia
- Raniganj
- Bokaro
- Godavari Valley
- Mahanadi Valley
- Son Valley
- Wardha Valley

The Damodar Valley coalfields are in West Bengal and Jharkhand.

## Tertiary Coal

Tertiary coal occurs in north-eastern states:

- Meghalaya
- Assam
- Arunachal Pradesh
- Nagaland

## Why Industries Locate Near Coalfields

Coal is bulky and loses weight after use because it turns into ash. Therefore, heavy industries and thermal power stations are often located on or near coalfields to reduce transport costs.

## Exam Answer: Coal Distribution

Coal in India is found mainly in Gondwana and Tertiary deposits. Gondwana coal, which is over 200 million years old, is found in the Damodar Valley of West Bengal and Jharkhand, including Jharia, Raniganj, and Bokaro. Other Gondwana deposits occur in the Godavari, Mahanadi, Son, and Wardha valleys. Tertiary coal, about 55 million years old, is found in north-eastern states such as Meghalaya, Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, and Nagaland. Lignite occurs in Neyveli, Tamil Nadu.

# Unit 5M: Petroleum

## Importance

Petroleum, or mineral oil, is the next major energy source in India after coal.

## Uses

Petroleum provides:

- fuel for heating
- fuel for lighting
- lubricants for machinery
- raw material for industries

## Petroleum Refineries as Nodal Industries

Petroleum refineries act as nodal industries because they support many other industries, such as:

- synthetic textiles
- fertilizers
- chemicals

## Occurrence

Most petroleum in India is associated with anticlines and fault traps in tertiary rock formations.

## How Oil Is Trapped

Oil occurs in porous limestone or sandstone layers. It is prevented from rising or sinking by non-porous layers.

Gas is lighter and usually occurs above oil.

## Tidal Energy

## How It Works

1. Floodgate dams are built across inlets.
2. During high tide, water flows into the inlet.
3. The gate closes and traps water.
4. After the tide falls outside the floodgate, trapped water flows back to the sea.
5. This water passes through turbines and generates electricity.

## Ideal Regions in India

- Gulf of Khambhat, Gujarat
- Gulf of Kachchh, Gujarat
- Gangetic delta in the Sunderban region, West Bengal

## Geothermal Energy

## Meaning

Geothermal energy is heat and electricity produced using heat from inside the Earth.

## Why It Exists

The Earth becomes hotter with increasing depth. In areas with a high geothermal gradient, high temperatures are found at shallow depths.

Groundwater absorbs heat from rocks and becomes hot. When it rises to the surface, it may turn into steam. This steam can drive turbines and generate electricity.

## Projects in India

Two experimental geothermal projects are mentioned:

- Parvati Valley near Manikarn in Himachal Pradesh
- Puga Valley in Ladakh

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. Distinguish between ferrous and non-ferrous minerals.

Ferrous minerals contain iron, such as iron ore and manganese. They support iron, steel, and metallurgical industries. Non-ferrous minerals do not contain iron, such as copper, bauxite, lead, zinc, and gold. They are important in electrical, engineering, chemical, and metallurgical industries.

## Q2. Distinguish between conventional and non-conventional sources of energy.

Conventional energy sources have been used for a long time, such as firewood, cattle dung cake, coal, petroleum, natural gas, and hydel or thermal electricity. Non-conventional sources are newer or alternative sources such as solar, wind, tidal, geothermal, biogas, and atomic energy. Many non-conventional sources are renewable and cleaner.

## Q3. What is a mineral?

A mineral is a homogeneous, naturally occurring substance with a definable internal structure. Minerals are found in rocks and have different physical and chemical properties such as colour, hardness, crystal form, lustre, and density.

## Q4. How are minerals formed in igneous and metamorphic rocks?

In igneous and metamorphic rocks, minerals occur in cracks, crevices, faults, and joints. Smaller occurrences are called veins and larger ones are called lodes. They form when molten or gaseous minerals move upward through cavities, cool, and solidify.

## Q5. Why do we need to conserve mineral resources?

Mineral resources are finite and non-renewable. They take millions of years to form, while current consumption is very rapid. Workable mineral deposits are a tiny part of the Earth's crust. Extraction becomes costlier with depth and lower ore quality. Therefore, minerals must be conserved through recycling, planned use, scrap metal, substitutes, and improved technology.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Describe the distribution of coal in India.

Coal in India occurs mainly in Gondwana and Tertiary deposits. Gondwana coal is over 200 million years old and includes major metallurgical coal deposits. The most important Gondwana coalfields are in the Damodar Valley of West Bengal and Jharkhand, including Jharia, Raniganj, and Bokaro. Other Gondwana coal deposits are found in the Godavari, Mahanadi, Son, and Wardha valleys.

Tertiary coal is about 55 million years old and occurs in north-eastern states such as Meghalaya, Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, and Nagaland. Lignite, a low-grade brown coal, is found mainly in Neyveli, Tamil Nadu, and is used for electricity generation. Since coal is bulky and loses weight after use, thermal power stations and heavy industries are often located near coalfields.

## Q2. Why does solar energy have a bright future in India?

Solar energy has a bright future in India because India is a tropical country and receives abundant sunlight. Photovoltaic technology can convert sunlight directly into electricity. Solar energy is renewable, clean, and useful in rural and remote areas where grid electricity may be difficult.

It can reduce dependence on firewood and cattle dung cakes in rural households. This helps conserve forests and allows cattle dung to be used as manure in agriculture. Solar energy also reduces dependence on coal, petroleum, and natural gas, which are limited and polluting. Therefore, solar energy supports sustainable development and energy security.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing mineral and ore as the same thing. | Ore is a mineral accumulation from which extraction is commercially viable. |
