## How to Understand This Chapter

Goods and services do not move by themselves. They must be transported from places of supply to places of demand. People, ideas, money, information, and services also need networks.

This is why transport and communication are called lifelines.

The chapter's logic is:

**production + movement + communication + trade = economic development**

If a country produces goods but cannot move them, sell them, communicate information, or connect people, development slows down.

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Transport connects supply and demand

Goods are produced in one place and consumed in another. Transport connects farms, factories, mines, markets, ports, towns, and villages.

### 2. Communication connects people and information

Communication allows people to exchange messages, news, knowledge, business information, entertainment, and government programmes.

### 3. Trade depends on transport and communication

Local, state, national, and international trade cannot work without transport routes and communication networks.

### 4. A dense network supports development

Efficient roads, railways, waterways, airways, pipelines, postal networks, telecom, internet, and mass media speed up development.

### 5. Tourism is also trade

Tourism brings foreign exchange, employment, local market growth, cultural exchange, and national integration.

# Unit 7A: Why Transport and Communication Matter

## Core Idea

Development depends not only on production but also on movement. Goods and services must move from supply locations to demand locations.

## Example

Foodgrains grown in Punjab may be consumed in Kerala. Coal mined in Jharkhand may be used in a thermal plant in another state. Software developed in Bengaluru may be exported internationally. All of this needs transport and communication.

## Transport Domains

Transport can be classified according to the three domains of Earth:

| Domain | Transport Type |
| --- | --- |
| Land | Roadways, railways, pipelines |
| Water | Inland waterways, sea routes |
| Air | Domestic and international airways |

## Transport, Communication, and Trade Are Complementary

Transport moves goods and people.

Communication moves information.

Trade connects buyers and sellers.

Together they create:

- local markets
- national integration
- international trade
- industrial growth
- agricultural exchange
- tourism
- social and cultural contact

# Unit 7B: Roadways

## Importance

India has one of the largest road networks in the world. The chapter mentions about 54.7 lakh km of roads in 2014-15.

Roadways came before railways in India and still have several advantages.

## Merits of Roadways

| Merit | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Lower construction cost | Roads cost less to build than railway lines. |
| Difficult terrain | Roads can cross dissected and undulating topography. |
| Mountain slopes | Roads can negotiate higher gradients and reach mountains. |
| Short-distance transport | Economical for few passengers and small quantities of goods over short distances. |
| Door-to-door service | Reduces loading and unloading costs. |
| Feeder role | Links villages, farms, railway stations, ports, and airports. |
| Flexibility | Routes and timing are more flexible than railways. |

## Road Classification in India

Roads are classified into six major classes:

1. Golden Quadrilateral Super Highways
2. National Highways
3. State Highways
4. District Roads
5. Other Roads
6. Border Roads

---

## Golden Quadrilateral Super Highways

The Golden Quadrilateral links:

- Delhi
- Kolkata
- Chennai
- Mumbai
- Delhi

It is a six-lane super highway project.

## North-South and East-West Corridors

| Corridor | Connects |
| --- | --- |
| North-South Corridor | Srinagar in Jammu and Kashmir to Kanniyakumari in Tamil Nadu |
| East-West Corridor | Silchar in Assam to Porbandar in Gujarat |

## Objective

The main objective is to reduce time and distance between India's mega cities and major regions.

## Implementing Agency

These projects are implemented by the National Highways Authority of India.

---

## National Highways

National Highways link extreme parts of the country. They are the primary road system.

They are laid and maintained by the Central Public Works Department.

Important examples from the chapter:

| Highway | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Sher Shah Suri Marg | National Highway No. 1 between Delhi and Amritsar |
| National Highway 7 | Longest highway in the chapter's data, between Varanasi and Kanniyakumari via Jabalpur, Nagpur, Hyderabad, Bengaluru, and Madurai |
| National Highway 8 | Connects Delhi and Mumbai |
| National Highway 15 | Covers most of Rajasthan |

---

## State Highways

State Highways link a state capital with different district headquarters.

They are constructed and maintained by the State Public Works Department in states and Union Territories.

## District Roads

District roads connect district headquarters with other places in the district.

They are maintained by the Zila Parishad.

## Other Roads

Other roads include rural roads that link villages and rural areas with towns.

The Pradhan Mantri Grameen Sadak Yojana gave special support to rural roads so that every village could be linked to a major town by all-season motorable roads.

## Border Roads

Border Roads Organisation constructs and maintains roads in border areas.

It was established in 1960 for strategic roads in northern and north-eastern border areas.

## Significance of Border Roads

Border roads:

- improve accessibility in difficult terrain
- help defence movement
- support strategic security
- encourage economic development in border areas
- connect remote communities
- support administration and supply movement

---

## Metalled and Unmetalled Roads

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Metalled Roads | Made of cement, concrete, or bitumen; all-weather roads |
| Unmetalled Roads | Not all-weather; often unusable during rainy season |

## Road Density

Road density means the length of road per 100 sq km of area.

## Road Density Variation

Road distribution is not uniform in India.

The chapter mentions:

- Jammu and Kashmir: 12.14 km per 100 sq km
- Kerala: 517.77 km per 100 sq km
- National average: 142.68 km per 100 sq km

## Problems of Road Transport

Road transport faces several problems:

- road network is inadequate for traffic and passengers
- many roads are unmetalled
- unmetalled roads cannot be used easily in rainy season
- National Highways are inadequate
- city roads are highly congested
- bridges and culverts are old and narrow

# Unit 7C: Railways

## Importance

Railways are the principal mode of transportation for freight and passengers in India.

They carry people and goods over long distances and support:

- business
- sightseeing
- pilgrimage
- agriculture
- industry
- national integration

## Historical Beginning

The first train in India ran from Mumbai to Thane in 1853, covering 34 km.

## Indian Railways as Integrating Force

Indian Railways have been a great integrating force for more than 150 years because they connect distant regions, cultures, markets, and production areas.

## Why Railways Are Important for the Economy

Railways:

- carry heavy goods over long distances
- move passengers affordably
- connect raw material areas with industries
- connect farms with markets
- support defence movement
- promote tourism and pilgrimage
- accelerate industrial and agricultural development

## Railway Distribution Factors

Railway network distribution is influenced by:

- physiography
- economic factors
- administrative needs
- population density
- agricultural resources
- industrial activity

## Where Railways Are Most Convenient

Rail transport is most convenient in the northern plains because:

- land is level
- population density is high
- agricultural resources are rich
- towns and markets are numerous
- construction is easier than in mountains

## Obstacles to Railway Construction

| Region | Difficulty |
| --- | --- |
| Northern plains | Many rivers require bridges |
| Peninsular hills | Tracks need gaps, passes, and tunnels |
| Himalayan region | High relief, sparse population, fewer economic opportunities |
| Western Rajasthan | Sandy plains |
| Gujarat | Swamps |
| Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Jharkhand | Forested tracts |
| Western Ghats | Need passes through the Sahyadri |

## Konkan Railway

The Konkan Railway along the west coast improved movement of passengers and goods in an important economic region.

Problems faced:

- sinking of tracks in some stretches
- landslides

## Problems of Rail Transport

- ticketless travel
- theft of railway property
- damage to railway property
- unnecessary chain pulling
- stopping trains without reason
- delays and losses

# Unit 7D: Pipelines

## Meaning

Pipeline transport moves liquids, gases, and slurry through pipelines.

Earlier pipelines were mainly used to transport water to cities and industries. Now they transport:

- crude oil
- petroleum products
- natural gas
- slurry

## Advantages

| Advantage | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Low running cost | Initial cost is high, but later operating cost is low. |
| No trans-shipment loss | Goods do not need repeated loading and unloading. |
| No delays | Continuous movement is possible. |
| Inland refineries possible | Refineries far from coasts can operate through pipelines. |
| Useful for gas-based industries | Fertilizer and power plants can be far from gas fields. |

## Important Pipeline Networks

### 1. Upper Assam to Kanpur Pipeline

Route:

- upper Assam oilfields
- Guwahati
- Barauni
- Allahabad
- Kanpur

Branches:

- Barauni to Haldia via Rajbandh
- Rajbandh to Maurigram
- Guwahati to Siliguri

### 2. Salaya to Jalandhar Pipeline

Route:

- Salaya in Gujarat
- Viramgam
- Mathura
- Delhi
- Sonipat
- Jalandhar in Punjab

Branches connect Koyali near Vadodara, Chakshu, and other places.

### 3. Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur Pipeline

This gas pipeline connects:

- Hazira in Gujarat
- Vijaipur in Madhya Pradesh
- Jagdishpur in Uttar Pradesh

Branches go to:

- Kota in Rajasthan
- Shahjahanpur in Uttar Pradesh
- Babrala in Uttar Pradesh
- other places

## Exam Point

Maharashtra is not connected with the HVJ pipeline in the NCERT MCQ options.

Air travel is:

- fastest

- prestigious
- useful over difficult terrain

It can cross:

- high mountains

Air travel is preferred in the north-eastern states because the region has:

- big rivers

- frequent floods
- international frontiers

Mass communication provides:

- entertainment

# Unit 7I: International Trade

## Meaning of Trade

Trade means exchange of goods among people, states, or countries.

The market is the place where exchange takes place.

## Types of Trade

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Local Trade | Trade within villages, towns, or cities |
| State-Level Trade | Trade between two or more states |
| International Trade | Trade between two or more countries |

## International Trade Routes

International trade may take place through:

- sea routes
- air routes
- land routes

## Why International Trade Matters

No country can survive without international trade because resources are space-bound. Different countries have different resources, technologies, climates, skills, and markets.

International trade is considered an economic barometer of a country because it reflects economic prosperity and global linkages.

## Export and Import

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Export | Goods and services sold to another country |
| Import | Goods and services bought from another country |

## Balance of Trade

Balance of trade is the difference between the value of exports and imports.

| Type | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Favourable balance of trade | Export value is greater than import value |
| Unfavourable balance of trade | Import value is greater than export value |

## India's Trade

India has trade relations with all major trading blocs and geographical regions.

Examples of exports mentioned:

- agriculture and allied products
- base metals
- gems and jewellery
- chemicals and related products

Examples of imports mentioned:

- petroleum crude and products
- gems and jewellery
- chemicals
- base metals
- electronic items
- machinery
- agriculture and allied products

## Changing Nature of International Trade

International trade has changed over recent decades.

Earlier, trade focused more on commodities and physical goods. Now, information and knowledge have become very important.

India has emerged as a software giant and earns foreign exchange through export of information technology and software services.

# Short Answer Questions

## Q1. State any three merits of roadways.

Roadways have lower construction cost than railways, can cross dissected and undulating terrain, and can provide door-to-door service. They are also economical for short distances and act as feeders to railways, ports, and airports.

## Q2. Where and why is rail transport the most convenient means of transportation?

Rail transport is most convenient in the northern plains because the land is level, population density is high, agricultural resources are rich, and towns and markets are numerous. These conditions support easy construction and high use of railways.

## Q3. What is the significance of border roads?

Border roads are important for strategic defence, movement of troops and supplies, and accessibility in difficult border terrain. They also help economic development of remote northern and north-eastern areas by connecting them with the rest of the country.

## Q4. What is trade? What is the difference between international and local trade?

Trade is the exchange of goods among people, states, or countries. Local trade takes place within villages, towns, or cities. International trade takes place between two or more countries through sea, air, or land routes.

# Long Answer Questions

## Q1. Why are means of transportation and communication called the lifelines of a nation and its economy?

Means of transportation and communication are called lifelines because they connect different parts of a country and support economic development. Transport moves goods and people from supply areas to demand areas. It connects farms, factories, markets, mines, ports, towns, villages, and international trade routes.

Communication allows fast exchange of information, ideas, news, business messages, government programmes, and knowledge. Transport and communication together support agriculture, industry, trade, tourism, defence, administration, and national integration.

They also connect India with the rest of the world. Railways, roadways, waterways, airways, pipelines, newspapers, radio, television, internet, and telecom networks contribute to social and economic progress. Without them, production cannot reach markets and people cannot remain connected.

## Q2. Write a note on the changing nature of international trade in recent decades.

International trade has changed greatly in recent decades. Earlier, trade was mainly based on exchange of physical commodities and goods. Countries exported agricultural products, minerals, manufactured goods, and imported resources they lacked.

Today, the exchange of information, knowledge, technology, and services has become very important. India has emerged as a software giant at the international level and earns large foreign exchange through export of information technology and software services.

India still trades in goods such as agriculture and allied products, gems and jewellery, base metals, chemicals, petroleum products, machinery, electronic items, and other commodities. However, services, IT, and knowledge-based trade now form an important part of India's international economic relations.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing transport alone is the lifeline. | Transport, communication, and trade together act as lifelines. |
| Confusing East-West and North-South Corridor. | East-West: Silchar-Porbandar. North-South: Srinagar-Kanniyakumari. |
| Calling pipelines cheap to build. | Initial cost is high, but running cost is low. |
| Confusing Kandla and Mumbai ports. | Kandla is a tidal port; Mumbai is the biggest port with natural harbour. |
| Confusing Vishakhapatnam and Chennai. | Vishakhapatnam is deepest landlocked and well-protected; Chennai is an old artificial port. |
| Writing trade only means international trade. | Trade can be local, state-level, national, or international. |
| Forgetting balance of trade. | Balance of trade is export value minus import value. |
| Treating tourism as only recreation. | Tourism is trade because it creates income, jobs, and foreign exchange. |

## Lifelines

Transport moves goods and people. Communication moves information. Trade connects buyers and sellers. Together they support development.

## Corridors

Golden Quadrilateral: Delhi-Kolkata-Chennai-Mumbai-Delhi.

North-South: Srinagar-Kanniyakumari.

East-West: Silchar-Porbandar.

## Pipelines

Upper Assam-Kanpur.

Salaya-Jalandhar.

HVJ: Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur.

## Ports

Kandla tidal, Mumbai biggest, JNPT decongests Mumbai, Marmagao iron ore, New Mangalore Kudremukh, Kochchi lagoon, Tuticorin south-east, Chennai artificial, Vishakhapatnam deepest landlocked, Paradwip iron ore, Kolkata riverine, Haldia relieves Kolkata.

## Trade

International trade = between countries.

Favourable balance = exports greater than imports.

Unfavourable balance = imports greater than exports.

## Tourism

Earns foreign exchange, creates jobs, supports handicrafts, promotes culture, national integration, and international understanding.
