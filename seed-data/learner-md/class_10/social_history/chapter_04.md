## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter challenges a simple idea:

**Industrialisation was not just a smooth story of machines replacing hand labour and factories replacing homes.**

The chapter shows a more complex history.

Before factories became important, large-scale production already existed. Merchants controlled production, but workers produced goods in village homes. This early form is called **proto-industrialisation**.

Even after factories appeared, hand labour remained important. In Britain, many industrialists preferred workers instead of machines because labour was plentiful, machines were expensive, and many products required skill.

In India, industrialisation took place under colonial rule. Indian textiles had once dominated world markets, but colonial power changed trade routes, controlled weavers, brought in cheap machine-made cloth from Manchester, and weakened many traditional producers. Later, Indian mills grew, but they grew in a colonial economy shaped by British interests.

The key idea is:

**Industrialisation created new production systems, but it also created conflict over labour, technology, markets, colonial control, and consumer choice.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Factories were not the beginning of industrial production

Before factories, merchants already organised large-scale production for world markets through rural households.

### 2. Industrialisation was uneven

Factories grew, but traditional industries, workshops, handloom weaving, and household production continued for a long time.

### 3. Technology did not spread automatically

Machines were costly, could break down, needed repair, and were not always suitable for all types of work.

### 4. Labour conditions mattered

Industrialists made choices based on wages, labour supply, seasonality, skill, and cost. Workers faced unemployment, long hours, insecurity, and poverty.

### 5. Colonialism changed Indian industry

Indian producers did not compete in a free market. They worked under colonial power, Company monopoly, gomasthas, cheap imports, and changing trade routes.

### 6. Indian industry still adapted

Indian entrepreneurs built mills, handloom weavers adopted new tools, and nationalist politics encouraged swadeshi goods.

### 7. Markets had to be created

Industrial goods did not sell automatically. Manufacturers used labels, images, calendars, gods, rulers, and nationalist symbols to persuade consumers.

# Unit 4A: Industrialisation and the Idea of Progress

## Core Idea

The chapter begins with images that presented industrialisation as a glorious sign of progress.

One image showed an "angel of progress" moving into the future with symbols such as railways, machines, cameras, factories, and the printing press. Another image compared an eastern magician, Aladdin, with a modern mechanic from the West.

These images suggested that:

| Image Message | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Machines represent progress | Industrial society was presented as superior and modern. |
| The West represents modernity | Europe was shown as advanced and rational. |
| The East represents the past | Asia was shown through stereotypes as traditional or backward. |
| Technology creates wonder | Bridges, ships, railways, and factories were shown as magical achievements. |

## Why the Chapter Questions This Image

The chapter asks us not to accept this picture blindly.

Industrialisation did produce factories, railways, ships, machines, and mass production. But it also produced:

- harsh working conditions,
- unemployment,
- insecurity for hand workers,
- colonial exploitation,
- destruction of old trading networks,
- pressure on weavers,
- uneven growth,
- environmental and social problems.

## Learner Understanding

When you see the word **progress**, ask:

| Question | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Progress for whom? | Industrialists may gain while workers suffer. |
| Progress at what cost? | Machines may increase output but reduce jobs. |
| Who controls production? | Merchants, companies, or colonial rulers may control workers. |
| Who gets the market? | Imported machine goods may destroy local producers. |
| What is hidden? | Factories may be visible, but home-based labour and small workshops may remain invisible. |

## Exam Point

Industrialisation should not be described only as technological progress. In history, it must be studied through workers, producers, markets, colonies, capital, and power.

# Unit 4B: Before the Industrial Revolution

## Core Idea

Large-scale industrial production existed even before factories became common.

This early system is called **proto-industrialisation**.

## What Was Proto-Industrialisation?

Proto-industrialisation was a system in which merchants from towns supplied money and raw materials to rural peasants and artisans, who produced goods for international markets.

It was:

- large-scale,
- market-oriented,
- controlled by merchants,
- based in the countryside,
- carried out within family households,
- not based on factories.

## Why Did Merchants Move to the Countryside?

Merchants wanted to expand production, but towns had powerful guilds.

| Reason | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Guild control | Guilds controlled training, prices, competition, and entry into trades. |
| Monopoly rights | Rulers granted guilds exclusive rights to produce and sell certain goods. |
| Difficulty in towns | New merchants could not easily start production in towns. |
| Growing world demand | Colonies and world trade increased demand for goods. |
| Rural labour available | Poor peasants and artisans needed extra income. |

## Why Did Peasants Agree?

Peasants and cottagers were under pressure because common lands were being enclosed.

Earlier, poor peasants depended on commons for:

- firewood,
- berries,
- vegetables,
- hay,
- straw,
- grazing,
- supplementary survival.

When commons disappeared, they needed new sources of income.

Merchant advances helped them:

- stay in the countryside,
- continue cultivating small plots,
- use family labour,
- earn extra income,
- survive shrinking agricultural income.

## How Cloth Production Worked

Proto-industrial cloth production moved through many stages.

| Stage | Worker or Process |
| --- | --- |
| Wool purchased | Merchant clothier bought wool from a wool stapler. |
| Spinning | Wool was taken to rural spinners. |
| Weaving | Yarn went to weavers. |
| Fulling | Cloth was gathered and thickened by fullers. |
| Dyeing | Cloth was dyed. |
| Finishing | Final finishing was done in London. |
| Export | Export merchant sold cloth in international markets. |

## Town-Countryside Link

Proto-industrialisation created a close relationship between towns and villages:

- merchants were based in towns,
- production happened in rural homes,
- goods moved through many workers,
- London became a finishing centre,
- export markets shaped rural labour.

## Important Fact

Each merchant could control hundreds of workers because each stage employed many people.

## Answer Frame: What is Proto-Industrialisation?

Proto-industrialisation refers to the phase of large-scale production before factories. In seventeenth- and eighteenth-century Europe, merchants from towns moved to the countryside because guilds restricted production in towns. They gave advances to peasants and artisans, who produced goods in their homes for international markets. This system connected towns and countryside and allowed merchants to control production without using factories.

# Unit 4C: The Coming Up of the Factory

## Core Idea

Factories appeared in England in the eighteenth century, but they became important gradually.

## Early Factories

| Fact | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Earliest factories | Came up in England by the 1730s. |
| Growth | Factory numbers multiplied in the late eighteenth century. |
| First symbol | Cotton became the main symbol of the new industrial age. |
| Cotton import | Britain imported 2.5 million pounds of raw cotton in 1760. |
| Cotton import by 1787 | It increased to 22 million pounds. |

## Why Cotton Production Expanded

A series of inventions improved the production process.

They increased efficiency in:

- carding,
- twisting,
- spinning,
- rolling.

These inventions:

- increased output per worker,
- produced stronger thread and yarn,
- made machine-based production more attractive.

## Richard Arkwright and the Cotton Mill

Richard Arkwright created the cotton mill.

This was important because production could now be brought under one roof.

| Before the Mill | After the Mill |
| --- | --- |
| Production spread across villages | Production concentrated in one place |
| Family households produced cloth | Workers worked under factory management |
| Supervision was difficult | Supervision became easier |
| Quality control was limited | Quality could be watched closely |
| Labour was scattered | Labour could be regulated |

## Why Factories Dazzled People

Factories became visible signs of industrial modernity:

- large buildings,
- big machines,
- concentrated workers,
- smoke-filled industrial towns,
- disciplined production,
- dramatic increase in output.

But this visibility also created a problem:

**People began noticing factories and ignoring small workshops and household production that continued outside factories.**

# Unit 4D: The Pace of Industrial Change

## Core Idea

Industrial change was not as fast, complete, or machine-dominated as people often imagine.

## First Point: Cotton and Metals Were Dynamic

Cotton led the first phase of industrialisation up to the 1840s.

After that, iron and steel became more important.

| Industry | Reason for Growth |
| --- | --- |
| Cotton | Demand for textile production and exports. |
| Iron and steel | Expansion of railways in Britain and colonies. |

Important fact:

By 1873, Britain exported iron and steel worth about 77 million pounds, which was double the value of its cotton exports.

## Second Point: Traditional Industries Survived

New industries did not easily replace traditional industries.

Even at the end of the nineteenth century, less than 20 percent of the total workforce worked in technologically advanced industrial sectors.

Textiles were dynamic, but much textile output was still produced outside factories in domestic units.

## Third Point: Small Innovations Mattered

Traditional sectors were not stagnant.

Growth occurred in non-mechanised sectors such as:

- food processing,
- building,
- pottery,
- glass work,
- tanning,
- furniture making,
- production of tools and implements.

This means that industrialisation included both big machines and small improvements.

## Fourth Point: Technology Spread Slowly

Machines did not spread everywhere immediately.

| Problem With New Technology | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Expensive | Merchants and industrialists hesitated before investing. |
| Breakdowns | Machines often broke down. |
| Repair cost | Repair was costly. |
| Exaggerated claims | Machines were not always as effective as inventors claimed. |
| Labour availability | In places with plenty of labour, machines were less urgent. |

## Steam Engine Example

James Watt improved Newcomen's steam engine and patented the improved engine in 1781.

His industrialist friend Mathew Boulton manufactured the new model.

But buyers were few for many years.

At the beginning of the nineteenth century:

| Use of Steam Engines in England | Number |
| --- | --- |
| Total steam engines | No more than 321 |
| Cotton industries | 80 |
| Wool industries | 9 |
| Others | Mining, canal works, and iron works |

## Important Conclusion

The typical worker in mid-nineteenth-century Britain was not necessarily a machine operator. Many workers were still traditional craftspersons and labourers.

# Unit 4E: Hand Labour and Steam Power

## Core Idea

In Victorian Britain, industrialists often preferred hand labour over machines.

This seems surprising, but the reasons were practical.

## Why Industrialists Preferred Hand Labour

### 1. Labour was abundant

Poor peasants and migrants moved to cities in large numbers looking for work.

When labour supply is high, wages remain low.

Low wages made hand labour cheaper than expensive machines.

### 2. Machines required large capital

Machines were costly to buy, maintain, and repair.

Industrialists did not want to invest heavily if cheap workers were available.

### 3. Many industries had seasonal demand

Some industries needed extra workers only during busy seasons.

| Industry | Busy Season or Reason |
| --- | --- |
| Gas works | Cold months |
| Breweries | Cold months |
| Bookbinding | Before Christmas |
| Printing | Before Christmas |
| Ship repair | Winter at the waterfront |

For seasonal industries, hiring temporary workers was easier than buying machines.

### 4. Some goods required skill

Machines were useful for uniform goods, but many products needed detailed human skill.

Examples:

- 500 varieties of hammers,
- 45 kinds of axes,
- goods with intricate designs,
- goods with special shapes.

### 5. Upper classes preferred handmade goods

The aristocracy and bourgeoisie preferred handmade products because they symbolised:

- refinement,
- class,
- careful design,
- individuality,
- better finish.

Machine-made goods were often sent for export to colonies.

## Comparison: Hand Labour and Machines

| Basis | Hand Labour | Machines |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cost in Britain | Cheap because labour was abundant | Expensive to install and repair |
| Flexibility | Easy to hire seasonally | Less flexible for seasonal demand |
| Product type | Good for detailed and varied goods | Good for uniform mass goods |
| Social value | Handmade goods valued by elites | Machine goods often sold to mass markets and colonies |
| Worker impact | Provided employment but often insecure | Increased output but could reduce labour demand |

# Unit 4F: Life of Workers in Britain

## Core Idea

Industrialisation created work, but not security.

Workers faced irregular employment, poverty, dependence on networks, and fear of machines.

## Getting Work Was Difficult

Many people came to cities after hearing about possible jobs.

But employment depended on:

- existing vacancies,
- friendship networks,
- kinship ties,
- knowledge of factories,
- help from someone already employed.

If a worker had a relative or friend in a factory, getting work was easier.

Those without connections often waited for weeks.

## Where Did Job-Seekers Stay?

Many job-seekers had no secure housing.

They spent nights:

- under bridges,
- in night shelters,
- in Night Refuges run by private individuals,
- in Casual Wards maintained by Poor Law authorities.

## Seasonal Work Created Insecurity

When the busy season ended, workers were thrown out again.

Some returned to the countryside when rural labour demand opened. Others searched for odd jobs.

Until the mid-nineteenth century, odd jobs were hard to find.

## Wages Did Not Tell the Full Story

Even if wages rose somewhat, worker welfare did not necessarily improve.

Why?

| Factor | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Price rise | Same wage bought fewer goods. |
| Napoleonic Wars | Prices rose sharply, reducing real wages. |
| Irregular employment | Income depended on number of days worked. |
| Trade differences | Some trades paid better than others. |
| Economic slumps | Unemployment increased sharply. |

At the best of times until the mid-nineteenth century, about 10 percent of the urban population was extremely poor.

During slumps like the 1830s, unemployment rose to between 35 and 75 percent in different regions.

## Why Workers Opposed the Spinning Jenny

The Spinning Jenny was introduced by James Hargreaves in 1764.

It allowed one worker to spin several threads at the same time.

Women who survived on hand spinning saw it as a threat because:

- it reduced demand for hand spinners,
- it could take away their livelihood,
- it concentrated work under machines,
- it allowed fewer workers to produce more,
- it weakened the bargaining position of hand workers.

## Why Building Activity Helped Workers After the 1840s

After the 1840s, building activity increased in cities.

This created more work in:

- road widening,
- railway stations,
- railway lines,
- tunnels,
- drainage,
- sewers,
- embankments,
- construction sites.

The number of workers in transport doubled in the 1840s and doubled again in the next 30 years.

## Exam Point

Do not write that machines immediately improved all workers' lives. Many workers feared machines because machines could reduce employment and weaken hand-based livelihoods.

# Unit 4G: Industrialisation in the Colonies

## Core Idea

India's industrialisation happened under colonial rule, so it followed a different path from Britain.

The chapter focuses mainly on textiles.

## The Age of Indian Textiles

Before machine industries, Indian silk and cotton goods dominated international textile markets.

Coarser cottons were made in many countries, but fine textiles often came from India.

## Trade Routes of Indian Textiles

| Route | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Land route | Armenian and Persian merchants carried goods from Punjab to Afghanistan, eastern Persia, and Central Asia. |
| Sea route | Fine textiles moved through major pre-colonial ports. |
| Surat | Linked India with Gulf and Red Sea ports. |
| Masulipatam | Linked Coromandel coast with overseas trade. |
| Hoogly | Linked Bengal with Southeast Asian ports. |

## Role of Indian Merchants and Bankers

Indian merchants and bankers:

- financed production,
- gave advances,
- connected port towns with inland weaving regions,
- carried goods to ports,
- supplied exporters,
- negotiated with brokers,
- supported long-distance trade.

## Decline of Old Ports

By the 1750s, the old trade network controlled by Indian merchants began breaking down.

European companies gained power by:

- securing concessions from local courts,
- gaining monopoly rights,
- controlling trade routes,
- using European ships,
- shifting trade to colonial ports.

## Decline of Surat

Important fact:

The gross value of trade through Surat was about Rs 16 million in the last years of the seventeenth century. By the 1740s it had fallen to about Rs 3 million.

## Bombay and Calcutta Grew

As Surat and Hoogly declined, Bombay and Calcutta grew.

This showed:

- the decline of the old trading order,
- the rise of colonial ports,
- greater control by European companies,
- the growth of a colonial economy.

# Unit 4H: What Happened to Indian Weavers?

## Core Idea

The East India Company first wanted Indian textiles for export, but after gaining political power it controlled weavers more harshly.

## Before Company Control

Before the Company established strong political power:

- French, Dutch, Portuguese, English, and local traders competed for woven cloth.
- Weavers could bargain.
- Supply merchants could sell to the best buyer.
- Company officials complained about high prices and difficulty in securing supply.

## After Company Power

After the Company gained political power in Bengal and Carnatic in the 1760s and 1770s, it tried to control production directly.

## How the Company Controlled Weavers

### Step 1: It eliminated existing traders and brokers

The Company wanted direct control over weavers.

It appointed a paid servant called the **gomastha**.

The gomastha:

- supervised weavers,
- collected supplies,
- examined cloth quality,
- enforced Company orders.

### Step 2: It stopped weavers from selling to others

The Company gave advances or loans to weavers.

Once weavers accepted advances:

- they had to buy raw material,
- produce for the Company,
- hand over cloth to the gomastha,
- avoid selling to any other trader.

## Why Advances Became a Trap

At first, weavers accepted advances because demand for fine textiles was rising and they hoped to earn more.

But soon problems grew:

- they had to lease out small plots of land,
- entire families had to work in weaving,
- they lost freedom to sell to different buyers,
- prices paid by the Company were low,
- loans tied them to the Company,
- gomasthas acted arrogantly and used coercion.

## Gomastha vs Earlier Supply Merchant

| Earlier Supply Merchant | Company Gomastha |
| --- | --- |
| Often lived near or within weaving villages | Usually an outsider |
| Had long-term social ties with weavers | Had no close village relationship |
| Helped weavers in times of crisis | Enforced Company control |
| Negotiated through local relations | Used sepoys and peons |
| Was part of older trade networks | Represented colonial monopoly |

## Weaver Resistance

In many places:

- weavers deserted villages,
- some migrated to villages where they had family ties,
- some revolted with local traders,
- some refused Company loans,
- some closed workshops,
- some became agricultural labourers.

## Exam Answer: Why Did the Company Appoint Gomasthas?

The East India Company appointed gomasthas to control textile production directly. The gomasthas supervised weavers, collected supplies, checked cloth quality, and prevented weavers from selling to other buyers. This helped the Company eliminate competition, control costs, and ensure regular supplies of cotton and silk goods for export.

# Unit 4I: Manchester Comes to India

## Core Idea

Indian weavers faced a new crisis in the nineteenth century when machine-made British textiles entered Indian markets.

## Decline of Indian Textile Exports

In 1772, Company official Henry Patullo believed Indian textiles would always remain in demand because of their quality.

But by the early nineteenth century, textile exports from India began declining.

| Year | Share of Piece-Goods in Indian Exports |
| --- | --- |
| 1811-12 | 33 percent |
| 1850-51 | About 3 percent |

## Why Did Indian Textile Exports Decline?

### 1. British industrialists wanted protection

As cotton industries grew in England, British industrialists worried about competition from Indian textiles.

They pressured the British government to impose import duties on cotton textiles.

This helped Manchester goods sell in Britain without Indian competition.

### 2. British goods were pushed into India

British industrialists also persuaded the East India Company to sell British manufactures in Indian markets.

### 3. Cheap machine goods flooded Indian markets

Machine-made cotton goods were cheaper than handwoven Indian cloth.

By 1850, cotton piece-goods formed more than 31 percent of the value of Indian imports. By the 1870s, the figure was over 50 percent.

## Two Problems Faced by Indian Weavers

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Export market collapsed | Indian textiles faced duties and declining demand abroad. |
| Local market shrank | Cheap Manchester imports flooded India. |

## Raw Cotton Problem in the 1860s

During the American Civil War, cotton supplies from the United States to Britain were cut off.

Britain turned to India for raw cotton.

As raw cotton exports from India increased:

- raw cotton prices rose,
- Indian weavers could not get good cotton easily,
- they had to buy cotton at very high prices,
- weaving became less profitable.

## New Problem: Indian Factories

By the end of the nineteenth century, Indian factories began producing machine-made goods.

This created another pressure on handloom weavers.

## Exam Point

Indian weavers were hit from three sides:

1. Company control and gomasthas.
2. Cheap Manchester imports.
3. Rising raw cotton prices and later competition from Indian mills.

# Unit 4J: Factories Come Up in India

## Core Idea

Modern factories began appearing in India in the nineteenth century, especially in cotton and jute.

## Early Indian Factories

| Year / Period | Development |
| --- | --- |
| 1854 | First cotton mill came up in Bombay. |
| 1856 | Bombay cotton mill went into production. |
| 1862 | Four Bombay mills were working with 94,000 spindles and 2,150 looms. |
| 1855 | First jute mill came up in Bengal. |
| 1862 | Another jute mill came up in Bengal. |
| 1860s | Elgin Mill started in Kanpur. |
| 1860s | First cotton mill of Ahmedabad was set up. |
| 1874 | First spinning and weaving mill of Madras began production. |

## Early Entrepreneurs

Indian industrial capital came from different trade networks.

### China Trade Connection

Many Indian business groups had links with trade with China.

The British exported opium from India to China and imported tea from China to England.

Indian businessmen participated as:

- financiers,
- suppliers,
- shippers,
- junior partners in trade.

Some later invested in industries.

## Important Entrepreneurs

| Entrepreneur / Group | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Dwarkanath Tagore | Made money in China trade; invested in shipping, mining, banking, plantations, insurance, and joint-stock companies. |
| Dinshaw Petit | Parsi industrialist in Bombay; wealth partly linked to China trade and raw cotton exports. |
| Jamsetjee Nusserwanjee Tata | Built major industrial enterprises; wealth partly linked to China trade and raw cotton shipments. |
| Seth Hukumchand | Marwari businessman; set up the first Indian jute mill in Calcutta in 1917. |
| G.D. Birla's family | Earlier generations were connected with trade, including China trade. |
| Madras merchants | Some traded with Burma, the Middle East, and East Africa. |

## Limits on Indian Merchants

As colonial control tightened:

- Indian merchants were barred from trading with Europe in manufactured goods,
- they had to export mostly raw materials and food grains,
- they exported raw cotton, opium, wheat, and indigo,
- they were edged out of the shipping business.

## European Managing Agencies

Until the First World War, European Managing Agencies controlled a large part of Indian industry.

Examples:

- Bird Heiglers & Co.,
- Andrew Yule,
- Jardine Skinner & Co.

These agencies:

- mobilised capital,
- set up joint-stock companies,
- managed industrial enterprises,
- made investment decisions.

In many cases, Indian financiers provided capital, but European agencies made the major business decisions.

Indian businessmen were often excluded from European chambers of commerce.

# Unit 4K: Where Did Indian Factory Workers Come From?

## Core Idea

Indian factories needed labour, but most workers came from nearby rural districts and remained connected with villages.

## Growth of Factory Workers

| Year | Number of Workers in Indian Factories |
| --- | --- |
| 1901 | 584,000 |
| 1946 | Over 2,436,000 |

## Sources of Labour

Factory workers often came from nearby districts.

| Industrial Region | Worker Source |
| --- | --- |
| Bombay cotton mills | More than 50 percent came from Ratnagiri district in 1911. |
| Kanpur mills | Many came from villages within Kanpur district. |
| Bombay textile mills | Workers also came from United Provinces. |
| Calcutta jute mills | Workers came from distant rural areas including United Provinces. |

## Village-City Link

Millworkers often moved between city and village.

They returned home during:

- harvest,
- sowing,
- festivals,
- family needs.

This means factory labour did not completely cut workers off from rural life.

## The Jobber System

Industrialists employed **jobbers** to recruit workers.

A jobber was often an old and trusted worker.

## Role of the Jobber

| Function | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Recruitment | Brought people from his village or region. |
| Settlement | Helped new workers settle in the city. |
| Support | Provided money or help during crisis. |
| Control | Became powerful because workers depended on him. |
| Corruption | Sometimes demanded money and gifts for jobs. |

## Worker Conditions

Workers faced:

- long shifts,
- insecure employment,
- dependence on jobbers,
- poor health,
- low bargaining power,
- movement between village and city,
- harsh working hours.

## Exam Point

Indian factory workers were not simply urban industrial workers. Many remained physically and emotionally connected to villages.

# Unit 4L: Peculiarities of Industrial Growth in India

## Core Idea

Industrial growth in India had a special character because it occurred inside a colonial economy.

## What European Managing Agencies Produced

European agencies invested mainly in export-oriented sectors such as:

- tea plantations,
- coffee plantations,
- mining,
- indigo,
- jute.

These products were mainly required for export, not for Indian domestic consumption.

## Why Indian Mills First Produced Yarn

Indian businessmen avoided direct competition with Manchester cloth.

Since yarn was not an important part of British imports into India, early Indian cotton mills produced coarse cotton yarn rather than fabric.

This yarn was:

- used by Indian handloom weavers,
- exported to China.

## Changes in the Early Twentieth Century

Several developments changed industrial patterns:

| Change | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Swadeshi movement | Nationalists encouraged boycott of foreign cloth. |
| Industrial associations | Indian industrial groups demanded tariff protection and concessions. |
| Decline of yarn exports to China | Chinese and Japanese mills supplied the Chinese market. |
| Shift from yarn to cloth | Indian mills began producing more cloth. |

Important fact:

Cotton piece-goods production in India doubled between 1900 and 1912.

## First World War and Indian Industry

The First World War created a new situation.

British mills became busy producing war goods for the army.

Manchester imports into India declined.

Indian mills suddenly had a larger home market.

Indian factories were also asked to supply war needs such as:

- jute bags,
- cloth for army uniforms,
- tents,
- leather boots,
- horse saddles,
- mule saddles,
- other war materials.

## Effects of the War

| Effect | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| New factories | More factories were set up. |
| Multiple shifts | Old factories worked longer. |
| More workers | More labour was employed. |
| Longer hours | Workers were made to work longer. |
| Production boom | Industrial production increased sharply. |
| British decline | After the war, Manchester could not regain its old Indian market. |
| Indian consolidation | Indian industrialists strengthened their position in the home market. |

## Model Answer: Why Did Industrial Production in India Increase During the First World War?

Industrial production in India increased during the First World War because British mills were busy producing war materials for the army, so Manchester imports into India declined. Indian mills got a larger home market. They were also required to produce war supplies such as jute bags, uniforms, tents, leather boots, and saddles. New factories were set up, older factories ran multiple shifts, workers worked longer hours, and industrial production boomed.

# Unit 4M: Small-Scale Industries Predominate

## Core Idea

Even after factories grew, small-scale industries and handloom production remained important.

## Large Industry Was Limited

Large industries formed only a small part of the economy.

Important facts:

| Year | Factory Labour Share |
| --- | --- |
| 1911 | Only about 5 percent of total industrial labour worked in registered factories. |
| 1931 | Only about 10 percent worked in registered factories. |

Most industrial workers continued in:

- small workshops,
- household units,
- alleys,
- bylanes,
- non-registered production.

## Handloom Production Expanded

Handloom cloth production almost trebled between 1900 and 1940.

This is important because it shows that hand production did not simply disappear.

## Why Handloom Weavers Survived

### 1. They adopted technology

Weavers used the **fly shuttle**.

The fly shuttle:

- increased productivity per worker,
- sped up production,
- reduced labour demand,
- allowed wider cloth to be woven.

By 1941, over 35 percent of handlooms in India had fly shuttles.

In some regions such as Travancore, Madras, Mysore, Cochin, and Bengal, the proportion was 70 to 80 percent.

### 2. Specialised products survived

Mills could not easily copy certain handloom goods, such as:

- saris with woven borders,
- Banarasi saris,
- Baluchari saris,
- Madras lungis,
- handkerchiefs.

### 3. Rich consumers bought finer cloth

Coarse cloth was bought by the poor, so its demand fell during famines and bad harvests.

Fine cloth was bought by the well-to-do, so its demand was more stable.

## But Weavers Did Not Necessarily Prosper

Survival did not mean comfort.

Weavers often:

- lived hard lives,
- worked long hours,
- involved women and children in production,
- faced unstable demand,
- competed with mills,
- earned little despite working intensely.

## Important Conclusion

Handicrafts people were not merely leftovers from the past. Their labour remained part of industrialisation.

# Unit 4N: Market for Goods

## Core Idea

Producing goods is not enough. Consumers must be persuaded to buy them.

This is where advertisements became important.

## Why Advertisements Matter

Advertisements:

- make products appear desirable,
- create new needs,
- shape consumer habits,
- build trust in brands,
- expand markets,
- connect products with emotions and identity.

## Manchester Labels in India

When Manchester manufacturers sold cloth in India, they placed labels on cloth bundles.

Labels had two purposes:

| Purpose | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Familiarity | Buyers learned the company name and place of manufacture. |
| Quality mark | "Made in Manchester" was meant to create confidence. |

## Use of Indian Images

Labels often used Indian gods, goddesses, kings, and historical figures.

This was not accidental.

| Image Used | Intended Message |
| --- | --- |
| Gods and goddesses | The product appeared blessed or familiar. |
| Krishna or Saraswati | Foreign goods appeared culturally closer to Indian buyers. |
| Emperors and nawabs | The product seemed royal, respected, and high quality. |
| Maharaja Ranjit Singh | Historical respect was transferred to the product. |

## Calendars and Advertising

By the late nineteenth century, manufacturers printed calendars to popularise products.

Calendars were effective because:

- even people who could not read used them,
- they were hung in homes, shops, offices, and tea stalls,
- people saw the advertisement daily throughout the year.

## Indian Manufacturers and Swadeshi

Indian manufacturers used nationalist messages.

Their advertisements suggested:

**If you care for the nation, buy Indian-made goods.**

Thus advertisements became vehicles of the swadeshi message.

## Exam Point

Advertisements did not only sell goods. They shaped consumer culture, built trust, used religious and royal imagery, and later carried nationalist messages.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Write in Brief: Q1(a) Women workers in Britain attacked the Spinning Jenny

Women workers attacked the Spinning Jenny because it reduced the demand for hand spinning. Many women earned their livelihood by spinning with hand tools. The Spinning Jenny allowed one worker to spin many threads at once, so fewer workers were needed. Women feared unemployment and loss of income, so they opposed the machine.

## Q1(b) Merchants employed peasants and artisans in villages

In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, merchants could not expand production in towns because guilds controlled crafts, prices, training, and entry into trades. At the same time, world demand for goods was increasing. In the countryside, poor peasants and artisans needed extra income because common lands were being enclosed. Merchants gave them advances and organised production in village homes.

## Q1(c) The port of Surat declined

Surat declined because European companies gained power and secured monopoly rights to trade. Trade shifted from old ports like Surat and Hoogly to new colonial ports like Bombay and Calcutta. European companies controlled trade through these ports and used European ships. As a result, the older Indian merchant-controlled network weakened.

## Q1(d) The Company appointed gomasthas

The East India Company appointed gomasthas to supervise weavers, collect supplies, check cloth quality, eliminate middlemen, and control production directly. This helped the Company prevent weavers from selling to other buyers and ensured regular supplies of cotton and silk goods at low prices.

## Q2: True or False

| Statement | Answer | Correction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| At the end of the nineteenth century, 80 percent of Europe's workforce was in advanced industrial sectors. | False | Less than 20 percent worked in technologically advanced industrial sectors. |
| The international market for fine textiles was dominated by India till the eighteenth century. | True | Indian silk and cotton goods were famous internationally. |
| The American Civil War reduced cotton exports from India. | False | It increased demand for Indian raw cotton because US cotton supplies were cut off. |
| The fly shuttle helped handloom workers improve productivity. | True | It increased weaving speed and output per worker. |

## Q3: What is proto-industrialisation?

Proto-industrialisation was the phase of large-scale industrial production before factories. Merchants from towns supplied money and raw material to peasants and artisans in the countryside. These rural households produced goods for international markets. This system allowed merchants to expand production despite guild restrictions in towns and gave poor peasants supplementary income.

## Discuss: Why did some industrialists prefer hand labour over machines?

Some industrialists preferred hand labour because labour was abundant and wages were low in Victorian Britain. Machines were expensive, could break down, and needed costly repair. Many industries had seasonal demand, so industrialists preferred hiring workers temporarily. Some products required intricate designs and special shapes that machines could not easily produce. Upper-class consumers also preferred handmade goods because they symbolised refinement and quality.

## Discuss: How did the East India Company procure cotton and silk textiles?

The East India Company used its political power to control textile production. It eliminated existing traders and brokers, appointed gomasthas to supervise weavers, gave advances to weavers, and prevented them from selling to other buyers. Weavers who accepted loans had to hand over their cloth to the Company. This system ensured regular supply but reduced weavers' freedom and bargaining power.

## Discuss: Britain and the history of cotton

A strong answer should include these points:

- Cotton became the first symbol of industrialisation in Britain.
- Raw cotton imports rose sharply from 2.5 million pounds in 1760 to 22 million pounds in 1787.
- Inventions improved carding, twisting, spinning, and rolling.
- Richard Arkwright's cotton mill brought production under one roof.
- Cotton led British industrialisation up to the 1840s.
- British manufacturers pressured the government to protect them from Indian textile competition.
- Manchester goods were later exported to India and flooded Indian markets.
- Cotton industrialisation in Britain was linked to colonial markets and raw materials.

## Discuss: Why did industrial production increase in India during the First World War?

Industrial production increased because British mills became busy with war production and Manchester imports into India declined. Indian mills got a larger home market. Indian factories also supplied war needs such as jute bags, cloth for uniforms, tents, leather boots, and saddles. New factories were set up, old factories ran multiple shifts, more workers were employed, and production increased.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Industrialisation began only with factories." | Proto-industrial production existed before factories. |
| "Machines immediately replaced all hand labour." | Hand labour remained important for a long time. |
| "All workers benefited from machines." | Many workers feared unemployment and wage insecurity. |
| "Indian textile decline happened only because of machines." | It also happened because of colonial policies, tariffs, Company monopoly, and market control. |
| "Gomasthas helped weavers." | Gomasthas mainly controlled weavers for the Company. |
| "Indian mills destroyed all handlooms." | Handloom production expanded in the twentieth century in some sectors. |
| "Advertisements only gave information." | Advertisements shaped desire, identity, trust, and nationalism. |
