## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter is not only about the invention of printing machines.

It is about what happened after written words, pictures, arguments, religious ideas, stories, newspapers, cartoons, and political criticism could be reproduced many times and circulated widely.

Print changed the world because it changed the relationship between:

- reader and knowledge,
- people and authority,
- religion and debate,
- government and public opinion,
- reformers and society,
- writers and readers,
- women and education,
- poor people and ideas,
- nationalism and communication.

The key idea is:

**Print made ideas more mobile, public, repeatable, and powerful. Because of that, it created new readers, new debates, new identities, new conflicts, and new forms of control.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Print has a history

Books, newspapers, calendars, advertisements, and posters feel ordinary today, but printed culture developed slowly across different regions.

### 2. Print did not begin in Europe

The earliest print technologies developed in China, Japan, and Korea. Europe later adopted and transformed print through mechanical presses.

### 3. Print created a new reading public

Printed books became cheaper and more available. Reading expanded beyond elites, though oral culture remained important.

### 4. Print encouraged debate and dissent

Religious reformers, critics, scientists, philosophers, social reformers, and political activists could circulate ideas widely.

### 5. Print was feared by authorities

Religious authorities, monarchs, and colonial governments feared that uncontrolled print could spread rebellion, criticism, heresy, or nationalism.

### 6. Print changed Indian society

In India, print shaped religious reform, public debate, women's education, caste protest, worker education, and nationalist politics.

### 7. Censorship shows the power of print

Governments censored print because newspapers, books, and pamphlets could create public opinion and political resistance.

# Unit 5A: Why Print Culture Matters

## Core Idea

Print is everywhere in modern life:

- books,
- journals,
- newspapers,
- calendars,
- advertisements,
- official circulars,
- cinema posters,
- theatre programmes,
- school textbooks,
- pamphlets,
- political cartoons,
- religious tracts.

Because print is so common, we may forget that there was a time before printed matter became widely available.

## What Print Changed

| Before Wide Print | After Wide Print |
| --- | --- |
| Knowledge moved slowly through manuscripts and oral recitation. | Texts and images could circulate quickly and widely. |
| Books were costly and limited. | Books became cheaper and more numerous. |
| Reading was mainly for elites. | New groups became readers. |
| Debate was limited to smaller circles. | Public debate expanded. |
| Authority controlled knowledge more easily. | Critics could circulate alternative views. |
| Local identities dominated. | Print connected people across regions. |

## Important Understanding

Print did not simply spread information. It changed how people argued, learned, imagined society, criticised authority, formed communities, and built political identities.

# Unit 5B: The First Printed Books in East Asia

## Core Idea

The earliest print technology developed in China, Japan, and Korea.

## Print in China

From AD 594 onwards, books in China were printed through woodblocks.

The method worked like this:

1. Text or image was carved on a wooden block.
2. Ink was applied to the carved surface.
3. Paper was rubbed against the inked block.
4. The printed sheet was folded and stitched.

## Why Chinese Books Had Accordion Form

Chinese paper was thin and porous.

Both sides could not be printed easily, so books were folded like an accordion and stitched at the side.

## Role of the Chinese Imperial State

For a long time, the Chinese imperial state was the major producer of printed material.

Why?

China had a large bureaucratic system. Officials were recruited through civil service examinations. Textbooks for these exams were printed in large numbers under state sponsorship.

## Growth of Print in China

| Period | Change |
| --- | --- |
| From AD 594 | Woodblock printing used for books. |
| Long imperial period | State printed examination textbooks. |
| Sixteenth century | More candidates meant more printed material. |
| Seventeenth century | Urban culture expanded and print uses diversified. |
| Late nineteenth century | Western mechanical presses entered China. |

## New Readers in China

By the seventeenth century, print was no longer used only by scholar-officials.

New users included:

- merchants,
- urban readers,
- rich women,
- writers of poetry and plays,
- courtesans,
- readers of fiction,
- readers of autobiographies and anthologies.

## Shanghai and Mechanical Printing

In the late nineteenth century, Western printing techniques and mechanical presses entered China as Western powers established outposts.

Shanghai became a major centre of new print culture, especially for Western-style schools.

# Unit 5C: Print in Japan

## Core Idea

Japan received hand-printing technology from Chinese Buddhist missionaries.

## Early Japanese Print

| Fact | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Introduction | Buddhist missionaries from China brought hand-printing to Japan. |
| Date | Around AD 768-770. |
| Oldest printed book | Buddhist Diamond Sutra, printed in AD 868. |
| Printed forms | Texts, woodcut illustrations, textiles, playing cards, and paper money. |

## Japanese Urban Print Culture

In medieval and early modern Japan, books became cheap and abundant.

In the late eighteenth century, Edo, later Tokyo, had a flourishing urban print culture.

Printed material included books on:

- women,
- musical instruments,
- calculations,
- tea ceremony,
- flower arrangements,
- etiquette,
- cooking,
- famous places.

## Ukiyo Prints

Kitagawa Utamaro, born in Edo in 1753, was famous for ukiyo prints.

**Ukiyo** means "pictures of the floating world."

These prints showed ordinary urban experiences, especially:

- artists,
- courtesans,
- teahouse gatherings,
- elegant city life.

## Global Influence

Japanese prints travelled to Europe and the United States and influenced artists such as Manet, Monet, and Van Gogh.

## Learner Point

Print culture was not only about religious books or textbooks. It also shaped art, urban leisure, popular taste, and global cultural exchange.

# Unit 5D: Print Comes to Europe

## Core Idea

Europe learned about paper and printing through contact with Asia, then developed mechanical printing.

## Paper Reaches Europe

For centuries, silk and spices moved from China to Europe through the Silk Route.

In the eleventh century, Chinese paper also reached Europe through this route.

Paper allowed the production of manuscripts written by scribes.

## Marco Polo and Woodblock Printing

In 1295, Marco Polo returned to Italy after years in China.

He brought knowledge of woodblock printing.

After this:

- Italians began producing books with woodblocks,
- the technique spread to other parts of Europe,
- cheaper printed copies reached merchants and students.

## Manuscripts Still Continued

Luxury books were still handwritten on expensive vellum for:

- aristocratic circles,
- rich monasteries,
- elite libraries.

Some elites dismissed printed books as cheap or vulgar.

## Why Manuscripts Could Not Meet Demand

| Problem | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Expensive | Copying by hand took skilled labour. |
| Slow | Each copy had to be written manually. |
| Fragile | Manuscripts could be damaged easily. |
| Awkward | They were difficult to handle and transport. |
| Limited circulation | Few copies could be made. |

## Growth of Woodblock Printing

By the early fifteenth century, woodblocks were widely used in Europe to print:

- textiles,
- playing cards,
- religious pictures,
- short texts.

But Europe needed faster and cheaper reproduction.

This need led to Gutenberg's printing press.

# Unit 5E: Gutenberg and the Printing Press

## Core Idea

Johann Gutenberg developed the first known printing press in Europe in the 1430s at Strasbourg, Germany.

## Gutenberg's Background

Gutenberg had knowledge of:

- wine presses,
- olive presses,
- polishing stones,
- goldsmith work,
- lead moulds.

He adapted these skills to create a printing system.

## How Gutenberg's Press Worked

| Element | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Olive press model | Inspired the pressing mechanism. |
| Metal moulds | Used to cast letters. |
| Movable type | Metal letters could be arranged to form words. |
| Platen | Pressed paper onto inked type. |
| Screw and handle | Created pressure for printing. |

## Gutenberg Bible

By 1448, Gutenberg had perfected his system.

The first major book he printed was the Bible.

Important facts:

- about 180 copies were printed,
- production took about three years,
- this was fast by the standards of that time.

## Printed Books First Looked Like Manuscripts

The new technology did not immediately destroy manuscript style.

Early printed books:

- imitated handwritten letters,
- used ornamental styles,
- left spaces for hand-painted decoration,
- had hand-illuminated borders,
- allowed wealthy buyers to choose illustrations.

## Expansion of Printing Presses

Between 1450 and 1550:

- printing presses spread across most European countries,
- German printers travelled to other countries,
- book production increased rapidly,
- about 20 million printed books circulated in the second half of the fifteenth century,
- by the sixteenth century, about 200 million copies were produced.

## The Print Revolution

The shift from hand printing to mechanical printing produced a **print revolution**.

This revolution was not only technical. It transformed society by changing access to knowledge, debate, religion, politics, and reading.

# Unit 5F: The Print Revolution and Its Impact

## Core Idea

The print revolution changed people's relationship with information, knowledge, institutions, and authority.

## A New Reading Public

Printing reduced:

- cost of books,
- labour needed to produce books,
- time required for copying.

It increased:

- number of copies,
- spread of books,
- availability of printed material,
- readership.

## From Hearing Public to Reading Public

Before print, many common people lived in oral culture.

They heard:

- sacred texts,
- ballads,
- folk tales,
- performances,
- sermons.

After print, books reached wider sections of people.

But literacy rates were still low in many places, so printers adapted.

## How Print Reached Non-Literate People

Printers published:

- popular ballads,
- folk tales,
- illustrated books,
- texts meant to be read aloud,
- songs and recitations for gatherings.

Printed material was often orally transmitted.

This means oral culture and print culture mixed together.

## Important Point

Print did not instantly create silent individual readers everywhere. It created a mixed world where printed texts were read, heard, sung, recited, and discussed.

# Unit 5G: Religious Debates and Fear of Print

## Core Idea

Print allowed ideas to circulate widely, so it encouraged debate, criticism, and dissent.

## Why Some People Celebrated Print

Print allowed reformers and thinkers to:

- spread ideas quickly,
- reach distant readers,
- challenge authority,
- persuade people,
- organise debate.

## Why Some People Feared Print

Religious authorities, monarchs, writers, and artists feared that printed books might:

- spread rebellious ideas,
- spread irreligious thoughts,
- weaken authority,
- circulate poor-quality writing,
- encourage people to question established beliefs,
- destroy respect for valued literature.

## Martin Luther and the Reformation

In 1517, Martin Luther wrote the **Ninety-Five Theses**, criticising practices of the Roman Catholic Church.

A printed copy was posted on a church door in Wittenberg.

Print helped Luther because:

- his ideas were reproduced quickly,
- his writings reached many readers,
- debate spread widely,
- the Church was challenged publicly.

His translation of the New Testament sold 5,000 copies within a few weeks, and a second edition appeared within three months.

Print helped create the conditions for the Protestant Reformation.

## Menocchio and Dissent

Menocchio, a miller in sixteenth-century Italy, read books available locally and developed his own interpretation of the Bible, God, and Creation.

The Roman Catholic Church considered his views dangerous.

He was tried and eventually executed.

This example shows that print could encourage ordinary people to interpret religion independently, which frightened religious authorities.

## Index of Prohibited Books

From 1558, the Roman Catholic Church began maintaining an Index of Prohibited Books.

This was a list of books considered dangerous or unacceptable.

## Erasmus's Fear of Print

Erasmus, a Catholic reformer, worried that printers were producing too many books, including foolish, scandalous, irreligious, and seditious works.

His fear shows that even learned reformers could be anxious about uncontrolled print.

## Exam Point

Print created religious debate because it allowed ideas to spread beyond the control of priests, rulers, and traditional authorities.

# Unit 5H: The Reading Mania in Europe

## Core Idea

In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, literacy increased and printed reading material multiplied.

## Rise in Literacy

Churches of different denominations set up schools in villages.

By the end of the eighteenth century, literacy rates in some parts of Europe reached 60 to 80 percent.

This created a "reading mania."

## New Popular Literature

Printers produced material for new audiences.

| Printed Form | Features |
| --- | --- |
| Almanacs | Calendars with astronomical and practical information. |
| Ballads | Songs and folk tales in verse. |
| Chapbooks | Cheap pocket-size books sold by chapmen in England. |
| Bibliotheque Bleue | Cheap small books in France printed on poor-quality paper with blue covers. |
| Romances | Short entertaining stories. |
| Histories | Stories about the past. |
| Periodicals | Newspapers and journals with news and entertainment. |

## Newspapers and Journals

The periodical press developed from the early eighteenth century.

It combined:

- current affairs,
- entertainment,
- news of wars,
- trade information,
- developments in other places.

## Science and Philosophy in Print

Print made scientific and philosophical ideas more accessible.

Printed works included:

- ancient scientific texts,
- medieval scientific texts,
- maps,
- scientific diagrams,
- discoveries of Isaac Newton,
- writings of Thomas Paine,
- writings of Voltaire,
- writings of Jean Jacques Rousseau.

## Learner Point

Print made the ideas of science, reason, rationality, and criticism available to wider reading publics.

# Unit 5I: Print, Enlightenment, and the French Revolution

## Core Idea

Many people in eighteenth-century Europe believed print could spread enlightenment and end despotism.

## Mercier's View

Louise-Sebastien Mercier, a French novelist, believed the printing press was a powerful engine of progress.

He believed reading could enlighten people and weaken tyranny.

## How Print Culture Helped Create Conditions for the French Revolution

Historians usually give three arguments.

### Argument 1: Print spread Enlightenment ideas

Writings of Enlightenment thinkers criticised:

- tradition,
- superstition,
- despotism,
- Church authority,
- absolute state power.

They promoted:

- reason,
- rationality,
- questioning,
- criticism.

Voltaire and Rousseau were widely read.

### Argument 2: Print created dialogue and debate

Printed material allowed values, norms, institutions, and authority to be questioned publicly.

People began discussing:

- monarchy,
- Church authority,
- social inequality,
- rights,
- reason,
- reform.

This created a new culture of public debate.

### Argument 3: Print mocked royalty

By the 1780s, pamphlets, cartoons, and underground literature mocked the monarchy and criticised royal morality.

They contrasted:

- luxury of nobles and royalty,
- suffering of common people.

This increased hostile feeling against the monarchy.

## Important Caution

Print did not directly cause the French Revolution by itself.

People read different kinds of literature:

- revolutionary writings,
- Church propaganda,
- royalist writings,
- criticism,
- entertainment.

Readers accepted some ideas, rejected others, and interpreted things in their own ways.

So the best answer is:

**Print did not directly create the French Revolution, but it opened the possibility of thinking differently and helped create a public culture of criticism.**

# Unit 5J: The Nineteenth Century - New Readers

## Core Idea

The nineteenth century saw mass literacy expand in Europe. Children, women, and workers became important readers.

## Children as Readers

Primary education became compulsory from the late nineteenth century.

This made children an important reading public.

Publishers produced:

- school textbooks,
- children's stories,
- fairy tales,
- folk tales,
- moral literature.

In France, a children's press was set up in 1857.

## Grimm Brothers

The Grimm Brothers in Germany collected traditional folk tales from peasants.

Their collection was published in 1812.

But the tales were edited before publication. Material considered vulgar or unsuitable for children was removed.

This shows that print preserved oral tales but also changed them.

## Women as Readers and Writers

Women became important readers of:

- penny magazines,
- novels,
- manuals on behaviour,
- housekeeping guides.

Women also became important writers.

Examples:

- Jane Austen,
- the Bronte sisters,
- George Eliot.

Their writings shaped a new idea of women as people with:

- will,
- personality,
- determination,
- intelligence,
- independent thought.

## Workers as Readers

Lending libraries existed from the seventeenth century, but in the nineteenth century they became important for:

- white-collar workers,
- artisans,
- lower-middle-class people,
- self-educated workers.

After working hours were shortened from the mid-nineteenth century, some workers wrote:

- political tracts,
- autobiographies,
- personal accounts.

## Print Technology Innovations

| Innovation | Importance |
| --- | --- |
| Metal press | Stronger and more durable than earlier presses. |
| Power-driven cylindrical press | Perfected by Richard M. Hoe; printed about 8,000 sheets per hour. |
| Offset press | Could print up to six colours at a time. |
| Electrically operated presses | Accelerated printing in the twentieth century. |
| Better paper feeding | Improved speed and regularity. |
| Better plates and colour controls | Improved appearance of printed texts. |

## Selling Strategies

Printers and publishers used new strategies:

- serialised novels in periodicals,
- cheap series such as the Shilling Series,
- dust covers and book jackets,
- cheap paperback editions during the Great Depression.

# Unit 5K: India Before Print

## Core Idea

India had a rich manuscript culture before printing became common.

## Manuscript Tradition

Manuscripts existed in:

- Sanskrit,
- Arabic,
- Persian,
- vernacular languages.

They were copied on:

- palm leaves,
- handmade paper.

They were often:

- illustrated beautifully,
- pressed between wooden covers,
- sewn together for preservation.

## Limits of Manuscripts

| Limitation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Expensive | Hand copying required skilled labour. |
| Fragile | Manuscripts had to be handled carefully. |
| Difficult to read | Scripts were written in different styles. |
| Limited circulation | Few copies could be made. |
| Not everyday objects | They were not widely used in daily life. |

## Learning Without Reading

In pre-colonial Bengal, village schools existed, but students often learned to write without reading many texts.

Teachers dictated from memory, and students wrote down the material.

So many became literate without becoming regular readers.

# Unit 5L: Print Comes to India

## Core Idea

Printing came to India through European missionaries and later expanded through newspapers, religious texts, and Indian publishers.

## Early Printing in India

| Date / Period | Development |
| --- | --- |
| Mid-sixteenth century | Printing press came to Goa with Portuguese missionaries. |
| By 1674 | About 50 books printed in Konkani and Kanara languages. |
| 1579 | First Tamil book printed by Catholic priests at Cochin. |
| 1713 | First Malayalam book printed by Catholic priests. |
| By 1710 | Dutch Protestant missionaries printed 32 Tamil texts. |
| Late seventeenth century | English East India Company began importing presses, but English press grew later. |

## Hickey's Bengal Gazette

In 1780, James Augustus Hickey began editing the **Bengal Gazette**.

It was a weekly magazine and described itself as independent.

It published:

- advertisements,
- commercial information,
- gossip about Company officials,
- criticism that annoyed the colonial government.

Governor-General Warren Hastings persecuted Hickey and encouraged officially supported newspapers to protect the government's image.

## Indian Newspapers

By the end of the eighteenth century, more newspapers and journals appeared.

The first Indian weekly Bengal Gazette was brought out by Gangadhar Bhattacharya, who was close to Rammohun Roy.

# Unit 5M: Religious Reform and Public Debates in India

## Core Idea

Print made religious and social debates public in nineteenth-century India.

## Reform and Orthodoxy

Different groups used print to discuss issues such as:

- widow immolation,
- monotheism,
- Brahmanical priesthood,
- idolatry,
- religious authority,
- social reform.

Reformers and orthodox groups both used print.

## Bengal Debates

Rammohun Roy published **Sambad Kaumudi** from 1821.

Hindu orthodoxy supported **Samachar Chandrika** to oppose his views.

From 1822:

- **Jam-i-Jahan Nama** appeared in Persian,
- **Shamsul Akhbar** appeared in Persian,
- **Bombay Samachar** appeared in Gujarati.

## Muslim Print Culture

In North India, the ulama were worried about:

- collapse of Muslim dynasties,
- possible conversion,
- changes in Muslim personal law,
- colonial interference.

They used cheap lithographic presses to publish:

- Persian translations,
- Urdu translations,
- holy scriptures,
- religious newspapers,
- tracts,
- fatwas.

## Deoband Seminary

The Deoband Seminary was founded in 1867.

It published thousands of fatwas explaining:

- everyday conduct,
- Islamic law,
- meanings of religious doctrines.

Urdu print helped religious groups debate publicly and expand their following.

## Hindu Religious Print

Print encouraged the reading of religious texts in vernacular languages.

Important examples:

- The first printed edition of Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas came out from Calcutta in 1810.
- By the mid-nineteenth century, cheap lithographic editions flooded North Indian markets.
- Naval Kishore Press in Lucknow published many religious texts.
- Shri Venkateshwar Press in Bombay published many religious texts.

## Wider Effect

Religious print:

- reached a wide circle of people,
- encouraged debates,
- connected communities,
- helped people across regions discuss common issues,
- contributed to pan-Indian identities through newspapers.

# Unit 5N: New Forms of Publication in India

## Core Idea

Print created demand for new kinds of writing and new visual culture.

## New Literary Forms

As more people read, they wanted literature that reflected their own lives, emotions, and relationships.

New forms included:

- novels,
- lyrics,
- short stories,
- essays,
- social criticism,
- political writing,
- serialised fiction.

The novel, which developed in Europe, acquired Indian forms and styles.

## Visual Culture

By the end of the nineteenth century, visual culture grew rapidly.

Printing presses reproduced images in multiple copies.

Painters such as Raja Ravi Varma produced images for mass circulation.

Cheap prints and calendars became available in bazaars.

Even poor people could buy them to decorate homes and workplaces.

These images shaped popular ideas about:

- religion,
- politics,
- tradition,
- modernity,
- society,
- culture.

## Cartoons and Caricatures

From the 1870s, journals and newspapers published cartoons and caricatures.

They commented on:

- social change,
- Westernisation,
- educated Indians,
- nationalism,
- imperial rule,
- family roles.

Some imperial cartoons mocked nationalists, while nationalist cartoons criticised colonial rule.

# Unit 5O: Women and Print

## Core Idea

Print expanded women's reading and writing, but it also created social anxiety.

## Growth of Women's Reading

Women's reading increased in middle-class homes.

Liberal fathers and husbands:

- educated women at home,
- sent girls to schools when women's schools opened,
- supported journals that explained why women should be educated.

Journals included:

- women's writings,
- syllabus for home education,
- suitable reading material,
- discussions on reform.

## Conservative Opposition

Not all families supported women's education.

Some conservative Hindus believed that a literate girl would become widowed.

Some Muslims feared that educated women would be corrupted by reading Urdu romances.

## Women Who Defied Restrictions

### Rashsundari Debi

Rashsundari Debi lived in an orthodox household in East Bengal.

She secretly learned to read in the kitchen.

She later wrote **Amar Jiban**, published in 1876.

It was the first full-length autobiography published in Bengali.

### Kailashbashini Debi

From the 1860s, Bengali women such as Kailashbashini Debi wrote about women's experiences, including:

- confinement at home,
- ignorance,
- hard domestic labour,
- unfair treatment.

### Tarabai Shinde and Pandita Ramabai

In the 1880s, Tarabai Shinde and Pandita Ramabai wrote strongly about the miserable lives of upper-caste Hindu women, especially widows.

### Begum Rokeya Sakhawat Hossein

Begum Rokeya supported women's education and criticised men who denied education to women in the name of religion.

## Hindi Print and Women

Hindi printing began seriously from the 1870s.

Soon, a large part of Hindi print was devoted to women's education.

In the early twentieth century, journals written for and sometimes edited by women became popular.

They discussed:

- women's education,
- widowhood,
- widow remarriage,
- the national movement,
- household lessons,
- fashion,
- short stories,
- serialised novels.

## Punjab and Bengal

In Punjab, Ram Chaddha published **Istri Dharm Vichar**, teaching women to be obedient wives.

The Khalsa Tract Society also published cheap booklets with similar messages.

In Bengal, the Battala area of Calcutta became known for cheap popular books, religious tracts, scriptures, and illustrated publications.

Pedlars carried Battala books to homes, helping women access reading material.

## Exam Point

Print did not automatically liberate women. It opened new spaces for women's education and expression, but it also carried conservative messages about obedience and domestic roles.

# Unit 5P: Print and Poor People

## Core Idea

Print gradually reached poor people through cheap books, public libraries, caste protest writing, and worker education.

## Cheap Books

In nineteenth-century Madras towns, very cheap small books were sold in markets and at crossroads.

This allowed poor people travelling to markets to buy printed material.

## Public Libraries

From the early twentieth century, public libraries expanded access to books.

They were located mostly in:

- cities,
- towns,
- prosperous villages.

For rich local patrons, founding a library also brought social prestige.

## Caste Protest and Print

From the late nineteenth century, caste discrimination became a subject of printed tracts and essays.

Important figures:

| Figure | Contribution |
| --- | --- |
| Jyotiba Phule | Wrote Gulamgiri in 1871, criticising caste injustice. |
| B.R. Ambedkar | Wrote powerfully against caste oppression in Maharashtra. |
| E.V. Ramaswamy Naicker (Periyar) | Wrote against caste hierarchy and social inequality in Madras. |

Local protest movements and sects also produced journals and tracts criticising old scriptures and imagining a just future.

## Workers and Print

Factory workers were often overworked and lacked education, so few wrote about their experiences.

But some worker-writers did emerge.

Examples:

- Kashibaba, a Kanpur millworker, wrote **Chhote Aur Bade Ka Sawal** in 1938, linking caste and class exploitation.
- Sudarshan Chakr, another Kanpur millworker, wrote poems between 1935 and 1955, later collected as **Sacchi Kavitayan**.
- By the 1930s, Bangalore cotton millworkers set up libraries to educate themselves.

## Learner Point

Print gave poor and oppressed groups tools to criticise inequality and educate themselves, though access remained unequal.

# Unit 5Q: Print and Censorship

## Core Idea

The colonial government tried to control print because printed criticism could damage authority and encourage political resistance.

## Early Company Period

Before 1798, the East India Company was not deeply concerned with censorship.

Its early controls targeted Englishmen in India who criticised Company misrule and particular Company officers.

The Company feared such criticism could be used in England against its trade monopoly.

## Press Controls in the 1820s

By the 1820s, the Calcutta Supreme Court passed regulations to control press freedom.

The Company encouraged newspapers that praised British rule.

## 1835 Press Freedom

In 1835, Governor-General Bentinck agreed to revise press laws after petitions from editors.

Thomas Macaulay formulated new rules that restored earlier freedoms.

## After the Revolt of 1857

After 1857, British attitudes hardened.

As vernacular newspapers became nationalist, the colonial government debated stricter controls.

## Vernacular Press Act, 1878

The Vernacular Press Act was passed in 1878.

It was modelled on Irish Press Laws.

It gave the government extensive power to censor reports and editorials in vernacular newspapers.

## How It Worked

| Step | Government Action |
| --- | --- |
| Monitoring | Government tracked vernacular newspapers in different provinces. |
| Warning | If a report was judged seditious, the newspaper was warned. |
| Punishment | If warning was ignored, the press could be seized. |
| Confiscation | Printing machinery could be confiscated. |

## Nationalist Newspapers

Despite repression, nationalist newspapers grew.

They:

- reported colonial misrule,
- encouraged nationalist activity,
- criticised government policy,
- spread political awareness.

## Tilak and Kesari

In 1907, Punjab revolutionaries were deported.

Bal Gangadhar Tilak wrote sympathetically about them in **Kesari**.

He was imprisoned in 1908, leading to protests across India.

## Later Controls

During the First World War, the Defence of India Rules forced several newspapers to furnish securities.

Many shut down rather than obey.

The Rowlatt-related Sedition Committee Report in 1919 strengthened controls.

During the Second World War, the Defence of India Act allowed censorship of war-related reports.

In August 1942, during the Quit India Movement, many newspapers were suppressed.

## Gandhi's Point

Gandhi connected swaraj with liberty of speech, liberty of the press, and freedom of association.

This means political freedom required the right to express and form public opinion.

# Important Comparisons

## Manuscript Culture vs Print Culture

| Basis | Manuscript Culture | Print Culture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Production | Handwritten | Mechanically reproduced |
| Cost | Expensive | Cheaper over time |
| Speed | Slow | Faster |
| Copies | Few | Many |
| Access | Mainly elite | Wider public |
| Durability | Fragile | Easier circulation |
| Reading style | Often formal and limited | Individual, collective, oral, public |

## Woodblock Printing vs Gutenberg Press

| Basis | Woodblock Printing | Gutenberg Press |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Method | Carved blocks | Movable metal type |
| Flexibility | Whole block needed for each page | Letters rearranged for different pages |
| Speed | Slower for text-heavy work | Faster for book production |
| Early region | East Asia | Europe |
| Use | Books, pictures, textiles, cards | Books, pamphlets, newspapers |

## Celebration of Print vs Fear of Print

| Celebration | Fear |
| --- | --- |
| Spread knowledge | Spread dangerous ideas |
| Encourage reason | Encourage rebellion |
| Challenge tyranny | Challenge Church or state authority |
| Educate readers | Flood society with bad writing |
| Create public debate | Create dissent and disorder |

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1(a): Why did woodblock print come to Europe after 1295?

Woodblock printing came to Europe after 1295 because Marco Polo returned to Italy from China with knowledge of Chinese woodblock printing. China already had this technology. After Marco Polo's return, Italians began using woodblocks to produce books, and the technique gradually spread to other parts of Europe.

## Q1(b): Why did Martin Luther praise print?

Martin Luther praised print because it allowed his religious ideas to spread quickly. His Ninety-Five Theses and other writings were reproduced in large numbers and read widely. His translation of the New Testament sold thousands of copies in a short time. Print helped spread Reformation ideas and challenge the Roman Catholic Church.

## Q1(c): Why did the Roman Catholic Church keep an Index of Prohibited Books?

The Roman Catholic Church feared that printed books could spread heretical and rebellious ideas. Ordinary readers began interpreting religious texts independently, as in the case of Menocchio. To control dangerous ideas and protect Church authority, the Church imposed controls on publishers and booksellers and began maintaining an Index of Prohibited Books from 1558.

## Q1(d): Why did Gandhi connect swaraj with liberty of press?

Gandhi believed that the fight for swaraj required liberty of speech, liberty of the press, and freedom of association. Without the freedom to express ideas, criticise government, publish opinions, and build public opinion, people could not struggle effectively against colonial rule.

## Q2(a): The Gutenberg Press

The Gutenberg press was developed by Johann Gutenberg in Germany in the 1430s and perfected by 1448. It used movable metal type and a press mechanism inspired by wine and olive presses. Gutenberg's first major printed book was the Bible, of which about 180 copies were produced in three years. The press made book production faster and helped create the print revolution in Europe.

## Q2(b): Erasmus's Idea of Printed Books

Erasmus was anxious about the uncontrolled growth of printed books. He believed that printers were flooding the world with too many books, including foolish, scandalous, irreligious, and seditious works. He feared that the excessive number of books would reduce the value of good scholarship and spread harmful ideas.

## Q2(c): The Vernacular Press Act

The Vernacular Press Act was passed in 1878 by the colonial government in India. It gave the government power to censor reports and editorials in vernacular newspapers. Newspapers judged as seditious could be warned, and if they ignored the warning, their press could be seized and printing machinery confiscated. The Act was aimed at controlling nationalist criticism in Indian-language newspapers.

## Q3(a): What did print mean to women?

Print expanded women's reading and writing. Journals discussed women's education and carried suitable reading material. Women such as Rashsundari Debi, Kailashbashini Debi, Tarabai Shinde, Pandita Ramabai, and Begum Rokeya used writing to express women's experiences and criticise injustice. However, print also carried conservative messages teaching women obedience and domestic duties.

## Q3(b): What did print mean to the poor?

Print gave poor people greater access to ideas through cheap books, public libraries, tracts, and journals. Cheap books were sold in markets and crossroads. Caste reformers such as Jyotiba Phule, Ambedkar, and Periyar used print to criticise caste oppression. Worker-writers such as Kashibaba and Sudarshan Chakr wrote about exploitation. Libraries helped workers educate themselves.

## Q3(c): What did print mean to reformers?

Print gave reformers a way to spread ideas, debate opponents, and reach a wider public. Rammohun Roy used Sambad Kaumudi to support reform, while orthodox groups used print to oppose him. Reformers used newspapers, tracts, religious translations, and essays to discuss widow immolation, idolatry, priesthood, women's education, caste, and social change.

## Discuss: Why did eighteenth-century Europeans think print would bring enlightenment?

Many Europeans believed print would spread reason, science, criticism, and knowledge. Writings of thinkers such as Voltaire and Rousseau became widely available. Print allowed people to question tradition, superstition, Church authority, and despotism. Writers like Mercier believed books could transform readers and help public opinion destroy tyranny.

## Discuss: Why did some people fear printed books?

Some feared printed books because they could spread rebellious, irreligious, or heretical ideas. In Europe, the Catholic Church feared independent interpretations of religion, as seen in the case of Menocchio and the Index of Prohibited Books. In India, colonial authorities feared nationalist newspapers, leading to laws like the Vernacular Press Act.

## Discuss: How did print culture assist Indian nationalism?

Print culture assisted nationalism by spreading news, criticism, and political ideas across regions. Newspapers reported colonial misrule and encouraged nationalist activities. Vernacular newspapers reached ordinary people in regional languages. Cartoons and pamphlets criticised imperial rule. When the colonial government censored the press, freedom of press became part of the nationalist struggle for swaraj.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Print began in Europe." | Early print developed in China, Japan, and Korea. |
| "Gutenberg invented all printing." | Gutenberg developed the first known European mechanical printing press using movable type. |
| "Print immediately made everyone literate." | Literacy expanded slowly; oral culture continued. |
| "Print only spread good knowledge." | It also spread conflict, fear, propaganda, and censorship. |
| "Print directly caused the French Revolution." | It helped create conditions for debate and criticism but did not directly control people's minds. |
| "Women were only readers." | Women also became writers, editors, critics, and autobiographers. |
| "The poor had no connection with print." | Cheap books, libraries, caste tracts, and worker writing reached poor readers. |
| "Censorship shows print was weak." | Censorship shows that authorities feared print's power. |
