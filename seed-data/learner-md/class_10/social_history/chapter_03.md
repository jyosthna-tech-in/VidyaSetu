## How to Understand This Chapter

Globalisation is often described as a recent process, but this chapter shows that global connections are very old.

People, goods, money, ideas, skills, food, technology, capital, labour, and diseases have crossed regions for centuries. The modern global world was made through many phases:

1. Pre-modern trade and cultural links.
2. European conquest and colonisation.
3. Nineteenth-century flows of goods, labour, and capital.
4. Technological change in transport and communication.
5. Colonial exploitation and forced labour.
6. World wars and economic crisis.
7. Post-war institutions like IMF and World Bank.
8. Decolonisation and demands for a fairer global order.
9. Modern globalisation through MNCs and low-wage production.

The central idea is:

**The global world created wealth, mobility, and exchange, but also conquest, disease, slavery, forced migration, poverty, and inequality.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Globalisation has a long history

The global world was not created only in the last 50 years. Ancient trade routes, religious journeys, migration, and disease already connected distant regions.

### 2. Trade and culture moved together

Silk, spices, textiles, pottery, gold, religion, stories, language, and technology travelled together.

### 3. Global exchange was not always peaceful

European conquest of America, slavery, colonial rule, rinderpest in Africa, and indentured labour show the violent side of globalisation.

### 4. The nineteenth century had three major flows

The global economy was shaped by the movement of goods, labour, and capital.

### 5. Technology changed food and trade

Railways, steamships, telegraph, and refrigerated ships allowed goods and food to move faster and cheaper across long distances.

### 6. World wars reshaped the global economy

The First World War weakened Europe and strengthened the United States. The Great Depression showed how connected the world economy had become.

### 7. Post-war institutions shaped modern economic order

The IMF and World Bank were created at Bretton Woods to stabilise the world economy, but their control remained with Western industrial powers.

### 8. Developing countries demanded fairness

Newly independent countries formed the G-77 and demanded a New International Economic Order.

# Unit 3A: The Pre-Modern Global World

## Core Idea

Global connections existed long before modern globalisation.

Travellers, traders, priests, pilgrims, migrants, and conquerors moved across long distances. They carried:

- goods
- money
- ideas
- skills
- inventions
- religions
- values
- diseases

## Early Examples

| Example | What It Shows |
| --- | --- |
| Indus Valley trade with West Asia around 3000 BCE | Ancient trade links |
| Cowries from Maldives reaching China and East Africa | Long-distance currency movement |
| Disease-carrying germs spreading by seventh century | Biological links |
| Travellers and pilgrims moving across regions | Cultural and religious exchange |

## Main Point

Even before modern industry and technology, the world was connected through trade, belief, migration, and disease.

# Unit 3B: Silk Routes

## Meaning

Silk routes were trade and cultural routes linking Asia with Europe and North Africa.

They included land routes and sea routes.

## Why Called Silk Routes?

They are called silk routes because Chinese silk travelled westward along these routes.

## Goods That Travelled

| From | Goods |
| --- | --- |
| China | Silk, pottery |
| India and Southeast Asia | Textiles, spices |
| Europe | Gold and silver flowing to Asia |

## Culture and Religion

Trade and culture moved together.

The silk routes helped the movement of:

- early Christian missionaries
- Muslim preachers
- Buddhism from eastern India
- ideas, stories, and artistic forms

## Exam Answer

The silk routes linked vast regions of Asia with Europe and North Africa. Chinese silk, pottery, Indian textiles, Southeast Asian spices, and European gold and silver moved along these routes. They also carried religions and cultures, including Buddhism, Christianity, and Islam. Thus, the silk routes show that trade and cultural exchange went together in the pre-modern world.

# Unit 3C: Food Travels

## Food as Cultural Exchange

Food shows how long-distance contact shaped daily life.

Examples:

- noodles may have travelled from China and become spaghetti
- Arab traders may have taken pasta to Sicily
- similar foods existed in India and Japan

The exact origin is uncertain, but the similarity shows cultural contact.

## Foods from the Americas

Many foods became known in Europe and Asia only after contact with the Americas:

- potatoes
- soya
- groundnuts
- maize
- tomatoes
- chillies
- sweet potatoes

## Potato and Europe

The potato changed European diets. Europe's poor ate better and lived longer.

But Ireland's poor became highly dependent on potatoes. When potato disease destroyed the crop in the mid-1840s, around one million people died of starvation and many more migrated.

## Key Understanding

Food exchange could improve life, but dependence on one crop could create disaster.

# Unit 3D: Conquest, Disease, and Trade

## The World Shrinks in the 1500s

The world "shrank" in the sixteenth century because European sailors found sea routes to Asia and crossed the western ocean to America. Distant regions became more connected through trade, conquest, and colonisation.

## America and Global Trade

After European contact, America's:

- lands
- crops
- minerals
- silver
- gold

began transforming global trade.

Silver from Peru and Mexico increased Europe's wealth and helped finance trade with Asia.

## Spanish Conquest and Smallpox

Portuguese and Spanish conquest of America was under way by the mid-sixteenth century.

The Spanish did not conquer only through guns. Smallpox was a more powerful weapon.

## Why Smallpox Was Deadly

America's original inhabitants had been isolated from Europe for a long time and had no immunity to European diseases.

Smallpox spread ahead of Europeans and killed whole communities.

## European Migration to America

Until the nineteenth century, Europe had:

- poverty
- hunger
- crowded cities
- deadly diseases
- religious conflicts
- persecution of dissenters

Many Europeans migrated to America.

## Slavery

By the eighteenth century, plantations in America used enslaved Africans to grow cotton and sugar for European markets.

## Shift of World Trade

Until the eighteenth century, India and China were among the world's richest countries and central to Asian trade.

China later restricted overseas contacts and became more isolated. The rising importance of the Americas moved the centre of world trade westward toward Europe.

# Unit 3E: The Nineteenth Century and the Three Flows

## Core Idea

The nineteenth century transformed the world economy.

Economists identify three flows:

| Flow | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Flow of trade | Movement of goods | Cloth, wheat, cotton |
| Flow of labour | Migration of people for work | Indian indentured workers, European migrants |
| Flow of capital | Movement of money for investment | London finance for railways and farms abroad |

These flows were connected. Goods needed labour and capital. Labour moved to places where land and investment expanded. Capital built railways, ports, farms, mines, and plantations.

# Unit 3F: World Economy Takes Shape

## Britain and Food Self-Sufficiency

In nineteenth-century Britain, self-sufficiency in food meant high food prices and social conflict.

Reasons:

- population increased
- cities grew
- industry expanded
- demand for foodgrains rose
- food prices rose
- landed groups pressured government to restrict imports

## Corn Laws

Corn Laws restricted import of foodgrains into Britain.

Industrialists and urban dwellers disliked them because they made food expensive.

## Abolition of Corn Laws

After the Corn Laws were abolished:

- food could be imported cheaply
- British agriculture could not compete
- land was left uncultivated
- agricultural workers lost jobs
- many migrated to cities or overseas
- food consumption in Britain increased

## Global Effects

To meet British demand:

- land was cleared in Eastern Europe, Russia, America, and Australia
- food production expanded
- railways linked farms to ports
- harbours were built and expanded
- homes and settlements were created
- capital flowed from London
- labour migrated to America and Australia

## Migration

Nearly 50 million people migrated from Europe to America and Australia in the nineteenth century.

Worldwide, about 150 million people left homes and crossed oceans or land in search of better futures.

## Canal Colonies in Punjab

In west Punjab, the British built irrigation canals to transform semi-desert land into fertile agricultural land for wheat and cotton export.

These areas were called Canal Colonies and were settled by peasants from other parts of Punjab.

## World Trade Growth

Between 1820 and 1914, world trade multiplied 25 to 40 times.

Nearly 60 percent of this trade was in primary products such as:

- wheat
- cotton
- coal

# Unit 3G: Role of Technology

## Important Technologies

The nineteenth-century global world depended on:

- railways
- steamships
- telegraph
- refrigerated ships

## Technology and Social Factors

Technology did not develop in isolation. It was shaped by social, political, and economic needs.

Colonisation encouraged investment in transport so goods could move cheaply and quickly from colonies and distant farms to markets.

## Refrigerated Ships and Meat Trade

Before refrigerated ships, live animals were transported from America to Europe.

Problems:

- animals occupied much ship space
- many died during the voyage
- some lost weight
- some became unfit to eat
- meat remained expensive

## After Refrigeration

Animals were slaughtered in America, Australia, or New Zealand and transported as frozen meat.

Effects:

- shipping costs fell
- meat prices fell
- European poor could eat more varied food
- diets improved with meat, butter, and eggs
- better living conditions supported social peace
- support for imperialism increased

## Technology Example Answer

Refrigerated ships changed food availability by allowing frozen meat to travel long distances. Meat became cheaper in Europe and was no longer a luxury only for the rich. Railways also helped by connecting farms to ports, making it possible to move wheat and other goods from distant regions to world markets.

# Unit 3H: Late Nineteenth-Century Colonialism

## Dark Side of Global Trade

Expanding trade also meant:

- loss of freedoms
- loss of livelihoods
- colonial conquest
- ecological damage
- forced labour
- social disruption

## Carving Up Africa

In 1885, European powers met in Berlin to divide Africa among themselves.

Britain and France expanded overseas territories. Belgium and Germany became new colonial powers. The US also became a colonial power in the late 1890s by taking colonies earlier held by Spain.

## Explorers and Imperialism

Explorers like Henry Morton Stanley mapped African regions, mobilised local labour, and helped imperial conquest.

Geographical exploration was not innocent science; it was linked to imperial projects.

# Unit 3I: Rinderpest in Africa

## Meaning

Rinderpest was a cattle plague that spread in Africa in the 1890s.

## Africa Before Rinderpest

Africa had:

- abundant land
- relatively small population
- livestock-based livelihoods
- little need for wage labour

Europeans wanted to establish plantations and mines, but Africans with land and cattle had little reason to work for wages.

## Labour Recruitment by Europeans

Europeans tried to create wage labour by:

- imposing heavy taxes payable only in cash
- changing inheritance laws so only one family member could inherit land
- confining mineworkers in compounds

## Arrival of Rinderpest

Rinderpest came in the late 1880s through infected cattle imported from British Asia to feed Italian soldiers invading Eritrea.

It spread from east to west like forest fire:

- reached Atlantic coast in 1892
- reached Cape five years later
- killed about 90 percent of African cattle

## Effects

| Effect | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Livelihood destruction | Africans lost cattle and economic independence. |
| Labour supply increased | Africans were forced into wage labour. |
| Colonial power strengthened | Planters, mine owners, and governments controlled scarce cattle resources. |
| Conquest easier | Control over cattle helped Europeans subdue Africans. |

## Exam Answer: Rinderpest

Rinderpest killed about 90 percent of African cattle in the 1890s. Since land and livestock sustained African livelihoods, the loss of cattle destroyed their independence. Europeans used this crisis to force Africans into wage labour for plantations and mines. By controlling scarce cattle resources, colonial governments, planters, and mine owners strengthened their power.

# Unit 3J: Indentured Labour Migration from India

## Meaning

Indentured labourers were bonded labourers hired under contracts to work for a fixed period, usually five years, in return for passage to a new country and possible return travel.

## Main Regions of Origin

Most Indian indentured workers came from:

- eastern Uttar Pradesh
- Bihar
- central India
- dry districts of Tamil Nadu

## Why People Migrated

These regions faced:

- decline of cottage industries
- rising land rents
- land cleared for mines
- land cleared for plantations
- poverty
- debt
- inability to pay rent

## Destinations

| Destination | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Caribbean islands | Trinidad, Guyana, Surinam |
| Mauritius | Plantation labour |
| Fiji | Plantation labour |
| Ceylon | Tamil migration |
| Malaya | Tamil migration |
| Assam | Tea plantations |

## Recruitment

Recruiters were paid commission by employers.

They often:

- gave false information
- hid the real destination
- hid the nature of work
- lied about living conditions
- did not explain the long sea voyage
- sometimes forcibly abducted migrants

## Why It Was Called a New System of Slavery

Indenture was called a new system of slavery because:

- living conditions were harsh
- work was heavy
- labourers had few legal rights
- employers had great control
- punishment was severe
- migrants were often deceived
- wages could be cut

## Worker Survival and Culture

Indentured workers resisted and survived by:

- escaping into forests
- creating new cultural forms
- blending old and new traditions
- joining collective celebrations

## Cultural Examples

| Cultural Form | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Hosay | Trinidad version of Muharram, joined by people of many races and religions |
| Rastafarianism | Protest religion in Caribbean, linked by some scholars to Indian migrant experiences |
| Chutney music | Music of Trinidad and Guyana reflecting post-indenture cultural fusion |

## Abolition

Indian nationalist leaders opposed indenture as cruel and abusive.

It was abolished in 1921.

# Unit 3K: Indian Entrepreneurs Abroad

## Need for Capital

Global agriculture and plantations required capital. Large plantations borrowed from banks, but peasants needed other sources.

## Indian Bankers and Traders

Indian groups financed export agriculture in Central and Southeast Asia.

Important groups:

- Shikaripuri Shroffs
- Nattukottai Chettiars

They used:

- their own funds
- money borrowed from European banks
- sophisticated money-transfer systems
- indigenous corporate organisation

## Indian Traders in Africa and Beyond

Indian traders and moneylenders followed European colonisers into Africa.

Hyderabadi Sindhi traders established emporia in busy ports worldwide from the 1860s, selling local and imported curios to tourists.

# Unit 3L: Indian Trade, Colonialism, and Global System

## Indian Cotton Before Industrialisation

Fine Indian cottons were exported to Europe.

## British Industrialisation

With industrialisation, British cotton manufacturers expanded and pressured the government to protect them.

Tariffs were imposed on cloth imports into Britain.

## Decline of Indian Textile Exports

Indian cotton textile exports declined:

- around 1800: about 30 percent of India's exports
- by 1815: about 15 percent
- by 1870s: below 3 percent

## Rise of Raw Material Exports

As manufactured exports declined, raw material exports increased.

Between 1812 and 1871, raw cotton exports rose from 5 percent to 35 percent.

Other important exports:

- indigo
- opium

## Opium and China

Britain grew opium in India and exported it to China. Money from opium sales helped finance British tea and other imports from China.

## British Trade Surplus with India

British manufactured goods flooded Indian markets. India exported foodgrains and raw materials, but British exports to India were worth more than British imports from India.

So Britain had a trade surplus with India.

## How Britain Used the Surplus

Britain used its surplus with India to:

- balance trade deficits with other countries
- pay home charges

Home charges included:

- private remittances by British officials and traders
- interest on India's external debt
- pensions of British officials in India

## Key Understanding

India played a crucial role in the late nineteenth-century world economy, but in a colonial way that served British interests.

# Unit 3M: First World War and the Inter-War Economy

## First World War

The First World War lasted from 1914 to 1918.

## Alliances

| Group | Members |
| --- | --- |
| Allies | Britain, France, Russia, later the US |
| Central Powers | Germany, Austria-Hungary, Ottoman Turkey |

## First Modern Industrial War

It was called the first modern industrial war because it used industrial weapons on a massive scale:

- machine guns
- tanks
- aircraft
- chemical weapons
- large ships
- trains

## Human Cost

About:

- 9 million dead
- 20 million injured

Most killed and injured were men of working age.

Effects:

- reduced able-bodied workforce
- household incomes declined
- women entered jobs earlier done by men
- societies reorganised for war

## US Becomes Creditor

Britain borrowed heavily from US banks and the US public.

After the war, the US became an international creditor, owning more overseas assets than foreign governments and citizens owned in the US.

# Unit 3N: Post-War Recovery

## Britain's Crisis

Britain faced a difficult post-war recovery because:

- industries developed in India and Japan during war
- Britain struggled to regain dominance in Indian market
- Britain struggled to compete with Japan internationally
- Britain had huge debts to the US
- war boom ended
- production fell
- unemployment rose
- government cut war spending

In 1921, one in five British workers was unemployed.

## Agricultural Crisis

Before the war, eastern Europe supplied wheat. During the war, Canada, America, and Australia expanded wheat production.

After the war:

- eastern European production revived
- wheat glut emerged
- grain prices fell
- rural incomes declined
- farmers fell into debt

# Unit 3O: Mass Production and Consumption

## US Recovery

The US recovered faster and grew strongly in the 1920s.

## Henry Ford and Assembly Line

Henry Ford adapted the assembly line from Chicago slaughterhouses to car production in Detroit.

## Assembly Line Features

- workers repeated one task
- conveyor belt controlled speed
- output per worker increased
- production became faster and cheaper
- workers had little control over work pace

Ford's cars came off the assembly line at three-minute intervals.

The T-Model Ford was the world's first mass-produced car.

## Worker Stress

Workers quit because the assembly line was stressful. Ford doubled wages to $5 a day in January 1914 but banned trade unions.

He recovered the wage cost by speeding up production.

## Mass Consumption

Mass production lowered prices. Higher wages allowed workers to buy consumer goods:

- cars
- refrigerators
- washing machines
- radios
- gramophones

Many goods were bought through hire purchase.

## US Boom

Housing and consumer goods created a cycle of:

employment -> income -> consumption -> investment -> more employment

The US resumed exporting capital in 1923 and became the largest overseas lender.

# Unit 3P: The Great Depression

## Meaning

The Great Depression began around 1929 and lasted till the mid-1930s.

It caused declines in:

- production
- employment
- incomes
- trade

Agricultural regions were worst affected because farm prices fell more and for longer than industrial prices.

## Causes

### 1. Agricultural Overproduction

Farmers produced too much.

As prices fell, they tried to produce more to maintain income. This created a glut and pushed prices lower.

Farm produce rotted because there were no buyers.

### 2. Withdrawal of US Loans

Many countries depended on US loans in the 1920s.

At the first sign of trouble, US lenders panicked and withdrew loans.

US overseas loans fell sharply from over $1 billion in early 1928 to one quarter of that amount a year later.

## Global Effects

| Region | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Europe | Banks failed; currencies like pound sterling collapsed |
| Latin America | Agricultural and raw material prices fell further |
| World trade | US doubled import duties, damaging trade |
| US | Banks collapsed, businesses failed, unemployment rose |

## US Depression

In the US:

- banks cut lending
- loans were called back
- farms could not sell crops
- households lost homes and cars
- businesses collapsed
- unemployment rose
- over 4,000 banks closed by 1933
- about 110,000 companies collapsed between 1929 and 1932

# Unit 3Q: India and the Great Depression

## Why India Was Affected

By the early twentieth century, India was integrated into the global economy as:

- exporter of agricultural goods
- importer of manufactured goods

So the world crisis affected India quickly.

## Trade Impact

India's exports and imports nearly halved between 1928 and 1934.

Wheat prices in India fell by 50 percent between 1928 and 1934.

## Peasants Suffered

Peasants suffered more than urban dwellers because:

- agricultural prices fell sharply
- colonial government did not reduce revenue
- export crop producers were badly hit
- debt increased
- savings were used up
- land was mortgaged
- jewellery and precious metals were sold

## Jute Producers in Bengal

Raw jute prices crashed by more than 60 percent because gunny exports collapsed.

Jute growers who had borrowed money fell deeper into debt.

## Gold Exports

During the Depression, India became an exporter of precious metals, especially gold.

John Maynard Keynes believed Indian gold exports helped global recovery and Britain's recovery, but they did little for Indian peasants.

## Urban India

The Depression was less severe for urban India because:

- prices fell
- fixed-income groups became better off
- salaried employees and rent-receiving landowners could buy more
- industrial investment grew due to tariff protection

## Link to National Movement

Rural unrest was high when Gandhi launched Civil Disobedience during the Depression.

# Unit 3R: Second World War and Post-War Reconstruction

## Second World War

The Second World War broke out about two decades after the First World War.

## Sides

| Group | Members |
| --- | --- |
| Axis Powers | Nazi Germany, Japan, Italy |
| Allies | Britain, France, Soviet Union, US |

## Destruction

At least 60 million people, about 3 percent of the world's 1939 population, died directly or indirectly.

More civilians than soldiers died.

Europe and Asia were devastated. Cities were destroyed by bombing and artillery.

## Two Crucial Post-War Influences

1. The US emerged as the dominant economic, political, and military power in the Western world.
2. The Soviet Union became a world power after defeating Nazi Germany and industrialising despite the Great Depression.

# Unit 3S: Bretton Woods System

## Lessons from Inter-War Experience

Economists and politicians learned two lessons:

### Lesson 1: Mass Production Needs Mass Consumption

Mass consumption requires:

- high and stable incomes
- stable employment
- full employment
- government intervention

Markets alone could not guarantee full employment.

### Lesson 2: Governments Need Economic Control

To achieve full employment, governments needed power to control flows of:

- goods
- capital
- labour

## Bretton Woods Conference

The United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference was held in July 1944 at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, USA.

Its aim was to create a stable post-war international economic system.

## Bretton Woods Institutions

| Institution | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| IMF | Deal with external surpluses and deficits of member nations |
| World Bank | Finance post-war reconstruction |

The IMF and World Bank are called Bretton Woods institutions or Bretton Woods twins.

They began financial operations in 1947.

## Control

Decision-making in IMF and World Bank was controlled by Western industrial powers. The US had effective veto power over key decisions.

## Fixed Exchange Rates

The Bretton Woods system was based on fixed exchange rates.

National currencies were pegged to the US dollar. The dollar was anchored to gold at $35 per ounce.

## Meaning of Bretton Woods Agreement

The Bretton Woods Agreement created a system of international economic stability after the Second World War. It established the IMF and World Bank, fixed exchange rates, and rules for managing external deficits, surpluses, and reconstruction.

# Unit 3T: Early Post-War Years

## Growth

The Bretton Woods system brought rapid growth for Western industrial nations and Japan.

Between 1950 and 1970:

- world trade grew over 8 percent annually
- incomes grew nearly 5 percent annually
- unemployment averaged less than 5 percent in most industrial countries

## Technology and Enterprise

Developing countries imported industrial plants and equipment to modernise and catch up with advanced countries.

---

# Unit 3U: Decolonisation and G-77

## Decolonisation

After the Second World War, many colonies in Asia and Africa became independent.

But they faced:

- poverty
- lack of resources
- weak economies
- colonial damage
- dependence on former colonial powers

## Shift of Bretton Woods Institutions

IMF and World Bank were originally designed for industrial countries. As Europe and Japan recovered, these institutions shifted attention to developing countries from the late 1950s.

## Continued Control

Former colonial powers and powerful corporations still controlled resources such as minerals and land in many ex-colonies.

## G-77

Most developing countries did not benefit from fast Western growth in the 1950s and 1960s.

They formed the Group of 77, or G-77, to demand a New International Economic Order.

## NIEO Demands

The New International Economic Order demanded:

- real control over natural resources
- more development assistance
- fairer prices for raw materials
- better access for manufactured goods in developed countries' markets

## G-77 as Reaction to Bretton Woods Twins

The IMF and World Bank were dominated by Western industrial powers and did not fully meet the needs of newly independent poor countries. G-77 was a reaction against this unequal order and demanded fairer rules for development and trade.

# Unit 3V: End of Bretton Woods and Modern Globalisation

## Why Bretton Woods Collapsed

From the 1960s, the rising cost of US overseas involvement weakened US finances and competitiveness.

The US dollar lost confidence as the world's main currency and could not maintain its value in relation to gold.

This led to:

- collapse of fixed exchange rates
- introduction of floating exchange rates

## Change in Finance

From the mid-1970s, developing countries increasingly borrowed from:

- Western commercial banks
- private lending institutions

This led to debt crises, lower incomes, and poverty, especially in Africa and Latin America.

## MNCs and Low-Wage Production

From the late 1970s, MNCs shifted production to low-wage Asian countries.

China became attractive because of:

- low wages
- new economic policies
- integration with world economy

The collapse of Soviet-style communism in Eastern Europe also brought more countries into the global economy.

## Effects

Relocation of industry to low-wage countries:

- stimulated world trade
- increased capital flows
- transformed economic geography
- helped countries like China, India, and Brazil undergo rapid economic change

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1. Give two examples of global exchanges before the seventeenth century.

One Asian example is the silk routes, which carried Chinese silk, pottery, Indian textiles, spices, religions, and ideas between Asia, Europe, and North Africa. One American example is the spread of foods such as potatoes, maize, tomatoes, chillies, groundnuts, and sweet potatoes to Europe and Asia after European contact with the Americas.

## Q2. How did disease help colonise the Americas?

European conquerors carried germs such as smallpox to the Americas. The original inhabitants had no immunity because they had been isolated from Europe for a long time. Smallpox spread rapidly and killed whole communities, often before Europeans reached them. This weakened resistance and helped Spanish conquest.

## Q3a. Effects of abolishing Corn Laws

After the Corn Laws were abolished, food imports into Britain became cheaper. British agriculture could not compete, so land was left uncultivated and workers lost jobs. Many migrated to cities or overseas. British food consumption rose, and land was cleared in America, Australia, Russia, and Eastern Europe to grow food for Britain.

## Q3b. Effects of rinderpest in Africa

Rinderpest killed about 90 percent of African cattle, destroying livelihoods based on livestock. Africans lost economic independence and were forced into wage labour. Colonial governments, planters, and mine owners controlled scarce cattle resources and strengthened their power.

## Q3c. Effects of death of working-age men in Europe due to World War

The First World War killed and injured millions, mostly men of working age. This reduced Europe's able-bodied workforce. Household incomes declined because fewer earning members remained. Women entered jobs earlier done by men, and societies were reorganised for war and recovery.

## Q3d. Effects of Great Depression on India

India's exports and imports nearly halved between 1928 and 1934. Wheat prices fell by 50 percent. Peasants suffered because agricultural prices fell but colonial revenue demands were not reduced. Jute growers in Bengal were hit badly as raw jute prices crashed. Indebtedness increased and many sold gold, jewellery, and land. Urban fixed-income groups were less badly affected because prices fell.

## Q3e. Effects of MNCs relocating production to Asian countries

MNCs shifted production to low-wage Asian countries from the late 1970s. This increased investment, world trade, and capital flows. Countries like China became major manufacturing centres because wages were low. The global economic geography changed as China, India, and Brazil grew rapidly.

## Q4. Give two examples of technology affecting food availability.

First, refrigerated ships allowed frozen meat to be transported from America, Australia, and New Zealand to Europe. This reduced meat prices and improved diets. Second, railways connected agricultural regions to ports, allowing wheat and other foodgrains from distant regions to reach Britain and other markets.

## Q5. What is the Bretton Woods Agreement?

The Bretton Woods Agreement was the post-Second World War economic framework created at the 1944 conference in Bretton Woods, USA. It established the IMF to manage external surpluses and deficits and the World Bank to finance reconstruction. It created a fixed exchange rate system in which currencies were pegged to the dollar, and the dollar was linked to gold.

## Q6. Letter from an indentured labourer: answer points

Include these details:

- left India due to poverty, debt, and lack of work
- recruiter gave false promises
- long sea voyage
- harsh plantation work
- few rights
- wage deductions and punishment
- homesickness
- new friendships and cultural forms
- hope for freedom after contract

## Q7. Explain three flows with India examples.

The first flow is trade in goods, such as India's export of raw cotton, indigo, opium, and agricultural products. The second is labour migration, such as Indian indentured workers going to the Caribbean, Mauritius, Fiji, Ceylon, Malaya, and Assam. The third is capital movement, such as Shikaripuri Shroffs and Nattukottai Chettiars financing export agriculture in Central and Southeast Asia.

## Q8. Causes of the Great Depression

The Great Depression was caused by agricultural overproduction and the withdrawal of US loans. As agricultural prices fell, farmers produced more to maintain income, creating a glut and further price fall. Many countries depended on US loans, but US lenders withdrew loans at the first sign of trouble. This caused bank failures, currency collapse, falling trade, unemployment, and business failures.

## Q9. What were G-77 countries? How were they a reaction to Bretton Woods twins?

G-77 was a group of developing countries that demanded a New International Economic Order. They wanted control over natural resources, more development assistance, fairer prices for raw materials, and better access to developed-country markets. They were a reaction to IMF and World Bank because these institutions were dominated by Western industrial powers and did not adequately address poverty and development needs of former colonies.
