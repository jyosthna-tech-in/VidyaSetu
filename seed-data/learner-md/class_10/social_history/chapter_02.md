## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter explains how Indian nationalism grew in a colonial context.

Nationalism in India did not grow only from speeches by leaders. It grew because many different groups experienced colonial rule as oppression. But they did not experience it in the same way. Peasants, workers, business groups, women, Dalits, Muslims, plantation labourers, and tribal communities all had different problems and different expectations from freedom.

Mahatma Gandhi and the Congress tried to bring these diverse groups into one national movement. This unity was powerful, but it was also fragile because different groups imagined swaraj differently.

The key idea is:

**Indian nationalism was a common anti-colonial movement with many voices, many hopes, and many tensions.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Colonial oppression created unity

Different groups began to feel that British rule was connected to their hardships. This created a shared anti-colonial feeling.

### 2. Unity did not mean sameness

Different groups joined national movements for different reasons. Swaraj meant different things to peasants, workers, business groups, women, Dalits, Muslims, and tribal communities.

### 3. Gandhian politics changed mass struggle

Gandhi introduced satyagraha, non-violence, boycott, non-cooperation, and civil disobedience as methods of mass political action.

### 4. Symbols made nationalism emotional

Bharat Mata, Vande Mataram, flags, folklore, and reinterpretation of history helped people imagine India as one nation.

### 5. National unity had limits

The movement faced tensions around caste, class, religion, separate electorates, workers' demands, peasants' demands, and communal representation.

# Unit 2A: Nationalism and Anti-Colonialism

## Core Idea

In colonies like India, modern nationalism developed through struggle against colonial rule.

People discovered unity while fighting colonialism. The shared experience of being oppressed by British rule created a bond among different groups.

## But Unity Was Not Simple

Every group experienced colonialism differently:

| Group | Main Concern |
| --- | --- |
| Peasants | High revenue, rent, begar, eviction |
| Tribal communities | Forest restrictions and forced labour |
| Plantation workers | Restricted movement and desire to return home |
| Business classes | Colonial restrictions on trade and industry |
| Industrial workers | Low wages and poor working conditions |
| Women | Public participation and service to nation |
| Dalits | Social oppression and political representation |
| Muslims | Minority safeguards and representation |

The Congress tried to bring these groups together, but unity often involved negotiation and conflict.

# Unit 2B: Effects of the First World War

## Why the War Was Important

The First World War created a new economic and political situation in India.

## Effects of the War

| Effect | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Defence expenditure increased | War costs rose sharply. |
| War loans | British financed war through loans. |
| Taxes increased | Customs duties were raised and income tax was introduced. |
| Prices rose | Prices doubled between 1913 and 1918. |
| Hardship for common people | Food, cloth, and basic goods became costly. |
| Forced recruitment | Villages were forced to supply soldiers. |
| Crop failures | Crops failed in 1918-19 and 1920-21. |
| Food shortages | Many regions faced acute shortages. |
| Influenza epidemic | Millions died during epidemic and famine conditions. |

According to the 1921 census, 12 to 13 million people died due to famines and the epidemic.

## Why This Helped Nationalism

People expected relief after the war, but hardship continued. Anger against colonial rule increased. This created conditions for mass movements under Gandhi.

## Answer Frame: First World War and Nationalism

The First World War increased defence expenditure, taxes, war loans, and prices. Prices doubled between 1913 and 1918, causing hardship. Villages faced forced recruitment. Crop failures and influenza caused severe suffering, and millions died. People expected relief after the war, but conditions did not improve. This anger helped the national movement spread to new social groups.

# Unit 2C: The Idea of Satyagraha

## Gandhi Returns to India

Mahatma Gandhi returned to India in January 1915 from South Africa, where he had used satyagraha against racist laws.

## Meaning of Satyagraha

Satyagraha means struggle based on truth and non-violence.

Its key principles:

- truth has moral power
- injustice can be resisted without violence
- physical force is not necessary
- a satyagrahi does not seek revenge
- the oppressor's conscience must be appealed to
- truth will ultimately triumph
- non-violence can unite people

## Why Gandhi Called It Active Resistance

Satyagraha was not passive. It required courage, discipline, sacrifice, action, and willingness to suffer without hatred.

## Early Satyagraha Movements in India

| Year | Place | Issue |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1917 | Champaran, Bihar | Peasants against oppressive plantation system |
| 1918 | Ahmedabad | Cotton mill workers |
| 1918 | Kheda, Gujarat | Peasants demanded revenue relaxation after crop failure and plague |

These successes gave Gandhi confidence to launch wider movements.

# Unit 2D: Rowlatt Act and Jallianwala Bagh

## Rowlatt Act, 1919

The Rowlatt Act was passed in 1919 despite opposition from Indian members of the Imperial Legislative Council.

It gave the government power to:

- repress political activities
- detain political prisoners without trial for two years

## Why Indians Were Outraged

Indians were outraged because:

- the Act was unjust
- it denied civil liberties
- it allowed detention without trial
- it was passed despite Indian opposition
- it continued wartime repression after the war

## Rowlatt Satyagraha

Gandhi launched nationwide non-violent civil disobedience against the Rowlatt Act.

It began with a hartal on 6 April 1919.

Events:

- rallies in cities
- railway workshop strikes
- shops closed
- British arrested local leaders
- Gandhi was barred from entering Delhi
- police fired on a peaceful procession in Amritsar on 10 April
- martial law was imposed

## Jallianwala Bagh, 13 April 1919

A large crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh in Amritsar.

Some came to protest against repressive laws. Others came for the Baisakhi fair and did not know about martial law.

General Dyer:

- entered the enclosed ground
- blocked exit points
- opened fire on the crowd
- killed hundreds

He later said he wanted to create a moral effect, meaning terror and awe in the minds of satyagrahis.

## Aftermath

After the massacre:

- strikes spread
- clashes took place
- government buildings were attacked
- British responded with brutal repression
- people were flogged
- satyagrahis were humiliated
- villages around Gujranwala were bombed

Seeing violence spread, Gandhi called off the movement.

# Unit 2E: Khilafat and Non-Cooperation

## Why Gandhi Wanted a Broad Movement

The Rowlatt Satyagraha was widespread but mostly limited to cities and towns. Gandhi wanted a broader movement that could include more people.

He also believed Hindu-Muslim unity was necessary.

## Khilafat Issue

After the First World War, Ottoman Turkey was defeated. There were rumours that a harsh peace treaty would be imposed on the Ottoman emperor, the Khalifa, who was considered the spiritual head of the Islamic world.

To defend the Khalifa's temporal powers, the Khilafat Committee was formed in Bombay in March 1919.

Young Muslim leaders Muhammad Ali and Shaukat Ali discussed united mass action with Gandhi.

## Gandhi's Strategy

Gandhi saw Khilafat as an opportunity to bring Muslims into a unified national movement.

At the Calcutta Congress session in September 1920, he convinced leaders to start a non-cooperation movement in support of Khilafat and swaraj.

## Why Non-Cooperation?

In Hind Swaraj, Gandhi argued that British rule survived because Indians cooperated with it.

If Indians refused to cooperate, British rule would collapse and swaraj would come.

## Proposed Stages

Non-Cooperation would unfold in stages:

1. Surrender of titles.
2. Boycott of civil services.
3. Boycott of army.
4. Boycott of police.
5. Boycott of courts.
6. Boycott of legislative councils.
7. Boycott of schools.
8. Boycott of foreign goods.
9. If repression followed, civil disobedience would begin.

## Congress Debate

Some Congress leaders worried about:

- boycotting council elections
- possible violence
- practical difficulties

At Nagpur in December 1920, a compromise was reached and the Non-Cooperation programme was adopted.

# Unit 2F: Non-Cooperation in Towns

## Middle-Class Participation

The Non-Cooperation-Khilafat Movement began in January 1921.

In towns:

- students left government schools and colleges
- headmasters and teachers resigned
- lawyers gave up legal practice
- council elections were boycotted in most provinces

## Exception: Madras

In Madras, the Justice Party participated in council elections because it represented non-Brahmans and saw councils as a way to gain power usually controlled by Brahmans.

## Economic Boycott

Foreign goods were boycotted.

Liquor shops were picketed.

Foreign cloth was burnt in bonfires.

## Economic Impact

The import of foreign cloth halved between 1921 and 1922.

Its value dropped from Rs. 102 crore to Rs. 57 crore.

Indian textile mills and handlooms expanded production.

## Why the Movement Slowed in Cities

| Reason | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Khadi was expensive | Poor people could not afford it for long. |
| Indian institutions were slow to develop | Students and teachers returned to government schools. |
| Courts remained necessary | Lawyers returned to government courts. |
| Practical difficulties | Boycott required alternatives that were not ready. |

# Unit 2G: Rebellion in the Countryside

## Awadh Peasant Movement

In Awadh, peasants were led by Baba Ramchandra, a sanyasi who had earlier worked in Fiji as an indentured labourer.

## Peasant Grievances

Peasants opposed:

- high rents
- cesses
- begar
- forced labour on landlords' farms
- insecurity of tenure
- regular eviction

## Demands

They demanded:

- reduction of revenue
- abolition of begar
- social boycott of oppressive landlords

## Nai-Dhobi Bandh

In many places, panchayats organised nai-dhobi bandhs, depriving landlords of services of barbers and washermen.

## Oudh Kisan Sabha

In June 1920, Jawaharlal Nehru began visiting Awadh villages to understand peasant grievances.

By October 1920, the Oudh Kisan Sabha was formed under:

- Jawaharlal Nehru
- Baba Ramchandra
- other leaders

Within a month, over 300 branches were set up.

## Tension with Congress

The Congress tried to integrate the peasant struggle into the national movement, but peasants sometimes acted in ways the Congress disliked:

- attacks on talukdars' houses
- looting bazaars
- taking over grain hoards
- interpreting Gandhi's name as permission for no taxes and land redistribution

# Unit 2H: Tribal Movement in Gudem Hills

## Region

In the Gudem Hills of Andhra Pradesh, a militant guerrilla movement spread in the early 1920s.

## Causes

Tribal people were angry because the colonial government:

- closed large forest areas
- prevented grazing
- prevented collection of fuelwood and fruits
- denied traditional forest rights
- forced begar for road building

## Leader: Alluri Sitarama Raju

Raju claimed he had special powers:

- astrological predictions
- healing
- surviving bullet shots

People believed he was an incarnation of God.

## Relation with Gandhi

Raju admired Gandhi and persuaded people to:

- wear khadi
- give up drinking

But he differed from Gandhi because he believed India could be liberated only by force, not non-violence.

## Actions

The Gudem rebels:

- attacked police stations
- tried to kill British officials
- used guerrilla warfare

Raju was captured and executed in 1924 and later became a folk hero.

# Unit 2I: Swaraj in Plantations

## Plantation Workers' Meaning of Freedom

For Assam plantation workers, freedom meant:

- right to move freely
- right to leave plantations
- connection with their home villages
- hope of getting land in their own villages

## Inland Emigration Act, 1859

Under this Act, plantation workers could not leave tea gardens without permission, and permission was rarely given.

## Response to Non-Cooperation

When workers heard of the movement:

- thousands defied authorities
- left plantations
- headed home
- believed Gandhi Raj was coming

But they were stranded by railway and steamer strikes, caught by police, and brutally beaten.

## Key Understanding

Their idea of swaraj was not the same as the Congress programme. For them it meant relief from plantation confinement and return to village life.

# Unit 2J: Chauri Chaura and Withdrawal

## Chauri Chaura, 1922

At Chauri Chaura in Gorakhpur, a peaceful demonstration turned into a violent clash with police.

## Gandhi's Decision

In February 1922, Gandhi withdrew the Non-Cooperation Movement because:

- it was becoming violent
- satyagrahis needed training
- non-violence was central to his method

## Exam Answer: Why Withdraw?

Gandhi withdrew the Non-Cooperation Movement after the Chauri Chaura incident because he felt the movement was turning violent in many places. Since satyagraha required non-violence, discipline, and moral strength, he believed people needed proper training before participating in mass struggles.

# Unit 2K: Towards Civil Disobedience

## Debate Within Congress

After Non-Cooperation was withdrawn, Congress leaders debated the next step.

## Swaraj Party

C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru formed the Swaraj Party within Congress.

They wanted to:

- enter provincial councils
- oppose British policies from inside
- argue for reform
- show councils were not democratic

## Younger Leaders

Jawaharlal Nehru and Subhas Chandra Bose wanted:

- more radical mass agitation
- full independence

## Economic Depression

Agricultural prices began falling from 1926 and collapsed after 1930.

Effects:

- demand for agricultural goods fell
- exports declined
- peasants could not sell harvests
- peasants found it hard to pay revenue
- countryside was in turmoil by 1930

## Simon Commission, 1928

The British government formed a Statutory Commission under Sir John Simon to review the constitutional system in India.

Problem:

- it had no Indian member
- all members were British

When it arrived in India in 1928, it was greeted with:

**Go back Simon**

All parties, including Congress and Muslim League, joined demonstrations.

Lala Lajpat Rai was injured during a protest and later died.

## Dominion Status Offer

In October 1929, Viceroy Lord Irwin offered vague dominion status in an unspecified future and a Round Table Conference.

This did not satisfy Congress.

## Lahore Congress, 1929

In December 1929, under Jawaharlal Nehru's presidency, the Lahore Congress formalised the demand for Purna Swaraj, or complete independence.

26 January 1930 was declared Independence Day.

People were asked to take a pledge for complete independence.

# Unit 2L: Salt March and Civil Disobedience

## Why Salt?

Gandhi chose salt because:

- it was used by rich and poor alike
- it was essential food item
- salt tax affected everyone
- government monopoly showed oppressive British rule
- it could unite many classes

## Gandhi's Eleven Demands

On 31 January 1930, Gandhi sent Viceroy Irwin a letter with eleven demands.

The demands were wide-ranging so that different classes could identify with them.

The most stirring demand was abolition of the salt tax.

## Salt March

| Detail | Information |
| --- | --- |
| Start | Sabarmati Ashram |
| Destination | Dandi, Gujarat coast |
| Distance | Over 240 miles |
| Volunteers | 78 trusted volunteers |
| Duration | 24 days |
| Daily walk | About 10 miles per day |
| Law broken | Salt law |
| Date at Dandi | 6 April 1930 |

At Dandi, Gandhi made salt by boiling seawater.

This began the Civil Disobedience Movement.

## Difference Between Non-Cooperation and Civil Disobedience

| Non-Cooperation | Civil Disobedience |
| --- | --- |
| Refusal to cooperate with British institutions | Refusal to cooperate and deliberate breaking of colonial laws |
| Boycott of schools, courts, councils, goods | Breaking salt law, refusing revenue, violating forest laws |
| Began in 1921 | Began in 1930 |
| Linked with Khilafat | Linked with Salt March and Purna Swaraj |

## Spread of Civil Disobedience

People:

- broke salt laws
- manufactured salt
- demonstrated outside salt factories
- boycotted foreign cloth
- picketed liquor shops
- refused to pay revenue
- refused chaukidari taxes
- resigned from village offices
- violated forest laws

## British Repression

The government arrested Congress leaders.

Examples:

- Abdul Ghaffar Khan arrested in April 1930
- crowds in Peshawar faced armoured cars and firing
- Gandhi arrested a month later
- workers in Sholapur attacked police posts, municipal buildings, courts, and railway stations
- about 100,000 people were arrested
- women and children were beaten
- peaceful satyagrahis were attacked

## Gandhi-Irwin Pact, 5 March 1931

Gandhi agreed to attend the Round Table Conference in London.

The government agreed to release political prisoners.

## After the Pact

Gandhi went to London in December 1931, but negotiations failed.

Back in India:

- Ghaffar Khan and Nehru were jailed
- Congress was declared illegal
- meetings, demonstrations, and boycotts were restricted

Gandhi relaunched Civil Disobedience. It continued for over a year but lost momentum by 1934.

# Unit 2M: How Social Groups Saw Civil Disobedience

Different groups participated in the Civil Disobedience Movement for different reasons.

## Rich Peasants

Examples:

- Patidars of Gujarat
- Jats of Uttar Pradesh

## Why They Joined

They were hit by:

- trade depression
- falling prices
- declining cash income
- inability to pay revenue
- government refusal to reduce revenue

For rich peasants, swaraj meant struggle against high revenue.

## Why They Later Withdrew

They were disappointed when the movement was called off in 1931 without revenue rates being reduced. When the movement restarted in 1932, many did not participate.

---

## Poor Peasants

Poor peasants were often small tenants renting land from landlords.

## Their Demands

They wanted:

- rent remission
- relief from landlord dues
- support for no-rent campaigns

## Congress Position

Congress was unwilling to support no-rent campaigns in most places because it feared upsetting rich peasants and landlords.

So the relationship between poor peasants and Congress remained uncertain.

---

## Business Classes

During the First World War, Indian merchants and industrialists made huge profits and became powerful.

## Their Demands

They wanted:

- protection against foreign imports
- favourable rupee-sterling exchange ratio
- freedom from colonial restrictions
- space for Indian trade and industry to grow

## Organisations

| Year | Organisation |
| --- | --- |
| 1920 | Indian Industrial and Commercial Congress |
| 1927 | Federation of Indian Chamber of Commerce and Industries |

Prominent leaders included Purshottamdas Thakurdas and G.D. Birla.

## Support to Movement

Business groups:

- gave financial support
- refused to buy or sell imported goods
- supported Civil Disobedience initially

## Why Support Declined

After the failure of the Round Table Conference, many business groups became less enthusiastic because they feared:

- militant activities
- prolonged disruption of business
- growing socialist influence in Congress

---

## Industrial Workers

Industrial workers did not participate in large numbers, except in the Nagpur region.

## Why Limited Participation?

As industrialists came closer to Congress, workers stayed aloof.

Congress was reluctant to include workers' demands because it feared alienating industrialists and dividing anti-imperial forces.

## Worker Participation

Some workers used Gandhian ideas in their own struggles against:

- low wages
- poor working conditions

Examples:

- railway workers' strike in 1930
- dockworkers' strike in 1932
- Chotanagpur tin mine workers wore Gandhi caps and joined protest rallies and boycotts

---

## Women

Women participated on a large scale in Civil Disobedience.

They:

- listened to Gandhi during Salt March
- joined protest marches
- manufactured salt
- picketed foreign cloth shops
- picketed liquor shops
- went to jail

## Social Background

In urban areas, many were from high-caste families.

In rural areas, many were from rich peasant households.

## Meaning for Women

Many women saw service to the nation as a sacred duty.

## Limitation

Women's public role increased, but Congress and Gandhi did not fully challenge traditional gender roles. Gandhi believed women should also be good mothers and wives, and Congress was reluctant to give women positions of authority.

# Unit 2N: Revolutionary Nationalism

Not all nationalists believed British rule could be defeated through non-violence.

## HSRA

The Hindustan Socialist Republican Army was founded in 1928 at Ferozeshah Kotla ground in Delhi.

Leaders included:

- Bhagat Singh
- Jatin Das
- Ajoy Ghosh

## Actions

HSRA targeted symbols of British power.

Examples:

- Bhagat Singh and Batukeswar Dutta threw a bomb in the Legislative Assembly in April 1929.
- There was an attempt to blow up the train carrying Lord Irwin.

## Bhagat Singh's Idea

Bhagat Singh did not want merely to glorify bombs and pistols. He wanted a social revolution where labourers and common people would be central.

His slogan:

**Inquilab Zindabad**

# Unit 2O: Limits of Civil Disobedience

Not all groups were equally moved by the abstract idea of swaraj.

Two important areas of tension were:

1. Dalit political demands.
2. Muslim political concerns.

---

## Dalits and the National Movement

From around the 1930s, "untouchables" began calling themselves Dalit, meaning oppressed.

## Congress and Dalits

Congress had long ignored Dalit issues because it feared offending conservative high-caste Hindus, called sanatanis.

## Gandhi's Efforts

Gandhi:

- said swaraj would not come for a hundred years if untouchability was not removed
- called "untouchables" Harijan
- organised satyagraha for temple entry
- worked for access to public wells, tanks, roads, and schools
- cleaned toilets to dignify bhangi work
- urged upper castes to give up untouchability

## Ambedkar's Position

Many Dalit leaders wanted political solutions.

They demanded:

- reserved seats in educational institutions
- separate electorates
- political representation

Dr B.R. Ambedkar formed the Depressed Classes Association in 1930.

## Clash at Second Round Table Conference

Ambedkar demanded separate electorates for Dalits.

Gandhi opposed this because he believed separate electorates would slow integration of Dalits into society.

## Poona Pact, 1932

The British accepted Ambedkar's demand for separate electorates. Gandhi began a fast unto death.

Ambedkar eventually accepted Gandhi's position.

The Poona Pact gave Depressed Classes:

- reserved seats in provincial and central legislative councils
- election through general electorate

The Dalit movement remained apprehensive of Congress-led nationalism.

---

## Muslims and Civil Disobedience

Many Muslim organisations responded lukewarmly to Civil Disobedience.

## Reasons

After the decline of Non-Cooperation-Khilafat:

- many Muslims felt alienated from Congress
- Congress became associated with Hindu religious nationalist groups such as Hindu Mahasabha
- Hindu-Muslim relations worsened
- religious processions led to clashes and riots
- each riot deepened distrust

## Congress-League Negotiations

In 1927, Congress and Muslim League tried to form unity.

The main issue was representation in future assemblies.

## Jinnah's Position

Muhammad Ali Jinnah was willing to give up separate electorates if Muslims got:

- reserved seats in Central Assembly
- representation according to population in Muslim-majority provinces such as Bengal and Punjab

## Failure of Compromise

At the All Parties Conference in 1928, M.R. Jayakar of the Hindu Mahasabha opposed compromise.

## Muslim Concerns

Many Muslim leaders feared that:

- Muslims would remain a minority
- their culture and identity would be submerged
- Hindu majority domination would shape politics

## Iqbal

In 1930, Sir Muhammad Iqbal, as president of the Muslim League, emphasised separate electorates as safeguards for Muslim political interests.

# Unit 2P: Sense of Collective Belonging

## Core Idea

Nationalism spreads when people begin to feel they are part of one nation.

This sense of collective belonging came through:

- shared struggles
- history
- fiction
- folklore
- songs
- popular prints
- symbols
- flags
- reinterpretation of the past

---

## Bharat Mata

The image of Bharat Mata visually associated India with a mother figure.

## Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay

Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay created the image of Bharat Mata and wrote Vande Mataram in the 1870s as a hymn to the motherland.

Vande Mataram was later included in his novel Anandamath and widely sung during the Swadeshi movement in Bengal.

## Abanindranath Tagore

Abanindranath Tagore painted Bharat Mata as:

- calm
- composed
- divine
- spiritual
- ascetic
- giving learning, food, and clothing

Devotion to Bharat Mata came to be seen as nationalism.

## Limitation

Some forms of Bharat Mata used Hindu iconography. People from other communities could feel excluded.

---

## Folklore

Nationalists collected folk tales, songs, legends, ballads, nursery rhymes, and myths.

They believed folklore:

- showed true traditional culture
- preserved national identity
- restored pride in the past
- resisted colonial damage to culture

## Tagore and Natesa Sastri

Rabindranath Tagore collected ballads, nursery rhymes, and myths in Bengal.

Natesa Sastri published The Folklore of Southern India, a four-volume collection of Tamil folk tales. He called folklore national literature and a trustworthy expression of people's real thoughts and characteristics.

---

## National Flags

## Swadeshi Flag

During the Swadeshi movement in Bengal, a tricolour flag of red, green, and yellow was designed.

It had:

- eight lotuses representing eight provinces of British India
- crescent moon representing Hindus and Muslims

## Swaraj Flag, 1921

Gandhi designed the Swaraj flag in 1921.

It was a tricolour:

- red
- green
- white

It had a spinning wheel in the centre, representing the Gandhian ideal of self-help.

Carrying the flag during marches became a symbol of defiance.

---

## Reinterpretation of History

Nationalists reinterpreted Indian history to create pride.

British writers presented Indians as:

- backward
- primitive
- incapable of governing themselves

Indian nationalists responded by highlighting ancient achievements in:

- art
- architecture
- science
- mathematics
- religion
- culture
- law
- philosophy
- crafts
- trade

They argued that India had a glorious past, followed by decline under colonial rule.

## Limitation

If the past glorified was mainly Hindu and symbols came from Hindu iconography, people from other communities could feel left out.

# Unit 2Q: Quit India Movement

## Background

The failure of the Cripps Mission and the effects of the Second World War created discontent.

## Wardha Resolution

On 14 July 1942, the Congress Working Committee at Wardha passed the Quit India resolution, demanding immediate transfer of power to Indians.

## Bombay Session

On 8 August 1942, the All India Congress Committee endorsed the resolution in Bombay.

Gandhi gave the famous call:

**Do or Die**

## Nature of the Movement

The Quit India Movement was a mass movement.

People:

- observed hartals
- held demonstrations
- carried processions
- sang national songs
- raised slogans
- disrupted colonial administration in many areas

## Participants

It included:

- students
- workers
- peasants
- ordinary people
- leaders such as Jayaprakash Narayan, Aruna Asaf Ali, Ram Manohar Lohia
- women such as Matangini Hazra, Kanaklata Barua, and Rama Devi

The British used force, but it took more than a year to suppress the movement.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1. Why is nationalism in colonies linked to anti-colonial movement?

Nationalism in colonies is linked to anti-colonial movements because people discover unity through the struggle against colonial rule. Colonialism creates common experiences of exploitation, repression, economic hardship, and loss of freedom. In India, different groups were affected differently, but the sense of being oppressed by British rule created a shared bond. Anti-colonial struggle helped people imagine themselves as part of one nation.

## Q2. How did the First World War help the growth of nationalism in India?

The war increased defence expenditure, taxes, and war loans. Prices doubled between 1913 and 1918, creating hardship. Villages faced forced recruitment. Crop failures in 1918-19 and 1920-21 caused food shortages, and influenza killed millions. People expected relief after the war, but hardship continued. This anger against colonial rule helped nationalism spread.

## Q3. Why were Indians outraged by the Rowlatt Act?

Indians were outraged because the Rowlatt Act gave the government power to repress political activity and detain political prisoners without trial for two years. It was passed despite opposition from Indian members of the Imperial Legislative Council. It violated civil liberties and continued wartime repression in peacetime.

## Q4. Why did Gandhi withdraw the Non-Cooperation Movement?

Gandhi withdrew the Non-Cooperation Movement after the Chauri Chaura incident in 1922, where a peaceful demonstration turned violent. He believed the movement was becoming violent and that satyagrahis needed proper training in non-violence before mass struggle could continue.

## Q5. What is meant by satyagraha?

Satyagraha is Gandhi's method of struggle based on truth and non-violence. It means resisting injustice without hatred or physical force. A satyagrahi appeals to the conscience of the oppressor and is willing to suffer for truth. Gandhi believed truth would ultimately triumph and non-violence could unite Indians.

## Q6. Why was the Salt March an effective symbol of resistance?

The Salt March was effective because salt was used by rich and poor alike and was an essential item of food. The salt tax and government monopoly showed the oppressive nature of British rule. By walking from Sabarmati to Dandi and making salt, Gandhi turned an everyday item into a symbol of national resistance. It allowed people across classes to join Civil Disobedience.

## Q7. Why did different social groups join Civil Disobedience?

Rich peasants joined because they wanted revenue reduction. Poor peasants wanted rent remission. Business groups wanted protection from foreign imports and freedom from colonial restrictions. Workers joined selectively for better wages and conditions. Women joined marches, made salt, picketed shops, and saw national service as sacred. Each group connected the movement to its own hopes.

## Q8. Why did political leaders differ over separate electorates?

Ambedkar and many Dalit leaders wanted separate electorates because they believed political empowerment would solve social disabilities. Gandhi opposed separate electorates for Dalits because he feared they would slow integration into Hindu society. Muslim leaders supported separate electorates as safeguards for minority political interests. Congress leaders worried that separate electorates would divide national unity.

## Q9. Compare Bharat Mata with Germania.

Both Bharat Mata and Germania personified the nation as a female figure. Germania represented Germany and used symbols such as the oak crown, sword, tricolour, and broken chains. Bharat Mata represented India as a mother figure. In Abanindranath Tagore's painting she appeared calm, spiritual, and ascetic, offering learning, food, and clothing. Both helped people emotionally identify with the nation.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing that all Indians wanted the same swaraj. | Different groups had different meanings of swaraj. |
| Confusing Non-Cooperation and Civil Disobedience. | Non-Cooperation refused cooperation; Civil Disobedience broke colonial laws. |
| Forgetting Khilafat in Non-Cooperation. | Gandhi linked Khilafat with swaraj to build Hindu-Muslim unity. |
| Saying Gandhi withdrew movements because they failed. | He withdrew Non-Cooperation because of violence and need for discipline. |
| Treating women participation as full equality. | Women joined in large numbers but were rarely given authority in Congress. |
| Ignoring Dalit and Muslim concerns. | The chapter highlights limits of national unity. |
| Writing Bharat Mata appealed equally to everyone. | Some forms used Hindu imagery and could exclude other communities. |
