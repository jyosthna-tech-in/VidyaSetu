## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter is about the making of the modern nation-state in Europe.

Before the nineteenth century, Europe was not divided into nation-states the way we know it today. Many people lived under dynastic empires, kingdoms, duchies, principalities, and multi-national empires. People often identified themselves by region, language, religion, ruler, local community, or empire rather than by a modern nation.

The chapter shows how nationalism grew through:

1. Revolutionary ideas from France.
2. Liberal demands of the middle classes.
3. Economic integration.
4. Cultural movements.
5. Popular revolts.
6. Conservative state power.
7. Wars of unification.
8. Symbols and allegories.
9. Imperial rivalries.

The key learning is:

**Nationalism was not born in one way everywhere. In France it came through revolution; in Germany and Italy through war and state power; in Britain through gradual domination by England; in the Balkans through conflict and imperial rivalry.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Nations are made, not naturally given

People do not automatically become a nation just because they live in one area. A nation is created through shared memories, struggles, institutions, symbols, political movements, and a sense of belonging.

### 2. Nationalism can be democratic or aggressive

Early nationalism in Europe often spoke of liberty, equality, rights, and popular sovereignty. Later, nationalism was also used by conservative rulers and imperial powers for domination and war.

### 3. The French Revolution changed the meaning of sovereignty

Sovereignty shifted from the king to the people. The nation was now imagined as a community of citizens.

### 4. Liberal nationalism had limits

Liberals demanded constitutional government and freedom, but many supported voting rights only for property-owning men. Women and non-propertied men were often excluded.

### 5. Culture helped create national feeling

Folk songs, folk dances, language, poetry, legends, and historical memories helped people imagine a shared national past.

### 6. Germany and Italy were unified by state power and war

After 1848, nationalism moved away from democratic revolution and became linked with monarchy, military power, diplomacy, and conservative leadership.

### 7. The Balkans show the dangerous side of nationalism

Nationalism mixed with imperial rivalry created tension, wars, and eventually contributed to the First World War.

# Unit 1A: Sorrieu's Utopian Vision

## Core Idea

In 1848, French artist Frederic Sorrieu made a series of prints imagining a world of democratic and social republics.

His first print shows people of Europe and America marching in a long procession, paying homage to the statue of Liberty.

## What the Print Shows

| Element | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Men and women of all ages and classes | People across society participating in a democratic future |
| Statue of Liberty | Freedom and Enlightenment |
| Torch | Light of Enlightenment |
| Charter of the Rights of Man | Rights, citizenship, and revolution |
| Broken symbols of absolutism | Fall of monarchies and repressive institutions |
| Flags and national costumes | Nations imagined as distinct communities |
| United States and Switzerland leading | They were already nation-states |
| France with tricolour | Revolutionary France as a model of nationalism |
| German black, red, and gold flag | Liberal hope for German unification |
| Christ, saints, and angels | Fraternity among nations |

## Why It Is Utopian

It is utopian because it imagines an ideal world where all nations live as democratic republics in harmony. In reality, Europe was still full of monarchies, empires, wars, inequalities, and competing national claims.

## Answer Frame: Sorrieu's Vision

Sorrieu's print depicts a utopian vision because it imagines all peoples of Europe and America as free nations marching together under Liberty. It shows broken symbols of absolutism, national flags, the Rights of Man, and divine figures symbolising fraternity. This ideal democratic world did not actually exist in 1848, so the image represents a hopeful but unrealistic vision.

# Unit 1B: Nation and Nation-State

## What Is a Nation-State?

A nation-state is a modern state in which citizens develop a sense of common identity, shared history, and political belonging.

It is different from a dynastic empire because loyalty is not only to a ruler. People imagine themselves as part of a nation.

## Nation-State vs Dynastic Empire

| Point | Nation-State | Dynastic Empire |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Basis | Common national identity | Rule of a dynasty or emperor |
| People | Citizens imagine shared belonging | Subjects may belong to many languages and cultures |
| Power | Nation and citizens are central | Monarch or dynasty is central |
| Example in chapter | France after Revolution, Germany after 1871 | Habsburg Empire, Ottoman Empire |

## Ernst Renan's View of a Nation

Ernst Renan argued that a nation is not simply based on:

- common language
- race
- religion
- territory

For him, a nation is built through:

- shared past
- sacrifice
- devotion
- common memories
- common will in the present
- desire to perform great deeds together
- consent of the people

He called the existence of a nation a daily plebiscite, meaning a continuing act of consent.

## Why Renan Thought Nations Were Important

Renan believed nations were important because they protected liberty. If the whole world had only one law and one master, freedom would be lost.

# Unit 1C: The French Revolution and the Idea of the Nation

## Core Idea

The first clear expression of nationalism came with the French Revolution in 1789.

Before the Revolution, France was ruled by an absolute monarch. After the Revolution, sovereignty shifted from the monarchy to the people.

## New Idea

The Revolution declared that:

**the people would constitute the nation and shape its destiny.**

## Measures Taken to Create Collective Identity

| Measure | How It Created National Feeling |
| --- | --- |
| La patrie | Created idea of a shared fatherland |
| Le citoyen | Created idea of equal citizens |
| Tricolour flag | Replaced royal standard with national symbol |
| Estates General renamed National Assembly | Shifted authority from monarchy to citizens |
| New hymns and oaths | Built emotional loyalty to the nation |
| Martyrs commemorated | Created shared memory of sacrifice |
| Centralised administration | Unified political system |
| Uniform laws | Applied same law to all citizens |
| Internal customs duties abolished | Encouraged economic unity |
| Uniform weights and measures | Made exchange easier |
| Regional dialects discouraged | Promoted French as common national language |

## Mission of French Revolutionaries

French revolutionaries believed it was the mission of France to liberate other peoples of Europe from despotism and help them become nations.

## Jacobin Clubs

When news of the French Revolution spread, students and educated middle-class people in European cities formed Jacobin clubs. Their activities prepared the way for French armies that moved into Holland, Belgium, Switzerland, and parts of Italy in the 1790s.

# Unit 1D: Napoleon and Nationalism

## Napoleon's Contradiction

Napoleon destroyed democracy in France by returning to monarchy, but he also introduced revolutionary administrative reforms in the territories he controlled.

## Napoleonic Code, 1804

The Civil Code of 1804, known as the Napoleonic Code, introduced:

- equality before law
- abolition of privileges based on birth
- right to property

## Reforms in Conquered Territories

Napoleon:

- simplified administrative divisions
- abolished feudal system
- freed peasants from serfdom and manorial dues
- removed guild restrictions in towns
- improved transport
- improved communication systems
- encouraged uniform laws
- supported common currency and standardised measures

## Why Business Groups Supported These Changes

Businessmen and small producers liked:

- uniform laws
- standard weights and measures
- common currency
- easier movement of goods
- easier movement of capital

These changes helped economic exchange.

## Mixed Reaction to French Rule

| Initial Reaction | Later Reaction |
| --- | --- |
| French armies were welcomed in places such as Holland, Switzerland, Brussels, Mainz, Milan, and Warsaw as bringers of liberty. | Enthusiasm turned to hostility because of taxation, censorship, forced conscription, and lack of political freedom. |

## Answer Frame: Napoleon's Administrative Changes

Napoleon introduced the Civil Code of 1804, abolished privileges based on birth, established equality before law, and secured the right to property. He simplified administrative divisions, abolished feudalism, freed peasants from serfdom, removed guild restrictions, improved transport and communication, and promoted uniform laws and measures. These reforms made administration more rational and efficient.

# Unit 1E: Europe Before Nation-States

## Political Fragmentation

In the mid-eighteenth century, many present-day countries did not exist as nation-states.

Examples:

- Germany was divided into many principalities.
- Italy was divided into several states.
- Switzerland had cantons.
- Eastern and Central Europe were ruled by autocratic monarchies.

## Habsburg Empire

The Habsburg Empire was a patchwork of many regions and peoples.

| Region or Group | Identity |
| --- | --- |
| Tyrol, Austria, Sudetenland | Alpine regions |
| Bohemia | Aristocracy mainly German-speaking |
| Lombardy and Venetia | Italian-speaking provinces |
| Hungary | Magyar speakers and other dialect groups |
| Galicia | Polish-speaking aristocracy |
| Peasant peoples | Bohemians, Slovaks, Slovenes, Croats, Roumans |

## Why Unity Was Difficult

People spoke different languages, had different cultures, and did not share a single collective political identity. The main tie binding them was allegiance to the emperor.

# Unit 1F: Aristocracy and the New Middle Class

## Aristocracy

The landed aristocracy was the dominant class in Europe.

Features:

- owned estates in countryside
- owned town houses
- spoke French in diplomacy and high society
- connected by marriage ties
- shared a common lifestyle across regions
- numerically small but politically powerful

## Peasantry

Most people were peasants.

| Region | Peasant Condition |
| --- | --- |
| Western Europe | Land farmed by tenants and small owners |
| Eastern and Central Europe | Large estates cultivated by serfs |

## New Middle Classes

Industrialisation and trade created new social groups:

- industrialists
- businessmen
- professionals
- working-class population

The educated liberal middle classes became supporters of national unity and abolition of aristocratic privileges.

# Unit 1G: Liberal Nationalism

## Meaning of Liberalism

The word liberalism comes from the Latin word liber, meaning free.

For the new middle classes, liberalism meant:

- freedom for the individual
- equality before law
- government by consent
- constitution
- representative government through parliament
- end of autocracy
- end of clerical privileges
- right to private property
- freedom of markets
- free movement of goods and capital

## Political Liberalism

Politically, liberalism supported:

- constitutional rule
- representative government
- parliament
- individual rights
- equality before law

## Limits of Liberalism

Liberalism did not always mean democracy for everyone.

In revolutionary France:

- only property-owning men could vote and stand for election
- men without property were excluded
- women were excluded
- only briefly under the Jacobins did all adult males get suffrage

The Napoleonic Code reduced women to the status of minors under the authority of fathers and husbands.

## Economic Liberalism

Economically, liberalism meant:

- freedom of markets
- abolition of state restrictions on movement of goods and capital
- unified economic territory

## German Confederation Problem

Napoleon created a confederation of 39 German states. Each had its own:

- currency
- weights
- measures
- customs barriers

A merchant travelling from Hamburg to Nuremberg in 1833 had to cross 11 customs barriers and pay duty at each one.

## Zollverein, 1834

The Zollverein was a customs union formed in 1834 at the initiative of Prussia and joined by most German states.

It:

- abolished tariff barriers
- reduced currencies from over thirty to two
- encouraged economic unity
- helped free movement of goods
- promoted railway networks
- strengthened nationalist sentiment

## Why Zollverein Matters

Economic unity helped people think of political unity. Shared markets encouraged the idea of a German nation.

# Unit 1H: Conservatism after 1815

## Core Idea

After Napoleon's defeat in 1815, European governments became conservative.

## What Conservatives Believed

Conservatives wanted to preserve:

- monarchy
- Church
- social hierarchy
- property
- family
- traditional institutions

However, they did not simply want to return to the pre-revolutionary past. They believed modernisation could strengthen monarchy.

## Modernisation for Conservatives

Conservatives supported:

- modern army
- efficient bureaucracy
- dynamic economy
- abolition of feudalism and serfdom when useful

They wanted modern tools to strengthen traditional rule.

## Congress of Vienna, 1815

In 1815, Britain, Russia, Prussia, and Austria met at Vienna after defeating Napoleon.

The Congress was hosted by Austrian Chancellor Duke Metternich.

## Treaty of Vienna

The Treaty of Vienna aimed to undo many Napoleonic changes and restore conservative order.

Main decisions:

| Decision | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Bourbon dynasty restored in France | Restore monarchy |
| France lost territories annexed under Napoleon | Reduce French power |
| Kingdom of Netherlands set up in north | Prevent French expansion |
| Genoa added to Piedmont in south | Strengthen states around France |
| Prussia gained western territories | Strengthen Prussia |
| Austria gained control of northern Italy | Strengthen Austria |
| German confederation of 39 states left untouched | Maintain balance |
| Russia got part of Poland | Reward Russia |
| Prussia got part of Saxony | Reward Prussia |

## Conservative Repression

Conservative regimes:

- were autocratic
- did not tolerate criticism
- curbed dissent
- imposed censorship on newspapers, books, plays, and songs
- tried to control ideas of liberty and freedom

# Unit 1I: Revolutionaries and Mazzini

## Secret Societies

After 1815, many liberal-nationalists went underground because of repression.

Secret societies were formed to:

- train revolutionaries
- spread nationalist ideas
- oppose monarchy
- fight for liberty
- create nation-states

## Giuseppe Mazzini

Giuseppe Mazzini was an Italian revolutionary born in Genoa in 1807.

Key points:

- joined the secret society Carbonari
- exiled in 1831 for attempting revolution in Liguria
- founded Young Italy in Marseilles
- founded Young Europe in Berne
- believed nations were natural units of mankind
- wanted Italy to become a single unified republic
- opposed monarchy
- inspired secret societies in Germany, France, Switzerland, and Poland

## Metternich's View

Metternich described Mazzini as the most dangerous enemy of the social order because Mazzini's democratic nationalism threatened monarchy and conservative rule.

# Unit 1J: Age of Revolutions, 1830-1848

## July Revolution, 1830

The first upheaval took place in France in July 1830.

The Bourbon kings were overthrown by liberal revolutionaries, and a constitutional monarchy was installed under Louis Philippe.

Metternich said that when France sneezes, the rest of Europe catches cold. This meant revolutionary events in France influenced other parts of Europe.

## Belgium

The July Revolution sparked an uprising in Brussels, leading Belgium to break away from the United Kingdom of the Netherlands.

## Greek War of Independence

Greece had been part of the Ottoman Empire since the fifteenth century.

The Greek struggle for independence began in 1821.

Support came from:

- Greeks in exile
- West Europeans who admired ancient Greek culture
- poets and artists
- Lord Byron, who organised funds and went to fight

Lord Byron died of fever in 1824.

The Treaty of Constantinople in 1832 recognised Greece as an independent nation.

## Why Greek Independence Was Important

It mobilised nationalist feelings across Europe and showed how culture, history, and sympathy could support political struggle.

# Unit 1K: Romanticism and National Feeling

## Meaning

Romanticism was a cultural movement that helped develop nationalist sentiment.

Romantic artists and poets criticised excessive glorification of reason and science. They valued:

- emotions
- intuition
- mystical feeling
- folk traditions
- national heritage
- shared cultural past

## Herder and Volksgeist

German philosopher Johann Gottfried Herder argued that true German culture was found among the common people, or das volk.

The spirit of the nation, or volksgeist, was expressed through:

- folk songs
- folk poetry
- folk dances
- legends
- popular traditions

## Culture and Nationalism

Culture created nationalism by:

- giving people shared memories
- preserving language
- collecting folk traditions
- creating pride in common heritage
- connecting people emotionally to a nation

## Poland and Language

Language played an important role in nationalism.

In Poland, after Russian occupation, the Polish language was forced out of schools and Russian was imposed.

In 1831, an armed rebellion against Russian rule was crushed.

After this, many Polish clergy used Polish for church gatherings and religious instruction. As a result, many priests and bishops were punished.

Polish became a symbol of national struggle against Russian dominance.

# Unit 1L: Economic Hardship and Popular Revolt

## Europe in the 1830s

Europe faced serious economic hardship:

- population growth
- unemployment
- migration from villages to cities
- overcrowded slums
- competition from cheap machine-made goods from England
- hardship among small producers
- peasants burdened by feudal dues
- food price rise
- bad harvests
- pauperism in towns and countryside

## 1848 in France

Food shortages and unemployment brought people of Paris onto the roads.

Barricades were erected.

Louis Philippe fled.

A National Assembly proclaimed a republic, granted suffrage to all adult males over 21, guaranteed the right to work, and set up national workshops for employment.

## Silesian Weavers' Uprising, 1845

Weavers in Silesia revolted against contractors who supplied raw material and ordered finished textiles but drastically reduced payments.

The weavers:

- demanded higher wages
- marched to the contractor's mansion
- were treated with threats and scorn
- broke into the house and storehouse
- destroyed furniture and cloth

The army was called, and eleven weavers were shot.

## Cause of Silesian Uprising

The main cause was economic exploitation by contractors, falling payments, poverty, and unemployment among textile workers.

# Unit 1M: 1848 Revolution of the Liberals

## Core Idea

In 1848, revolts of poor peasants and workers happened alongside a revolution led by educated middle classes.

## Liberal Middle-Class Demands

Liberal middle classes demanded:

- constitutionalism
- national unification
- parliamentary government
- constitution
- freedom of press
- freedom of association

## Frankfurt Parliament

In German regions, political associations of middle-class professionals, businessmen, and prosperous artisans met in Frankfurt.

On 18 May 1848, 831 elected representatives marched to the Church of St Paul to take seats in the Frankfurt Parliament.

They drafted a constitution for a German nation headed by a monarchy subject to parliament.

## Why Frankfurt Parliament Failed

| Reason | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| King rejected crown | Friedrich Wilhelm IV of Prussia rejected the crown offered by elected deputies. |
| Monarchs opposed assembly | Other monarchs joined opposition. |
| Aristocracy and military grew stronger | Conservative forces suppressed the parliament. |
| Middle-class limits | Parliament resisted demands of workers and artisans. |
| Loss of social support | Workers and artisans withdrew support. |
| Military repression | Troops forced the assembly to disband. |

## Women and 1848

Women participated actively in liberal movements.

They:

- formed political associations
- founded newspapers
- attended political meetings
- joined demonstrations

But they were denied suffrage and admitted to the Frankfurt Parliament only as observers in the visitors' gallery.

## Liberalism's Contradiction

The 1848 movement spoke of liberty but excluded women from political rights. This shows that liberal ideology was not fully equal or democratic.

## After 1848

Conservatives suppressed liberal movements, but they could not restore the old order completely.

After 1848:

- monarchs realised concessions were necessary
- serfdom and bonded labour were abolished in Habsburg lands and Russia
- Habsburg rulers gave more autonomy to Hungarians in 1867

# Unit 1N: Unification of Germany

## Core Idea

After 1848, nationalism moved away from democracy and revolution. It became linked with conservative power, monarchy, army, and bureaucracy.

## Failed Liberal Attempt

Middle-class Germans tried to unite German regions through an elected parliament in 1848, but the effort failed.

## Prussia's Leadership

After 1848, Prussia led the movement for German unification.

## Otto von Bismarck

Bismarck, chief minister of Prussia, was the architect of German unification.

He used:

- Prussian army
- bureaucracy
- diplomacy
- war

## Three Wars

Three wars over seven years were fought with:

- Denmark
- Austria
- France

Prussian victory completed unification.

## Proclamation of German Empire

On 18 January 1871, Prussian king William I was proclaimed German Emperor in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles.

## Nature of German Nation-Building

German unification showed the dominance of Prussian state power.

The new German state focused on modernising:

- currency
- banking
- legal system
- judicial system

## Answer Frame: German Unification

German unification was led by Prussia after the failure of the liberal Frankfurt Parliament. Otto von Bismarck used the Prussian army and bureaucracy to achieve unity through three wars with Denmark, Austria, and France. In January 1871, William I of Prussia was proclaimed German Emperor at Versailles. The new state reflected Prussian power and modernised German currency, banking, legal, and judicial systems.

# Unit 1O: Unification of Italy

## Fragmentation of Italy

In the mid-nineteenth century, Italy was divided into seven states.

Only Sardinia-Piedmont was ruled by an Italian princely house.

| Region | Control |
| --- | --- |
| North | Austrian Habsburgs |
| Centre | Pope |
| South | Bourbon kings of Spain |
| Sardinia-Piedmont | Italian princely house |

Italian language also had many regional and local variations.

## Mazzini's Role

In the 1830s, Giuseppe Mazzini tried to create a programme for a unitary Italian republic and formed Young Italy.

His revolts in 1831 and 1848 failed.

## Cavour's Role

Count Camillo de Cavour was chief minister of Sardinia-Piedmont.

He was:

- not a revolutionary
- not a democrat
- a wealthy educated elite
- skilled in diplomacy

He formed a diplomatic alliance with France and defeated Austria in 1859.

## Garibaldi's Role

Giuseppe Garibaldi was a famous Italian freedom fighter.

Key points:

- joined Young Italy
- participated in uprising in Piedmont in 1834
- lived in exile in South America
- supported Victor Emmanuel II
- led the Expedition of the Thousand in 1860
- volunteers were called Red Shirts
- marched into South Italy and Kingdom of the Two Sicilies
- gained support of peasants to drive out Spanish rulers

## Victor Emmanuel II

In 1861, Victor Emmanuel II was proclaimed king of united Italy.

## Final Stage

In 1870, after France withdrew troops from Rome during war with Prussia, the Papal States joined Italy.

## Important Limitation

Many Italians, especially illiterate peasants, did not understand liberal-nationalist ideology. Some peasants who supported Garibaldi had never heard of Italia and thought La Talia was Victor Emmanuel's wife.

# Unit 1P: The Strange Case of Britain

## Core Idea

Britain's nation-state formation was unlike Germany and Italy. It was not the result of sudden revolution or war of unification. It was a long process led by England's growing power.

## Before the Eighteenth Century

There was no British nation.

People had ethnic identities such as:

- English
- Welsh
- Scot
- Irish

Each had its own cultural and political traditions.

## England's Role

As England became wealthier and more powerful, it extended influence over other nations of the British Isles.

The English Parliament became the instrument through which a nation-state with England at the centre was created.

## Act of Union, 1707

The Act of Union between England and Scotland created the United Kingdom of Great Britain.

In effect, England imposed its influence on Scotland.

## Scotland

Scottish culture and institutions were suppressed.

Scottish Highlanders:

- faced repression
- were forbidden to speak Gaelic
- were forbidden to wear national dress
- were driven out of their homeland in large numbers

## Ireland

Ireland was divided between Catholics and Protestants.

The English helped Protestants dominate a largely Catholic country.

Catholic revolts were suppressed.

After the failed revolt of Wolfe Tone and United Irishmen in 1798, Ireland was incorporated into the United Kingdom in 1801.

## British Identity

A new British nation was promoted through:

- Union Jack
- national anthem
- English language
- dominant English culture

Older nations survived as subordinate partners.

## Answer Frame: Britain Unlike the Rest of Europe

Nationalism in Britain developed through a long process rather than sudden revolution. England grew in wealth and power and extended its influence over Scotland, Wales, and Ireland. The Act of Union in 1707 brought Scotland into the United Kingdom, while Ireland was incorporated in 1801 after suppression of Catholic revolts. British identity was promoted through the Union Jack, national anthem, English language, and dominant English culture, while older identities were subordinated.

# Unit 1Q: Visualising the Nation

## Why Personify a Nation?

A ruler can be shown through a portrait or statue. But a nation is an abstract idea. Artists gave the nation a face by personifying it as a female figure.

## Allegory

An allegory represents an abstract idea through a person or object.

Examples:

- Liberty as a woman with a red cap or broken chain
- Justice as a blindfolded woman with weighing scales

## Marianne

Marianne was the female allegory of France.

Her features came from Liberty and the Republic:

- red cap
- tricolour
- cockade

Marianne statues were placed in public squares, and her image appeared on coins and stamps.

Purpose:

- remind people of national unity
- persuade people to identify with the nation
- make the abstract nation visible

## Germania

Germania was the female allegory of Germany.

She wore a crown of oak leaves because the German oak symbolised heroism.

## Symbols of Germania

| Attribute | Significance |
| --- | --- |
| Broken chains | Being freed |
| Breastplate with eagle | Strength of German empire |
| Crown of oak leaves | Heroism |
| Sword | Readiness to fight |
| Olive branch around sword | Willingness to make peace |
| Black, red, and gold tricolour | Flag of liberal nationalists in 1848 |
| Rays of rising sun | Beginning of a new era |

# Unit 1R: Nationalism and Imperialism

## Change in Nationalism

By the last quarter of the nineteenth century, nationalism lost much of its idealistic liberal-democratic character.

It became:

- narrow
- aggressive
- intolerant
- linked with war
- manipulated by imperial powers

## The Balkans

The Balkans were the most serious source of nationalist tension after 1871.

The region included modern:

- Romania
- Bulgaria
- Albania
- Greece
- Macedonia
- Croatia
- Bosnia-Herzegovina
- Slovenia
- Serbia
- Montenegro

The inhabitants were broadly known as Slavs.

## Why the Balkans Became Explosive

| Cause | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Ethnic diversity | Many Slavic and other groups lived in the region. |
| Ottoman control | Large parts were under Ottoman Empire. |
| Ottoman decline | Subject nationalities broke away one by one. |
| Romantic nationalism | Groups used history to claim past independence. |
| Territorial jealousy | Balkan states wanted more territory from each other. |
| Big power rivalry | Russia, Germany, England, and Austro-Hungary competed for influence. |
| Imperial interests | Trade, colonies, naval power, and military power intensified rivalry. |

## Result

The Balkan conflict led to wars and eventually contributed to the First World War in 1914.

## Anti-Imperial Nationalism

Meanwhile, colonised peoples in other parts of the world began opposing imperial domination. These anti-imperial movements were nationalist because they struggled to form independent nation-states.

European nationalism was not copied exactly everywhere. Different societies developed their own forms of nationalism.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Write a Note On

### Giuseppe Mazzini

Giuseppe Mazzini was an Italian revolutionary born in Genoa in 1807. He joined the Carbonari and was exiled in 1831 for attempting revolution in Liguria. He founded Young Italy and Young Europe. Mazzini believed nations were natural units of mankind and wanted Italy to become a unified republic. His opposition to monarchy and vision of democratic republics frightened conservatives.

### Count Camillo de Cavour

Cavour was the chief minister of Sardinia-Piedmont. He was neither a revolutionary nor a democrat, but a skilled diplomat. He used alliance with France to defeat Austria in 1859 and helped Sardinia-Piedmont lead Italian unification. He represented the role of elite diplomacy and state power in Italian nationalism.

### Greek War of Independence

Greece had been under Ottoman rule since the fifteenth century. The Greek struggle for independence began in 1821. Greek nationalists received support from Greeks in exile and West Europeans who admired ancient Greek culture. Lord Byron raised funds and joined the struggle, dying in 1824. The Treaty of Constantinople in 1832 recognised Greece as an independent nation.

### Frankfurt Parliament

In 1848, 831 elected representatives met in the Church of St Paul in Frankfurt to draft a constitution for a united Germany under a monarchy subject to parliament. The Prussian king Friedrich Wilhelm IV rejected the crown offered by the parliament. The assembly lost support because it ignored workers' and artisans' demands, and troops finally forced it to disband.

### Role of Women in Nationalist Struggles

Women actively participated in nationalist struggles by forming political associations, founding newspapers, attending meetings, and joining demonstrations. However, liberal movements often denied them political rights. In the Frankfurt Parliament, women were admitted only as observers in the visitors' gallery. Feminists such as Louise Otto-Peters argued that liberty was indivisible and must include women.

## Q1. What steps did the French revolutionaries take to create collective identity?

French revolutionaries introduced la patrie and le citoyen to emphasise fatherland and equal citizenship. They adopted the tricolour flag, renamed the Estates General as the National Assembly, composed new hymns, took oaths, commemorated martyrs, created a centralised administration, introduced uniform laws, abolished internal customs duties, adopted uniform weights and measures, and promoted French as the common language.

## Q2. Who were Marianne and Germania? Why was their portrayal important?

Marianne was the female allegory of France, and Germania was the female allegory of Germany. They were used to personify the nation, which is otherwise an abstract idea. Marianne carried symbols of Liberty and the Republic such as the red cap, tricolour, and cockade. Germania wore an oak crown symbolising heroism and carried symbols such as sword, broken chains, and tricolour. Their images helped people identify emotionally with the nation.

## Q3. Briefly trace the process of German unification.

After the failure of the Frankfurt Parliament in 1848, Prussia led German unification. Otto von Bismarck, chief minister of Prussia, used the Prussian army and bureaucracy to unify Germany. Three wars over seven years with Denmark, Austria, and France ended in Prussian victory. On 18 January 1871, William I of Prussia was proclaimed German Emperor at Versailles. The new German state reflected Prussian power and modernised currency, banking, law, and judiciary.

## Q4. What changes did Napoleon introduce to make administration more efficient?

Napoleon introduced the Civil Code of 1804, abolished birth-based privileges, established equality before law, and secured the right to property. He simplified administrative divisions, abolished feudalism, freed peasants from serfdom and manorial dues, removed guild restrictions, improved transport and communication, and promoted uniform laws, standard weights, measures, and currency.

## Q5. Explain the 1848 revolution of the liberals and their ideas.

The 1848 revolution of the liberals was led by educated middle classes in regions such as Germany, Italy, Poland, and the Austro-Hungarian Empire. They demanded national unification, constitutionalism, representative government, freedom of press, freedom of association, and parliamentary rule. Socially, they supported equality before law and abolition of aristocratic privileges. Economically, they supported free markets and removal of restrictions on movement of goods and capital. However, many liberals limited political rights to property-owning men and excluded women.

## Q6. Choose three examples showing contribution of culture to nationalism.

First, Romanticism helped create national feeling by valuing emotions, folk traditions, poetry, and shared heritage. Second, Herder argued that true German culture lived among the common people, and folk songs, dances, and poetry expressed the volksgeist. Third, language became a symbol of national resistance in Poland, where Polish was used in church gatherings after Russian was imposed in schools. These cultural forms helped people imagine a shared nation.

## Q7. How was nationalism in Britain unlike the rest of Europe?

In Britain, nationalism did not arise from a sudden revolution or war of unification. It developed gradually through the growing power of England. The Act of Union in 1707 joined England and Scotland, and Ireland was incorporated in 1801 after suppression of revolts. English culture, language, Parliament, Union Jack, and national anthem shaped British identity, while Scottish, Welsh, and Irish identities became subordinate.

## Q8. Why did nationalist tensions emerge in the Balkans?

Nationalist tensions emerged in the Balkans because the region had many ethnic groups and was largely under Ottoman control. As the Ottoman Empire weakened, subject nationalities demanded independence and used history to claim lost freedom. Balkan states became jealous of one another and wanted more territory. Big powers such as Russia, Germany, England, and Austro-Hungary competed for influence. This made the region explosive and led to wars, contributing to the First World War.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| Writing that nationalism existed naturally from ancient times. | Modern nationalism was constructed through struggle, culture, politics, and state formation. |
| Confusing nation-state with empire. | Nation-state is based on shared national identity; empire may include many peoples under one ruler. |
| Treating liberalism as full democracy. | Nineteenth-century liberalism often excluded women and non-propertied men. |
| Forgetting economic nationalism. | Zollverein helped German economic unity and encouraged political nationalism. |
| Writing that Bismarck was a liberal democrat. | Bismarck used conservative state power, army, and bureaucracy. |
| Confusing Mazzini, Cavour, and Garibaldi. | Mazzini was revolutionary thinker, Cavour was diplomat, Garibaldi was military volunteer leader. |
| Writing Britain unified like Germany. | Britain formed through gradual English domination, not a single unification war. |
| Ignoring the Balkans. | The Balkans are central to the chapter's final argument about nationalism and imperialism. |
