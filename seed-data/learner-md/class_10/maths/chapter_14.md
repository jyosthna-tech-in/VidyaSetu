# Unit 14A: Theoretical Probability

If all outcomes are equally likely, then:

$$
P(E)=\frac{\text{number of outcomes favourable to }E}{\text{number of all possible outcomes}}
$$

Probability is always between `0` and `1`:

$$
0\le P(E)\le 1
$$

## Complement Rule

If `E` is an event, then:

$$
P(E)+P(\text{not }E)=1
$$

So:

$$
P(\text{not }E)=1-P(E)
$$

This is often the fastest method for questions involving "at least one", "not", or "none".

## Question 2: Equally Likely Outcomes

### Part I: Car Starts or Does Not Start

Not necessarily equally likely.

A car in good condition is more likely to start than not start.

### Part II: Player Shoots or Misses

Not necessarily equally likely.

The chance depends on the player's skill, distance, and conditions.

### Part III: True-False Question

Equally likely only if the answer is guessed randomly.

If the student knows the answer, the outcomes are not equally likely.

### Part IV: Baby Born as Boy or Girl

In an elementary probability model, boy and girl may be treated as equally likely.

In real population data, the two outcomes are not exactly equally likely.

## Question 3: Tossing a Coin for Football

A fair coin has two equally likely outcomes:

So neither team is favoured.

That is why tossing a coin is considered a fair way to decide which team gets the ball.

## Question 22: Sum on Two Dice

There are:

$$
36
$$

equally likely outcomes.

| Sum | Probability |
|---:|---:|
| 2 | `1/36` |
| 3 | `2/36` |
| 4 | `3/36` |
| 5 | `4/36` |
| 6 | `5/36` |
| 7 | `6/36` |
| 8 | `5/36` |
| 9 | `4/36` |

The sums are not equally likely. For example:

## Question 25: Correct or Incorrect Arguments

### Part I

Argument: two coins have three possible outcomes: two heads, two tails, or one of each, each with probability `1/3`.

This is incorrect.

The elementary outcomes are:

$$
HH,\quad HT,\quad TH,\quad TT
$$

The event "one of each" has two outcomes:

$$
HT,\quad TH
$$

So:

$$
P(\text{one of each})=\frac{2}{4}=\frac{1}{2}
$$

not `1/3`.

### Part II

Argument: if a die is thrown, the possible outcomes odd and even have probability `1/2` each.

This is correct.

Odd outcomes:

$$
1,\quad3,\quad5
$$

Even outcomes:

$$
2,\quad4,\quad6
$$

Each has 3 favourable outcomes out of 6.

So:

$$
P(\text{odd})=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}
$$

# Unit 14D: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Why it is wrong | Correct habit |
|---|---|---|
| Treating grouped outcomes as equally likely | Groups may contain different numbers of elementary outcomes | Count elementary outcomes |
| Forgetting complement rule | Makes "not" and "at least one" questions longer | Use `1 - P(E)` |
| Saying probability can be more than 1 | Probability is a ratio of favourable to total outcomes | Keep answers between 0 and 1 |
| Counting cards without suits | A deck has 4 suits | Use 52 total cards |
| Forgetting order in two dice | `(1,4)` and `(4,1)` are different outcomes | Use ordered pairs |
| Treating repeated trials as one trial | Tossing three coins has 8 outcomes | Multiply possibilities |

# Final One-Page Summary

1. Probability formula:

$$
P(E)=\frac{\text{favourable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}
$$

2. Complement rule:

$$
P(E)+P(\text{not }E)=1
$$

3. Impossible event:

$$
P(E)=0
$$

4. Sure event:

$$
P(E)=1
$$

5. All probabilities satisfy:

$$
0\le P(E)\le 1
$$

6. One coin has 2 outcomes.
7. Two coins have 4 outcomes.
8. Three coins have 8 outcomes.
9. One die has 6 outcomes.
10. Two dice have 36 outcomes.
11. A deck has 52 cards.
12. Always count elementary outcomes, not just named categories.
