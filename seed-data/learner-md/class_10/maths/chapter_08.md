# Unit 8A: Why Trigonometry Exists

Trigonometry helps us find unknown heights and distances when direct measurement is difficult.

Examples:

- finding the height of a tower without climbing it;
- finding the width of a river without crossing it;
- finding the altitude of an object in the sky;
- working in navigation, surveying, astronomy, physics, and engineering.

The word trigonometry comes from Greek roots:

| Root | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `tri` | three |
| `gon` | sides |
| `metron` | measure |

So, trigonometry is connected with measuring triangles.

In Class 10, we mainly study right triangles.

# Unit 8B: Sides of a Right Triangle

Consider a right triangle `ABC`, right-angled at `B`.

If we focus on angle `A`, then:

- `AC` is the hypotenuse;
- `BC` is opposite to angle `A`;
- `AB` is adjacent to angle `A`.

If we focus on angle `C`, then:

- `AC` is still the hypotenuse;
- `AB` is opposite to angle `C`;
- `BC` is adjacent to angle `C`.

Important:

The hypotenuse never changes, but opposite and adjacent sides depend on which acute angle you choose.

# Unit 8C: The Six Trigonometric Ratios

For an acute angle `A` in a right triangle:

## Sine

$$
\sin A = \frac{\text{side opposite to }A}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

## Cosine

$$
\cos A = \frac{\text{side adjacent to }A}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

## Tangent

$$
\tan A = \frac{\text{side opposite to }A}{\text{side adjacent to }A}
$$

## Cosecant

$$
\cosec A = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{side opposite to }A}
$$

So:

$$
\cosec A = \frac{1}{\sin A}
$$

## Secant

$$
\sec A = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{side adjacent to }A}
$$

So:

$$
\sec A = \frac{1}{\cos A}
$$

## Cotangent

$$
\cot A = \frac{\text{side adjacent to }A}{\text{side opposite to }A}
$$

So:

$$
\cot A = \frac{1}{\tan A}
$$

# Unit 8D: Ratio Relationships

## Reciprocal Relationships

| Ratio | Reciprocal |
|---|---|
| `sin A` | `cosec A` |
| `cos A` | `sec A` |
| `tan A` | `cot A` |

So:

$$
\sin A \cdot \cosec A = 1
$$

$$
\cos A \cdot \sec A = 1
$$

$$
\tan A \cdot \cot A = 1
$$

## Quotient Relationships

Since:

$$
\sin A = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

and:

$$
\cos A = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

then:

$$
\frac{\sin A}{\cos A}
=
\frac{\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}}{\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}}
$$

So:

$$
\tan A = \frac{\sin A}{\cos A}
$$

Similarly:

$$
\cot A = \frac{\cos A}{\sin A}
$$

# Unit 8E: Important Notation Rules

## `sin A` Is One Symbolic Expression

The expression `sin A` means "sine of angle A".

It does not mean:

$$
\sin \times A
$$

Similarly:

- `cos A` does not mean `cos` multiplied by `A`;
- `tan A` does not mean `tan` multiplied by `A`.

## Meaning of Powers

The notation:

$$
\sin^2 A
$$

means:

$$
(\sin A)^2
$$

Similarly:

$$
\cos^2 A = (\cos A)^2
$$

and:

$$
\tan^2 A = (\tan A)^2
$$

## Important Warning About Inverse Notation

The expression:

$$
\cosec A
$$

means:

$$
\frac{1}{\sin A}
$$

But:

$$
\sin^{-1} A
$$

does not mean `cosec A`.

The symbol `sin^{-1} A` has a different meaning studied in higher classes.

# Unit 8F: Ratios Do Not Depend on Triangle Size

If two right triangles have the same acute angle, they are similar by the AA similarity criterion.

Corresponding sides of similar triangles are proportional.

Therefore, side ratios such as:

$$
\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

and:

$$
\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

remain the same.

That is why `sin A`, `cos A`, and the other trigonometric ratios depend on the angle `A`, not on the size of the right triangle.

# Unit 8G: Finding All Ratios From One Ratio

## Method

When one trigonometric ratio is given:

1. convert the ratio into sides;
2. introduce a positive multiplier such as `k`;
3. use Pythagoras theorem to find the missing side;
4. write all six ratios.

## Worked Example 1: Given `tan A = 4/3`

Given:

$$
\tan A = \frac{4}{3}
$$

Since:

$$
\tan A = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}
$$

take:

$$
\text{opposite} = 4k
$$

and:

$$
\text{adjacent} = 3k
$$

Now find the hypotenuse:

$$
\text{hypotenuse}^2 = (4k)^2 + (3k)^2
$$

$$
\text{hypotenuse}^2 = 16k^2 + 9k^2
$$

$$
\text{hypotenuse}^2 = 25k^2
$$

Therefore:

$$
\text{hypotenuse} = 5k
$$

Now:

$$
\sin A = \frac{4k}{5k} = \frac{4}{5}
$$

$$
\cos A = \frac{3k}{5k} = \frac{3}{5}
$$

$$
\tan A = \frac{4}{3}
$$

$$
\cosec A = \frac{5}{4}
$$

$$
\sec A = \frac{5}{3}
$$

$$
\cot A = \frac{3}{4}
$$

## Worked Example 2: Given `sin A = 1/3`

Given:

$$
\sin A = \frac{1}{3}
$$

Since sine is opposite over hypotenuse, take:

$$
\text{opposite} = k
$$

and:

$$
\text{hypotenuse} = 3k
$$

Find adjacent side:

$$
\text{adjacent}^2 = (3k)^2 - k^2
$$

$$
\text{adjacent}^2 = 9k^2 - k^2 = 8k^2
$$

Therefore:

$$
\text{adjacent} = 2\sqrt{2}k
$$

So:

$$
\cos A = \frac{2\sqrt{2}k}{3k} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}
$$

and:

$$
\tan A = \frac{k}{2\sqrt{2}k} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{2}}
$$

# Unit 8H: Solved Exercise 8.1

## Question 1: Right Triangle `ABC`, Right-Angled at `B`

Given:

$$
AB = 24\text{ cm},\quad BC = 7\text{ cm}
$$

By Pythagoras theorem:

$$
AC^2 = AB^2 + BC^2
$$

$$
AC^2 = 24^2 + 7^2
$$

$$
AC^2 = 576 + 49 = 625
$$

$$
AC = 25\text{ cm}
$$

### Part I: `sin A`, `cos A`

For angle `A`:

- opposite side is `BC`;
- adjacent side is `AB`;
- hypotenuse is `AC`.

Therefore:

$$
\sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{7}{25}
$$

$$
\cos A = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{24}{25}
$$

### Part II: `sin C`, `cos C`

For angle `C`:

- opposite side is `AB`;
- adjacent side is `BC`;
- hypotenuse is `AC`.

Therefore:

$$
\sin C = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{24}{25}
$$

$$
\cos C = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{7}{25}
$$

## Question 2: Find `tan P - cot R`

The raw text refers to Fig. 8.13, but the extracted file does not include the figure. The result can still be understood from the right-triangle relationship.

In a right triangle `PQR` right-angled at `Q`, angles `P` and `R` are complementary.

For angle `P`:

$$
\tan P = \frac{QR}{PQ}
$$

For angle `R`:

$$
\cot R = \frac{QR}{PQ}
$$

So:

$$
\tan P = \cot R
$$

Therefore:

$$
\tan P - \cot R = 0
$$

## Question 3: If `sin A = 3/4`, Find `cos A` and `tan A`

Given:

$$
\sin A = \frac{3}{4}
$$

Take:

$$
\text{opposite} = 3k,\quad \text{hypotenuse} = 4k
$$

Find adjacent:

$$
\text{adjacent}^2 = (4k)^2 - (3k)^2
$$

$$
\text{adjacent}^2 = 16k^2 - 9k^2 = 7k^2
$$

$$
\text{adjacent} = \sqrt{7}k
$$

Therefore:

$$
\cos A = \frac{\sqrt{7}k}{4k} = \frac{\sqrt{7}}{4}
$$

and:

$$
\tan A = \frac{3k}{\sqrt{7}k} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{7}}
$$

Rationalised form:

$$
\tan A = \frac{3\sqrt{7}}{7}
$$

## Question 4: Given `15 cot A = 8`, Find `sin A` and `sec A`

Given:

$$
15\cot A = 8
$$

So:

$$
\cot A = \frac{8}{15}
$$

Since cotangent is adjacent over opposite, take:

$$
\text{adjacent} = 8k,\quad \text{opposite} = 15k
$$

Find hypotenuse:

$$
\text{hypotenuse}^2 = (8k)^2 + (15k)^2
$$

$$
\text{hypotenuse}^2 = 64k^2 + 225k^2 = 289k^2
$$

$$
\text{hypotenuse} = 17k
$$

Therefore:

$$
\sin A = \frac{15k}{17k} = \frac{15}{17}
$$

and:

$$
\sec A = \frac{17k}{8k} = \frac{17}{8}
$$

## Question 5: Given `sec theta = 13/12`

Given:

$$
\sec \theta = \frac{13}{12}
$$

Since:

$$
\sec \theta = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}
$$

take:

$$
\text{hypotenuse} = 13k,\quad \text{adjacent} = 12k
$$

Find opposite:

$$
\text{opposite}^2 = (13k)^2 - (12k)^2
$$

$$
\text{opposite}^2 = 169k^2 - 144k^2 = 25k^2
$$

$$
\text{opposite} = 5k
$$

Therefore:

$$
\sin \theta = \frac{5}{13}
$$

$$
\cos \theta = \frac{12}{13}
$$

$$
\tan \theta = \frac{5}{12}
$$

$$
\cosec \theta = \frac{13}{5}
$$

$$
\sec \theta = \frac{13}{12}
$$

$$
\cot \theta = \frac{12}{5}
$$

## Question 6: If `cos A = cos B`, Show `A = B`

Let `A` and `B` be acute angles.

In an acute-angle range, each value of cosine corresponds to only one angle.

So:

$$
\cos A = \cos B
$$

implies:

$$
A = B
$$

Geometric explanation:

If two right triangles have equal cosine values, then:

$$
\frac{\text{adjacent side}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

is the same in both triangles. The remaining side ratio is also fixed by Pythagoras theorem. Therefore, the triangles are similar and the corresponding acute angles are equal.

## Question 7: If `cot theta = 7/8`

Given:

$$
\cot \theta = \frac{7}{8}
$$

### Part I

Evaluate:

$$
\frac{(1+\sin\theta)(1-\sin\theta)}{(1+\cos\theta)(1-\cos\theta)}
$$

Use:

$$
(1+\sin\theta)(1-\sin\theta) = 1 - \sin^2\theta
$$

and:

$$
(1+\cos\theta)(1-\cos\theta) = 1 - \cos^2\theta
$$

So the expression becomes:

$$
\frac{1-\sin^2\theta}{1-\cos^2\theta}
$$

Using:

$$
1-\sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta
$$

and:

$$
1-\cos^2\theta = \sin^2\theta
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin^2\theta} = \cot^2\theta
$$

Now:

$$
\cot^2\theta = \left(\frac{7}{8}\right)^2
$$

Therefore:

$$
\frac{(1+\sin\theta)(1-\sin\theta)}{(1+\cos\theta)(1-\cos\theta)} = \frac{49}{64}
$$

### Part II

Evaluate:

$$
\cot^2\theta
$$

Since:

$$
\cot \theta = \frac{7}{8}
$$

we have:

$$
\cot^2\theta = \frac{49}{64}
$$

## Question 8: If `3 cot A = 4`, Check the Given Equality

Given:

$$
3\cot A = 4
$$

So:

$$
\cot A = \frac{4}{3}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\tan A = \frac{3}{4}
$$

Check the left-hand side:

$$
\frac{1-\tan A}{1+\tan A}
=
\frac{1-\frac{3}{4}}{1+\frac{3}{4}}
$$

$$
=
\frac{\frac{1}{4}}{\frac{7}{4}}
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{7}
$$

Now find the right-hand side.

Since:

$$
\cot A = \frac{4}{3}
$$

take:

$$
\text{adjacent} = 4k,\quad \text{opposite} = 3k
$$

Then:

$$
\text{hypotenuse} = 5k
$$

So:

$$
\cos A = \frac{4}{5},\quad \sin A = \frac{3}{5}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\cos^2 A - \sin^2 A
=
\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2
$$

$$
= \frac{16}{25} - \frac{9}{25}
$$

$$
= \frac{7}{25}
$$

Since:

$$
\frac{1}{7} \ne \frac{7}{25}
$$

the equality is not true.

## Question 10: Triangle `PQR`, Right-Angled at `Q`

Given:

$$
PR + QR = 25\text{ cm}
$$

and:

$$
PQ = 5\text{ cm}
$$

$$

## Question 11: True or False

### Part I

Statement: The value of `tan A` is always less than `1`.

This is false.

Example:

$$
\tan 60^\circ = \sqrt{3}
$$

and:

$$
\sqrt{3} > 1
$$

### Part II

Statement: `sec A = 12/5` for some value of angle `A`.

This is true.

Since:

$$
\sec A = \frac{12}{5}
$$

means:

$$
\cos A = \frac{5}{12}
$$

This is possible for an acute angle because cosine can have any value between `0` and `1`.

### Part III

Statement: `cos A` is the abbreviation used for cosecant of angle `A`.

This is false.

`cos A` means cosine of angle `A`.

Cosecant is written as:

$$
\cosec A
$$

### Part IV

Statement: `cot A` is the product of `cot` and `A`.

This is false.

`cot A` means cotangent of angle `A`, not multiplication.

### Part V

Statement:

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{4}{3}
$$

for some angle `theta`.

This is false in the Class 10 range.

For any angle in this chapter:

$$
0 \le \sin\theta \le 1
$$

But:

$$
\frac{4}{3} > 1
$$

So this is not possible.

## How to Remember the Sine Row

For angles:

$$
0^\circ,\quad 30^\circ,\quad 45^\circ,\quad 60^\circ,\quad 90^\circ
$$

write:

$$
\sin A = \frac{\sqrt{0}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{1}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{4}}{2}
$$

So:

$$
0,\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},1
$$

The cosine row is the sine row in reverse.

# Unit 8J: Deriving Special Values

## Values for `45^\circ`

In a right isosceles triangle:

$$
AB = BC = a
$$

By Pythagoras theorem:

$$
AC^2 = a^2 + a^2 = 2a^2
$$

So:

$$
AC = a\sqrt{2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\sin 45^\circ = \frac{a}{a\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
$$

$$
\cos 45^\circ = \frac{a}{a\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
$$

$$
\tan 45^\circ = \frac{a}{a} = 1
$$

## Values for `30^\circ` and `60^\circ`

Take an equilateral triangle of side `2a`.

Draw an altitude. It bisects the base, making two right triangles.

In one right triangle:

- hypotenuse is `2a`;
- shorter leg is `a`;
- longer leg is `a\sqrt{3}`.

For the `30^\circ` angle:

$$
\sin 30^\circ = \frac{a}{2a} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
\cos 30^\circ = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{2a} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

$$
\tan 30^\circ = \frac{a}{a\sqrt{3}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

For the `60^\circ` angle:

$$
\sin 60^\circ = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{2a} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

$$
\cos 60^\circ = \frac{a}{2a} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
\tan 60^\circ = \frac{a\sqrt{3}}{a} = \sqrt{3}
$$

## Values for `0^\circ` and `90^\circ`

By definition in this chapter:

$$
\sin 0^\circ = 0,\quad \cos 0^\circ = 1
$$

and:

$$
\sin 90^\circ = 1,\quad \cos 90^\circ = 0
$$

From reciprocal and quotient relations:

$$
\tan 0^\circ = 0
$$

$$
\sec 0^\circ = 1
$$

$$
\cosec 90^\circ = 1
$$

$$
\cot 90^\circ = 0
$$

But:

$$
\cosec 0^\circ
$$

is not defined because it requires division by:

$$
\sin 0^\circ = 0
$$

Similarly:

$$
\tan 90^\circ
$$

and:

$$
\sec 90^\circ
$$

are not defined because they require division by:

$$
\cos 90^\circ = 0
$$

# Unit 8K: Solved Exercise 8.2

## Question 1: Evaluate

### Part I

Evaluate:

$$
\sin 60^\circ\cos 30^\circ + \sin 30^\circ\cos 60^\circ
$$

Substitute values:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
+
\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
= \frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}
$$

$$
= 1
$$

### Part II

Evaluate:

$$
2\tan^2 45^\circ + \cos^2 30^\circ - \sin^2 60^\circ
$$

Substitute values:

$$
2(1)^2 + \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)^2
$$

$$
= 2 + \frac{3}{4} - \frac{3}{4}
$$

$$
= 2
$$

### Part III

The standard printed expression is:

$$
\frac{\cos 45^\circ}{\sec 30^\circ + \cosec 30^\circ}
$$

Substitute values:

$$
\frac{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}}{\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} + 2}
$$

Simplify:

$$
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{1}{2+\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}}
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2+2\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
= \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2\sqrt{2}(1+\sqrt{3})}
$$

# Unit 8L: Trigonometric Identities

An identity is an equation that is true for every allowed value of the variable.

For example:

$$
(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2
$$

is an algebraic identity.

Similarly, a trigonometric identity is true for every allowed value of the angle.

## Identity 1

In a right triangle:

$$
(\text{adjacent})^2 + (\text{opposite})^2 = (\text{hypotenuse})^2
$$

Divide by hypotenuse squared:

$$
\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right)^2
+
\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right)^2
= 1
$$

So:

$$
\cos^2 A + \sin^2 A = 1
$$

Usually written as:

$$
\sin^2 A + \cos^2 A = 1
$$

## Identity 2

Divide Pythagoras theorem by adjacent squared:

$$
1 + \left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\right)^2
=
\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}\right)^2
$$

So:

$$
1 + \tan^2 A = \sec^2 A
$$

Equivalent form:

$$
\sec^2 A - \tan^2 A = 1
$$

## Identity 3

Divide Pythagoras theorem by opposite squared:

$$
\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}}\right)^2 + 1
=
\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}}\right)^2
$$

So:

$$
\cot^2 A + 1 = \cosec^2 A
$$

Equivalent form:

$$
\cosec^2 A - \cot^2 A = 1
$$

# Unit 8M: How to Prove Trigonometric Identities

## Good Strategy

1. Start with the more complicated side.
2. Convert `tan`, `cot`, `sec`, and `cosec` into `sin` and `cos` if needed.
3. Use:

$$
\sin^2 A + \cos^2 A = 1
$$

4. Factor expressions like:

$$
1-\sin^2 A = \cos^2 A
$$

and:

$$
1-\cos^2 A = \sin^2 A
$$

5. Do not cross-multiply unless you are careful about undefined denominators.

## Worked Example: Express in Terms of `sin A`

Express `cos A`, `tan A`, and `sec A` in terms of `sin A`.

From:

$$
\sin^2 A + \cos^2 A = 1
$$

we get:

$$
\cos^2 A = 1-\sin^2 A
$$

Since `A` is acute, cosine is positive:

$$
\cos A = \sqrt{1-\sin^2 A}
$$

Now:

$$
\tan A = \frac{\sin A}{\cos A}
$$

So:

$$
\tan A = \frac{\sin A}{\sqrt{1-\sin^2 A}}
$$

Also:

$$
\sec A = \frac{1}{\cos A}
$$

So:

$$
\sec A = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\sin^2 A}}
$$

## Worked Example: Prove `sec A(1 - sin A)(sec A + tan A) = 1`

Start with LHS:

$$
\sec A(1-\sin A)(\sec A+\tan A)
$$

Write `sec A` and `tan A` in terms of sine and cosine:

$$
= \frac{1}{\cos A}(1-\sin A)\left(\frac{1}{\cos A}+\frac{\sin A}{\cos A}\right)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{\cos A}(1-\sin A)\left(\frac{1+\sin A}{\cos A}\right)
$$

$$
= \frac{(1-\sin A)(1+\sin A)}{\cos^2 A}
$$

Use:

$$
(1-\sin A)(1+\sin A) = 1-\sin^2 A
$$

So:

$$
= \frac{1-\sin^2 A}{\cos^2 A}
$$

Since:

$$
1-\sin^2 A = \cos^2 A
$$

we get:

$$
= \frac{\cos^2 A}{\cos^2 A}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\sec A(1-\sin A)(\sec A+\tan A) = 1
$$

So LHS becomes:

$$
\frac{\sin\theta(2\cos^2\theta-1)}{\cos\theta(2\cos^2\theta-1)}
$$

Cancel:

$$
= \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}
$$

Therefore:

$$
= \tan\theta
$$

Hence proved.

### Part VIII

Prove:

$$
(\sin A+\cosec A)^2+(\cos A+\sec A)^2
=
7+\tan^2 A+\cot^2 A
$$

Start with LHS:

$$
(\sin A+\cosec A)^2+(\cos A+\sec A)^2
$$

Expand:

$$
= \sin^2 A+2\sin A\cosec A+\cosec^2 A
+
\cos^2 A+2\cos A\sec A+\sec^2 A
$$

Use reciprocal products:

$$
\sin A\cosec A=1
$$

and:

$$
\cos A\sec A=1
$$

So:

$$
= \sin^2 A+\cos^2 A+2+2+\cosec^2 A+\sec^2 A
$$

Use:

$$
\sin^2 A+\cos^2 A=1
$$

Then:

$$
= 5+\cosec^2 A+\sec^2 A
$$

Now:

$$
\cosec^2 A = 1+\cot^2 A
$$

and:

$$
\sec^2 A = 1+\tan^2 A
$$

So:

$$
= 5+(1+\cot^2 A)+(1+\tan^2 A)
$$

Therefore:

$$
= 7+\tan^2 A+\cot^2 A
$$

Hence proved.

### Part IX

Prove:

$$
(\cosec A-\sin A)(\sec A-\cos A)
=
\frac{1}{\tan A+\cot A}
$$

Simplify LHS:

$$
\cosec A-\sin A
=
\frac{1}{\sin A}-\sin A
$$

$$
= \frac{1-\sin^2 A}{\sin A}
$$

$$
= \frac{\cos^2 A}{\sin A}
$$

Also:

$$
\sec A-\cos A
=
\frac{1}{\cos A}-\cos A
$$

$$
= \frac{1-\cos^2 A}{\cos A}
$$

$$
= \frac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A}
$$

Therefore:

$$
(\cosec A-\sin A)(\sec A-\cos A)
=
\frac{\cos^2 A}{\sin A}\cdot\frac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A}
$$

$$
= \sin A\cos A
$$

Now simplify RHS:

$$
\frac{1}{\tan A+\cot A}
$$

Write in sine and cosine:

$$
=
\frac{1}{\frac{\sin A}{\cos A}+\frac{\cos A}{\sin A}}
$$

$$
=
\frac{1}{\frac{\sin^2 A+\cos^2 A}{\sin A\cos A}}
$$

Since:

$$
\sin^2 A+\cos^2 A=1
$$

we get:

$$
=
\frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sin A\cos A}}
$$

$$
= \sin A\cos A
$$

Both sides are equal. Hence proved.

### Part X

Prove:

$$
\frac{1+\tan^2 A}{1+\cot^2 A}
=
\left(\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}\right)^2
=
\tan^2 A
$$

First prove:

$$
\frac{1+\tan^2 A}{1+\cot^2 A}
=
\tan^2 A
$$

Use:

$$
1+\tan^2 A=\sec^2 A
$$

and:

$$
1+\cot^2 A=\cosec^2 A
$$

So:

$$
=
\frac{\sec^2 A}{\cosec^2 A}
$$

Convert to sine and cosine:

$$
=
\frac{\frac{1}{\cos^2 A}}{\frac{1}{\sin^2 A}}
$$

$$
=
\frac{\sin^2 A}{\cos^2 A}
$$

$$
=
\tan^2 A
$$

Now prove:

$$
\left(\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}\right)^2
=
\tan^2 A
$$

Use:

$$
\cot A=\frac{1}{\tan A}
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}
=
\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\frac{1}{\tan A}}
$$

Simplify the denominator:

$$
1-\frac{1}{\tan A}
=
\frac{\tan A-1}{\tan A}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}
=
\frac{1-\tan A}{\frac{\tan A-1}{\tan A}}
$$

Since:

$$
\tan A-1=-(1-\tan A)
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}
=
-\tan A
$$

Therefore:

$$
\left(\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}\right)^2
=
(-\tan A)^2
$$

$$
=
\tan^2 A
$$

Hence:

$$
\frac{1+\tan^2 A}{1+\cot^2 A}
=
\left(\frac{1-\tan A}{1-\cot A}\right)^2
=
\tan^2 A
$$

Hence proved.

# Unit 8P: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Why it is wrong | Correct approach |
|---|---|---|
| Thinking opposite side is fixed | Opposite depends on selected angle | Identify the angle first |
| Calling hypotenuse adjacent | Hypotenuse is never adjacent in trig ratios | Hypotenuse is always opposite the right angle |
| Writing `sin A = adjacent/hypotenuse` | That is cosine | Sine is opposite over hypotenuse |
| Forgetting reciprocal ratios | Leads to wrong sec/cosec/cot values | Use reciprocal pairs |
| Using `sin^{-1} A` as cosec | Inverse sine is different | `cosec A = 1/sin A` |
| Dividing by zero | Some ratios are undefined | Check denominator before using a ratio |
| Assuming `sin(A+B)=sin A+sin B` | This is not a Class 10 identity | Use standard values or later identities |
| Dropping square roots | Changes values like `tan 30` | Keep radicals carefully |
| Confusing `tan^2 A` with `tan 2A` | These are different expressions | `tan^2 A = (tan A)^2` |

1. Trigonometry studies side-angle relationships in right triangles.

\sin A=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

$$
\cos A=\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
$$

$$

$$
