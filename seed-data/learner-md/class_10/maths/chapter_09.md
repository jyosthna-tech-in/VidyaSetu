## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Line of sight | The line from the observer's eye to the object being viewed |
| Horizontal line | A line parallel to the ground through the observer's eye |
| Angle of elevation | Angle made by the line of sight with the horizontal when the object is above the observer |
| Angle of depression | Angle made by the line of sight with the horizontal when the object is below the observer |
| Height | A vertical distance |
| Distance from foot | A horizontal distance from the base of an object |
| No slack string | The string is treated as a straight line segment |

# Unit 9A: Angle of Elevation and Depression

## Angle of Elevation

When an observer looks upward at an object, the line of sight is above the horizontal.

The angle between the horizontal and the upward line of sight is called the angle of elevation.

Typical examples:

- looking at the top of a tower;
- looking at a kite;
- looking at the top of a building;
- looking at a balloon in the sky.

## Angle of Depression

When an observer looks downward at an object, the line of sight is below the horizontal.

The angle between the horizontal and the downward line of sight is called the angle of depression.

Typical examples:

- looking at a ship from a lighthouse;
- looking at a car from the top of a tower;
- looking at the foot of a building from a higher building;
- looking at a river bank from a bridge.

## Important Parallel-Line Idea

If a person looks down at an object at an angle of depression, the same angle can often be used as the angle of elevation from the object to the observer.

This happens because the two horizontal lines are parallel, and the line of sight acts as a transversal.

So in diagrams:

$$
\text{angle of depression} = \text{corresponding angle of elevation}
$$

# Unit 9B: Choosing the Correct Ratio

In most height-and-distance questions, the right triangle has:

- vertical side: height;
- horizontal side: distance from foot;
- slant side: line of sight, rope, ladder, slide, or string.

## Ratio Choice Table

| Given and required | Best ratio |
|---|---|
| Height and horizontal distance | `tan` |
| Height and slant length | `sin` |
| Horizontal distance and slant length | `cos` |
| Need horizontal distance from height and angle | `tan` or `cot` |
| Need height from horizontal distance and angle | `tan` |

## Standard Values Used Often

$$
\sin 30^\circ=\frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
\cos 30^\circ=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
\sin 60^\circ=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

$$
\cos 60^\circ=\frac{1}{2}
$$

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\sqrt{3}
$$

$$
\tan 45^\circ=1
$$

# Unit 9C: General Solving Method

Use this method for nearly every problem in this chapter:

1. Draw a rough diagram.
2. Mark vertical heights and horizontal distances.
3. Mark the angle of elevation or depression.
4. Identify the right triangle.
5. Decide which side is opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse.
6. Select `sin`, `cos`, or `tan`.
7. Form an equation.
8. Solve for the unknown.
9. Add or subtract observer height, building height, or pedestal height if needed.

## Common Diagram Translations

| Situation | Mathematical translation |
|---|---|
| Top of tower seen from ground | Right triangle with tower as vertical side |
| Rope tied from top of pole to ground | Rope is hypotenuse |
| Ladder leaning against pole | Ladder is hypotenuse |
| Shadow length | Shadow is horizontal distance |
| Observer has height | Work from eye level, then add observer height |
| Statue on pedestal | Total height = pedestal + statue |
| Tower on building | Total height = building + tower |
| Two angles from same point | Usually two right triangles share the same horizontal distance |
| Object moves horizontally | Height stays same; horizontal distance changes |

# Unit 9D: Worked Examples Rebuilt

## Example 1: Tower Height

A point on the ground is `15 m` from the foot of a tower. The angle of elevation of the top is `60^\circ`. Find the height.

Let the tower height be `h`.

Use:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{h}{15}
$$

Since:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\sqrt{3}
$$

we get:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{h}{15}
$$

Therefore:

$$
h=15\sqrt{3}
$$

The tower height is:

$$
15\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Example 2: Ladder Against a Pole

An electrician must reach a point `3.7 m` above the ground on a pole. The ladder makes an angle of `60^\circ` with the ground.

Let ladder length be `L`.

Use:

$$
\sin 60^\circ=\frac{3.7}{L}
$$

Since:

$$
\sin 60^\circ=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=\frac{3.7}{L}
$$

So:

$$
L=\frac{7.4}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Using:

$$
\sqrt{3}=1.73
$$

we get:

$$
L\approx 4.28\text{ m}
$$

To find distance of ladder foot from pole, use:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{3.7}{d}
$$

So:

$$
d=\frac{3.7}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Using `\sqrt{3}=1.73`:

$$
d\approx 2.14\text{ m}
$$

## Example 3: Observer Height Correction

An observer is `1.5 m` tall and stands `28.5 m` from a chimney. The angle of elevation from the observer's eyes to the top is `45^\circ`.

Let the chimney height be `H`.

Height above eye level:

$$
H-1.5
$$

Use:

$$
\tan 45^\circ=\frac{H-1.5}{28.5}
$$

Since:

$$
\tan 45^\circ=1
$$

we get:

$$
1=\frac{H-1.5}{28.5}
$$

So:

$$
H-1.5=28.5
$$

Therefore:

$$
H=30
$$

The chimney height is:

$$
30\text{ m}
$$

# Unit 9E: Solved Exercise 9.1

## Question 1: Circus Artist on a Rope

A `20 m` long rope is tied from the top of a vertical pole to the ground. The rope makes an angle of `30^\circ` with the ground.

The rope is the hypotenuse.

Let the pole height be `h`.

Use:

$$
\sin 30^\circ=\frac{h}{20}
$$

Since:

$$
\sin 30^\circ=\frac{1}{2}
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{1}{2}=\frac{h}{20}
$$

Therefore:

$$
h=10
$$

Answer:

$$
10\text{ m}
$$

## Question 2: Broken Tree

A tree breaks in a storm. The broken part touches the ground and makes an angle of `30^\circ` with the ground. The distance from the foot of the tree to the point where the top touches the ground is `8 m`.

Let the remaining vertical stump height be `h`.

The ground distance is the adjacent side:

$$
8\text{ m}
$$

Use:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{8}
$$

Since:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{h}{8}
$$

So:

$$
h=\frac{8}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Now find the length of the broken part. Let it be `l`.

Use:

$$
\cos 30^\circ=\frac{8}{l}
$$

Since:

$$
\cos 30^\circ=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
$$

we get:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=\frac{8}{l}
$$

So:

$$
l=\frac{16}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Original height of tree:

$$
h+l=\frac{8}{\sqrt{3}}+\frac{16}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
=\frac{24}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
=8\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
8\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 3: Slides in a Park

For children below 5 years:

- height of slide top = `1.5 m`;
- angle with ground = `30^\circ`.

Let slide length be `L_1`.

Use:

$$
\sin 30^\circ=\frac{1.5}{L_1}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{2}=\frac{1.5}{L_1}
$$

Therefore:

$$
L_1=3
$$

For elder children:

- height of slide top = `3 m`;
- angle with ground = `60^\circ`.

Let slide length be `L_2`.

Use:

$$
\sin 60^\circ=\frac{3}{L_2}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=\frac{3}{L_2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
L_2=\frac{6}{\sqrt{3}}=2\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
3\text{ m}
$$

and:

$$
2\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 4: Tower from a Point 30 m Away

The point is `30 m` from the foot of the tower. The angle of elevation is `30^\circ`.

Let height be `h`.

Use:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{30}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{h}{30}
$$

Therefore:

$$
h=\frac{30}{\sqrt{3}}=10\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
10\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 5: Kite String

A kite is flying at a height of `60 m`. The string makes an angle of `60^\circ` with the ground. There is no slack in the string.

The string is the hypotenuse.

Let its length be `L`.

Use:

$$
\sin 60^\circ=\frac{60}{L}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=\frac{60}{L}
$$

Therefore:

$$
L=\frac{120}{\sqrt{3}}=40\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
40\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 6: Boy Walking Towards a Building

A boy is `1.5 m` tall and a building is `30 m` tall.

The vertical height from the boy's eyes to the top of the building is:

$$
30-1.5=28.5\text{ m}
$$

Let the initial distance from the building be `x`.

At angle `30^\circ`:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{28.5}{x}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{28.5}{x}
$$

Therefore:

$$
x=28.5\sqrt{3}
$$

Let the later distance from the building be `y`.

At angle `60^\circ`:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{28.5}{y}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{28.5}{y}
$$

Therefore:

$$
y=\frac{28.5}{\sqrt{3}}=9.5\sqrt{3}
$$

Distance walked:

$$
x-y=28.5\sqrt{3}-9.5\sqrt{3}
$$

$$
=19\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
19\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 7: Transmission Tower on a Building

A tower is fixed on top of a `20 m` high building. From a point on the ground:

- angle of elevation of bottom of tower = `45^\circ`;
- angle of elevation of top of tower = `60^\circ`.

Let horizontal distance from the building be `x`.

Since the bottom of the tower is the top of the building:

$$
\tan 45^\circ=\frac{20}{x}
$$

So:

$$
1=\frac{20}{x}
$$

Therefore:

$$
x=20
$$

Let tower height be `h`.

Total height to the top of tower:

$$
20+h
$$

Use:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{20+h}{20}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{20+h}{20}
$$

Therefore:

$$
20+h=20\sqrt{3}
$$

So:

$$
h=20(\sqrt{3}-1)
$$

Answer:

$$
20(\sqrt{3}-1)\text{ m}
$$

## Question 8: Statue on a Pedestal

A statue of height `1.6 m` stands on a pedestal.

From a point on the ground:

- angle of elevation of top of statue = `60^\circ`;
- angle of elevation of top of pedestal = `45^\circ`.

Let pedestal height be `h` and distance from the point be `x`.

For top of pedestal:

$$
\tan 45^\circ=\frac{h}{x}
$$

So:

$$
1=\frac{h}{x}
$$

Therefore:

$$
x=h
$$

For top of statue:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{h+1.6}{x}
$$

Use `x=h`:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{h+1.6}{h}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}h=h+1.6
$$

$$
h(\sqrt{3}-1)=1.6
$$

Therefore:

$$
h=\frac{1.6}{\sqrt{3}-1}
$$

Rationalise:

$$
h=\frac{1.6(\sqrt{3}+1)}{3-1}
$$

$$
h=0.8(\sqrt{3}+1)
$$

Answer:

$$
0.8(\sqrt{3}+1)\text{ m}
$$

## Question 9: Building and Tower

The angle of elevation of the top of a building from the foot of a tower is `30^\circ`. The angle of elevation of the top of the tower from the foot of the building is `60^\circ`. The tower is `50 m` high.

Let:

- distance between building and tower = `d`;
- building height = `h`.

From foot of building to top of tower:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{50}{d}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{50}{d}
$$

Therefore:

$$
d=\frac{50}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

From foot of tower to top of building:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{d}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{h}{d}
$$

Therefore:

$$
h=\frac{d}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Substitute:

$$
h=\frac{50}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
h=\frac{50}{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
\frac{50}{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 10: Two Equal Poles Across a Road

Two equal poles stand opposite each other on either side of an `80 m` wide road. From a point between them, the angles of elevation are `60^\circ` and `30^\circ`.

Let:

- distance from the point to the nearer pole = `x`;
- distance to the other pole = `80-x`;
- height of each pole = `h`.

For the pole with angle `60^\circ`:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{h}{x}
$$

So:

$$
h=\sqrt{3}x
$$

For the pole with angle `30^\circ`:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{80-x}
$$

So:

$$
h=\frac{80-x}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Equate both expressions for `h`:

$$
\sqrt{3}x=\frac{80-x}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Multiply by `\sqrt{3}`:

$$
3x=80-x
$$

$$
4x=80
$$

$$
x=20
$$

Then:

$$
80-x=60
$$

Height:

$$
h=\sqrt{3}\cdot 20=20\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

The point is `20 m` from the pole whose angle of elevation is `60^\circ`, and `60 m` from the pole whose angle of elevation is `30^\circ`.

Each pole has height:

$$
20\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 11: TV Tower on a Canal Bank

A TV tower stands on one bank of a canal.

From the opposite bank, the angle of elevation is `60^\circ`.

From another point `20 m` farther away on the same line, the angle of elevation is `30^\circ`.

Let:

- width of canal = `x`;
- height of tower = `h`.

From the nearer point:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{h}{x}
$$

So:

$$
h=x\sqrt{3}
$$

From the farther point, distance from tower is:

$$
x+20
$$

Use:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{x+20}
$$

So:

$$
h=\frac{x+20}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Equate:

$$
x\sqrt{3}=\frac{x+20}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Multiply by `\sqrt{3}`:

$$
3x=x+20
$$

$$
2x=20
$$

$$
x=10
$$

Then:

$$
h=10\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

Width of canal:

$$
10\text{ m}
$$

Height of tower:

$$
10\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 13: Two Ships from a Lighthouse

A lighthouse is `75 m` high. From its top, the angles of depression of two ships on the same side are `30^\circ` and `45^\circ`. One ship is exactly behind the other.

The nearer ship has the larger angle of depression, `45^\circ`.

Let distance of nearer ship from lighthouse base be `x`.

Use:

$$
\tan 45^\circ=\frac{75}{x}
$$

So:

$$
x=75
$$

Let distance of farther ship from lighthouse base be `y`.

Use:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{75}{y}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{75}{y}
$$

Therefore:

$$
y=75\sqrt{3}
$$

Distance between ships:

$$
y-x=75\sqrt{3}-75

$$
=75(\sqrt{3}-1)

## Question 14: Balloon Moving Horizontally

A girl is `1.2 m` tall. A balloon moves horizontally at a height of `88.2 m` from the ground.

Height of balloon above the girl's eyes:

$$
88.2-1.2=87\text{ m}
$$

Let the first horizontal distance be `x`, when angle is `60^\circ`.

Use:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{87}{x}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{87}{x}
$$

Therefore:

$$
x=\frac{87}{\sqrt{3}}=29\sqrt{3}
$$

Let the second horizontal distance be `y`, when angle is `30^\circ`.

Use:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{87}{y}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{87}{y}
$$

Therefore:

$$
y=87\sqrt{3}
$$

Distance travelled by the balloon:

$$
y-x=87\sqrt{3}-29\sqrt{3}
$$

$$
=58\sqrt{3}
$$

Answer:

$$
58\sqrt{3}\text{ m}
$$

## Question 15: Car Approaching a Tower

A man at the top of a tower observes a car on a straight highway.

First angle of depression:

$$
30^\circ
$$

After `6` seconds, angle of depression:

$$
60^\circ
$$

Let tower height be `h`.

Let initial distance of car from tower foot be `x`.

At `30^\circ`:

$$
\tan 30^\circ=\frac{h}{x}
$$

So:

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\frac{h}{x}
$$

Therefore:

$$
x=h\sqrt{3}
$$

Let later distance from tower foot be `y`.

At `60^\circ`:

$$
\tan 60^\circ=\frac{h}{y}
$$

So:

$$
\sqrt{3}=\frac{h}{y}
$$

Therefore:

$$
y=\frac{h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Distance travelled in 6 seconds:

$$
x-y=h\sqrt{3}-\frac{h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
=\frac{3h-h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

$$
=\frac{2h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

The remaining distance is:

$$
y=\frac{h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

Since speed is uniform, time is proportional to distance.

If:

$$
\frac{2h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

is covered in `6` seconds, then:

$$
\frac{h}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

will be covered in:

$$
3\text{ seconds}
$$

Answer:

$$
3\text{ seconds}
$$

# Unit 9F: Fast Revision Table

| Problem type | Typical equation |
|---|---|
| Tower height from distance and elevation | `tan angle = height / distance` |
| Rope or ladder length | `sin angle = height / slant length` |
| Shadow length | `tan solar altitude = height / shadow` |
| Observer height included | Use height difference from eye level |
| Object on pedestal/building | Total height = base height + object height |
| Depression from high point | Treat as elevation from lower point |
| Moving object at same height | Height fixed, horizontal distance changes |
| Equal poles | Same height gives two equations |

# Unit 9G: Common Mistakes and Corrections

| Mistake | Why it is wrong | Correct habit |
|---|---|---|
| Using full building height when observer has height | The angle starts from eye level | Use height difference |
| Confusing rope with height | Rope is slanted | Rope is hypotenuse |
| Treating angle of depression as a new formula | It is handled using parallel lines | Convert to angle of elevation |
| Forgetting to add pedestal or observer height | Triangle only gives part of height | Add or subtract outside-triangle heights |
| Using `sin` when height and ground distance are known | `sin` needs hypotenuse | Use `tan` |
| Assuming farther object has larger angle | Farther object has smaller angle for same height | Compare with geometry |
| Rounding too early | Can change final exact answer | Keep radicals until the end |

# Final One-Page Summary

1. Line of sight is the line from the observer to the object.
2. Angle of elevation is used when looking upward.
3. Angle of depression is used when looking downward.
4. In depression problems, use parallel lines to convert the angle into an elevation angle inside the right triangle.
5. Most questions use:

$$
\tan\theta=\frac{\text{height}}{\text{horizontal distance}}
$$

6. If a rope, string, ladder, or slide is involved, it is usually the hypotenuse.
7. If the observer has height, subtract eye height from object height before applying trigonometry.
8. If an object is placed on another object, add the lower height after finding the required part.
9. Keep exact answers in terms of `\sqrt{3}` unless the question asks for approximation.
