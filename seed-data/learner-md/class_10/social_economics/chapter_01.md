## Big Ideas

Development is a question about life, not only money. People want income, but they also want respect, safety, education, health, equality, and freedom.

Different people can have different ideas of development because their life situations are different. A landless labourer, a farmer, an urban unemployed youth, a rich student, and an adivasi displaced by a dam do not face the same problems.

Average income is useful for comparing countries, but it can hide inequality. A country where one person is extremely rich and others are poor may have the same average as a country where income is more equally distributed.

Human development requires public facilities. A person cannot individually buy clean air, disease control, universal schooling, or community-level safety.

Development must be sustainable. If present development destroys groundwater, forests, soil, or crude oil reserves, it may create comfort today but crisis tomorrow.

# Unit 1A: Meaning of Development

## Core Idea

Development means improvement in the way people live. It includes better income, but also better opportunities, better social conditions, and better future possibilities.

Development asks questions such as:

- What kind of life do people want?
- Can everyone have a decent standard of living?
- Can there be more equality?
- Can people live with dignity and freedom?
- Can progress happen without damaging nature?

## Why Development Is Complex

Development is not a single fixed target. A person may call a change "development" if it improves their life, but another person may suffer because of the same change.

Example:

| Situation | Why It Looks Like Development | Why It May Create Harm |
|---|---|---|
| Building a large dam | More electricity and irrigation for some regions. | Displacement of tribals, loss of forests, submergence of land. |
| More industries | More production, jobs, and income. | Pollution, unsafe working conditions, land loss, or resource overuse. |
| Large roads through forests | Faster transport and trade. | Loss of biodiversity and livelihood for forest communities. |

## Learner Insight

In economics, development is not judged only by asking, "Did income rise?" A better question is:

Did people's lives become more secure, equal, healthy, educated, free, and sustainable?

# Unit 1B: Different People, Different Goals

## Main Point

People have different developmental goals because their needs, problems, social positions, and opportunities are different.

## Examples of Developmental Goals

| Person / Group | Likely Developmental Goals |
|---|---|
| **Landless rural labourer** | More days of work, better wages, no social discrimination, school for children, leadership rights in village. |
| **Prosperous farmer in Punjab** | High crop prices, cheap labour, better irrigation, modern machines, good income, children settled well. |
| **Rain-dependent farmer** | Reliable rainfall, irrigation, crop insurance, fair price, protection from drought. |
| **Rural woman from land-owning family** | Freedom equal to her brother, education, respect, decision-making power, shared household work. |
| **Urban unemployed youth** | A decent job, skill training, stable income, dignity, affordable housing. |
| **Rich urban boy** | High-quality education, career opportunity, lifestyle, personal freedom. |
| **Rich urban girl** | Same opportunities as boys, safety, freedom, higher studies, equality at home and outside. |
| **Adivasi from Narmada valley** | Protection of land, forest, culture, livelihood, and fair rehabilitation if displaced. |

## Two Clear Conclusions

| Conclusion | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Different people have different goals** | People do not live in the same conditions, so they do not want exactly the same things. |
| **Goals may conflict** | What helps one group may harm another group. |

## Conflicting Goals

| Conflict | Why It Happens |
|---|---|
| Industrialists want large dams; displaced people oppose them. | Dams may help industry and electricity supply but submerge land and homes. |
| A girl wants equal freedom; her brother may resist sharing household work. | Equality changes old power relations inside the family. |
| Urban expansion needs land; farmers may lose fields. | City development may reduce rural livelihood security. |
| A factory wants cheaper labour; workers want secure wages and rights. | Profit goals and labour dignity may clash. |

## Exam Language

Different people have different developmental goals because development depends on their life situation. A landless labourer may want more work and wages, while a prosperous farmer may want higher crop prices. Sometimes goals conflict; a dam may give electricity to industries but displace local communities.

# Unit 1C: Income and Other Goals

## Why Income Matters

Income matters because it helps people buy goods and services:

- food;
- clothes;
- shelter;
- books;
- transport;
- medicines;
- tools;
- electricity;
- communication services.

Without income, many basic needs remain unmet.

## Why Income Is Not Enough

Money cannot buy everything needed for a good life.

| Non-Income Goal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Equal treatment** | People want dignity, not discrimination. |
| **Freedom** | People want to choose education, work, movement, and life decisions. |
| **Security** | People need stable work, safe homes, and protection from violence. |
| **Respect** | Social dignity is essential for well-being. |
| **Friendship and family time** | Emotional life cannot be reduced to money. |
| **Clean environment** | Clean air and water depend on collective action, not only personal income. |
| **Health and education** | These require public systems and social support. |

## Job Example

Suppose a person has two job options:

| Job | Income | Other Conditions | Development Judgment |
|---|---:|---|---|
| **Job A** | High salary | No job security, unsafe workplace, no family time. | Income is high, but quality of life may be poor. |
| **Job B** | Lower salary | Regular employment, safe atmosphere, learning opportunity. | Income is lower, but security and dignity may be better. |

## Women's Work Example

Paid work can increase a woman's dignity and bargaining power inside the household. But dignity also requires respect, safety, sharing of housework, and acceptance of women working outside.

So development for women is not only "more income". It also includes equality, safety, respect, and freedom.

# Unit 1D: National Development

## Core Idea

National development means thinking about what is desirable and fair for the whole country.

It asks:

- Would this policy benefit many people or only a small group?
- Would it reduce inequality or increase it?
- Would it protect the poor and vulnerable?
- Would it damage the environment?
- Would it be fair to future generations?

## Why National Development Is Difficult

Different groups imagine national development differently.

| Group | Possible View of National Development |
|---|---|
| Industrialists | More factories, power, roads, exports, and investment. |
| Farmers | Irrigation, crop prices, credit, insurance, and stable markets. |
| Workers | Secure employment, fair wages, social security, and safe conditions. |
| Students | Better schools, colleges, skill training, and jobs. |
| Adivasis | Protection of land, forests, culture, and livelihood. |
| Environmentalists | Conservation, pollution control, and sustainable resource use. |

## Fair Development

A good national development path should:

1. improve the lives of a large number of people;
2. reduce inequality;
3. protect rights and dignity;
4. avoid destroying livelihoods;
5. use resources responsibly;
6. include democratic discussion.

# Unit 1E: Comparing Countries and States

## Why Comparison Is Needed

To understand whether one country or state is more developed than another, we need indicators.

An indicator is a measurable sign of development.

Examples:

- per capita income;
- literacy rate;
- infant mortality rate;
- school attendance;
- life expectancy;
- health facilities;
- access to clean water;
- environmental quality.

## Income as a Criterion

Income is often used because it is measurable and affects many life conditions.

Countries with higher income can usually provide more goods and services. But income alone cannot show whether everyone benefits.

## Total Income

Total income means the income of all residents of a country or state added together.

Problem:

Total income is not good for comparing countries with different populations.

Example:

| Country | Total Income | Population | Why Total Income Misleads |
---|---:|---:|---|
| Country X | Very high | Very large | Average person may not be rich. |
| Country Y | Lower | Very small | Average person may be richer. |

## Per Capita Income

Per capita income gives average income per person.

```text
Per capita income = Total income of country / Total population of country
```

It is also called average income.

## World Bank Criterion

The World Bank uses per capita income to classify countries into income groups.

In the NCERT data:

| Category | Per Capita Income Criterion |
|---|---|
| **Rich countries** | US$ 12,236 per annum and above in 2016. |
| **Low-income countries** | US$ 1,005 per annum or less in 2016. |
| **India's position in 2016** | Low middle income, with per capita income around US$ 1,840 per annum. |

## Limitation

Per capita income does not show:

- whether income is equally distributed;
- whether people are healthy;
- whether children attend school;
- whether women are safe;
- whether the environment is protected;
- whether public facilities are available.

# Unit 1F: Average Income and Inequality

## Average Income Formula

```text
Average income = Total income / Total population
```

## Example: Two Countries

Assume two countries have five citizens each.

| Citizen | Country A Income | Country B Income |
|---|---:|---:|
| I | 9,500 | 500 |
| II | 10,500 | 500 |
| III | 9,800 | 500 |
| IV | 10,000 | 500 |
| V | 10,200 | 48,000 |
| **Total** | **50,000** | **50,000** |
| **Average** | **10,000** | **10,000** |

Both countries have the same average income, but they are not equally developed in a real sense.

## What the Example Teaches

| Country | Income Distribution | Development Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Country A** | Income is fairly equal. | Most citizens have a decent income. |
| **Country B** | One person is extremely rich; four are very poor. | Average hides poverty and inequality. |

## Key Lesson

Averages are useful for comparison, but they hide disparities.

For development, we must ask:

- Who has the income?
- How is income distributed?
- Are the poor improving?
- Are basic services reaching everyone?

# Unit 1G: Income and Other Criteria

## Haryana, Kerala, and Bihar Example

NCERT compares Haryana, Kerala, and Bihar using per capita income and social indicators.

### Per Capita Income

| State | Per Capita Income, 2015-16 |
|---|---:|
| Haryana | Rs 1,62,034 |
| Kerala | Rs 1,55,516 |
| Bihar | Rs 34,168 |

If only income is used, Haryana appears ahead of Kerala.

But development cannot be judged only by income.

### Social Indicators

| State | Infant Mortality Rate, 2015-16 | Literacy Rate | Net Attendance Ratio, age 14-15 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Haryana | 36 | 82 | 61 |
| Kerala | 12 | 94 | 83 |
| Bihar | 42 | 62 | 43 |

## What the Data Shows

Kerala has slightly lower per capita income than Haryana, but it performs much better in:

- infant survival;
- literacy;
- school attendance;
- health and education outcomes.

## Why Kerala Performs Better

Kerala has better provision of basic health and educational facilities. This shows that money in people's pockets is not enough. Public systems matter.

## Important Exam Conclusion

Per capita income is useful, but it is incomplete. Kerala shows that a state with lower per capita income can have better human development if it provides better health and education facilities.

# Unit 1H: Public Facilities

## Meaning

Public facilities are services that people use collectively and that are often provided or supported by government.

Examples:

- schools;
- hospitals;
- clean drinking water;
- roads;
- electricity;
- public transport;
- sanitation;
- ration shops;
- vaccination;
- pollution control;
- public parks;
- law and order.

## Why Public Facilities Matter

Many important goods cannot be purchased individually in an efficient or fair way.

| Need | Why Individual Purchase Is Not Enough |
|---|---|
| **Pollution-free environment** | One rich person cannot buy clean air if the whole area is polluted. |
| **Disease control** | Infectious diseases require community-level prevention. |
| **Schooling** | A child can study properly only when schools, teachers, roads, and social support exist. |
| **Security** | Collective security is cheaper and more effective than every house hiring private guards. |
| **Food security** | A working Public Distribution System can help many families access food grains. |

## Public Distribution System Example

If ration shops function well, poor households can access food grains at affordable prices. This improves nutrition and reduces vulnerability.

NCERT compares Tamil Nadu and West Bengal:

| State | Rural People Using Ration Shops |
|---|---:|
| Tamil Nadu | 90% |
| West Bengal | 35% |

People are likely to be better off where the ration system works more effectively, because more households can access subsidised food.

## Learner Insight

Public facilities are not charity. They are part of development because they expand people's real ability to live well.

# Unit 1I: Human Development

## Why Human Development Is Needed

Once we understand that income alone is incomplete, we need broader indicators.

Human development focuses on:

- people's health;
- people's education;
- people's income;
- quality of life.

## UNDP's Human Development Report

The Human Development Report published by UNDP compares countries using indicators such as:

| Indicator | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| **Per capita income / GNI per capita** | Average command over goods and services. |
| **Life expectancy at birth** | Health and survival conditions. |
| **Mean years of schooling** | Educational achievement of adults. |
| **HDI rank** | Combined position based on health, education, and income. |

## India and Neighbouring Countries, 2015 Data

| Country | GNI per capita, 2015 PPP $ | Life Expectancy | Mean Years of Schooling | HDI Rank, 2016 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Sri Lanka | 10,789 | 75 | 10.9 | 73 |
| India | 5,663 | 68.3 | 6.3 | 131 |
| Myanmar | 4,943 | 66.1 | 4.7 | 145 |
| Pakistan | 5,031 | 66.4 | 5.1 | 147 |
| Nepal | 2,377 | 70 | 4.1 | 145 |
| Bangladesh | 3,341 | 72 | 5.2 | 139 |

## How to Read the Table

Sri Lanka performs better than India in life expectancy, schooling, income, and HDI rank.

Bangladesh and Nepal have lower per capita income than India, but their life expectancy is higher in the given data.

This proves that income is important but not the only measure of development.

## HDI Exam Point

The World Bank mainly uses income. UNDP uses income plus health and education. Therefore, UNDP gives a broader picture of development.

# Unit 1J: Sustainability of Development

## Meaning

Sustainability means development should continue without destroying the resources and environment needed by future generations.

A famous idea from the chapter:

```text
We have not inherited the world from our forefathers; we have borrowed it from our children.
```

This means present generations must use resources responsibly.

## Renewable Resources

Renewable resources are replenished by nature, but they can still be overused.

Examples:

- groundwater;
- forests;
- fisheries;
- soil fertility;
- crops;
- solar and wind energy.

## Groundwater Example

Groundwater is renewable because rainfall can recharge it. But if people pump out more groundwater than rain can replenish, groundwater levels fall.

NCERT notes that many districts in India have reported serious decline in groundwater levels. Overuse is common in agriculturally prosperous regions, hard rock plateau areas, coastal areas, and rapidly growing urban settlements.

## Why Groundwater Is Overused

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Irrigation demand** | Farmers pump groundwater for water-intensive crops. |
| **Urban growth** | Cities need water for households, construction, and services. |
| **Industry** | Factories use groundwater for production and processing. |
| **Weak regulation** | Many users extract water privately without enough community control. |
| **Uneven rainfall** | Areas with uncertain rainfall depend more on groundwater. |

## Non-Renewable Resources

Non-renewable resources exist in fixed stock and get exhausted after use.

Examples:

- crude oil;
- coal;
- natural gas;
- minerals.

## Crude Oil Example

| Region / Country | Reserves, 2016 | Years Reserves Would Last |
|---|---:|---:|
| Middle East | 803 thousand million barrels | 69.9 years |
| United States of America | 48 thousand million barrels | 10.6 years |
| World | 1691.5 thousand million barrels | 50.6 years |

This shows that if extraction continues at the same rate, crude oil reserves will not last forever.

## Why Sustainability Is Important

Sustainability is important because:

1. resources are limited;
2. environmental damage affects everyone;
3. future generations have equal right to resources;
4. development can become destructive if it causes pollution, water crisis, or climate stress;
5. poor people are often harmed first by environmental degradation.

## Sustainable Development Examples

| Problem | Sustainable Alternative |
|---|---|
| Overuse of groundwater | Rainwater harvesting, drip irrigation, crop planning, groundwater regulation. |
| Fossil fuel dependence | Solar, wind, public transport, energy efficiency. |
| Deforestation | Afforestation, community forest management, controlled use. |
| Pollution | Cleaner technology, strict regulation, waste treatment. |
| Unequal development | Public facilities, rights protection, democratic planning. |

## Solved Exercise Guide

### 1. Development of a country can generally be determined by which indicators?

Correct option: **(iv) all the above**.

Development can be determined by per capita income, literacy level, and health status. Income alone is incomplete, so education and health must also be considered.

### 2. Which neighbouring country performs better than India in human development?

Correct option: **(ii) Sri Lanka**.

In the given NCERT data, Sri Lanka has a better HDI rank, higher life expectancy, and higher mean years of schooling than India.

### 3. Average Income Problem

Four families have average per capita income of Rs 5000.

```text
Total income = 5000 x 4 = Rs 20,000
Known income = 4000 + 7000 + 3000 = Rs 14,000
Fourth family income = 20,000 - 14,000 = Rs 6,000
```

Correct option: **(iv) Rs 6000**.

### 4. World Bank criterion and limitation

The World Bank classifies countries mainly on the basis of **per capita income**.

Limitations:

- it ignores income distribution;
- it does not show health and education;
- it ignores public facilities;
- it does not show environmental sustainability;
- it can hide poverty and inequality behind an average.

### 5. Difference between World Bank and UNDP criteria

| World Bank | UNDP |
|---|---|
| Mainly uses per capita income. | Uses income, health, and education. |
| Gives an income-based classification. | Gives a broader human development comparison. |
| Does not directly include literacy, schooling, or life expectancy. | Includes life expectancy and schooling indicators. |

### 6. Why do we use averages? What are their limitations?

We use averages because countries and states have different populations. Average income helps compare what one person earns on average.

Limitation:

Averages hide disparities. Country A and Country B may have the same average income, but Country B may have one very rich person and many poor people.

### 7. Is per capita income useless because Kerala performs better than Haryana in human development?

No. Per capita income is useful, but it is not sufficient.

Kerala has lower per capita income than Haryana but better health and education indicators. This shows that income should be used along with other indicators, not rejected completely.

### 8. Present and future energy sources

Present sources:

- coal;
- petroleum;
- natural gas;
- firewood;
- hydroelectricity;
- nuclear energy;
- solar energy;
- wind energy.

Future possibilities:

- more solar power;
- wind energy;
- green hydrogen;
- electric public transport;
- biogas;
- improved energy storage;
- energy-efficient appliances.

### 9. Why is sustainability important for development?

Sustainability is important because present development may overuse resources and damage the environment. If groundwater, forests, soil, and fossil fuels are exhausted, future generations will suffer. True development must improve present lives without destroying future possibilities.

### 10. Relevance of the statement about need and greed

The earth has enough resources for human needs, but not for unlimited greed. This means fair and careful use of resources can support development, but wasteful consumption and overuse create scarcity, pollution, and inequality.

### 11. Examples of environmental degradation

Examples:

- polluted rivers;
- falling groundwater levels;
- air pollution in cities;
- deforestation;
- soil erosion;
- plastic waste;
- untreated industrial waste;
- loss of biodiversity;
- noise pollution;
- shrinking lakes and wetlands.

### 12. Top and bottom countries in Table 1.6

Using the given data:

| Indicator | Top | Bottom |
|---|---|---|
| GNI per capita | Sri Lanka | Nepal |
| Life expectancy | Sri Lanka | Myanmar |
| Mean years of schooling | Sri Lanka | Nepal |
| HDI rank | Sri Lanka | Pakistan |

Note: A lower HDI rank number means better position.

### 13. BMI and undernutrition table

Kerala has lower percentage of adults with below-normal BMI than Madhya Pradesh. This suggests better nutrition and health conditions in Kerala.

Even when enough food exists in the country, many people may remain undernourished because of poverty, unequal access, low income, poor public distribution, lack of awareness, disease, and social inequality.
