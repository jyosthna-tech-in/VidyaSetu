## Big Ideas

Money makes exchange easier because people do not need to find someone who wants exactly what they sell and sells exactly what they want.

Modern money is linked to the banking system. Currency and demand deposits together work as money because both can be used for payments.

Banks do not keep all deposits idle. They keep a small share as cash and lend the rest to borrowers. This connects people with surplus money to people who need money.

Credit is not automatically good or bad. It depends on the purpose of borrowing, risk, interest rate, collateral, repayment terms, and support in case of loss.

Formal credit is usually cheaper and safer than informal credit, but poor borrowers often cannot access it because they lack collateral and documents.

Self-help groups are important because they help poor households, especially women, overcome the collateral problem and borrow at reasonable rates.

# Unit 3A: Money as a Medium of Exchange

## Barter System

In a barter system, goods are exchanged directly for other goods.

Example:

```text
Shoes are exchanged directly for wheat.
```

The problem is that the shoe seller must find a wheat farmer who wants shoes and is also willing to give wheat in exchange.

## Double Coincidence of Wants

Double coincidence of wants means:

```text
What one person wants to sell must be exactly what the other person wants to buy, and vice versa.
```

This is difficult and time-consuming.

## How Money Solves the Problem

Money breaks barter into two simple steps:

1. Shoe manufacturer sells shoes for money.
2. Shoe manufacturer uses money to buy wheat.

Now the shoe manufacturer does not need to find a wheat farmer who wants shoes.

## Why Money Is a Medium of Exchange

Money acts as an intermediate in the exchange process.

| Without Money | With Money |
|---|---|
| Goods must be exchanged directly. | Goods can be sold for money. |
| Double coincidence of wants is required. | Double coincidence is not required. |
| Exchange is difficult. | Exchange becomes easier. |
| Market is limited. | Market expands. |

## Everyday Examples

| Situation | Role of Money |
|---|---|
| Buying vegetables | Buyer gives money instead of another good. |
| Paying school fees | Service is exchanged for money. |
| Paying wages | Labour is exchanged for money. |
| Buying a bus ticket | Transport service is paid through money. |
| Online payment | Bank deposit is transferred as money. |

# Unit 3B: Modern Forms of Money

## Earlier Forms of Money

Before modern currency, many things were used as money:

- grains;
- cattle;
- gold coins;
- silver coins;
- copper coins.

These had some value or use of their own.

## Modern Currency

Modern currency includes:

- paper notes;
- coins.

Modern currency may not have value because of its material. A paper note is not valuable as paper. It is valuable because the government authorises it.

## Why Rupee Is Accepted

The rupee is accepted because:

1. it is authorised by the government;
2. RBI issues currency notes on behalf of the central government;
3. Indian law legalises rupee as a medium of payment;
4. no individual in India can legally refuse payment made in rupees.

## Demand Deposits

People keep money in bank accounts. These deposits can be withdrawn whenever needed.

They are called demand deposits because they are payable on demand.

## Why Demand Deposits Are Money

Demand deposits are considered money because:

- they can be withdrawn as cash;
- they can be used to make cheque payments;
- they can be transferred digitally;
- they are widely accepted for settling payments.

## Cheque Payments

A cheque is an instruction to the bank.

Example:

M. Salim has to pay his leather supplier. He writes a cheque. The supplier deposits it in his own bank account. The bank transfers money from Salim's account to the supplier's account.

The transaction is completed without cash.

## Currency and Deposits Together

Modern money includes:

| Form | Why It Counts as Money |
|---|---|
| Currency | Legal tender used directly for payment. |
| Demand deposits | Can be withdrawn or transferred for payment. |

# Unit 3C: Banks and Deposits

## Why People Deposit Money

People do not need all their money in cash all the time. They deposit extra money in banks.

Advantages:

- money remains safe;
- bank pays interest;
- money can be withdrawn when needed;
- payments can be made by cheque or digital transfer;
- deposits help banks provide loans.

## Demand Deposit Flow

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| 1 | Person opens bank account. |
| 2 | Person deposits money. |
| 3 | Bank records deposit. |
| 4 | Person can withdraw money on demand. |
| 5 | Person can make payments by cheque, card, or digital transfer. |

## What If All Depositors Withdraw Together?

Banks keep only a portion of deposits as cash because normally only some depositors withdraw money on a given day.

If all depositors withdraw at the same time, banks would face a crisis because most deposits are already lent out as loans.

This is why trust and regulation are essential in banking.

# Unit 3D: Loan Activities of Banks

## How Banks Use Deposits

Banks keep a small share of deposits as cash and lend the major portion.

NCERT notes that banks in India keep about 15 percent of deposits as cash for daily withdrawal needs.

## Bank as Mediator

Banks mediate between:

- depositors who have surplus money;
- borrowers who need money.

## Bank Income

Banks pay interest to depositors and charge interest from borrowers.

```text
Bank income = Interest charged on loans - Interest paid on deposits
```

This difference is the main source of income for banks.

## Flow of Banking Activity

| Depositors | Bank | Borrowers |
|---|---|---|
| Deposit surplus money. | Keeps some cash reserve. | Take loans for production, business, housing, education, farming, etc. |
| Earn interest. | Lends major share of deposits. | Repay loan with interest. |
| Withdraw when needed. | Earns from interest difference. | Use credit for economic activity. |

# Unit 3E: Two Different Credit Situations

## What Is Credit?

Credit is a loan agreement where the lender gives money, goods, or services now and the borrower promises future repayment.

Credit can be helpful or harmful.

## Salim's Credit Situation

Salim, a shoe manufacturer, receives a large order for 3000 pairs of shoes.

He needs credit for:

- hiring extra workers;
- buying leather;
- meeting production expenses;
- completing order on time.

He receives:

- leather on credit from supplier;
- advance money from trader.

Outcome:

He completes production, earns profit, and repays the loan.

## Why Credit Helped Salim

| Factor | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Purpose | Productive use: working capital for production. |
| Demand | He already had an order. |
| Repayment source | Sale of shoes. |
| Outcome | Higher income and business growth. |

## Swapna's Credit Situation

Swapna is a small farmer growing groundnut. She borrows from a moneylender for cultivation.

Risk:

- crop is hit by pests;
- harvest fails;
- she cannot repay;
- debt grows;
- she takes another loan;
- she finally sells part of her land.

## Why Credit Harmed Swapna

| Factor | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Purpose | Crop production, but dependent on uncertain harvest. |
| Risk | Pest attack and crop failure. |
| Lender | Moneylender likely charges high interest. |
| Repayment source | Farming income, which failed. |
| Outcome | Debt trap and loss of land. |

## Key Comparison

| Question | Salim | Swapna |
|---|---|---|
| Why need credit? | To complete a large order. | To pay cultivation expenses. |
| Risk | Production delay or failure to deliver. | Crop failure, pests, climate, high interest. |
| Outcome | Profit and repayment. | Debt trap and land sale. |
| Role of credit | Positive. | Harmful due to risk and terms. |

## Exam Conclusion

Credit is useful when it increases income and repayment is possible. Credit becomes harmful when risk is high, income fails, and terms of credit are unfavourable.

# Unit 3F: Terms of Credit

## Meaning

Terms of credit are the conditions under which a loan is given.

## Main Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Interest rate** | Extra amount paid for borrowing money. |
| **Collateral** | Asset pledged as security. |
| **Documentation** | Papers proving income, identity, employment, land, house, or business. |
| **Repayment schedule** | Time and method for returning principal and interest. |
| **Mode of repayment** | Cash, instalments, crop sale, wage work, bank transfer, etc. |

## Collateral

Collateral is an asset that the borrower owns and uses as a guarantee.

Examples:

- land title;
- house papers;
- vehicle;
- livestock;
- bank deposits;
- jewellery in some informal cases.

If the borrower fails to repay, the lender may sell the collateral to recover the loan.

## House Loan Example

Megha takes a bank loan to buy a house.

Terms:

- loan amount: Rs 5 lakh;
- interest rate: 12 percent per year;
- repayment: monthly instalments over 10 years;
- documents: employment records and salary details;
- collateral: house papers retained by bank until repayment.

## Why Terms Matter

Good credit terms can help borrowers. Bad terms can trap them.

| Favourable Terms | Unfavourable Terms |
|---|---|
| Low interest. | High interest. |
| Flexible repayment. | Harsh repayment. |
| Fair collateral demand. | Excessive collateral. |
| Written records. | No proper records. |
| Legal protection. | Harassment or forced sale. |

# Unit 3G: Credit Arrangements in Rural Areas

## Main Demand for Rural Credit

In rural areas, the main demand for credit is for crop production.

Farmers need money for:

- seeds;
- fertilisers;
- pesticides;
- water;
- electricity;
- equipment repair;
- labour;
- storage and transport.

There is a gap between buying inputs and selling harvest, so farmers often borrow at the beginning of the season and repay after harvest.

## Sonpur Village Examples

### Arun

Arun has seven acres of land and receives a bank loan for cultivation.

Features:

- interest rate is 8.5 percent per year;
- repayment possible within three years;
- he can sell part of the crop and store the rest;
- he can use cold storage receipt for another bank loan.

Arun has better credit access and therefore better income possibilities.

### Rama

Rama is an agricultural labourer.

She borrows from her employer-landowner for:

- daily expenses;
- sudden illness;
- family functions.

Terms:

- interest rate 5 percent per month;
- repayment through work;
- dependence on employer;
- fresh loans before old loans are repaid.

Rama is trapped in dependence and debt.

### Shyamal

Shyamal is a small farmer. He may have to borrow from informal sources such as moneylenders or traders, often at high interest and with pressure to sell crops at low prices.

## Cooperatives

Cooperatives are another source of rural credit.

Members pool resources and provide loans to members.

Example: Krishak Cooperative has farmer members, accepts deposits, obtains bank loans, and lends for:

- agricultural implements;
- cultivation;
- trade;
- fishery;
- house construction;
- other productive needs.

## Why Cooperatives Matter

Cooperatives can provide cheaper credit than moneylenders and can be closer to rural borrowers than commercial banks.

# Unit 3H: Formal and Informal Sources of Credit

## Formal Sources

Formal sources include:

- banks;
- cooperatives;
- government-backed institutions.

They are supervised and expected to follow rules.

## Informal Sources

Informal sources include:

- moneylenders;
- traders;
- employers;
- landlords;
- relatives;
- friends.

They are usually not supervised by a formal authority.

## Comparison Table

| Basis | Formal Credit | Informal Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Sources | Banks, cooperatives. | Moneylenders, traders, employers, relatives, friends. |
| Supervision | RBI or formal rules. | No regular supervision. |
| Interest rate | Usually lower. | Often very high. |
| Records | Written documents. | Often informal or no records. |
| Collateral | Often required. | May or may not be required. |
| Access for poor | Difficult without documents/collateral. | Easier but costly. |
| Risk | Safer and regulated. | Can lead to exploitation and debt trap. |

## Rural Credit Data

In the NCERT rural household credit data, moneylenders form a major source of credit. Commercial banks and cooperative societies/banks are also important sources.

Important learning:

Formal credit does not fully meet rural credit needs, so many people still depend on informal lenders.

# Unit 3I: RBI Supervision

## Role of RBI

The Reserve Bank of India supervises formal sources of credit.

It monitors:

- whether banks maintain cash reserves;
- how much banks lend;
- to whom banks lend;
- interest rates;
- whether banks lend not only to profit-making businesses but also to small cultivators, small industries, and small borrowers.

## Why Supervision Is Necessary

Supervision is necessary because:

1. depositors' money must be safe;
2. banks must keep enough cash for withdrawals;
3. lending should not benefit only rich borrowers;
4. small borrowers need access to formal credit;
5. banking system must remain trustworthy;
6. unfair practices must be controlled.

## Why Supervising Informal Lenders Is Difficult

Informal lenders are scattered, local, personal, and often undocumented. Many transactions are verbal. This makes monitoring interest rates and recovery methods difficult.

# Unit 3J: Credit and Inequality

## Who Gets Formal Credit?

Richer households are more likely to get formal credit because they usually have:

- collateral;
- documents;
- regular income;
- bank accounts;
- connections with banks;
- repayment capacity.

Poor households often lack these, so they depend on informal lenders.

## Urban Credit Pattern

NCERT shows that:

| Household Group | Informal Credit Share | Formal Credit Share |
|---|---:|---:|
| Poor households | 85% | 15% |
| Households with few assets | 72% | 28% |
| Well-off households | 53% | 47% |
| Rich households | 10% | 90% |

## What This Shows

The poor borrow mostly from informal sources, which are usually expensive. The rich borrow mostly from formal sources, which are cheaper.

This creates inequality because:

- poor borrowers pay high interest;
- a larger share of their income goes to repayment;
- they may fall into debt traps;
- they cannot easily invest in business or farming;
- rich borrowers get cheaper funds and grow faster.

## Development Conclusion

Cheap and affordable credit is crucial for development. Formal credit must expand and be distributed more equally.

# Unit 3K: Self-Help Groups for the Poor

## Why SHGs Are Needed

Banks often refuse loans to poor households because they lack collateral and documents.

Moneylenders give loans more easily but charge high interest and may harass borrowers.

Self-help groups help solve this problem.

## Structure of SHG

A typical SHG has:

- 15-20 members;
- usually people from one neighbourhood;
- often women members;
- regular meetings;
- regular savings;
- small loans among members;
- group decision-making.

## How SHGs Work

| Step | Explanation |
|---|---|
| 1 | Members save regularly, such as Rs 25 to Rs 100 or more. |
| 2 | Savings are pooled. |
| 3 | Members take small loans from the group. |
| 4 | Group decides purpose, amount, interest, and repayment schedule. |
| 5 | After regular savings for a year or two, SHG may get a bank loan. |
| 6 | Bank lends to the group, not just an individual. |
| 7 | Group ensures repayment through collective responsibility. |

## Uses of SHG Loans

SHG loans may be used for:

- releasing mortgaged land;
- buying seeds and fertilisers;
- buying raw material like bamboo or cloth;
- buying sewing machines;
- buying cattle;
- starting small businesses;
- housing material;
- meeting urgent family needs.

## Why Banks Lend to SHGs Without Collateral

Banks trust SHGs because:

- group members save regularly;
- repayment is monitored by the group;
- members know each other;
- peer responsibility reduces default;
- the group has a record of financial discipline.

## Benefits of SHGs

| Benefit | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Credit access** | Poor people get loans without traditional collateral. |
| **Lower interest** | Interest is usually lower than moneylender rates. |
| **Women empowerment** | Women become financially active and self-reliant. |
| **Collective strength** | Members discuss health, nutrition, domestic violence, and social issues. |
| **Self-employment** | Loans help start income-generating work. |
| **Reduced dependence** | Poor borrowers depend less on moneylenders. |

## Grameen Bank Example

Grameen Bank of Bangladesh is a major example of providing credit to poor people, especially women, at reasonable terms. It showed that poor women can be reliable borrowers and can run small income-generating activities successfully.

## Solved Exercise Guide

### 1. High-risk credit can create further problems

In high-risk situations, borrowers may fail to repay because income is uncertain. Swapna borrowed for cultivation, but pests destroyed her crop. She could not repay, debt increased, and she had to sell land. Thus, credit can become a debt trap when risk is high and support is absent.

### 2. How money solves double coincidence of wants

Money removes the need for both parties to want each other's goods. A potter can sell pots for money and then use money to buy rice. The rice seller does not need to want pots.

### 3. How banks mediate between savers and borrowers

Banks accept deposits from people with surplus money and pay them interest. Banks keep a small portion as cash and lend the rest to borrowers. Borrowers repay with interest. Thus banks connect depositors and borrowers.

### 4. What is written on a 10 rupee note?

A rupee note carries the promise of the RBI Governor and is issued by the Reserve Bank of India. It shows that the note is authorised by the central monetary authority and is accepted as legal tender.

### 5. Why expand formal sources of credit?

Formal credit must expand because it is usually cheaper, regulated, and safer. It reduces dependence on moneylenders, prevents debt traps, supports production, and helps small farmers, small industries, and poor households develop.

### 6. Basic idea behind SHGs

SHGs organise poor people, especially women, into small groups that save regularly and lend to members. After building discipline, the group can get a bank loan without individual collateral. This helps poor borrowers access cheaper credit.

### 7. Why banks may not lend to certain borrowers

Banks may refuse loans if borrowers:

- lack collateral;
- lack documents;
- have irregular income;
- have no repayment record;
- request risky loans;
- cannot show employment or land proof.

### 8. How RBI supervises banks

RBI supervises banks by checking cash reserves, monitoring lending, collecting information on loans and interest rates, and ensuring banks lend to small borrowers, cultivators, and small industries along with businesses.

### 9. Role of credit in development

Credit helps people invest in farming, business, education, housing, and production. It can increase income if used productively and repaid under fair terms. But high-interest informal credit can create debt traps. Therefore, affordable credit is essential for development.

### 10. Manav: bank or moneylender?

Manav should compare:

- interest rate;
- collateral requirement;
- documents needed;
- repayment schedule;
- urgency of loan;
- risk of harassment;
- total cost of borrowing.

If he has documents and collateral, a bank is better because interest is lower and terms are safer. If he lacks collateral, he may be forced toward informal sources, but that can be costly.

### 11. Small farmers and credit

**(a) Why banks may be unwilling:** Small farmers may lack collateral, documents, and stable repayment capacity.

**(b) Other sources:** Moneylenders, traders, employers, landlords, relatives, friends, cooperatives, and SHGs.

**(c) Unfavourable terms:** A trader may lend before harvest and force the farmer to sell crop at low price after harvest.

**(d) Ways to get cheap credit:** More rural banks, cooperatives, SHGs, Kisan credit facilities, lower collateral requirements, crop insurance, and government support.

5. **Collateral** is an asset used as guarantee until loan is repaid.
