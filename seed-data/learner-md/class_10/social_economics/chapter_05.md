## Big Ideas

Markets do not automatically protect consumers. Sellers may be powerful, consumers may be scattered, and information may be unequal.

Consumer rights exist because buyers need safety, information, choice, redressal, representation, and education.

Consumer protection is not only about court cases. It includes awareness, bills, labels, certification marks, public action, and collective pressure.

COPRA gave consumers a legal route to seek compensation, but the movement still needs active participation because redressal can be slow and many people remain unaware.

An alert consumer is a safer consumer.

# Unit 5A: Consumer in the Marketplace

## Consumer and Producer

People participate in markets in two ways:

| Role | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Producer** | Makes goods or provides services. | Farmer, factory worker, teacher, doctor, shopkeeper. |
| **Consumer** | Buys goods or services for use. | Person buying food, medicine, transport, education, mobile service. |

The same person can be a producer in one situation and a consumer in another.

## Why Consumers Need Protection

Consumers often buy in small quantities and are scattered. Sellers may be better organised, better informed, and more powerful.

Problems arise when:

- seller refuses responsibility after sale;
- product is defective;
- information is hidden;
- consumer lacks bill or proof;
- company uses misleading advertisement;
- consumer does not know legal rights.

## Simple Market Rule

A fair market requires:

1. correct information;
2. safe products;
3. honest weights and prices;
4. choice;
5. complaint mechanism;
6. compensation for harm;
7. active consumer awareness.

# Unit 5B: Exploitation in the Marketplace

## Common Forms of Exploitation

| Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Underweight goods** | Seller gives less quantity than paid for. | Weighing 900 g but charging for 1 kg. |
| **Adulteration** | Mixing inferior/harmful material. | Adulterated edible oil or spices. |
| **Defective goods** | Product does not work properly. | Faulty iron, camera, pressure cooker. |
| **Hidden charges** | Extra costs not told earlier. | Service fee added after purchase. |
| **Overcharging** | Charging more than MRP. | Selling packaged goods above printed price. |
| **False advertising** | Misleading claims to attract consumers. | Product claiming impossible health benefits. |
| **Unsafe services** | Poor safety in hospitals, theatres, buildings, transport. | Medical negligence or unsafe LPG cylinder. |

## Why Exploitation Happens

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Unequal power** | Producers/sellers may be few and powerful, consumers scattered. |
| **Information gap** | Seller knows more about product quality than buyer. |
| **Weak enforcement** | Rules exist but may not be followed. |
| **Low awareness** | Consumers may not know their rights or complaint process. |
| **No proof** | Consumers may not collect bills, warranty cards, or records. |

## False Claims Examples

The chapter discusses cases where companies made misleading claims, such as baby milk powder being projected as better than mother's milk, or tobacco companies resisting responsibility for health harms.

Lesson:

Consumers need protection not only from small shop cheating but also from powerful companies and misleading information.

# Unit 5C: Consumer Movement

## Why the Consumer Movement Began

The consumer movement began because consumers were dissatisfied with unfair practices.

In India, it gained organised form in the 1960s due to:

- food shortages;
- hoarding;
- black marketing;
- adulteration;
- unfair ration shop practices;
- overcrowding and poor transport services;
- lack of legal remedy for consumers.

## Evolution in India

| Period | Main Activity |
|---|---|
| **1960s** | Consumer movement began in organised form due to shortages and unfair practices. |
| **1970s** | Consumer organisations wrote articles, held exhibitions, and formed groups. |
| **1980s** | Stronger pressure for legal protection. |
| **1986** | Consumer Protection Act (COPRA) enacted. |
| **After COPRA** | Consumer courts, consumer forums, awareness campaigns, and government departments expanded. |

## International Consumer Movement

In 1985, the United Nations adopted Guidelines for Consumer Protection. These became a foundation for consumer protection policies across countries.

Consumers International became an umbrella organisation for consumer groups around the world.

## Main Achievement

The movement shifted responsibility from "buyer alone must be careful" to "seller and producer must ensure quality, safety, and fair practice."

# Unit 5D: Right to Safety

## Meaning

Consumers have the right to be protected against goods and services that are hazardous to life and property.

## Why Safety Matters

Some products and services can cause serious harm if quality is poor.

| Product / Service | Required Safety Concern |
|---|---|
| LPG cylinder | Leak prevention, proper seal, safe valve. |
| Pressure cooker | Working safety valve. |
| Medicines | Correct composition, expiry date, safe dosage. |
| Edible oil | No harmful adulteration. |
| High-rise building | Fire safety, structural safety, emergency exits. |
| Cinema theatre / marriage pandal | Fire safety, crowd control, emergency exits. |
| Hospital | Qualified staff, safe procedure, correct anaesthesia. |

## Reji Mathew Case

Reji, a Class IX student, was admitted for a tonsil operation. Improper anaesthesia caused serious brain damage and lifelong disability. His father filed a complaint. The National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission held the hospital responsible and ordered compensation.

## Lesson from Reji's Case

Services such as healthcare also fall under consumer protection. A consumer can seek compensation for negligence and deficiency in service.

# Unit 5E: Right to Information

## Meaning

Consumers have the right to know details about goods and services before buying.

## Information on Packaged Goods

| Information | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Ingredients | Helps identify content, allergies, quality. |
| MRP | Prevents overcharging. |
| Batch number | Helps trace defective batch. |
| Date of manufacture | Shows age of product. |
| Expiry date | Prevents unsafe consumption. |
| Manufacturer address | Helps complaint and accountability. |
| Directions for use | Helps safe usage. |
| Side effects / risks | Important for medicines and chemicals. |

## MRP

MRP means Maximum Retail Price. A seller cannot legally charge more than the MRP printed on a product.

Consumers may bargain and buy below MRP, but should not be charged above it.

## Expiry Date

For medicines and food products, expiry date is critical. Selling expired medicines can cause serious harm and can invite legal action.

## RTI Act Example: Amritha

Amritha applied for a government job but did not receive results. Officials did not respond. She used the Right to Information Act to ask for information. She was informed about the delay and received her appointment call letter.

## Lesson

Right to information extends beyond packaged goods. Citizens can demand information from government departments.

# Unit 5F: Right to Choice

## Meaning

Consumers have the right to choose whether to continue with a service or buy a product. They should not be forced to buy something they do not want.

## Denial of Choice

| Situation | Why It Violates Choice |
|---|---|
| Shop sells toothpaste only if toothbrush is also bought. | Consumer is forced into unwanted purchase. |
| Gas dealer insists buyer purchase stove from same dealer. | Consumer's freedom to choose seller is denied. |
| Coaching institute refuses reasonable refund after poor service. | Consumer is forced to continue or lose money. |

## Abirami Case

Abirami joined a two-year coaching course and paid the full fee in advance. After one year, she left because teaching quality was poor. The institute refused refund. Consumer court directed refund and compensation.

## Lesson

Consumers receiving services have the right to choose whether to continue, especially when service quality is poor or unfair terms are imposed.

# Unit 5G: Right to Redressal

## Meaning

Consumers have the right to seek redressal against unfair trade practices and exploitation.

Redressal may include:

- replacement;
- refund;
- repair;
- compensation;
- removal of defect;
- stopping unfair practice;
- penalty on seller or service provider.

## Prakash Money Order Case

Prakash sent a money order to his daughter for her marriage. The money did not reach on time and the post office did not respond properly.

Steps:

1. He sent money order.
2. He learned money had not reached.
3. He asked the post office.
4. The response was unsatisfactory.
5. He approached a consumer protection council for advice.
6. He filed a case in consumer court.
7. Court heard both sides.
8. Judge gave verdict.

## Lesson

Consumers can file complaints for poor services, not only defective goods.

# Unit 5H: COPRA and Consumer Courts

## COPRA

COPRA stands for Consumer Protection Act, 1986.

It was enacted to protect consumers and provide a legal system for redressal.

## Three-Tier Consumer Redressal System

| Level | Institution | Claim Amount in NCERT |
|---|---|---|
| **District** | District Forum / District Consumer Court | Up to Rs 20 lakh. |
| **State** | State Commission | Rs 20 lakh to Rs 1 crore. |
| **National** | National Commission | Above Rs 1 crore. |

If a case is dismissed at district level, the consumer can appeal to the state level and then national level.

## Consumer Protection Councils vs Consumer Courts

| Consumer Protection Council | Consumer Court / Forum |
|---|---|
| Guides consumers. | Hears cases and gives verdicts. |
| Creates awareness. | Orders refund, replacement, compensation, or penalty. |
| May help prepare complaint. | Has quasi-judicial authority. |
| May represent consumers in some cases. | Decides disputes between consumer and seller/service provider. |

## Right to Representation

Consumers can represent themselves in consumer courts, with or without lawyers.

# Unit 5I: Becoming Well-Informed Consumers

## Consumer Duties

Rights become useful only when consumers act carefully.

| Duty | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Ask for bill/cash memo. | Proof is needed for complaint. |
| Check MRP. | Prevents overcharging. |
| Check expiry date. | Prevents unsafe use. |
| Check ingredients. | Important for health and quality. |
| Check certification marks. | Assures standards for some products. |
| Read warranty conditions. | Helps claim repair/replacement. |
| Keep documents safely. | Needed in consumer court. |
| Avoid misleading offers. | Prevents impulsive purchase. |
| Compare price and quality. | Supports informed choice. |
| Complain when cheated. | Builds consumer movement. |

## Certification Marks

| Mark | Used For |
|---|---|
| **ISI** | Industrial products meeting standards. |
| **Agmark** | Agricultural products such as edible oil and cereals. |
| **Hallmark** | Jewellery purity, especially gold. |
| **ISO** | Quality management/process standards for organisations. |

## Why Certification Is Not on Every Product

Standard certification is not compulsory for all goods. It is mandatory mainly for certain products that affect health, safety, or mass consumption, such as LPG cylinders, food colours/additives, cement, and packaged drinking water.

# Unit 5J: Taking the Consumer Movement Forward

## Progress

India observes 24 December as National Consumers' Day because COPRA was enacted on that day in 1986.

Achievements:

- exclusive consumer courts;
- consumer organisations;
- awareness campaigns;
- government departments of consumer affairs;
- legal recognition of consumer rights;
- compensation in many cases.

## Limitations

| Problem | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Slow process | Cases can take time. |
| Expensive process | Lawyers and repeated visits may be costly. |
| Lack of bills | Evidence is hard to collect. |
| Small purchases | Consumers may not complain for low-value items. |
| Low awareness | Many people do not know rights or procedures. |
| Weak enforcement | Rules may exist but not be followed. |
| Unclear compensation | Injury from defective products can be hard to prove. |

## What Is Needed

The consumer movement needs:

- active consumers;
- voluntary organisations;
- stronger enforcement;
- simple complaint systems;
- school-level awareness;
- proper bills and records;
- responsible media;
- honest producers;
- accessible consumer courts.

## Solved Exercise Guide

### 1. Why are rules and regulations required in the marketplace?

Rules are needed because sellers may exploit consumers through adulteration, underweight goods, hidden charges, defective products, overpricing, or false claims. Consumers are scattered and often weaker than producers. Rules protect safety, information, choice, and redressal.

### 2. Factors that gave birth to consumer movement in India

The movement arose due to food shortages, hoarding, black marketing, adulteration, ration shop malpractices, transport problems, and lack of legal protection. It began in organised form in the 1960s, grew through articles/exhibitions in the 1970s, and gained legal strength with COPRA in 1986.

### 3. Need for consumer consciousness

Consumer consciousness is needed because rights are useful only when consumers know and use them.

Examples:

- checking MRP prevents overcharging;
- checking expiry date prevents unsafe medicine or food use;
- keeping bills helps file complaints.

### 4. Factors causing exploitation

Factors include:

- limited information;
- illiteracy or low awareness;
- no bill/proof;
- false advertisements;
- few powerful producers;
- weak enforcement;
- urgent need of consumer;
- sellers shifting responsibility after sale.

### 5. Rationale behind COPRA

COPRA was enacted to provide consumers legal protection and an accessible redressal system against unfair trade practices, defective goods, and poor services.

### 6. Duties as a consumer

When visiting a shopping complex, a consumer should:

- check MRP;
- ask for bill;
- check expiry date;
- read labels;
- compare quality and price;
- check warranty;
- avoid misleading offers;
- complain if cheated.

### 7. Honey and biscuit packet: which mark?

For honey and biscuits, consumers should check quality marks where applicable, ingredients, expiry date, batch number, manufacturer details, and MRP. For agricultural/food products, **Agmark** may be relevant where certified.

### 8. Legal measures to empower consumers

Legal measures include:

- Consumer Protection Act, 1986;
- three-tier consumer courts;
- right to redressal;
- right to representation;
- government consumer affairs departments;
- RTI Act for government information;
- quality certification systems.

### 9. Consumer rights

| Right | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Right to safety | Protection from hazardous goods/services. |
| Right to information | Access to details such as MRP, ingredients, expiry, risks. |
| Right to choice | Freedom to choose goods/services. |
| Right to redressal | Compensation or correction against exploitation. |
| Right to representation | Right to be heard in consumer forums. |
| Right to consumer education | Right to learn how to act as an aware consumer. |

### 10. Consumer solidarity

Consumers can express solidarity by forming consumer groups, joining awareness campaigns, reporting unfair practices, helping others file complaints, supporting consumer organisations, and demanding proper enforcement.

### 11. Progress of consumer movement

The movement has made progress through COPRA, consumer courts, awareness campaigns, and consumer organisations. But it remains limited because cases can be slow, costly, and evidence-dependent. Many consumers are still unaware or hesitant to complain.
