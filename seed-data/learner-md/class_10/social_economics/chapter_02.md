## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Economic activity** | Work that produces goods or services and contributes to income or production. |
| **Sector** | A group of economic activities classified using a common criterion. |
| **Primary sector** | Activities that directly use natural resources, such as agriculture, dairy, fishing, forestry, and mining. |
| **Secondary sector** | Activities that transform natural products into manufactured goods, such as cloth, sugar, bricks, and steel. |
| **Tertiary sector** | Service activities that support production and daily life, such as transport, banking, trade, education, health, and communication. |
| **Service sector** | Another name for the tertiary sector because it mainly produces services, not physical goods. |
| **Final goods** | Goods and services that reach final users and are not used up to produce another good. |
| **Intermediate goods** | Goods used as inputs in producing final goods, such as wheat used to make flour and biscuits. |
| **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** | Value of all final goods and services produced within a country during a particular year. |
| **Employment** | Work through which people earn income or livelihood. |
| **Underemployment** | Situation where people are working less than their capacity or potential. |
| **Disguised unemployment** | Hidden unemployment where extra workers appear employed but their removal does not reduce output. |
| **Open unemployment** | Visible unemployment where a person is willing to work but has no job. |
| **MGNREGA 2005** | Law guaranteeing 100 days of wage employment in a year to rural households whose adult members demand work. |
| **Organised sector** | Registered workplaces with regular employment terms, rules, job security, and benefits. |
| **Unorganised sector** | Small, scattered, often unregistered workplaces with low wages, irregular jobs, and little protection. |
| **Public sector** | Sector where government owns assets and provides services. |
| **Private sector** | Sector where private individuals or companies own assets and usually work for profit. |

## Big Ideas

Classification helps us understand the economy. The same activity can be studied by nature of work, employment condition, or ownership.

The primary, secondary, and tertiary sectors are different but interdependent. Agriculture needs industry and services; industry needs raw material and transport; services depend on production and people.

GDP measures the size of the economy, but only final goods and services are counted to avoid double counting.

India has seen the tertiary sector become the largest contributor to GDP, but employment has not shifted equally. Many people remain in agriculture, causing underemployment.

Most workers in India work in the unorganised sector. This makes worker protection a development issue, not only a labour issue.

The public sector matters because many services needed by society cannot be provided fairly or cheaply by profit-driven private firms.

# Unit 2A: Sectors of Economic Activities

## Core Idea

An economy has thousands of activities: farming, fishing, weaving, building, teaching, transport, banking, software work, selling goods, and many more. To understand this complexity, we classify activities into sectors.

Classification means grouping activities using a criterion.

## Why Classification Helps

| Purpose | How Classification Helps |
|---|---|
| **Understand production** | We can see which sector produces how much. |
| **Understand employment** | We can see where most people work. |
| **Find problems** | We can identify underemployment, low wages, or lack of protection. |
| **Plan policy** | Government can decide where jobs, credit, roads, schools, or support are needed. |
| **Compare change over time** | We can see whether the economy is shifting from agriculture to industry or services. |

## Main Classifications in This Chapter

| Classification | Criterion Used |
|---|---|
| **Primary / Secondary / Tertiary** | Nature of economic activity. |
| **Organised / Unorganised** | Employment conditions and formal rules. |
| **Public / Private** | Ownership of assets and responsibility for services. |

# Unit 2B: Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Sectors

## Primary Sector

The primary sector includes activities that directly use natural resources.

It is called primary because it forms the base for many other products.

| Activity | Natural Resource Used | Product |
|---|---|---|
| Farming | Land, rainfall, soil, seeds | Crops |
| Dairy | Animals, fodder, water | Milk |
| Fishing | Rivers, ponds, sea | Fish |
| Forestry | Forests | Timber, bamboo, honey, medicinal plants |
| Mining | Mineral deposits | Coal, iron ore, bauxite |

The primary sector is also called the **agriculture and related sector** because many activities are linked to agriculture, dairy, fishing, forestry, and mining.

## Secondary Sector

The secondary sector includes activities that change natural products into other forms through manufacturing.

| Raw Material | Manufacturing Activity | Final Product |
|---|---|---|
| Cotton | Spinning and weaving | Cloth |
| Sugarcane | Processing | Sugar or gur |
| Clay / earth | Moulding and baking | Bricks |
| Iron ore | Smelting and shaping | Steel |
| Wheat | Milling and baking | Flour, bread, biscuits |

It is also called the **industrial sector**.

## Tertiary Sector

The tertiary sector includes services that support primary and secondary sectors or meet people's direct needs.

| Service | How It Helps |
|---|---|
| Transport | Moves raw material and finished goods. |
| Storage | Keeps goods safe before sale. |
| Communication | Helps producers, traders, and consumers connect. |
| Banking | Provides credit and payment facilities. |
| Trade | Helps goods reach buyers. |
| Education | Builds skills and knowledge. |
| Health | Keeps workers and citizens healthy. |
| IT services | Supports modern communication, business, and global work. |

It is also called the **service sector**.

## Quick Difference Table

| Sector | Nature of Activity | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Produces natural goods. | Farming, fishing, mining, dairy. |
| **Secondary** | Manufactures goods from natural products. | Sugar mill, textile factory, brick kiln. |
| **Tertiary** | Provides services. | Transport, banking, teaching, healthcare, trade. |

# Unit 2C: Interdependence of Sectors

## Core Idea

The three sectors are not isolated. They depend on each other.

## Sugarcane and Sugar Mill Example

| Link | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Farmers grow sugarcane. | Primary sector produces raw material. |
| Sugar mill processes sugarcane. | Secondary sector turns it into sugar or gur. |
| Trucks transport sugar. | Tertiary sector moves goods to market. |
| Shops sell sugar. | Tertiary sector connects producer and consumer. |
| Banks give loans. | Tertiary sector supports production and trade. |

If farmers refuse to sell sugarcane, the mill shuts down. If transporters strike, goods do not reach cities. If fertiliser prices rise, farmers' costs increase.

## Cotton Example

| Sector | Role |
|---|---|
| Primary | Cotton cultivation. |
| Secondary | Spinning yarn and weaving cloth. |
| Tertiary | Transport, storage, banking, wholesale, retail, export. |

## Exam Point

Economic activities are interdependent because production, processing, transport, finance, and sale are connected. A disturbance in one sector affects the others.

# Unit 2D: GDP and Final Goods

## Why Measuring Production Is Difficult

An economy produces many different goods and services:

- wheat;
- coconuts;
- cars;
- computers;
- furniture;
- education;
- transport;
- banking;
- medical services.

We cannot simply add quantities because 10 cars, 5000 coconuts, and 1 hospital service are different things.

So economists add their **value in money**.

## Value Example

| Good | Quantity | Price | Value |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Wheat | 10,000 kg | Rs 8 per kg | Rs 80,000 |
| Coconuts | 5,000 | Rs 10 each | Rs 50,000 |

## Final Goods and Intermediate Goods

Only final goods and services are counted in GDP.

### Wheat-Flour-Biscuit Example

| Stage | Good | Type | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Farmer sells wheat | Wheat | Intermediate good | Used by flour mill. |
| Mill sells flour | Flour | Intermediate good | Used by biscuit company. |
| Company sells biscuits | Biscuits | Final good | Bought by final consumers. |

If wheat, flour, and biscuits are all counted separately, the same value is counted many times.

This is called **double counting**.

## GDP Definition

GDP is the value of all final goods and services produced within a country during a particular year.

```text
GDP = Value of final goods and services in primary sector
    + Value of final goods and services in secondary sector
    + Value of final goods and services in tertiary sector
```

## Why GDP Matters

GDP shows the size of an economy. It helps compare production over time and across countries, but it does not directly show equality, environment, or quality of life.

# Unit 2E: Historical Change in Sectors

## General Pattern in Developed Countries

Many developed countries passed through a broad historical shift:

| Stage | Dominant Sector | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Early stage** | Primary sector | Most people worked in agriculture and related activities. |
| **Industrial stage** | Secondary sector | Factories expanded and manufacturing became important. |
| **Modern developed stage** | Tertiary sector | Services such as education, health, banking, communication, transport, IT, and administration expanded. |

## Why the Shift Happens

1. Agriculture becomes more productive.
2. Fewer people are needed to produce food.
3. Factories grow and absorb labour.
4. Income rises and demand for services increases.
5. Education, health, finance, transport, and technology become more important.

## Important Caution

This shift is not automatic. A country may increase production in industry and services without creating enough jobs there. That is one of India's major problems.

# Unit 2F: Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Sectors in India

## Production Trend

Between 1973-74 and 2013-14, production increased in all three sectors, but it increased the most in the tertiary sector.

By 2013-14, tertiary sector became the largest producing sector in India.

## Why Tertiary Sector Became Important

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Basic services are needed** | Hospitals, schools, post, police, courts, defence, transport, banks, and insurance are required in every country. |
| **Agriculture and industry create service demand** | More farming and factories require transport, storage, trade, banking, and communication. |
| **Income growth changes demand** | People start demanding restaurants, tourism, shopping, private hospitals, private schools, and training. |
| **Information technology grows** | IT, call centres, software, ATMs, and digital services expand rapidly. |

## Employment Trend

India's employment did not shift as strongly as GDP.

The primary sector still employs a very large share of workers even though its share in GDP is much smaller.

## The Main Problem

More than half of workers remain in primary sector, especially agriculture, but agriculture contributes only a smaller share of GDP.

This means many people are working in agriculture even when not all of them are needed for the same output.

# Unit 2G: Underemployment and Disguised Unemployment

## Underemployment

Underemployment happens when people are working less than their full capacity.

They may appear busy, but their labour is not fully used.

## Disguised Unemployment

Disguised unemployment is hidden unemployment. People appear employed, but if some workers are removed, total production does not fall.

## Rural Example: Laxmi's Family

Laxmi owns two hectares of unirrigated land. Five family members work on the farm because they have nowhere else to go.

But the farm does not actually need five people. If two people move to another job, farm output may remain the same and family income may rise.

This is disguised unemployment.

## Urban Example

In cities, many casual workers wait for daily jobs as painters, plumbers, repair workers, cart-pushers, or small sellers. They may work the whole day but earn very little because better opportunities are unavailable.

This is urban underemployment.

## Open vs Disguised Unemployment

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Open unemployment** | Person has no job despite wanting work. | Educated youth searching for work. |
| **Disguised unemployment** | Person appears employed but is not needed for current output. | Extra family members working on a small farm. |

## Why Underemployment Is Serious

Underemployment means:

- low income;
- wasted labour power;
- poverty continues;
- productivity remains low;
- families cannot improve living standards;
- national production stays below potential.

# Unit 2H: How to Create More Employment

## Employment Through Agriculture Improvement

| Measure | How It Creates Employment |
|---|---|
| **Irrigation wells and canals** | Farmers can grow a second crop and need more labour. |
| **Dams and canals** | Larger areas get water and cropping intensity rises. |
| **Rural roads** | Crops can be transported to markets. |
| **Storage facilities** | Farmers can store onions, potatoes, grains, and sell at better prices. |
| **Cheap credit** | Farmers can buy seeds, fertilisers, equipment, and pumpsets on time. |
| **Marketing support** | Farmers get better prices and avoid distress sale. |

## Employment Through Rural Industry

Semi-rural industries can create jobs without forcing people to migrate far away.

Examples:

- dal mills for pulses;
- cold storage for vegetables;
- honey collection centres near forests;
- food processing units;
- gur making;
- rice mills;
- craft-based industries;
- dairy processing.

## Employment Through Education and Health

If more children attend school, the country needs:

- school buildings;
- teachers;
- support staff;
- books and materials;
- transport;
- midday meal workers.

If health services improve, the country needs:

- doctors;
- nurses;
- health workers;
- pharmacists;
- ambulance staff;
- sanitation workers.

So education and health create jobs and also improve human development.

## Employment Through Tourism and Regional Potential

Every region may have potential in:

- tourism;
- local craft;
- food processing;
- IT services;
- repair services;
- transport;
- local markets.

Government planning and support can convert these into employment.

## MGNREGA 2005

MGNREGA stands for **Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005**.

| Feature | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Who gets work?** | Rural people who are able and in need of work. |
| **Guarantee** | 100 days of employment in a year. |
| **Responsibility** | Government must provide work. |
| **If work is not given** | Unemployment allowance must be paid. |
| **Preferred works** | Activities that increase future productivity of land, such as water conservation, roads, irrigation, and land development. |

## Why It Is Called Right to Work

It is called "Right to Work" because the law gives rural households a legal guarantee of employment. If the government fails, people are entitled to unemployment allowance.

# Unit 2I: Organised and Unorganised Sectors

## Criterion Used

This classification is based on employment conditions:

- Is the workplace registered?
- Are rules followed?
- Is work regular?
- Are wages paid properly?
- Is there job security?
- Are benefits given?

## Organised Sector

The organised sector includes workplaces where employment terms are regular and rules are followed.

Features:

- registered with government;
- follows laws such as Factories Act, Minimum Wages Act, Payment of Gratuity Act, Shops and Establishments Act;
- regular salary;
- fixed working hours;
- overtime payment;
- paid leave;
- provident fund;
- gratuity;
- medical benefits;
- safe working conditions;
- pension or retirement benefits in many cases.

## Kanta Example

Kanta works in an office from 9:30 a.m. to 5:30 p.m. She gets regular salary, provident fund, medical allowance, paid Sundays, and an appointment letter.

She represents organised sector work.

## Unorganised Sector

The unorganised sector includes small and scattered units that are largely outside government control.

Features:

- low-paid jobs;
- irregular work;
- no job security;
- no overtime payment;
- no paid leave;
- no formal appointment letter;
- unsafe working conditions;
- workers may be removed without reason;
- rules exist but are often not followed.

## Kamal Example

Kamal works as a daily wage labourer in a grocery shop from morning to evening. He gets only wages, no paid leave, no security, no appointment letter, and can be removed anytime.

He represents unorganised sector work.

## Comparison Table

| Basis | Organised Sector | Unorganised Sector |
|---|---|---|
| Registration | Registered with government. | Often unregistered. |
| Job security | Generally secure. | Insecure. |
| Wages | Regular salary/wage. | Low and irregular. |
| Working hours | Fixed hours. | Long and uncertain hours. |
| Overtime | Paid overtime. | Usually no overtime payment. |
| Leave | Paid leave and holidays. | Usually no paid leave. |
| Benefits | Provident fund, gratuity, medical benefits, pension. | Usually absent. |
| Safety | Legal requirement for safe conditions. | Often unsafe. |

# Unit 2J: Protecting Workers in the Unorganised Sector

## Why Protection Is Needed

Most workers in India are in the unorganised sector. They often face low wages, irregular work, exploitation, unsafe conditions, and social discrimination.

## Worker Groups Needing Protection

| Area | Vulnerable Workers |
|---|---|
| **Rural areas** | Landless agricultural labourers, small farmers, marginal farmers, sharecroppers, artisans. |
| **Urban areas** | Street vendors, construction workers, garment workers, rag pickers, headload workers, transport workers, small workshop workers. |
| **Social groups** | Many workers from Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and backward communities are concentrated in unorganised work. |

## Small and Marginal Farmers

Nearly 80 percent of rural households in India are in the small and marginal farmer category.

They need support such as:

- timely seeds;
- fertilisers and inputs;
- affordable credit;
- irrigation;
- storage;
- marketing outlets;
- fair prices;
- crop insurance.

## Urban Unorganised Workers

Urban unorganised workers need:

- minimum wages;
- safety equipment;
- identity cards;
- health insurance;
- social security;
- shelter and basic services;
- protection from sudden eviction or harassment;
- support for raw material and marketing.

## Development Link

Protecting unorganised workers is necessary for both economic and social development because it raises income, dignity, safety, equality, and productivity.

# Unit 2K: Public and Private Sectors

## Criterion Used

This classification is based on ownership of assets and responsibility for delivery of services.

## Public Sector

In the public sector, government owns most assets and provides services.

Examples:

- Indian Railways;
- post office;
- government schools;
- government hospitals;
- public roads;
- dams and irrigation projects;
- police and courts;
- public sector electricity boards.

## Private Sector

In the private sector, assets are owned by private individuals or companies.

Examples:

- Tata Iron and Steel Company;
- Reliance Industries;
- private hospitals;
- private schools;
- private shops;
- private airlines;
- private factories.

## Main Difference

| Basis | Public Sector | Private Sector |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership | Government. | Private individuals or companies. |
| Main motive | Public welfare and service, not only profit. | Profit. |
| Funding | Taxes, fees, borrowing, public revenue. | Private investment and user payments. |
| Examples | Railways, roads, government schools, ration shops. | Private factories, private hospitals, private shops. |

## Why Government Must Provide Some Services

Some activities need huge investment and benefit society as a whole.

Examples:

- roads;
- bridges;
- railways;
- harbours;
- electricity generation;
- irrigation dams;
- schools;
- hospitals;
- safe drinking water;
- nutrition support;
- housing for the poor.

Private companies may not provide these at affordable prices because they focus on profit.

## Government Support Examples

| Support | Why It Is Needed |
|---|---|
| **Affordable electricity** | Helps small industries continue production. |
| **Procurement of wheat and rice** | Supports farmers through fair price. |
| **Ration shops** | Supports consumers through lower food prices. |
| **Schools and health facilities** | Develops human capital and improves quality of life. |
| **Spending in poor regions** | Reduces regional inequality. |

### 6. Is primary-secondary-tertiary classification useful?

Yes. It helps us understand:

- nature of economic activity;
- contribution to GDP;
- employment distribution;
- interdependence of sectors;
- structural change in the economy;
- where unemployment and underemployment exist.

### 7. Why focus on employment and GDP?

GDP shows production and economic size. Employment shows livelihood and distribution of work.

Other issues to examine:

- income level;
- job security;
- worker benefits;
- working conditions;
- environmental impact;
- gender equality;
- regional balance.

### 8. Classifying adult work around us

Adult work can be classified by:

- nature: primary, secondary, tertiary;
- employment condition: organised, unorganised;
- ownership: public, private;
- skill: skilled, semi-skilled, unskilled;
- location: rural, urban.

### 9. How is tertiary sector different?

Tertiary sector provides services rather than producing natural or manufactured goods.

Examples:

- teachers;
- doctors;
- transporters;
- traders;
- bankers;
- software workers;
- police;
- lawyers.

### 10. Disguised unemployment

Disguised unemployment is hidden unemployment where extra workers appear employed but their removal does not reduce output.

Rural example: five family members work on a small farm that needs only three.

Urban example: too many people work in a small shop or street vending activity where each earns very little.

### 11. Open vs disguised unemployment

| Open Unemployment | Disguised Unemployment |
|---|---|
| Person has no job. | Person appears employed. |
| Visible. | Hidden. |
| Common among educated job seekers. | Common in agriculture and informal work. |
| Output can rise if person gets work. | Output does not fall if extra worker leaves current work. |

### 12. Is tertiary sector insignificant?

No. The tertiary sector plays a major role because:

- it is the largest contributor to GDP;
- it supports agriculture and industry;
- it provides basic services;
- IT and communication services are growing;
- trade, transport, banking, education, and health are essential for development.

But all service jobs are not high-quality. Many service workers earn very little.

### 13. Two kinds of people in service sector

1. Highly skilled and educated workers in limited services such as IT, banking, management, and professional services.
2. Large number of low-paid workers such as shopkeepers, repair workers, transport workers, vendors, and casual workers.

### 14. Are workers exploited in the unorganised sector?

Yes. They often face low wages, no job security, no paid leave, no overtime, unsafe conditions, and no formal protection. Many can be removed anytime and may also face social discrimination.

### 15. Classification by employment conditions

Activities are classified into:

- organised sector;
- unorganised sector.

The criterion is whether employment is regular, registered, rule-bound, and protected.

### 16. Organised vs unorganised conditions

Use the Kanta-Kamal comparison:

Kanta has fixed hours, salary, leave, provident fund, and security. Kamal works long hours, gets only daily wages, has no leave or security, and can be dismissed anytime.

### 17. Objective of MGNREGA 2005

MGNREGA aims to provide livelihood security in rural areas by guaranteeing 100 days of wage employment in a year to rural households that demand work. It also gives priority to work that improves land productivity and rural assets.
