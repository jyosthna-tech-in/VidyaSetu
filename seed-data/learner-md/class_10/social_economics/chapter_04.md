## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Globalisation** | Rapid integration or interconnection between countries through trade, investment, technology, services, and movement of goods. |
| **Multinational Corporation (MNC)** | A company that owns or controls production in more than one country. |
| **Foreign investment** | Investment made by an MNC or foreign company in another country to buy assets or set up production. |
| **Foreign trade** | Exchange of goods and services between countries. |
| **Integration of markets** | Linking of markets across countries so producers and consumers in different countries compete and exchange goods. |
| **Trade barrier** | Government restriction on trade, such as tax on imports or quota on quantity imported. |
| **Liberalisation** | Removing or reducing government restrictions on trade and investment. |
| **World Trade Organization (WTO)** | International organisation that sets and supervises rules for global trade. |
| **Special Economic Zone (SEZ)** | Industrial zone with world-class facilities and tax concessions to attract investment. |
| **Flexible labour laws** | Policies allowing firms to hire workers temporarily or adjust labour according to business needs. |
| **Fair globalisation** | Globalisation that creates opportunities for all and shares benefits more equally. |

## Big Ideas

Globalisation has changed markets. Indian consumers now see many brands, models, and imported products that were not available earlier.

MNCs do not only sell globally; they also produce globally. A product may be designed in one country, parts made in another, assembled in a third, and supported through customer service in a fourth.

Foreign trade benefits consumers through choice and lower prices, but it can hurt local producers who cannot compete with cheaper imports.

Liberalisation reduced barriers on trade and investment, but free trade does not automatically mean fair trade. Developed countries may still protect their producers while asking developing countries to open markets.

Globalisation has benefited well-off consumers, some large Indian companies, MNCs, and skilled service providers. Many small producers and workers have faced intense competition, job insecurity, and low wages.

Fair globalisation requires government action, worker protection, support for small producers, and more democratic international trade rules.

# Unit 4A: Meaning of Globalisation

## Core Idea

Globalisation is the process by which countries become more connected and interdependent.

This connection happens through:

- foreign trade;
- foreign investment;
- movement of goods and services;
- technology and communication;
- MNC-led production;
- global supply chains;
- movement of capital and ideas.

## Everyday Signs

| Everyday Example | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| Many foreign car brands in India | Global companies selling in Indian markets. |
| Mobile phones with parts from different countries | Production spread across countries. |
| Online services from India for foreign companies | Services can be traded globally. |
| Imported toys, electronics, clothes, and food products | Markets are more open and connected. |
| Call centres in India serving foreign customers | Technology connects work across borders. |

## Simple Definition

Globalisation means rapid integration of countries through greater foreign trade, foreign investment, technology, and global production networks.

# Unit 4B: Production Across Countries

## How Production Was Earlier

Until the middle of the twentieth century, production was mostly organised within countries. Trade existed, but mainly raw materials, food, and finished goods crossed borders.

Colonies such as India exported raw materials and imported finished goods.

## Role of MNCs

MNCs changed this pattern. They spread production across many countries.

An MNC sets up offices or factories where it can get:

- cheap labour;
- skilled labour;
- raw materials;
- large markets;
- lower production cost;
- favourable government policies;
- transport and communication links.

## Why MNCs Spread Production

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Lower cost** | Labour and other resources may be cheaper in some countries. |
| **Access to markets** | Producing near customers reduces transport cost and increases sales. |
| **Skilled workforce** | Countries like India provide engineers, IT workers, and English-speaking service workers. |
| **Government support** | Tax benefits, SEZs, and flexible labour laws attract foreign companies. |
| **Profit** | Lower cost and wider markets increase profits. |

## Global Production Example

A large MNC may:

- design a product in the United States;
- manufacture components in China;
- assemble them in Mexico or Eastern Europe;
- run customer care from India;
- sell finished products worldwide.

This is global production.

# Unit 4C: Interlinking Production Across Countries

MNCs link production in different countries in several ways.

## 1. Setting Up Factories and Offices

MNCs set up factories where labour and resources are available at lower cost and markets are nearby.

Example: Ford Motors set up a large plant near Chennai in 1995.

## 2. Joint Production with Local Companies

MNCs may work with local companies.

Local companies benefit because MNCs can provide:

- money for investment;
- new machines;
- better technology;
- global marketing links.

## 3. Buying Local Companies

MNCs with huge wealth can buy successful local companies.

Example:

Cargill Foods bought Parakh Foods, an Indian company with a strong marketing network and oil refineries. Cargill became a major edible oil producer.

## 4. Placing Orders with Small Producers

Large MNCs often place orders with small producers in developing countries.

Examples:

- garments;
- footwear;
- sports items;
- toys;
- components.

The MNC sells these goods under its own brand name.

## MNC Power Over Small Producers

MNCs can control:

- price;
- quality;
- delivery schedule;
- labour conditions;
- product design.

This gives small producers market access, but also puts pressure on them.

# Unit 4D: Foreign Trade and Integration of Markets

## Meaning of Foreign Trade

Foreign trade means buying and selling goods and services across countries.

It allows producers to sell beyond domestic markets and buyers to access goods produced elsewhere.

## Basic Function of Foreign Trade

| For Producers | For Consumers |
|---|---|
| Access to larger markets. | More choice of goods. |
| Opportunity to export. | Often lower prices. |
| Competition with foreign producers. | Better quality and new designs. |
| Possibility of business expansion. | Access to products not made locally. |

## Chinese Toys Example

Chinese toy producers export toys to India. Indian buyers now choose between Indian and Chinese toys.

Because Chinese toys are cheaper and have new designs, many buyers prefer them. Toy shops begin replacing Indian toys with Chinese toys.

## Effects of Foreign Trade in the Toy Example

| Group | Effect |
|---|---|
| Indian consumers | More choice and lower prices. |
| Chinese producers | Bigger market and more business. |
| Indian toy producers | Losses due to reduced sales. |
| Indian toy workers | Possible job loss if local units close. |

## Integration of Markets

Foreign trade connects markets in different countries.

When trade opens:

- goods move from one country to another;
- prices of similar goods may become closer;
- producers in different countries compete;
- consumers get wider choice;
- markets become interlinked.

# Unit 4E: Factors Enabling Globalisation

## 1. Technology

Technology has made globalisation faster and cheaper.

### Transport Technology

Improvements in transport have reduced cost and time.

Examples:

- containers;
- faster ships;
- better ports;
- cheaper air transport;
- improved road and rail links.

Containers are important because goods can be packed once and moved across ships, trains, trucks, and planes.

### Information and Communication Technology

ICT allows instant communication across the world.

Examples:

- mobile phones;
- internet;
- email;
- video calls;
- e-banking;
- satellite communication;
- software systems.

## ICT Example

A magazine for London readers may be designed in Delhi. Text is sent by internet, design instructions come through communication facilities, payment is transferred by e-banking, and printed copies are flown to London.

This shows how services can move globally without workers physically moving.

## 2. Liberalisation

Government policies changed to allow freer trade and investment.

## 3. WTO and International Pressure

International organisations encouraged countries to reduce trade barriers and liberalise policies.

# Unit 4F: Liberalisation and WTO

## Trade Barriers

Trade barriers are restrictions used by government to regulate trade.

Examples:

- tax on imports;
- quotas limiting quantity imported;
- restrictions on foreign investment;
- licensing rules.

## Why India Used Barriers After Independence

After independence, India protected domestic producers because industries were still developing.

If foreign goods had entered freely in the 1950s and 1960s, Indian industries may not have survived.

India allowed imports mainly for essential items such as:

- machinery;
- fertilisers;
- petroleum.

## Liberalisation After 1991

Around 1991, India removed many barriers on trade and foreign investment.

This allowed:

- easier imports and exports;
- foreign companies to set up factories and offices;
- Indian producers to face global competition;
- more MNC investment.

## Meaning of Liberalisation

Liberalisation means removing or reducing restrictions set by the government on trade and investment.

## WTO

The World Trade Organization aims to liberalise international trade.

It:

- sets trade rules;
- encourages free trade;
- supervises whether rules are followed.

## Free Trade vs Fair Trade

WTO is supposed to promote free trade for all. But developing countries argue that developed countries often keep unfair advantages.

Example:

Developed countries may continue to support their farmers heavily. Their farmers can sell agricultural products cheaply in world markets, hurting farmers in developing countries.

## Exam Point

Free trade is not always fair trade. Fair trade requires equal rules, protection for vulnerable producers, and space for developing countries to support their people.

# Unit 4G: Impact of Globalisation in India

## Benefits

Globalisation has benefited some groups.

| Group | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Well-off urban consumers | More choice, better quality, lower prices for many goods. |
| MNCs | More markets, lower cost, higher profits. |
| Large Indian companies | New technology, collaborations, global expansion. |
| Skilled professionals | More jobs in IT, banking, engineering, services, and outsourcing. |
| Local suppliers to MNCs | More orders and business opportunities. |

## Examples of Indian Companies Becoming Global

Some Indian companies expanded internationally, such as:

- Tata Motors;
- Infosys;
- Ranbaxy;
- Asian Paints;
- Sundaram Fasteners.

## New Service Opportunities

India gained opportunities in:

- call centres;
- software;
- data entry;
- accounting;
- administrative tasks;
- engineering services;
- magazine/design work;
- banking and IT-enabled services.

## Costs and Challenges

Globalisation has not benefited everyone.

| Group | Problem |
|---|---|
| Small producers | Competition from cheaper imports and MNC brands. |
| Workers | Temporary jobs, long hours, low wages, insecurity. |
| Informal units | Difficulty competing with large firms. |
| Local industries | Some shut down due to import competition. |
| Farmers in developing countries | May be hurt by subsidised imports from rich countries. |

# Unit 4H: Workers and Uncertain Employment

## Flexible Employment

To reduce labour cost, many companies hire workers temporarily or flexibly.

This means:

- no regular job security;
- no long-term contract;
- no benefits;
- unpaid leave;
- pressure to work overtime;
- dismissal when orders fall.

## Garment Worker Example: Sushila

Sushila worked in a garment export industry. Earlier she had permanent work, health insurance, provident fund, and overtime pay. After her factory closed, she found work far away as a temporary worker, earned less, worked longer, and had no benefits.

## Why Garment Workers Face Pressure

Large MNCs order garments from Indian exporters. These MNCs seek the cheapest goods to maximise profit.

Indian exporters try to cut costs. Raw material cost cannot be reduced much, so labour cost is cut.

Result:

- temporary workers;
- low wages;
- long hours;
- night shifts;
- overtime pressure;
- fewer benefits.

## Ravi's Capacitor Unit Example

Ravi produced capacitors in Tamil Nadu. After import restrictions were removed, imported capacitors became cheaper. His clients preferred imports. His production fell, workers reduced, and similar units closed.

This shows how small producers may suffer under global competition.

# Unit 4I: Fair Globalisation

## Meaning

Fair globalisation means globalisation that:

- creates opportunities for all;
- protects workers;
- supports small producers;
- prevents exploitation;
- shares benefits more equally;
- follows fair international trade rules.

## Role of Government

Government can make globalisation fair by:

| Action | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Enforce labour laws | Protect workers' wages, safety, and rights. |
| Support small producers | Help with credit, technology, marketing, and infrastructure. |
| Use trade barriers when necessary | Protect vulnerable sectors temporarily. |
| Negotiate at WTO | Demand fairer rules from developed countries. |
| Align with developing countries | Strengthen bargaining power. |
| Invest in education and skills | Help workers access better jobs. |
| Regulate MNCs | Prevent exploitation and environmental damage. |

## Role of People

People can influence fair globalisation through:

- campaigns;
- trade unions;
- consumer movements;
- people's organisations;
- public debate;
- pressure on governments;
- international solidarity.

## Main Conclusion

Globalisation is a reality. The key question is not simply whether to accept or reject it, but how to shape it so that workers, small producers, consumers, and future generations benefit fairly.

## Solved Exercise Guide

### 1. What is globalisation?

Globalisation is the process of rapid integration or interconnection between countries through foreign trade, foreign investment, technology, services, and MNC-led production.

### 2. Why did India remove trade barriers?

India removed many trade barriers around 1991 because the government believed Indian producers should compete globally and improve quality. Powerful international organisations also supported liberalisation.

### 3. How do MNCs set up or control production?

MNCs control production by:

- setting up factories;
- joint production with local companies;
- buying local companies;
- placing orders with small producers;
- using suppliers across countries;
- controlling price, quality, delivery, and labour conditions.

### 4. Difference between foreign trade and foreign investment

| Foreign Trade | Foreign Investment |
|---|---|
| Buying and selling goods/services across countries. | Money spent by foreign companies to buy assets or set up production in another country. |
| Example: Chinese toys sold in India. | Example: Ford setting up a plant near Chennai. |

### 5. How does foreign trade integrate markets?

Foreign trade allows goods to move from one country to another. Buyers get more choice, prices may become closer, and producers in different countries compete. Thus markets become connected.

### 6. Why is technology important for globalisation?

Technology reduces distance and cost. Better transport moves goods quickly, containers reduce handling cost, and ICT allows instant communication, e-banking, outsourcing, and coordination of global production.

### 7. What is liberalisation?

Liberalisation is the removal or reduction of government barriers on trade and investment. It allows businesses to import, export, and invest more freely.

### 8. Why is WTO criticised?

WTO is criticised because although it promotes free trade, developed countries often continue to protect their own producers, especially farmers, while developing countries are pressured to remove barriers.

### 9. Who benefited from globalisation in India?

Well-off consumers, MNCs, large Indian companies, skilled workers, IT services, and some local suppliers benefited through more choice, jobs, technology, and markets.

### 10. Who suffered from globalisation?

Small producers, workers in insecure jobs, informal units, and some local industries suffered due to cheaper imports, intense competition, temporary work, low wages, and loss of job security.

### 11. Explain "impact of globalisation has not been uniform."

Globalisation helped well-off consumers and large companies through choice, technology, and markets. But many small producers and workers faced competition, closure of units, temporary jobs, and low wages. Therefore, benefits were uneven.

### 12. How can globalisation be made fair?

Government should protect workers, support small producers, enforce labour laws, negotiate fairer WTO rules, use trade barriers if necessary, and invest in skills and public services. People can also campaign for fair rules.
