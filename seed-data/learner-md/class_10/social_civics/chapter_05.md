## How to Understand This Chapter

Democracy is not only about elections.

Citizens also influence government between elections through:

- protests,
- strikes,
- movements,
- petitions,
- pressure groups,
- public campaigns,
- media mobilisation,
- negotiations,
- social organisations.

This chapter explains the indirect ways in which people can influence politics.

The key idea is:

**Democracy grows through popular struggles because people constantly pressure governments to respond to their needs, rights, and grievances.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Democracy involves conflict

Different groups have different interests and viewpoints. Democracy requires balancing these demands.

### 2. Popular struggle shapes democracy

Important democratic changes often happen through protest, mass mobilisation, and organised struggle.

### 3. People influence politics indirectly

Not everyone forms political parties or contests elections. Many influence government through pressure groups and movements.

### 4. Pressure groups do not seek power directly

They try to influence policy without directly forming government.

### 5. Movements are often looser than pressure groups

Movements depend more on mass participation and flexible organisation.

### 6. Pressure politics can deepen democracy

When many groups are active, governments hear different interests and no one group can dominate fully.

# Unit 5A: Popular Struggle in Nepal

## Core Idea

Nepal's 2006 movement shows how people can struggle to restore democracy.

## Background

Nepal had won democracy in 1990 as part of the "third wave" of democracy.

The king remained the formal head of state, but real power was exercised by elected representatives.

King Birendra accepted the transition from absolute monarchy to constitutional monarchy.

In 2001, King Birendra was killed in a royal family massacre.

King Gyanendra became the new king and refused to fully accept democratic rule.

## Crisis

In February 2005, King Gyanendra:

- dismissed the Prime Minister,
- dissolved the elected Parliament,
- took advantage of weakness and unpopularity of the elected government.

## April 2006 Movement

The movement aimed to regain popular control over government from the king.

All major political parties in Parliament formed the **Seven Party Alliance (SPA)**.

The SPA called a four-day strike in Kathmandu.

The strike became an indefinite strike.

Participants included:

- SPA parties,
- Maoist insurgents,
- labour unions,
- indigenous people's organisations,
- teachers,
- lawyers,
- human rights groups,
- ordinary citizens.

## Mass Mobilisation

People defied curfew and came to the streets.

Security forces could not control gatherings of more than one lakh people almost every day.

On 21 April, protesters reached between three and five lakhs and gave an ultimatum to the king.

## Demands

The movement demanded:

1. restoration of Parliament,
2. power to an all-party government,
3. a new Constituent Assembly.

## Outcome

On 24 April 2006, the king accepted all three demands.

Girija Prasad Koirala became Prime Minister of the interim government.

The restored Parliament passed laws taking away most powers of the king.

SPA and Maoists agreed on electing a Constituent Assembly.

In 2008, monarchy was abolished and Nepal became a federal democratic republic.

In 2015, Nepal adopted a new Constitution.

## Learner Point

Nepal shows that democratic power can be restored when political parties, movements, organisations, and ordinary citizens mobilise together.

# Unit 5B: Bolivia's Water War

## Core Idea

Bolivia's Water War shows that popular struggles continue even after democracy is established.

## Background

Bolivia is a poor country in Latin America.

The World Bank pressured the Bolivian government to give up control over municipal water supply.

The government sold water supply rights in Cochabamba to a multinational company.

## Price Rise

The company increased water prices by four times.

Many people received monthly water bills of around Rs 1000 in a country where average monthly income was about Rs 5000.

## Protest Begins

In January 2000, an alliance of labour, human rights, and community leaders organised a four-day general strike.

The government agreed to negotiate, so the strike was called off.

But nothing changed.

## Escalation

When agitation restarted in February, police used brutal repression.

Another strike followed in April.

The government imposed martial law.

But people's power forced the MNC officials to flee the city.

## Outcome

The government accepted protesters' demands.

The contract with the MNC was cancelled.

Water supply was restored to the municipality at old rates.

This struggle became known as **Bolivia's Water War**.

## Learner Point

Bolivia shows that citizens can challenge government policy even when the government is elected, especially when policy harms ordinary people.

# Unit 5C: Lessons From Nepal and Bolivia

## Core Idea

Nepal and Bolivia were different struggles, but both show how democracy is shaped by popular mobilisation.

## Comparison

| Point | Nepal | Bolivia |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main issue | Restoration of democracy | Water privatisation |
| Target | King and monarchy | Elected government and MNC contract |
| Level of impact | Foundation of political system | Specific public policy |
| Main actors | SPA, Maoists, unions, citizens | FEDECOR, workers, farmers, students, citizens |
| Method | Strike, protest, mass mobilisation | Strike, protest, resistance |
| Outcome | Monarchy weakened and later abolished | Water contract cancelled |

## Shared Features

Both struggles:

- involved political conflict,
- depended on mass mobilisation,
- used public demonstration,
- involved organised groups,
- forced authorities to respond.

## Three Conclusions

### 1. Democracy evolves through popular struggles

Defining moments of democracy often involve conflict between those who hold power and those who demand a share in power.

These moments may happen during:

- transition to democracy,
- expansion of democracy,
- deepening of democracy.

### 2. Democratic conflict is resolved through mass mobilisation

Sometimes conflict is resolved through Parliament or courts.

But in deep disputes, institutions may themselves become part of the conflict, and resolution comes from people's mobilisation.

### 3. Mobilisation needs organisations

Spontaneous public anger becomes effective when organised by:

- political parties,
- pressure groups,
- movement groups,
- unions,
- community organisations.

# Unit 5D: Mobilisation and Organisations

## Core Idea

Popular struggles require organisations that coordinate people and demands.

## Organisations in Nepal

The Nepal movement was not led by one organisation alone.

Participants included:

- Seven Party Alliance,
- Nepalese Communist Party (Maoist),
- labour unions,
- federations of workers,
- indigenous people's organisations,
- teachers,
- lawyers,
- human rights groups.

## Organisations in Bolivia

The protest against water privatisation was led by **FEDECOR**.

FEDECOR included:

- local professionals,
- engineers,
- environmentalists,
- community leaders.

It was supported by:

- farmers who depended on irrigation,
- factory workers' unions,
- middle-class students,
- homeless street children,
- Socialist Party.

In 2006, the Socialist Party came to power in Bolivia.

## Direct and Indirect Political Participation

People can participate in politics in two broad ways:

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct participation | Contesting elections and forming government | Political parties |
| Indirect participation | Influencing government without seeking power directly | Pressure groups, movements, petitions, protests |

## Learner Point

Voting is not the only democratic activity. Citizens can influence government through organised pressure and collective action.

# Unit 5E: Pressure Groups and Movements

## Pressure Groups

Pressure groups are organisations that try to influence government policies.

They do not aim to directly control or share political power like political parties.

They are formed when people with common:

- occupation,
- interest,
- aspiration,
- opinion

come together to achieve a common objective.

## Movements

A movement also tries to influence politics, but it is usually more flexible and loosely organised than an interest group.

Movements depend more on:

- spontaneous mass participation,
- public campaigns,
- protests,
- loose networks,
- shared causes.

Examples:

- Narmada Bachao Andolan,
- Movement for Right to Information,
- Anti-liquor Movement,
- Women's Movement,
- Environmental Movement.

## Pressure Group vs Movement

| Basis | Pressure Group | Movement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Organisation | More organised | Looser and flexible |
| Membership | Often defined | Often broad and changing |
| Aim | Influence policy | Influence politics around a cause |
| Participation | Members act through organisation | Mass participation is important |
| Duration | Can be long-term and stable | Can be short-term or long-term |

# Unit 5F: Sectional Interest Groups and Public Interest Groups

## Sectional Interest Groups

Sectional interest groups promote the interests of a particular section of society.

Examples:

- trade unions,
- business associations,
- lawyers' associations,
- doctors' associations,
- teachers' associations,
- employees' unions,
- caste-based associations,
- religious organisations.

Their main concern is the well-being of their members, not society as a whole.

## Public Interest Groups

Public interest groups promote common or general interests.

They aim to help groups beyond their own members.

They are also called promotional groups.

Examples:

- human rights organisations,
- groups fighting bonded labour,
- environmental groups,
- groups campaigning for Right to Information,
- FEDECOR in Bolivia,
- BAMCEF in India.

## BAMCEF Example

BAMCEF stands for Backward and Minority Communities Employees Federation.

It is largely made up of government employees.

It campaigns against caste discrimination.

It addresses problems faced by its members, but its wider concern is social justice and equality for society.

## Comparison

| Basis | Sectional Interest Group | Public Interest Group |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main aim | Interest of one section | Common or collective good |
| Beneficiaries | Members of group | Wider society or disadvantaged groups |
| Examples | Trade union, business association | Human rights group, anti-bonded labour group |
| Scope | Narrower | Broader |

# Unit 5G: Movement Groups

## Core Idea

Movements can be issue-specific or long-term.

## Issue-Specific Movements

These movements seek one specific objective within a limited time frame.

Example: Nepalese movement for democracy.

It aimed to reverse the king's order and restore democracy.

Example: Narmada Bachao Andolan.

It began with the issue of people displaced by the Sardar Sarovar Dam on the Narmada River.

Its original aim was to stop dam construction.

Later it became a wider movement questioning large dams and the model of development based on them.

## Long-Term Movements

These movements seek broad goals over a long period and involve many issues.

Examples:

- women's movement,
- environmental movement.

There may not be one organisation controlling the whole movement.

Many organisations and campaigns work separately but share a broad objective.

## Umbrella Organisations

Sometimes movements have a loose umbrella organisation.

Example: National Alliance for Peoples' Movements (NAPM).

NAPM coordinates many people's movements working on specific issues in India.

# Unit 5H: How Pressure Groups and Movements Influence Politics

## Core Idea

Pressure groups and movements influence politics in many ways.

## Method 1: Public Support and Sympathy

They try to gain support by:

- information campaigns,
- meetings,
- public discussions,
- petitions,
- awareness drives,
- media coverage.

They try to influence the media to highlight their issues.

## Method 2: Protest Activity

They organise:

- strikes,
- demonstrations,
- dharnas,
- disruption of government programmes,
- public marches,
- boycotts.

Workers' organisations, employees' associations, and movements often use these tactics to force government attention.

## Method 3: Lobbying and Advertisements

Business groups may:

- hire professional lobbyists,
- sponsor expensive advertisements,
- influence public debate,
- contact decision-makers.

## Method 4: Participation in Committees

Some members of pressure groups or movements participate in official committees and advisory bodies.

This gives them a role in policy discussion.

## Method 5: Influence on Political Parties

Pressure groups and movements do not usually contest elections directly, but they influence political parties.

They may:

- support parties,
- oppose policies,
- raise new issues,
- provide leadership,
- shape party agendas.

# Unit 5I: Relationship Between Pressure Groups and Political Parties

## Core Idea

Pressure groups, movements, and political parties may relate in direct or indirect ways.

## Form 1: Pressure Groups Linked to Parties

Some pressure groups are formed or led by political party leaders.

Examples:

- trade unions affiliated to political parties,
- student organisations linked to parties.

Their leaders are often party activists.

## Form 2: Parties Grow Out of Movements

Sometimes movements become political parties.

Examples:

- Assam Movement led to Asom Gana Parishad.
- DMK and AIADMK in Tamil Nadu grew from a long social reform movement of the 1930s and 1940s.

## Form 3: Indirect Relationship

In many cases, pressure groups and parties do not have direct links.

They may:

- oppose each other,
- negotiate,
- exchange ideas,
- push parties to take up new issues,
- provide new leaders to political parties.

## Learner Point

The boundary between movements and party politics is not fixed. Movements can influence parties, and sometimes parties emerge from movements.

# Unit 5J: Is Their Influence Healthy?

## Core Idea

Pressure groups and movements can create problems, but overall they deepen democracy.

## Concerns

Some people worry that pressure groups:

- promote only one section's interest,
- are not accountable like political parties,
- may use money power,
- may hijack public debate,
- may influence government without facing elections.

These concerns are valid, especially when wealthy groups dominate public discussion.

## Why They Are Still Useful

Pressure groups and movements are useful because:

- they put pressure on rulers,
- they give ordinary citizens a voice,
- they counter the influence of rich and powerful groups,
- they remind government of neglected needs,
- they represent conflicting interests,
- they prevent one group from dominating society,
- they create a rough balance of power.

## Balanced Conclusion

Putting pressure on rulers is not unhealthy in democracy if everyone has the opportunity to do so.

Where different groups are active, the government hears many viewpoints and must accommodate conflicting interests.

# Important Case Studies

## Kittiko-Hachchiko Movement

In 1984, Karnataka government set up Karnataka Pulpwood Limited.

About 30,000 hectares of land was given almost free to the company for 40 years.

Local farmers used much of this land as grazing land.

The company planted eucalyptus trees for paper pulp.

In 1987, the Kittiko-Hachchiko movement began.

Meaning: "pluck and plant."

People plucked eucalyptus plants and planted useful local saplings.

This was a non-violent protest against loss of common resources.

## Land Rights Protest in Indonesia

In June 2004, about 15,000 landless farmers from West Java travelled to Jakarta.

They demanded land reform and return of farms.

Their slogan was "No land, No vote."

They threatened to boycott Indonesia's first direct presidential election if candidates ignored land reform.

## Green Belt Movement in Kenya

The Green Belt Movement planted millions of trees in Kenya.

Its leader Wangari Maathai linked environmental protection with democracy and people's rights.

The movement showed that environmental struggles can also be struggles against corruption, land grabbing, and political domination.

## Pressure Group vs Political Party

| Basis | Pressure Group | Political Party |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Aim | Influence policy | Capture political power |

| Power | Does not directly control government | Tries to form government |
| Membership | Often issue or interest-based | Broad political programme |

| Example | Trade union, FEDECOR | Congress, BJP, Socialist Party |

## Sectional vs Public Interest Group

| Basis | Sectional Group | Public Interest Group |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Whose interest? | One section | Wider public or disadvantaged groups |
| Benefit | Selective | Collective |

## Issue-Specific vs Long-Term Movement

| Basis | Issue-Specific Movement | Long-Term Movement |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Aim | Single issue | Broad goal |
| Time frame | Limited | Long-term |
| Organisation | Often clearer leadership | Loose network |
| Example | Nepal democracy movement, early Narmada Bachao Andolan | Women's movement, environmental movement |

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1: How do pressure groups and movements exert influence on politics?

Pressure groups and movements influence politics by organising campaigns, meetings, petitions, protests, strikes, dharnas, and demonstrations. They try to gain public support and media attention. Some groups lobby decision-makers or sponsor advertisements. Some members participate in government committees. They also influence political parties by raising issues, negotiating, opposing policies, and sometimes providing new leadership.

## Q2: Forms of relationship between pressure groups and political parties

Their relationship can take three forms. First, some pressure groups are formed or led by political party leaders, such as trade unions and student organisations affiliated to parties. Second, some political parties grow out of movements, such as Asom Gana Parishad after the Assam Movement. Third, many pressure groups and parties have indirect relations; they may oppose, negotiate, or influence each other.

## Q3: How are pressure groups useful in democracy?

Pressure groups are useful because they make governments listen to different sections of society. They counter the influence of rich and powerful groups, raise neglected issues, represent ordinary citizens, and create a balance of pressure. They deepen democracy by allowing people to participate between elections and by forcing governments to respond to public demands.

## Q4: What is a pressure group? Give examples

A pressure group is an organisation that tries to influence government policy without directly forming government. Examples include trade unions, business associations, teachers' associations, lawyers' associations, FEDECOR in Bolivia, BAMCEF, and human rights organisations.

## Q5: Difference between pressure group and political party

A political party contests elections and tries to form government. A pressure group does not seek to directly control political power; it tries to influence policy from outside. Parties have broad political programmes, while pressure groups usually focus on specific interests or causes.

## Q6: Fill in the blank

Organisations that promote interests of specific social sections such as workers, employees, teachers, and lawyers are called **sectional interest groups**.

## Q7: Special feature distinguishing pressure group from political party

Correct option: **(c) Pressure groups do not seek to get into power, while political parties do.**

## Q11: Mewat example

The Mewat example shows that movements can raise local demands, pressure political parties, and force the government to act. Social organisations demanded a separate district, created public awareness, and pushed political parties to support the demand before elections. The government then created the new district in July 2005.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Democracy only works through elections." | Movements and pressure groups also influence democracy. |
| "All protests are anti-democratic." | Peaceful protest is a normal democratic method. |
| "Pressure groups and parties are same." | Parties seek power; pressure groups influence power. |
| "Movements always have formal organisation." | Movements are often loose and flexible. |
| "Sectional groups are useless." | They represent real interests and balance other groups. |
| "Public interest groups help only their members." | They aim at wider collective good. |
| "Mass mobilisation means might is right." | Democratic mobilisation must connect with public reasoning, rights, and negotiation. |
