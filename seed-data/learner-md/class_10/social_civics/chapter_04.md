## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter continues the idea from the previous chapter:

**Social diversity does not automatically threaten democracy.**

But social differences can become inequalities when one group is treated as superior and another as inferior. In India, gender, religion, and caste are three major forms of social difference that influence politics.

The chapter does not say that politics should ignore these differences. If politics ignores injustice, the injustice remains hidden. But the chapter also warns that using identity only for domination, hatred, or vote-bank politics can damage democracy.

The key idea is:

**Democracy must allow disadvantaged groups to express their problems, but it must reject domination, discrimination, communalism, casteism, and gender inequality.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Gender division is social, not natural

Gender inequality is not simply based on biology. It is produced by social expectations, stereotypes, and unequal power.

### 2. Household work is political

If politics is about power, then male dominance in the household and unequal division of labour are political issues.

### 3. Religion and politics are not always a bad combination

Moral values from religion can guide politics, and religious communities can express needs. The danger begins when religion is used for domination.

### 4. Communalism is different from religious faith

Communalism uses religion to divide people into hostile political communities.

### 5. Secularism is a foundation of India

India has no official religion, protects religious freedom, prohibits discrimination, and allows state intervention for equality.

### 6. Caste has changed, but not disappeared

Urbanisation, education, and occupational mobility weakened caste hierarchy, but endogamy, untouchability, and caste-linked inequality continue.

### 7. Caste and politics affect each other

Caste influences elections, but politics also changes caste identities by forcing negotiation, coalition, and mobilisation.

# Unit 4A: Gender and Politics

## Core Idea

Gender division is a hierarchical social division. It exists almost everywhere, but it is often treated as natural.

The chapter argues that gender inequality is not natural and unchangeable. It is produced by social expectations and stereotypes.

## Public / Private Division

Boys and girls are often brought up to believe that women's main responsibility is:

- housework,
- child care,
- cooking,
- cleaning,
- washing,
- caring for family members.

Men are expected to do work outside the home.

This is called **sexual division of labour**.

## Important Clarification

It is not that men cannot do household work.

Many men work as cooks, tailors, cleaners, and care workers when these jobs are paid.

It is also not true that women do not work outside.

Women work in:

- fields,
- forests,
- water and fuel collection,
- domestic work,
- offices,
- factories,
- schools,
- professional jobs.

But women's work is often unpaid, invisible, and undervalued.

## Time Use Survey Example

A time use survey in six Indian states showed:

| Activity | Men | Women |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Income-generating work | 6 hours | 2 hours 40 minutes |
| Household and related work | 30 minutes | 5 hours |
| Talking / gossip | 1 hour 25 minutes | 1 hour 20 minutes |
| No work / leisure | 3 hours 40 minutes | 3 hours 50 minutes |
| Sleep, self-care, reading, etc. | 12 hours 25 minutes | 11 hours 10 minutes |

The survey showed that women worked longer overall, but much of their work was unpaid and invisible.

## Feminist Movements

Women in different parts of the world organised movements for equal rights.

They demanded:

- voting rights,
- equal legal status,
- education,
- career opportunities,
- equality in family life,
- equality in personal life,
- political representation.

These are called feminist movements.

## Gains Made

Political mobilisation around gender helped women enter fields earlier considered unsuitable for them.

Women now work as:

- scientists,
- doctors,
- engineers,
- lawyers,
- managers,
- teachers,
- political leaders.

In Scandinavian countries such as Sweden, Norway, and Finland, women's participation in public life is very high.

# Unit 4B: Patriarchy and Discrimination in India

## Core Idea

India has made progress, but it remains a male-dominated patriarchal society.

Women face disadvantage and discrimination in many ways.

## Main Areas of Discrimination

### 1. Education

The textbook states that the literacy rate among women is 54 percent compared with 76 percent among men.

Girls often perform as well as boys in school, but many drop out because families may prefer to spend resources on boys' education.

### 2. Work and Wages

Women are present in many forms of paid and unpaid work.

But:

- highly paid jobs have fewer women,
- women's unpaid work is not valued,
- women are often paid less than men for the same work.

The Equal Remuneration Act, 1976 says equal wages should be paid for equal work.

Yet wage inequality continues in fields such as:

- agriculture,
- factories,
- sports,
- cinema,
- informal labour.

### 3. Child Sex Ratio

In many areas, families prefer sons.

Sex-selective abortion has contributed to the decline of child sex ratio.

The textbook notes that the child sex ratio declined to 919 girls per thousand boys.

In some states, it fell below 850 or even 800.

### 4. Violence and Safety

Women face:

- harassment,
- exploitation,
- domestic violence,
- public insecurity,
- unsafe urban spaces,
- violence inside homes.

## Learner Point

Gender inequality is not only about laws. It is also about family practices, social expectations, economic valuation, safety, education, and representation.

# Unit 4C: Women's Political Representation

## Core Idea

Women's issues do not receive adequate attention unless women have a fair share in power.

## Representation in Legislatures

India has had low representation of women in legislative bodies.

Important facts from the chapter:

| Body | Women's Representation |
| --- | --- |
| Lok Sabha | Reached 14.36 percent of total strength in 2019 |
| State Assemblies | Less than 5 percent |
| Local governments | One-third seats reserved for women |

India remains behind many regions of the world in women's parliamentary representation.

Cabinets are often mostly male even when a woman becomes Chief Minister or Prime Minister.

## Panchayati Raj and Women

One-third of seats in local government bodies, including panchayats and municipalities, are reserved for women.

There are more than 10 lakh elected women representatives in rural and urban local bodies.

## Women's Reservation Act, 2023

The Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam, or Women's Reservation Act, 2023, provides 33 percent reservation of seats for women in:

- Lok Sabha,
- State Legislative Assemblies,
- Delhi Assembly.

## Why Representation Matters

More women representatives can:

- bring women's problems into public debate,
- influence policy,
- challenge male dominance,
- increase visibility of gender issues,
- make democracy more inclusive.

## Exam Point

Gender division is an example where political expression of social division can be positive because it helps disadvantaged groups demand equality.

# Unit 4D: Religion and Politics

## Core Idea

Religion and politics are not always a harmful combination. The problem begins when religion is used for domination and exclusion.

## Healthy Relationship Between Religion and Politics

Religion can enter politics in acceptable ways when:

- moral values guide politics,
- communities express genuine needs,
- government protects minorities,
- state reforms religious practices to prevent discrimination,
- all religions are treated equally.

## Examples

| Example | Why It Is Not Necessarily Wrong |
| --- | --- |
| Gandhi said religion cannot be separated from politics | He meant moral values common to religions, not rule by one religion. |
| Human rights groups demand protection of religious minorities | This supports equality and security. |
| Women's movements demand reform of family laws | This seeks gender justice within communities. |

## Family Laws

Family laws deal with:

- marriage,
- divorce,
- adoption,
- inheritance.

In India, different family laws apply to followers of different religions.

Women's movements argue that family laws of all religions should be made more equitable.

# Unit 4E: Communalism

## Core Idea

Communalism is the harmful use of religion in politics.

It begins when religion is treated as the basis of the nation and religious communities are seen as hostile political groups.

## What Communal Politics Believes

Communalism assumes:

- followers of one religion form one community,
- their interests are the same,
- differences within the community do not matter,
- people of different religions cannot belong to the same social community,
- different religions have conflicting interests,
- one religious group must dominate or form a separate nation.

## Why This Belief Is Wrong

People of one religion do not have identical interests in every situation.

Every person has several identities:

- class,
- region,
- gender,
- caste,
- language,
- occupation,
- political belief,
- personal experience.

There are many voices within every religious community.

To treat all followers of one religion as one political block suppresses internal diversity.

## Forms of Communalism

| Form | Explanation | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Everyday beliefs | Prejudices, stereotypes, belief in superiority of one's religion | Casual jokes or suspicion about another religion |
| Quest for dominance | Majority domination or minority separatism | Demand that one religious community should control the state |
| Political mobilisation | Use of religious symbols, leaders, fear, and emotional appeals for votes | Appealing only to voters of one religion |
| Communal violence | Riots, massacres, violent conflict | Partition riots; post-Independence communal violence |

## Learner Point

Communalism is not the same as being religious. Communalism is using religion to divide, dominate, or create hostility in politics.

# Unit 4F: Secular State

## Core Idea

India adopted the model of a secular state to fight communalism and protect equality.

## Constitutional Features of Indian Secularism

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| No official religion | Indian state does not give special status to any religion. |
| Religious freedom | People may profess, practise, and propagate any religion, or follow none. |
| No discrimination | Constitution prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion. |
| State intervention for equality | State may intervene in religious matters to ensure equality, such as banning untouchability. |

## Comparison

| Country | Religious Position |
| --- | --- |
| India | No official religion |
| Sri Lanka | Buddhism given special status |
| Pakistan | Islam has special status |
| England | Christianity has special status |

## Why Secularism Matters

Communalism threatens the very idea of India.

A secular Constitution is necessary, but not sufficient.

Communal prejudices must be challenged:

- in everyday life,
- in political debate,
- in elections,
- in media,
- in education,
- through law and public action.

# Unit 4G: Caste Inequalities

## Core Idea

Caste division is special to India and has both social and political effects.

## What Makes Caste Different?

All societies have social inequality and division of labour.

But caste system made occupation hereditary and gave it ritual sanction.

Members of a caste were expected to:

- practise similar occupation,
- marry within the caste,
- avoid eating with members of other castes,
- accept a hierarchy of higher and lower status.

## Untouchability

Caste system excluded and discriminated against "outcaste" groups.

They were subjected to untouchability.

## Reformers Against Caste Inequality

Important leaders and reformers who worked against caste inequality:

- Jotiba Phule,
- Mahatma Gandhi,
- B.R. Ambedkar,
- Periyar Ramaswami Naicker.

They advocated a society without caste inequality.

# Unit 4H: How Caste Has Changed

## Core Idea

Caste has changed due to social and economic transformation, but it has not disappeared.

## Factors Weakening Caste Hierarchy

| Factor | Effect |
| --- | --- |
| Economic development | New occupations and social mobility emerged. |
| Urbanisation | People from different castes interact in cities. |
| Literacy and education | Old caste beliefs are questioned. |
| Occupational mobility | Younger generations take up occupations different from ancestors. |
| Weakening of landlords | Old rural power structures became weaker. |
| Constitution | Prohibits caste-based discrimination. |

## What Continues?

Caste has not disappeared.

Continuing features:

- most people still marry within caste or tribe,
- untouchability has not ended completely,
- historical advantages and disadvantages continue,
- upper castes are over-represented in urban middle classes,
- caste remains linked to economic status.

## Social and Religious Diversity Facts

According to 2011 Census:

| Group | Share |
| --- | --- |
| Hindus | 79.8 percent |
| Muslims | 14.2 percent |
| Christians | 2.3 percent |
| Sikhs | 1.7 percent |
| Buddhists | 0.7 percent |
| Jains | 0.4 percent |
| Scheduled Castes | 16.6 percent |
| Scheduled Tribes | 8.6 percent |

The National Sample Survey 2004-05 estimated OBC population around 41 percent.

SC, ST, and OBC together account for about two-thirds of the country's population.

## Caste and Economic Inequality

Caste continues to be linked to economic status.

The textbook explains:

- upper castes are generally better off,
- Dalits and Adivasis are worse off,
- backward classes are often in between,
- every caste has some poor and some rich members,
- upper castes remain over-represented among the rich,
- lower castes remain under-represented among the rich.

# Unit 4I: Caste in Politics

## Core Idea

Caste affects politics in several ways.

## Forms of Caste in Politics

### 1. Candidate Selection

Political parties consider the caste composition of voters when choosing candidates.

They nominate candidates from different castes to gather support.

### 2. Government Formation

Parties try to include representatives from different castes and tribes when forming governments.

### 3. Appeals to Caste Sentiment

Candidates and parties may appeal to caste identity to gain votes.

Some parties are seen as favouring particular castes.

### 4. Democratic Mobilisation

Universal adult franchise and one-person-one-vote forced political leaders to seek support from groups earlier treated as inferior.

This created political awareness among disadvantaged caste groups.

## Why Caste Alone Cannot Determine Elections

Elections are not only about caste.

Reasons:

| Reason | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| No single caste majority | No parliamentary constituency has a clear majority of one caste. |
| Parties need wider support | Candidates must win more than one caste or community. |
| No caste votes as one block | No party gets all voters of one caste. |
| Multiple candidates | More than one candidate may belong to the same caste. |
| Ruling parties lose | If caste preference were fixed, sitting MPs and MLAs would not lose often. |
| Other factors matter | Party loyalty, class, gender, leader popularity, and government performance matter. |

## Learner Point

Caste matters in elections, but it does not decide everything.

# Unit 4J: Politics in Caste

## Core Idea

The relationship between caste and politics is two-way.

Caste influences politics, but politics also changes caste identities.

## How Politics Changes Caste

### 1. Castes Try to Become Bigger

Caste groups may include neighbouring castes or sub-castes to increase political strength.

### 2. Castes Enter Coalitions

Different caste groups negotiate with other castes and communities.

This creates dialogue and compromise.

### 3. New Political Categories Appear

Politics creates broader categories such as:

- backward castes,
- forward castes,
- Dalits,
- OBCs.

## Positive Role of Caste Politics

Caste politics can give disadvantaged communities:

- voice,
- representation,
- dignity,
- access to decision making,
- access to land,
- access to resources,
- access to opportunities.

Dalits and OBCs have gained better access to power through political mobilisation.

## Negative Role of Caste Politics

Exclusive focus on caste identity can be harmful.

It can:

- divert attention from poverty,
- ignore development,
- hide corruption,
- create caste tension,
- lead to violence.

## Balanced Conclusion

Raising caste injustice in politics can strengthen democracy.

But politics based only on caste identity is unhealthy.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1: Aspects of life where women are discriminated or disadvantaged

Women face discrimination in education, employment, wages, political representation, safety, and family life. Female literacy is lower than male literacy. Girls often drop out because families prioritise boys' education. Women do much unpaid household work and are paid less than men even for equal work. Child sex ratio has declined due to son preference and sex-selective abortion. Women also face harassment, domestic violence, and low representation in legislatures.

## Q2: Forms of communal politics with examples

Communal politics appears in several forms. First, it appears in everyday prejudices and stereotypes about other religions. Second, it appears as a quest for political dominance, such as majority domination or minority separatism. Third, it appears in political mobilisation using religious symbols, leaders, fear, and emotional appeals. Fourth, it takes the ugly form of communal violence, riots, and massacres, such as the Partition riots.

## Q3: How caste inequalities continue in India

Caste inequalities continue because many people still marry within caste or tribe, untouchability has not completely ended, and historical advantages and disadvantages continue. Groups that had access to education earlier are over-represented in urban middle classes, while Dalits and Adivasis remain more likely to face poverty. Caste continues to be linked with economic status and social opportunities.

## Q4: Two reasons caste alone cannot determine election results

First, no parliamentary constituency has a clear majority of one caste, so candidates must win support from several castes and communities. Second, no party gets all votes of one caste; people vote differently based on party loyalty, economic status, gender, leader popularity, and government performance. Also, ruling parties and sitting representatives frequently lose elections.

## Q5: Status of women's representation in India

Women's representation in Indian legislatures has been low. Women reached 14.36 percent in Lok Sabha in 2019, while their share in State Assemblies has been less than 5 percent. However, one-third of seats in local bodies are reserved for women, creating more than 10 lakh elected women representatives. The Women's Reservation Act, 2023 provides 33 percent reservation for women in Lok Sabha, State Assemblies, and Delhi Assembly.

## Q6: Two constitutional provisions that make India secular

India has no official religion. The Constitution gives all individuals and communities freedom to profess, practise, and propagate any religion or follow none. It prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion and allows the state to intervene in religious matters to ensure equality.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Gender division is biological." | It is mainly social, based on expectations and stereotypes. |
| "Household work is not economic work." | Household work is real work, though often unpaid and invisible. |
| "Feminism divides society." | Feminism demands equality and challenges domination. |
| "All use of religion in politics is communal." | Moral values and minority protection are not communal if all religions are treated equally. |
| "Secularism means hostility to religion." | Indian secularism means equal respect and no official religion. |
| "Caste has disappeared in cities." | Caste hierarchy has weakened, but caste still affects marriage, status, and opportunities. |
| "Elections are only about caste." | Caste matters, but party, class, gender, performance, leadership, and issues also matter. |
