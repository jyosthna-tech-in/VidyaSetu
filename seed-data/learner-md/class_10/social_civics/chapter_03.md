## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter asks a very practical question:

**Do social differences always divide people and damage democracy?**

The answer is no.

Every society has differences. People differ by language, region, race, religion, caste, gender, class, physical ability, occupation, and culture. These differences do not automatically become social divisions. A social division becomes serious when one difference overlaps with another and when groups begin to feel separated, unequal, or discriminated against.

Democracy does not remove social differences. Instead, it gives people a way to express their grievances, demand recognition, and fight injustice.

The key idea is:

**Social diversity is normal. It becomes dangerous only when differences become rigid, exclusive, unequal, and politically mishandled. Democracy is the best way to accommodate diversity when people, leaders, and governments act responsibly.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Social differences are normal

All societies have differences based on birth, choice, culture, religion, caste, class, gender, language, and region.

### 2. Every difference does not become a division

Differences can separate people, but they can also connect people across group boundaries.

### 3. Identities are multiple

A person is never only one thing. The same person may be a student, girl, Muslim, athlete, Delhi resident, sister, bus passenger, and Indian at the same time.

### 4. Overlapping differences create deeper divisions

When one kind of difference reinforces another, conflict becomes more likely.

### 5. Cross-cutting differences reduce conflict

When groups share some interests but differ on others, it becomes harder to divide society into two hostile camps.

### 6. Politics can worsen or heal divisions

Political competition can turn social divisions into conflict, but democratic politics can also give voice to marginalised groups.

### 7. Democracy is best for accommodation

Democracy allows peaceful, constitutional expression of demands and gives groups a chance to negotiate.

# Unit 3A: Mexico Olympics and the Civil Rights Movement

## Core Idea

The chapter begins with a famous act of protest at the 1968 Mexico Olympics.

Two African-American athletes, Tommie Smith and John Carlos, used the medal ceremony to protest racial discrimination in the United States.

## What Happened?

| Person | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Tommie Smith | US athlete; won gold medal in 200 metres race. |
| John Carlos | US athlete; won bronze medal in 200 metres race. |
| Peter Norman | White Australian athlete; won silver medal and supported the protest. |

During the medal ceremony:

- Smith and Carlos stood with bowed heads.
- They raised black-gloved clenched fists.
- They wore black socks and no shoes.
- Peter Norman wore a human rights badge.

## Meaning of the Symbols

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Raised clenched fists | Black Power and resistance against racism |
| Black gloves | Collective strength and protest |
| Bowed heads | Silent protest during the national anthem |
| Black socks with no shoes | Black poverty |
| Human rights badge | Support for racial equality |

## Civil Rights Movement

The Civil Rights Movement in the USA lasted from 1954 to 1968.

It aimed to end legal racial discrimination against African-Americans.

It was led by figures such as Martin Luther King Jr.

It used non-violent methods of civil disobedience against discriminatory laws and practices.

## Black Power Movement

The Black Power Movement emerged in 1966 and lasted till 1975.

It was more militant than the Civil Rights Movement.

It argued for strong Black assertion and was willing to support more confrontational methods, even violence if necessary, to end racism.

## Consequences for the Athletes

The International Olympic Association held Smith and Carlos guilty of making a political statement.

Their medals were taken back.

They faced criticism at home.

Peter Norman also suffered and was not included in the next Australian Olympic team.

But their action gained international attention for the struggle against racial discrimination.

## Learner Point

The Mexico Olympics protest shows that social divisions are not only private matters. Discrimination can become a political issue when people publicly demand equality and recognition.

# Unit 3B: Social Differences - Birth and Choice

## Core Idea

Social differences can come from birth or from choice.

## Differences Based on Birth

Many social identities are not chosen by us.

Examples:

- gender,
- race,
- caste,
- family,
- physical appearance,
- disability,
- community of birth.

We belong to these groups because we are born into them.

## Differences Based on Choice

Some differences are based on choices we make.

Examples:

- religion one chooses to follow,
- atheism,
- what to study,
- occupation,
- sport,
- cultural activities,
- political beliefs.

## Important Point

Every social difference does not create a social division.

Differences can divide people, but they can also unite people.

## Example: Smith, Carlos, and Norman

Smith and Carlos were African-American. Norman was white and Australian.

They were different in race and nationality.

But they were similar in another way:

- all were athletes,
- all opposed racial discrimination,
- all used the ceremony to support human rights.

So one identity separated them, but another identity united them.

## Multiple Identities

Every person has more than one identity.

Example:

A student named Imrana may be:

- a girl at home,
- a Muslim in terms of religion,
- a student of Class X,
- a member of a particular section,
- a kho-kho player,
- a resident of trans-Yamuna area,
- a sister,
- a daughter,
- a member of a biradari,
- an Indian citizen.

## Learner Point

Conflicts become sharper when people are forced to see themselves through only one identity. Democracy works better when people accept multiple identities.

# Unit 3C: When Does a Social Difference Become a Social Division?

## Core Idea

A social difference becomes a social division when it overlaps with other differences and creates inequality, discrimination, or separation.

## Overlapping Differences

Social division takes place when one social difference overlaps with another.

Example from the USA:

The difference between Blacks and Whites became a social division because African-Americans were more likely to be:

- poor,
- homeless,
- discriminated against,
- excluded from equal opportunities.

Example from India:

Dalits tend to face:

- poverty,
- landlessness,
- discrimination,
- social injustice.

Here caste difference overlaps with economic inequality and social discrimination.

## Why Overlapping Differences Are Dangerous

When one difference becomes more important than all others, people begin to feel they belong to separate and unequal communities.

This can create:

- resentment,
- injustice,
- social distance,
- political conflict,
- violence.

# Unit 3D: Cross-Cutting Differences

## Core Idea

Cross-cutting differences occur when social differences cut across one another.

This means people who are together on one issue may be on different sides on another issue.

## Why Cross-Cutting Differences Reduce Conflict

If groups are not divided into the same two camps on every issue, it becomes harder to turn difference into deep conflict.

People learn to cooperate with different groups in different contexts.

## Northern Ireland vs Netherlands

Both Northern Ireland and the Netherlands are predominantly Christian societies divided between Catholics and Protestants.

But the nature of division is different.

| Feature | Northern Ireland | Netherlands |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main religious groups | Catholics and Protestants | Catholics and Protestants |
| Class and religion | Overlap | Cut across each other |
| Catholics | More likely to be poor and historically discriminated against | About equally likely to be rich or poor |
| Protestants | More linked with political dominance | About equally likely to be rich or poor |
| Result | Conflict between communities | Easier accommodation |

## Lesson

Overlapping differences create deep social divisions and tensions.

Cross-cutting differences are easier to accommodate.

# Unit 3E: Diversity in Modern Societies

## Core Idea

Social divisions exist in most countries, whether small or large.

India is a large and diverse country.

Belgium is small but still has many communities.

Countries once seen as homogeneous, such as Germany and Sweden, have become more diverse because of migration.

## Homogeneous Society

A homogeneous society has similar kinds of people and few significant ethnic differences.

But in the modern world, migration changes societies.

## Migrants

Migrants move from one region or country to another, often for work or economic opportunity.

They bring:

- language,
- culture,
- religion,
- food habits,
- social practices,
- new identities.

## Learner Point

Most countries today are multicultural. The question is not whether diversity exists, but how societies handle it.

# Unit 3F: Politics of Social Divisions

## Core Idea

Politics and social divisions can be a dangerous combination, but they do not always produce conflict.

## Why It Can Be Dangerous

Democracy involves competition among political parties.

If parties compete only by using existing social divisions, they can turn social differences into political conflict.

This can lead to:

- tension,
- violence,
- disintegration,
- distrust between communities.

## Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland, a region of the United Kingdom, faced a long ethno-political conflict.

Population:

- 53 percent Protestants,
- 44 percent Roman Catholics.

Political positions:

| Group | Political Demand |
| --- | --- |
| Nationalists, mostly Catholics | Wanted Northern Ireland to unite with the Republic of Ireland. |
| Unionists, mostly Protestants | Wanted Northern Ireland to remain with the United Kingdom. |

Hundreds of civilians, militants, and security forces were killed.

A peace treaty was reached in 1998, after which armed struggle was suspended.

## Yugoslavia

In Yugoslavia, political competition along religious and ethnic lines led to disintegration.

The country broke into six independent countries.

## But Social Divisions in Politics Are Not Always Bad

In a democracy, it is natural that:

- parties talk about social divisions,
- communities voice grievances,
- political leaders promise representation,
- policies are made to help disadvantaged groups,
- voting preferences may reflect social identities.

This does not automatically break a country.

## Learner Point

Political expression of social divisions can be healthy when it gives marginalised groups a peaceful and constitutional way to demand justice.

# Unit 3G: Three Determinants of Outcomes

## Core Idea

The outcome of politics of social divisions depends on three major factors.

## Factor 1: How People Perceive Their Identities

If people see their identity as singular and exclusive, accommodation becomes difficult.

Example:

If people in Northern Ireland see themselves only as Catholic or only as Protestant, conflict becomes harder to solve.

If people see multiple and complementary identities, accommodation becomes easier.

Example:

Many Belgians see themselves as Belgian as well as Dutch-speaking, French-speaking, or German-speaking.

In India, people often see themselves as Indian as well as belonging to a state, language group, religion, caste, or community.

## Factor 2: How Political Leaders Raise Demands

Demands are easier to accommodate when they:

- stay within the constitutional framework,
- are not at the cost of another community,
- seek equality and recognition,
- allow negotiation.

Demands become dangerous when they are exclusive or deny another group's identity.

Example:

The demand for "only Sinhala" in Sri Lanka was at the cost of Tamil language and identity.

In Yugoslavia, leaders of ethnic communities made demands that could not be accommodated within one country.

## Factor 3: How Government Responds

Government response is crucial.

If rulers share power and accommodate reasonable demands of minorities, social divisions become less threatening.

If rulers suppress demands in the name of national unity, the result may be opposite.

Forced integration can sow seeds of disintegration.

Examples:

- Belgium accommodated diversity and remained united.
- Sri Lanka suppressed Tamil demands and faced conflict.

## Summary Table

| Determinant | Positive Outcome | Negative Outcome |
| --- | --- | --- |
| People's identities | Multiple and complementary | Singular and exclusive |
| Leaders' demands | Constitutional and negotiable | Exclusive and at others' cost |
| Government response | Accommodation and power sharing | Suppression and forced integration |

# Unit 3H: Democracy and Diversity

## Core Idea

Social diversity should not automatically be seen as a danger.

In democracy, political expression of social divisions is normal and can be healthy.

## Why Political Expression Can Be Healthy

It allows disadvantaged groups to:

- express grievances,
- demand justice,
- seek recognition,
- get government attention,
- participate in elections,
- organise peacefully,
- negotiate rights.

## How Multiple Divisions Can Reduce Intensity

When many social divisions exist, they may cancel one another out.

For example, a person may vote based on class in one election, language in another issue, and local development in another context.

This prevents one giant division from dominating everything.

## Democracy as the Best Method

People who are marginalised or discriminated against often have to fight injustice.

In a democracy, this struggle can take a peaceful and constitutional path:

- public campaigns,
- elections,
- petitions,
- movements,
- debates,
- representation,
- court action.

Sometimes struggles may become violent if injustice becomes extreme, but history shows democracy is the best way to fight for recognition and accommodate diversity.

# Important Comparisons

## Social Difference vs Social Division

| Basis | Social Difference | Social Division |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meaning | Difference between people or groups | Difference that creates separation, inequality, or conflict |
| Source | Birth or choice | Overlapping inequality and identity |
| Effect | May unite or divide | Often produces tension |
| Example | Different religions, regions, games, occupations | Race plus poverty and discrimination in the US |

## Overlapping vs Cross-Cutting Differences

| Basis | Overlapping Differences | Cross-Cutting Differences |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Meaning | One difference reinforces another | Differences cut across one another |
| Conflict risk | High | Lower |
| Example | Blacks in US more likely to be poor and discriminated against | Catholics and Protestants in Netherlands are both rich and poor |
| Result | Deep social division | Easier accommodation |

## Northern Ireland vs Netherlands

| Point | Northern Ireland | Netherlands |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Religious division | Catholic and Protestant | Catholic and Protestant |
| Class link | Overlaps with religion | Cuts across religion |
| Conflict | Serious and violent | Less intense |
| Lesson | Overlap deepens division | Cross-cutting reduces conflict |

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1: Discuss three factors that determine outcomes of politics of social divisions

The outcomes of politics of social divisions depend on three factors. First, it depends on how people perceive their identities. If identities are singular and exclusive, conflict becomes difficult to accommodate. Second, it depends on how political leaders raise demands. Demands within the constitutional framework and not at the cost of another community are easier to accept. Third, it depends on how governments respond. If governments share power and accommodate reasonable demands, divisions become less dangerous; if they suppress demands, conflict may increase.

## Q2: When does a social difference become a social division?

A social difference becomes a social division when it overlaps with other differences and creates inequality, discrimination, or separation between groups. For example, in the United States, race became a social division because African-Americans were often poor, homeless, and discriminated against. In India, caste becomes a division when Dalits face poverty, landlessness, and discrimination.

## Q3: How do social divisions affect politics? Give two examples

Social divisions affect politics because political parties may appeal to communities, promise representation, and raise demands of disadvantaged groups. This can be positive when it gives marginalised groups a voice, but dangerous when it creates conflict. For example, in Northern Ireland, religious and political divisions caused long conflict. In Belgium, power sharing accommodated linguistic divisions and helped the country remain united.

## Q4: Fill in the blanks

**Overlapping** social differences create possibilities of deep social divisions and tensions.

**Cross-cutting** social differences do not usually lead to conflicts.

## Q5: Statement NOT correct about democracy

Correct option: **(d) Democracy always leads to disintegration of society on the basis of social divisions.**

This is wrong because democracy can accommodate diversity through peaceful and constitutional methods.

## Q6: Correct statements

A is correct: Social divisions take place when social differences overlap.

B is correct: A person can have multiple identities.

C is wrong: Social divisions exist in small and large countries.

Correct option: **(b) A and B**

## Q7: Logical sequence

Correct sequence:

D. Some social differences may result in social divisions.

B. Social divisions of one kind or another exist in most countries.

C. Parties try to win political support by appealing to social divisions.

A. But all political expression of social divisions need not always be dangerous.

Correct option: **(a) D, B, C, A**

## Q8: Country that disintegrated due to religious and ethnic politics

Correct option: **(c) Yugoslavia**

## Q9: Martin Luther King Jr. speech

Martin Luther King Jr. was talking about racial division and discrimination against African-Americans. His aspiration was that people should be judged by character, not skin colour, and that all people should live as equals. His anxiety was that racial prejudice denied freedom and dignity. The speech is related to the Mexico Olympics protest because Smith and Carlos also protested racial discrimination and demanded equality for Black Americans.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "All social differences become social divisions." | Only some differences become divisions, especially when they overlap with inequality. |
| "Social divisions exist only in India." | They exist in most countries, including Europe and the US. |
| "Politics of social divisions is always bad." | It can be healthy when it gives voice to disadvantaged groups. |
| "Democracy removes diversity." | Democracy accommodates diversity. |
| "One person has only one identity." | People have multiple identities. |
| "Overlapping and cross-cutting differences are the same." | Overlapping deepens conflict; cross-cutting reduces it. |
| "Suppressing demands protects unity." | Suppression can increase conflict and cause disintegration. |
