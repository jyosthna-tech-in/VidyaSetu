## How to Understand This Chapter

This chapter begins with a simple but important democratic problem:

**If one group has more numbers, should it be allowed to rule in any way it wants?**

Democracy is not only about majority rule. A democracy must also respect minorities, regions, languages, cultures, and different social groups. If power is concentrated in one group, other groups may feel excluded. This can create conflict and even threaten national unity.

Belgium and Sri Lanka are used as contrasting examples.

Belgium recognised diversity and shared power among linguistic communities and regions. Sri Lanka followed majoritarian policies that favoured the Sinhala-speaking majority and alienated Tamil-speaking people.

The key idea is:

**Power sharing does not weaken democracy. It makes democracy more stable, fair, participatory, and legitimate.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Democracy is more than majority rule

Majority rule is important, but if the majority ignores minorities, democracy becomes oppressive.

### 2. Power sharing reduces conflict

When communities feel respected and represented, conflict is less likely to become violent.

### 3. Power sharing is morally democratic

People affected by power should have a voice in how that power is used.

### 4. Power can be shared in different ways

Power can be shared among organs of government, levels of government, social groups, political parties, pressure groups, and movements.

### 5. Belgium and Sri Lanka teach opposite lessons

Belgium shows accommodation. Sri Lanka shows the danger of majoritarianism.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Power Sharing | Distribution of political power among different organs, levels, groups, or parties so that no single group controls everything. |
| Democracy | System in which people are the source of political power and participate through representatives and institutions. |
| Ethnic Group | Social group based on shared culture, common descent, physical type, language, or cultural similarity. |
| Majoritarianism | Belief that the majority community should rule in any way it wants, ignoring minority wishes and needs. |
| Civil War | Violent conflict between groups within the same country, becoming intense like a war. |
| Accommodation | Adjusting political arrangements to respect different communities and reduce conflict. |
| Prudential Reason | Reason based on careful calculation of gains, losses, and practical outcomes. |
| Moral Reason | Reason based on what is right, fair, and democratic in principle. |
| Tyranny of Majority | Situation where the majority uses its power oppressively against minorities. |
| Horizontal Distribution | Sharing power among different organs of government at the same level, such as legislature, executive, and judiciary. |
| Checks and Balances | System in which each organ of government can check the others so that power is not unlimited. |
| Vertical Distribution | Sharing power among governments at different levels, such as central, state, and local governments. |
| Federal Government | System where power is divided between a general government and regional governments. |
| Community Government | Government elected by people of one language community, with power over cultural, educational, and language issues. |
| Reserved Constituency | Electoral arrangement that gives representation to weaker sections or specific groups. |
| Coalition Government | Government formed by two or more political parties that share power. |
| Pressure Group | Organisation that tries to influence government policy without directly forming government. |

# Unit 1A: Belgium - Diversity and Tension

## Core Idea

Belgium is a small European country with a complex ethnic and linguistic composition.

## Location and Size

Belgium has borders with:

- France,
- Netherlands,
- Germany,
- Luxembourg.

It is smaller in area than Haryana and has a population of a little over one crore.

## Ethnic Composition of Belgium

| Community / Region | Share and Language |
| --- | --- |
| Flemish region | 59 percent of population; Dutch-speaking |
| Wallonia region | 40 percent of population; French-speaking |
| German-speaking people | 1 percent of population |
| Brussels | 80 percent French-speaking; 20 percent Dutch-speaking |

## Why Belgium Had Tension

The French-speaking minority was relatively rich and powerful.

The Dutch-speaking majority received benefits of economic development and education later.

This created resentment and tension during the 1950s and 1960s.

## Brussels Problem

Brussels created a special problem:

- Dutch speakers were a majority in Belgium as a whole.
- But Dutch speakers were a minority in Brussels.
- French speakers were a minority in Belgium as a whole.
- But French speakers were a majority in Brussels.

This made simple majority rule difficult and dangerous.

## Learner Point

Belgium shows that a majority in one place may be a minority in another place. Democracy must handle this carefully.

# Unit 1B: Sri Lanka - Diversity and Majoritarianism

## Core Idea

Sri Lanka is an island country with Sinhala-speaking and Tamil-speaking communities. Its government adopted majoritarian policies after independence.

## Social Composition of Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka has about two crore people.

| Community | Share / Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Sinhala-speakers | 74 percent |
| Tamil-speakers | 18 percent |
| Sri Lankan Tamils | 13 percent; Tamil natives of Sri Lanka |
| Indian Tamils | Descendants of plantation workers brought from India during colonial period |
| Christians | About 7 percent; both Tamil and Sinhala |

Most Sinhala-speaking people are Buddhists.

Most Tamils are Hindus or Muslims.

Sri Lankan Tamils are concentrated in the north and east of the country.

## Sri Lanka After Independence

Sri Lanka became independent in 1948.

Sinhala leaders tried to secure dominance because they had a numerical majority.

The elected government adopted majoritarian measures.

## Majoritarian Measures

| Measure | Effect on Tamils |
| --- | --- |
| 1956 Act made Sinhala the only official language | Tamil language was disregarded. |
| Preferential policies for Sinhala applicants | Tamils felt discriminated against in universities and government jobs. |
| Constitution protected and fostered Buddhism | Tamil Hindus, Muslims, and Christians felt excluded. |

## Tamil Alienation

Sri Lankan Tamils felt:

- their language was ignored,
- their culture was not respected,
- they did not get equal political rights,
- jobs and educational opportunities were unequal,
- major political parties were insensitive to their interests.

## Tamil Demands

Tamil groups demanded:

- recognition of Tamil as an official language,
- regional autonomy,
- equality in education and jobs,
- protection of culture and language.

These demands were repeatedly denied.

By the 1980s, some organisations demanded an independent Tamil Eelam in northern and eastern Sri Lanka.

## Civil War

Distrust turned into conflict and then civil war.

Effects:

- thousands killed,
- families became refugees,
- livelihoods were lost,
- social and cultural life suffered,
- economic development was set back.

The civil war ended in 2009.

## Learner Point

Sri Lanka shows that majoritarianism may seem powerful in the short run but can damage national unity in the long run.

# Unit 1C: Accommodation in Belgium

## Core Idea

Belgian leaders chose accommodation instead of majoritarian domination.

Between 1970 and 1993, Belgium amended its constitution four times to create a power-sharing arrangement.

## Main Features of Belgian Model

### 1. Equal Ministers in Central Government

The Belgian Constitution says the number of Dutch-speaking and French-speaking ministers in the central government must be equal.

This prevents one community from dominating decisions.

### 2. Special Laws Need Support From Both Groups

Some special laws require support from a majority of members from each linguistic group.

This means no single community can make important decisions unilaterally.

### 3. Powers Given to State Governments

Many powers of the central government were given to state governments of the two regions.

These state governments are not subordinate to the central government.

### 4. Brussels Has Equal Representation

Brussels has a separate government in which both French-speaking and Dutch-speaking communities have equal representation.

The French-speaking people accepted equal representation in Brussels because the Dutch-speaking people accepted equal representation in the central government.

### 5. Community Government

Belgium has a community government elected by people of one language community:

- Dutch,
- French,
- German-speaking.

This government has powers over:

- culture,
- education,
- language-related issues.

## Why Belgian Model Worked

Belgium's arrangement is complicated, but it helped:

- avoid civil strife,
- prevent division of the country,
- respect linguistic communities,
- protect Brussels from conflict,
- keep Belgium united.

When the European Union developed, Brussels was chosen as its headquarters.

## Belgium vs Sri Lanka

| Belgium | Sri Lanka |
| --- | --- |
| Recognised diversity | Ignored minority demands |
| Shared power | Concentrated power in majority |
| Amended constitution for accommodation | Used majoritarian policies |
| Protected language communities | Made Sinhala the only official language |
| Avoided major conflict | Faced civil war |

# Unit 1D: Why Power Sharing Is Desirable

## Core Idea

Power sharing is desirable for two types of reasons:

1. prudential reasons,
2. moral reasons.

## Prudential Reason

Power sharing is good because it reduces the possibility of conflict between social groups.

This is practical because social conflict can lead to:

- violence,
- instability,
- distrust,
- division,
- breakdown of national unity.

## Why Majority Domination Is Risky

Imposing the will of the majority may seem attractive in the short run.

But in the long run, it:

- alienates minorities,
- creates resistance,
- weakens unity,
- increases conflict,
- damages both majority and minority communities.

The tyranny of the majority is not only oppressive for minorities; it can also harm the majority because it destabilises the whole country.

## Moral Reason

Power sharing is the spirit of democracy.

Democratic rule means power must be shared with people affected by that power.

People have the right to be consulted on how they are governed.

A legitimate government is one in which citizens participate and feel they have a stake.

## Prudential vs Moral Reason

| Basis | Prudential Reason | Moral Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Main concern | Better outcomes | Democratic principle |
| Focus | Stability and unity | People's right to participate |
| Why power sharing is good | Reduces conflict | It is the essence of democracy |
| Example | Belgium avoided conflict by sharing power | Citizens should be consulted because they are affected by government |

## Model Answer

Power sharing is desirable for both prudential and moral reasons. Prudentially, it reduces conflict between social groups and helps maintain political stability. The example of Belgium shows that power sharing can avoid civil strife. Morally, power sharing is the spirit of democracy because people affected by power have a right to be consulted and participate in government.

# Unit 1E: Forms of Power Sharing

## Core Idea

Modern democracies use many forms of power sharing.

## Form 1: Power Shared Among Organs of Government

Power is shared among:

- legislature,
- executive,
- judiciary.

This is called **horizontal distribution of power** because these organs operate at the same level.

## Why Horizontal Distribution Matters

No organ can exercise unlimited power.

Each organ checks the others.

This creates a balance of power.

## Examples

| Organ | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Legislature | Makes laws and represents people. |
| Executive | Implements laws and runs administration. |
| Judiciary | Interprets laws and checks unconstitutional actions. |

Example:

Ministers and officials exercise power, but they are responsible to Parliament or State Assemblies.

Judges are appointed by the executive, but they can check the executive or laws made by legislatures.

This is called **checks and balances**.

---

## Form 2: Power Shared Among Different Levels of Government

Power can be shared between:

- central or union government,
- state or regional governments,
- local governments such as municipalities and panchayats.

This is called **vertical distribution of power** because power is divided among higher and lower levels.

## Federal Division of Power

In a federal system, the constitution clearly lays down powers of different levels of government.

India has:

- Union Government,
- State Governments,
- local governments.

Belgium also gave powers to regional governments.

Sri Lanka refused meaningful autonomy to Tamil-populated regions, which deepened conflict.

---

## Form 3: Power Shared Among Social Groups

Power may be shared among different social groups, such as religious and linguistic communities.

Belgium's community government is a good example.

Power sharing among social groups may also include legal and constitutional arrangements for:

- minorities,
- women,
- Scheduled Castes,
- Scheduled Tribes,
- socially weaker sections.

Reserved constituencies in India are meant to give representation to groups that might otherwise feel alienated.

## Why This Matters

When diverse groups get a fair share in power, they feel included in the government.

This reduces alienation and strengthens democracy.

---

## Form 4: Power Shared Among Political Parties, Pressure Groups, and Movements

In a democracy, citizens must have freedom to choose among different contenders for power.

This creates competition among political parties.

In the long run, power is shared among parties representing different:

- ideologies,
- social groups,
- regions,
- interests.

## Coalition Government

Sometimes two or more parties form an alliance to contest elections.

If the alliance wins, they form a coalition government and share power directly.

## Pressure Groups and Movements

Groups such as:

- traders,
- businesspersons,
- industrialists,
- farmers,
- workers,
- social movements

can influence government decisions.

They may participate in committees or influence decision-making from outside.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1: Different forms of power sharing with examples

Power sharing in modern democracies takes four main forms:

| Form | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Among organs of government | Legislature, executive, and judiciary share power through checks and balances. |
| Among levels of government | Union, State, and local governments in India share powers. |
| Among social groups | Community government in Belgium; reserved constituencies in India. |
| Among parties and groups | Coalition governments; pressure groups influencing policy. |

## Q2: One prudential and one moral reason for power sharing with Indian example

Prudential reason: Power sharing reduces conflict and maintains stability. In India, federal sharing of power between Union and State governments helps accommodate regional diversity.

Moral reason: Power sharing is the spirit of democracy because people have the right to participate in decisions that affect them. Panchayati Raj institutions give local people a voice in local governance.

## Q3: Thomman, Mathayi, and Ouseph

The best conclusion is Ouseph's: every society needs some form of power sharing, even if it is small or does not have major social divisions.

Reason:

Democracy requires power sharing because power should not be concentrated in one hand. Even a society without deep ethnic divisions needs checks and balances among organs, sharing among levels, and competition among parties.

## Q4: Ban on speaking French in a Belgian town school

The ban is not in keeping with the spirit of Belgium's power-sharing arrangements. Belgium's model is based on respecting linguistic diversity and accommodating both Dutch-speaking and French-speaking communities. A ban on French would create exclusion and go against the principle of mutual respect.

## Q5: Prudential reason from Panchayati Raj passage

One prudential reason is that giving power to panchayats can reduce corruption and increase administrative efficiency. When people participate in planning and implementation, they can control schemes better and reduce the role of corrupt middlemen.

## Q6: Arguments in favour of power sharing

Correct arguments:

- A: reduces conflict among different communities,
- B: decreases the possibility of arbitrariness,
- D: accommodates diversities,
- F: promotes people's participation in government.

Correct option: **(a) A B D F**

## Q7: Belgium and Sri Lanka statements

Statement A is wrong because Belgium did not allow the Dutch-speaking majority to impose domination on the French-speaking minority.

Statements B, C, and D are correct.

Correct option: **(d) B, C and D**

## Q8: Matching forms of power sharing

| List I | List II |
| --- | --- |
| Power shared among organs | Separation of powers |
| Power shared among levels | Federal government |
| Power shared by social groups | Community government |
| Power shared by parties | Coalition government |

Correct option: **(c) B D A C**

## Q9: Two statements on power sharing

A is true: Power sharing is good for democracy.

B is true: It reduces possibility of conflict between social groups.

Correct option: **(b) Both A and B are true**

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Power sharing means weakening the country." | Power sharing can strengthen unity by reducing conflict. |
| "Democracy only means majority rule." | Democracy also means minority rights, consultation, and participation. |
| "Belgium's model is simple." | It is complicated but effective in accommodation. |
| "Sri Lanka was undemocratic because it held elections." | Sri Lanka was democratic electorally, but its majoritarian policies damaged equality and unity. |
| "Prudential and moral reasons are the same." | Prudential is about practical outcomes; moral is about democratic principle. |
| "Horizontal and vertical sharing are same." | Horizontal is among organs at same level; vertical is among different levels of government. |
| "Pressure groups form governments." | Pressure groups influence power but do not normally form government. |
