## How to Understand This Chapter

Political parties are among the most visible institutions in democracy.

People often criticise parties, blame them for corruption or division, and distrust them. Yet modern representative democracy cannot function without political parties.

Why?

Large societies need organisations that can:

- bring together different opinions,
- contest elections,
- form governments,
- make policies,
- run administration,
- oppose government,
- represent people,
- connect citizens with the state.

The key idea is:

**Political parties are imperfect but necessary. Democracy becomes better not by removing parties, but by reforming them and improving public participation.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Parties are necessary for representative democracy

Without parties, representatives would remain scattered and no one would be responsible for running the country.

### 2. Parties are partisan by nature

Parties stand for particular policies, interests, and viewpoints. This is why they are called parties.

### 3. Party systems differ across countries

No party system is perfect for all countries. It depends on history, social diversity, regional divisions, and election systems.

### 4. India has a multiparty system

India's diversity cannot easily be represented by two or three parties, so many parties compete and form alliances.

### 5. Parties face serious challenges

Internal democracy, dynastic succession, money and muscle power, and lack of meaningful choice weaken parties.

### 6. Reform requires law and public participation

Legal reforms help, but citizens, media, pressure groups, and party members must also push parties to improve.

# Unit 6A: Meaning of Political Party

## Core Idea

A political party is a group of people who come together to contest elections and hold power in government.

They agree on policies and programmes for society with the aim of promoting what they believe is the collective good.

## Why Parties Are Partisan

Different people have different ideas about what is good for society.

Parties try to persuade voters that their policies are better.

They seek power through elections to implement those policies.

This means parties represent a "part" of society.

A party is known by:

- which social groups it speaks for,
- which policies it supports,
- whose interests it upholds,
- what ideology it follows.

## Components of a Political Party

Every political party has three main components:

| Component | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Leaders | People who guide and represent the party. |
| Active members | People who work regularly for the party. |
| Followers | Supporters and voters who identify with the party. |

# Unit 6B: Functions of Political Parties

## Core Idea

Political parties fill political offices and exercise political power.

They perform several functions.

## Function 1: Contest Elections

In most democracies, elections are fought mainly among candidates put up by parties.

Candidate selection differs:

- in the USA, members and supporters may choose candidates,
- in India, top party leaders often choose candidates.

## Function 2: Put Forward Policies and Programmes

People have many opinions.

Government cannot handle every individual view separately.

Parties group similar opinions into basic policy positions.

Voters choose among these programmes during elections.

## Function 3: Make Laws

Formally, laws are debated and passed in legislatures.

But most legislators belong to parties and usually follow party leadership.

So parties play a decisive role in law-making.

## Function 4: Form and Run Governments

Parties recruit and train leaders.

They make them ministers.

The political executive comes from parties and takes major policy decisions.

## Function 5: Role of Opposition

Parties that lose elections play the role of opposition.

They:

- criticise government,
- point out failures,
- oppose wrong policies,
- offer alternative views,
- mobilise public opinion.

## Function 6: Shape Public Opinion

Parties raise issues and highlight problems.

They have members and activists across the country.

Many pressure groups are linked to parties.

Parties may also launch movements.

Public opinion often forms around positions taken by parties.

## Function 7: Provide Access to Government

Ordinary citizens often find it easier to approach a local party leader than a government officer.

Parties help people access:

- welfare schemes,
- government offices,
- local administration,
- political representatives.

## Functions Summary Table

| Function | Key Idea |
| --- | --- |
| Contest elections | Select candidates and compete for votes |
| Policies and programmes | Offer choices to voters |
| Law-making | Guide legislators |
| Government formation | Run administration |
| Opposition | Criticise and check government |
| Public opinion | Raise and shape issues |
| Access | Connect citizens to government |

# Unit 6C: Why Political Parties Are Necessary

## Core Idea

Modern democracies cannot function without political parties.

## Imagine Elections Without Parties

If every candidate were independent:

- no one could promise major policy change,
- government formation would be uncertain,
- representatives would be accountable only to local constituencies,
- no one would be responsible for the direction of the whole country,
- law-making would become disorganised.

## Representative Democracy Needs Parties

Large societies need representative democracy.

Representative democracy needs agencies that:

- gather different views,
- organise representatives,
- form responsible government,
- support or restrain government,
- make policies,
- justify policies,
- oppose policies.

Political parties fulfil these needs.

## Exam Conclusion

Political parties are a necessary condition for democracy because they organise elections, government, law-making, opposition, and public opinion.

# Unit 6D: How Many Parties Should a Democracy Have?

## Core Idea

Different countries have different party systems.

## One-Party System

Only one party is allowed to control and run government.

Example: China, where only the Communist Party effectively rules.

This is not a democratic option because people do not get a fair choice.

Democracy must allow at least two parties to compete and have a fair chance to come to power.

## Two-Party System

Power usually changes between two main parties.

Other parties may exist but only two have a serious chance to form government.

Examples:

- United States of America,
- United Kingdom.

## Multiparty System

Several parties compete for power.

More than two parties have a reasonable chance to come to power either alone or in alliance.

India has a multiparty system.

## Coalition and Alliance

In a multiparty system, parties may join hands to contest elections or form government.

This is called an alliance or front.

Examples from India:

- National Democratic Alliance,
- United Progressive Alliance,
- Left Front.

## Is Multiparty System Good or Bad?

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
| --- | --- |
| Represents many interests and opinions | Can appear messy |
| Fits diverse society | May create instability |
| Gives space to regional parties | Coalition management can be difficult |
| Strengthens federalism | Policy-making may need compromise |

## Important Understanding

A country cannot simply choose a party system like choosing a product.

Party systems evolve over time based on:

- social diversity,
- regional divisions,
- history,
- political culture,
- election system.

India's multiparty system exists because India's diversity cannot be absorbed by only two parties.

# Unit 6E: Political Participation and Trust

## Core Idea

People often distrust political parties, but they still participate in party politics.

Survey evidence shows:

- political parties do not enjoy much trust in South Asia,
- parties are among the least trusted institutions worldwide,
- participation in party activities is fairly high in India,
- party membership in India is higher than in many advanced countries,
- reported party membership has increased over recent decades,
- the proportion of people feeling close to a party has also increased.

## Learner Point

Distrust of parties does not mean parties are irrelevant. People criticise parties but still rely on them for political participation and representation.

# Unit 6F: National and State Parties

## Core Idea

In federal democracies, parties may operate nationally or mainly in one state.

## Recognised Political Parties

Every party must register with the Election Commission.

The Election Commission gives special facilities to large and established parties.

Recognised parties receive:

- unique election symbol,
- official recognition,
- other special facilities.

## State Party Criteria

A party is recognised as a State party if it:

- secures at least 6 percent of total votes in a State Legislative Assembly election,
- wins at least 2 seats.

## National Party Criteria

A party is recognised as a national party if it:

- secures at least 6 percent of total votes in Lok Sabha elections or Assembly elections in four states,
- wins at least 4 seats in Lok Sabha.

## Six Recognised National Parties

As per the 2023 Election Commission notification used in the current textbook, there are six recognised national parties.

## Aam Aadmi Party (AAP)

Formed on 26 November 2012 after the 2011 anti-corruption movement.

Main ideas:

- accountability,
- clean administration,
- transparency,
- good governance.

It formed governments in Delhi and Punjab.

It emerged as a significant force in Gujarat politics after the 2022 Assembly election.

In the 2019 Lok Sabha election, it secured one seat.

## Bahujan Samaj Party (BSP)

Formed in 1984 under Kanshi Ram.

Seeks to represent the **bahujan samaj**, including:

- Dalits,
- Adivasis,
- OBCs,
- religious minorities.

Inspired by:

- Sahu Maharaj,
- Mahatma Phule,
- Periyar Ramaswami Naicker,
- B.R. Ambedkar.

Main base:

- Uttar Pradesh,
- presence in neighbouring states.

In the 2019 Lok Sabha election, it polled about 3.63 percent votes and won 10 seats.

## Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP)

Founded in 1980 by reviving the Bharatiya Jana Sangh, formed by Syama Prasad Mukherjee in 1951.

Main ideas:

- strong and modern India,
- inspiration from ancient culture and values,
- integral humanism,
- Antyodaya,
- cultural nationalism or Hindutva.

Supports:

- full territorial and political integration of Jammu and Kashmir,
- uniform civil code,
- ban on religious conversions.

Expanded from north and west into south, east, north-east, rural areas.

Came to power in 1998 as leader of NDA.

In 2019, emerged as largest party with 303 Lok Sabha members.

Currently leads the NDA government at the Centre.

## Communist Party of India (Marxist) - CPI(M)

Founded in 1964.

Believes in Marxism-Leninism.

Supports:

- socialism,
- secularism,
- democracy,
- socio-economic justice.

Opposes:

- imperialism,
- communalism.

Strong support in:

- West Bengal,
- Kerala,
- Tripura.

Support base includes:

- poor,
- factory workers,
- farmers,
- agricultural labourers,
- intelligentsia.

Critical of new economic policies allowing free flow of foreign capital and goods.

Was in power in West Bengal for 34 years.

In 2019, won about 1.75 percent votes and 3 seats.

## Indian National Congress (INC)

Founded in 1885.

One of the oldest parties in the world.

Played a dominant role after Independence.

Under Jawaharlal Nehru, it sought to build a modern, secular, democratic republic.

Ideology:

- centrist,
- secular,
- welfare of weaker sections,
- welfare of minorities,
- new economic reforms with a human face.

Ruled at the Centre till 1977 and from 1980 to 1989.

Led UPA government from 2004 to 2019 according to the current textbook wording.

In 2019 Lok Sabha election, won 19.5 percent votes and 52 seats.

## National People's Party (NPP)

Formed in July 2013 under P.A. Sangma.

First political party from North East India to get national party status.

Believes in:

- diversity of India,
- different developmental challenges in different regions,
- education and employment for all,
- empowerment of all sections.

Formed government in Meghalaya and has presence in several North-Eastern states.

In 2019, secured one Lok Sabha seat.

# Unit 6G: State Parties

## Core Idea

State parties are often called regional parties, but they need not be regional in ideology.

Some state parties have national-level organisation but are successful mainly in certain states.

Examples:

- Samajwadi Party,
- Rashtriya Janata Dal.

Some parties are strongly linked with state identity:

- Biju Janata Dal,
- Sikkim Democratic Front,
- Mizo National Front,
- Telangana Rashtra Samithi.

## Importance of State Parties

Over the last three decades, state parties have become stronger.

Their rise has:

- made Parliament more diverse,
- forced national parties to form alliances,
- contributed to coalition politics,
- strengthened federalism,
- deepened democracy.

Since 1996, many state parties have participated in national coalition governments.

# Unit 6H: Challenges to Political Parties

## Core Idea

Political parties are necessary, but they face serious problems.

## Challenge 1: Lack of Internal Democracy

Power often concentrates in one or a few top leaders.

Problems:

- parties do not keep proper membership registers,
- organisational meetings are irregular,
- internal elections are not held regularly,
- ordinary members lack information,
- members cannot influence decisions,
- leaders make decisions in the name of the party,
- personal loyalty becomes more important than party principles.

## Challenge 2: Dynastic Succession

Because parties do not use open and transparent procedures, ordinary workers find it hard to rise.

Top leaders may favour family members or close associates.

This is unfair and bad for democracy because inexperienced people may gain power through family connections rather than public support.

## Challenge 3: Money and Muscle Power

Parties focus heavily on winning elections and may use shortcuts.

They may nominate candidates who:

- are rich,
- can raise large funds,
- can use muscle power,
- have criminal influence.

Rich people and companies that fund parties may influence policies.

This worries democrats everywhere.

## Challenge 4: Lack of Meaningful Choice

Parties must be different enough to offer real choice.

But ideological differences have reduced in many countries.

Examples:

- Labour and Conservative parties in Britain have become less different on major policy issues.
- In India, major parties have fewer differences on economic policy than before.
- Leaders often shift from one party to another.

This leaves voters with limited meaningful choice.

# Unit 6I: Reforms of Political Parties

## Core Idea

Political parties must be reformed, but reform is difficult because party leaders themselves must pass many laws.

## Reform Already Taken: Anti-Defection Law

The Constitution was amended to prevent elected MLAs and MPs from changing parties.

If an MLA or MP changes party after election, they can lose their seat.

Benefits:

- reduced defection,
- reduced switching for office or money.

Limitations:

- makes dissent within parties difficult,
- MPs and MLAs must often accept party leadership decisions.

## Reform Already Taken: Candidate Affidavits

The Supreme Court made it mandatory for candidates to file affidavits giving details of:

- property,
- criminal cases pending.

Benefit:

- public gets information.

Limitation:

- weak system for checking whether information is true,
- unclear impact on rich and criminal influence.

## Reform Already Taken: Election Commission Orders

Election Commission required parties to:

- hold organisational elections,
- file income tax returns.

Benefit:

- creates formal accountability.

Limitation:

- sometimes becomes a formality,
- unclear effect on internal democracy.

## Suggested Reforms

### 1. Regulate Internal Affairs

A law could require parties to:

- maintain member registers,
- follow their constitution,
- hold open internal elections,
- create independent dispute authorities,
- follow transparent procedures.

### 2. Reservation for Women

Parties should be required to give at least one-third tickets to women candidates.

There should also be a quota for women in party decision-making bodies.

### 3. State Funding of Elections

Government could support election expenses of parties.

Support may be:

- in kind, such as petrol, paper, telephone facilities,
- in cash based on votes secured in previous election.

## Caution About Legal Reform

Over-regulation can be counter-productive.

Parties may find ways to cheat the law.

Also, parties may not pass laws they dislike.

## Two Non-Legal Ways to Reform Parties

### 1. Public Pressure

Citizens, media, pressure groups, and movements can pressure parties through:

- petitions,
- publicity,
- agitations,
- campaigns.

If parties fear losing support, they may reform.

### 2. Join Political Parties

Parties improve when good citizens join them.

It is difficult to reform politics only by criticising from outside.

The problem of bad politics can be solved by more and better politics.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q1: Functions of political parties

Political parties perform several functions. They contest elections, put forward policies and programmes, make laws, form and run governments, act as opposition, shape public opinion, and provide people access to government machinery and welfare schemes.

## Q2: Challenges faced by political parties

Political parties face four main challenges: lack of internal democracy, dynastic succession, growing role of money and muscle power, and failure to offer meaningful choice to voters. These problems weaken parties and reduce public trust.

## Q3: Reforms to strengthen parties

Reforms include laws to regulate internal party affairs, compulsory membership registers, internal elections, independent dispute mechanisms, one-third tickets for women, quotas for women in party decision-making bodies, state funding of elections, affidavits by candidates, and public pressure through media, movements, and citizens. Good citizens should also join parties to improve them from within.

## Q4: What is a political party?

A political party is a group of people who come together to contest elections and hold power in government. They agree on policies and programmes and seek public support to implement them.

## Q5: Characteristics of a political party

A political party contests elections, tries to win political power, has policies and programmes, seeks to promote what it considers collective good, and has leaders, active members, and followers.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "Parties only divide people." | Parties organise views and make representative democracy possible. |
| "One-party system is efficient, so it is democratic." | Democracy requires free competition and fair chance to replace rulers. |
| "Multiparty system is always bad." | It can be messy but represents diversity. |
| "Recognised party means ruling party." | Recognition is given by Election Commission based on votes and seats. |
| "State parties are always narrow-minded regional parties." | Many have broader ideologies but succeed mainly in some states. |
| "Anti-defection law solves all party problems." | It reduces switching but also limits dissent. |
| "Legal reforms alone can fix politics." | Citizens must participate and pressure parties. |
