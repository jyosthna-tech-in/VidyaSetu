## How to Understand This Chapter

In the previous chapter, power sharing was discussed as a democratic principle. This chapter focuses on one major form of power sharing:

**power sharing among different levels of government.**

This is called **federalism**.

India is a vast and diverse country. It has many languages, religions, regions, cultures, social groups, and local needs. A single government sitting at the Centre cannot manage everything effectively or democratically. That is why power is divided among Union, State, and local governments.

The key idea is:

**Federalism allows a country to remain united while respecting regional diversity.**

## Big Ideas of the Chapter

### 1. Federalism is vertical power sharing

Power is divided between a central authority and regional units.

### 2. Federalism needs trust

Different levels of government must agree to rules and trust each other to follow them.

### 3. India is federal even without using the word "federation"

The Constitution calls India a "Union of States" but its structure follows federal principles.

### 4. Federalism is not only constitutional

Federalism succeeds because of political practice: linguistic states, language accommodation, coalition governments, and court protections.

### 5. India has three-tier federalism

India originally had Union and State governments. Later, Panchayats and Municipalities became the third tier.

# Unit 2A: What Is Federalism?

## Core Idea

Federalism is a system in which power is divided between a central authority and constituent units of a country.

Usually, a federation has two levels:

| Level | Main Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| National / Central Government | Matters of common national interest |
| State / Provincial Government | Day-to-day administration of states or provinces |

Both levels enjoy power independent of each other.

## Federalism vs Unitary Government

| Basis | Federal Government | Unitary Government |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Levels | Two or more levels of government | One level, or sub-units under Centre |
| Power | Constitution divides power | Central government controls power |
| State authority | States have independent powers | States or provinces are subordinate |
| Accountability | Each level answerable to people | Lower units answerable to Centre |
| Example | India, Belgium, USA | Sri Lanka, for practical purposes |

## Belgium and Sri Lanka Connection

Belgium shifted from a unitary system to a federal system in 1993.

Regional governments in Belgium received constitutional powers that could not simply be withdrawn by the central government.

Sri Lanka continues, for practical purposes, as a unitary system. Tamil leaders demanded federalism because they wanted regional autonomy.

## Learner Point

Federalism is not just about having provinces or states. The real test is whether those units have constitutionally protected powers.

# Unit 2B: Key Features of Federalism

## Core Idea

A federal system has certain essential features.

## Seven Features

| Feature | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| Two or more tiers | There are two or more levels of government. |
| Same citizens, different powers | Different tiers govern the same citizens but have separate jurisdictions. |
| Constitutional guarantee | Powers of each tier are specified in the Constitution. |
| No unilateral change | One level cannot change basic power-sharing provisions alone. |
| Courts as umpire | Courts interpret the Constitution and settle disputes between governments. |
| Financial autonomy | Revenue sources are specified for each level. |
| Dual objective | Federalism promotes unity while accommodating regional diversity. |

## Two Requirements for Federal Success

Federalism works when there is:

1. agreement on rules of power sharing,
2. mutual trust that each level will follow those rules.

## Dual Objective of Federalism

Federalism must do two things at the same time:

- safeguard and promote national unity,
- accommodate regional diversity.

## Exam Point

If an answer asks for "features of federalism," write the seven points clearly and mention that courts and the Constitution protect the division of power.

# Unit 2C: Two Routes to Federation

## Core Idea

Federations are formed in different historical contexts.

## Coming Together Federations

Independent states come together voluntarily to form a larger unit.

They pool sovereignty while retaining identity.

Purpose:

- increase security,
- strengthen unity,
- retain separate identities.

Examples:

- USA,
- Switzerland,
- Australia.

Features:

- constituent states usually have equal powers,
- states are strong compared to the federal government.

## Holding Together Federations

A large country decides to divide power between national government and constituent states.

Examples:

- India,
- Spain,
- Belgium.

Features:

- central government tends to be more powerful,
- constituent units may have unequal powers,
- some units may have special powers.

## Comparison

| Basis | Coming Together | Holding Together |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Formation | Independent states join | Large country divides power |
| Main purpose | Pool sovereignty and increase security | Maintain unity with diversity |
| State power | Usually equal and strong | Often unequal; Centre stronger |
| Examples | USA, Switzerland, Australia | India, Spain, Belgium |

# Unit 2D: What Makes India a Federal Country?

## Core Idea

India is federal because its Constitution divides power among different levels of government.

The Constitution declares India as a **Union of States**.

It does not use the word "federation," but the Indian Union is based on federal principles.

## Three Tiers in India

Originally, the Constitution provided two tiers:

1. Union Government,
2. State Governments.

Later, a third tier was added:

3. Local Governments: Panchayats and Municipalities.

## Three-Fold Distribution of Legislative Powers

The Constitution divides subjects into three lists:

1. Union List,
2. State List,
3. Concurrent List.

---

## Union List

The Union List includes subjects of national importance.

Only the Union Government can make laws on these subjects.

Examples:

- defence,
- foreign affairs,
- banking,
- communications,
- currency.

Why?

These matters need a uniform policy throughout India.

---

## State List

The State List includes subjects of state and local importance.

State Governments usually make laws on these subjects.

Examples:

- police,
- trade,
- commerce,
- agriculture,
- irrigation.

Why?

These matters are closely connected with local conditions and state needs.

---

## Concurrent List

The Concurrent List includes subjects of common interest to both Union and State Governments.

Both can make laws.

Examples:

- education,
- forest,
- trade unions,
- marriage,
- adoption,
- succession.

If Union and State laws conflict, the Union law prevails.

---

## Residuary Subjects

Some subjects were not known when the Constitution was written.

Example:

- computer software.

These are called residuary subjects.

In India, the Union Government has power to make laws on residuary subjects.

## Quick Classification Table

| Subject | List |
| --- | --- |
| Defence | Union List |
| Foreign affairs | Union List |
| Banking | Union List |
| Communications | Union List |
| Currency | Union List |
| Police | State List |
| Agriculture | State List |
| Irrigation | State List |
| Trade and commerce | State List |
| Education | Concurrent List |
| Forest | Concurrent List |
| Marriage | Concurrent List |
| Adoption | Concurrent List |
| Trade unions | Concurrent List |
| Computer software | Residuary subject |

# Unit 2E: Unequal Powers and Special Provisions

## Core Idea

All Indian states do not have identical powers.

India is a holding together federation, so some units may have special arrangements.

## Special Powers

States such as Assam, Nagaland, Arunachal Pradesh, and Mizoram enjoy special powers under Article 371 and related provisions because of special social and historical circumstances.

These provisions may protect:

- land rights of indigenous people,
- culture,
- local customs,
- preferential employment in some government services.

## Union Territories

Some areas are too small to become full states or cannot be merged with existing states.

Examples:

- Chandigarh,
- Lakshadweep,
- Delhi.

These are called Union Territories.

They do not have the same powers as full states.

The Central Government has special powers in running them.

## Changing Federal Power Sharing

The division of power between Union and States is basic to the Constitution.

Parliament cannot change it alone.

Any change must:

1. be passed by both Houses of Parliament with at least two-thirds majority,
2. be ratified by legislatures of at least half of the total states.

## Judiciary's Role

The High Courts and Supreme Court interpret the Constitution and settle disputes about power distribution.

This makes the judiciary an umpire in federal disputes.

## Financial Powers

Union and State Governments can raise resources by levying taxes to carry out their responsibilities.

Financial autonomy is necessary because a government without money cannot perform its duties.

# Unit 2F: How Federalism Is Practised in India

## Core Idea

Constitutional provisions are necessary but not enough. Federalism works because of democratic politics.

Three major practices strengthened Indian federalism:

1. linguistic states,
2. language policy,
3. changing Centre-State relations.

---

## Linguistic States

The creation of linguistic states was the first major test of Indian federalism.

After Independence, state boundaries were changed so that people speaking the same language could live in the same state.

Some states were created not mainly on language but on:

- culture,
- ethnicity,
- geography.

Examples:

- Nagaland,
- Uttarakhand,
- Jharkhand.

## Why Some Leaders Feared Linguistic States

Some national leaders feared that language-based states would divide India and encourage separatism.

The Central Government resisted the demand at first.

## What Actually Happened

The formation of linguistic states:

- made the country more united,
- made administration easier,
- gave language groups recognition,
- reduced alienation,
- strengthened federalism.

## Exam Point

Language-based states did not break India. They helped accommodate diversity inside the Union.

# Unit 2G: Language Policy

## Core Idea

India handled language diversity through accommodation, not imposition.

## No National Language

The Constitution did not give national language status to any one language.

Hindi was identified as the official language.

But Hindi is the mother tongue of only about 40 percent of Indians in the textbook discussion, and the 2011 Census records Hindi as mother tongue for about 43.63 percent.

Therefore, safeguards were needed for other languages.

## Scheduled Languages

Besides Hindi, 21 other languages are recognised as Scheduled Languages, making 22 Scheduled Languages in total.

Candidates for central government examinations may choose any of these languages.

States also have their own official languages.

Much government work is done in the official language of the concerned state.

## English and Hindi

According to the Constitution, use of English for official purposes was to stop in 1965.

Many non-Hindi speaking states demanded that English continue.

In Tamil Nadu, the movement against Hindi imposition became intense and violent.

The Central Government agreed to continue English along with Hindi for official purposes.

## Promotion vs Imposition

Promoting Hindi remains official policy, but the Centre cannot impose Hindi on states where people speak other languages.

## Why This Policy Helped India

India's flexible language policy helped avoid the type of language conflict seen in Sri Lanka.

## Important Language Facts from 2011 Census

| Fact | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Mother tongues recorded | More than 1300 |
| Major languages after grouping | 121 |
| Scheduled Languages | 22 |
| Hindi mother tongue share | 43.63 percent |
| English mother tongue share | 0.02 percent |
| People knowing English as second or third language | About 11 percent |

# Unit 2H: Centre-State Relations

## Core Idea

Federalism became stronger in India after political practice changed, especially after 1990.

## Early Period

For a long time, the same party ruled at the Centre and in most states.

State Governments often did not exercise their full autonomy.

When a different party ruled a state, the Central Government sometimes tried to undermine it.

The Centre sometimes misused constitutional provisions to dismiss state governments controlled by rival parties.

This weakened the spirit of federalism.

## Change After 1990

After 1990:

- regional parties became stronger,
- coalition governments emerged at the Centre,
- no single party often had a clear majority in the Lok Sabha,
- national parties needed regional allies,
- State Governments gained more respect.

## Coalition Era

Coalition government means a government formed by at least two political parties.

Coalition politics created a new culture of power sharing between national and regional parties.

## Supreme Court Support

A major Supreme Court judgement made it difficult for the Central Government to dismiss state governments arbitrarily.

This strengthened federal power sharing.

## Learner Point

Indian federalism became more effective not only because of the Constitution, but because politics became more regional, coalition-based, and respectful of state autonomy.

# Unit 2I: Decentralisation in India

## Core Idea

India needs power sharing below the state level because states themselves are large and diverse.

When power is taken from Central and State Governments and given to local governments, it is called **decentralisation**.

## Why Decentralisation Is Needed

Local people know:

- local problems,
- local needs,
- where money should be spent,
- how schemes should be managed.

Decentralisation helps:

- solve local problems,
- increase participation,
- build democratic habits,
- improve accountability,
- support local self-government.

## Before 1992

Panchayats and municipalities existed, but they were weak.

Problems:

- controlled by State Governments,
- elections not held regularly,
- no independent powers,
- inadequate resources,
- little real decentralisation.

## 1992 Constitutional Amendment

A major step was taken in 1992 to make local government more powerful and effective.

Main features:

| Reform | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Regular elections | Constitutionally mandatory for local bodies. |
| Reservations | Seats reserved for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Other Backward Classes. |
| Women reservation | At least one-third of all positions reserved for women. |
| State Election Commission | Independent body in each state conducts local elections. |
| Sharing powers and revenue | State Governments required to share some powers and revenue with local bodies. |

# Unit 2J: Rural Local Government - Panchayati Raj

## Core Idea

Rural local government in India is called Panchayati Raj.

## Gram Panchayat

Each village, or group of villages, has a gram panchayat.

It consists of:

- ward members, often called panch,
- president, called sarpanch.

They are directly elected by adult voters in the village or ward.

The gram panchayat is the decision-making body for the village.

## Gram Sabha

The gram panchayat works under the supervision of the gram sabha.

The gram sabha includes all voters in the village.

It must meet at least twice or thrice a year to:

- approve the annual budget,
- review gram panchayat performance,
- discuss village matters.

## Panchayat Samiti / Block / Mandal

A few gram panchayats together form a panchayat samiti, block, or mandal.

Members are elected by panchayat members of that area.

## Zilla Parishad

All panchayat samitis or mandals in a district together constitute the zilla parishad.

Members include:

- elected members,
- Lok Sabha members from the district,
- MLAs from the district,
- some officials of district-level bodies.

The zilla parishad chairperson is the political head of the zilla parishad.

---

# Unit 2K: Urban Local Government

## Core Idea

Urban local government exists for towns and cities.

## Municipalities

Municipalities are set up in towns.

They are controlled by elected bodies of people's representatives.

The municipal chairperson is the political head of a municipality.

## Municipal Corporations

Big cities have municipal corporations.

The political head of a municipal corporation is called the mayor.

## Rural vs Urban Local Government

| Rural Local Government | Urban Local Government |
| --- | --- |
| Gram Panchayat | Municipality |
| Gram Sabha | Ward or urban citizen bodies |
| Panchayat Samiti / Block / Mandal | Municipal Council |
| Zilla Parishad | Municipal Corporation in large cities |
| Sarpanch | Municipal Chairperson or Mayor |

---

# Unit 2L: Achievements and Limitations of Local Government

## Achievements

India's local government system is one of the largest democratic experiments in the world.

There are about 36 lakh elected representatives in panchayats and municipalities.

Benefits:

- deepened democracy,
- increased participation,
- increased women's representation,
- gave representation to SCs, STs, and OBCs,
- strengthened local self-government.

## Limitations

Problems remain:

- gram sabhas are not held regularly in many places,
- many State Governments have not transferred significant powers,
- local bodies often lack adequate resources,
- real self-government is still incomplete.

## Learner Point

Decentralisation is constitutionally stronger after 1992, but its success depends on actual transfer of powers, money, and participation.

# Unit 2M: Participatory Budgeting Example

## Porto Alegre, Brazil

Porto Alegre in Brazil combined decentralisation with participatory democracy.

Citizens participated in decisions about the city budget.

The city was divided into sectors like wards.

People attended meetings to discuss:

- local needs,
- city-wide issues,
- budget priorities.

About 20,000 people participated every year.

Effects:

- money was not spent only on rich colonies,
- buses reached poor colonies,
- builders could not evict slum dwellers without resettlement.

## Indian Example

Some areas in Kerala also experimented with ordinary people's participation in local development planning.

# NCERT Exercise Guide

## Q3: Similarity and difference between Indian and Belgian federalism

Similarity: Both India and Belgium divide power between central and regional governments to accommodate diversity.

Difference: Belgium has special community government based on language communities, while India has a three-tier system including Union, State, and local governments. India is also a holding together federation where the Centre is stronger.

## Q4: Difference between federal and unitary government

In a federal government, power is divided between central and regional governments, and each level has constitutionally guaranteed authority. For example, in India, Union and State Governments have separate powers. In a unitary government, power is concentrated at the Centre, and provincial units are subordinate. Sri Lanka is, for practical purposes, a unitary system.

## Q5: Two differences before and after 1992 amendment

Before 1992, local government elections were not held regularly and local bodies had little power. After the 1992 amendment, regular elections became constitutionally mandatory and local bodies received constitutional status. Also, seats were reserved for SCs, STs, OBCs, and women, and State Election Commissions were created.

## Q6: Fill in the blanks

Since the United States is a **coming together** type of federation, all the constituent States have equal powers and States are **strong** vis-a-vis the federal government. But India is a **holding together** type of federation and some States have more power than others. In India, the **Central** government has more powers.

## Q7: Language policy reactions

Best supported position: Sangeeta - the policy of accommodation has strengthened national unity.

Argument:

India did not impose Hindi on non-Hindi speaking states. English was continued along with Hindi for official purposes, and 22 Scheduled Languages are recognised. Linguistic states gave language groups recognition. This flexibility helped India avoid language conflict like Sri Lanka.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
| --- | --- |
| "India is not federal because the word federation is not used." | India is federal in structure because powers are constitutionally divided. |
| "Every country with provinces is federal." | Provinces must have constitutionally guaranteed powers. |
| "Federalism is only for large countries." | Belgium is small but adopted federalism to manage diversity. |
| "Linguistic states divided India." | They actually strengthened unity by accommodating language diversity. |
| "Hindi is India's national language." | India has no national language; Hindi is an official language. |
| "Local government existed only after 1992." | It existed before, but became constitutionally stronger after 1992. |
| "Union Territories have the same powers as states." | Union Territories have less power and more central control. |
| "Concurrent List means only Centre makes laws." | Both Union and State can make laws, but Union law prevails in conflict. |
