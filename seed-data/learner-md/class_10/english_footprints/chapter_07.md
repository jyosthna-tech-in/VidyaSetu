## Big Ideas

"The Necklace" shows how a desire to appear wealthy can destroy a life. Matilda wants admiration, elegance, jewels, and a life she believes she deserves. For one evening she gets that dream, but the borrowed necklace is lost.

Instead of confessing, Matilda and her husband replace it with an expensive diamond necklace. They spend ten years paying the debt. At the end, Matilda learns that the original necklace was false and worth very little.

The story's tragedy is not only the loss of the necklace. It is the loss of ten years because of pride and fear of truth.

# Unit 7A: Matilda Loisel

## What Kind of Person Is Mme Loisel?

Matilda Loisel is pretty and young, but deeply dissatisfied. She is born into a family of clerks and marries a clerk, but she believes she deserves luxury, admiration, and high society.

## Why Is She Always Unhappy?

She is unhappy because she compares her actual life with an imagined luxurious life.

| Her Real Life | Her Dream Life |
|---|---|
| Small apartment | Elegant mansion |
| Worn chairs | Fine furniture |
| Simple meals | Exquisite dinners |
| Ordinary clothes | Beautiful gowns |
| No jewels | Diamonds and ornaments |
| Clerk husband | Rich, distinguished social circle |

## Her Main Weakness

Matilda's weakness is not poverty alone. It is discontent.

She suffers because she constantly thinks of what she lacks rather than what she has.

## Her Rich Friend

Matilda has a wealthy school friend, Mme Forestier. She avoids visiting her because seeing wealth makes her feel even more miserable.

This shows that comparison deepens her unhappiness.

# Unit 7B: The Invitation

## M. Loisel's Good News

M. Loisel brings home an invitation to a grand official party at the Minister's residence. He expects Matilda to be delighted because such invitations are rare for clerks.

## Matilda's Reaction

Instead of joy, Matilda reacts angrily. She says she has nothing suitable to wear.

This reveals:

- her insecurity;
- her concern for appearance;
- her inability to enjoy opportunity without luxury;
- her fear of looking poor among rich women.

## What Kind of Person Is Her Husband?

M. Loisel is:

- simple;
- loving;
- patient;
- content with ordinary pleasures;
- willing to sacrifice;
- practical;
- loyal.

## The Dress

Matilda says a suitable dress would cost four hundred francs. M. Loisel has saved exactly that amount to buy a gun for hunting. He gives the money to Matilda instead.

This shows his generosity and his desire to make her happy.

## Husband and Wife Contrast

| M. Loisel | Mme Loisel |
|---|---|
| Content with simple food | Dreams of elegant dinners |
| Happy with invitation | Upset about clothes |
| Sacrifices savings | Wants social admiration |
| Practical and kind | Proud and dissatisfied |

# Unit 7C: The Necklace

## Fresh Problem

After getting the dress, Matilda becomes unhappy again. She has no jewellery.

She feels that without jewels she will look poor among rich women.

## Husband's Suggestions

M. Loisel first suggests natural flowers. Matilda rejects this because she thinks it will look humiliating.

Then he suggests borrowing jewellery from Mme Forestier.

## How the Problem Is Solved

Matilda visits Mme Forestier and asks to borrow jewels. Mme Forestier lets her choose from a jewel case.

Matilda chooses a superb-looking diamond necklace.

## Why the Necklace Matters Symbolically

The necklace symbolises:

- appearance;
- social status;
- borrowed glamour;
- false value;
- vanity;
- the danger of judging by surface.

It looks precious, but it is fake. This matches Matilda's desire to appear richer than she is.

# Unit 7D: The Ball and the Loss

## Matilda's Triumph

At the ball, Matilda is a great success. She is admired, noticed, and desired. For one evening, she lives the fantasy she has always imagined.

She feels:

- elegant;
- beautiful;
- victorious;
- joyful;
- admired.

## M. Loisel at the Ball

Her husband waits sleepily while she enjoys herself. He is not the centre of attention, but he supports her experience.

## Leaving the Ball

Matilda hurries away because she does not want other women to see her modest wraps. Her pride makes her avoid being seen in ordinary clothing.

This hurry may contribute to the necklace being lost.

## The Missing Necklace

At home, Matilda looks in the mirror and sees that the necklace is gone.

This is the story's turning point.

## What Do They Do Next?

They:

- search her dress and cloak;
- consider the cab;
- regret not noting the cab number;
- retrace the route;
- go to police and cab offices;
- put advertisement in newspapers;
- offer reward;
- write to Mme Forestier saying the clasp is broken.

The lie about the clasp gives them time.

# Unit 7E: Replacement and Debt

## Replacing the Necklace

After a week, they lose hope of finding it. They decide to replace it.

They find a similar diamond necklace valued at forty thousand francs and buy it for thirty-six thousand.

## How They Pay

| Source | Amount / Role |
|---|---|
| M. Loisel's inheritance | Eighteen thousand francs. |
| Loans | Remaining amount borrowed. |
| Usurers and lenders | High-interest debt. |
| Extra work | Husband works evenings and nights. |
| Household labour | Matilda does all domestic work herself. |

## Mme Forestier's Reaction

Mme Forestier is cold because the necklace is returned late. She does not open the box, so she does not discover the substitution.

Matilda is relieved at the time, but this silence leads to ten years of suffering.

## Ten Years of Hardship

Matilda's life changes completely.

| Before Debt | During Debt |
|---|---|
| Dreams of luxury | Lives in an attic |
| Wants servants | Sends away maid |
| Hates poverty | Performs hard household labour |
| Loves beauty | Becomes rough and aged |
| Avoids ordinary work | Carries water, washes clothes, bargains for food |
| Wants admiration | Lives in exhaustion |

## M. Loisel's Hardship

M. Loisel works evenings and does copying at night. He shares the burden quietly.

His loyalty is important: he suffers for a necklace he did not borrow for himself.

# Unit 7F: Final Revelation

## Meeting Mme Forestier

After ten years, Matilda meets Mme Forestier in the Champs-Elysees. Mme Forestier does not recognise her because Matilda has changed so much.

Matilda finally tells the truth.

## The Twist

Mme Forestier reveals that the original necklace was false and worth only about five hundred francs.

## Why the Ending Is Ironic

| What Matilda Believed | Reality |
|---|---|
| Necklace was diamond. | It was imitation. |
| Replacement had to be expensive. | Confession would have solved everything. |
| Ten years of debt were necessary. | They were unnecessary. |
| Appearance proved value. | Appearance was deceptive. |

## The Cost of Silence

Matilda and her husband lose ten years because they are afraid to confess. Their pride and fear cost more than the necklace.

# Unit 7G: Thinking About It

## 1. How Did the Necklace Change the Loisels' Life?

The necklace changes their life by pushing them into debt and poverty. They spend ten years repaying loans. Matilda loses youth, beauty, comfort, and dreams. Her husband loses savings and peace.

It also changes Matilda's character. She becomes hardworking, strong, and practical, but at terrible cost.

## 2. What Was the Cause of Matilda's Ruin?

Several causes combine:

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Discontent** | She cannot accept ordinary life. |
| **Vanity** | She wants admiration and social glamour. |
| **Pride** | She refuses to appear poor. |
| **Borrowing the necklace** | Creates risk. |
| **Losing it** | Immediate disaster. |
| **Dishonesty** | They do not confess. |
| **Fear of judgement** | They choose debt instead of truth. |

The deepest cause is prideful discontent.

## How Could She Have Avoided Ruin?

She could have:

- accepted natural flowers;
- worn no jewellery;
- borrowed something less valuable;
- been more careful;
- told Mme Forestier the truth immediately;
- checked the necklace's real value;
- lived with contentment.

## 3. What If She Had Confessed?

If Matilda had confessed, Mme Forestier would likely have told her the necklace was imitation and inexpensive. Matilda might have paid a small amount instead of ruining ten years of life.

Even if Mme Forestier were upset, the truth would have been less costly than the lie.

## 4. What Would You Do in a Similar Situation?

A wise answer:

- tell the truth immediately;
- apologise sincerely;
- offer to replace or pay for the lost item;
- ask the owner about its value;
- avoid taking loans without full information;
- learn from the mistake.

## 5. What Language Do the Characters Speak?

The story is written in English for readers, but the characters are French and likely speak French.

Clues:

- names like Matilda Loisel and Mme Forestier;
- abbreviations M. and Mme;
- currency francs and sous;
- Paris locations such as Champs-Elysees and Palais-Royal;
- French social setting.

## 6. Honesty Is the Best Policy

The story strongly supports this idea. One honest confession could have prevented ten years of suffering.

## 7. We Should Be Content with What Life Gives Us

The story also supports contentment. Matilda's misery begins before the necklace is lost because she despises her own life.

Contentment does not mean giving up ambition. It means not destroying peace by constant comparison and vanity.

## Final Chapter Summary

- Matilda Loisel is pretty but unhappy because she dreams of luxury.
- Her husband brings an invitation to a grand party.
- He sacrifices four hundred francs for her dress.
- Matilda borrows a necklace from Mme Forestier.
- She enjoys great admiration at the ball.
- On returning home, she discovers that the necklace is lost.
- The Loisels hide the truth and buy an expensive replacement.
- They spend ten years repaying the debt.
- Matilda becomes old, rough, and poor through hard labour.
- At the end, Mme Forestier reveals that the original necklace was fake and worth very little.
- The story teaches the cost of vanity, pride, and dishonesty.
