## Big Ideas

"Bholi" is a story about the power of education and kindness. Bholi begins life as a neglected girl mocked for her appearance and speech. Her family does not expect much from her. They send her to school not because they value her education, but because they think she has little chance of marriage.

At school, one teacher treats her with respect. That respect becomes the beginning of Bholi's confidence.

Years later, when a greedy bridegroom demands dowry after seeing her pock-marked face, Bholi speaks clearly and refuses the marriage. The girl once called a dumb cow becomes Sulekha, a person with dignity, courage, and voice.

# Unit 8A: Bholi's Childhood

## Who Is Bholi?

Bholi's real name is Sulekha. She is the youngest daughter of Numberdar Ramlal. Since childhood, people call her Bholi, meaning simpleton.

## Why Is She Called Bholi?

When she is ten months old, she falls from a cot and injures her head. The family believes this affects her brain. She grows up as a backward child and is treated as unintelligent.

## Smallpox and Stammering

At the age of two, Bholi suffers from smallpox. Her eyes are saved, but her body and face are marked by deep pock-marks.

She does not speak until she is five. When she finally speaks, she stammers.

## Effect of Mockery

Other children laugh at her and mimic her. As a result, she speaks very little.

This shows how ridicule can silence a child more than the speech problem itself.

## Family Treatment

| Other Children | Bholi |
|---|---|
| Sons are sent to city for education. | Bholi is neglected. |
| Daughters are considered marriageable. | Bholi is seen as a burden. |
| Good clothes are available. | Bholi gets old, unwashed dresses. |
| Healthy and strong. | Treated as weak, ugly, and foolish. |

## Why Ramlal Is Worried

Ramlal worries because Bholi has:

- pock-marked face;
- speech difficulty;
- lack of confidence;
- reputation for being simple;
- low marriage prospects.

His worry is not mainly about her happiness. It is about marriage and family reputation.

# Unit 8B: Sent to School

## Why Is Bholi Sent to School?

A girls' primary school opens in the village. The Tehsildar tells Ramlal that as a government representative, he must set an example by sending his daughters to school.

Ramlal's wife objects because she believes educated girls may not get husbands.

## The Unusual Reason

Bholi is sent because the family thinks she is least likely to be married anyway. Her mother says teachers can worry about her.

This is ironic: the family sends Bholi to school because they do not value her, but school becomes the place where she gains value in her own eyes.

# Unit 8C: First Day at School

## Bholi's Fear

When Ramlal says he will take her to school, Bholi is terrified. She remembers their old cow Lakshmi being taken away and sold. She fears she may also be removed from home permanently.

## What Makes Her Think School May Be Better?

Before going, she is:

- given a clean dress;
- bathed;
- given oil in her hair;
- treated with unusual attention.

Since she has rarely received care at home, she begins to think she may be going somewhere better.

## In the Classroom

Bholi sees:

- girls of her own age;
- rooms with children reading and writing;
- mats and slates;
- colourful pictures on the wall;
- animals in pictures that remind her of familiar village animals.

The pictures attract her and make the classroom less frightening.

## Does Bholi Enjoy Her First Day?

At first, no. She is frightened and cries when asked her name because she cannot speak clearly.

Later, yes. The teacher's kindness gives her relief and hope.

# Unit 8D: The Teacher's Role

## How Is the Teacher Different from People at Home?

| People at Home / Village | Teacher |
|---|---|
| Call her fool or simpleton. | Calls her gently by name. |
| Laugh at her stammer. | Encourages each attempt. |
| Neglect her appearance. | Gives emotional attention. |
| Assume she has no future. | Promises learning and respect. |
| Silence her. | Helps her speak. |

## Teacher's Method

The teacher:

- speaks softly;
- does not command harshly;
- asks her name patiently;
- praises partial success;
- encourages her to try again;
- tells her fear can be removed;
- gives her a picture book;
- promises progress through education.

## Why This Works

Bholi has been laughed at for speaking. The teacher creates a safe space where effort is praised. This helps Bholi attempt speech without fear.

## Hope at the End of the First Day

Bholi feels as if bells are ringing and trees have blossomed. This image shows inner joy. School gives her a new life.

## Teacher's Long-Term Impact

The teacher gives Bholi:

- confidence;
- education;
- speech practice;
- self-respect;
- courage to think;
- hope for a useful future.

Without the teacher, Bholi may never have spoken against Bishamber.

# Unit 8E: Marriage Proposal

## Why Do Bholi's Parents Accept Bishamber's Proposal?

They accept because Bishamber:

- is well-to-do;
- owns a shop;
- has a house;
- has money in the bank;
- initially asks for no dowry;
- is from another village and does not know about Bholi's pock-marks.

They ignore important concerns:

- he is much older;
- he limps;
- he has grown children from his first wife;
- Bholi's consent is not seriously considered.

## Unequal Match

The match is unequal because Bholi is young and Bishamber is old enough to be near her father's age. The parents accept because they fear no one else will marry her.

## Bholi's Initial Silence

Bholi hears the conversation but does not object at first. She is willing to marry him for the sake of her father's honour.

This shows duty and sacrifice, not weakness.

# Unit 8F: Bholi's Refusal

## Bishamber's Dowry Demand

During the wedding, Bishamber sees Bholi's pock-marked face. He demands five thousand rupees to marry her.

This reveals his greed and cruelty.

## Ramlal's Humiliation

Ramlal places his turban at Bishamber's feet, symbolising surrender of honour. He begs him not to humiliate the family.

Bishamber refuses to show kindness.

## Bholi Speaks

Bholi throws the garland into the fire and says clearly that she will not marry Bishamber.

This is the story's turning point.

## Why Does She Reject the Marriage?

She rejects it because Bishamber is:

- mean;
- greedy;
- cowardly;
- contemptible;
- willing to exploit her father's fear;
- demanding dowry after seeing her face.

## What This Tells Us About Her

Bholi has become:

- self-respecting;
- courageous;
- morally clear;
- educated enough to judge injustice;
- no longer afraid of public shame;
- able to speak for herself.

## The Clear Voice

Her speech has no stammer at this moment. This does not mean the speech difficulty magically disappears forever. It symbolises that courage and self-respect overcome fear.

## Her Decision for the Future

Bholi says she will serve her parents in old age and teach in the same school where she learned so much.

She does not define her worth by marriage.

# Unit 8G: Thinking About It

## 1. Bholi's Apprehensions About School

Bholi fears school because she does not know what it is. She thinks she might be taken away like the cow Lakshmi.

She begins to feel it may be better than home when she receives clean clothes, a bath, and attention.

## 2. Teacher's Role in Changing Her Life

The teacher changes Bholi's life by giving her:

- kindness;
- confidence;
- education;
- speech practice;
- human respect;
- a vision of future independence.

The teacher sees a person where others see a burden.

## 3. Why Does Bholi First Agree to the Unequal Match?

She agrees because:

- she respects her parents;
- she wants to protect family honour;
- she has been taught to obey;
- she knows society considers her difficult to marry.

## Why Does She Later Reject It?

She rejects it because Bishamber reveals greed and moral ugliness by demanding dowry.

Bholi can accept personal sacrifice, but she refuses injustice and humiliation.

## 4. Why Is She Called Sulekha at the End?

At the beginning, she is called Sulekha only formally, but society reduces her to Bholi, the simpleton.

At the end, she is called Sulekha again because she has reclaimed her identity. She is no longer the helpless girl people mocked. She becomes a thinking, speaking, self-respecting person.

## 5. Are Girl Children Treated Equally?

The story shows unequal treatment:

- sons get education;
- daughters are prepared for marriage;
- Bholi is neglected because she is considered less marriageable;
- family honour is placed above her choice;
- dowry pressure humiliates the bride's family.

The story argues for equal education and dignity.

## Beti Bachao Beti Padhao Poster Ideas

Poster slogans:

- Save the girl child. Educate the girl child.
- A daughter is not a burden; she is a citizen.
- Education gives every child a voice.
- Stop dowry. Support dignity.
- Equal rights, equal respect, equal future.

Poster elements:

- open book;
- girl raising her hand in class;
- family supporting a daughter;
- crossed-out dowry demand;
- school building and sunlight.

## 6. How Can We Change Social Attitudes?

We can:

- speak against dowry;
- support girls' education;
- avoid mocking speech or disability;
- include children who are different;
- respect consent in marriage;
- share household and education opportunities equally;
- challenge jokes that insult girls;
- encourage confidence in quiet children.

## 7. Should Girls Know and Assert Their Rights?

Yes. Girls and boys should have the same human rights, dignity, educational opportunities, duties, and privileges.

Human rights are not divided by gender. Society may treat genders differently, but justice requires equal respect.
