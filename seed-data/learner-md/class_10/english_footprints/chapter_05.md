## Big Ideas

Griffin is brilliant but irresponsible. He discovers how to make the human body invisible, but he uses this discovery for revenge, theft, violence, and escape.

The story asks an important question: is knowledge valuable if it is used without morality?

Griffin's invisibility gives him power, but it does not make him free. He becomes homeless, cold, hunted, and increasingly violent.

# Unit 5A: Opening Mystery

## Read and Find Out Questions

| Question | Short Answer |
|---|---|
| How did the invisible man first become visible? | He stepped in mud, so his footprints became visible. Later clothes and disguise also made his form visible. |
| Why was he wandering the streets? | He had removed his clothes to escape unseen after setting fire to his landlord's house. |

## The Footprints

Two boys see fresh muddy footprints appearing by themselves on the steps of a house in London. No person is visible. The footprints move down the street and gradually fade.

This opening creates mystery by showing an effect without showing the cause.

## Why the Footprints Disappear

The invisible man's feet are muddy at first. As he walks, the mud wears off, so the footprints become fainter and finally vanish.

## Science-Fiction Hook

The story begins with a strange image:

- visible footprints;
- invisible body;
- ordinary London street;
- impossible event treated as real.

That combination is typical of science fiction.

# Unit 5B: Griffin the Scientist

## Who Is Griffin?

Griffin is a scientist who discovers how to make the human body transparent and invisible. His body becomes like glass in visibility, though it remains solid.

## Scientific Brilliance

Griffin is brilliant because:

- he conducts repeated experiments;
- he discovers rare drugs;
- he proves invisibility is possible in the story's world;
- he turns scientific imagination into practical result.

## Moral Failure

Griffin is lawless because:

- he takes revenge on his landlord;
- he sets fire to a house;
- he steals clothes and food;
- he attacks a shopkeeper;
- he robs people;
- he terrifies Mrs Hall;
- he assaults the constable;
- he uses science for selfish survival and crime.

## Why He Becomes Homeless

His landlord dislikes him and tries to eject him. In revenge, Griffin sets fire to the house. To escape unseen, he removes his clothes. This leaves him invisible but naked, homeless, cold, and without money.

## Important Contrast

| Scientific Ability | Moral Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Brilliant experimenter | Criminal and violent |
| Makes body invisible | Cannot control temper |
| Understands science | Misuses discovery |
| Powerful knowledge | Weak ethics |

# Unit 5C: London Store and Theatre Shop

## The London Store

Griffin enters a big London store after closing time because it is winter and he needs warmth.

Inside, he:

- puts on warm clothes;
- becomes visible again through clothing;
- eats meat, coffee, sweets, and wine;
- sleeps on quilts.

## Why He Must Escape Again

He wakes too late in the morning. Store assistants arrive. To escape, he removes his clothes and becomes invisible again.

This leaves him naked in the cold January air.

## Drury Lane and Theatrical Disguise

Griffin goes to Drury Lane, the theatre district, hoping to find clothing and materials to disguise the empty space above his shoulders.

He gets:

- bandages;
- dark glasses;
- false nose;
- bushy side-whiskers;
- large hat;
- clothes.

These items make him appear like a strange but visible person.

## Violence Against the Shopkeeper

To escape unseen, Griffin attacks the shopkeeper from behind and robs him of money.

This shows that Griffin's crimes are becoming more direct and violent.

# Unit 5D: Iping Inn

## Arrival at Iping

Griffin travels by train to Iping, a village, and books two rooms at the local inn. A stranger arriving in winter already attracts attention. His strange appearance makes people talk even more.

## Why Mrs Hall Finds Him Eccentric

Mrs Hall finds Griffin eccentric because:

- he has an unusual covered appearance;
- he wears bandages, dark glasses, false nose, whiskers, and hat;
- he refuses friendly conversation;
- he wants solitude;
- he says an accident affected his face;
- he has an irritable temper;
- he keeps his door locked;
- his behaviour is secretive and rude.

## Why She Tolerates Him

Mrs Hall tolerates Griffin because he pays in advance. Money makes her overlook his strange habits for a while.

## Money Runs Out

The stolen money does not last. Griffin says he is expecting a cheque, but soon suspicious events occur.

## The Clergyman's Study

Very early one morning, a clergyman and his wife hear noises in the study. They hear money being taken from the desk. When the clergyman opens the door, the room seems empty.

The money is missing.

This is possible because Griffin is invisible.

# Unit 5E: Invisible Violence at the Inn

## Mrs Hall's Furniture

Mrs Hall and her husband enter Griffin's room when it appears empty. They see his clothes and bandages lying around.

Then strange things happen:

- Mrs Hall hears a sniff near her ear;
- a hat flies into her face;
- a chair seems to come alive;
- the chair pushes them out;
- the door seems to slam and lock by itself.

Actually, Griffin is invisible and moving the objects.

## Why People Think of Witchcraft

The villagers do not understand invisibility. They explain the strange events through superstition and think of spirits or witchcraft.

This shows how people react when science appears like magic.

## Suspicion Grows

When news of the clergyman's stolen money spreads, suspicion falls on Griffin because:

- he had recently admitted having no money;
- he suddenly has cash;
- strange events occur around him;
- he behaves secretly.

## Griffin Reveals Himself

When Mrs Hall questions him, Griffin becomes furious. He removes his bandages, whiskers, spectacles, and false nose.

The people see a headless man.

## Constable Jaffers

Jaffers arrives to arrest Griffin. He is surprised, but he tries to do his duty. He believes that if there is a warrant, the person must be arrested whether or not he has a visible head.

## Escape

Griffin removes more clothing and becomes harder to see. Jaffers struggles with an invisible person. People are hit by unseen blows. Jaffers is knocked unconscious, and Griffin escapes.

# Unit 5F: Thinking About It

## 1. "Griffin Was Rather a Lawless Person"

This statement is correct.

Evidence:

| Action | Why It Is Lawless |
|---|---|
| Sets fire to landlord's house | Revenge and destruction of property. |
| Steals from London store | Takes clothes and food without paying. |
| Attacks shopkeeper | Uses violence. |
| Robs shopkeeper | Steals money. |
| Steals clergyman's money | Commits burglary. |
| Frightens Mrs Hall | Misuses invisibility to terrorise. |
| Assaults Jaffers | Resists arrest violently. |

Griffin's lawlessness is repeated, not accidental.

## 2. How Would You Assess Griffin as a Scientist?

Balanced answer:

Griffin is scientifically brilliant but morally dangerous. His discovery is extraordinary, but he lacks responsibility, compassion, and self-control.

| As Scientist | As Human Being |
|---|---|
| Brilliant | Selfish |
| Innovative | Violent |
| Determined | Lawless |
| Successful experimenter | Socially irresponsible |
| Imaginative | Ethically weak |

## Why Science Needs Ethics

Science gives power. Ethics guides how power should be used.

Without ethics, discovery can become:

- a tool for crime;
- a method of domination;
- a danger to society;
- a way to escape responsibility.

# Unit 5G: Talk About It

## Would You Like to Become Invisible?

Possible advantages:

- observe nature without disturbing it;
- escape danger;
- study people or animals secretly;
- perform rescue work;
- avoid crowds.

Possible disadvantages:

- loneliness;
- suspicion;
- temptation to misuse power;
- difficulty eating, dressing, or living normally;
- danger in traffic or crowds;
- loss of identity;
- legal and moral problems.

## Invisible Forces Around Us

Some forces or aspects of matter are invisible to the naked eye.

| Invisible Thing | How We Know It Exists |
|---|---|
| **Magnetism** | It moves iron objects and affects compass needles. |
| **Gravity** | Objects fall; planets orbit. |
| **Electric current** | Devices work, bulbs glow, shocks occur. |
| **Air** | We feel wind and need oxygen. |
| **Germs** | Seen through microscopes; cause disease. |
| **Atoms and molecules** | Studied through scientific instruments and effects. |

## What If We Could See Invisible Forces?

The world might look crowded with lines of force, air currents, microbes, heat patterns, and radiation. It could help science and safety, but it might also overwhelm ordinary vision.

## Can a Human Become Transparent?

In real science, full human invisibility is far more complicated than the story suggests. A transparent body would create problems:

- eyes need to absorb light to see;
- blood, bones, and organs have different optical properties;
- clothing and food would remain visible unless also treated;
- biological processes would be affected.

Science fiction imagines possibilities beyond current reality. Sometimes such imagination inspires future science, but Griffin's invisibility remains fictional.

## Final Chapter Summary

- Two boys see muddy footprints without a visible person.
- The footprints belong to Griffin, a scientist who has made himself invisible.
- Griffin is brilliant but lawless.
- He sets fire to his landlord's house and escapes without clothes.
- He enters a London store for warmth, food, and clothing.
- He later steals a theatrical disguise and attacks a shopkeeper.
- At Iping, Mrs Hall finds him strange but tolerates him because he pays.
- Griffin steals the clergyman's money and frightens the Halls with invisible actions.
- He reveals himself as a headless man by removing his disguise.
- Constable Jaffers tries to arrest him, but Griffin becomes fully invisible and escapes.
- The story warns that scientific power without ethics becomes dangerous.
