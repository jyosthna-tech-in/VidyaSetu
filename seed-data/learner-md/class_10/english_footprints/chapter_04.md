## Big Ideas

The story tests two sayings:

- You must set a thief to catch a thief.
- There is honour among thieves.

The story supports the first more than the second. The young lady, who is herself a thief, understands Horace's situation and uses it to trap him. There is no honour among them; she deceives him and escapes with the jewels while he is punished.

Horace's biggest error is not technical. He knows locks and plans carefully. His error is moral and psychological: he trusts someone because she appears confident and respectable.

# Unit 4A: Before Reading

## The Two Sayings

| Saying | Meaning | Connection to Story |
|---|---|---|
| **Set a thief to catch a thief** | A criminal may understand another criminal's methods. | The young lady, a thief, outwits Horace. |
| **Honour among thieves** | Thieves may be loyal to one another. | The story disproves this; the lady betrays Horace. |

## Opening Question

Which saying does the story illustrate?

Best answer: It mainly illustrates **set a thief to catch a thief**, because another thief tricks Horace more effectively than the police could at first.

It also rejects **honour among thieves**, because the lady uses Horace and lets him take the blame.

# Unit 4B: Horace Danby

## Public Image vs Secret Life

| Public Image | Secret Reality |
|---|---|
| Good citizen | Annual burglar. |
| Respectable businessman | Opens safes and steals jewels. |
| Lock-maker with helpers | Uses lock knowledge for crime. |
| Lover of books | Steals to buy rare books. |
| Unmarried, orderly life | Secret criminal routine. |

## Why Horace Steals Every Year

Horace loves rare and expensive books. He steals once a year, sells stolen goods, and uses the money to buy books through an agent.

He does not steal randomly every day. He plans carefully, steals enough for a year, and returns to respectable life.

## Why He Is Not a Typical Thief

Horace is not a typical thief because:

- he is socially respectable;
- he has a successful business;
- he steals only once a year;
- he steals for books, not luxury;
- he plans carefully;
- he dislikes violence;
- he hates prison;
- he thinks of himself as different from ordinary criminals.

But he is still a thief. His refined taste does not make theft honest.

# Unit 4C: Shotover Grange

## Horace's Planning

Horace studies Shotover Grange for two weeks.

He observes:

- rooms;
- electric wiring;
- paths;
- garden;
- servants' routine;
- location of the safe;
- kitchen-door key;
- burglar alarm.

This shows that he is meticulous.

## Why the Target Attracts Him

There are jewels worth about fifteen thousand pounds in the safe. Horace expects to sell them gradually and get enough money to buy rare books coming up for sale.

## Entry

Horace uses the kitchen-door key that the housekeeper leaves outside. He wears gloves to avoid fingerprints.

## Sherry the Dog

A small dog named Sherry is in the kitchen. Horace keeps it quiet by calling it by name and showing affection.

This detail shows:

- Horace has studied the house carefully;
- he understands animals;
- he avoids noise;
- he is calm and experienced.

## Hay Fever

The flowers in the drawing room trigger Horace's hay fever. He sneezes, which draws attention to him.

This is one of the accidents that helps the young lady catch him in conversation.

# Unit 4D: The Young Lady

## Who Speaks to Horace?

A young woman dressed in red speaks to Horace from the doorway. Horace assumes she is the lady of the house.

This assumption is wrong.

## How She Appears Convincing

| Her Action | Why Horace Believes Her |
|---|---|
| Speaks calmly and firmly | Sounds like someone who has authority in the house. |
| Knows the dog's name | Makes her seem connected to the household. |
| Straightens ornaments | Behaves like someone familiar with the room. |
| Mentions returning unexpectedly | Explains why she is there. |
| Talks about her husband | Supports the idea that she is the owner's wife. |
| Says she needs her jewels | Gives a believable reason to open the safe. |
| Shows no panic | Confidence makes her identity seem genuine. |

## Why Horace Does Not Suspect Her

Horace is under pressure. He has been caught in the act, he is sneezing, and he wants to escape prison. The young lady's calmness makes him believe she controls the situation.

He also wants to believe her because she offers him a way out.

## Her Psychological Strategy

She first frightens him with the possibility of police. Then she presents herself as someone who may forgive him. Finally, she asks for a favour: open the safe.

This sequence traps Horace:

1. fear;
2. hope;
3. gratitude;
4. obedience.

# Unit 4E: The Trick

## The Young Lady's Request

She says she has forgotten the safe combination and needs the jewels for a party. Horace offers to open the safe for her.

Because he believes she is the owner's wife, he thinks he is helping her and earning his freedom.

## Horace's Fatal Mistake

Horace removes his gloves to give her a lighter. Later he opens the safe without gloves.

His fingerprints are left in the room and on the safe area.

## Why This Is a Serious Mistake

Horace's main protection has always been carefulness. Without gloves, all his skill becomes useless. The evidence directly links him to the crime.

## What the Lady Gains

The young lady gets:

- the jewels;
- no fingerprints;
- no need to open the safe herself;
- a scapegoat;
- time to escape.

Horace gets:

- false relief;
- no jewels;
- no books;
- police arrest;
- prison.

# Unit 4F: Arrest and Irony

## Who Is the Real Culprit?

The real culprit is the young lady dressed in red. She is also a thief.

Horace is guilty of attempted theft and safe-breaking, but he does not get the jewels. He is tricked into helping the real thief.

## Why Horace Is Arrested

The police arrest Horace because:

- jewels are stolen from Shotover Grange;
- his fingerprints are all over the room;
- he has a criminal past;
- his explanation sounds unbelievable;
- the real wife is not young but a grey-haired woman of sixty.

## Why No One Believes Him

His story sounds absurd: he claims the owner's wife asked him to break open her own safe. When the actual wife denies it, his defence collapses.

## Ending

Horace becomes assistant librarian in prison. This is ironic because:

- he loves books;
- he steals to buy books;
- now he works with books in prison;
- his love of books leads indirectly to his imprisonment.

## Why He Gets Angry at "Honour Among Thieves"

The young lady was in the same profession as Horace, but she deceived him. There was no loyalty or honour. She used him as a tool.

# Unit 4G: Thinking About It

## 1. Did You Suspect the Lady?

Possible answer:

At first, the lady seems genuine because she is calm and knows the dog. Suspicion may arise when she asks Horace to open the safe instead of calling the police. Her request is unusual, but Horace is too desperate to question it.

## 2. Subtle Ways She Deceives Horace

| Deception | Effect |
|---|---|
| Uses calm authority | Makes Horace feel she belongs there. |
| Calls the dog by name | Shows apparent household familiarity. |
| Arranges ornaments | Acts like owner of the room. |
| Talks about husband and jewels | Creates believable domestic story. |
| Offers to let him go | Makes Horace trust her kindness. |
| Lets him open the safe | Avoids leaving her own evidence. |

## 3. Why the Description of Horace Is Apt

"Good and respectable, but not completely honest" is apt because Horace is orderly, successful, gentle, and non-violent, yet he steals every year.

He cannot be called honest simply because his manners are respectable.

## 4. Where Does Horace Go Wrong?

Horace goes wrong when he:

- believes the young lady too quickly;
- removes his gloves;
- opens the safe for her;
- forgets that another thief may be more clever;
- lets fear of prison overcome judgement;
- trusts appearance over evidence.

## 5. Was Horace Unfairly Punished?

Balanced answer:

Horace did not steal the jewels for himself, so in that sense he is tricked. But he entered the house to rob it and opened the safe illegally. He is not innocent.

He may be unlucky, but he is not unfairly punished.

## 6. Do Intentions Justify Actions?

Horace steals to buy books, not to hurt people. But a refined intention does not justify theft.

Wrong action remains wrong when it:

- violates another person's property;
- breaks trust;
- depends on deception;
- harms society;
- excuses selfish desire.

Some emergency situations may complicate honesty, but Horace's situation is not an emergency. He steals for personal pleasure.

## Final Chapter Summary

- Horace Danby appears respectable but secretly steals once a year.
- He steals to buy rare and expensive books.
- He carefully plans to rob the safe at Shotover Grange.
- A young lady appears and Horace assumes she is the owner's wife.
- She cleverly convinces him to open the safe for her.
- Horace removes his gloves and leaves fingerprints.
- The lady escapes with the jewels.
- The real wife is a grey-haired woman of sixty, so Horace's story is not believed.
- Horace is arrested and becomes assistant librarian in prison.
- The story shows that there is no honour among thieves and that Horace is caught by another thief's cleverness.
