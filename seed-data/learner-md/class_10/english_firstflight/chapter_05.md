## Big Ideas

This chapter gives three prose glimpses of India:

- Goa through bread and memory;
- Coorg through landscape, people, and adventure;
- Assam through tea gardens and legends.

The poem "The Trees" shifts from regional description to symbolic imagination. It presents trees breaking out of an artificial indoor space and returning to the forest.

The prose celebrates cultural variety. The poem asks what happens when nature, or people, are confined too long.

# Unit 5B: A Baker from Goa

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | A Baker from Goa |
| **Form** | Pen-portrait / nostalgic prose |
| **Main Figure** | Traditional Goan baker, called pader |
| **Main Theme** | Bread-making remains important in Goan culture and memory. |

## Short Summary

The narrator remembers traditional Goan village bakers from childhood. Elders speak nostalgically of Portuguese times and famous loaves of bread. Though the Portuguese are gone, the bakers and furnaces remain.

The baker, called the pader, used to come twice a day: once while setting out to sell bread and again after emptying his basket. Children ran to him not for ordinary loaves but for bread-bangles and sweet bread.

Bread was important in Goan social life. Sweet bread called bol was essential for marriage gifts; sandwiches were needed for engagement; cakes and bolinhas were necessary for Christmas and festivals.

The baker wore distinctive clothing. In old Portuguese times he wore the kabai. In the narrator's childhood, bakers wore shirts and trousers shorter than full-length ones but longer than half pants. Baking was profitable, and bakers looked prosperous.

# Unit 5C: Coorg

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Coorg |
| **Form** | Travel prose |
| **Region** | Kodagu / Coorg, Karnataka |
| **Main Features** | Coffee, rainforests, spices, martial people, wildlife, adventure sports |

## Short Summary

Coorg, also called Kodagu, lies between Mysore and Mangalore. It is Karnataka's smallest district and is known for coffee, spices, evergreen rainforests, rolling hills, and brave people.

The Coorg people are described as fiercely independent and possibly of Greek or Arabic descent. Their hospitality, martial traditions, and connection to the Indian Army are emphasised.

Coorg also offers wildlife, river experiences, rafting, canoeing, rappelling, rock climbing, mountain biking, trekking, Brahmagiri hills, Nisargadhama island, and the Tibetan settlement at Bylakuppe.

# Unit 5D: Tea from Assam

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Tea from Assam |
| **Main Characters** | Rajvir and Pranjol |
| **Setting** | Train journey to Assam and Dhekiabari Tea Estate |
| **Main Theme** | Tea culture, Assam's plantations, and curiosity about history. |

## Short Summary

Rajvir travels with his classmate Pranjol to Assam during summer vacation. Pranjol's father manages a tea garden in Upper Assam. On the train, Rajvir notices almost everyone drinking tea and says that over eighty crore cups are drunk daily in the world.

As the train enters tea country, Rajvir is fascinated by the green landscape, tea bushes, shade trees, and tea workers. Pranjol, being raised on a plantation, is less excited.

Rajvir shares legends about tea: one about a Chinese emperor whose boiling water was flavoured by leaves, and another about Bodhidharma, whose eyelids supposedly became tea plants. He also explains that tea was first drunk in China long ago and came to Europe in the sixteenth century.

At Dhekiabari Tea Estate, Rajvir identifies the second-flush period, which lasts from May to July and produces the best tea.

## Tea Facts

| Fact | Information |
|---|---|

| China | Tea was first drunk there as far back as 2700 BCE. |

| Early European use | More as medicine than beverage. |
| Best sprouting period | Second flush, May to July. |

# Unit 5F: The Trees

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | The Trees |
| **Poet** | Adrienne Rich |
| **Main Image** | Trees inside a house moving out into the forest |
| **Main Theme** | Nature, freedom, confinement, and release |
| **Tone** | Mysterious, urgent, symbolic |

## Short Summary

The poem imagines trees inside a house moving out into an empty forest. The roots try to free themselves from cracks in the veranda floor. Leaves strain toward glass, twigs stiffen with effort, and branches move like newly discharged patients.

The poet sits inside writing letters, barely mentioning the forest's departure from the house. The smell of leaves and lichen reaches inside. By the end, glass breaks, trees stumble into the night, winds rush to meet them, and the moon appears broken like a mirror through the tallest oak.

## Stanza 1: Empty Forest

Three things that cannot happen in a treeless forest:

- no bird can sit;
- no insect can hide;
- no sun can bury its feet in shadow.

The sun's "feet" may mean rays of sunlight reaching the ground. Without trees, there is no shadow for the sun to enter or rest in.

## Stanza 2: Trees Struggle to Leave

| Part | Action |
|---|---|
| **Roots** | Work to disengage from cracks in the veranda floor. |
| **Leaves** | Strain toward the glass. |
| **Twigs** | Become stiff with exertion. |
| **Boughs** | Shuffle under the roof. |

The branches are compared to newly discharged patients moving half-dazed toward clinic doors.

## Moon Imagery

| Moment | Description |
|---|---|
| Beginning of third stanza | The whole moon shines in an open sky. |
| End of poem | The moon is broken like a mirror. |

The change happens because the trees move out, and their branches divide the moonlight into flashing pieces.

## What Happens to the House?

The house loses its indoor forest. The glass breaks, the smell of leaves and lichen changes, and the silence of the next day is expected after the trees leave.

## Why Does the Poet Barely Mention It in Letters?

Possible reasons:

- the event is too strange to explain;
- people often remain silent about important changes;
- the poet may be embarrassed by the unexpected departure;
- the real meaning may be symbolic and hard to state directly.

## Possible Meanings

| Interpretation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Nature breaking free** | Trees kept indoors for decoration return to the forest. |
| **Human freedom** | Trees may symbolise people, especially those confined by social expectations, breaking out. |
| **Environmental warning** | Empty forests suggest human damage to nature. |
| **Inner liberation** | The movement may represent suppressed life and energy finally escaping. |

## Connection with "A Tiger in the Zoo"

| A Tiger in the Zoo | The Trees |
|---|---|
| Tiger is trapped in a cage. | Trees are trapped inside a house. |
| Wild habitat is imagined. | Empty forest waits for trees. |
| Captivity creates quiet rage. | Confinement creates pressure and breaking glass. |
| Animal freedom is denied. | Nature breaks out to reclaim freedom. |

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Personification** | Trees move, roots work, leaves strain. |
| **Simile** | Boughs like newly discharged patients. |
| **Imagery** | Moon, glass, leaves, roots, veranda, forest. |
| **Symbolism** | Trees symbolise nature or confined human beings. |
| **Sound and movement** | Breaking glass and stumbling trees create urgency. |
