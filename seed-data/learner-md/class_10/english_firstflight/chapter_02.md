## Big Ideas

The prose and poem both ask what freedom means.

Mandela's prose shows political freedom after generations of racial oppression. Freedom is not only personal; it must include dignity, equality, justice, and liberation from poverty and discrimination.

The poem "A Tiger in the Zoo" shows physical captivity. The tiger is alive, but his strength and natural dignity are trapped behind bars.

Together, the chapter teaches that life without freedom is incomplete, whether for people or animals.

# Unit 2B: Nelson Mandela - Long Walk to Freedom

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|

| **Form** | Autobiographical extract |
| **Speaker** | Nelson Mandela |
| **Main Event** | Presidential inauguration in 1994 |
| **Main Themes** | Freedom, democracy, sacrifice, courage, reconciliation, and human dignity |

## Short Summary

Mandela describes the bright morning of 10 May when his inauguration took place in Pretoria. International leaders and dignitaries came from many countries. The Union Buildings, once a symbol of white supremacy, became the site of a rainbow gathering for South Africa's first democratic, non-racial government.

Mandela took the oath and pledged to uphold the Constitution and serve the Republic and its people. He thanked international guests and declared the achievement a common victory for justice, peace, and human dignity.

The military display symbolised loyalty to the new democratic government. Mandela noticed the deep contrast: generals who would once have arrested him now saluted him. Two national anthems were sung, symbolising reconciliation between old divisions.

Mandela then reflects on South Africa's painful history. Apartheid created a deep wound, but it also produced courageous leaders. He says courage is not the absence of fear, but the triumph over it. He believes love is more natural than hate because people must learn to hate, and therefore can be taught to love.

Finally, Mandela explains freedom. As a child, he thought freedom meant simple pleasures. Later, he understood that true freedom means dignity, opportunity, family life, lawful living, and the freedom of all people. He concludes that the oppressor and the oppressed both lose humanity when freedom is denied.

## Mandela's Major Ideas

| Idea | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Freedom is collective** | One person is not truly free while others are oppressed. |
| **Courage conquers fear** | Brave people feel fear but overcome it. |
| **Love is natural** | Hatred is learned; love can be taught. |
| **Oppressor also loses humanity** | Prejudice imprisons the oppressor's mind. |
| **History matters** | The inauguration is meaningful because of past sacrifices. |
| **Political freedom is not enough** | Freedom must include liberation from poverty, deprivation, and discrimination. |

# Unit 2C: Prose Comprehension

## Oral Comprehension Check 1

### 1. Where did the ceremonies take place?

The ceremonies took place in the sandstone amphitheatre formed by the Union Buildings in Pretoria.

Indian public buildings made of sandstone include the Red Fort, Rashtrapati Bhavan, and several buildings in New Delhi's central administrative area.

### 2. How is 10 May an autumn day in South Africa?

South Africa is in the Southern Hemisphere, where seasons are opposite to those in the Northern Hemisphere. May is autumn there.

### 3. What is the "extraordinary human disaster" and the "glorious human achievement"?

The extraordinary human disaster is apartheid, a long system of racial oppression. The glorious human achievement is the establishment of democratic, non-racial government and political emancipation.

### 4. What does Mandela thank international leaders for?

He thanks them for coming to share in South Africa's victory for justice, peace, and human dignity.

### 5. What ideals does he set for South Africa?

He pledges to liberate people from poverty, deprivation, suffering, gender discrimination, and other forms of discrimination. He wants no person to oppress another again.

## Oral Comprehension Check 2

### 1. What do the military generals do? How has their attitude changed?

The generals salute Mandela and pledge loyalty. Earlier, they would have arrested him. Their changed attitude shows loyalty to democracy and the newly elected government.

### 2. Why were two national anthems sung?

Two anthems were sung to symbolise unity and reconciliation between groups that had once opposed each other. It represented the joining of old and new South Africa.

### 3. How does Mandela describe South African government in two periods?

| Period | Description |
|---|---|
| **First decade of twentieth century** | A system of racial domination was created. |
| **Final decade of twentieth century** | That system was replaced by one recognising rights and freedoms of all people. |

### 4. What does courage mean to Mandela?

Courage is not the absence of fear. It is the triumph over fear.

### 5. Which is natural, love or hate?

Mandela believes love is natural. People must learn to hate, and if they can learn hate, they can also be taught love.

## Oral Comprehension Check 3

### 1. What twin obligations does Mandela mention?

He mentions obligations to family and obligations to people, community, and country.

### 2. What did freedom mean to him as a boy and student?

As a boy, freedom meant running in fields, swimming, roasting mealies, and following tribal customs. As a student, it meant temporary freedoms such as staying out at night, reading what he liked, and going where he chose. Later, he wanted basic and honourable freedoms: earning a living, marrying, having a family, and living lawfully.

### 3. Does Mandela think the oppressor is free?

No. The oppressor is also not free because he is imprisoned by hatred, prejudice, and narrow-mindedness. Both oppressor and oppressed are robbed of humanity.

## Thinking About the Text

### 1. Why did many international leaders attend?

They attended because the inauguration marked the end of apartheid and the birth of democratic South Africa. It signified the triumph of justice, peace, and human dignity.

### 2. What does Mandela mean by being the sum of African patriots?

He means that his achievement belongs to all the freedom fighters before him. Their sacrifices, courage, and suffering made the day possible.

### 3. Do depths of oppression create heights of character?

Mandela suggests that severe oppression produced leaders of extraordinary courage and generosity. People such as Oliver Tambo and Walter Sisulu grew into great figures because they resisted injustice with dignity. Similar examples can be found in many freedom struggles where hardship produced moral courage.

### 4. How did his understanding of freedom change?

His idea of freedom grew from personal freedom to social freedom. At first, he wanted freedom for himself. Later, he realised that his own freedom was incomplete while his people were not free.

### 5. How did his hunger for freedom change his life?

It transformed him from a frightened young man into a bold one, from a law-abiding attorney into a person treated as a criminal, and from a family-loving man into someone forced to live apart from home.

## Definite Article with Proper Names

Normally, we do not use **the** before proper names. But when used with proper names, **the** can mean "people of that type".

| Sentence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| the Oliver Tambos, the Walter Sisulus | men like Oliver Tambo and Walter Sisulu; leaders of that type |
| the Amitabh Bachchans and Shah Rukh Khans | famous film stars and celebrities of that type |
| Madhuri Dixit is the Madhubala of our times | Madhuri Dixit is comparable to Madhubala in beauty, talent, or fame |
| the Alexanders, Napoleons and Hitlers | conquerors, rulers, and dictators of those types |

## Colour Prejudice Paragraph

Model paragraph:

Colour prejudice exists when people judge others by skin tone. In many societies, lighter skin is unfairly associated with beauty, status, or success. This prejudice appears in jokes, marriage preferences, advertising, and casual comments. It is harmful because it reduces a person's worth to appearance. A fair society must value character, ability, kindness, and dignity instead of colour. Schools and families can help by challenging biased language and celebrating all skin tones equally.

# Unit 2E: Speaking and Writing

## Speech Topic

**True liberty is freedom from poverty, deprivation, and all forms of discrimination.**

## Points for Speech

| Point | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Political freedom** | Right to vote and participate in government. |
| **Economic freedom** | Freedom from hunger, poverty, and unemployment. |
| **Social freedom** | Freedom from caste, gender, race, religion, and class discrimination. |
| **Human rights** | Constitutionally protected dignity and equality. |
| **Responsibility** | Citizens must oppose prejudice and injustice. |

## Two-Minute Speech Model

Respected teacher and my dear friends,

Today I will speak on the idea that true liberty is freedom from poverty, deprivation, and discrimination. A country may be politically independent, but people are not truly free if they are hungry, denied education, or treated unfairly because of gender, caste, religion, race, or class.

Nelson Mandela teaches us that freedom must include dignity. If a child cannot study because of poverty, that child is not fully free. If a woman is denied opportunity because of gender, society is not fully free. If a person is judged by colour or background, liberty remains incomplete.

Therefore, true liberty requires justice, equal opportunity, education, and respect for human rights. Freedom is meaningful only when it reaches every person. Thank you.

# Unit 2F: A Tiger in the Zoo

## Text Identity

| **Poet** | Leslie Norris |
| **Main Theme** | Captivity versus freedom |
| **Central Contrast** | Tiger in cage and tiger in natural habitat |
| **Mood** | Sad, reflective, and critical |

## Short Summary

The poem describes a tiger locked in a zoo cage. In the cage, he walks only a few steps and is full of quiet rage. The poet imagines where the tiger should be: in the forest, sliding through grass, near a water hole, or at the jungle's edge.

Instead, he is trapped in a concrete cell, ignoring visitors. At night, he hears patrolling cars and stares at the stars. The poem highlights the loss of freedom and dignity caused by captivity.

## Debate: Are Zoos Necessary?

| For Zoos | Against Zoos |
|---|---|
| Protect endangered species. | Animals lose natural freedom. |
| Help public learn about wildlife. | Captivity causes stress. |
| Support breeding programmes. | Cages cannot replace habitats. |
| Provide medical care. | Animals become entertainment. |

Balanced view:

Zoos may be justified only when they focus on conservation, rescue, research, and ethical care. They should not exist merely for entertainment.
