## Big Ideas

"Madam Rides the Bus" is a story about an eight-year-old girl's first independent journey outside her village. The bus ride begins as a dream of excitement and freedom, but it becomes a deeper experience when Valli sees death on the roadside.

"The Tale of Custard the Dragon" is a humorous ballad. It reverses expectations: the character called cowardly becomes the real hero, while the loudly brave characters run away.

Both texts question appearances:

- Valli looks like a child but plans like a careful adult.
- Custard looks terrifying but is called cowardly.
- The boastful pets sound brave but fail when danger arrives.
- A joyful bus ride suddenly reveals the reality of death.

# Unit 7B: Madam Rides the Bus - Part I

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Madam Rides the Bus |
| **Form** | Short story |
| **Main Character** | Valliammai, called Valli |
| **Age** | Eight years old |
| **Setting** | A village, a bus route, and the nearby town |
| **Central Event** | Valli's first bus journey alone |
| **Main Theme** | A child's first independent step into the larger world |

## Valli's Favourite Pastime

Valli has no playmates of her age in her street. Her favourite pastime is standing at the front doorway of her house and watching the street.

For other children, this may seem ordinary. For Valli, it is exciting because every passer-by, vehicle, and conversation gives her a new experience.

## Why the Bus Fascinates Her

The bus travels between her village and the nearest town. It passes through her street every hour, once going toward town and once returning.

The bus gives Valli:

- new passengers to observe;
- stories from outside the village;
- a sense of movement;
- a dream of travel;
- curiosity about the unknown town.

## Valli's Strongest Desire

Her strongest desire is to ride the bus at least once.

The desire grows slowly:

1. She watches the bus.
2. She sees passengers getting on and off.
3. She hears people describe the town.
4. She imagines the journey.
5. The wish becomes an overwhelming desire.

## Details Valli Finds Out

Valli carefully gathers information before acting.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Distance to town** | Six miles. |
| **Fare one way** | Thirty paise. |
| **Fare both ways** | Sixty paise. |
| **Time one way** | Forty-five minutes. |
| **Possible schedule** | Leave at one o'clock, reach town at one forty-five, return by about two forty-five. |
| **How to return** | Stay in the same bus and pay another fare. |

## How She Finds Out

Valli does not ask openly because she wants to keep her plan secret. She:

- listens to conversations;
- observes passengers;
- asks careful questions;
- remembers details;
- calculates timings and money.

## Oral Comprehension Check

| Question | Model Answer |
|---|---|
| What was Valli's favourite pastime? | Standing at the front doorway and watching the street. |
| What was a source of unending joy for her? | The bus passing through her street. |
| What was her strongest desire? | To ride the bus at least once. |
| What did she find out about the bus journey? | Distance, fare, travel time, and how to return on the same bus. |
| How did she find out these details? | By listening to people and asking discreet questions. |
| What was she planning to do? | She was planning to ride the bus to town and back alone. |

## Character Trait: Curiosity with Planning

Valli is not merely dreamy. She is observant and practical. Her curiosity becomes a plan because she collects facts and works out the journey.

# Unit 7C: Madam Rides the Bus - Part II

## Valli Boards the Bus

One spring afternoon, Valli stops the bus and says she has to go to town. She has the money ready. The conductor is amused by her confidence and calls her "madam" jokingly.

This moment shows:

- courage;
- preparation;
- self-confidence;
- desire to be treated seriously.

## Why the Conductor Calls Her "Madam"

The conductor calls Valli "madam" because she behaves with adult-like seriousness. He is teasing her affectionately, not insulting her. He enjoys her boldness and treats her like a grown passenger.

## Valli's Reaction to Help

The conductor offers to help her get on the bus. Valli refuses, saying she can get on by herself.

This tells us that she:

- wants independence;
- dislikes being treated as helpless;
- is proud of managing alone;
- wants to prove she is not a small child.

## Inside the Bus

Valli notices the bus carefully.

| Detail | What Valli Notices |
|---|---|
| **Exterior** | White paint with green stripes. |
| **Interior** | Shining overhead bars. |
| **Clock** | Beautiful clock above the windshield. |
| **Seats** | Soft and luxurious. |
| **Window** | Lower view blocked by a canvas blind. |

## Why Valli Stands on the Seat

The lower part of the window is covered, so Valli cannot see properly while sitting. She stands on the seat to look over the blind.

From the bus she sees:

- a canal;
- palm trees;
- grassland;
- distant mountains;
- blue sky;
- a ditch;
- green fields.

The repetition of green in the description shows her wonder.

## Adult Concern vs Child Pride

An elderly man asks her to sit down because standing may be unsafe. Valli becomes annoyed because she thinks he is treating her like a child.

When he calls her a child, she says there is no child there and reminds him that she has paid her fare like everyone else.

## The Elderly Woman

An elderly woman sits beside Valli and asks questions. Valli dislikes her appearance, the smell of betel nut, and her interference.

Valli does not want to make friends with her because:

- she feels repelled by the woman's appearance and chewing;
- she does not want to be questioned;
- she wants privacy;
- she wants to enjoy the journey independently.

## Oral Comprehension Check

| Question | Model Answer |
|---|---|
| Why does the conductor call Valli "madam"? | He jokingly treats her like a grown-up because she is confident and serious. |
| Why does Valli stand up on the seat? | She wants to see outside over the canvas blind. |
| What does she see? | Canal, palm trees, grassland, mountains, sky, ditch, and green fields. |
| What does she tell the elderly man? | She says she is not a child and has paid her fare like everyone else. |
| Why does she avoid the elderly woman? | She dislikes the woman's appearance, betel chewing, and intrusive questions. |

## Dialogue and Humour

The conductor's teasing creates humour. Valli is small, but she speaks with great seriousness. The contrast between her age and her adult-like tone makes the scene lively.

# Unit 7D: Madam Rides the Bus - Part III

## How Valli Saves Money

Valli needs sixty paise for the round trip. This is difficult for her because she rarely gets money.

She saves by:

- keeping stray coins;
- not buying peppermints;
- avoiding toys;
- resisting balloons;
- giving up the merry-go-round at the fair.

This shows strong self-control. Her dream matters more than immediate pleasure.

## How She Leaves the House

Valli chooses the afternoon because her mother usually naps after lunch from about one to four. She uses this time to leave secretly, ride to town, return, and come back before she is missed.

Her plan is risky, but it is carefully timed.

## The Journey to Town

During the journey, Valli watches the landscape and movement around her.

The bus seems to:

- cut across bare land;
- rush through hamlets;
- pass wayside shops;
- almost swallow vehicles and pedestrians;
- make trees seem to run toward and away from it.

The description captures how a child sees speed and motion.

## The Cow Episode

A young cow runs in front of the bus with its tail high. The driver honks repeatedly, but the frightened animal runs faster instead of moving aside.

Valli finds this extremely funny and laughs until there are tears in her eyes.

This scene shows her innocence. She sees the cow as a lively, comic creature.

## Arrival in Town

Valli sees:

- a railway crossing;
- a train passing with noise and force;
- a station;
- a busy shopping street;
- large shops;
- colourful displays;
- crowds;
- goods for sale.

She is struck with wonder, but she does not get off the bus.

## Why Valli Does Not Get Off

Valli does not get off because she only planned a bus ride, not a town visit. She is also afraid to move around in town alone.

She pays another thirty paise and decides to return in the same bus.

## Why She Refuses the Drink

The conductor offers to buy her a cold drink. Valli refuses.

This shows:

- self-respect;
- caution;
- independence;
- unwillingness to accept charity;
- discipline about money and plan.

## Oral Comprehension Check

| Question | Model Answer |
|---|---|
| How did Valli save money? | She saved stray coins and gave up small pleasures like sweets, toys, balloons, and a fair ride. |
| Was it easy? | No. It required discipline and sacrifice. |
| What made her laugh on the way? | A young cow running in front of the bus. |
| Why did she not get off at the bus station? | She was afraid to wander alone and had planned only to ride the bus. |
| Why did she refuse the drink? | She did not want a free treat and wanted to manage with her own money. |
| What does this tell us about her? | She is proud, careful, independent, and self-respecting. |

# Unit 7E: Madam Rides the Bus - Part IV

## Return Journey

On the way back, Valli is still excited at first. She sees the same sights again but does not feel bored. The world outside the village still seems wonderful.

Then the mood changes.

## The Dead Cow

Valli sees a young cow lying dead by the roadside. It is the same cow that made her laugh earlier.

This shocks her because the animal that had seemed lively and funny has suddenly lost its life.

## Emotional Change

| Before Seeing the Dead Cow | After Seeing the Dead Cow |
|---|---|
| Joyful | Sad |
| Curious | Quiet |
| Laughing | Haunted |
| Looking outside eagerly | Refuses to look out |
| Enjoying movement | Aware of death |

This is the story's emotional turning point.

## Why Valli Stops Looking Out

The sight of the dead cow disturbs her deeply. Her enthusiasm is dampened, and the memory haunts her. She no longer wants the same view because the outside world now contains sorrow as well as beauty.

## Return Home

Valli reaches her village and says goodbye politely to the conductor. She returns home and finds her mother talking to her aunt.

Her mother says that many things happen around us and in the outside world that we may know about but not fully understand.

Valli agrees because she has just experienced something like that: she has seen life, joy, death, and secrecy during one short journey.

## Why Valli Smiles Secretly

Valli smiles because her mother and aunt do not know what she has done. She has had a private experience that has changed her. The adults think she is merely a child interfering in conversation, but Valli knows she has seen something they do not know about.

## Oral Comprehension Check

| Question | Model Answer |
|---|---|
| Why does Valli refuse to look out on the way back? | She has seen the dead cow and feels deeply saddened. |
| What does she mean by agreeing with her mother? | She means that things can happen without others knowing, and even when known, they may not be fully understood. |
| Why is the cow important to the story? | It changes the journey from pure excitement into an encounter with death. |
| Why does Valli keep quiet at home? | She wants to keep her bus journey secret. |

# Unit 7F: Thinking About the Text

## Valli's Deepest Desire

Valli's deepest desire is to ride the bus. The story shows this through descriptions of her wish growing, her long looks at passengers, her listening to conversations, and her careful planning.

Key clues:

## Why the Conductor Calls Valli "Madam"

The conductor is amused by Valli's formal seriousness. He sees that she is a child trying to act grown-up, so he jokingly calls her "madam". The joke is affectionate because he helps her and keeps the conversation friendly.

## Lines and Moments Showing Enjoyment

Valli enjoys the ride when:

- she devours the bus interior with her eyes;
- she stands to see the landscape;
- she watches fields, sky, canal, and mountains;
- she laughs at the cow;
- she is amazed by the train and town shops;
- she feels the same excitement again on the return journey before seeing the dead cow.

## Why She Refuses to Look Out on the Way Back

The dead cow destroys her innocent joy. Earlier she had laughed at the same animal's playful panic. Seeing it lifeless makes her understand that the world outside contains pain and death too.

## Meaning of Valli's Agreement with Her Mother

Valli's mother says that many things happen in the world and that even when we know about them, we do not always understand them fully.

Valli agrees because:

- her own bus journey happened without her mother's knowledge;
- she saw the cow alive and then dead;
- she now knows death exists in a more personal way;
- she understands that experience is deeper than information.

## Child's Point of View

The story is told in a way that respects how a child sees the world.

| Evidence | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Bus as wonder | Adults may see a bus as ordinary; Valli sees it as magical. |
| Thirty paise as fortune | The fare seems huge to a child with little money. |
| Adult concern as interference | Valli thinks safety advice is an insult. |
| Cow as comedy | A running cow appears hilarious to her. |
| Dead cow as shock | Death becomes real through a familiar creature. |
| Town as dazzling | Shops and crowds seem grand and overwhelming. |

The story contains many voices:

- speeding vehicles;

A ticket collage can help students compare:

- bus tickets;
- train tickets;
- cinema tickets;
- plane tickets;

Valli saves carefully. Learners can discuss:

Valli is troubled by the dead cow. Learners can discuss an incident that discouraged or disturbed them and how they responded.

# Unit 7H: Poem - The Tale of Custard the Dragon

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | The Tale of Custard the Dragon |
| **Poet** | Ogden Nash |
| **Form** | Humorous ballad / comic narrative poem |
| **Main Character** | Custard, the dragon |
| **Main Theme** | Real courage may appear where others least expect it. |
| **Tone** | Comic, playful, mock-heroic, rhythmic. |

## What Is a Ballad?

A ballad is a poem or song that tells a story. Traditional ballads often deal with adventure, danger, love, courage, or heroic action.

This poem uses ballad form humorously. It sounds like a story of heroic courage, but it makes fun of boastful bravery and celebrates the supposedly cowardly dragon.

## Characters and Pet Names

| Character | Pet Name / Description |
|---|---|
| **Belinda** | Little girl who lives in the white house. |
| **Black kitten** | Ink. |
| **Grey mouse** | Blink. |
| **Yellow dog** | Mustard. |
| **Dragon** | Custard. |
| **Pirate** | Intruder and villain. |

## Plot Summary

Belinda lives with her pets: Ink the kitten, Blink the mouse, Mustard the dog, and Custard the dragon. The other characters claim to be brave, but Custard often cries for a safe cage. Because of this, everyone calls him cowardly and laughs at him.

One day a pirate enters the house. The supposedly brave characters panic and hide or run away. Custard, the one mocked as a coward, attacks the pirate and saves everyone.

Afterward, Belinda and the pets praise Custard, but soon the old boasting returns. Mustard, Ink, and Blink claim they would have been brave if circumstances had been different. Custard politely agrees and continues asking for a safe cage.

## Why Custard Is Called Cowardly

Custard is called cowardly because he keeps asking for a safe cage despite being a dragon. Dragons are usually imagined as fierce creatures with teeth, claws, scales, and fire. The contrast between his frightening appearance and timid wish creates humour.

## Why Belinda Tickles Him

Belinda tickles Custard because she and the other pets tease him for being timid. Their laughter is unkind, but the poem presents it in a comic tone.

## Appearance of Custard

Custard looks physically dangerous. He has:

- sharp teeth;
- spikes;
- scales;
- a fiery-looking mouth;
- a chimney-like nose;
- dagger-like toes.

The joke is that a creature who looks so fearsome wants safety and comfort.

## The Pirate Scene

The pirate appears suddenly and looks dangerous. He has weapons and a threatening appearance.

When danger becomes real:

| Character | Reaction |
|---|---|
| **Belinda** | Cries for help. |
| **Mustard** | Runs away frightened. |
| **Ink** | Slips away to hide. |
| **Blink** | Hides cleverly. |
| **Custard** | Attacks the pirate. |

This reversal is the heart of the poem.

## Real Courage vs Boasting

| False Bravery | Real Courage |
|---|---|
| Loudly claiming to be brave. | Acting when danger arrives. |
| Laughing at someone else's fear. | Protecting others despite fear. |
| Imagining heroic actions. | Facing the actual threat. |
| Making excuses afterward. | Doing what is needed. |

Custard is the real hero because he acts.

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Simile** | Comparisons are used to make courage, movement, and attack vivid. |
| **Repetition** | Repeated phrases about the little house, pets, and Custard create song-like rhythm. |
| **Alliteration** | Repeated consonant sounds make lines playful and musical. |
| **Humour** | A fierce dragon wanting a safe cage is comic. |
| **Parody** | The poem imitates heroic ballads but turns them into playful comedy. |
| **Imagery** | Teeth, spikes, weapons, and action create strong visual pictures. |
| **Onomatopoeia-like sound** | Invented and sound-rich words create comic noise and movement. |
| **Contrast** | Boastful pets hide; timid Custard fights. |

## Rhyme Scheme

Many stanzas follow paired rhymes, commonly close to `aabb`.

Example pattern:

| Line Ending Pattern | Effect |
|---|---|
| **1 rhymes with 2** | Creates a quick comic beat. |
| **3 rhymes with 4** | Completes the stanza neatly. |
| **Regular rhyme** | Makes the poem easy to read aloud. |

Because Ogden Nash often plays with sound, some rhymes are deliberately comic or exaggerated rather than smooth and serious.

## Rhythm

The poem is meant to be read aloud. Its rhythm is energetic and playful, like a story-song.

Reading aloud helps learners notice:

- repeated names;
- quick action;
- comic pauses;
- dramatic danger;
- exaggerated rhymes.

## Is the Poem Serious or Light-Hearted?

The poem is mostly light-hearted. It contains danger, but the style is comic.

Reasons:

- funny pet names;
- exaggerated bravery;
- mock-heroic description;
- comic rhymes;
- Custard's polite humility;
- boastful excuses after the danger is over.

However, it also teaches a serious lesson: real courage is proved through action, not claims.

## Images in the Poem

| Image | Effect |
|---|---|
| Little white house | Creates a simple, storybook setting. |
| Black kitten, grey mouse, yellow dog | Gives visual colour and childlike charm. |
| Dragon's teeth, spikes, and scales | Makes Custard look fearsome. |
| Pirate with weapons | Creates danger and adventure. |
| Pets hiding or fleeing | Creates comedy through reversal. |
| Dragon attacking | Creates heroic action. |

## Important Questions and Answers

| Question | Model Answer |
|---|---|

# Unit 7I: Writing Your Own Ballad

## Purpose of a Ballad

A ballad should:

- tell a simple story;
- interest or entertain the reader;
- include a few memorable characters;
- build toward a clear event or conflict;
- use rhythm and rhyme;
- possibly repeat a refrain.

## Planning a Ballad

| Step | Question |
|---|---|
| **Theme** | What is the ballad about? Courage, friendship, mistake, journey, rescue? |
| **Characters** | Who appears in the story? |
| **Setting** | Where does the action happen? |
| **Conflict** | What problem or danger appears? |
| **Action** | What do the characters do? |
| **Twist** | Is there a surprise or reversal? |
| **Ending** | What changes by the end? |

## Suggested Structure

| Stanza | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **1** | Introduce setting and characters. |
| **2** | Show habits or claims. |
| **3** | Introduce danger or conflict. |
| **4** | Show reactions. |
| **5** | Show the hero's action. |
| **6** | End with humour, lesson, or repeated refrain. |

## Revision Checklist

Before finalising a ballad, check:

- Does it tell a complete story?
- Are there only a few major characters?
- Does the rhythm work aloud?
- Do the rhymes sound natural or deliberately comic?
- Is there a strong central event?
- Is the ending satisfying?
- Have unnecessary lines been removed?
