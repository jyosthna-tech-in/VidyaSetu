## Big Ideas

The prose and poems in this chapter are short, but each carries a larger meaning.

**A Letter to God** asks: What happens when pure faith meets ordinary human systems? Lencho's faith is admirable, but his suspicion of the post office workers creates the story's irony.

**Dust of Snow** shows that even a small natural incident can save a bad day.

**Fire and Ice** shows that intense desire and cold hatred are both powerful enough to destroy.

Together, the chapter explores belief, emotion, nature, and human response.

# Unit 1B: A Letter to God

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | A Letter to God |
| **Genre** | Short story |
| **Main Character** | Lencho |
| **Setting** | A lonely house in a valley, fields of corn, and a post office |
| **Central Conflict** | Lencho's crop is destroyed, and he turns to God for help. |
| **Central Irony** | Lencho calls the helpful postal workers crooks. |

## Short Summary

Lencho is a farmer who lives with his family in the only house in a valley. He depends on his cornfield. When clouds gather, he hopes for rain because the crop needs water. At first, the rain fills him with joy, and he compares raindrops to new coins because they promise a good harvest.

But the rain turns into a hailstorm. The hail destroys the cornfield completely. Lencho and his family fear hunger.

Lencho has complete faith in God. He writes a letter asking God for one hundred pesos so that he can sow the field again and survive until the next crop.

At the post office, the postman and postmaster read the letter. They laugh at first, but the postmaster is moved by Lencho's faith. To preserve that faith, he collects money from employees and friends. He cannot collect the full hundred pesos, so he sends a little more than half and signs the letter as "God".

Lencho receives the money but is not surprised because he fully expected God to help him. However, he becomes angry when he finds only seventy pesos. He writes another letter asking God to send the remaining money, but not through the mail, because he believes the post office employees are crooks.

## Character Sketch: Lencho

| Trait | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Hard-working** | He knows his fields intimately and works like an animal. |
| **Faithful** | He writes directly to God and expects help. |
| **Innocent / naive** | He believes God will send money through the post. |
| **Simple** | He sees the world through direct trust and suspicion. |
| **Unquestioning** | He does not doubt God's ability to help. |
| **Ironically ungrateful** | He accuses the very people who helped him. |

## Character Sketch: Postmaster

| Trait | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Amiable** | He is described as friendly. |
| **Sensitive** | He understands the depth of Lencho's faith. |
| **Generous** | He contributes part of his salary. |
| **Responsible** | He collects money to answer the letter. |
| **Respectful of faith** | He signs the letter as God to preserve Lencho's belief. |

## Main Themes

| Theme | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Faith** | Lencho's belief in God is absolute. |
| **Irony** | Human kindness is mistaken for dishonesty. |
| **Nature's power** | Rain becomes hail and destroys the crop. |
| **Poverty** | One ruined crop can mean hunger. |
| **Human compassion** | The postmaster and employees help a stranger. |
| **Innocence and suspicion** | Lencho trusts God completely but mistrusts people. |

# Unit 1C: Prose Comprehension

## Oral Comprehension Check 1

### 1. What did Lencho hope for?

Lencho hoped for rain, especially a downpour or at least a shower, because his ripe cornfield needed water.

### 2. Why did Lencho say raindrops were like new coins?

He said this because rain meant a good harvest, and a good harvest meant money. The big drops seemed like ten-cent pieces and the smaller drops like five-cent pieces.

### 3. How did the rain change? What happened to the fields?

The rain changed into a hailstorm with strong wind and large hailstones. The hail destroyed the cornfield, flowers, and leaves.

### 4. What were Lencho's feelings when the hail stopped?

Lencho felt sorrowful and hopeless. He believed the family would have no corn and might go hungry.

## Oral Comprehension Check 2

### 1. Who or what did Lencho have faith in?

Lencho had complete faith in God. He wrote a letter asking God for one hundred pesos.

### 2. Who read the letter?

A post office employee, who was also a postman, first read the letter. Then he showed it to the postmaster.

### 3. What did the postmaster do?

The postmaster decided to answer the letter to preserve Lencho's faith. He collected money from employees, friends, and his own salary, then sent it to Lencho signed as "God".

## Oral Comprehension Check 3

### 1. Was Lencho surprised to receive money?

No. He was not surprised because he had complete faith that God would help him.

### 2. What made him angry?

He became angry because he received only seventy pesos instead of the hundred pesos he had requested.

## Thinking About the Text

### 1. Who does Lencho have complete faith in? Which sentences show this?

Lencho has complete faith in God. This is shown when he writes a letter to God, expects help from God, and believes God could not make a mistake or deny him what he asked for.

### 2. Why does the postmaster send money and sign as God?

He sends money because he is moved by Lencho's faith and does not want to shake it. He signs as God so Lencho will believe that God answered the letter.

### 3. Did Lencho try to find out who sent the money?

No. Lencho did not try to find out because he was convinced the money came from God.

### 4. Who does Lencho think took the rest? What is the irony?

Lencho thinks the post office employees took the missing money. The irony is that those same employees had helped collect and send the money.

### 5. What kind of person is Lencho?

Lencho is best described as **naive**, **unquestioning**, and **comical** in his innocence. He is not greedy in the ordinary sense; he asks for what he thinks he needs. He appears ungrateful because he misunderstands the situation.

### 6. What are the two conflicts in the story?

| Conflict Type | Illustration |
|---|---|
| **Humans versus nature** | The hailstorm destroys Lencho's crop. |
| **Humans versus humans** | Lencho suspects the postal workers, though they helped him. |

## Relative Clauses

### What Is a Relative Clause?

A relative clause gives extra information about a noun.

Common relative pronouns:

- who;
- whom;
- whose;
- which.

### Non-Defining Relative Clause

It gives extra information about a known person or thing.

Example:

Lencho, who knew his fields intimately, looked at the sky.

## Relative Clause Answers

| Original Sentences | Joined Sentence |
|---|---|
| I often go to Mumbai. Mumbai is the commercial capital of India. | I often go to Mumbai, which is the commercial capital of India. |
| My mother is going to host a TV show on cooking. She cooks very well. | My mother, who cooks very well, is going to host a TV show on cooking. |
| These sportspersons are going to meet the President. Their performance has been excellent. | These sportspersons, whose performance has been excellent, are going to meet the President. |
| Lencho prayed to God. His eyes see into our minds. | Lencho prayed to God, whose eyes see into our minds. |
| This man cheated me. I trusted him. | This man, whom I trusted, cheated me. |

# Unit 1F: Dust of Snow

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | Dust of Snow |
| **Poet** | Robert Frost |
| **Form** | Short lyric poem |
| **Main Event** | A crow shakes snow from a hemlock tree onto the speaker. |
| **Main Theme** | A small natural incident can change a person's mood. |

## Short Summary

The speaker is in a bad mood and regrets part of the day. A crow shakes a little snow from a hemlock tree onto him. This small incident changes his mood and saves part of the day from being wasted in regret.

## Key Images

| Image | Usual Association | Effect in Poem |
|---|---|---|
| **Crow** | dark, harsh, often considered unlucky | becomes part of a healing moment |
| **Hemlock tree** | poisonous, gloomy | becomes source of mood change |
| **Dust of snow** | fine particles of snow | small but refreshing natural touch |

## Question Answers

### 1. What is "dust of snow"?

"Dust of snow" means fine powdery snow or tiny particles of snow.

### 2. What changed the poet's mood?

The fall of snow dust from a hemlock tree, shaken down by a crow, changed the poet's mood.

### 3. How did the mood change?

The mood changed from regretful, sad, or depressed to lighter and more hopeful.

### 4. How does Frost present nature?

Frost presents nature as surprising and healing. He does not use traditionally beautiful images like songbirds or flowering trees. Instead, he uses a crow and a hemlock tree to show that even ordinary or gloomy things can bring positive change.

## Symbolism

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Crow** | sorrow, darkness, or ordinary nature |
| **Hemlock** | gloom, poison, or negative mood |
| **Snow dust** | purity, freshness, unexpected change |
| **Falling snow** | sudden emotional shift |

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Imagery** | Crow, hemlock tree, dust of snow. |
| **Alliteration** | sounds repeated in phrases such as "Has given my heart". |
| **Symbolism** | Crow and hemlock represent gloom; snow represents transformation. |
| **Contrast** | Dark symbols bring a positive mood change. |
| **Rhyme scheme** | ABAB CDCD. |

## Main Message

Small moments can transform our mental state. Nature does not have to appear grand or conventionally beautiful to heal us.

# Unit 1G: Fire and Ice

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | Fire and Ice |
| **Poet** | Robert Frost |
| **Form** | Short symbolic poem |
| **Main Theme** | Desire and hatred are both destructive. |
| **Central Symbols** | Fire and ice |

## Short Summary

The speaker reflects on two possible ways the world may end: fire or ice. Fire is associated with desire, greed, fury, and uncontrolled passion. Ice is associated with hatred, coldness, indifference, and emotional hardness. The speaker first agrees with those who favour fire, but then says ice would also be enough for destruction.

## Symbol Table

| Symbol | Represents |
|---|---|
| **Fire** | desire, greed, passion, anger, lust, fury |
| **Ice** | hate, coldness, indifference, rigidity, cruelty |
| **World ending** | destruction caused by human emotions |

## Question Answers

### 1. What do fire and ice stand for?

Fire stands for intense desire, greed, anger, and passion. Ice stands for hatred, coldness, indifference, and emotional cruelty.

### 2. What is the rhyme scheme?

The rhyme scheme is:

**ABAABCBCB**

### 3. How does the rhyme scheme help?

The tight rhyme scheme gives the poem a compact, controlled shape. This control contrasts with the dangerous emotions described in the poem. The alternating rhymes also bring fire and ice into tension.

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Symbolism** | Fire and ice symbolise destructive emotions. |
| **Alliteration** | Repetition in phrases such as "favour fire". |
| **Anaphora** | Repetition of "Some say" at the beginning. |
| **Contrast** | Fire versus ice; desire versus hate. |
| **Understatement** | Saying ice would "suffice" calmly describes total destruction. |

## Main Message

The poem warns that the world can be destroyed not only by fiery passion and desire but also by cold hatred and indifference. Human emotions, when uncontrolled, can become destructive.

## Final Chapter Summary

- Lencho is a poor but hard-working farmer.
- He hopes for rain, but the rain turns into hail.
