## Big Ideas

This chapter has two prose stories and two poems.

The flying stories show that fear is overcome in different ways. The young seagull flies because hunger forces him into action. The pilot survives because he follows mysterious guidance in the storm.

The poems shift the mood. "How to Tell Wild Animals" uses danger comically, while "The Ball Poem" turns a small loss into a lesson about growing up.

Together, the chapter moves from physical flight to emotional maturity.

# Unit 3B: His First Flight

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | His First Flight |
| **Main Character** | Young seagull |
| **Conflict** | Fear of flying |
| **Turning Point** | Hunger drives him to dive for fish. |
| **Main Theme** | Courage often begins when necessity forces action. |

## Short Summary

A young seagull is alone on a ledge because his brothers and sister have already flown away. He is afraid that his wings will not support him. His parents scold, threaten, and call to him, but he cannot gather courage.

The next day, he is very hungry. His mother stands on the opposite plateau with a piece of fish. She flies toward him but stops just out of reach. Maddened by hunger, the seagull dives at the fish and falls from the ledge.

At first he is terrified, but then his wings spread naturally. He begins to soar, flap, and fly. His family circles around him and encourages him. Finally, he lands on the sea, panics briefly, then realises he can float. His first flight is complete.

## Why the Seagull Was Afraid

| Fear | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Height** | The sea looked far below the ledge. |
| **Self-doubt** | He thought his wings would not support him. |
| **Comparison** | His siblings had flown, but he could not. |
| **Unknown experience** | He had never flown before. |

## Role of the Parents

The parents use several methods:

- calling;
- scolding;
- threatening;
- refusing to feed him on the ledge;
- using food as temptation;
- encouraging him after he begins flying.

They are not cruel. They know he can fly, but he must take the first step himself.

## Question Answers

### 1. Why was the young seagull afraid to fly?

He was afraid because the sea looked far below, and he believed his wings would not support him. His fear was mental, not physical.

### 2. Are all young birds afraid? Is it like a baby learning to walk?

Some young birds may be more timid than others, just as some children are more hesitant. A baby taking first steps also faces uncertainty, balance, and fear of falling.

### 3. What does "The sight of the food maddened him" suggest?

It suggests that hunger became stronger than fear. The fish made him desperate enough to dive from the ledge.

### 4. What compelled him to fly?

Hunger and his mother's clever action compelled him. She brought the fish close but not close enough, forcing him to leap.

### 5. Why did the parents threaten and cajole him?

They wanted him to overcome fear and learn survival. A seagull must fly to find food and live independently.

### 6. Was success guaranteed?

In the seagull's case, flying was natural, but his fear made it difficult. In human experiences, success may not be guaranteed, but trying is necessary for growth.

## Theme

The story teaches that fear can disappear only after action begins. Courage does not always come before the attempt; sometimes it grows during the attempt.

# Unit 3C: The Black Aeroplane

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Title** | The Black Aeroplane |
| **Narrator** | Pilot of an old Dakota aeroplane |
| **Setting** | Night flight from France toward England |
| **Conflict** | Pilot enters storm clouds and loses instruments. |
| **Mystery** | A black aeroplane guides him, but no other plane appears on radar. |

## Short Summary

The narrator is flying an old Dakota aeroplane from France to England at night. The sky is clear, and he is excited to reach home for breakfast. Paris Control instructs him to turn twelve degrees west.

Soon he sees huge storm clouds ahead. He does not have enough fuel to fly around them, and he should return to Paris. However, he takes the risk because he wants to get home.

Inside the storm, everything becomes black. The compass, radio, and other instruments stop working. He is lost.

Suddenly, he sees another black aeroplane without lights. The other pilot signals him to follow. The narrator follows obediently. When his fuel is nearly finished, the black plane guides him down. He sees runway lights and lands safely.

At the control centre, a woman tells him no other aeroplane was flying that night. His was the only one visible on radar. The story ends with a mystery: who helped him?

## The Risk

| Risk | Reason He Takes It |
|---|---|
| Flying into storm clouds without enough fuel to return safely. | He wants to reach England, be with family, and have breakfast. |

The risk is partly practical and partly emotional. His desire to get home overrules caution.

## Experience Inside the Storm

Inside the storm:

- everything becomes black;
- the plane jumps and twists;
- the compass spins uselessly;
- radio contact fails;
- instruments stop working;
- the pilot loses direction;
- fear returns.

## Mystery of the Black Aeroplane

Possible interpretations:

| Interpretation | Reason |
|---|---|
| **A real but unknown plane** | The narrator clearly sees a pilot guiding him. |
| **A supernatural helper** | No plane appears on radar, and it has no lights. |
| **A vision caused by fear** | The pilot is under stress and may imagine guidance. |
| **Symbol of inner courage** | The black plane may represent the pilot's will to survive. |

The story deliberately leaves the answer open.

## Question Answers

### 1. What is the risk, and why does the narrator take it?

The risk is flying into dangerous storm clouds with limited fuel. He takes it because he wants to reach England and be with his family.

### 2. Describe his experience in the storm.

The plane is surrounded by darkness, jumps violently, and loses direction. The compass and radio stop working. The pilot cannot see outside and feels lost and frightened.

### 3. Why was he not sorry to walk away from the old Dakota?

He had just survived a dangerous flight. The plane had become unsafe inside the storm, and he was relieved to be alive and on the ground.

### 4. Why did the woman in the control centre look at him strangely?

She looked strangely because he asked about another plane, but radar showed no other aircraft in the storm.

### 5. Who helped him reach safely?

The answer is uncertain. It may have been a mysterious pilot, a supernatural presence, or the narrator's imagination under pressure. The strongest point is that the help cannot be explained by ordinary radar evidence.

# Unit 3E: How to Tell Wild Animals

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | How to Tell Wild Animals |
| **Poet** | Carolyn Wells |
| **Tone** | Humorous and playful |
| **Main Method** | Absurd advice for identifying dangerous animals |
| **Central Humour** | You identify animals only when they attack you. |

## Short Summary

The poem humorously tells readers how to identify wild animals such as the Asian lion, Bengal tiger, leopard, bear, hyena, crocodile, and chameleon. The advice is deliberately ridiculous because the animal is often identified only after it has attacked, eaten, hugged, or confused the observer.

## Animal Identification Table

| Animal | Comic Identification |
|---|---|
| **Asian lion** | Roars at you while you are dying. |
| **Bengal tiger** | Has black stripes on yellow and eats you. |
| **Leopard** | Has spotted hide and leaps on you repeatedly. |
| **Bear** | Gives a very hard hug. |
| **Hyena** | Appears to smile. |
| **Crocodile** | Appears to weep. |
| **Chameleon** | Hard to see; if nothing appears on the tree, it may be the chameleon. |

## Question Answers

### 1. Does "dyin'" rhyme with "lion"?

It can be made to rhyme if "dying" is pronounced informally as "dyin" with a sound close to "lion". The poet uses this playful pronunciation for rhyme and humour.

### 2. How does the poet suggest identifying lion and tiger?

The lion is identified by its roar when it attacks. The tiger is identified by black stripes on a yellow body and by the fact that it eats the observer. The humour is that identification comes too late.

### 3. Are "lept" and "lep" correct spellings?

No. Standard spelling would be **leapt** or **leaped**. The poet uses altered forms for rhyme, rhythm, and comic effect.

### 4. What is a bearhug?

A bearhug is a very strong hug. In the poem it becomes comic because the bear's "hug" is dangerous, not affectionate.

### 5. Correct "A novice might nonplus."

Correct prose form:

**A novice might be nonplussed.**

The poem's version is better poetically because it fits rhythm and rhyme.

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Humour** | Dangerous attacks are described as identification tips. |
| **Irony** | The advice is useless because the observer may not survive. |
| **Hyperbole** | The danger is exaggerated for comic effect. |
| **Rhyme** | Playful rhymes help create rhythm. |
| **Poetic liberty** | Spellings and grammar are bent for sound and humour. |

## Main Message

The poem is not a serious guide to wildlife. It is a comic poem that uses absurd logic and playful language to entertain readers.

# Unit 3F: The Ball Poem

## Text Identity

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Poem** | The Ball Poem |
| **Poet** | John Berryman |
| **Main Event** | A boy loses his ball in water. |
| **Main Theme** | Loss teaches responsibility and emotional maturity. |
| **Tone** | Serious, reflective, sympathetic |

## Short Summary

A boy loses his ball when it bounces into the water. The ball is not expensive, and another ball can be bought. Yet the boy is deeply upset because the lost ball is tied to memory and childhood. The poet does not interrupt him or offer money because the boy must learn the meaning of loss.

The poem suggests that everyone must learn that possessions are temporary and cannot always be restored. The boy is learning how to stand up after loss.

## Why the Ball Matters

| Surface View | Deeper Meaning |
|---|---|
| A cheap ball is lost. | Childhood memory and emotional attachment are lost. |
| Another ball can be bought. | The original experience cannot be replaced. |
| The boy is upset. | He is learning responsibility and loss. |

## Question Answers

### 1. Why does the poet not intrude or offer money?

The poet does not intrude because the boy must experience and understand loss himself. Money can buy another ball, but it cannot replace the emotional value of the lost one.

### 2. Is the ball linked to memories?

Yes. The phrase about his young days going into the harbour suggests that the ball is connected with memories of childhood and play.

### 3. What does "world of possessions" mean?

It means the world of things people own, value, lose, and must take responsibility for.

### 4. Has the boy lost anything earlier?

The poem suggests this is his first serious experience of loss. The phrase "first responsibility" points to this.

### 5. What is the boy learning?

He is learning that things will be lost, money cannot replace every loss, and one must accept loss and stand up again.

## Key Ideas

| Idea | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Loss** | A normal part of life. |
| **Responsibility** | Understanding that possessions can disappear. |
| **Memory** | Objects may carry emotional history. |
| **Maturity** | Growing up means accepting irreversible loss. |
| **Money is external** | Money cannot replace emotional attachment. |

## Poetic Devices

| Device | Example / Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Repetition** | "What, what" shows confusion and distress. |
| **Imagery** | Ball bouncing into water; boy staring into harbour. |
| **Symbolism** | The ball symbolises childhood and personal loss. |
| **Enjambment** | Lines flow into one another, showing emotional movement. |
| **Philosophical tone** | The poem reflects on the nature of loss. |
